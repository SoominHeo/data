How do we deal with body odor?

“에어컨 빵빵 틀며 살고 싶어요” 어느 중년남의 소망  

June 07,2013
The speed of gas diffusion is inversely proportional to temperature. Odor is gaseous, and the diffusion of odor is also inversely proportional to temperature.
We all know that odor spreads faster when it’s hot.

Restaurants in Hawaii know the science of odor very well and apply it to their business operation. Their air conditioning systems blow out cold air at full blast, and you get goose bumps in five minutes.

Search “what to bring to Hawaii,” and you’ll often see, “Make sure you bring a cardigan as restaurants have their ACs on very high.”

I always wondered why they kept the temperature so low and thought they were wasting energy
as I’ve always been taught to be frugal. But I realized during a seminar in Hawaii last year that it was a
prejudice, one that would break so easily.

An assistant of the seminar explained that it was an inevitable choice and business strategy. “It’s all because of the odor,” he said. All kinds of ethnic groups come to Hawaii, and due to its tropical climate, people get sweaty all year. And cold air keeps the odor from spreading. “Restaurants in Hawaii serve iced water in order to cover up the chlorine smell,” he added. I tried the water without ice, and the chlorine smell was intense.

Hawaii also taught me what “old man’s odor” was like. A colleague who attended the seminar with me always rode the bus while I walked 40 minutes every morning to the seminar venue. I always wondered why he would ride the bus. He said, “I don’t want to sweat and smell.” Then I realized that he didn’t sit next to me at the events.

I was also reminded of the “grandpa’s smell” from childhood. Young children often tried to stay away from the grandfather and would just snatch their allowance or snack and run away.

Last month, the Korea Institute of Science and Technology announced a study that found 85 percent of the sense of smell is acquired. Even the smells you dislike are acquired from learning. It is comforting that we learn to be sensitive to the “old man’s smell.”

However, it doesn’t solve the problem.

A few years ago, my wife bought me a fragrance and said, “After you are 50 years old, you begin to smell bad.” I had to take her advice, and sometimes, wearing too much perfume bothers people around me. The heat wave has already begun in early June.

And power shortages are expected in the summer, and Koreans are not likely to keep the air conditioners at full blast. Then, how should we deal with body odor in the hot summer?

*The author is an editorial writer of the JoongAng Ilbo.

by Yi Jung-jae
