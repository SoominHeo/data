Reinventing Lotte Group

롯데, 명실상부한 국민 기업으로 거듭나야  

Aug 18,2015
The fierce family feud over control of the Lotte Group, the fifth-largest conglomerate in Korea, has ended up with the victory of Shin Dong-bin, chairman of Lotte Hotel and the second son of the founder, Shin Kyuk-ho. In yesterday’s extraordinary shareholders meeting of Lotte Holdings of Japan in Tokyo, they passed two key agendas - the appointment of outside board members and management based on the law and principles. The two motions led by Chairman Shin can be passed only with the attendance of a majority of shareholders and the approval by two-thirds of them.

The passage of the motions means that Chairman Shin has taken control of the board. As Lotte Holdings is a company on the top of Lotte Group’s governance structure, the approval means Shin has taken control of the entire business group. The possibility of his elder brother Shin Dong-joo, former vice chairman of Lotte Holdings, continuing to fight for control of the group appears slim.

As the shareholders meeting is just the beginning of the reinvention of the conglomerate, Chairman Shin must fist reestablish the identity of Lotte. The family feud made our people seriously wonder if the conglomerate is really a Korean company given the weird governance structure in which Lotte Holdings of Japan dominates the Lotte Group in Korea. New Chairman Shin has repeatedly underscored that Lotte is a Korean company. He also pledged to cut off the complex links of governance structure between Korea and Japan through easing intricately connected cross-shareholdings among a myriad of affiliates and turning Lotte Hotel in Seoul into a listed firm in the local bourse. We will watch if he keeps his promise.

In the age of globalization, companies’ nationalities do not carry great significance. But Lotte is different as it was able to achieve remarkable growth thanks to the government’s granting of special benefits and public support. The group is also running duty-free shops across the country. Given such privileges offered, Lotte must be born again as a Korean business group to repay the favors.

At the same time, we must end the meaningless debate over the nationality of Lotte. Blaming the owners just because they speak Japanese or are a pro-Japanese company is not simply right. In the age of borderless competition, companies deserve credit for creating quality jobs. Lotte hires 350,000 Korean workers - the greatest number of employees among conglomerates. Building anti-corporate sentiment in the wake of the Lotte debacle is unfortunate for us.

JoongAng Ilbo, Aug. 18, page 34
