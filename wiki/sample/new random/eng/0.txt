<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">H II region</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>An <b>H II region</b> or HII region is a region of interstellar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_hydrogen" title="Atomic hydrogen">atomic hydrogen</a> that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionized" title="Ionized">ionized</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-definition_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-definition-1">[1]</a></sup> (H is the chemical symbol for hydrogen, and "II" is the Roman numeral for 2. It is customary in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> to use the Roman numeral I for neutral atoms, II for singly-ionised—H II is H<sup>+</sup> in other sciences—III for doubly-ionised, e.g. O III is O<sup>++</sup>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> H II, or H<sup>+</sup>, consists of free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>.) It is typically a cloud of partially ionized <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> has recently taken place, with a size ranging from one to hundreds of light years, and density from a few to about a million particles per cubic cm. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hertzsprung-Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung-Russell diagram">The short-lived blue stars</a> created in these regions emit copious amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light that ionize the surrounding gas. H II regions—sometimes several hundred <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> across—are often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_molecular_clouds" title="Giant molecular clouds">giant molecular clouds</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a>, now known to be an H II region, was observed in 1610 by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc" title="Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc">Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc</a> by telescope, the first such object discovered.</p>
<p>The term H II is pronounced "H two" by astronomers (an <a href="/wiki/H_I_region" title="H I region">H I region</a> being <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">neutral</a> atomic hydrogen, and a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular cloud</a> being <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecular</a> hydrogen, H<sub>2</sub>. In spoken discussion with non-astronomers there is sometimes confusion between the identical spoken forms of "H II" and "H<sub>2</sub>"). Such regions may be of any shape, because the distribution of the stars and gas inside them is irregular. They often appear clumpy and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/filamentary#en-n-astro" title="wikt:filamentary">filamentary</a>, sometimes showing bizarre shapes such as the <a href="/wiki/Horsehead_Nebula" title="Horsehead Nebula">Horsehead Nebula</a>. H II regions may give birth to thousands of stars over a period of several million years. In the end, <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> explosions and strong <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar winds</a> from the most massive stars in the resulting <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star cluster</a> will disperse the gases of the H II region, leaving behind a cluster of stars which have formed, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a>.</p>
<p>H II regions can be seen to considerable distances in the universe, and the study of extragalactic H II regions is important in determining the distance and chemical composition of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">Spiral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irregular_galaxy" title="Irregular galaxy">irregular</a> galaxies contain many H II regions, while <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical</a> galaxies are almost devoid of them. In spiral galaxies, including our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_(galaxy)" title="Milky Way (galaxy)">Milky Way</a>, H II regions are concentrated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_arm" title="Spiral arm">spiral arms</a>, while in irregular galaxies they are distributed chaotically. Some galaxies contain huge H II regions, which may contain tens of thousands of stars. Examples include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30_Doradus" title="30 Doradus">30 Doradus</a> region in the <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">Large Magellanic Cloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/NGC_604" title="NGC 604">NGC 604</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">Triangulum Galaxy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:247px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="2434" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg/245px-Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg/368px-Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg/490px-Eagle_nebula_pillars.jpg 2x" width="245"/></a>

Dark star-forming regions within the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Nebula" title="Eagle Nebula">Eagle Nebula</a> commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Creation" title="Pillars of Creation">Pillars of Creation</a></div>

<p>A few of the brightest H II regions are visible to the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a>. However, none seem to have been noticed before the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> in the early 17th century. Even <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a> did not notice the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a> when he first observed the <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star cluster</a> within it (previously cataloged as a single star, θ Orionis, by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bayer" title="Johann Bayer">Johann Bayer</a>). The French observer <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc" title="Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc">Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc</a> is credited with the discovery of the Orion Nebula in 1610.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-3">[3]</a></sup> Since that early observation large numbers of H II regions have been discovered in the Milky Way and other galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> observed the Orion Nebula in 1774, and described it later as "an unformed fiery mist, the chaotic material of future suns".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glyn_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glyn-5">[5]</a></sup> In early days astronomers distinguished between "diffuse nebulae" (now known to be H II regions), which retained their fuzzy appearance under magnification through a large telescope, and nebulae that could be resolved into stars, now know to be galaxies external to our own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Confirmation of Herschel's hypothesis of star formation had to wait another hundred years, when <a href="/wiki/William_Huggins" title="William Huggins">William Huggins</a> together with his wife <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Lindsay_Huggins" title="Margaret Lindsay Huggins">Mary Huggins</a> turned his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscope</a> on various nebulae. Some, such as the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Nebula</a>, had spectra quite similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, but turned out to be galaxies consisting of hundreds of millions of individual stars. Others looked very different. Rather than a strong continuum with absorption lines superimposed, the Orion Nebula and other similar objects showed only a small number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huggins_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huggins-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_nebulae" title="Planetary nebulae">planetary nebulae</a>, the brightest of these spectral lines was at a <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of 500.7 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a>, which did not correspond with a line of any known <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a>. At first it was hypothesized that the line might be due to an unknown element, which was named <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy#Nebulae" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">nebulium</a>—a similar idea had led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> through analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s spectrum in 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tennyson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tennyson-8">[8]</a></sup> However, while helium was isolated on earth soon after its discovery in the spectrum of the sun, nebulium was not. In the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell" title="Henry Norris Russell">Henry Norris Russell</a> proposed that rather than being a new element, the line at 500.7 nm was due to a familiar element in unfamiliar conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Interstellar matter, considered dense in an astronomical context, is at high vacuum by laboratory standards. Physicists showed in the 1920s that in gas at extremely low <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> can populate excited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy levels</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, which at higher densities are rapidly de-excited by collisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen-10">[10]</a></sup> Electron transitions from these levels in <a href="/wiki/Doubly_ionized_oxygen" title="Doubly ionized oxygen">doubly ionized oxygen</a> give rise to the 500.7 nm line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODell2001_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODell2001-11">[11]</a></sup> These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines" title="Spectral lines">spectral lines</a>, which can only be seen in very low density gases, are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_line" title="Forbidden line">forbidden lines</a>. Spectroscopic observations thus showed that planetary nebulae consisted largely of extremely rarefied ionised oxygen gas (OIII).</p>
<p>In H II regions, however, the dominant spectral line has a wavelength of 656.3 nm. This is the well-known <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">H-alpha</a> line emitted by atomic hydrogen. Specifically, a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> of this wavelength is emitted when the electron of a hydrogen atom changes its <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excitation state</a> from n=3 to n=2. Such state-changes happen very frequently when an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> is captured by an ionised hydrogen atom (a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>), and the electron cascades down from some higher excitation state to n=1. Thus, it was concluded that H II regions consist of a mix of electrons and ionised hydrogen that are constantly recombining into hydrogen atoms.</p>
<p>During the 20th century, observations showed that H II regions often contained <a href="/wiki/OB_star" title="OB star">hot, bright stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODell2001_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODell2001-11">[11]</a></sup> These stars are many times more massive than the Sun, and are the shortest-lived stars, with total lifetimes of only a few million years (compared to stars like the Sun, which live for several billion years). Therefore, it was surmised that H II regions must be regions in which new stars were forming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODell2001_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODell2001-11">[11]</a></sup> Over a period of several million years, a cluster of stars will form in an H II region, before <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> from the hot young stars causes the nebula to disperse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a> are an example of a cluster which has 'boiled away' the H II region from which it was formed. Only a trace of <a href="/wiki/Reflection_nebula" title="Reflection nebula">reflection nebulosity</a> remains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_lifetime">Origin and lifetime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and lifetime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">Stellar evolution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:250px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1087" data-file-width="1076" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg/248px-Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg/372px-Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg/496px-Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg 2x" width="248"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tarantula_nebula_detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A small portion of the <a href="/wiki/Tarantula_Nebula" title="Tarantula Nebula">Tarantula Nebula</a>, a giant H II region in the <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">Large Magellanic Cloud</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The precursor to an H II region is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_molecular_cloud" title="Giant molecular cloud">giant molecular cloud</a> (GMC). A GMC is a cold (10–20 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) and dense cloud consisting mostly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> GMCs can exist in a stable state for long periods of time, but shock waves due to <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>, collisions between clouds, and magnetic interactions can trigger its collapse. When this happens, via a process of collapse and fragmentation of the cloud, stars are born (see <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a> for a lengthier description).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>As stars are born within a GMC, the most massive will reach temperatures hot enough to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionisation" title="Ionisation">ionise</a> the surrounding gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> Soon after the formation of an ionising radiation field, energetic <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> create an ionisation front, which sweeps through the surrounding gas at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> speeds. At greater and greater distances from the ionising star, the ionisation front slows, while the pressure of the newly ionised gas causes the ionised volume to expand. Eventually, the ionisation front slows to <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">subsonic</a> speeds, and is overtaken by the shock front caused by the expansion of the material ejected from the nebula. The H II region has been born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franco1990_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franco1990-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lifetime of an H II region is of the order of a few million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popIII_HII_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-popIII_HII-14">[14]</a></sup> Radiation pressure from the hot young stars will eventually drive most of the gas away. In fact, the whole process tends to be very inefficient, with less than 10 percent of the gas in the H II region forming into stars before the rest is blown off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-12">[12]</a></sup> Contributing to the loss of gas are the supernova explosions of the most massive stars, which will occur after only 1–2 million years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction_of_stellar_nurseries">Destruction of stellar nurseries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Destruction of stellar nurseries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:247px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1498" data-file-width="1480" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg/245px-Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg/368px-Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg/490px-Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg 2x" width="245"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bok_globules_in_IC2944.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bok_globule" title="Bok globule">Bok globules</a> in H II region <a href="/wiki/IC_2944" title="IC 2944">IC 2944</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stars form in clumps of cool molecular gas that hide the nascent stars. It is only when the radiation pressure from a star drives away its 'cocoon' that it becomes visible. The hot, blue stars that are powerful enough to ionize significant amounts of hydrogen and form H II regions will do this quickly, and light up the region in which they just formed. The dense regions which contain younger or less massive still-forming stars and which have not yet blown away the material from which they are forming are often seen in silhouette against the rest of the ionised nebula. These dark patches are known as <a href="/wiki/Bok_globule" title="Bok globule">Bok globules</a>, after astronomer <a href="/wiki/Bart_Bok" title="Bart Bok">Bart Bok</a>, who proposed in the 1940s that they might be stellar birthplaces,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bok_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bok-15">[15]</a></sup> a hypothesis which was shown to be correct in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bok_globule_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bok_globule-16">[16]</a></sup> The hot young stars dissipate these globules, as the radiation from the stars powering the H II region drives the material away. In this sense, the stars which generate H II regions act to destroy stellar nurseries. In doing so, however, one last burst of star formation may be triggered, as radiation pressure and mechanical pressure from supernova may act to squeeze globules, thereby enhancing the density within them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stahler_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-stahler-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The young stars in H II regions show evidence for containing planetary systems. The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> has revealed hundreds of <i>protoplanetary disks</i> (<a href="/wiki/Proplyd" title="Proplyd">proplyds</a>) in the Orion Nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ricci_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ricci-18">[18]</a></sup> At least half the young stars in the Orion Nebula appear to be surrounded by disks of gas and dust,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odell1994_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odell1994-19">[19]</a></sup> thought to contain many times as much matter as would be needed to create a planetary system like the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Messier_17_ESO.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1320" data-file-width="1280" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Messier_17_ESO.jpg/220px-Messier_17_ESO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Messier_17_ESO.jpg/330px-Messier_17_ESO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Messier_17_ESO.jpg/440px-Messier_17_ESO.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Messier_17_ESO.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_17" title="Messier 17">Messier 17</a> is an H II region in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">Sagittarius</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>H II regions vary greatly in their physical properties. They range in size from so-called <i>ultra-compact</i> (UCHII) regions perhaps only a <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-year</a> or less across, to giant H II regions several hundred light-years across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> Their size is also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stromgren_radius" title="Stromgren radius">Stromgren radius</a> and essentially depends on the intensity of the source of ionising photons and the density of the region. Their densities range from over a million particles per cm³ in the ultra-compact H II regions to only a few particles per cm³ in the largest and most extended regions. This implies total masses between perhaps 100 and 10<sup>5</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_masses" title="Solar masses">solar masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flynn_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flynn-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also "ultra-dense H II" regions (UDHII).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kobulnicky99_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-kobulnicky99-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the size of an H II region there may be several thousand stars within it. This makes H II regions more complicated than planetary nebulae, which have only one central ionising source. Typically H II regions reach temperatures of 10,000 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> They are mostly ionised gases with weak <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> with strengths of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanotesla" title="Nanotesla">nanoteslas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Nevertheless, H II regions are almost always associated with a cold molecular gas, which originated from the same parent GMC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> Magnetic fields are produced by these weak moving electric charges in the ionised gas, suggesting that H II regions might contain <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric fields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LHA_120-N_159.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="773" data-file-width="1280" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/LHA_120-N_159.jpg/220px-LHA_120-N_159.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/LHA_120-N_159.jpg/330px-LHA_120-N_159.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/LHA_120-N_159.jpg/440px-LHA_120-N_159.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LHA_120-N_159.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stellar nursery N159 is an HII region over 150 light-years across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A number of H II regions also show signs of being permeated by a plasma with temperatures exceeding 10,000,000 K, sufficiently hot to emit X-rays. X-ray observatories such as <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">Einstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra</a> have noted diffuse X-ray emissions in a number of star-forming regions, notably the Orion Nebula, Messier 17, and the Carina Nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The hot gas is likely supplied by the strong stellar winds from O-type stars, which may be heated by supersonic shock waves in the winds, through collisions between winds from different stars, or through colliding winds channeled by magnetic fields. This plasma will rapidly expand to fill available cavities in the molecular clouds due to the high speed of sound in the gas at this temperature. It will also leak out through holes in the periphery of the H II region, which appears to be happening in Messier 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chemically, H II regions consist of about 90% hydrogen. The strongest hydrogen emission line at 656.3 nm gives H II regions their characteristic red colour. Most of the rest of an H II region consists of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, with trace amounts of heavier elements. Across the galaxy, it is found that the amount of heavy elements in H II regions decreases with increasing distance from the galactic centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This is because over the lifetime of the galaxy, star formation rates have been greater in the denser central regions, resulting in greater enrichment of those regions of the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> with the products of <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers_and_distribution">Numbers and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Numbers and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:253px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Messier51_sRGB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7965" data-file-width="11477" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Messier51_sRGB.jpg/251px-Messier51_sRGB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Messier51_sRGB.jpg/377px-Messier51_sRGB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Messier51_sRGB.jpg/502px-Messier51_sRGB.jpg 2x" width="251"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Messier51_sRGB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Strings of red H II regions delineate the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Whirlpool_Galaxy" title="Whirlpool Galaxy">Whirlpool Galaxy</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>H II regions are found only in <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a> like the Milky Way and <a href="/wiki/Irregular_galaxy" title="Irregular galaxy">irregular galaxies</a>. They are not seen in <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a>. In irregular galaxies, they may be dispersed throughout the galaxy, but in spirals they are most abundant within the spiral arms. A large spiral galaxy may contain thousands of H II regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flynn_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flynn-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reason H II regions rarely appear in elliptical galaxies is that ellipticals are believed to form through galaxy mergers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hau2008_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hau2008-28">[28]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a>, such mergers are frequent. When galaxies collide, individual stars almost never collide, but the GMCs and H II regions in the colliding galaxies are severely agitated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hau2008_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hau2008-28">[28]</a></sup> Under these conditions, enormous bursts of star formation are triggered, so rapid that most of the gas is converted into stars rather than the normal rate of 10% or less.</p>
<p>Galaxies undergoing such rapid star formation are known as <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">starburst galaxies</a>. The post-merger elliptical galaxy has a very low gas content, and so H II regions can no longer form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hau2008_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hau2008-28">[28]</a></sup> Twenty-first century observations have shown that a very small number of H II regions exist outside galaxies altogether. These intergalactic H II regions may be the remnants of tidal disruptions of small galaxies, and in some cases may represent a new generation of stars in a galaxy's most recently accreted gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Str%C3%B6mgren_sphere" title="Strömgren sphere">Strömgren sphere</a></div>
<p>H II regions come in an enormous variety of sizes. They are usually clumpy and inhomogeneous on all scales from the smallest to largest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-4">[4]</a></sup> Each star within an H II region ionises a roughly spherical region—known as a <i>Strömgren sphere</i>—of the surrounding gas, but the combination of ionisation spheres of multiple stars within a H II region and the expansion of the heated nebula into surrounding gases creates sharp <a href="/wiki/Density_gradient" title="Density gradient">density gradients</a> that result in complex shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Townsley_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Townsley-30">[30]</a></sup> Supernova explosions may also sculpt H II regions. In some cases, the formation of a large star cluster within an H II region results in the region being hollowed out from within. This is the case for <a href="/wiki/NGC_604" title="NGC 604">NGC 604</a>, a giant H II region in the <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">Triangulum Galaxy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tullmann_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tullmann-31">[31]</a></sup> For a H II region which cannot be <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">resolved</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Laplace_transform#Inferring_spatial_structure_from_spectrum" title="Laplace transform">information on the spatial structure</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Electron_density" title="Electron density">electron density</a> as a function of the distance from the center, and an estimate of the clumpiness) can be inferred by performing an <a href="/wiki/Inverse_Laplace_transform" title="Inverse Laplace transform">inverse Laplace transform</a> on the frequency spectrum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_H.C2.A0II_regions">Notable H II regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable H II regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="395" data-file-width="791" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg/390px-Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg/585px-Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg/780px-Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg 2x" width="390"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trapezium_cluster_optical_and_infrared_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An optical image (left) reveals clouds of gas and dust in the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a>; an infrared image (right) reveals new stars shining within.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Notable Galactic H II regions include the Orion Nebula, the <a href="/wiki/Carina_Nebula" title="Carina Nebula">Eta Carinae Nebula</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/NGC_7822" title="NGC 7822">Berkeley 59 / Cepheus OB4 Complex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2008_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2008-32">[32]</a></sup> The Orion Nebula, about 500 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">pc</a> (1,500 light-years) from Earth, is part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OMC-1" title="OMC-1">OMC-1</a>, a GMC that, if visible, would be seen to fill most of the constellation of <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODell2001_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-ODell2001-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Horsehead_Nebula" title="Horsehead Nebula">Horsehead Nebula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnard%27s_Loop" title="Barnard's Loop">Barnard's Loop</a> are two other illuminated parts of this cloud of gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horsehead_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horsehead-33">[33]</a></sup> The Orion Nebula is actually a thin layer of ionised gas on the outer border of the OMC-1 cloud. The stars in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trapezium_cluster" title="Trapezium cluster">Trapezium cluster</a>, and especially <a href="/wiki/Theta1_Orionis_C" title="Theta1 Orionis C">θ<sup>1</sup> Orionis</a>, are responsible for this ionisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODell2001_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-ODell2001-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">Large Magellanic Cloud</a>, a satellite galaxy of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> at about 50 kpc (<span class="nowrap">160 thousand light years</span>), contains a giant H II region called the <a href="/wiki/Tarantula_Nebula" title="Tarantula Nebula">Tarantula Nebula</a>. Measuring at about <span class="nowrap">200 pc</span> (<span class="nowrap">650 light years</span>) across, this nebula is the most massive and the second-largest H II region in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebouteiller_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lebouteiller-34">[34]</a></sup> It is much bigger than the Orion Nebula, and is forming thousands of stars, some with masses of over 100 times that of the sun—<a href="/wiki/OB_star" title="OB star">OB</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" title="Wolf-Rayet star">Wolf-Rayet stars</a>. If the Tarantula Nebula were as close to Earth as the Orion Nebula, it would shine about as brightly as the full moon in the night sky. The supernova <a href="/wiki/SN_1987A" title="SN 1987A">SN 1987A</a> occurred in the outskirts of the Tarantula Nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Townsley_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Townsley-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another giant H II region—<a href="/wiki/NGC_604" title="NGC 604">NGC 604</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">M33</a> spiral galaxy, which is at 817 kpc (2.66 million light years). Measuring at approximately <span class="nowrap">240 × 250 pc</span> (<span class="nowrap">800 × 830 light years</span>) across, NGC 604 is the second-most-massive H II region in the Local Group after the Tarantula Nebula, although it is slightly larger in size than the latter. It contains around 200 hot OB and Wolf-Rayet stars, which heat the gas inside it to millions of degrees, producing bright <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> emissions. The total mass of the hot gas in NGC 604 is about 6,000 Solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tullmann_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tullmann-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_issues">Current issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H_II_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:340px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ssc2005-02a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ssc2005-02a.jpg/338px-Ssc2005-02a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ssc2005-02a.jpg/507px-Ssc2005-02a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ssc2005-02a.jpg/676px-Ssc2005-02a.jpg 2x" width="338"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ssc2005-02a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Trifid_Nebula" title="Trifid Nebula">Trifid Nebula</a> seen at different <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As with planetary nebulae, estimates of the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> in H II regions are subject to some uncertainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsamis_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tsamis-35">[35]</a></sup> There are two different ways of determining the abundance of metals (metals in this case are elements other than hydrogen and helium) in nebulae, which rely on different types of spectral lines, and large discrepancies are sometimes seen between the results derived from the two methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebouteiller_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lebouteiller-34">[34]</a></sup> Some astronomers put this down to the presence of small temperature fluctuations within H II regions; others claim that the discrepancies are too large to be explained by temperature effects, and hypothesise the existence of cold knots containing very little hydrogen to explain the observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsamis_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tsamis-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The full details of massive star formation within H II regions are not yet well known. Two major problems hamper research in this area. First, the distance from Earth to large H II regions is considerable, with the nearest H II (<a href="/wiki/California_Nebula" title="California Nebula">California Nebula</a>) region at 300 pc (1,000 light-years);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Straizys_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Straizys-36">[36]</a></sup> other H II regions are several times that distance from Earth. Secondly, the formation of these stars is deeply obscured by dust, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> observations are impossible. <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">Radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light can penetrate the dust, but the youngest stars may not emit much light at these <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horsehead_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horsehead-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
