<p>The <b>Japan–Korea Treaty of 1910</b>, also known as the <b>Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty</b>, was made by representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a> on August 22, 1910. In this treaty, Japan formally annexed Korea following the <a href="/wiki/Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1905" title="Japan–Korea Treaty of 1905">Japan–Korea Treaty of 1905</a> by which Korea became the protectorate of Japan and <a href="/wiki/Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1907" title="Japan–Korea Treaty of 1907">Japan–Korea Treaty of 1907</a> by which Korea was deprived of the administration of internal affairs.</p>
<p>Japanese commentators predicted that Koreans would easily assimilate into the Japanese Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965 the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_Basic_Relations_between_Japan_and_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Treaty on Basic Relations between Japan and the Republic of Korea">Treaty of Basic Relations</a> between South Korea and Japan confirmed this treaty is "<i>already null and void</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The treaty was proclaimed to the public (and became effective) on August 29, 1910, officially starting the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">period of Japanese rule</a> in Korea. The treaty had eight articles, the first being: "His Majesty the Emperor of Korea makes the complete and permanent cession to His Majesty the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a> of all rights of sovereignty over the whole of Korea".</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">Gojong of the Korean Empire</a> later called the treaty a "<i>neugyak</i> (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">늑약 勒約</span>)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The alternative term used in lieu of "<i>joyak</i> (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">조약 條約</span>)" implies the treaty was coerced to Koreans by Japanese. "<i>Gyeongsul Gukchi</i> (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">경술국치 庚戌國恥</span>)" and "<i>Gukchi-il</i> (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">국치일 國恥日</span>)" are alternative terms for the year and date the treaty was signed, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_the_British_government">Role of the British government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role of the British government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> had already acquiesced to the annexation of Korea by Japan, via the British connection to Imperial Japan via the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Japanese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Japanese Alliance">Anglo-Japanese Alliance</a> of 1902.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legality">Legality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The legality of the Treaty would later be disputed by the exiled Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea as well as the South Korean government. While the treaty was affixed with the national seal of the Korean Empire, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Sunjong_of_Korea" title="Sunjong of Korea">Sunjong of Korea</a> refused to sign the treaty as required under Korean law. The treaty was instead signed by Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Wan-yong" title="Lee Wan-yong">Lee Wan-yong</a> of the Korean Empire, and <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Korea" title="Governor-General of Korea">Resident General</a> <a href="/wiki/Terauchi_Masatake" title="Terauchi Masatake">Count Terauchi Masatake</a> of the Empire of Japan.</p>
<p>This issue caused considerable difficulty in negotiating the establishment of basic diplomatic relations between the countries. Korea insisted on including a chapter stipulating "The treaty was null and void". A compromise was reached in language of Article II of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_Basic_Relations_between_Japan_and_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Treaty on Basic Relations between Japan and the Republic of Korea">1965 Treaty on Basic Relations</a>:</p>
<p>"It is confirmed that all treaties or agreements concluded between the Empire of Japan and the Empire of Korea on or before August 22, 1910 are already null and void."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conference_to_discuss_legality_of_the_treaty">Conference to discuss legality of the treaty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conference to discuss legality of the treaty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, an academic research of the legality for Korea's annexation by Japan from 1910 to 1945 which was titled <i>A reconsideration of Japanese Annexation of Korea from the Historical and International Law Perspectives</i> was held at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> with a support of <a href="/wiki/Korea_Foundation" title="Korea Foundation">Korea Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The conference was held 3 times, namely on January, April and November and related scholars of history and international law participated from the South Korea, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, Japan, the United States, United Kingdom, Germany and Canada.</p>
<p>Anthony Carty, a Professor of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Derby" title="University of Derby">University of Derby</a> stated "During the height of the imperialism, it is difficult to find an international law sufficient to determine the legality/illegality of a particular treaty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura2002-7">[7]</a></sup> In his book on International Law, "Carty prefers seeing the relationship between Japan and Korea at the time with reference to the reality of the then international community dominated by Western powers, rather than viewing it in terms of treaty law as argued by Korean scholars."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexis Dudden, a Professor of University of Connecticut discussed about the <a href="/wiki/Nitobe_Inaz%C5%8D" title="Nitobe Inazō">Nitobe Inazō</a>'s science of colonial policy. She is known as the author of <i>Japan's Colonization of Korea: Discourse and Power</i>, in which she discusses how Japanese policymakers carefully studied and then invoked international law to annex Korea legally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura2002_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura2002-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Kan_Kimura" title="Kan Kimura">Kan Kimura</a>, the bottom line of this conference is that the Korean claim "The annexation was illegal" was totally unaccepted by the participated Western scholars, among others by those specialized in international law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimura2002_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kimura2002-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Treaty_of_1910&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 28, 2007, regarding the General Power of Attorney by Sunjong, Korean news paper <a href="/wiki/The_Dong-a_Ilbo" title="The Dong-a Ilbo">Dong-a Ilbo</a> reported that Korean monarchs did not sign in the official documents with their real names traditionally. But, the Korean Emperor was forced by Japan to follow a new custom to sign with his real name, which originated from the western hemisphere. It mentioned Sunjong's signature may be compulsory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 23, 2010, 75 South Korean congressmen suggested the legal nullification of the Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty to the Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Kan" title="Naoto Kan">Naoto Kan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 6, 2010, Korean and Japanese progressive Christian groups gathered in Tokyo's Korean <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a> chapter and jointly declared that the Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty was unjustified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 28, 2010, approximately 1000 intellectuals in Korea and Japan issued a Joint Statement that the Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty was never valid in the first place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
