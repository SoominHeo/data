<p><b>John Demjanjuk</b> (born <b>Ivan Mykolaiovych Demianiuk</b>; <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">Іван Миколайович Дем'янюк</span>; 3 April 1920 – 17 March 2012) was a retired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian-American" title="Ukrainian-American">Ukrainian-American</a> auto worker, a former soldier in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">POW</a> during the Second World War.</p>
<p>John (Ivan) Demjanjuk was convicted in 2011 in Germany as an accessory to the murder of 28,060 Jews while acting as a guard at the <a href="/wiki/Sobib%C3%B3r_extermination_camp" title="Sobibór extermination camp">Sobibór extermination camp</a> in occupied Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012-3">[3]</a></sup> Since his conviction was pending appeal at the time of his death,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nydailynews20March2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nydailynews20March2012-5">[5]</a></sup> Demjanjuk remained not guilty under German law, his conviction not having undergone the appeal judgment. According to the Munich state court, Demjanjuk does not have a criminal record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAARETZ2332012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HAARETZ2332012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk was born in <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_People%27s_Republic" title="Ukrainian People's Republic">Ukraine</a>, and during World War II was drafted into the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>, where he was captured as a German <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a>. In 1952, he emigrated from <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> to the United States and was granted citizenship in 1958, whereupon he formally <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicized</a> his name from "<a href="/wiki/Ivan_(name)" title="Ivan (name)">Ivan</a>" to "John".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, he was <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">deported</a> to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> to stand trial for war crimes, after being identified by eleven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust_survivor" title="Holocaust survivor">Holocaust survivors</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ynetnews12_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ynetnews12-9">[9]</a></sup> many from Israel, as "<a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible_(Treblinka_guard)" title="Ivan the Terrible (Treblinka guard)">Ivan the Terrible</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagenaar1989_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagenaar1989-10">[10]</a></sup> a guard at the <a href="/wiki/Treblinka_extermination_camp" title="Treblinka extermination camp">Treblinka extermination camp</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_occupied_Poland" title="Nazi occupied Poland">Nazi occupied Poland</a>. Demjanjuk was accused of committing murder and acts of extraordinarily savage violence against camp prisoners during 1942–43. He was convicted of having committed <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">sentenced to death</a> <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Israel" title="Capital punishment in Israel">there</a> in 1988. The verdict was overturned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israeli_Supreme_Court" title="Israeli Supreme Court">Israeli Supreme Court</a> in 1993, based on new evidence that cast <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_doubt" title="Reasonable doubt">reasonable doubt</a> over the identity of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible_(Treblinka_guard)" title="Ivan the Terrible (Treblinka guard)">"Ivan the Terrible"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After the trial, in September 1993, Demjanjuk returned to his home in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. In 1998, his citizenship was restored after a United States federal appeals court ruled that prosecutors had suppressed <a href="/wiki/Exculpatory_evidence" title="Exculpatory evidence">exculpatory evidence</a> concerning his identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, with new evidence, Demjanjuk was charged again, this time on the grounds that he had served as a guard named Ivan Demjanjuk at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobibor_extermination_camp" title="Sobibor extermination camp">Sobibor</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majdanek" title="Majdanek">Majdanek</a> camps in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_occupied_Poland" title="Nazi occupied Poland">Nazi occupied Poland</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp" title="Flossenbürg concentration camp">Flossenbürg</a> camp in Germany; separate documents were found in archives from each respective camp. Demjanjuk became again a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stateless_person" title="Stateless person">stateless person</a> in 2002 (until his death in 2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nydailynews20March2012_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nydailynews20March2012-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stateless1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-stateless1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> His deportation was again ordered in 2005, but after exhausting his appeals in 2008 he still remained in the United States, as no country would agree to accept him at that time. On 2 April 2009, Germany announced that Demjanjuk would be deported to Germany, where he would stand trial. On 11 May, Demjanjuk left his Cleveland home by ambulance, and was taken to the airport, where he was deported by plane, arriving in Germany the next morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On 13 July, he was formally charged with 27,900 counts of acting as an accessory to murder, one for each person who died at Sobibor during the time he was alleged to have served as a guard. On 30 November, Demjanjuk's trial began in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 May 2011, Demjanjuk was convicted pending appeal by an ordinary German criminal court as an <a href="/wiki/Accessory_(legal_term)" title="Accessory (legal term)">accessory</a> to the murder of 27,900 Jews at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobibor_extermination_camp" title="Sobibor extermination camp">Sobibor</a> and sentenced to five years in prison. The interim conviction was later annulled, because Demjanjuk died before his appeal could be heard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matussek_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matussek-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-welt.de_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-welt.de-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Connor-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraemer_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kraemer-22">[22]</a></sup> After the conviction, he was released pending trial and final verdict by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Justice_of_Germany" title="Federal Court of Justice of Germany">German Appellate Court</a>. He lived at a German nursing home in <a href="/wiki/Bad_Feilnbach" title="Bad Feilnbach">Bad Feilnbach</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stateless1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-stateless1-13">[13]</a></sup> where he died on 17 March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Despite decades of legal wrangling and controversy, Demjanjuk died a free man and technically legally innocent as his appeal had not been heard and decided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_Holocaust_involvement">Background and Holocaust involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and Holocaust involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Demjanjuk was born in Dubovi Makharyntsi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oblasts_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-oblasts-26">[26]</a></sup> Ukraine, a farming village, and grew up during the man-made famine called the <a href="/wiki/Holodomor" title="Holodomor">Holodomor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> When Demjanjuk was convicted in 2011 his son claimed, in an email to <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>, "My dad is a survivor of the genocide famine in Ukraine. "<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup> When interviewed in late December 2009, residents of Dubovi Makharyntsi declared that Demjanjuk got along well with the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Ukraine" title="History of the Jews in Ukraine">Jewish families living nearby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Before joining the Soviet army, Demjanjuk worked as a tractor driver on a Soviet collective farm. In 1941, after the Nazi attack on <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%9345)#Treatment_of_Polish_citizens_under_Soviet_occupation" title="Occupation of Poland (1939–45)">Soviet-occupied Poland</a>, Demjanjuk was drafted into the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>. After a battle in Eastern Crimea he was captured and became a German <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> (POW) and was moved to a Nazi German concentration camp for Soviet Russian POWs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_crimes_against_Soviet_POWs" title="Nazi crimes against Soviet POWs">See: Nazi crimes against Soviet POWs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>He survived the ordeal, and volunteered to be sent to the <a href="/wiki/Trawniki_concentration_camp" title="Trawniki concentration camp">Trawniki concentration camp</a> division utilized for the training <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawnikis" title="Trawnikis">Hiwi guards</a> recruited from Soviet POWs, later allegedly serving in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majdanek" title="Majdanek">Majdanek</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobibor" title="Sobibor">Sobibor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flossenb%C3%BCrg" title="Flossenbürg">Flossenbürg</a> Nazi camps, and during the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Ghetto_Uprising" title="Warsaw Ghetto Uprising">Warsaw Ghetto Uprising</a>. Demjanjuk claims that, in early 1945, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Liberation_Army" title="Russian Liberation Army">Russian Liberation Army</a> (Russian: Russkaya osvoboditel'naya armiya, Русская освободительная армия, abbreviated in Cyrillic as РОА, in Latin as ROA, also known as the Vlasov army), led by <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Vlasov" title="Andrey Vlasov">Andrey Vlasov</a>, which was organized by Nazi Germany to fight against the Soviet Red Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosen_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosen-30">[30]</a></sup> Many Soviet prisoners of war volunteered to serve under the Nazi command in order to get out of the POW camps, where 2.8 million Soviet POWs died through starvation, exposure, and summary execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Such non-German volunteers that served in Germany's foreign legions were referred to by the German command as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_non-Germans_in_the_German_armed_forces_during_World_War_II" title="European non-Germans in the German armed forces during World War II">Freiwillige</a>. the German word for volunteer, and also as Hilfswilliger, literally one willing to help, often shortened to <a href="/wiki/Hiwi_(volunteer)" title="Hiwi (volunteer)">Hiwi</a>, in auxiliary non-combat paramilitary and civilian capacities. According to Allied general Lt. Gen. <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Anders" title="Władysław Anders">Władysław Anders</a>, by 1942, there were about a million such volunteers from the USSR alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk, his wife and their child arrived in New York City aboard the <a href="/wiki/USS_General_W._G._Haan_(AP-158)" title="USS General W. G. Haan (AP-158)">USS <i>General W. G. Haan</i></a> on 9 February 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosen_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-rosen-30">[30]</a></sup> On 14 November 1958, Demjanjuk became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalized_citizen" title="Naturalized citizen">naturalized citizen</a> of the United States. He and his wife, whom he met in a <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" title="Displaced person">displaced persons</a>' camp, moved to <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> with their daughter and then to the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/Seven_Hills,_Ohio" title="Seven Hills, Ohio">Seven Hills</a>, where Demjanjuk became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Auto_Workers" title="United Auto Workers">UAW</a> diesel engine mechanic at the nearby Ford auto plant. Demjanjuk and his wife later had two more children.</p>
<p>In 1975, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Hanusiak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Hanusiak (page does not exist)">Michael Hanusiak</a>, editor of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ukrainian_News&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ukrainian News (page does not exist)">Ukrainian News</a>, presented a list of ethnic Ukrainians living in the United States suspected of collaborating with Germans in World War II to what was then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="US Immigration and Naturalization Service">US Immigration and Naturalization Service</a>; Demjanjuk's name was on that list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP2032012Semotiuk_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-KP2032012Semotiuk-33">[33]</a></sup> Soviet newspapers and archives had provided the names during Hanusiak's visit to the Soviet Union in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Hanusiak also handed over a purported copy of a Nazi ID card issued to Demjanjuk at <a href="/wiki/Trawniki" title="Trawniki">Trawniki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HM30112009Douglas_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-HM30112009Douglas-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1977, the Justice Department submitted a request to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Northern_District_of_Ohio" title="United States District Court for the Northern District of Ohio">United States District Court for the Northern District of Ohio</a> that Demjanjuk's citizenship be revoked on the basis that he had allegedly concealed his involvement with Nazi death camps on his immigration application in 1951. The request followed a lengthy investigation by the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="Immigration and Naturalization Service">INS</a> after Demjanjuk was identified by five Holocaust survivors on a photo spread used in the investigation of <a href="/wiki/Feodor_Fedorenko" title="Feodor Fedorenko">Feodor Fedorenko</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treblinka" title="Treblinka">Treblinka</a> concentration camp guard. Fedorenko was later extradited to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> on his admission that he was such a guard and that he lied on his US immigration applications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_in_Israel">Trial in Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trial in Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="520" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg/250px-John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg/375px-John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg/500px-John_Demjanjuk_2.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>In October 1983, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> issued an <a href="/wiki/Extradition" title="Extradition">extradition</a> request for Demjanjuk to stand trial on Israeli soil under the Nazis and Nazi Collaborators (Punishment) Law of 1950. Demjanjuk appealed his deportation with the case heard on 8 July 1985. During the appeal, the authenticity of the identity card was questioned when it was argued that the card's number, 1393, had been issued in June/July 1942 with the seal of SS Commissioner, <a href="/wiki/Odilo_Globocnik" title="Odilo Globocnik">Odilo Globocnik</a>, who had in fact been dismissed from this post in March 1942 at which time his seal was destroyed. The three identity cards supplied by the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> that the experts had used to compare their paper and ink with Demjanjuk's card to prove its authenticity were also disputed. Among other contentions such as graphic inconsistencies, the cards, all dated 1942, carried Waffen SS stamps despite their not taking control of the Trawniki until 1943. Two bore the signature of Corporal Teufel when he was at that time a Sergeant while the other card, which had been issued earlier in the year when Teufel was still a Corporal, had his rank listed as Sergeant. The cards were also signed by Captain Hermann Hoefle, who was actually a Major. The court ruled on this claim: <i>the record before us lends no support to this very serious charge, and we reject it. Witnesses fully qualified to testify on the subject stated their opinions that the Trawniki documents were authentic. Even if this documentary evidence had been rejected, the eyewitness evidence alone was found sufficient. Since the district court did not rely on the "Trawniki card", its validity is not before the court.</i> The appeal was dismissed on 31 October 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The Trawniki card was later authenticated in subsequent trials.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk was deported to Israel on 28 February 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-37">[37]</a></sup> His trial took place in the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> District Court between 26 November 1986 and 18 April 1988, before a special tribunal comprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Israel" title="Supreme Court of Israel">Israeli Supreme Court</a> Judge <a href="/wiki/Dov_Levin" title="Dov Levin">Dov Levin</a> and Jerusalem District Court Judges Zvi Tal and <a href="/wiki/Dalia_Dorner" title="Dalia Dorner">Dalia Dorner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk was at first represented by N.Y attorney Mark J. O'Connor, but fired him in July 1987 just a week before he was scheduled to testify at his trial. In his place, Demjanjuk hired Israeli trial lawyer Yoram Sheftel whom O'Connor had hired as co-counsel. One of the reasons why Yoram Sheftel defended Demjanjuk was that he was convinced he was not at Treblinka. The defense originally used testimony from other Ukrainian auxiliary police (such as Ignat Danielchenko) to place Demjanjuk in different camps (specifically Sobibor) to prove (and they did) that he was not at Treblinka, however halfway through the trial, Sheftel subtly moved away from this method, fearing future prosecution on other charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawnikis" title="Trawnikis">Trawniki-trained camp guards</a> with the exception of Eastern-European <i><a href="/wiki/Volksdeutsche" title="Volksdeutsche">Volksdeutsche</a></i> did not know German. The <i>Volksdeutsche</i> who relayed the orders for them were predominantly from ethnic German colonies in Ukraine and Russia, or were taught German. John Demjanjuk had no significant fluency in German.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The prosecution team consisted of State Attorney Yonah Blatman, Michael Shaked of the Jerusalem District Attorney's Office, Dennis Goldman and Eli Gabay of the International Section of the State Attorney's Office and others. According to prosecutors, Demjanjuk had been recruited into the Soviet army in 1940, and he had fought until he was captured by German troops in Eastern <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> in May 1942. He was then brought to a German prisoner of war camp in <a href="/wiki/Che%C5%82m" title="Chełm">Chełm</a> in July 1942. Prosecutors claimed that Demjanjuk volunteered to collaborate with the Germans and was sent to the camp at <a href="/wiki/Trawniki" title="Trawniki">Trawniki</a>, where he was trained to guard prisoners and was given a firearm, a uniform, and an ID card with his photograph. The principal allegation was that three former prisoners identified Demjanjuk as "Иван Грозный" (Ivan Grozny, Russian for "Ivan the Terrible") of Treblinka, who operated the diesel engines sending gas to the death chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[nb 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prosecutors based part of these allegations on an ID card referred to as the "Trawnicki card." This ID card was obtained from the USSR and provided to Israel by American industrialist <a href="/wiki/Armand_Hammer" title="Armand Hammer">Armand Hammer</a>, a close associate of several Kremlin leaders, including Lenin and Stalin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> The defense claimed that the card was forged by Soviet authorities to discredit Demjanjuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKudryashov2004228_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKudryashov2004228-43">[42]</a></sup> The card had Demjanjuk's photograph, which he identified as his picture at the time. The prosecution called expert witnesses to testify on the authenticity of the card including its signatures by various Nazi officers, paper, and ink. The defense used some evidence supplied by the Soviets to support their case while calling other pieces of evidence supplied by the Soviets "forgeries".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nizkor_trial_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nizkor_trial-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 March 1992, German weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Stern_(magazine)" title="Stern (magazine)">Stern</a></i> published a claim that the Israeli prosecution concealed crucial information about the Travniki card being a forgery, and that the Israelis had the full co-operation of the German police and the Ministry of Justice. The article stated that on 23 January 1986, three weeks before the trial, Superintendent Amnon Bezaleli took the original Trawniki card for examination at the German police force's main criminal-identification laboratory at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Criminal_Police_Office_(Germany)" title="Federal Criminal Police Office (Germany)">Federal Criminal Police Office</a> (BKA) in <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a>. Bezaleli was the prosecution's central witness on the Trawniki document. According to Stern, the BKA, after a cursory examination, told Bezaleli that this was a counterfeit document forged in a more or less amateur way. The laboratory's preliminary analysis addressed the following points: the face in the photograph, which the prosecution identified as Demjanjuk's, had been pasted onto the uniform using photomontage techniques; the picture was not originally attached to the card, but had been transferred from another document; there was no match between the seal on the Travniki picture and that on the document itself. The analysts did not have time to compare Demjanjuk's known signature with the Demjanjuk signature on the Travniki document. Dr. Louis Ferdinand Werner, head of the BKA, speculated to Bezaleli in a private conversation that the card was counterfeit. Bezaleli consulted people from the state prosecutor's office in Jerusalem, then instructed Werner to stop all tests on the card.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other controversial evidence included Demjanjuk's tattoo. Demjanjuk admitted the scar under his armpit was a <a href="/wiki/Waffen-SS" title="Waffen-SS">Waffen-SS</a> tattoo, which he removed after the war, as did many soldiers to avoid summary execution by the Soviets. The tattoo was probably an <a href="/wiki/SS_blood_group_tattoo" title="SS blood group tattoo">SS blood group tattoo</a> given to him when he joined the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Liberation_Army" title="Russian Liberation Army">Russian Liberation Army</a>. The blood group tattoo was applied by army medics and used by combat soldiers in <a href="/wiki/Waffen-SS_foreign_volunteers_and_conscripts" title="Waffen-SS foreign volunteers and conscripts">Waffen-SS foreign volunteers and conscripts</a> because they were likely to need blood or give transfusions. There is no evidence that POWs trained as police auxiliaries at Trawniki were required to receive such tattoos, although it was an option for those that volunteered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the trial, he was again identified on the photo spread by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Horn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Otto Horn (page does not exist)">Otto Horn</a>, a former German SS guard at Treblinka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nizkor_trial_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nizkor_trial-44">[43]</a></sup> This testimony became controversial later because it was discovered by Yoram Sheftel that on 14 November 1979, Otto Horn was interviewed by the newly created US Department of Justice Office of Special Investigations (OSI). The OSI showed him the picture of Demjanjuk, but at that time Horn failed to identify Demjanjuk as Ivan the Terrible, or even as a guard from Treblinka, or as anyone he ever knew in the SS forces. The OSI report regarding Otto Horn's failure to identify John Demjanjuk as Ivan the Terrible would have been exculpatory, but was withheld by the OSI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>One remarkable event involved a star witness for the prosecution, Eliyahu Rosenberg. Asked by the prosecution if he recognized Demjanjuk, Rosenberg asked that the defendant remove his glasses "so I can see his eyes." Rosenberg approached and peered closely at Demjanjuk's face. When Demjanjuk smiled and offered his hand, Rosenberg recoiled and shouted "Grozny!" meaning "Terrible" in Polish and Russian. "Ivan", Rosenberg said. "I say it unhesitatingly, without the slightest shadow of a doubt. It is Ivan from Treblinka, from the gas chambers, the man I am looking at now." "I saw his eyes, I saw those murderous eyes", Rosenberg told the court, glaring at Demjanjuk. Rosenberg then exclaimed directly to Demjanjuk: "How dare you put out your hand, murderer that you are!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> It was later revealed with great embarrassment for the prosecution that Eliyahu Rosenberg had previously testified in a 1947 deposition that "Ivan the Terrible" had been killed during a prisoner uprising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk testified during the trial that he was imprisoned in a camp in Chełm until 1944, when he was transferred to another camp in Austria, where he remained until he joined an anti-Soviet Ukrainian army group. At that time, Ukrainian military groups like the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_National_Army" title="Ukrainian National Army">Ukrainian National Army</a> were forming in western Austria. Demjanjuk claimed that a few months later he joined an anti-Soviet Russian military organization, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Liberation_Army" title="Russian Liberation Army">Russian Liberation Army</a> (Vlasov Army) funded by the Nazi German government until the surrender of Nazi Germany to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> in 1945. Demjanjuk's memory regarding specifics of this period may have been impaired by severe trauma and malnutrition during his internment as a POW in Nazi concentration camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> The reliability of witnesses' memory, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Confabulation" title="Confabulation">confabulation</a>, stress-impaired memory, <a href="/wiki/Memory_errors" title="Memory errors">memory errors</a> and the psychology of witnesses' testimony, e.g. unconscious transference and mistaken identity, positive response bias, weapon focus effect, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a>, were also key issues of expert testimony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 April 1988, the court found Demjanjuk "unhesitatingly and with utter conviction" guilty of all charges and being Ivan the Terrible. One week later it sentenced him to death by <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sentence_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sentence-52">[51]</a></sup> Demjanjuk was placed in <a href="/wiki/Solitary_confinement" title="Solitary confinement">solitary confinement</a> during the appeals process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israeli_Supreme_Court_ruling">Israeli Supreme Court ruling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Israeli Supreme Court ruling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 29 July 1993, a five-judge panel of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Israel" title="Supreme Court of Israel">Israeli Supreme Court</a> overturned the guilty verdict on appeal. Their ruling was based on the written statements of 37 former guards at Treblinka that identified Ivan the Terrible as "Ivan Marchenko."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup> Central to the new evidence was a photograph of Ivan the Terrible and a description that did not match the 1942 appearance of Demjanjuk. The accounts of 21 guards who were tried in the Soviet Union on war crimes gave details that differentiate Demjanjuk from Ivan the Terrible—that his surname was Marchenko.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> One described Ivan the Terrible as having brown hair, hazel eyes and a large scar down to his neck; Demjanjuk was blond with grayish-blue eyes and no such scar. US officials had originally been aware, without informing Demjanjuk's attorneys, of the testimony of two of these German guards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_1993_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-time_1993-55">[54]</a></sup> However the Israeli justices mentioned how Demjanjuk had incorrectly listed his mother's maiden name as "Marchenko" in his 1951 application for US visa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup> Demjanjuk said he just wrote a common Ukrainian surname after he forgot his mother's real name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_1993_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-time_1993-55">[54]</a></sup> The former guards' statements were obtained after World War II by the Soviets, who prosecuted USSR citizens who assisted the Nazis as auxiliary forces during the war. Most of the guards were executed after the war by the Soviets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKudryashov2004237_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKudryashov2004237-56">[55]</a></sup> and their written statements were not obtained by Israeli authorities until 1991, when the Soviet Union collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_1993_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-time_1993-55">[54]</a></sup> Russian historian Sergei Kudryashov pointed out that if the defense's claims of a KGB plot to frame Demjanjuk had been the case, then the Soviet files that established that Demjanjuk was not "Ivan the Terrible" would have never been made available to the defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKudryashov_2004238_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKudryashov_2004238-57">[56]</a></sup> After the collapse of the USSR, the governments of Ukraine and Russia were for a time more open than the previous Soviet regimes. The Soviet KGB was known to use <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a>, in particular through the use of forgeries, to discredit, denigrate, and confuse its enemies, including Ukrainian communities in the West that supported Ukrainian independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Israeli Supreme Court's 405-page ruling included the following: <i>"The main issue of the indictment sheet filed against the appellant was his identification as Ivan the Terrible, an operator of the gas chambers in the extermination camp at Treblinka... By virtue of this gnawing [new evidence indicating mistaken identity]... we restrained ourselves from convicting the appellant of the horrors of Treblinka. Ivan Demjanjuk has been acquitted by us, because of doubt, of the terrible charges attributed to Ivan the Terrible of Treblinka. This was the proper course for judges who cannot examine the heart and mind, but have only what their eyes see and read."</i> They also added: <i>"The facts proved the appellant's participation in the extermination process. The matter is closed – but not complete, the complete truth is not the prerogative of the human judge."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The court judgment addressed evidence against Demjanjuk that was not included in his indictment. The judges agreed that Demjanjuk most likely served as a Nazi <i>Wachmann</i> (guard) in the Trawniki unit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-4"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup> and had been posted at Sobibor extermination camp and two other camps. Evidence to assist this claim included an identification card from Trawniki bearing Demjanjuk's picture and personal information<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-5"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup> — allegedly found in the Soviet archives – in addition to German documents that mentioned Wachmann Demjanjuk and mentioned his date and place of birth. The 1949 statement of another Wachmann (Danilchenko), identified a Demjanjuk in passing as someone who allegedly served with him at Sobibor in 1944 (a year after the camp was razed) and noted he had been born the same year as himself (1923). In 1979, Danilchenko made another statement adding that Demjanjuk was allegedly three years older than himself and had been in Sobibor in 1943. None of Danilchenko's allegations were ever subjected to cross-examination. The alleged Trawniki certificate also implied that he served at Sobibor, as did the German orders of March 1943 posting the Trawniki unit to the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acquittal_53-6"><a href="#cite_note-Acquittal-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the trial, the prosecution argued that Demjanjuk should be tried for alleged crimes at Sobibor; however, Justice Aharon Barak was not convinced, stating "We know nothing about him at Sobibor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup>"</p>
<p>After Demjanjuk's acquittal, the <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Israel" title="Attorney General of Israel">Israeli Attorney-General</a> decided to release him rather than to pursue charges of committing crimes at Sobibor. Ten petitions against the decision were made to the Supreme Court. On 18 August 1993, the court rejected the petitions on the grounds that (1) the principle of <a href="/wiki/Double_jeopardy" title="Double jeopardy">double jeopardy</a> would be infringed, (2) that new charges would be unreasonable given the seriousness of those of which he had been acquitted, (3) that conviction on the new charges would be unlikely, and (4) that Demjanjuk was extradited from the United States specifically to stand trial for offenses attributed to Ivan the Terrible of Treblinka, and not for other alternative charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yoram Sheftel's 1994 book <i>The Demjanjuk Affair: The Rise and Fall of a Show-Trial</i> became an immediate best-seller in Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> In reviewing many issues pertaining to the law, Sheftel revealed serious errors, deliberate fabrications, and "fraud on the court" perpetrated by the US Department of Justice Office of Special Investigations (OSI) lawyers in their prosecution of the Demjanjuk case.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Simon_Wiesenthal" title="Simon Wiesenthal">Simon Wiesenthal</a>, an iconic figure in Nazi hunting, first believed Demjanjuk was guilty, but after Demjanjuk's acquittal by the Israeli Supreme Cour said he too would have cleared him given the new evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk was released to return to the United States. In 1993, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Sixth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit">Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals</a> ruled that Demjanjuk was a victim of fraud on the court, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_federal_government" title="United States federal government">United States federal government</a> trial lawyers with the Office of Special Investigations had recklessly failed to disclose <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a>, and the extradition order previously granted was rescinded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> In a report submitted to the Sixth Circuit prior to the Israeli acquittal, federal judge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Wiseman,_Jr." title="Thomas A. Wiseman, Jr.">Thomas A. Wiseman, Jr.</a> concluded that American federal officials had erred in asserting that Demjanjuk was Ivan the Terrible, but that evidence instead pointed to Demjanjuk being a lesser SS agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_1993_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-time_1993-55">[54]</a></sup> After the Court of Appeals remanded the matter to Judge Wiseman, Judge Wiseman dismissed the denaturalization petition proceedings in 1998, effectively restoring Demjanjuk's citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> Transcripts of Demjanjuk's 1988 trial, and the character of Demjanjuk himself, appear in <a href="/wiki/Philip_Roth" title="Philip Roth">Philip Roth</a>'s 1993 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Shylock" title="Operation Shylock">Operation Shylock</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charges_and_deportation">Charges and deportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Charges and deportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="933" data-file-width="700" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg/200px-Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg/300px-Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg/400px-Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wikipedia-sobibor-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobibor" title="Sobibor">Sobibor</a> "Road to Heaven" in 2007</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 20 February 1998, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_District_Court" title="Federal District Court">Federal District Court</a> judge, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Matia" title="Paul Matia">Paul Matia</a>, ruled that Demjanjuk's citizenship could be restored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-database_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-database-65">[64]</a></sup> On 19 May 1999, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Justice Department</a> filed a new civil complaint against Demjanjuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-database_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-database-65">[64]</a></sup> No mention was made in the new complaint of the previous allegations that Demjanjuk was Ivan the Terrible. Instead, the complaint alleged that Demjanjuk served as a guard at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobib%C3%B3r" title="Sobibór">Sobibór</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majdanek" title="Majdanek">Majdanek</a> camps in Poland under German occupation and at the Flossenburg camp in Germany. It additionally accused Demjanjuk of being a member of an SS-run unit that took part in capturing nearly two million Jews in the <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government of Poland</a>. He was put on trial again in 2001. In February 2002 Judge Matia revoked Demjanjuk's US citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClevelandJewishNews22032012_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClevelandJewishNews22032012-66">[65]</a></sup> On 21 February 2002, Judge Matia ruled that Demjanjuk had not produced any credible evidence of his whereabouts during the war and that the Justice Department had proved its case against him. On 30 April 2004, a three-judge panel of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Sixth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit">6th US Circuit Court of Appeals</a> ruled that Demjanjuk could be again stripped of his US citizenship because the Justice Department had presented "clear, unequivocal and convincing evidence" of Demjanjuk's service in Nazi death camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCOTUS" title="SCOTUS">United States Supreme Court</a> declined to hear his appeal in November 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-database_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-database-65">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_court_order">New court order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New court order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 28 December 2005, an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=US_Immigration_Judge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="US Immigration Judge (page does not exist)">immigration judge</a> ordered Demjanjuk deported to Germany, Poland or Ukraine. In an attempt to avoid deportation, Demjanjuk sought protection under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_against_Torture" title="United Nations Convention against Torture">United Nations Convention against Torture</a>, claiming that he would be prosecuted and tortured if he were deported to Ukraine. Chief US Immigration Judge Michael Creppy ruled there was no evidence to substantiate Demjanjuk's claim that he would be mistreated if he were sent to Ukraine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 December 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Immigration_Appeals" title="Board of Immigration Appeals">Board of Immigration Appeals</a> upheld the deportation order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCNews_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNews-69">[68]</a></sup> On 30 January 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Sixth_Circuit" title="Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit">Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit</a> denied Demjanjuk's request for review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-X-70">[nb 2]</a></sup> On 19 May 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">US Supreme Court</a> denied Demjanjuk's petition for <a href="/wiki/Certiorari" title="Certiorari">certiorari</a>, declining to hear his case against the deportation order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Y_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Y-71">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup> The Supreme Court's denial of review meant that the order of removal was final; no other appeal was possible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deportation_proceedings_and_actions">Deportation proceedings and actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Deportation proceedings and actions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One month after the US Supreme Court's refusal to hear Demjanjuk's case, on 19 June 2008, Germany announced it would seek the extradition of Demjanjuk to Germany. The file on Demjanjuk was compiled by the special German office investigating Nazi crimes. <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schrimm" title="Kurt Schrimm">Kurt Schrimm</a>, who heads the office, said that investigators "have managed to obtain hundreds of documents and have also found a number of witnesses who spoke out against Demjanjuk". "For the first time we have even found lists of names of the people who Demjanjuk personally led into the gas chambers. We have no doubt that he is responsible for the death of over 29,000 Jews" at the Nazis' Sobibor death camp, he said. Kurt Schrimm said that Demjanjuk could be brought to Germany by the end of 2008; however, this did not occur<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 10 November 2008, German federal prosecutor Kurt Schrimm directed prosecutors to file in Munich for extradition, since Demjanjuk once lived there. On 9 December 2008, a German federal court declared that Demjanjuk could be tried for his alleged role in the Holocaust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> Some three months later, on 11 March 2009, Demjanjuk was charged with more than 29,000 counts of accessory to murder of Jewish prisoners at the Sobibor extermination camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Office_(Germany)" title="Foreign Office (Germany)">German foreign ministry</a> announced on 2 April 2009 that Demjanjuk would be transferred to Germany the following week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup> and would face trial beginning 30 November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk sued Germany on 30 April 2009, to try to block the German government's agreement to accept Demjanjuk from the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-79">[77]</a></sup> His filing with the German Administrative Court in Berlin claimed that the procedure by which he was to be moved from the US to Germany was illegal, and asked the Court to suspend the declaration by the German government that allowed Demjanjuk to enter the country pursuant to US deportation, until a court determination is made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-79">[77]</a></sup> The German Administrative Court rejected Demjanjuk's claim on 6 May; Demjanjuk's German attorney stated that he would appeal the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-80">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motion_to_reopen_the_case">Motion to reopen the case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Motion to reopen the case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On that same day – 2 April 2009 – Demjanjuk filed a motion in an immigration trial court in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. The motion sought to reopen the matter of the removal order against him; that order of removal had been originally issued by an immigration court in 2005, had been upheld by the BIA on administrative appeal in late 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCNews_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNews-69">[68]</a></sup> and was further upheld by the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-X-70">[nb 2]</a></sup> after these two appeals, the US Supreme Court had, as noted above, denied any review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Y_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Y-71">[69]</a></sup> In connection with the motion to reopen, Demjanjuk also asked the immigration court to stay the removal order, pending the decision on whether or not to reopen the matter.</p>
<p>On 3 April 2009, US Immigration Judge Wayne Iskra temporarily stayed Demjanjuk's deportation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters-2009-04-03_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-2009-04-03-81">[79]</a></sup> but reversed himself three days later, on 6 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup> As the Government noted, a motion to reopen, such as Demjanjuk's, could only properly be filed with the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Immigration_Appeals" title="Board of Immigration Appeals">Board of Immigration Appeals</a> (BIA) in Washington, D.C., and not an immigration trial court. The issuance of the stay by the immigration trial court was therefore improper, as that court had no jurisdiction over the matter. Accordingly, Demjanjuk re-filed his motion to reopen, and for an attendant stay, with the BIA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X1_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-X1-83">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-84">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk's motion to reopen argued that, under the circumstances, his deportation to Germany would constitute torture. His attorney stated that his health had deteriorated seriously in the last four years. Demjanjuk argued that his request was supported by the past actions of the very same Office of Special Investigations where in the Tannenbaum case the DOJ allowed another sick old man to remain in the United States who had admitted to mistreatment of prisoners in a Nazi camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[83]</a></sup> On 10 April, the BIA found there was "little likelihood of success that [Demjanjuk's] pending motion to re-open the case will be granted", and accordingly denied his motion for a stay pending the disposition of his motion to reopen. This removed any obstacles to federal agents seizing him at any time for deportation to Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-84">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Government has responded to Demjanjuk's torture claim as a basis for reopening the deportation order – a case that was decided against Demjanjuk in 2005 and that has been thrice affirmed against him on appeal – by stating that his claim is "patently frivolous". Rabbi Abraham Cooper, Associate Dean of the Simon Wiesenthal Center and its Museum of Tolerance, wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huffington_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huffington-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This attempt by a former guard at a death camp to cast himself as the victim is [..] nothing less than a grotesque debasement of the word "torture", a characterization that makes a mockery of the terrible suffering inflicted on genuine victims of torture at places like the Sobibor extermination center. Ironically, Petitioner's argument is advanced by one who has been confirmed by US to have contributed to the mass-asphyxiation of thousands of civilians at a human extermination center on behalf of a regime responsible for some of the largest-scale tortures and murders in history." —<small>Rabbi Abraham Cooper</small> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huffington_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huffington-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sixth_Circuit.27s_stay">Sixth Circuit's stay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sixth Circuit's stay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 14 April 2009, immigration agents removed Demjanjuk from his home in preparation for deportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP1-87">[85]</a></sup> The same day, Demjanjuk's son filed a motion in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Sixth_Circuit" title="Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit">Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit</a> asking that the deportation be stayed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP1-87">[85]</a></sup> which was subsequently granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-88">[86]</a></sup> The Government argued that the Court of Appeals has no jurisdiction to review the decision of the Board of Immigration Appeals, which denied the stay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[87]</a></sup> Demjanjuk later won a last-minute stay of deportation, shortly after US immigration agents carried him from his home in a wheelchair to face trial in Germany. The BIA denied Demjanjuk's motion to reopen his deportation case. The Sixth Circuit, whose stay of the deportation order remained in effect, asked lawyers for both parties to provide it with more information from the government, physicians and Demjanjuk himself. On 20 April, the government filed a motion with the Sixth Circuit asking for the stay against deportation to be lifted, arguing that Demjanjuk had sought the stay in order to provide an opportunity for the BIA to rule upon his motion to reopen the deportation order. Since the BIA denied the motion, the government argued, the basis for the Sixth Circuit's stay was no longer valid, and the stay should accordingly be dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JP_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-JP-90">[88]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jerusalem_Post" title="The Jerusalem Post">Jerusalem Post</a></i> also reported that the Simon Wiesenthal Center had declared Demjanjuk to be the most wanted Nazi war criminal on its list, displacing SS doctor <a href="/wiki/Aribert_Heim" title="Aribert Heim">Aribert Heim</a>. According to a spokesman for the Center, the revised ranking was intended to reflect the importance attributed to the US efforts to deport Demjanjuk to Germany to stand trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JP_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-JP-90">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sixth_Circuit.27s_stay_lifted">Sixth Circuit's stay lifted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sixth Circuit's stay lifted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 1 May 2009, the Sixth Circuit lifted the <a href="/wiki/Preliminary_injunction" title="Preliminary injunction">stay</a> that it had imposed against Demjanjuk's deportation order. The Court stated in its opinion that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Based on the medical information before the court and the government's representations about the conditions under which it will transport the petitioner, which include an aircraft equipped as a medical air ambulance and attendance by medical personnel, the court cannot find that the petitioner's removal to Germany is likely to cause irreparable harm sufficient to warrant a stay of removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[89]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Court also ruled that it was unlikely that Demjanjuk could demonstrate that he would be "tortured" in Germany and that this claim did not sufficiently support his request for a stay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-79">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reut_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reut-92">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CA6_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-CA6-93">[91]</a></sup> Although the stay was lifted, and the government freed of legal constraints that prevented it from deporting Demjanjuk, his appeal for the substantive review of the BIA decision on the merits (wherein the BIA effectively upheld the deportation order itself, by refusing to reopen the case on the ground that "torture" in Germany was more likely than not) was still pending before the Court of Appeals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[nb 3]</a></sup> However, the Court stated in its opinion denying the stay (in the process of addressing the question of the extent to which Demjanjuk was likely to succeed on the merits of his motion to reopen) that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...the petitioner has not shown a strong or substantial likelihood of success on the merits of his challenge to... the [BIA's] denial of his motion to reopen. At most, he has offered speculation that German authorities may not adequately attend to his medical needs while he is in ... custody.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CA6_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-CA6-93">[91]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Petition_to_Supreme_Court">Petition to Supreme Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Petition to Supreme Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On Tuesday, 5 May 2009, Demjanjuk announced through counsel that he was filing a petition with the US Supreme Court, seeking review of the adverse decision of the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals that had denied his stay. His counsel expected the filing to be made on Wednesday, 6 May. The filing characterized the unanimous decision of the three-judge Sixth Circuit panel as "incomprehensible". On Thursday, 7 May 2009, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCOTUS" title="SCOTUS">United States Supreme Court</a>, via Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a>, declined to consider Demjanjuk's case for review, thereby denying Demjanjuk any further stay of deportation. Justice Stevens issued his decision without comment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sp_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sp-96">[93]</a></sup> Demjanjuk's counsel thereafter stated that his client would not seek to have the entire Court consider Justice Stevens' decision and that accordingly all legal avenues in the United States to halt the deportation had been exhausted.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Torture.22_as_basis_for_reopening_the_case">"Torture" as basis for reopening the case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title='Edit section: "Torture" as basis for reopening the case'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Demjanjuk's motion to reopen was based on the claim that he would be tortured as a result of being deported. Demjanjuk claimed that, because he was old and sick, transferring him to Germany would constitute "<a href="/wiki/Torture_and_the_United_States" title="Torture and the United States">torture</a>" within the meaning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_Against_Torture" title="United Nations Convention Against Torture">Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment</a> (the Convention), and that this circumstance was sufficient to reopen consideration of his case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[nb 4]</a></sup> His attorney, John Broadley, argued before the BIA that deporting Demjanjuk would constitute torture because of his health problems, including pre-leukemia, kidney problems, spinal problems and "a couple of types of gout."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-AP1-87">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-88">[86]</a></sup> To summarize the foregoing, his essential contention in his legal pleadings and public statements was, that it would be very hard on him, and perhaps life-threatening, to be taken away from his home in his present condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[nb 5]</a></sup> He claimed that this circumstance would constitute "torture."</p>
<p>While Demjanjuk was removed from his house in a wheelchair and appeared to be "slack-jawed and unmoving", on 23 April 2009, federal prosecutors submitted surveillance video evidence taken after the removal of Demjanjuk; the footage showed Demjanjuk walking with no assistance to a car in a parking lot while "alert and engaged in conversation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_doctor_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn_doctor-99">[94]</a></sup> They also provided the Court of Appeals with a medical report that supported their claim that Demjanjuk is medically fit to be moved to Germany; a flight surgeon who examined Demjanjuk while in government custody for alleged back pain later noticed that Demjanjuk "moved his back with apparent ease".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_doctor_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-cnn_doctor-99">[94]</a></sup> The government lawyers stated that even if the court lifts the stay, they would defer deportation until at least 1 May, to allow Demjanjuk time to file an appeal to the US Supreme Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_doctor_99-2"><a href="#cite_note-cnn_doctor-99">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[95]</a></sup> On 23 April 2009, a group of students from the Hebrew Academy of the Five Towns and Rockaway petitioned the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals to uphold the deportation order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deportation_to_Germany">Deportation to Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Deportation to Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Demjanjuk was deported to Germany, leaving Cleveland, Ohio, on 11 May 2009, to arrive in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> on 12 May. Upon his arrival, he was arrested and sent to Munich's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadelheim_prison" title="Stadelheim prison">Stadelheim prison</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Demjanjuk_deemed_fit_to_stand_trial">Demjanjuk deemed fit to stand trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Demjanjuk deemed fit to stand trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 3 July 2009, prosecutors deemed Demjanjuk fit to stand trial. On 13 July 2009, prosecutors charged him with 27,900 counts of accessory to murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[98]</a></sup> Doctors restricted the time Demjanjuk could be tried in court each day to two sessions of 90 minutes each, said Munich State Prosecutor Anton Winkler. Demjanjuk's attorney said that due to the complexity of the evidence, the mandated short sessions could cause the trial to last three to five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap_demjanjuk_att.27y_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap_demjanjuk_att.27y-104">[99]</a></sup> Due to this and what he called Demjanjuk's ill health, Demjanjuk's attorney called for the case to be closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap_demjanjuk_att.27y_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap_demjanjuk_att.27y-104">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 September 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lviv</a> Regional Council adopted an appeal to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ukraine" title="President of Ukraine">President of Ukraine</a> <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Yushchenko" title="Viktor Yushchenko">Viktor Yushchenko</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Prime_Minister" title="Ukrainian Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Yulia_Tymoshenko" title="Yulia Tymoshenko">Yulia Tymoshenko</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Verkhovna_Rada" title="Verkhovna Rada">Verkhovna Rada</a> (Ukraine's parliament). Members of the Council asked them to intercede on behalf of Demjanjuk. The deputies also called the prosecution of Demjanjuk an "international conspiracy aimed at discrediting Ukraine and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainians</a> in the eyes of world public opinion"; they believed "Without a doubt, the materials of the case were forged and fabricated by <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Displaced_person_claim">Displaced person claim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Displaced person claim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 21 April 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Australian" title="The Australian">The Australian</a></i> reported that Demjanjuk registered in 1948 as a <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" title="Displaced person">displaced person</a>, a category largely consisting of Eastern European refugees who were housed in <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">Displaced persons camps</a> after the war until they emigrated to new homes and lives. The <a href="/wiki/International_Tracing_Service" title="International Tracing Service">International Tracing Service</a>, a German agency that maintains data on Nazi victims, provided a copy of Demjanjuk's 3 March 1948, application to <i>The Australian</i>. In that application, Demjanjuk indicated he was a refugee, sought assistance, and asked for transfer to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. His application stated that he had worked as a driver in Sobibor Poland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[101]</a></sup> in the town of Sobibor, in eastern Poland, after which the extermination camp was named. Demjanjuk later claimed this was a coincidence and that he picked the name Sobibor randomly from a map. After the war, Demjanjuk lived in southern Germany, working as a driver for various international refugee organisations, according to Spiegel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[102]</a></sup> Demjanjuk found a job as a driver in a displaced persons camp in the Bavarian city of Landshut, and was subsequently transferred to camps in other southern German cities until he ended up in the town of Feldafing near Munich in May 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[103]</a></sup> When interviewed in connection with the story, historian Hans-Jürgen Bömelburg (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Giessen" title="University of Giessen">University of Giessen</a>) stated that war criminals sometimes tried to escape justice after 1945 by presenting themselves victims of Nazi persecution, rather than as perpetrators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[104]</a></sup> Many former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_crimes_against_Soviet_POWs" title="Nazi crimes against Soviet POWs">Soviet POWs</a> and former <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_laborers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Slave laborers (page does not exist)">slave laborers</a> and concentration camp inmates who found themselves as refugees were motivated to seek anonymity, create aliases and hide their Soviet Union origins to avoid forced repatriation to Stalinist Russia under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Agreement</a>. Many former Soviet POWs, and Soviets who fought against the Red Army were also motivated to conceal their Soviet origins to avoid being executed under secret Allied military operations such as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Keelhaul" title="Operation Keelhaul">Operation Keelhaul</a>. After the war ended, there were millions of former forced laborers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OST-Arbeiter" title="OST-Arbeiter">OST-Arbeiter</a> in occupied Germany, most of whom were Ukrainian, but also Russian, Belarusian, Crimean Tatar, and others. Many of them, having experienced the brutal and genocidal policies of the Stalin regime, wanted to emigrate to the US or other western democracies rather than being forcibly repatriated to the Soviet Union.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Gitta_Sereny" title="Gitta Sereny">Gitta Sereny</a>'s <i>The German Trauma</i> (2000, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780140292633">ISBN 978-0-14-029263-3</a>) there was a strong reason why Demjanjuk could have put down the Polish town of Sobibor, 5 km west of the Soviet border, as the place where he worked from 1937 to 1943 as a farmhand (according to Sereny) in the application he made on 3 March 1948. Applicants for <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" title="Displaced person">displaced person</a> status had to fill in a form that required them to say where they had been for the previous twelve years. Unless they could show that they had been living outside the Soviet Union on 1 September 1939, they could not, under the terms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yalta_agreement" title="Yalta agreement">Yalta agreement</a>, be accepted as displaced persons and would be returned to the Soviet Union. Officials of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Refugee_Organisation" title="International Refugee Organisation">International Refugee Organisation</a>, which operated the <a href="/wiki/Landshut" title="Landshut">Landshut</a> office, therefore encouraged applicants to select places outside the Soviet Union. Sobibor was such a place. Sereny further relates that at Demjanjuk's trial in Israel on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treblinka" title="Treblinka">Treblinka</a> charges, Judge Dov Levin had asked Demjanjuk, "Why did you put Sobibor?" Demjanjuk had replied that he had no idea what to put on the form and that another applicant had had an atlas and told him to put down Sobibor as there had been many Soviets there. Evidence that Demjanjuk might have been at Sobibor rather than Treblinka contributed to his successful appeal against his conviction in Israel on the Treblinka charges. He was not prosecuted in Israel on any charges relating to Sobibor as, <i>inter alia</i>, his extradition from the US had been obtained on the basis of the Treblinka charges. An 11 August 2010, <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)"><i>Esquire</i> magazine</a> article written and researched by Scott Raab questioned the whole idea of Demjanjuk's trial, crime, and punishment, pointing out many of the absurdities of this particular case, stating specifically "Worse, Demjanjuk is essentially on trial not for anything he did, but simply for being at Sobibor. No specific criminal acts need be alleged, much less proved. Page through transcripts of previous Nazi trials and you'll find a rigorous focus on particulars, because that is what should be required to convict a defendant. No one in any such trial ever was convicted simply on the basis of being present at the scene."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[105]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_in_Germany">Trial in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Trial in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 30 November 2009, Demjanjuk's trial, expected to last for several months, began in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[106]</a></sup> Demjanjuk arrived in the courtroom in a wheelchair pushed by a German police officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some 35 plaintiffs were admitted to file in the case, including four survivors of the Sobibor concentration camp and 26 relatives of victims. The <a href="/wiki/Central_Council_of_Jews_in_Germany" title="Central Council of Jews in Germany">Central Council of Jews in Germany</a> issued a statement in regard to the extradition and the trial, stressing their symbolic significance. "All NS (National Socialist) criminals still living shall know that there won't be mercy for them, regardless of their age. They have to be held accountable for their inhuman deeds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[108]</a></sup> In February 2010, Alexei Vaitsen, an 87-year-old Holocaust survivor living in <a href="/wiki/Ryazan" title="Ryazan">Ryazan</a>, Russia who escaped from Sobibor during the 1943 uprising, claimed that he recognized Demjanjuk as being one of the camp's personnel in a radio interview in Russia; however, the prosecuting attorney and defense lawyer in Demjanjuk current trial were skeptical about this particular claim. On 14 April 2010, Anton Dallmeyer, an <a href="/wiki/Expert_witness" title="Expert witness">expert witness</a>, testified that the typeset and handwriting on an ID card being used as key evidence matched four other ID cards believed to have been issued at the SS training camp at Trawniki. Demjanjuk's lawyer argued that all of the ID cards could be forgeries, and that there was no point comparing them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>The so-called <a href="/wiki/Trawniki_concentration_camp" title="Trawniki concentration camp">Trawniki ID card</a> on which the German prosecutors case relied heavily was the same controversial card used previously as evidence by Israeli prosecutors. It was controversial for several reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP2032012Semotiuk_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-KP2032012Semotiuk-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Count <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Tolstoy" title="Nikolai Tolstoy">Nikolai Tolstoy</a>, a British historian, pointed out to Yoram Sheftel, the Israeli attorney who defended Demjanjuk in Israel, that there was an entire Soviet KGB division known as Division 14 that dealt solely with the forgery of documents. Sheftel indicated that the components of such cards, including pre-signed Nazi signatures, seized by Soviet troops were available to the Soviets to make up false papers.</li>
<li>Dr. Grant, who Sheftel claimed was the world's foremost forensic expert and the man who revealed the forgeries of the "Mussolini diaries" and the "Hitler diaries", testified in Israel that the Demjanjuk signature on the card differed from all the others attributed to him. Dr. Grant pointed out that there were two puncture holes in the right side of the photo on the card but not through the paper of the card. Judging by the purple ink found inside the holes which was similar to ink used by the KGB and the nature of the spacing of the holes, Dr. Grant concluded that the photograph was probably unstapled from some other Soviet document and attached to the ID card in the Soviet Union. Dr. Grant concluded his evidence by saying "The Trawniki document cannot be an authentic document belonging to the defendant Demjanjuk."</li>
<li>Michael Shaked, the prosecutor in the Israeli case, indicated that on 23 January 1987 the original Trawniki card was provided for examination to the German police force's main criminal-identification laboratory in Wiesbaden. The laboratory analysts indicated that even after a cursory examination it was evident that the document was a forgery. The picture was not originally attached to the card, but had been transferred from another document.</li>
</ol>
<p>On 24 February 2010, a star witness for the prosecution, Alex Nagorny, who agreed to serve the Nazi Germans after his capture, raised doubts about the case against Demjanjuk, telling the court the man on trial in Munich state court didn't look like his fellow guard at the Flossenbuerg camp. (There were three different former guards named Nagorny and it was not immediately clear whether the man who testified in the Demjanjuk trial was the same who allegedly served in Treblinka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[110]</a></sup> Nagorny told the Munich state court that he knew Demjanjuk from the Flossenbuerg camp. "We were brought there and Ivan was already there", the 92-year-old testified, referring to Demjanjuk by his birth name. "He was a guard there. He did the same thing I did. I did not know him before Flossenbuerg." Nagorny has previously told investigators he arrived at Flossenbuerg with Demjanjuk. Nagorny testified he lived with Demjanjuk in a barracks room in Flossenbuerg and then shared an apartment with him in Landshut, Germany, after the war. When asked to identify Demjanjuk in the courtroom, Nagorny could not. Nagorny walked over to the bed where Demjanjuk lay and looked at him closely. When Demjanjuk removed the sunglasses that he was wearing, Nagorny said quickly: "That's definitely not him – no resemblance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2012_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2012-116">[111]</a></sup> Nagorny also cast doubt on the provenance of the Trawnicki card testifying that in the final days of the war the Trawniki guards destroyed their cards before being taken prisoner by the Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2012_116-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2012-116">[111]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 15 January 2011, Spain requested a European <a href="/wiki/Arrest_warrant" title="Arrest warrant">arrest warrant</a> be issued for alleged Nazi war crimes while Demjanjuk was still on trial in Germany; specifically, it alleged that Demjanjuk was involved in the deaths of 60 Spaniards imprisoned at Flossenbürg while serving as a guard there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[112]</a></sup> Germany denied Spain's extradition request on 31 May 2011, citing jurisdictional concerns and insufficient evidence provided by Spanish authorities of Demjanjuk's involvement in the crimes alleged by them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 April 2011, a 1985 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> report that had been marked secret for the previous 25 years was declassified and found by reporters of the Associated Press News Media in the National Archives. It brought to light a judgement that the Trawnicki ID card that had been provided by a Soviet source to the US was "quite likely fabricated" evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 May 2012, Dr. Ulrich Busch, Demjanjuk's lawyer, claimed that documents and witness accounts from Trawniki on Demjanjuk differed so widely that there must have been about six different Demjanjuks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[115]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 May 2011, aged 91, Demjanjuk was convicted as an <a href="/wiki/Accessory_(legal_term)" title="Accessory (legal term)">accessory</a> to the murder of 27,900 Jews and sentenced to five years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matussek_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matussek-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-welt.de_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-welt.de-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Connor-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraemer_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kraemer-22">[22]</a></sup> Presiding Judge Ralph Alt ordered Demjanjuk released from custody pending his appeal. The judge suspended the remainder of the sentence, noting Demjanjuk had already served two years during trial, was 91 years old, and had served 8 years in Israel on related charges that were later overturned. Alt said there were no grounds to hold Demjanjuk. "It's the law, and so it's justice, <i>he added. "I say he's guilty, but it's not a final verdict.</i> Demjanjuk's interim conviction had the potential to set a new legal precedent in Germany, opening the possibility of hundreds of new investigations. This was the first time someone has been convicted solely on the basis of serving as a camp guard, with no evidence of being involved in the death of any specific inmate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>The feasibility of mistaken identity was bolstered when on 11 June 2011, the Kyiv Post reported that in his home village of Dubovye Makharintsy, Vinnycia Oblast of Ukraine, records show that two Ivan Demjanjuks served in the Soviet Red Army in WW II. Reportedly, one Ivan Andriyevych Demjanjuk committed suicide in 1971, after he learned that Soviet KGB agents had come to question him. According to a Red Army veteran and witness, in the early 1980s, the KGB came to Dubovi Makharyntsi and took all of John Demjanjuk's family photos and letters he had written to his mother and sister, who remained in the village after the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[118]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demjanjuk's conviction was invalidated upon his death because the German Appellate Court had not tried the appeal, nor ruled on the validity of the legal precedent. According to German law, no conviction stands until all appeal rights have been exhausted. The court did not explain why the appeal hearings were delayed. As a consequence, Demjanjuk remains legally innocent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP2032012Semotiuk_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-KP2032012Semotiuk-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The unique nature of the legal argument he was convicted under has been noted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[119]</a></sup> Christiaan F. Rüter, Professor of Law and expert on NS trials in Germany, who researched the subject at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam" title="University of Amsterdam">University of Amsterdam</a> for 40 years, expressed reservations against the commencement of proceedings stating that to him "it is a complete mystery, how anyone who knows the German jurisdiction up to now, would be able to assume that Demjanjuk could be sentenced based on the given evidence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HM30112009Douglas_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-HM30112009Douglas-35">[35]</a></sup> The trial has been criticized for its lack of evidence other than an interview summary supposedly conducted by the Soviet Union <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> of another prison guard who died some time in the 1990s, and a Soviet-supplied ID card from the Trawniki camp that a 1985 report from the Cleveland office of the FBI concluded was "quite likely" a KGB forgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP2032012Semotiuk_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-KP2032012Semotiuk-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the trial, Demjanjuk accused Germany of using him as a <a href="/wiki/Scapegoat" title="Scapegoat">scapegoat</a> "to make them (the war crimes) forgotten and to claim that the true criminals of the Nazi crimes were me, the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainians</a> and the European neighbours of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP2032012Semotiuk_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-KP2032012Semotiuk-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yoram Sheftel, the lawyer who represented Demjanjuk during the Israel trial in the 1980s, criticized the German court for conducting a show trial. "There was a shameful farce here", he said. "Certainly the German court did not believe its own ruling." "Nothing has changed since then", he said. "Even during the trial in Germany, there was not one person who testified that Demjanjuk was Ivan from Sobibor, by virtue that he was seen there, and as such the conviction is a farce." Sheftel said he was convinced that the Israeli Supreme Court's acquittal and ruling regarding Demjanjuks innocence was correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[122]</a></sup> Sheftel said there was "no credible evidence to produce any crime against Demjanjuk to convict him on any charge connected to the Holocaust.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[123]</a></sup></p>
<p>Congressman <a href="/wiki/James_Traficant" title="James Traficant">James Traficant</a> championed the unpopular cause of Demjanjuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[124]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_Piper" title="Michael Collins Piper">Michael Collins Piper</a> claimed that Traficant was prosecuted and targeted by the Department of Justice for his defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[125]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Fringe_theories#Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Fringe theories"><span title="Material near this tag may use an unreliable fringe source or non-independent unreliable source. (June 2016)">Unreliable fringe source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_consequences_for_others">Legal consequences for others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Legal consequences for others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 6 April 2013 the <a href="/wiki/Central_Office_of_the_State_Justice_Administrations_for_the_Investigation_of_National_Socialist_Crimes" title="Central Office of the State Justice Administrations for the Investigation of National Socialist Crimes">Central Office of the State Justice Administrations for the Investigation of National Socialist Crimes</a> announced it would charge 50 former guards at <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a> with accessory to murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAIN6413D50_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-RAIN6413D50-131">[126]</a></sup> The investigation lacked direct witnesses, but the agency hoped that available written records would suffice in court, as was the case with Demjanjuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAIN6413D50_131-1"><a href="#cite_note-RAIN6413D50-131">[126]</a></sup> In April 2015, the trial of <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Gr%C3%B6ning" title="Oskar Gröning">Oskar Gröning</a> began.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_posthumous_appeal_to_restore_US_citizenship">Death and posthumous appeal to restore US citizenship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Demjanjuk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Death and posthumous appeal to restore US citizenship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>John Demjanjuk died at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Care_home" title="Care home">home for the elderly</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bad_Feilnbach" title="Bad Feilnbach">Bad Feilnbach</a>, Germany on 17 March 2012, aged 91.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[127]</a></sup> As a consequence of his appeal not having been heard, Demjanuk's conviction of May 2011 by a lower court was invalidated; and he died without a criminal record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAARETZ2332012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HAARETZ2332012-6">[6]</a></sup> Demjanjuk's lawyer, Dr. Ulrich Busch, had demanded that the Munich court publish a clarifying statement that Demjanjuk was presumed innocent and without a criminal record "Given the false statements in international media reports that Demjanjuk died a convicted war criminal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAARETZ2332012_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HAARETZ2332012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his death, his relatives requested that he be buried in the United States, where he used to live. Jewish organizations have opposed this, claiming that his burial site would become a centre for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Nazi" title="Neo-Nazi">neo-Nazi</a> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-DemjanjukBBCNews2032012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[128]</a></sup> On 31 March 2012, John Demjanjuk was reported to be buried at an undisclosed US location, now known to be the Ukrainian section of the Brooklyn Heights cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Parma,_Ohio" title="Parma, Ohio">Parma, Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[129]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 April 2012, Demjanjuk's attorney's filed an appeal to posthumously restore his US citizenship. The motion filed in the 6th Circuit Court of Appeals in Cincinnati alleged that the US government withheld a key FBI document that could have helped him in his bid to keep his citizenship. In 2011 the Associated Press had uncovered a secret 1985 FBI report which indicated that a Nazi ID card showing that Demjanjuk had served as a death camp guard was a Soviet-made fake. The bulk of the defense motion was in regard to the government withholding that document along with an ongoing pattern of withholding other exculpatory materials. The new suit claimed, however, that a matter does not rest on a singular document, but that "hundreds of files" recently declassified by a National Archives and Records Administration in Maryland could have aided Demjanjuk's efforts to recover US citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[130]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 June 2012, the 6th US Circuit Court of Appeals in Cincinnati ruled that Demjanjuk could not regain his citizenship posthumously. The three-judge panel said in its ruling that his death made the case moot, and that the appeal was without merit, given the court's 2004 decision that Demjanjuk's denaturalization should not be revoked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[131]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[132]</a></sup> On 11 September 2012, the court denied Demjanjuk's request to have the appeal reheard <i><a href="/wiki/En_banc" title="En banc">en banc</a></i> by the full court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[133]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early June 2012, Ulrich Busch, Demjanjuk's attorney, filed a complaint with Bavarian prosecutors claiming that the pain medication Novalgin (known in the US as <a href="/wiki/Metamizole" title="Metamizole">metamizole</a> or dipyrone) that had been administered to Demjanjuk helped lead to his death. Busch asked prosecutors to open an investigation of five doctors and a nurse on suspicion of manslaughter and causing bodily harm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[134]</a></sup> The investigation was closed in November 2012 after no evidence emerged to support the allegations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[135]</a></sup></p>
