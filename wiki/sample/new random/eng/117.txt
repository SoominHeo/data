<p><b>Takuan Sōhō</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">沢庵 宗彭</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, December 24, 1573 – January 27, 1645)</span> was a major figure in the <a href="/wiki/Rinzai_school" title="Rinzai school">Rinzai school</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhism</a>.</p>
<p>Takuan Sōhō was born into a family of farmers in the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izushi" title="Izushi">Izushi</a>, in what was called <a href="/wiki/Tajima_Province" title="Tajima Province">Tajima Province</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Hy%C5%8Dgo_Prefecture" title="Hyōgo Prefecture">Hyōgo Prefecture</a>). At the age of 8 in 1581, Takuan began his religious studies; two years later he entered a Buddhist monastery of the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Ddo-sh%C5%AB" title="Jōdo-shū">Pure Land Sect</a>. By the age of 14 in 1587, Takuan started studying the system of the <a href="/wiki/Rinzai_school" title="Rinzai school">Rinzai school</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhism</a> under the tutelage of his <a href="/wiki/Sensei" title="Sensei">sensei</a> Shun'oku Soen (春屋宗園:1529–1611).</p>
<p>By age 36 in 1608, Takuan was made abbot of the <a href="/wiki/Daitoku-ji" title="Daitoku-ji">Daitoku-ji</a> Temple in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, Japan. Takuan's appointment was shortened as he left for a prolonged period of traveling. Throughout his journeys, Takuan raised and collected funds for the renovation of <a href="/wiki/Daitoku-ji" title="Daitoku-ji">Daitoku-ji</a> Temple and other Zen temples.</p>
<p>In 1629, Takuan was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banish" title="Banish">banished</a> to northern Japan by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shogunate" title="Shogunate">Shogunate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Hidetada" title="Tokugawa Hidetada">Tokugawa Hidetada</a> due to his protest of political interference in Buddhist temple matters pertaining to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> appointments. By 1632, there was a general <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a> after the death of Tokugawa Hidetada, and Takuan’s period of banishment came to an end. Later, after a meeting between the two arranged by <a href="/wiki/Yagy%C5%AB_Munenori" title="Yagyū Munenori">Yagyū Munenori</a> in Kyoto, Takuan was ordered by <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Iemitsu" title="Tokugawa Iemitsu">Tokugawa Iemitsu</a> (1604–51), who was deeply struck by the encounter, to become the first abbot of Tōkaiji Temple (東海寺) in <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a>, which he had constructed especially for Takuan in order to ensure he could draw on Takuan's counsel at any time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takuan_S%C5%8Dh%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Takuan Sōhō died in <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>) in December 1645. At the moment before his death, Takuan painted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a> 夢 ("<a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">dream</a>"), laid down his brush and died. Despite instructions to bury him in unmarked ground and carry on the normal routine of life without further ado or ceremony, monuments were raised, one being the tomb which may be found in the <a href="/wiki/Shinagawa" title="Shinagawa">Shinagawa</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> at Oyama Cemetery of Tōkaiji Temple.</p>
<p>Takuan advised and befriended many people, from all social strata. Some of these include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Itt%C5%8Dsai" title="Itō Ittōsai">Itō Ittōsai</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">Kenjutsu</a> master, founder of the Ittō-ryū)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mikogami_Tenzen" title="Mikogami Tenzen">Mikogami Tenzen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">Kenjutsu</a> master, successor of Itō Ittōsai)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yagy%C5%AB_Munenori" title="Yagyū Munenori">Yagyū Munenori</a> (<i>daimyō</i> and <a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">kenjutsu</a> master, head of <a href="/wiki/Yagy%C5%AB_Shinkage-ry%C5%AB" title="Yagyū Shinkage-ryū">Yagyū Shinkage-ryū</a> style of swordsmanship) - Takuan's writings to <a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">kenjutsu</a> master, Lord Yagyū Munenori and Mikogami Tenzen, are commonly studied by contemporary martial artists.</li>
<li>Matsudaira Dewa no Kami (<i>daimyō</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ishida_Mitsunari" title="Ishida Mitsunari">Ishida Mitsunari</a> (<i>daimyō</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kuroda_Nagamasa" title="Kuroda Nagamasa">Kuroda Nagamasa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <i>daimyō</i>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Mizunoo_of_Japan" title="Emperor Go-Mizunoo of Japan">Go-Mizunoo</a> (abdicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Emperor" title="Japanese Emperor">Japanese Emperor</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Iemitsu" title="Tokugawa Iemitsu">Tokugawa Iemitsu</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">Shogun</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Takuan remained largely unaffected by his popularity and famed reputation. Known for his acerbic wit and integrity of character, Takuan exerted himself to bring the spirit of Zen Buddhism to many and diverse aspects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_culture" title="Japanese culture">Japanese culture</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_swordsmanship" title="Japanese swordsmanship">Japanese swordsmanship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_rock_garden" title="Japanese rock garden">gardening</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumi-e" title="Sumi-e">Sumi-e</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shodo" title="Shodo">Shodo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony" title="Japanese tea ceremony">Sado</a>. His collected writings total six volumes and over 100 published poems, including his best known treatise, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Unfettered_Mind" title="The Unfettered Mind">The Unfettered Mind</a></i>. His influence permeates the work of many present-day exponents of Zen Buddhism and <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial arts</a>. He has been credited with the invention of the yellow pickled <a href="/wiki/Daikon" title="Daikon">daikon</a> radish that carries the name "<a href="/wiki/Takuan" title="Takuan">takuan</a>".</p>
<p>Of the three essays included in "The Unfettered Mind", two were letters: Fudochishinmyoroku, "The Mysterious Record of Immovable Wisdom", written to <a href="/wiki/Yagy%C5%AB_Munenori" title="Yagyū Munenori">Yagyū Munenori</a>, head of the Yagyū Shinkage school of swordsmanship and teacher to two generations of shoguns; and Taiaki, "Annals of the Sword Taia", written perhaps to Munenori or possibly to Ono Tadaaki, head of the Itto school of swordsmanship and also an official instructor to the shogun's family and close retainers. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_appearances">Fictional appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takuan_S%C5%8Dh%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fictional appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He is featured as a character in <i><a href="/wiki/Vagabond_(manga)" title="Vagabond (manga)">Vagabond</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> series, which is largely based on <a href="/wiki/Eiji_Yoshikawa" title="Eiji Yoshikawa">Eiji Yoshikawa</a>'s equally successful book, <i><a href="/wiki/Musashi_(novel)" title="Musashi (novel)">Musashi</a></i>.</p>
<p>Director/writer <a href="/wiki/Yoshiaki_Kawajiri" title="Yoshiaki Kawajiri">Yoshiaki Kawajiri</a> in his popular animated film <i><a href="/wiki/Ninja_Scroll" title="Ninja Scroll">Ninja Scroll</a></i> created one of main characters Dakuan as a homage to Takuan Soho.</p>
<p>In director <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Inagaki" title="Hiroshi Inagaki">Hiroshi Inagaki</a>'s Samurai trilogy (<a href="/wiki/Samurai_I:_Musashi_Miyamoto" title="Samurai I: Musashi Miyamoto">Samurai I: Musashi Miyamoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samurai_II:_Duel_at_Ichijoji_Temple" title="Samurai II: Duel at Ichijoji Temple">Samurai II: Duel at Ichijoji Temple</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samurai_III:_Duel_at_Ganryu_Island" title="Samurai III: Duel at Ganryu Island">Samurai III: Duel at Ganryu Island</a>), Takuan is played by Kuroemon Onoe and is portrayed as the mentor of <a href="/wiki/Miyamoto_Musashi" title="Miyamoto Musashi">Miyamoto Musashi</a>.</p>
