<p><b>NGC 6946</b> (also known as the <b>Fireworks Galaxy</b>, <b>Arp 29</b>, and <b>Caldwell 12</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_spiral_galaxy" title="Intermediate spiral galaxy">intermediate spiral galaxy</a> about 22 million <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a> <a href="/wiki/Cepheus_(constellation)" title="Cepheus (constellation)">Cepheus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation)" title="Cygnus (constellation)">Cygnus</a>. It was discovered by <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> on September 9, 1798. NGC 6946 is highly obscured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_matter" title="Interstellar matter">interstellar matter</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy" title="Milky Way galaxy">Milky Way galaxy</a>, as it is quite close to the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_plane" title="Galactic plane">galactic plane</a>. The diameter of the galaxy is approximately 40,000 light-years or just about a third of the size of the Milky Way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the past century, eight supernovas have been observed to explode in the arms of this galaxy. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandra_Space_Telescope" title="Chandra Space Telescope">Chandra Space Telescope</a> observations have, in fact, revealed three of the oldest supernovas ever detected in X-rays, giving more credence to its nickname of the "Fireworks Galaxy". This composite image also includes optical data from the Gemini Observatory in red, yellow, and cyan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supernovae">Supernovae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6946&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Supernovae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernovae" title="Supernovae">supernovae</a> have been observed in NGC 6946 in the last 100 years: <a href="/wiki/SN_1917A" title="SN 1917A">SN 1917A</a>, <a href="/wiki/SN_1939C" title="SN 1939C">SN 1939C</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_1948B&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 1948B (page does not exist)">SN 1948B</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_1968D&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 1968D (page does not exist)">SN 1968D</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_1969P&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 1969P (page does not exist)">SN 1969P</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_1980K&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 1980K (page does not exist)">SN 1980K</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_2002hh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 2002hh (page does not exist)">SN 2002hh</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_2004et&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 2004et (page does not exist)">SN 2004et</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SN_2008S&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SN 2008S (page does not exist)">SN 2008S</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-list_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-list-6">[6]</a></sup> This makes it the most prolific known galaxy for this type of event over a period of 100 years. By comparison, the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy, which has double the number of stars as NGC 6946, averages one supernova event per century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It also contained the failed supernova potential blackhole forming star <a href="/wiki/N6946-BH1" title="N6946-BH1">N6946-BH1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
