<p>The <b>cloudy catshark</b> (<i>Scyliorhinus torazame</i>) is a common <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Catshark" title="Catshark">catshark</a>, belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Catshark" title="Catshark">Scyliorhinidae</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic_fish" title="Benthic fish">bottom-dweller</a> that inhabits rocky <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a> in the northwestern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, from the shore to a depth of 320Â m (1,050Â ft). Growing up to 50Â cm (20Â in) long, this small, slim shark has a narrow head with a short blunt snout, no grooves between the nostrils and mouth, and furrows on the lower but not the upper jaw. It is also characterized by extremely rough skin and coloration consisting of a series of dark brown saddles along its back and tail, along with various darker and lighter spots in larger individuals.</p>
<p>The diet of the cloudy catshark consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fishes</a>. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a>, with females laying <a href="/wiki/Egg_case_(Chondrichthyes)" title="Egg case (Chondrichthyes)">encapsulated eggs</a> two at a time in nursery areas. The <a href="/wiki/Clasper" title="Clasper">claspers</a> of the male bear numerous hooks that likely serve to facilitate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copulation" title="Copulation">copulation</a>. This harmless shark can be readily maintained in captivity and is used as a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> for biological research. It is <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">caught incidentally</a>, and generally discarded, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_fisheries" title="Commercial fisheries">commercial fisheries</a>. These activities do not appear to have negatively affected its population, leading it to be listed under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudy_catshark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original description of the cloudy catshark was published in 1908 by <a href="/wiki/Shigeho_Tanaka" title="Shigeho Tanaka">Shigeho Tanaka</a> in the <i>Journal of the Faculty of Science, University of Tokyo</i>. He gave it the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>torazame</i>, which is its Japanese name (èé®«, literally "tiger shark"), and assigned it to the genus <i>Catulus</i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen">type specimen</a> was a 45Â cm (18Â in) long adult male caught off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misaki,_Kanagawa" title="Misaki, Kanagawa">Misaki, Kanagawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanaka_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanaka-3">[3]</a></sup> Subsequent authors have <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonymized</a> <i>Catulus</i> with <i>Scyliorhinus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-compagno-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudy_catshark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="549" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg/220px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg/330px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg/440px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scyliorhinus_torazame_enoshima.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The cloudy catshark reaches 50Â cm (20Â in) long and has a thin, deep, and firm body. The narrow head makes up slightly under one-sixth of the total length, and is two-thirds as wide as it is long. The snout is short and rounded. The large nostrils are preceded by small, triangular flaps of skin that do not reach the wide mouth. The medium-sized eyes are horizontally oval, equipped with rudimentary <a href="/wiki/Nictitating_membrane" title="Nictitating membrane">nictitating membranes</a> (protective third eyelids), and followed by moderate <a href="/wiki/Spiracle" title="Spiracle">spiracles</a>. There are no grooves between the nostrils and the mouth. There are furrows extending from the corners of the mouth over the lower jaw only. The small teeth have a long central cusp typically flanked by two pairs of cusplets. The five pairs of <a href="/wiki/Gill_slit" title="Gill slit">gill slits</a> are short, with the fourth pair over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral fin</a> origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-compagno-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nakaya_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nakaya-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fins</a> are placed towards the back of the body, with the first originating over the rear of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fin</a> bases. The first dorsal fin has a rounded apex and is larger than the second dorsal fin, which has a more angular shape. The pectoral and pelvic fins are moderate in size. In males, the inner margins of the pelvic fins are merged to form an "apron" over the long, cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Clasper" title="Clasper">claspers</a>. The origin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a> lies approximately between the dorsal fins. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_peduncle" title="Caudal peduncle">caudal peduncle</a> is about as deep as the body, and leads to a low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a> with an indistinct lower lobe and a ventral notch near the tip of the upper lobe. The skin is thick and very rough due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_denticle" title="Dermal denticle">dermal denticles</a>, which are large and upright with three backward-pointing teeth. This species is brown on the back and sides, with 6â10 indistinct darker dorsal saddles, and plain yellowish on its ventral side. Larger sharks also have many large, irregularly shaped light and dark spots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-compagno-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nakaya_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nakaya-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudy_catshark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cloudy catshark is common in the northwestern Pacific off <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_et_al_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-compagno_et_al-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">Bottom-dwelling</a> in nature, this species can be found from the shore out to a depth of 320Â m (1,050Â ft) on the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> and upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> It favors rocky reefs and does not appear to be <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-michael-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_ecology">Biology and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudy_catshark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The cloudy catshark feeds primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fishes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-7">[7]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predator</a> of both this shark and its <a href="/wiki/Egg_case_(Chondrichthyes)" title="Egg case (Chondrichthyes)">egg cases</a> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blotchy_swell_shark" title="Blotchy swell shark">blotchy swell shark</a> (<i>Cephaloscyllium umbratile</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taniuchi_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-taniuchi-8">[8]</a></sup> A known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasite</a> of this species is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myxosporidian" title="Myxosporidian">myxosporidian</a> <i>Chloromyxum scyliorhinum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noble_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-noble-9">[9]</a></sup> Reproduction is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a>; adult females have a single functional <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovary</a> and two functional <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviducts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nakaya_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-nakaya-5">[5]</a></sup> As a prelude to <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>, the male bites at the female's pectoral fin, side, and gill region. Once he has a grip, he wraps his body around hers and inserts one of his claspers into her <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copulation" title="Copulation">Copulation</a> may last between 15 seconds and 4 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pratt_and_carrier_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pratt_and_carrier-10">[10]</a></sup> The claspers of the male are unusual in that each has a row of around a hundred hooks running along the inner margin. These hooks likely serve to anchor the male to the female during copulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt-11">[11]</a></sup> The female is capable of storing <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> within her <a href="/wiki/Nidamental_gland" title="Nidamental gland">nidamental gland</a> (an organ that secretes egg cases) for many months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motoyasu_et_al_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-motoyasu_et_al-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Females produce two mature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> at a time, one per oviduct. The eggs are enclosed in smooth, translucent yellow, vase-shaped capsules measuring 1.9Â cm (0.75Â in) across and 5.5Â cm (2.2Â in) long. There are long tendrils at the four corners of the capsule. The eggs are laid in defined nursery areas: One such area is located at a depth of 100Â m (330Â ft) off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakodate" title="Hakodate">Hakodate</a>. When the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a> is 3.6Â cm (1.4Â in) long, it has external gills, undeveloped fins, and no pigmentation. At an embryonic length of 5.8Â cm (2.3Â in), the external gills have all but disappeared, and a covering of small denticles is present. By a length of 7.9Â cm (3.1Â in), the embryo has well-developed fins and pigmentation, and generally resembles the adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nakaya_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-nakaya-5">[5]</a></sup> The eggs take 15 months to hatch at 11.3Â Â°C (52.3Â Â°F), and 7â9 months to hatch at 14.5Â Â°C (58.1Â Â°F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-michael-6">[6]</a></sup> The newly hatched shark measures 8Â cm (3.1Â in) long or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-compagno-4">[4]</a></sup> Maturation size tends to increase with decreasing water temperature: Off northerly Hakodate, both sexes mature at over 38Â cm (15Â in) long, while some females remain immature even at 47Â cm (19Â in) long. By contrast, off southerly <a href="/wiki/Tsushima_Island" title="Tsushima Island">Tsushima Island</a> both sexes mature at around 33Â cm (13Â in) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nakaya_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-nakaya-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horie_and_tanaka_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-horie_and_tanaka-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_lifespan" title="Maximum lifespan">maximum lifespan</a> is at least 12 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-michael-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_interactions">Human interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudy_catshark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Human interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg/220px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg/330px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg/440px-Scyliorhinus_torazame_shinagawa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Harmless to humans, the cloudy catshark adapts well to captivity and has reproduced in the aquarium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-michael-6">[6]</a></sup> It is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bentley_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bentley-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaas_et_al_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaas_et_al-15">[15]</a></sup> On September 25, 1995, Masuda Motoyashi and colleagues used this species to perform the first successful <a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Elasmobranchii" title="Elasmobranchii">shark or ray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motoyasu_et_al_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-motoyasu_et_al-12">[12]</a></sup> The cloudy catshark is <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">caught incidentally</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_fisheries" title="Commercial fisheries">commercial fisheries</a> with bottom <a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">fishing nets</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawls</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>, as well as on bottom <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longlines</a>. Captured individuals are typically discarded, possibly with a high survival rate due to their hardiness. Some 40% of the fish discarded in <a href="/wiki/Yamaguchi_Prefecture" title="Yamaguchi Prefecture">Yamaguchi Prefecture</a> fisheries are of this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> The bottom trawl fishery operating off <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Prefecture" title="Fukushima Prefecture">Fukushima Prefecture</a> may catch over a ton of cloudy catsharks annually, which are also discarded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sakaguchi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sakaguchi-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite heavy fishing pressure within its range, the cloudy catshark remains common, perhaps because it may be more <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(ecology)" title="Productivity (ecology)">biologically productive</a> than most other sharks. As a result, it has been assessed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> Cloudy catsharks from a number of locations off Japan have been found to be contaminated with <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs) and <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene" title="Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene">dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene</a> (DDEs), which they <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">acquire from their food</a>. One likely source of these <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> is the use of the <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nation" title="Developing nation">developing nations</a> in southern Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horie_et_al_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-horie_et_al-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
