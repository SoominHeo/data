<p>The <b>Frosty Leo Nebula</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_nebula" title="Protoplanetary nebula">protoplanetary nebula</a> (PPN) located roughly at 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (<a href="#CITEREFDavisSmithGledhillVarricatt2005">Davis et al. 2005</a>) away from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Leo_(constellation)" title="Leo (constellation)">Leo</a>. It is a spectral<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Omont1990_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Omont1990-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_nebula" title="Bipolar nebula">bipolar nebula</a>. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_star" title="Central star">central star</a> is of optical spectral type K7II,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davisetal2005_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davisetal2005-2">[2]</a></sup> by itself called <i>Frosty Leo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is unusual in that it has an extremely deep absorption feature at 3.1 µm and is unusually located at more than 900 pc above the plane of our galaxy.(<a href="#CITEREFBourkeHylandRobinsonLuhman2000">Bourke et al. 2000</a>) Further, as of 1990, it has the only known PPN circumstellar outflow in which crystalline ice dominates the long-wavelength emission spectrum and the only known PPN with point-reflection-symmetric deviations from axial symmetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frosty_Leo_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Frosty Leo Nebula has two lobes that are separated by 2″ between which is an almost edge-on dust ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> It also has two relatively faint but prominent compact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebulosity" title="Nebulosity">nebulosities</a>, or <i>ansae</i>, separated by ~23″ along the polar axis of the PPN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> The PPN as a whole has an <a href="/wiki/Hourglass" title="Hourglass">hourglass</a> like shape. It has an inclination angle of 15° relative to the plane of the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davisetal2005_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davisetal2005-2">[2]</a></sup> Its molecular envelope is expanding at a rate of ~25 km/<a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1987_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1987-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">Observation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frosty_Leo_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>This PPN was first noticed in the <a href="/wiki/IRAS" title="IRAS">IRAS</a> survey due to its exceptionally cold IRAS <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">color temperatures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> It also has a uniquely sharp maximum at 60-μM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1987_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1987-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Point_symmetry">Point symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frosty_Leo_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Point symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is the first bipolar PPN known to have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_symmetry" title="Point symmetry">point reflection symmetry</a> (all others being <a href="/wiki/Axial_symmetry" title="Axial symmetry">axially symmetric</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> Point symmetry is a fairly common trait of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_nebulae" title="Planetary nebulae">planetary nebulae</a> as found in <a href="/wiki/NGC_2022" title="NGC 2022">NGC 2022</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGC_2371-2" title="NGC 2371-2">NGC 2371-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGC_6309" title="NGC 6309">NGC 6309</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula" title="Cat's Eye Nebula">Cat's Eye Nebula</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6563&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NGC 6563 (page does not exist)">NGC 6563</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dumbbell_Nebula" title="Dumbbell Nebula">Dumbbell Nebula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Nebula" title="Saturn Nebula">Saturn Nebula</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A24_(nebula)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A24 (nebula) (page does not exist)">A24</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hb5&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hb5 (page does not exist)">Hb5</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="#CITEREFMorrisReipurth1990">Morris &amp; Reipurth 1990</a> postulate that point symmetry is either due to the <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_outflow" title="Bipolar outflow">bipolar outflow</a> being directed by a precessing disc or a precessing common envelope <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frosty_Leo_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="#CITEREFForveilleMorrisOmontLikkel1987">Forveille et al. 1987</a> dubbed IRAS 09371+1212 as the "Frosty Leo Nebula" because of their interpretation of the object's extremely unusual <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">far infrared</a> spectrum that <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is largely depleted in its gaseous state by ice condensation into grains and for its location in the Leo constellation. Their interpretation was subsequently verified in 1988 by three independent papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="#CITEREFOmontForveilleMoseleyGlaccum1990">Omont et al. 1990</a> further observed in the band between 35 and 65 μM that very cold (&lt;50 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> dust grains, abundantly coated with crystalline ice, are responsible for the 60-μM excess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris1990_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1990-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
