<p>The <b>bowmouth guitarfish</b> (<i>Rhina ancylostoma</i>), also called the <b>shark ray</b> or <b>mud skate</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">ray</a> and the sole member of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Rhinidae</b>. Its evolutionary affinities are not fully resolved, though it may be related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinobatidae" title="Rhinobatidae">true guitarfishes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skate_(fish)" title="Skate (fish)">skates</a>. This rare species occurs widely in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> coastal waters of the western <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific" title="Indo-Pacific">Indo-Pacific</a>, at depths of up to 90 m (300 ft). Highly distinctive in appearance, the bowmouth guitarfish has a wide and thick body with a rounded snout and large shark-like <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">tail fins</a>. Its mouth forms a W-shaped undulating line, and there are multiple thorny ridges over its head and back. It has a dorsal color pattern of many white spots over a bluish gray to brown background, with a pair of prominent black markings over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral fins</a>. This large species can reach a length of 2.7 m (8.9 ft) and weight of 135 kg (298 lb).</p>
<p>Usually found near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a>, the bowmouth guitarfish prefers sandy or muddy areas near underwater structures. It is a strong-swimming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predator</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>. This species <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viviparous" title="Viviparous">gives live birth</a> to litters of two to eleven pups, which are nourished during <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yolk" title="Yolk">yolk</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) has assessed the bowmouth guitarfish as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> because it is widely caught by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisanal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fisheries</a> for its valuable fins and meat. It is viewed as a nuisance by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawlers</a>, however, because its bulk and thorny skin cause it to damage netted catches. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_degradation" title="Habitat degradation">Habitat degradation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">destruction</a> pose an additional, significant challenge to this ray's survival. The bowmouth guitarfish adapts well to captivity and is displayed in <a href="/wiki/Public_aquarium" title="Public aquarium">public aquariums</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_phylogeny">Taxonomy and phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowmouth_guitarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalist" title="Naturalist">naturalists</a> <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Elieser_Bloch" title="Marcus Elieser Bloch">Marcus Elieser Bloch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Gottlob_Schneider" title="Johann Gottlob Schneider">Johann Gottlob Schneider</a> described the bowmouth guitarfish in their 1801 <i>Systema Ichthyologiae</i>. Their account was based on a 51 cm (20 in) long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen">specimen</a>, now lost, collected off the <a href="/wiki/Coromandel_Coast" title="Coromandel Coast">Coromandel Coast</a> of India. The genus name <i>Rhina</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>rhinos</i> ("snout"); the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>ancylostoma</i> is derived from the Greek <i>ankylos</i> ("curved" or "crooked") and <i>stoma</i> ("mouth").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloch_and_schneider_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloch_and_schneider-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paepke_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-paepke-4">[4]</a></sup> Although Block and Schneider wrote the epithet as <i>ancylostomus</i> and that form appears in some literature, most modern sources regard the correct form to be <i>ancylostoma</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cas_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cas-5">[5]</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a> for this species include shark ray, mud skate, shortnose mud skate, bow-mouthed angel fish, and bow-mouthed angel shark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The evolutionary relationships between the bowmouth guitarfish and other rays are debated. <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">Morphological</a> evidence generally points to a close relationship between <i>Rhina</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhynchobatus" title="Rhynchobatus">Rhynchobatus</a></i>, which are a group of rays known as the wedgefishes that also have large, shark-like fins. Morphological analyses have tended to place these two genera <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basally</a> among rays, though some have them as basal to just the <a href="/wiki/Guitarfish" title="Guitarfish">guitarfishes</a> (Rhinobatidae) and <a href="/wiki/Skate_(fish)" title="Skate (fish)">skates</a> (Rajidae) while others have them basal to all other rays except <a href="/wiki/Sawfish" title="Sawfish">sawfishes</a> (Pristidae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nishida_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nishida-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mceachran_and_aschliman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mceachran_and_aschliman-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aschliman_et_al_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-aschliman_et_al-9">[9]</a></sup> A 2012 study based on <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> upheld <i>Rhina</i> and <i>Rhynchobatus</i> as sister <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> related to the guitarfishes, but also unexpectedly found that they formed a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> with the sawfishes rather than the skates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naylor_et_al_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-naylor_et_al-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In terms of classification, Bloch and Schneider originally placed the bowmouth guitarfish in the order Abdominales, a now-obsolete grouping of fishes defined by the positioning of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a> directly behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral fins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloch_and_schneider_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloch_and_schneider-3">[3]</a></sup> Modern sources have included it variously in the order <a href="/wiki/Rajiformes" title="Rajiformes">Rajiformes</a>, Rhinobatiformes, Rhiniformes, or the newly proposed Rhinopristiformes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mceachran_and_aschliman_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mceachran_and_aschliman-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naylor_et_al_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-naylor_et_al-10">[10]</a></sup> The placement of the bowmouth guitarfish in the family Rhinidae originates from the group "Rhinae", consisting of <i>Rhina</i> and <i>Rhynchobatus</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Peter_M%C3%BCller" title="Johannes Peter Müller">Johannes Müller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Gustav_Jakob_Henle" title="Friedrich Gustav Jakob Henle">Jakob Henle</a>'s 1841 <i>Systematische Beschreibung der Plagiostomen</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muller_and_henle_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-muller_and_henle-11">[11]</a></sup> Later authors have also assigned this species to the family Rhinobatidae or Rhynchobatidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mceachran_and_aschliman_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-mceachran_and_aschliman-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_and_last_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-compagno_and_last-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_S._Nelson" title="Joseph S. Nelson">Joseph Nelson</a>, in the 2006 fourth edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Fishes_of_the_World" title="Fishes of the World">Fishes of the World</a></i>, placed this species as the sole member of Rhinidae in the order Rajiformes, which is supported by morphological but not molecular data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aschliman_et_al_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-aschliman_et_al-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nelson_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowmouth_guitarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1968" data-file-width="3000" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg/260px-Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg/390px-Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg/520px-Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhina_ancylostoma_georgia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The rounded head, humpbacked profile, and large fins of the bowmouth guitarfish give it a unique appearance.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bowmouth guitarfish is a heavily built fish growing to 2.7 m (8.9 ft) long and 135 kg (298 lb) in weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-6">[6]</a></sup> The head is short, wide, and flattened with an evenly rounded snout; the front portion of the head, including the medium-sized eyes and large <a href="/wiki/Spiracle" title="Spiracle">spiracles</a>, is clearly distinct from the body. The long nostrils are transversely oriented and have well-developed skin flaps on their anterior margins. The lower jaw has three protruding lobes that fit into corresponding depressions in the upper jaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randall_and_hoover_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-randall_and_hoover-14">[14]</a></sup> There are around 47 upper and 50 lower tooth rows arranged in winding bands; the teeth are low and blunt with ridges on the crown. The five pairs of ventral <a href="/wiki/Gill_slit" title="Gill slit">gill slits</a> are positioned close to the lateral margins of the head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_et_al_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith_et_al-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The body is deepest in front of the two tall and falcate (sickle-shaped) <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fins</a>. The first dorsal fin is about a third larger than the second and originates over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fin</a> origins. The second dorsal fin is located midway between the first dorsal and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a>. The broad and triangular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral fins</a> have a deep indentation where their leading margins meet the head. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a> are much smaller than the pectoral fins, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a> is absent. The tail is much longer than the body and ends in a large, crescent-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a>; the lower caudal fin lobe is more than half the length of the upper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_and_last_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-compagno_and_last-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randall_and_hoover_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-randall_and_hoover-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The entire dorsal surface of the bowmouth guitarfish has a grainy texture from a dense covering of tiny <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_denticle" title="Dermal denticle">dermal denticles</a>. A thick ridge is present along the midline of the back, which bears a band of sharp, robust thorns. There are also a pair of thorn-bearing ridges in front of the eyes, a second pair running from above the eyes to behind the spiracles, and a third pair on the "shoulders". This species is bluish to brownish gray above, lightening towards the margins of the head and over the pectoral fins. There are prominent white spots scattered over the body and fins, a white-edged black marking above each pectoral fin, and two dark transverse bands atop the head between the eyes. The underside is light gray to white. Young rays are more vividly colored than adults, which are browner with fainter patterning and proportionately smaller spots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowmouth_guitarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While uncommon, the bowmouth guitarfish is widely distributed in the coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> waters of the western <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific" title="Indo-Pacific">Indo-Pacific</a>. In the Indian Ocean, it is found from <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a> in South Africa to the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>), across the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Shark_Bay" title="Shark Bay">Shark Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Its Pacific range extends northward to Korea and southern Japan, eastward to New Guinea, and southward to <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randall_and_hoover_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-randall_and_hoover-14">[14]</a></sup> Found between 3 and 90 m (10 and 300 ft) deep, this ray spends most of its time near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a> but can occasionally be seen swimming in midwater. It favors sandy or muddy <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>, and can also be found in the vicinity of rocky and <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-michael-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_ecology">Biology and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowmouth_guitarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tigershark2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tigershark2.jpg/220px-Tigershark2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tigershark2.jpg/330px-Tigershark2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tigershark2.jpg/440px-Tigershark2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tigershark2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The tiger shark preys on the bowmouth guitarfish.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bowmouth guitarfish is a strong swimmer that propels itself with its tail like a shark. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">more active at night</a> and is not known to be territorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferrari_and_ferrari_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferrari_and_ferrari-17">[17]</a></sup> This species feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Demersal_fish" title="Demersal fish">demersal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fishes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sciaenidae" title="Sciaenidae">croakers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> are also consumed. Its bands of flattened teeth allow it to crush hard-shelled prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compagno_and_last_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-compagno_and_last-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raje_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-raje-18">[18]</a></sup> Curiously, two bowmouth guitarfishes examined in a 2011 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotope</a> study were found to have fed on <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic</a> rather than demersal animals, in contrast to previous observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borrell_et_al_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-borrell_et_al-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tiger_shark" title="Tiger shark">tiger shark</a> (<i>Galeocerdo cuvier</i>) is known to prey on the bowmouth guitarfish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpfendorfer_et_al_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpfendorfer_et_al-20">[20]</a></sup> The ray is protected by the thorns on its head and back, and it may ram perceived threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">Parasites</a> documented from this species include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapeworm" title="Tapeworm">tapeworms</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carpobothrium_rhinei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carpobothrium rhinei (page does not exist)">Carpobothrium rhinei</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sarada_et_al_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-sarada_et_al-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dollfusiella_michiae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dollfusiella michiae (page does not exist)">Dollfusiella michiae</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_and_beveridge_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-campbell_and_beveridge-22">[22]</a></sup> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nybelinia_southwelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nybelinia southwelli (page does not exist)">Nybelinia southwelli</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palm_and_walter_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-palm_and_walter-23">[23]</a></sup> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stoibocephalum_arafurense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stoibocephalum arafurense (page does not exist)">Stoibocephalum arafurense</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cielocha_and_jensen_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cielocha_and_jensen-24">[24]</a></sup> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tylocephalum_carnpanulatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tylocephalum carnpanulatum (page does not exist)">Tylocephalum carnpanulatum</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-butler-25">[25]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Leech" title="Leech">leech</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pontobdella_macrothela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pontobdella macrothela (page does not exist)">Pontobdella macrothela</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_silva_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_silva-26">[26]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trematode" title="Trematode">trematode</a> <i><a class="new" id="cite_ref-bray_et_al_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bray_et_al-27">[27]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Monogenea" title="Monogenea">monogeneans</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Branchotenthes_robinoverstreeti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Branchotenthes robinoverstreeti (page does not exist)">Branchotenthes robinoverstreeti</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bullard_and_dippenaar_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bullard_and_dippenaar-28">[28]</a></sup> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monocotyle_ancylostomae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monocotyle ancylostomae (page does not exist)">Monocotyle ancylostomae</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_et_al_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-zhang_et_al-29">[29]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nesippus_vespa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nesippus vespa (page does not exist)">Nesippus vespa</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dippenaar_et_al_30-0"><a class="reference" id="cite_ref-izawa_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-izawa-31">[31]</a></sup> There is a record of a bowmouth guitarfish being cleaned by <a href="/wiki/Bluestreak_cleaner_wrasse" title="Bluestreak cleaner wrasse">bluestreak cleaner wrasses</a> (<i>Labroides dimidiatus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-michael-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reproduction in the bowmouth guitarfish is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viviparous" title="Viviparous">viviparous</a>, with the developing <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryos</a> sustained to term by <a href="/wiki/Yolk" title="Yolk">yolk</a>. Adult females have a single functional <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>. The litter size varies between two and eleven pups, and newborns measure 45–51 cm (18–20 in) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-michael-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-devadoss_and_matcha_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-devadoss_and_matcha-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_et_al_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-last_et_al-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">Sexual maturity</a> is attained at lengths of 1.5–1.8 m (4.9–5.9 ft) for males and over 1.8 m (5.9 ft) in females. Females grow larger than males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raje_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-raje-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_interactions">Human interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowmouth_guitarfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Human interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shark_ray_newport.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Shark_ray_newport.jpg/220px-Shark_ray_newport.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Shark_ray_newport.jpg/330px-Shark_ray_newport.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Shark_ray_newport.jpg/440px-Shark_ray_newport.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shark_ray_newport.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Sweet Pea", a female bowmouth guitarfish at the Newport Aquarium.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Throughout its range, the bowmouth guitarfish is caught incidentally or intentionally by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisanal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fisheries</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawls</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>, and line gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> The fins are extremely valuable due to their use in <a href="/wiki/Shark_fin_soup" title="Shark fin soup">shark fin soup</a>, and are often the only parts of the fish kept and brought to market. However, the meat may also be sold fresh or dried and salted, and it is highly esteemed in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raje_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-raje-18">[18]</a></sup> When caught as <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> in trawls, the bowmouth guitarfish is considered a nuisance because its strength and rough skin make it difficult to handle, and as the heavy ray thrashes in the net it can damage the rest of the catch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup> In Thailand, the enlarged thorns of this species are used to make bracelets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fowler_et_al_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-fowler_et_al-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) has assessed the bowmouth guitarfish as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a>. It is threatened by fishing and by <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_degradation" title="Habitat degradation">degradation</a>, particularly from <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">blast fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a>. Its numbers are known to have declined substantially in Indonesian waters, where it is one of the large rays targeted by a mostly unregulated gillnet fishery. The IUCN has given this species a regional assessment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> in Australian waters, where it is not a targeted species but is taken as bycatch in bottom trawls. The installation of <a href="/wiki/Turtle_excluder_device" title="Turtle excluder device">turtle excluder devices</a> on some Australian trawlers has benefited this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> Since it is rare and faces many conservation threats, the bowmouth guitarfish has been called "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panda" title="Panda">panda</a> of the aquatic world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-underwatertimes_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-underwatertimes-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a popular subject of <a href="/wiki/Public_aquarium" title="Public aquarium">public aquariums</a> and fares relatively well, with one individual having lived for seven years in captivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_and_stevens_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-last_and_stevens-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michael_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-michael-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Aquarium" title="Newport Aquarium">Newport Aquarium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> initiated the world's first <a href="/wiki/Captive_breeding" title="Captive breeding">captive breeding</a> program for this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-underwatertimes_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-underwatertimes-35">[35]</a></sup> Newport Aquarium announced in January 2014 that the female, "Sweet Pea", had become pregnant and given birth to seven pups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquariumworks_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-aquariumworks-36">[36]</a></sup> By February 2014, all seven pups had died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On January 7, 2016, Sweet Pea gave birth to nine shark pups<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> which were eating on their own and still gaining weight by February 10, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Newport Aquarium later announced that the pups would be moved into a coral reef exhibit where they can be viewed by the public starting on June 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The species also bred at the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Life_Park" title="Marine Life Park">S.E.A. Aquarium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
