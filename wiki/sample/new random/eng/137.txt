<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Karelian question</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Karelian question</b> or <b>Karelian issue</b> (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <span lang="fi" xml:lang="fi"><i>Karjala-kysymys</i></span>) is a dispute in <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Finland" title="Politics of Finland">Finnish politics</a> over whether or not to try to regain <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Karelia" title="Finnish Karelia">Finnish Karelia</a> and other territories ceded to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Continuation_War" title="Continuation War">Continuation War</a>. Despite the name "Karelian question", the term may refer also to the return of <a href="/wiki/Pechengsky_District" title="Pechengsky District">Petsamo</a>, ceded parts of <a href="/wiki/Salla" title="Salla">Salla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kuusamo" title="Kuusamo">Kuusamo</a>, and four islands in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a>. Sometimes the phrase "debate on the return of the ceded territories" is used. The Karelian question remains a matter of public debate rather than a political issue.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Karelian question arose when Finland was forced to cede territories to the Soviet Union after the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_peace_treaty" title="Moscow peace treaty">Moscow peace treaty</a> in 1940. Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_citizen" title="Finnish citizen">Finnish citizens</a> were <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_of_Finnish_Karelia" title="Evacuation of Finnish Karelia">evacuated from the ceded areas</a>. Most of them returned during the <a href="/wiki/Continuation_War" title="Continuation War">Continuation War</a> and eventually were evacuated again in 1944. The Soviet Union insisted the ceded areas be completely evacuated in 10 days. The evacuees were partly compensated for their losses; farmers, for example, received land in proportion to their earlier holdings. Usually, the compensation was about one third of the original farm. Compensation for movable property was much less. However, all evacuee families had a right to receive a small farm, and/or a plot for a detached house or a flat. The land used for these grants was confiscated by the state from municipalities and private owners. Financial compensation was funded by a general property tax of 10 to 30%, levied over a period of several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Because the vast majority of the evacuees who had to settle in the rest of Finland were from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Karelia" title="Finnish Karelia">ceded Karelia</a>, the question was labeled <i>The Karelian Question</i>. After the Winter War, Karelian municipalities and parishes established <a href="/wiki/Karjalan_Liitto" title="Karjalan Liitto">Karjalan Liitto</a> (the Karelian Association) to defend the rights of Karelians in Finland.</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the Karelian-born Finnish politician <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Virolainen" title="Johannes Virolainen">Johannes Virolainen</a> lobbied for the return of Karelia. President <a href="/wiki/Urho_Kekkonen" title="Urho Kekkonen">Urho Kekkonen</a> also tried to reacquire the territory, especially when the Soviet Union returned the peninsula of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porkkala" title="Porkkala">Porkkala</a> to Finland in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There was, however, no significant public controversy about the case, because Kekkonen wanted to keep it quiet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The last time Kekkonen tried to raise it was in 1972, but he had no success, and public discussion died out in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breakup_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Breakup of the Soviet Union">breakup of the Soviet Union</a>, the Karelian question re-surfaced. According to an article by the Finnish newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Helsingin_Sanomat" title="Helsingin Sanomat">Helsingin Sanomat</a></i> in August 2007, the Russian president <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> unofficially offered to sell ceded Karelia to Finland in 1991 but was declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, according to many Finnish political leaders and the Russian vice Prime Minister of the time, there were no such offers, only unofficial probing of the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Andrei Fyodorov, an advisor of Boris Yeltsin, told the <i>Helsingin Sanomat</i> that he was part of a group that was tasked by the government of Russia in 1991–1992 with calculating the price of returning Karelia to Finland. This price was set at 15 billion US dollars. According to Fyodorov, Finnish president <a href="/wiki/Mauno_Koivisto" title="Mauno Koivisto">Mauno Koivisto</a> and Finnish foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Paavo_V%C3%A4yrynen" title="Paavo Väyrynen">Paavo Väyrynen</a> were aware of these unofficial discussions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Street_in_Vyborg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Street_in_Vyborg.jpg/220px-Street_in_Vyborg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Street_in_Vyborg.jpg/330px-Street_in_Vyborg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Street_in_Vyborg.jpg/440px-Street_in_Vyborg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Street_in_Vyborg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Karjalan_Liitto" title="Karjalan Liitto">Karjalan Liitto</a> is an interest group of Karelian evacuees which hopes that Karelia will once again become part of Finland at some point, but does not openly demand it. Some smaller groups, such as <a href="/wiki/ProKarelia" title="ProKarelia">ProKarelia</a>, continue to campaign for the peaceful return of Karelia. However, no serious political party has openly supported this goal, and Finnish politicians generally say there is no need for it, citing Finland's peace treaty with Russia. There are some individual politicians who support the return of Karelia, such as <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> <a href="/wiki/Ari_Vatanen" title="Ari Vatanen">Ari Vatanen</a>, and two candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_presidential_election,_2006" title="Finnish presidential election, 2006">presidential election of 2006</a>: <a href="/wiki/Timo_Soini" title="Timo Soini">Timo Soini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arto_Lahti" title="Arto Lahti">Arto Lahti</a>. Other candidates have stated that Finland has signed a <a href="/wiki/Peace_treaty" title="Peace treaty">peace treaty</a> and should not campaign for the return of what are now Russian-developed territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During a debate prior to the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_presidential_election,_2012" title="Finnish presidential election, 2012">2012 presidential election</a> Timo Soini reiterated his view that, if elected, he would advance the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_opinions">Official opinions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Official opinions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both Russia and Finland have repeatedly stated that no open territorial dispute exists between the two countries. Finland's official stance is that the borders may be changed through peaceful negotiations, although there is currently no need to hold open talks, as Russia has shown no intention of returning the ceded areas, or discussing the question. In 1994 <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> commented that "seizure of Finnish Karelia" was an example of Stalin's totalitarian and aggressive politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Later in 1997 he stated that the matter was closed. In 2000 President Putin stated that such discussions may endanger Finnish–Russian relations, and in 2001 he said that "changing borders is not the best way to resolve problems", but that possible solutions would be "integration and cooperation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998 <a href="/wiki/President_of_Finland" title="President of Finland">Finnish President</a> <a href="/wiki/Martti_Ahtisaari" title="Martti Ahtisaari">Martti Ahtisaari</a> said that "Finland's official position is that it does not have territorial demands on Russia. However, if Russia wants to discuss returning the ceded areas, Finland is ready for that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Several other politicians holding government office, such as the former foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Erkki_Tuomioja" title="Erkki Tuomioja">Erkki Tuomioja</a> and prime minister <a href="/wiki/Matti_Vanhanen" title="Matti Vanhanen">Matti Vanhanen</a>, have made statements along the same lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polls_and_popular_opinion">Polls and popular opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polls and popular opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The latest polls show that approximately 26% to 38% of Finns would like to see Karelia return to Finnish control and some 51% to 62% would oppose such a move. In Russia, people associate the word "Karelia" with the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Karelia" title="Republic of Karelia">Republic of Karelia</a> instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Karelia" title="Finnish Karelia">Finnish Karelia</a>, which makes conducting polls more difficult. In a 1999 poll by <a href="/wiki/MTV3" title="MTV3">MTV3</a>, 34% of the people of <a href="/wiki/Vyborg" title="Vyborg">Vyborg</a> supported returning Karelia to Finland and 57% were opposed. <a href="/wiki/Vyborg" title="Vyborg">Vyborg</a> and the rest of the ceded Karelia outside the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Karelia" title="Republic of Karelia">Republic of Karelia</a> nowadays contain very few ethnic Finns, and is almost exclusively inhabited by people who moved there during the Soviet era and their descendants.</p>
<p>In the latest poll in Finland about the question, 36 percent of Finns supported the return of ceded territories, compared to 51% who are opposed. The poll was conducted by the newspaper <i>Karjala</i> and the research institute <i>MC-Info Oy</i> on 13 October 2005. In August 2005, a poll by <i><a href="/wiki/Helsingin_Sanomat" title="Helsingin Sanomat">Helsingin Sanomat</a></i> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suomen_Gallup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suomen Gallup (page does not exist)">Suomen Gallup</a>, determined that 30% of Finns supported and 62% opposed the return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_Gallup_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-HS_Gallup-19">[19]</a></sup> In a poll by Taloustutkimus and Karjalan Liitto conducted in May 2005, support was 26% while 58% were opposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A year earlier, a poll by STT showed 38% supporting and 57% opposing. A poll by Taloustutkimus was criticized by <a href="/wiki/ProKarelia" title="ProKarelia">ProKarelia</a> for asking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leading_questions" title="Leading questions">leading questions</a>, such as, "Do you support the return of Karelia, even if it would mean more tense relations or even war with Russia?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> 5% of supporters and of those who declined to respond supported the return even under these circumstances (2.1% of all replies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the people who were born in Karelia and were evacuated want Karelia to become part of Finland. According to polls, older people (ages 65 and up) and young people (15-25) support the idea more strongly than the generation of their parents (25–65) who grew up during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_Gallup_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-HS_Gallup-19">[19]</a></sup> Former President <a href="/wiki/Mauno_Koivisto" title="Mauno Koivisto">Mauno Koivisto</a> has been against a discussion about the question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Support for regaining the ceded areas is also strong among minor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing">right-wing</a> groups.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_and_arguments">Problems and arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Problems and arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Svetogorsk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Svetogorsk.jpg/220px-Svetogorsk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Svetogorsk.jpg/330px-Svetogorsk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Svetogorsk.jpg/440px-Svetogorsk.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Svetogorsk.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Blocks of flats built in the Soviet era in <a href="/wiki/Svetogorsk" title="Svetogorsk">Svetogorsk</a> (Enso)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the main reasons for opposing the return is the fear of the costs it would bring. According to another poll conducted by <i><a href="/wiki/Helsingin_Sanomat" title="Helsingin Sanomat">Helsingin Sanomat</a></i>, 42% of Finns opposing the return list that as the most important single reason. The standard of living on the Russian side of the border is much lower than on the Finnish side. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita">GDP (PPP) per capita</a> in Finland is about double that of Russia.</p>
<p>The costs of bringing Karelia to the same level with the rest of Finland have been researched only by supporters of the idea. According to a survey conducted by ProKarelia, the area has natural advantages that, under Finnish rule, would make it a centre of trade with Russia and industry and thus bring economic growth fast enough to solve the entire problem. According to both ProKarelian research and Arto Lahti's estimate, the price of return would be about 30 billion <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reform_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-reform-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karelian_question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="769" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg/220px-Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg/330px-Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg/440px-Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sortavalan_maaseutua.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some people still live in houses that were built before 1939. A picture from <a href="/wiki/Sortavala" title="Sortavala">Sortavala</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The area is inhabited mostly by people who moved there from <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> and Russia, and their descendants. The fate of these people is a major issue in discussions of the return of Karelia to Finland. According to the poll by <i>Helsingin Sanomat</i>, 14% of people opposing return consider its greatest flaw the tensions that would be caused by a Russian-speaking minority being formed in Finland. In 2004, there were about 370,000 Russians living in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>If the inhabitants were allowed to stay in their homes, Finland would receive a few hundred thousand new Russian-speaking people with no experience of living in Finnish society. If services for them in their own language were to be provided, Finland would need many more officials capable of speaking Russian. In ProKarelia's vision, nearly half of the Russian population in Karelia would <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">expel</a> to Russia, and even more would leave if Finland paid their expenses for doing so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reform_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-reform-24">[24]</a></sup> However most of the Russian speaking population of Karelia were born there, have spent all their life in the region and consider it as their legal land.</p>
