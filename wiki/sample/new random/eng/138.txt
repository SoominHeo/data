<p><b>Whanganui</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/hw/ 'wh' in 'why'">hw</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <small>Māori: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_M%C4%81ori" title="Help:IPA for Māori">[ɸaŋanui]</a></span>), also spelt <b>Wanganui</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is a city on the west coast of the <a href="/wiki/North_Island" title="North Island">North Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_River" title="Whanganui River">Whanganui River</a>, New Zealand's longest navigable waterway, runs from Mount Tongariro to the sea. Whanganui is part of the <a href="/wiki/Manawatu-Wanganui" title="Manawatu-Wanganui">Manawatu-Wanganui</a> region.</p>
<p>Like several New Zealand centres, it was officially designated a <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_New_Zealand" title="List of cities in New Zealand">city</a> until administrative reorganisation in 1989, and is now run by a District Council.</p>
<p>Although called Wanganui from 1854, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic_Board" title="New Zealand Geographic Board">New Zealand Geographic Board</a> recommended that the name be changed to "Whanganui", and the government decided in December 2009 that, while either spelling was acceptable, <a href="/wiki/Public_sector_organisations_in_New_Zealand" title="Public sector organisations in New Zealand">Crown agencies</a> would use the Whanganui spelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FinalDecision_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FinalDecision-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whanganui is located on the <a href="/wiki/South_Taranaki_Bight" title="South Taranaki Bight">South Taranaki Bight</a>, close to the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_River" title="Whanganui River">Whanganui River</a>. It is 200 kilometres north of <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> and 75 kilometres northwest of <a href="/wiki/Palmerston_North" title="Palmerston North">Palmerston North</a>, at the junction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_State_Highway_network" title="New Zealand State Highway network">State Highways 3 and 4</a>. Most of the town lies on the river's northwestern bank, due to the greater extent of flat land.</p>
<p>Much of the town is on the river's northwest bank. The river is crossed by four bridges – Cobham Bridge, City Bridge, Dublin Street Bridge and Aramoho Railway Bridge (rail and pedestrians only).</p>
<p>Suburbs of the township include (clockwise from due south), Gonville, Castlecliff, Tawhero, Springvale, St. Johns Hill, <a href="/wiki/Otamatea" title="Otamatea">Otamatea</a>, Aramoho, Wanganui East, Bastia Hill, Durie Hill and Pūtiki. Of these, all except Wanganui East, Bastia Hill, Durie Hill and Pūtiki are on the northwest bank.</p>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ruapehu" title="Mount Ruapehu">Mount Ruapehu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Taranaki" title="Mount Taranaki">Mount Taranaki</a> can be seen from Durie Hill and other vantage points around the city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area around the mouth of the Whanganui river was a major site of pre-European <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> settlement. The <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81" title="Pā">pā</a> named Pūtiki (a contraction of Pūtikiwharanui) was and is home to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ng%C4%81ti_T%C5%ABpoho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ngāti Tūpoho (page does not exist)">Ngāti Tūpoho</a> <a href="/wiki/Hap%C5%AB" title="Hapū">hapū</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Iwi" title="Iwi">iwi</a> <a href="/wiki/Te_%C4%80ti_Haunui-a-P%C4%81p%C4%81rangi" title="Te Āti Haunui-a-Pāpārangi">Te Āti Haunui-a-Pāpārangi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MaoriMaps_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MaoriMaps-5">[5]</a></sup> It took its name from the legendary explorer Tamatea-pōkai-whenua, who sent a servant ashore to find flax for tying up his topknot (<i>pūtiki</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1820s coastal tribes in the area assaulted the <a href="/wiki/Kapiti_Island" title="Kapiti Island">Kapiti Island</a> stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Ng%C4%81ti_Toa" title="Ngāti Toa">Ngāti Toa</a> chief <a href="/wiki/Te_Rauparaha" title="Te Rauparaha">Te Rauparaha</a>. Te Rauparaha retaliated in 1830 sacking Pūtiki and slaughtering the inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WisesWanganui_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WisesWanganui-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first European traders arrived in 1831, followed in 1840 by missionaries <a href="/wiki/Octavius_Hadfield" title="Octavius Hadfield">Octavius Hadfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Williams_(missionary)" title="Henry Williams (missionary)">Henry Williams</a> who collected signatures for the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi" title="Treaty of Waitangi">Treaty of Waitangi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WisesWanganui_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WisesWanganui-7">[7]</a></sup> On 20 June 1840, the Revd John Mason, Mrs Mason, Mr Richard Matthews (a lay catechist) and his wife Johanna arrived to establish a mission station of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Missionary_Society" title="Church Missionary Society">Church Missionary Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pre1828m_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pre1828m-8">[8]</a></sup> Revd <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(missionary)" title="Richard Taylor (missionary)">Richard Taylor</a> joined the CMS mission station in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LMR1874_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LMR1874-9">[9]</a></sup> The Revd Mason drowned on 5 January 1843 while crossing the Turakina River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LMR1874_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-LMR1874-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By 1844 the brick church built by Mason was inadequate to meet the needs of the congregation and it had been damaged in an earthquake. A new church was built under the supervision of Taylor, with the timber supplied by each pā on the river in proportion to its size and number of Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-June1845_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-June1845-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Company" title="New Zealand Company">New Zealand Company</a> had settled <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> it looked for other suitable places for settlers. Edward Wakefield, son of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon_Wakefield" title="Edward Gibbon Wakefield">Edward Gibbon Wakefield</a>, negotiated the sale of 40,000 acres in 1840, and a town named Petre – after <a href="/wiki/William_Petre,_11th_Baron_Petre" title="William Petre, 11th Baron Petre">Lord Petre</a>, one of the directors of the New Zealand Company – was established four kilometres from the river mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WisesWanganui_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-WisesWanganui-7">[7]</a></sup> The settlement was threatened in 1846 by <a href="/wiki/Te_Mamaku" title="Te Mamaku">Te Mamaku</a>, a chief from up the Whanganui River. The British military arrived on 13 December 1846 to defend the township. Two stockades, the Rutland and York, were built to defend the settlers. Two minor battles were fought on 19 May and 19 July 1847 and after a stalemate the up river iwi returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dec1850_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dec1850-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1850 Te Mamaku was receiving Christian instruction from Revd Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dec1850_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dec1850-12">[12]</a></sup> There were further incidents in 1847 when four members of the Gilfillan family were murdered and their house plundered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jan1854_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jan1854-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name of the city was officially changed to Wanganui on 20 January 1854. The early years of the new city were problematic. Purchase of land from the local tribes had been haphazard and irregular, and as such many Māori were angered with the influx of <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81keh%C4%81" title="Pākehā">Pākehā</a> onto land that they still claimed. It was not until the town had been established for eight years that agreements were finally reached between the colonials and local tribes, and some resentment continued (and still filters through to the present day).</p>
<p>Wanganui grew rapidly after this time, with land being cleared for pasture. The town was a major military centre during the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Wars" title="New Zealand Wars">New Zealand Wars</a> of the 1860s, although local Māori at Pūtiki led by <a href="/wiki/Te_Keepa_Te_Rangihiwinui" title="Te Keepa Te Rangihiwinui">Te Keepa Te Rangihiwinui</a> remained friendly to settlers. In 1871 a town bridge was built, followed six years later by a railway bridge at Aramoho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WisesWanganui_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-WisesWanganui-7">[7]</a></sup> Wanganui was linked by rail to both <a href="/wiki/New_Plymouth" title="New Plymouth">New Plymouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> by 1886. The town was incorporated as a <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">Borough</a> on 1 February 1872, and declared a city on 1 July 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WisesWanganui_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-WisesWanganui-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Perhaps Wanganui's biggest scandal happened in 1920, when Mayor <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mackay_(mayor)" title="Charles Mackay (mayor)">Charles Mackay</a> shot and wounded a young poet, <a href="/wiki/Walter_D%27Arcy_Cresswell" title="Walter D'Arcy Cresswell">Walter D'Arcy Cresswell</a>, who had been blackmailing him over his homosexuality. Mackay served seven years in prison and his name was erased from the town's civic monuments, while Cresswell (himself homosexual) was praised as a "wholesome-minded young man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Mackay's name was restored to the foundation stone of the <a href="/wiki/Sarjeant_Gallery" title="Sarjeant Gallery">Sarjeant Gallery</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_River" title="Whanganui River">Whanganui River</a> catchment is seen as a sacred area to Māori, and the Whanganui region is still seen as a focal point for any resentment over land ownership. In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Moutoa_Gardens" title="Moutoa Gardens">Moutoa Gardens</a> in Wanganui, known to local Māori as <i>Pakaitore</i>, were occupied for 79 days in a mainly peaceful protest by the Whanganui iwi over land claims.</p>
<p>Wanganui was the site of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Police" title="New Zealand Police">New Zealand Police</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Law_Enforcement_System" title="National Law Enforcement System">Law Enforcement System</a> (LES) from 1976 to 1995. An early <a href="/wiki/Sperry_Corporation" title="Sperry Corporation">Sperry</a> <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computer</a>-based intelligence and data management system, it was known colloquially as the <i>"Wanganui Computer"</i>. The data centre housing it was subject to New Zealand's highest profile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_bombing" title="Suicide bombing">suicide bombing</a> in 1982 when anarchist <a href="/wiki/Neil_Roberts_(anarchist)" title="Neil Roberts (anarchist)">Neil Roberts</a> detonated a <a href="/wiki/Gelignite" title="Gelignite">gelignite</a> bomb in the entry foyer. Roberts was the only casualty of the bombing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Whāngā nui</i> means <i>big bay</i> or <i>big harbour</i>. Europeans called it <i>Petre</i> (pronounced Peter), after Lord Petre, an officer of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Company" title="New Zealand Company">New Zealand Company</a>, but the name did not persist.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_of_Wanganui.2FWhanganui">Spelling of Wanganui/Whanganui</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spelling of Wanganui/Whanganui">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the local dialect, <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> pronounce the phoneme <i>wh</i> as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˀw]</span>, a <i>w</i> combined with a glottal stop,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Whanganui#Dubious" title="Talk:Whanganui">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> and the name as something like "'Wanganui", hard to reproduce by non-locals. Until recently it was generally written as "Wanganui" and pronounced with a <i>w</i> by non-speakers of <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> and a <i>wh</i> (often <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[f]</span>) by those Māori speakers from other areas who knew its derivation.</p>
<p>Following an article about the river by David Young in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic" title="New Zealand Geographic">New Zealand Geographic</a> magazine that used "Whanganui" throughout, in accord with the wishes of the local <a href="/wiki/Iwi" title="Iwi">iwi</a>, the spelling of the river's name reverted to Whanganui in 1991. The region's name is now also spelt "Whanganui", but the city has a mixture of spellings.</p>
<p>A non-binding <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> was held in Wanganui in 2006, where 82 percent voted for Wanganui without an 'h'. Turnout was 55.4 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stuff.co.nz_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stuff.co.nz-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2009, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic_Board" title="New Zealand Geographic Board">New Zealand Geographic Board</a> received a proposal that the town's name should be spelt "Whanganui", and in late March found there was a good case for the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The public was given three months to comment on the proposed change, beginning in mid May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> About equal numbers of submissions supported and opposed the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whanganui_decision_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whanganui_decision-19">[19]</a></sup> Wanganui Mayor Michael Laws spoke strongly against the proposed change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stuff.co.nz_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-stuff.co.nz-16">[16]</a></sup> Another referendum was held in Wanganui in May 2009 and residents again rejected changing the town's name 77-22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Turnout was 60%. The Geographic Board decided in September 2009 that the name should be spelt "Whanganui", but the decision waited for review by the Minister for Land Information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whanganui_decision_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whanganui_decision-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2009 the government decided that while either spelling was acceptable, <a href="/wiki/Public_sector_organisations_in_New_Zealand" title="Public sector organisations in New Zealand">Crown agencies</a> would use the Whanganui spelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FinalDecision_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FinalDecision-4">[4]</a></sup> Despite this, some government agencies still use the Wanganui spelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 November 2015 <a href="/wiki/Land_Information_New_Zealand" title="Land Information New Zealand">Land Information New Zealand <i>Toitū te whenua</i></a> (LINZ) announced that Wanganui District would be renamed to Whanganui District.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This changed the official name of the District Council, and, because Whanganui is not a city but a district, the official name of the urban area as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, 2014, 2015 and 2016 Whanganui was included in the world's Smart21 Intelligent Communities by the Intelligent Community Forum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whanganui has a strong industry base, with a history of niche manufacturing. Current businesses include Q-West Boat Builders, based at the Port who have built boats for customers from around New Zealand and the world and were awarded a contract in 2015 to build two 34-meter passenger ferries for Auckland ferry company Fullers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Pacific Helmets is another example of award-winning niche manufacturing in the district, winning a Silver Pin at the Best Design Awards in October 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of Whanganui's economy relates directly to the fertile and prosperous farming hinterland near the town. Heads Road is Whanganui's main industrial area and is home to a number of manufacturing and engineering operations. The Wanganui Port, once the centre of industrial transport, still has some traffic but is more noted for the Q-West boat building operation there. <a href="/wiki/F._Whitlock_%26_Sons_Ltd" title="F. Whitlock &amp; Sons Ltd">F. Whitlock &amp; Sons Ltd</a> was a notable company first established in 1902.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Whanganui_Airport" title="Whanganui Airport">Whanganui Airport</a> is served by <a href="/wiki/Air_Chathams" title="Air Chathams">Air Chathams</a> to <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Airport" title="Auckland Airport">Auckland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It enjoys a temperate climate, with slightly above the national average sunshine (2100 hours per annum), and about 900 mm of annual rainfall. Several light frosts are normally experienced in winter. The river is prone to flooding after heavy rain in the catchment, and in June 2015 record flooding occurred with 100 households evacuated.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Whanganui_District">Whanganui District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Whanganui District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_District" title="Whanganui District">Whanganui District</a></div>
<p>The Whanganui District covers 2337 km<sup>2</sup>, the majority of which is hill country, with a narrow coastal strip of flat land and a major urban settlement on the lower banks of the Whanganui River. A large proportion of this is within the <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_National_Park" title="Whanganui National Park">Whanganui National Park</a>.</p>
<p>The region is known for its outstanding natural environment with the Whanganui Awa (River) at its heart. It is the second largest river in the North Island, the longest navigable waterway in the country, and covers 290 kilometres from the heights of Mount Tongariro to Wanganui's coast and the Tasman Sea. Every bend and rapid of the river (there are 239 listed rapids) has a guardian, or kaitiaki, who maintains the mauri (life force) of that stretch of the river.</p>
<p>Whanganui hapū (sub-tribes) were renowned for their canoeing skills and maintained extensive networks of weirs and fishing traps along the River. Generations of river iwi have learned to use and protect this great taonga (treasure) and on 13 September 2012 the Whanganui River became the first river in the world to gain recognition as a legal identity.</p>
<p>Today the River and its surrounds are used for a number of recreational activities including kayaking, jet boating, tramping, cycling and camping. A national cycleway has recently opened, which takes cyclists from the 'mountains to the sea'. The <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_National_Park" title="Whanganui National Park">Whanganui National Park</a> provides protection for native flora and fauna and was established in 1986.</p>
<p>In the local government reorganisation of the 1980s, Wanganui District Council resulted from the amalgamation in 1989 of Wanganui County Council, most of Waitotara County Council, a small part of Stratford County Council, and Wanganui City Council. Hamish McDouall was elected mayor in the 2016 local government elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>All but some 4,200 people in the Whanganui District live in the township itself, meaning there are few prominent outlying settlements. A small but notable village is <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem,_New_Zealand" title="Jerusalem, New Zealand">Jerusalem</a>, which was home to Mother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Joseph_Aubert" title="Mary Joseph Aubert">Mary Joseph Aubert</a> and the poet <a href="/wiki/James_K._Baxter" title="James K. Baxter">James K. Baxter</a>.</p>
<p>The Whanganui District is also home to other settlements with small populations, including Kaitoke, <a href="/wiki/Upokongaro" title="Upokongaro">Upokongaro</a>, Kai Iwi/Mowhanau, Aberfeldy, Westmere, <a href="/wiki/Maxwell,_New_Zealand" title="Maxwell, New Zealand">Maxwell</a>, Marybank, <a href="/wiki/Okoia" title="Okoia">Okoia</a> and Fordell.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wanganui_Collegiate_School" title="Wanganui Collegiate School">Wanganui Collegiate School</a> is located in Liverpool Street, central Whanganui. It was founded by a land grant in 1852 by the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_New_Zealand" title="Governor-General of New Zealand">Governor of New Zealand</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Edward_Grey" title="George Edward Grey">Sir George Grey</a>, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_New_Zealand" title="Bishop of New Zealand">Bishop of New Zealand</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Augustus_Selwyn" title="George Augustus Selwyn">George Augustus Selwyn</a>, for the purpose of establishing a school. It was originally a boys-only school but in 1991 began admitting girls at senior levels and went fully co-educational in 1999. The school celebrated its 150th anniversary in 2004. The school amalgamated with St George's School in 2010. The combined schools provide primary education for day students on the St George campus, and secondary education for day and boarding students on the Collegiate campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex">Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex</a>, spent two terms spanning 1982 and 1983 at the school as a junior master during his <a href="/wiki/Gap_year" title="Gap year">gap year</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Whanganui_City_College" title="Whanganui City College">Whanganui City College</a> is located in Ingestre Street, central Whanganui, and has had three names. Wanganui Technical College, established in 1911, became Wanganui Boys' College in 1964 and in 1994 it became Wanganui City College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Whanganui_High_School" title="Whanganui High School">Whanganui High School</a> is located in Purnell Street.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Whanganui_Girls%27_College" title="Whanganui Girls' College">Whanganui Girls' College</a> is located in Jones Street, Whanganui East, near the Dublin Street Bridge.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cullinane_College" title="Cullinane College">Cullinane College</a> is an integrated, co-educational college located in Peat Street, Aramoho.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Dominic%27s_College,_Wanganui" title="St. Dominic's College, Wanganui">St. Dominic's</a> College is in York Street, Gonville.</li>
<li>Te Kura Kaupapa Māori o Te Atihaunui-A-Paparangi is located in Anaua Street, Putiki.</li>
<li>Te Kura o Kokohuia is located at Matipo Street, Castlecliff.</li>
<li>UCOL, Universal College of Learning, was founded in 1907 and was known as the <a href="/wiki/Palmerston_North" title="Palmerston North">Palmerston North</a> Technical School. In 1971 it became the Palmerston North Technical Institute and in 1983 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manawatu" title="Manawatu">Manawatu</a> Polytechnic. At the time it specialised in trade [apprenticeship] courses, and in hobby, art, and craft classes, along with a range of night school programmes in <a href="/wiki/Business_studies" title="Business studies">business studies</a> for working adults. UCOL expanded in January 2001 with the incorporation of the <a href="/wiki/Wairarapa" title="Wairarapa">Wairarapa</a> Regional Polytechnic and the integration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wanganui" title="Wanganui">Wanganui</a> Regional Community Polytechnic on 1 April 2002.</li>
<li>The Wanganui Regional Community Polytechnic is now called Whanganui UCOL and incorporates the <a href="/wiki/Wanganui_School_of_Design" title="Wanganui School of Design">Wanganui School of Design</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_institutions">Cultural institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wanganui has a strong cultural and recreational focus. Queen's Park (Pukenamu) in the central township has several cultural institutions including the <a href="/wiki/Sarjeant_Gallery" title="Sarjeant Gallery">Sarjeant Gallery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_Regional_Museum" title="Whanganui Regional Museum">Whanganui Regional Museum</a>, the Davis Library, the Alexander Heritage and Research Library, and the Wanganui War Memorial Centre. Wanganui is home to New Zealand's only glass school and is renowned for its glass art.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sarjeant_Gallery_collection">Sarjeant Gallery collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sarjeant Gallery collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are more than 5,500 artworks in the <a href="/wiki/Sarjeant_Gallery" title="Sarjeant Gallery">Sarjeant Gallery</a>, initially focused on 19th and early 20th century British and European art but, given the expansive terms of the will of benefactor <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sarjeant" title="Henry Sarjeant">Henry Sarjeant</a>, the collection now spans 16th century through to 21st century.</p>
<p>Among the collections are historic and modern works in all media – on paper, sculptures, pottery, ceramics and glass; bronze works; video art; and paintings by contemporary artists and old masters.</p>
<p>Notable are works by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burne-Jones" title="Edward Burne-Jones">Edward Coley Burne-Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Piola" title="Domenico Piola">Domenico Piola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Brangwyn" title="Frank Brangwyn">Frank Brangwyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Poccetti" title="Bernardino Poccetti">Bernardino Poccetti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaspard_Dughet" title="Gaspard Dughet">Gaspard Dughet</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Blake_Richmond" title="William Blake Richmond">William Richmond</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Etty" title="William Etty">William Etty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lelio_Orsi" title="Lelio Orsi">Lelio Orsi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Goodall" title="Frederick Goodall">Frederick Goodall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augustus_John" title="Augustus John">Augustus John</a> and others.</p>
<p>Among its collection are six works by Wanganui artist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbert_Ivan_Babbage" title="Herbert Ivan Babbage">Herbert Ivan Babbage</a>.</p>
<p>Celebrated hometown collection of <a href="/wiki/Edith_Collier" title="Edith Collier">Edith Collier</a>'s work.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_artists">Glass artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Glass artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks_and_buildings">Landmarks and buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Landmarks and buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2765" data-file-width="1806" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg/150px-War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg/225px-War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg/300px-War_Memorial_Tower_Wanganui.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>

<p>Pukenamu–Queens Park in central Whanganui, formerly the hilltop location of the Rutland Stockade, is home to several iconic buildings. The <a href="/wiki/Sarjeant_Gallery" title="Sarjeant Gallery">Sarjeant Gallery</a>, a Category I Historic Place,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> was a bequest to the town by local farmer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sarjeant" title="Henry Sarjeant">Henry Sarjeant</a>, and opened in 1919. Since 2014 it has been in temporary premises on Taupo Quay while the heritage building is strengthened and redeveloped. The <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_Regional_Museum" title="Whanganui Regional Museum">Whanganui Regional Museum</a> (1928) and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Heritage_and_Research_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander Heritage and Research Library (page does not exist)">Alexander Heritage and Research Library</a> (1933) were both bequests of the Alexander family. The award-winning <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui_War_Memorial_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Whanganui War Memorial Hall (page does not exist)">Whanganui War Memorial Hall</a> (1960) is one of New Zealand's finest examples of modernist architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Wanganui_Opera_House" title="Royal Wanganui Opera House">Royal Wanganui Opera House</a> is located in St Hill Street in central Whanganui. Stewart House on the corner of Campbell and Plymouth Streets is now a private home, but it was formerly the <a href="/wiki/Plunket_Society" title="Plunket Society">Karitane</a> Home and later a boarding residence for secondary school students. It was built for philanthropist <a href="/wiki/John_Tiffin_Stewart" title="John Tiffin Stewart">John Tiffin Stewart</a> and social activist <a href="/wiki/Frances_Ann_Stewart" title="Frances Ann Stewart">Frances Ann Stewart</a>.</p>
<p>There are two large towers overlooking Whanganui: the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Durie_Hill_War_Memorial_Tower&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Durie Hill War Memorial Tower (page does not exist)">Durie Hill War Memorial Tower</a> and the Bastia Hill Water Tower. The Durie Hill Tower is a World War I memorial, unveiled in 1926. Nearby is the <a href="/wiki/Durie_Hill_Elevator" title="Durie Hill Elevator">Durie Hill Elevator</a> (1919), which links the hilltop with Anzac Parade via a 66 m elevator and a 200 m underground tunnel.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whanganui has three local newspapers. The <i><a href="/wiki/Wanganui_Chronicle" title="Wanganui Chronicle">Wanganui Chronicle</a></i>, founded in 1856, is New Zealand's oldest newspaper, and has been a daily paper since 1871. Its rival from the 1860s onward was the <i>Evening Herald</i> (later the <i>Wanganui Herald</i>), founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Ballance" title="John Ballance">John Ballance</a>. The two daily papers joined in the 1970s, and in 1986 the <i>Herald</i> became a free weekly, later renamed the <i>Wanganui Midweek</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The <i>River City Press</i> is the other free weekly paper.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby">Rugby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Rugby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wanganui_Rugby_Football_Union" title="Wanganui Rugby Football Union">Wanganui</a> is one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a> unions in New Zealand, but has never held the <a href="/wiki/Ranfurly_Shield" title="Ranfurly Shield">Ranfurly Shield</a>. The Whanganui environs have produced many <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_union_team" title="New Zealand national rugby union team">All Blacks</a> including <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Donald" title="Andrew Donald">Andrew Donald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Osborne" title="Bill Osborne">Bill Osborne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buff_Milner" title="Buff Milner">Buff Milner</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Bullock-Douglas" title="George Bullock-Douglas">George Bullock-Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glen_Osborne" title="Glen Osborne">Glen Osborne</a>, Harrison Rowley, John Blair, John Hogan, Moke Belliss, Mona Thomson, Pat Potaka, Peina Taituha, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Henderson_(sportsman)" title="Peter Henderson (sportsman)">Peter Henderson</a>, Peter Johns, Peter McDonnell, Peter Murray, Sandy McNicol and Keith Gudsell who also played three tests for the <a href="/wiki/Australia_national_rugby_union_team" title="Australia national rugby union team">Wallabies</a>. Although from the Manawatu, 1987 Rugby World Cup winning All Black Captain <a href="/wiki/David_Kirk" title="David Kirk">David Kirk</a> was a student at <a href="/wiki/Wanganui_Collegiate_School" title="Wanganui Collegiate School">Wanganui Collegiate School</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008 the Wanganui representative rugby team under the captaincy of David Gower, won the NZRFU's Heartland Championship (Meads Cup) by defeating Mid Canterbury 27-12 in the final. They had previously been the defeated finalist in 2006 and 2007. The 2008 side had an undefeated season – the first since 1947. The rugby squad, including coach and management, was accorded the honour of 'Freedom of the City' by the Whanganui District Council – the first time the award had been given to any sporting team.</p>
<p>The 2009 representative team repeated this feat by regaining the Meads Cup – again defeating Mid Canterbury in the final by 34 points to 13 (after trailing nil-13 at halftime). Unlike 2008, the 2009 did lose games (to Wellington, Wairarapa Bush and Mid Canterbury) but came good at the business end of the season. Ten Whanganui players were selected for the Heartland XV.</p>
<p>The Wanganui rugby jersey, due to its resemblance, is known as the butcher's apron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whanganui has several high quality sporting venues including <a href="/wiki/Cooks_Gardens" title="Cooks Gardens">Cooks Gardens</a> which is a major sporting venue used for cricket, athletics and rugby. On 27 January 1962, a world record time of 3 minutes 54.4 seconds for running the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_distance_track_event#Mile" title="Middle distance track event">mile</a> was set by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Snell" title="Peter Snell">Peter Snell</a> on the grass track at the gardens. The venue also has a world-class velodrome.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor-racing">Motor-racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Motor-racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_Circuit" title="Cemetery Circuit">Cemetery Circuit</a> is a temporary motorcycle street racetrack in downtown Whanganui which passes through the old cemetery and industrial area near to the centre of town. The event is usually held on Boxing Day each year.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rod_Coleman_(motorcycle_racer)" title="Rod Coleman (motorcycle racer)">Rod Coleman (motorcycle racer)</a> was a Grand Prix motorcycle road racer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earl_Bamber" title="Earl Bamber">Earl Bamber</a>, Racing driver and winner of the <a href="/wiki/2015_24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="2015 24 Hours of Le Mans">2015 24 Hours of Le Mans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Porsche_Supercup_season" title="2014 Porsche Supercup season">2014 Porsche Supercup</a></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sister_cities">Sister cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whanganui&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Sister cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whanganui has two <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" title="Twin towns and sister cities">sister cities</a>, as designated by <a href="/wiki/Sister_Cities_International" title="Sister Cities International">Sister Cities International</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="flagicon"><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia"><img alt="Australia" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="1280" height="12" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b9/Flag_of_Australia.svg/23px-Flag_of_Australia.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b9/Flag_of_Australia.svg/35px-Flag_of_Australia.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b9/Flag_of_Australia.svg/46px-Flag_of_Australia.svg.png 2x" width="23"/></a></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toowoomba,_Queensland" title="Toowoomba, Queensland">Toowoomba</a>, Queensland, Australia since 1983</li>
<li><span class="flagicon"><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan"><img alt="Japan" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/23px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/35px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/45px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png 2x" width="23"/></a></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagaizumi,_Shizuoka" title="Nagaizumi, Shizuoka">Nagaizumi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka_Prefecture" title="Shizuoka Prefecture">Shizuoka</a>, Japan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-International-47">[47]</a></sup> since 1988</li>
</ul>
<p>The Wanganui District Council decided in 2008 to formally end its sister city relationship with Reno, Nevada, USA after years of inactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The relationship was parodied on "The Prefect of Wanganui" episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Reno_911!" title="Reno 911!">Reno 911!</a></i>.</p>
<p>Instead, it has looked to partner a Samoan village in the wake of the 2009 tsunami tragedy.</p>
