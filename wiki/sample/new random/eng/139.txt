<p>The <b>Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle Mark III</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">भूस्थिर उपग्रह प्रक्षेपण यान एमके-३</span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAST" title="IAST">IAST</a>: <i>Bhūsthir Upagrah Prakșepaņ Yān MK-3</i>, also referred to as the <b>Launch Vehicle Mark 3</b>, <b>LVM3</b> or <b>GSLV-III</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hindu_LVM3_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hindu_LVM3-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> developed by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">Indian Space Research Organisation</a> (ISRO).</p>
<p>It is intended to launch satellites into <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> and as a launcher for an Indian <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">crew vehicle</a>. The GSLV-III features an Indian <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_rocket_engine" title="Cryogenic rocket engine">cryogenic</a> third stage and a higher payload capacity than the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GSLV" title="GSLV">GSLV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frontline_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-frontline-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Development for the GSLV-III began in the early 2000s, with the first launch planned for 2009-2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sawf20130405_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sawf20130405-10">[10]</a></sup> Several factors have delayed the program, including the <a href="/wiki/GSAT-4" title="GSAT-4">15 April 2010 failure</a> of the ISRO-developed cryogenic upper stage on the GSLV Mk II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sawf20130405_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-sawf20130405-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A suborbital flight test of the GSLV-III launcher, with a passive cryogenic third stage, was successfully carried out on 18 December 2014, and was used to test a crew module on a suborbital trajectory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_of_India10012014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times_of_India10012014-11">[11]</a></sup> The first orbital flight is planned to take place in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The first flight with a crew on board would take place after 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sawf20130405_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-sawf20130405-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="S200_static_test">S200 static test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: S200 static test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The S-200 <a href="/wiki/Solid_rocket_booster" title="Solid rocket booster">solid rocket booster</a> was successfully tested on 24 January 2010. The booster fired for 130 seconds and generated a peak thrust of about 500 tonnes. Nearly 600 ballistic and safety parameters were monitored during the test and indicated normal performance. A second successful static test was conducted on 4 September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnai3sb_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnai3sb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L110_stage_test">L110 stage test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: L110 stage test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">Indian Space Research Organisation</a> conducted the first static test of the L110 core stage at its Liquid Propulsion Systems Centre (LPSC) test facility at <a href="/wiki/Mahendragiri" title="Mahendragiri">Mahendragiri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> on 5 March 2010. Originally targeted for a full 200 second burn, the test was terminated at 150 seconds after a leakage in a control system was detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On 8 September 2010 ISRO successfully conducted a full 200 second test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isro_press_release_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-isro_press_release-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suborbital_flight_test">Suborbital flight test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Suborbital flight test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GSLV LVM-3 lifted off from the second launch pad, <a href="/wiki/Sriharikota" title="Sriharikota">Sriharikota</a>, at 9.30 am IST on 18 December 2014. The 630.5 tonne launch vehicle stacking was as follows : a functional S200 solid propulsion stage, a functional L110 liquid propulsion stage, a non-functional dummy stage (in lieu of <a href="/wiki/CE-20" title="CE-20">CE-20</a> cryogenic propulsion engine) and finally the 3.7-tonne <a href="/wiki/Crew_Module_Atmospheric_Re-entry_Experiment" title="Crew Module Atmospheric Re-entry Experiment">Crew Module Atmospheric Re-entry Experiment</a> (CARE) payload stage. Just over five minutes into the flight, the rocket ejected CARE at an altitude of 126 km. CARE then descended at high speed, controlled by its onboard motors. At an altitude of 80 km, the thrusters were shut down and the capsule began its ballistic re-entry into the atmosphere. CARE’s heat shield was expected to experience a temperature of around 1600 °C. ISRO downloaded launch telemetry during the ballistic coasting phase prior to the radio black-out to avoid data loss in the event of a splash-down failure. At an altitude of around 15 km, the module’s apex cover separated and the parachutes were deployed. CARE splashed down in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_description">Vehicle description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vehicle description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_1_.E2.80.93_solid_boosters">Stage 1 – solid boosters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stage 1 – solid boosters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GSLV-III uses <b>S200</b> solid motors. Each booster has a diameter of 3.2 metres, a length of 25 metres, and carries 207 tonnes of propellant. These boosters burn for 130 seconds and produce a peak thrust of about 5,150 kilonewtons (525 tf) each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isros200_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-isros200-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A separate facility has been established at Sriharikota to make the S200 boosters. Another major feature is that the S200’s large nozzle has been equipped with a ‘flex seal.’ The nozzle can therefore be gimballed when the rocket’s orientation needs correction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehindu.com6660089_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu.com6660089-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In flight, as the thrust from the S200 boosters begins to tail off, the decline in acceleration is sensed by the rocket’s onboard sensors and the twin Vikas engines on the ‘L110’ liquid propellant core stage are then ignited. Before the S200s separate and fall away from the rocket, the solid boosters as well as the Vikas engines operate together for a short period of time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehindu.com6660089_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu.com6660089-18">[18]</a></sup> similar of that American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_III" title="Titan III">Titan III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a> booster.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_2_.E2.80.93_liquid_motor">Stage 2 – liquid motor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stage 2 – liquid motor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The core stage, designated <b>L110</b>, is a 4-meter-diameter liquid-fueled stage carrying 110 tonnes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N2O4" title="N2O4">N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a>. It is the first Indian <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid</a>-engine cluster design, and uses two improved <a href="/wiki/Vikas_(rocket_engine)" title="Vikas (rocket engine)">Vikas engines</a>, each producing about 700 kilonewtons (70 tf).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLRG3_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-SLRG3-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l110t_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-l110t-7">[7]</a></sup> The improved Vikas engine uses <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_cooling_(rocket)" title="Regenerative cooling (rocket)">regenerative cooling</a>, providing improved weight and <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a>, compared to earlier rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space_Transportation_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space_Transportation-19">[19]</a></sup> The L110 core stage ignites 113 seconds after liftoff and burns for about 200 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l110t_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-l110t-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_3_.E2.80.93_cryogenic_upper_stage">Stage 3 – cryogenic upper stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stage 3 – cryogenic upper stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cryogenic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_stage" title="Upper stage">upper stage</a> is designated the <b>C25</b> and will be powered by the Indian-developed <a href="/wiki/CE-20" title="CE-20">CE-20</a> engine burning <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">LOX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">LH2</a>, producing 186 kilonewtons (19.0 tf) of thrust. The C-25 will be 4 metres (13 ft) in diameter and 13.5 metres (44 ft) long, and contain 27 tonnes of propellant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space_Transportation_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Space_Transportation-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>This engine was initially slated for completion and testing by 2015, it would have been the C25 stage and be put through a series of tests. ISRO crossed a major milestone in the development of CE-20 engine for the GSLV MKIII vehicle by the successful hot test for 640 seconds duration on 19.02.2016 at ISRO Propulsion Complex, Mahendragiri. The test demonstrated the repeatability of the engine performance with all its sub systems like thrust chamber, gas generator, turbo pumps and control components for the full duration. All the engine parameters were closely matching with the pre-test prediction.</p>
<p>The first C25 stage will be used on the GSLV-III D-1 mission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheHinduExStage2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheHinduExStage2-20">[20]</a></sup> in December 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup> This mission will put in orbit the GSAT-19E communication satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiantelevision.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-indiantelevision.com-22">[22]</a></sup> Work on the C25 stage and CE-20 engine for GSLV Mk-III upper stage was initiated in 2003, the project has been subject to many delays due to problems with ISRO's smaller cryogenic engine, the CE-7.5 for GSLV MK-II upper stage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Payload_fairing">Payload fairing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle_Mark_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Payload fairing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
