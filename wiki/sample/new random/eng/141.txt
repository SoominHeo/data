<p>The <b>Pont Saint-Bénézet</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[pɔ̃ sɛ̃ benezɛ]</a></span>), also known as the <b>Pont d'Avignon</b> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[pɔ̃ daviɲɔ̃]</a></span>), is a famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> bridge in the town of <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>, in southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>A bridge spanning the <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Rhône">Rhône</a> between <a href="/wiki/Villeneuve-l%C3%A8s-Avignon" title="Villeneuve-lès-Avignon">Villeneuve-lès-Avignon</a> and Avignon was built between 1177 and 1185. This early bridge was destroyed forty years later during the <a href="/wiki/Albigensian_Crusade" title="Albigensian Crusade">Albigensian Crusade</a> when <a href="/wiki/Louis_VIII_of_France" title="Louis VIII of France">Louis VIII of France</a> laid siege to Avignon. The bridge was rebuilt with 22 stone arches. It was very costly to maintain as the arches tended to collapse when the Rhône flooded. Eventually in the middle of the 17th century the bridge was abandoned. The four surviving arches on the bank of the Rhône are believed to have been built in around 1345 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VI" title="Pope Clement VI">Pope Clement VI</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Avignon_Papacy" title="Avignon Papacy">Avignon Papacy</a>. The Chapel of Saint Nicholas sits on the second pier of the bridge. It was constructed in the second half of 12th century but has since been substantially altered. The western terminal, the <a href="/wiki/Tour_Philippe-le-Bel" title="Tour Philippe-le-Bel">Tour Philippe-le-Bel</a>, is also preserved.</p>
<p>The bridge was the inspiration for the song <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sur_le_pont_d%27Avignon" title="Sur le pont d'Avignon">Sur le pont d'Avignon</a></i> and is considered a landmark of the city. In 1995, the surviving arches of the bridge, together with the <a href="/wiki/Palais_des_Papes" title="Palais des Papes">Palais des Papes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avignon_Cathedral" title="Avignon Cathedral">Cathédrale Notre-Dame des Doms</a> were classified as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bridge spanned the Rhône between Avignon and Villeneuve-lès-Avignon. It was built between 1177 and 1185, with an original length of some 900 m (980 yd). The bridge was destroyed during the siege of Avignon by Louis VIII of France in 1226 but beginning in 1234 it was rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabande191046-47_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabande191046-47-1">[1]</a></sup> Historians have suggested that the earlier bridge may have consisted of a wooden superstructure supported on stone piers and that only when rebuilt was the bridge constructed entirely in stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974223-224_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974223-224-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953145_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953145-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[a]</a></sup> The bridge was only 4.9 m (16 ft) in width, including the parapets at the sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> The arches were liable to collapse when the river flooded and were sometimes replaced with temporary wooden structures before being rebuilt in stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabande191046-47_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabande191046-47-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[b]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bridge fell into a state of disrepair during the 17th century. By 1644 the bridge was missing four arches and finally a catastrophic flood in 1669 swept away much of the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoulon1644.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DUmJAAAAAcAAJ.26hl.3Den.26pg.3DPA168_168.5D_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoulon1644.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DUmJAAAAAcAAJ.26hl.3Den.26pg.3DPA168_168.5D-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChampion1862.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DAl8JAAAAIAAJ.26pg.3DPA24_24.5D_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChampion1862.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DAl8JAAAAIAAJ.26pg.3DPA24_24.5D-10">[8]</a></sup> Since then, its surviving arches have successively collapsed or been demolished, and only four of the initial 22 arches remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVella2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVella2013-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The arches are <a href="/wiki/Circular_segment" title="Circular segment">segmental</a> rather than the semi-circular shape typically used in Roman bridges. Of the remaining arches the largest span is 35.8 m (117 ft) between the third and fourth piles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974225.2C_233_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974225.2C_233-12">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953135_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953135-13">[11]</a></sup> The piers have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starling_(architecture)" title="Starling (architecture)">cutwaters</a> that are pointed in both the upstream and the downstream direction. These reduce the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_scour" title="Bridge scour">scour</a> around the piers, one of the main threats to the stability of stone bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_B.C3.A9n.C3.A9zet_legend">Saint Bénézet legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Saint Bénézet legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bridge's construction was inspired by Saint <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet" title="Bénézet">Bénézet</a>, a shepherd boy from the hamlet of Villard in the <a href="/wiki/Ard%C3%A8che" title="Ardèche">Ardèche</a> who (according to tradition) while tending his flock heard the voice of Jesus Christ asking him to build a bridge across the river. Although he was ridiculed at first, he dramatically "proved" his divine inspiration by miraculously lifting a huge block of stone. He won support for his project and formed a Bridge Brotherhood to oversee its construction. After his death, he was interred on the bridge itself, in a small chapel standing on one of the bridge's surviving piers on the Avignon side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974219-220_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974219-220-15">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBouette_de_Bl.C3.A9mur1689.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3D2Ric3gnh3YcC.26pg.3DPA109_111-113.5D_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBouette_de_Bl.C3.A9mur1689.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3D2Ric3gnh3YcC.26pg.3DPA109_111-113.5D-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Nicholas_Chapel">Saint Nicholas Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Saint Nicholas Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Saint Nicholas Chapel sits on a platform on the upstream side of the second pier (between the second and third arches). The <a href="/wiki/Bridge_chapel" title="Bridge chapel">bridge chapel</a> has undergone a number of phases of reconstruction and restoration. It is now divided into two floors, each with a <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a>. The upper floor is on a level with the platform of the bridge and reduces the width of the walkway to 1.75 m (5.7 ft). The lower floor is accessed by a set of steps that descend from the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exterior of the chapel shows evidence of the rebuilding work with blocked windows on the south-eastern wall. The nave is covered with stone roof tiles which rest on a series of <a href="/wiki/Corbel" title="Corbel">corbels</a>. The polygonal apse has a flat roof and sits above the cutwater of the pier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lower chapel with its apse decorated with five arches dates from the second half of the 12th century. At a later date, perhaps as early as the 13th century when the level of the bridge was raised, a floor supported by a <a href="/wiki/Rib_vault" title="Rib vault">ribbed quadripartite vault</a> was inserted into the structure. The simple rectangular upper chapel with the barrel vaulted roof was consecrated in 1411. A side door was created in the lower chapel as the stonework of the raised bridge blocked the original entrance. In 1513 a pentagonal apse with gothic columns was added to the upper chapel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974229-232.2C_470-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1670, after the bridge was abandoned, the relics of Saint Bénézet were transferred to the Hôpital du Pont (also called the Hôpital St Bénézet) within the city walls next to the gatehouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouquette1974232_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouquette1974232-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bridge was also the site of devotion by the Rhône boatmen, whose patron saint was <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas" title="Saint Nicholas">Saint Nicholas</a>. They initially worshipped in the Saint Nicholas Chapel on the bridge itself (where Saint Bénézet's body was also interred) but the increasing dilapidation of the bridge made access difficult. In 1715 the <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternity</a> of boatmen built a chapel on dry land on the Avignon side of the bridge outside the ramparts next to the gatehouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPansier193026_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPansier193026-19">[17]</a></sup> This chapel was destroyed by the major flood of the Rhône that occurred in 1856. A residence for a caretaker was built on the ruins during the restoration work undertaken beginning around 1878.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953130_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953130-20">[18]</a></sup> The residence was demolished as part of the restoration work on the bridge and gatehouse carried out in the 1980s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gatehouses">Gatehouses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gatehouses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bridge had great strategic importance as when first built it was the only fixed river crossing between <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. It was also the only river crossing between the <a href="/wiki/Comtat_Venaissin" title="Comtat Venaissin">Comtat Venaissin</a>, an enclave controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>, and France proper under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">kings of France</a>. As such, it was closely guarded on both sides of the river. The right bank, which was controlled by the French crown, was overlooked by the fortress of the Tour Philippe-le-Bel which was built at the beginning of the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaigret2002_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaigret2002-21">[19]</a></sup> On the Avignon side, the bridge passed through a large <a href="/wiki/Gatehouse" title="Gatehouse">gatehouse</a> erected in the 14th century (with major modifications in the 15th century), passing through and over the city wall and exiting via a ramp (now destroyed) which led into the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPansier1930_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPansier1930-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1265 and 1309 another stone bridge was constructed across the Rhône, 40 km (25 mi) upstream from Avignon, at what is now <a href="/wiki/Pont-Saint-Esprit" title="Pont-Saint-Esprit">Pont-Saint-Esprit</a> but then known as Saint-Saturnin-du-Port. The Pont-Saint-Esprit bridge originally had 20 arches and a length of 900 m (980 yd). Although now somewhat modified, the medieval bridge has survived until the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMesqui2000521-522_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMesqui2000521-522-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the collapse of the Saint-Bénézet bridge the Rhône at Avignon was crossed by ferry until the 19th century. Between 1806 and 1819 a wooden bridge was built across the two branches of the river. The section between Avignon and the Île de la Barthelasse was replaced by a suspension bridge in 1843 but the wooden section between the island and Villeneuve was not replaced until 1905-1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGirard1958354_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGirard1958354-24">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chapel of Saint Nicholas and the four remaining arches were listed as a <i><a href="/wiki/Monument_historique" title="Monument historique">Monument historique</a></i> in 1840.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_bridge_hypothesis">Roman bridge hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman bridge hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A scholarly debate has taken place on whether a bridge existed prior to the construction of the Saint Bénézet bridge in the 12th century. An earlier bridge was first proposed by Henri Revoil at the French Archaeological Conference held in Avignon in 1882. His main argument was based on the appearance of the stonework at the base of the four surviving piers. At very low water, stone blocks were visible which were larger than those above and had features that appeared foreign to the existing bridge. The style of the masonry indicated to Revoil that there had been an earlier bridge dating from either the late Roman or <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty" title="Carolingian dynasty">Carolingian</a> periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESagnier1883.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fgallica.bnf.fr.2Fark:.2F12148.2Fbpt6k35651b.2Ff314.image_259-260.5D_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESagnier1883.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fgallica.bnf.fr.2Fark:.2F12148.2Fbpt6k35651b.2Ff314.image_259-260.5D-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A9195389-91_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A9195389-91-28">[26]</a></sup> In 1892 Louis Rochetin published an article suggesting that the projecting stone blocks at the base of the first pier and those on either side of the second pier supporting the chapel were the remains of <a href="/wiki/Springer_(architecture)" title="Springer (architecture)">springers</a> that would have supported earlier Roman arches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERochetin1892.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fgallica.bnf.fr.2Fark:.2F12148.2Fbpt6k5531312k.2Ff316.image_304.5D_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERochetin1892.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fgallica.bnf.fr.2Fark:.2F12148.2Fbpt6k5531312k.2Ff316.image_304.5D-29">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953118-122_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953118-122-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Denis-Marcel Marié, in his book on the bridge published in 1953, carefully reviewed all the previous publications and in the final chapter came out in support of the hypothesis that an early bridge had been built by the <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_culture" title="Gallo-Roman culture">Gallo-Romans</a> towards the end of the Roman occupation. He suggested that the bases of the surviving piers belonged to this earlier bridge and that the semi-circular arches employed during the Roman period meant that level of the roadway would have been higher than the top of the surviving chapel. Marié surmised that this early bridge had collapsed over the following seven centuries and the 12th century Bénézet bridge had consisted of a decking supported on wooden piles linking the ruined Roman piers. The piles were necessary because the gaps between the Roman stone piers would have been too large to span with wooden beams without the intervening support. The height of the Bénézet bridge would have been at the level of the lower chapel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953150-154_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953150-154-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further support for the existence of a Roman bridge came in an article by Perrot <i>et al.</i> published in 1971. The article described a survey undertaken in 1969 on the surviving vestiges of the piers in the Villeneuve branch of the river before their total destruction by the <a href="/wiki/Compagnie_Nationale_du_Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Compagnie Nationale du Rhône">Compagnie Nationale du Rhône</a> (CNR). The article also included long quotes from an unpublished report by Mr Mathian, an engineer working for the CNR, on a survey carried out in 1965 on the four intact piers on the Avignon side of the river. This survey had discovered a layer of wood, at least 20 cm in thickness, under the foundations of each of the four intact piers. A sample of the wood was dated by the <a href="/wiki/Centre_national_de_la_recherche_scientifique" title="Centre national de la recherche scientifique">Centre national de la recherche scientifique</a> (CNRS) using the <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon technique</a> to between 290 and 530 AD, corresponding to the end of the Roman Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197167-70_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197167-70-32">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[c]</a></sup> In the survey of the ruined piers in the Villeneuve channel, a pier (listed as number 14) was found to contain wooden beams within the masonry. A sample of this wood was radiodated to 890 AD.(error estimates were not specified).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197174_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197174-34">[31]</a></sup> During the dredging of the Villeneuve channel the remains of three large wooden piles were recovered, two of which were still shod with iron tips. Samples of wood from these were sent for dating, but at the time of the publication of the article, the results had not been received.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197185-86_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerrotGranierGagni.C3.A8re197185-86-35">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The archaeologist Dominique Carru, while accepting the radiocarbon date for the sample of wood, has argued that it is very unlikely that an earlier bridge existed. It is not mentioned in the surviving chronicles from the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">high medieval period</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarru1999117_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarru1999117-36">[33]</a></sup> and a bridge would have led to the development of an urban centre on the right bank of the Rhône opposite Avignon, similar to those at other locations in the Rhône valley, such as <a href="/wiki/Trinquetaille" title="Trinquetaille">Trinquetaille</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Romain-en-Gal" title="Saint-Romain-en-Gal">Saint-Romain-en-Gal</a> near <a href="/wiki/Vienne,_Is%C3%A8re" title="Vienne, Isère">Vienne</a>. There is no evidence for a significant early settlement near the terminus of the bridge. The main east-west route in the Roman period passed through <a href="/wiki/Tarascon" title="Tarascon">Tarascon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Beaucaire,_Gard" title="Beaucaire, Gard">Beaucaire</a>, 20 km (12 mi) to the south, and avoided the river at Avignon which was wide and variable in position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarru200119_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarru200119-37">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_song_.22Sur_le_Pont_d.27Avignon.22">The song "Sur le Pont d'Avignon"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The song &quot;Sur le Pont d'Avignon&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sur_le_Pont_d%27Avignon" title="Sur le Pont d'Avignon">Sur le Pont d'Avignon</a></div>
<p>The bridge has achieved worldwide fame through its commemoration by the song "<a href="/wiki/Sur_le_Pont_d%27Avignon" title="Sur le Pont d'Avignon">Sur le Pont d'Avignon</a>" ("On the Bridge of Avignon"). An earlier song with the same title was popular in the 16th and 17th centuries. The melody was published by <a href="/wiki/Ottaviano_Petrucci" title="Ottaviano Petrucci">Ottaviano Petrucci</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Harmonice_Musices_Odhecaton" title="Harmonice Musices Odhecaton">Harmonice Musices Odhecaton</a></i> of 1503-4. The 16th-century composer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Certon" title="Pierre Certon">Pierre Certon</a> used the melody in a mass with the title of "Sus le Pont d'Avignon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup> The modern version of the song dates from the mid-19th century when <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Adam" title="Adolphe Adam">Adolphe Adam</a> included it in the <a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ra_comique" title="Opéra comique">opéra comique</a> <i>Le Sourd ou l'Auberge pleine</i> which was first performed in Paris in 1853. The opera was an adaptation of the 1790 comedy by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Jean_Baptiste_Choudard_Desforges" title="Pierre Jean Baptiste Choudard Desforges">Desforges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pont_Saint-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9zet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 40em; -webkit-column-width: 40em; column-width: 40em; list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The bridge is mentioned in a letter sent by the prelates and barons of the army of Louis VIII to the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Frederick II</a> in 1236 justifying their conduct in the siege of Avignon. The letter refers to a wooden bridge built by the citizens of Avignon: "<i>quam transire permisserant per pontem ligneum quem extra villam facerant</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953145_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMari.C3.A91953145-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevicVaissete1879.5Bhttps:.2F.2Farchive.org.2Fstream.2Fhistoiregnra08viccuoft.23page.2F420.2Fmode.2F2up_cols._840-842.5D_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevicVaissete1879.5Bhttps:.2F.2Farchive.org.2Fstream.2Fhistoiregnra08viccuoft.23page.2F420.2Fmode.2F2up_cols._840-842.5D-4">[4]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There are records of repairs to the bridge in 1321, 1324, 1348, 1375, 1431, 1471, 1603 and 1633.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaigret200217_Note_24_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaigret200217_Note_24-7">[6]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-33">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The article gives no technical details of the radiocarbon dating procedure and does not specify from which pier the sample was taken.</span></li>
</ol>
