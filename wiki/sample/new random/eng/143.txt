<p><b>Françoise Barré-Sinoussi</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[fʁɑ̃swaz baʁesinusi]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Fr-Fran%C3%A7oise_Barr%C3%A9-Sinoussi.ogg" title="File:Fr-Françoise Barré-Sinoussi.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Fr-Fran%C3%A7oise_Barr%C3%A9-Sinoussi.ogg" title="Fr-Françoise Barré-Sinoussi.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; born 30 July 1947) is a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virologist" title="Virologist">virologist</a> and director of the Regulation of Retroviral Infections Division (<i>Unité de Régulation des Infections Rétrovirales</i>) at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institut_Pasteur" title="Institut Pasteur">Institut Pasteur</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Paris, France</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, Barré-Sinoussi performed some of the fundamental work in the identification of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_immunodeficiency_virus" title="Human immunodeficiency virus">human immunodeficiency virus</a> (HIV) as the cause of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>. In 2008, she was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a>, together with her former mentor, <a href="/wiki/Luc_Montagnier" title="Luc Montagnier">Luc Montagnier</a>, for their discovery of HIV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She mandatorily retired from active research on August 31, 2015 and will fully retire by some time in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fran%C3%A7oise_Barr%C3%A9-Sinoussi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="802" data-file-width="700" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg/220px-HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg/330px-HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg/440px-HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HTLV-1_and_HIV-1_EM_8241_lores.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The human T-lymphotropic virus type 1 (HTLV-1), and the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV).</div>

<p>Barré-Sinoussi joined the <a href="/wiki/Pasteur_Institute" title="Pasteur Institute">Pasteur Institute</a> in Paris in the early 1970s. She received her PhD in 1975 and interned at the U.S. National Institutes of Health before returning to the Pasteur Institute. Barré-Sinoussi's research quickly turned to a particular group of viruses, the retroviruses. Her knowledge in this field led her to discover <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> in 1983. This discovery revealed an urgent need for diagnostic tests to aid in controlling the spread of the disease. Barré-Sinoussi started her own laboratory at the Pasteur Institute in 1988.</p>
<p>Among Barré-Sinoussi's many recent research contributions are studies of various aspects of the adaptive immune response to viral infection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the role of innate immune defences of the host in controlling HIV/AIDS,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> factors involved in mother-to-child transmission of HIV,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and characteristics that allow a small percentage of HIV-positive individuals, known as elite suppressors or controllers, to limit HIV replication without antiretroviral drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She has co-authored over 240 scientific publications, has participated in over 250 international conferences, and has trained many young researchers.</p>
<p>Barré-Sinoussi has actively contributed to several scientific societies and committees at the Institut Pasteur as well as to other AIDS organizations, such as the National Agency for AIDS Research in France. She has also been implicated at an international level, notably as a consultant to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WHO" title="WHO">WHO</a> and the UNAIDS-HIV.</p>
<p>Since the 1980s, Barré-Sinoussi has initiated collaborations with developing countries whereby she has managed multidisciplinary networks with dedication. She constantly works on establishing permanent links between basic research and clinical research with the aim of achieving concrete improvements in the areas of prevention, clinical care, and treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, she wrote an open letter to Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedict_XVI" title="Benedict XVI">Benedict XVI</a> in protest over his statements that condoms are at best ineffective in the AIDS crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2012 Barré-Sinoussi became President of the <a href="/wiki/International_AIDS_Society" title="International AIDS Society">International AIDS Society</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fran%C3%A7oise_Barr%C3%A9-Sinoussi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barré-Sinoussi shared the 2008 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with Luc Montagnier for their co-discovery of HIV, and with Harald zur Hausen, who discovered the viral cause of cervical cancer that led to the development of the HPV vaccine.</p>
<p>In addition to the Nobel Prize, Barré-Sinoussi has received awards including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Sovac Prize</li>
<li>The Körber European Science Prize (<abbr title="also known as">aka</abbr> Körber Foundation Prize for the Promotion of European Science)</li>
<li>The Prize of the French Academy of Sciences (Académie des sciences)</li>
<li>The King Faisal International Prize</li>
<li>The International AIDS Society Prize <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Barré-Sinoussi was named an Officer of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">National Order of the Legion of Honour</a> (<b>Ordre national de la Légion d’honneur</b>) in 2006 and was raised to Commander in 2009. She was promoted to the dignity of Grand Officer in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>She received an honorary Doctor of Science degree from <a href="/wiki/Tulane_University" title="Tulane University">Tulane University</a> in May 2009, and an honorary Doctor of Medicine from the University of New South Wales in July 2014.</p>
