<p><b>Spix's red-handed howler</b> (<i>Alouatta discolor</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Howler_monkey" title="Howler monkey">Howler monkey</a> native to the southeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. It is threatened by <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and hunting.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spix%27s_red-handed_howler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Red-handed_howler#Taxonomy" title="Red-handed howler">Red-handed howler § Taxonomy</a></div>
<p>The species was previously considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Red-handed_howler" title="Red-handed howler">red-handed howler</a>, and is now variously treated as a synonym of the <a href="/wiki/Maranh%C3%A3o_red-handed_howler" title="Maranhão red-handed howler">Maranhão red-handed howler</a> or as a separate species (followed here).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spix%27s_red-handed_howler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spix's red-handed howler has a similar appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Red-handed_howler" title="Red-handed howler">red-handed howler</a>, but it has a yellowish-brown to reddish-brown back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Males weigh about 7.2 kg, females about 5.5 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spix%27s_red-handed_howler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species is endemic to central <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, specifically along the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> in the State of <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a>. It occurs mainly in the interfluvial land area between four rivers, <a href="/wiki/Tapaj%C3%B3s" title="Tapajós">Tapajós</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juruena_River" title="Juruena River">Juruena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xingu_River" title="Xingu River">Xingú</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iriri_River" title="Iriri River">Irirí</a>. The westernmost limit of native habitat lies on the right bank of the Tapajós River and Juruena River and stretches east to the banks of the Xingú River and Irirí River. This strip of land extends northward to Forte Curupá (<a href="/wiki/Gurup%C3%A1" title="Gurupá">Gurupá</a>), Brazil, which is the northernmost extent of the distribution of Spix's red-handed howler and also its type locality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spix's red-handed howler mainly inhabits lowland forests separated by dry areas as well as forests affected by seasonal flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spix%27s_red-handed_howler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary food source of this species is ripe and unripe fruit; it has the highest reliance on this food source of all species in this genus. A variety of species provide fruit year-round, but leaves supplement the sugars of the fruits with protein in their diets. When these food sources are limited, the howlers also supplement their diets with more mature leaves, flowers, woody plant matter, and sometimes fungi to possibly help digest the increased plant matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinto_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pinto-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spix's red-handed howlers generally move in groups of 4-11 individuals, and have relatively small home ranges, 5-45 ha in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spix%27s_red-handed_howler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spix's red-handed howler is currently listed as a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> species by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>, based on a population decrease of over 30% over the past 3 generations, mainly due to habitat loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main threat to the species is habitat destruction by <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> as well as land conversion for cattle ranching and soy plantations. <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">Habitat fragmentation</a> occurs through the presence of two major highways - the Cuiabá-Santarém highway runs through its range in a north-south direction and the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Amazonian_Highway" title="Trans-Amazonian Highway">Trans-amazonian Highway</a> in an east-west direction. These roadways also pose the risk of fatality by vehicle collision. The habitat of the species is also heavily hunted (both commercially and for subsistence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
