<p>The <b>Homunculus Nebula</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_nebula" title="Bipolar nebula">bipolar</a> <a href="/wiki/Emission_nebula" title="Emission nebula">emission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_nebula" title="Reflection nebula">reflection nebula</a> surrounding the massive star system <a href="/wiki/Eta_Carinae" title="Eta Carinae">Eta Carinae</a>. The nebula is embedded within the much larger <a href="/wiki/Carina_Nebula" title="Carina Nebula">Carina Nebula</a>, a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star-forming_region" title="Star-forming region">star-forming</a> <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">ionized hydrogen (H<sub>II</sub>) region</a>. From the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> meaning <i>Little Man</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Homunculus" title="Homunculus">Homunculus</a> itself is effectively a small H<sub>II</sub> region, with gas shocked into ionised and excited states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teodoro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-teodoro-3">[3]</a></sup> It also absorbs much of the light from the extremely luminous central stellar system and re-radiates it as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red" title="Infra-red">infra-red</a> (IR). It is the brightest object in the sky at mid-IR wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imposters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-imposters-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the Homunculus is a smaller <i>Little Homunculus</i>, and within that a shell of shocked material from stellar winds that has been called <i>Baby Homunculus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abraham_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-abraham-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_history">Observational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1914, Eta Carinae was reported to have a faint companion and also be non-stellar: "fuzzy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-innes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-innes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Observations in 1944 and 1945 showed a somewhat elongated nebulosity around 5" wide and 10" long. It was recognised to be expanding consistent with having originated in an explosion in the mid 19th century, measured at 3".2 - 7".5 per century. At that time the shape of the nebula showed a central bulge with a single large lump to the NW and two smaller extensions to the SE, which was described as a <i>Homunculus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaviola_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaviola-6">[6]</a></sup> Other observations at around the same time described a strongly orange (Hα) central region in a larger fainter green (O<sub>III</sub>) nebulosity. The phrase "red spade-beard" was applied, but this name didn't catch on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thackeray_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-thackeray-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">Shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1141" data-file-width="2028" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg/300px-3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg/450px-3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg/600px-3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:3D_Homunculus_Nebula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 3D model of the Homunculus Nebula.</div>

<p>The Homunculus consists of two lobes, referred to as NW and SE based on their orientation as seen from Earth, each approximately 7" wide by 5" long. There is also a ragged equatorial skirt of material which can be seen faintly in deep images at certain wavelengths. The lobes are mostly hollow with the material strongly concentrated towards the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abraham_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-abraham-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steffen_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-steffen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The equatorial skirt appears to contain material of the same age and younger than the lobes. It contains a much smaller mass of material than the lobes, shining mainly by reflected light which escapes most easily at equatorial latitudes. There is less dust and little molecular hydrogen compared to the lobes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bipolar nebula is angled so that the NW lobe is further away from us than the SE lobe. The whole nebula is expanding so that the SE lobe is blue-shifted and the NW lobe is red-shifted, relative to the central source. The lobes contain the majority of the material in the Homunculus Nebula, in relatively thin shells concentrated towards the poles. The shells consist of two components, an inner warm region and a more massive outer cool skin. The shells are smooth and thin suggesting they were ejected in as little as five years, but there are streaks of thicker dust detectable within the shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Each lobe has polar "hole" although it isn't clear if it is an actual gap in the shell of the lobe or just a deep indentation. Surrounding each polar hole is a "trench". The trenches are visible as approximate half-circles centred on the axis of the lobes but may form complete circles. There are other smaller irregular indentations and protrusions to the lobes, apparently symmetrical with the same features appearing on each lobe. The most striking are flattened protrusions at about 10°latitude, one on each lobe, but there are other smaller protrusions near the equatorial skirt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steffen_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-steffen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mass of the nebula cannot be determined directly. However the amount of dust can be measured fairly accurately and estimates of the gas:dust ratio used to calculate the total mass. The total dust mass is calculated at 0.4 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>, leading to estimates that up to 40 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub> of gas are contained in the Homunculus itself. Nearly as much material is detected within outer ejecta, but still formed within the last thousand years. Older calculations had produce consensus estimates of 10-15 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomez_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gomez-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weigelt_Blobs">Weigelt Blobs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weigelt Blobs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early speckle interferometry showed that the central region of the Homunculus contains four point-like sources, originally tagged A1, A2, A3, and A4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weigelt_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-weigelt-12">[12]</a></sup> The four speckle objects were later referred to as A, B, C, and D. Higher resolution studies showed that only the brightest source A was truly stellar, and the other three were small nebular condensations. The three <i>Weigelt Blobs</i> are visible primarily in light directly reflected from the Eta Carinae stars. The blobs are believed to lie near the equatorial plane of the stellar system, but their origin is unclear. Their speed has been measured, but within uncertainties they could have been emitted in the 1890 outburst or a 1941 event. The situation is complicated further by the likely acceleration of their slow movement due to the intense stellar winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dorland_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dorland-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectrum">Spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spectrum of the Homunculus is complex, consisting of reflected, thermal, and emission components at wavelengths across the electromagnetic spectrum. The dominant feature is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a> radiation from dust heated by the stars within. Overlaid on this is some light from the stars themselves reflected mostly from dense features within the nebulosity, showing strong visual and UV spectral lines in emission. There are also "nebular" emission lines from ionised gas where it collides with slower moving material or is excited by high energy electromagnetic radiation from the stars. The ionisation emission is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> but at lower levels of ionisation due to the lower temperatures of the central stars. The strongest lines are [Fe<sub>II</sub>] and [N<sub>II</sub>], similar to those from the stellar winds of the stars themselves, but with narrower profiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shockwaves at the outer edge of the ejecta are heated to millions of <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a> and emit x-ray radiation. The lobes of the Homunculus emit copious radio waves, most importantly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/21cm_line" title="21cm line">21cm line</a> of hydrogen.</p>
<p>The reflected spectrum of the Homunculus lobes varies with position, due to the central star emitting different radiation at different latitudes (i.e. in different directions). This is the only star for which such an effect can be observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eta_Carinae" title="Eta Carinae">Eta Carinae</a></div>
<p>The Homunculus was ejected in an enormous outburst from Eta Carinae. Light from this event reached Earth in 1841, creating a brightening event in the night sky which was visible from the Earth's surface at the time. During the event (as seen from Earth) Eta Carinae briefly became the second-brightest star in the sky, after <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a>; but the ejected gas and dust have since obscured much of its light. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernova_imposter" title="Supernova imposter">near-supernova</a> explosion produced two polar lobes, and a large but thin <a href="/wiki/Non-inclined_orbit" title="Non-inclined orbit">equatorial</a> disk, all moving outward at up to 670 km/s (1,500,000 mph).</p>
<p>The Homunculus Nebula is a virtually unique structure, believed to result from the extremely young age. This means that the shape and structure is almost entirely due to the original eruption rather than the interaction with surrounding interstellar material. Suggestions that the waist between the two lobes was formed by "pinching" from a dense surrounding material have been disproved, and the bipolar shells are now believed to be caused by concentrated polar outflows of material, with the equatorial skirt formed by breakout of faster ejected material through the thinnest parts of the shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steffen_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-steffen-8">[8]</a></sup> The ejection of material preferentially along the axis of rotation of the stars, or of the binary orbit, may be due to the rotation of Eta Carinae A itself resulting in stronger mass loss towards the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The thickness, or thinness, of the bipolar shells argues for their ejection within approximately five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup> Irregularities in the otherwise very smooth structure of the shells are conjectured to result from interactions between the winds of the two central stars, and from their orbital motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steffen_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-steffen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Homunculus_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Homunculus is assumed to be at the same distance as Eta Carinae, which is assumed to be at approximately the same distance as Trumpler 16 and the Carina Nebula, but the distances to these objects are not known with any great accuracy. Instead the distance of the Homunculus Nebula itself can be calculated in a unique way.</p>
<p>The velocity of particular locations within the thin shell of the Homunculus lobes can be measured using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">doppler shift</a> of the spectral lines at that point, assuming the lobes are symmetrical. There are two different doppler shifts visible in the spectrum: one for direct emission lines; and one for reflected lines from Eta Carinae A. The direct line doppler shift gives the expansion velocity of the shell project onto the line of sight. Assuming an origin in Eta Carinae's Great Eruption and a constant expansion velocity, this gives the linear distance of the shell from the central star projected along the line of sight. The difference between the reflected line doppler shift velocity and the direct line velocity gives the actual (i.e. not projected) distance of the shell from the central star, again assuming expansion at constant velocity since the Great Eruption.</p>
<p>Observations of the spectrum of the Homunculus at a particular angular distance from the central star has shown the actual linear distance of that point from the central star, which defines the distance. Values obtained using this method are around 2.3 kpc with a margin of error around 100pc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-davidson-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same calculations also return the inclination of the axis of the Homunculus relative to the line of sight. This turns out to be 41°, or 49° relative to the plane of the sky, which means we see it slightly more "end on" than "side on".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith2006_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-smith2006-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidson_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-davidson-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
