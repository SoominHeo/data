<p><b><a href="/wiki/Sailfish" title="Sailfish">Sailfish</a> OS</b> (also styled as <b>SailfishOS</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aard-earlyaccess_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-aard-earlyaccess-4">[4]</a></sup> or abbreviated to SFOS) is a general purpose Linux used commonly as <a href="/wiki/Mobile_operating_system" title="Mobile operating system">mobile operating system</a> combining the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a> for a particular hardware platform use, the open-source <a href="/wiki/Mer_(software_distribution)" title="Mer (software distribution)">Mer core</a> stack of middleware, a proprietary <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">UI</a> contributed by <a href="/wiki/Jolla" title="Jolla">Jolla</a> or an open source UI, and other third-party components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EULA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EULA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-License_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-License-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sailfish is being developed by Jolla, the Sailfish and Mer project communities, corporate members of the <a href="/wiki/Sailfish_Alliance" title="Sailfish Alliance">Sailfish Alliance</a> and various open community members. The Sailfish community members makes development requests and decides development priorities by voting. The Mer project receives contributions from the Jolla and its community, and Mer is the source of middleware for Jolla, thereby continuous development and compatibility of all MER based projects is maintained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The OS is shipped with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jolla_(mobile_phone)" title="Jolla (mobile phone)">Jolla smartphone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jolla_Tablet" title="Jolla Tablet">tablet</a> (discontinued<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>) and from other vendors licensing the OS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> More or less unofficially the OS is being ported by community enthusiasts to third-party mobile devices including smartphones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and tablets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The OS is an evolved continuation of the Linux <a href="/wiki/MeeGo" title="MeeGo">MeeGo</a> OS previously developed by alliance of <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> which itself relies on combined <a href="/wiki/Maemo" title="Maemo">Maemo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moblin" title="Moblin">Moblin</a>. The MeeGo legacy is contained in the Mer core in about 80% of its code; the Mer name thus expands to <b>ME</b>ego <b>R</b>econstructed. This base is extended by Jolla with a custom user interface and default applications. Jolla and MERproject.org follow a <a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">meritocratic system</a> to avoid the mistakes that lead to the MeeGo project's then-unanticipated discontinuation.</p>
<p>The main elements for Sailfish OS 2.0 include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Technically stronger OS core</li>
<li>Improved Android application compatibility</li>
<li>Support for ARM, Intel architecture, including the Intel Atom x3 processor, or any platform with kernel useable for MER core stack.</li>
<li>Design to provide visibility in the UI for digital content providers and to enable OS level integration for mobile commerce</li>
<li>Strong multitasking (one of the most important advantage of the OS and declared to be the best one at market)</li>
<li>Strong privacy and personalization features</li>
<li>Enhanced user interface with new UI/UX features, including simpler swipe access to main functions, enhanced notifications and events views.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_architecture">Software architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sailfish OS and the Sailfish <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kit</a> (SDK) are based on the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mer_(software_distribution)" title="Mer (software distribution)">Mer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SailfishOS.org_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SailfishOS.org-12">[12]</a></sup> Sailfish OS includes a multi-tasking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphical_shell" title="Graphical shell">graphical shell</a> called "Lipstick" built by Jolla on top of the <a href="/wiki/Wayland_(display_server_protocol)" title="Wayland (display server protocol)">Wayland display server protocol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osnews-jolla-review_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-osnews-jolla-review-13">[13]</a></sup> Jolla uses <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source_graphics_device_driver" title="Free and open-source graphics device driver">free and open-source graphics device drivers</a> but the <a href="/wiki/Hybris_(software)" title="Hybris (software)">Hybris</a> library allows use of proprietary drivers for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Jolla's stated goal is for Sailfish to be open source eventually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-License_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-License-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Currently Sailfish's website does not appear to claim that OS will be eventually opensourced. (February 2015)">needs update?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sailfish OS can run Android applications through a proprietary compatibility layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Targeted_device_classes">Targeted device classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Targeted device classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sailfish is commonly known to be targeted at mobile devices, but since it inherited around 80% of MeeGo code, Sailfish can be used as a complete general-purpose Linux OS on devices ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_car_entertainment" title="In car entertainment">in vehicle infotainment</a> (IVI), navigation, smart TV, desktops and notebooks, yachts, automotive, e-commerce, home appliances, measuring and control equipment, smart building equipment, etc. See use cases of original MeeGo to compare, and the <a href="#Devices">Devices</a> section for devices that run the Sailfish OS.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sailfish_OS_SDK">Sailfish OS SDK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sailfish OS SDK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sailfish OS SDK was announced in at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slush_Helsinki" title="Slush Helsinki">Slush Helsinki</a> conference in 2012, and the <a href="/wiki/Software_release_life_cycle#Alpha" title="Software release life cycle">alpha</a> was published in February 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The SDK, installation and coding tutorials are available for free download from the Sailfish OS website despite the overall license not being open source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EULA_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EULA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sailfish SDK uses <a href="/wiki/Qt_(software)" title="Qt (software)">Qt</a> with <a href="/wiki/VirtualBox" title="VirtualBox">VirtualBox</a> for development, compiling and <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulation</a> purposes, in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a> method.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This technique allows compilation on the Sailfish OS and full testing of developed software in the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machine" title="Virtual machine">virtual machine</a>, emulating—not simulating—the whole Sailfish OS.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This also separates development activities and side effects from everything else running on the host computer, leaving it undisturbed by developments and tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to Jolla, development with Sailfish SDK is development on Sailfish OS itself; there are no differences between developed software appearance and behaviour in the SDK and on a device running Sailfish OS.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The availability of source code to the SDK allows shaping and rebuilding to companies' or developers' specific needs<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, creating a context-specific environment that is set once and needs no preparation when the device is booted. The SDK runs on the operating systems <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>, <a href="/wiki/32-bit" title="32-bit">32-</a> and <a href="/wiki/64-bit_computing" title="64-bit computing">64-bit</a> versions of <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, 64-bit versions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It can be used for compiling software for Sailfish OS devices from Linux sources. Its general console/terminal mode follows a commonly used standard. Compatible binaries or libraries can also be used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_programming_interfaces">Application programming interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Application programming interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sailfish OS uses open source Qt APIs (Qt 5, QtQuick 2 etc.) and a closed source Sailfish Silica for the UI. Standard Linux APIs are provided by the Mer Core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sailfish, <a href="/wiki/Ubuntu_(operating_system)" title="Ubuntu (operating system)">Ubuntu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_Active" title="Plasma Active">Plasma Active</a> have been cooperating to share common APIs. When successful, this will make the platforms compatible on the API level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UI_supported_languages">UI supported languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: UI supported languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Officially Jolla declares supporting the following 14 languages for the user interface: <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">English (UK)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese (Mainland)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Cantonese" title="Hong Kong Cantonese">Chinese (Hong Kong)</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_.22Early_access.22_for_beta_testers_and_developers">Public "Early access" for beta testers and developers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: Public "Early access" for beta testers and developers'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After positive experiences with pushing early updates to a small group of opt-in users for Sailfish Update 9 and for the connectivity hotfix, Jolla has allowed all interested parties to try a new version of Sailfish OS about 1–2 weeks before official release, in a program called "Early access". It is expected to be useful for developers and technically minded users, and a step towards more community integration into the Sailfish release process, including improvement of quality by identifying critical issues which only show up in certain environments or device setups, before rolling the update out to the wider user audience. As an added bonus, it provides a window for developers to test their applications on new releases of Sailfish OS.</p>
<p>In the long term it will help Jolla to establish a developer program with early release candidate access for registered developers, and to have more community involvement in platform development. The first detail Jolla is hoping to learn from this is how it can gather feedback from a large audience in a reasonable way.</p>
<p>Basic details about the early access update:</p>
<ul>
<li>The early release access is meant primarily for advanced users and developers.</li>
<li>To sign up for the program there is a checkbox in the Jolla accounts profile page.</li>
<li>Installed early-access release cannot be downgraded. The only way to downgrade from early access releases is to do a factory reset after removing the sign up check from the user's account profile.</li>
<li>Early access releases should be considered "reasonably stable". Issues found during that period will either be fixed, or added to "known issues" on the release notes.</li>
<li>Signing up for the early access releases will not void warranty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aard-earlyaccess_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-aard-earlyaccess-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Porting">Porting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Porting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of projects successful on other platforms are migrating to become native Sailfish OS applications. This gives abandoned <a href="/wiki/MeeGo#MeeGo.2FHarmattan" title="MeeGo">Harmattan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Symbian" title="Symbian">Symbian</a> projects a new life. Porting Qt-written projects may take only a few hours. In support, sailfish.org collects and publishes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PortingHarmattan_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-PortingHarmattan-51">[51]</a></sup> an online compendium of knowledge, links and instructions on:</p>
<ul>
<li>software porting and migration to Sailfish OS</li>
<li>similarities and differences between Harmattan and Sailfish</li>
<li>guides how to port MeeGo 1.2 Harmattan applications for the Nokia N9 to the Sailfish OS devices</li>
<li>porting framework (Qt 4 to Qt 5, SDL 1.2 is SDL 2.0, Debian packaging is RPM packaging)</li>
<li>application porting tutorials and examples (QtQuick QML applications, the Flickr application Qt 5, SDL / OpenGL ES applications)</li>
<li>Qt Quick Components map to Sailfish Silica.</li>
</ul>
<p>As Sailfish is a GNU/Linux-based OS, it is also possible to install other GNU/Linux applications on it, be they sources for compilation or direct binaries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_Android_software_running_on_Sailfish_OS">Using Android software running on Sailfish OS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Using Android software running on Sailfish OS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to its native applications, Sailfish can run most Android applications. Problems can arise if these applications were coded without following Android standards about controls, which might not display correctly and so become unusable. Built-in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alien_Dalvik" title="Alien Dalvik">Alien Dalvik</a> plays the role of an Android compatibility layer. It <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulates</a>, but does not <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulate</a>, Android OS, and the environment is recognized as such by Android software, which thus runs at native speed without any perceivable <a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">performance slow-down</a>. Sailfish multitasking is always enabled by the nature of Linux, and this allows running both native Sailfish and Android software simultaneously, while the user can switch between them on the fly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware_overview">Hardware overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hardware overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_of_the_Mer_standard">Advantages of the Mer standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Advantages of the Mer standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sailfish OS can be used on any hardware with Linux-kernel support and compatible with the middleware of the <a href="/wiki/Mer_(software_distribution)" title="Mer (software distribution)">Mer core</a>. A number of devices running ported this way has been created by community enthusiasts. Instead of designation to a specific reference hardware platform, a <a href="/wiki/VirtualBox" title="VirtualBox">VirtualBox</a> implementation with the Sailfish OS SDK is available for development on Linux, OS X and Windows operating systems. This virtual machine implementation contains the whole Sailfish OS isolated from local resources and the local OS to enable convenient evaluation of the behavior and performance of coded or ported software before deployment on real devices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OS_development_status">OS development status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: OS development status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sailfish OS is promoted by Jolla and supported by the open <a href="/wiki/Sailfish_Alliance" title="Sailfish Alliance">Sailfish Alliance</a> established in 2011, a group established to unite OEM and ODM manufacturers, chipset providers, operators, application developers and retailers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> On 16 August 2012, the user interface was reported to be ready for release. Jolla's CEO Jussi Hurmola stated in a ZDNet interview, " ... Our UI is ready now, we haven't released it yet, we will save it for the product launch and the platform is getting up now so the project looks pretty nice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next day, Jolla's CEO Marc Dillon said on social networking website <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> that the company had reached the first development target. Sailfish was debuted by the Jolla team, including a worldwide internet stream, as a demo of the OS, and the <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">UI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">SDK</a> during the Slush event in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, Finland, on 21–22 November 2012. The alpha stage of Sailfish OS SDK was published at the end of February 2013 and was made available for free download.</p>
<p>On 16 September 2013, Jolla announced that its OS had been made compatible with Android applications and hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhushan_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bhushan-88">[88]</a></sup> The first telephone to use it was launched on 27 November 2013 at a pop-up <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DNA_Kauppa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DNA Kauppa (page does not exist)">DNA Kauppa</a> shop in Helsinki. The first 450 telephones were sold at this event, while the rest of the preordered devices were shipped shortly after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2015, version 1.1.9.28 "Eineheminlampi" was released, which added the main elements of the revamped Sailfish OS 2.0 user interface.</p>
<p>Sailfish 2.0 was launched with the Jolla Tablet, and existing devices, both smartphones and tablets, from Jolla's official distribution channels are supported with upgrade to Sailfish 2.0 and following updates.</p>
<p>In May 2016 Jolla announced the Sailfish Community Device Program, supporting developers and members of Sailfish OS community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooperation_and_OS_support">Cooperation and OS support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sailfish_OS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cooperation and OS support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jolla staff met with members of the Russian technology community to break ground on the new software and promote Sailfish OS, as part of Jolla's BRICS strategy. As a result of those efforts, on 18 May 2015 the Russian minister of communications Nikolai Nikiforov announced plans to replace Apple's iOS and Google's Android platforms with new software based on Sailfish, as an open-source mobile operating system, developed by Finnish phone maker Jolla. He intends it to cover 50% of Russian need in this area during next ten years, in comparison to the 95% currently covered with western technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
