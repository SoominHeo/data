<p>The <b>western honey bee</b> or <b>European honey bee</b> (<i>Apis mellifera</i>) is the most common of the 7-12 species of <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bee</a> worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engel99_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Engel99-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name <i>Apis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "bee", and <i>mellifera</i> means "honey-bearing", referring to the species' tendency to produce a large quantity of honey for storage over the winter.</p>
<p>Like all honey bees, the western honey bee is <a href="/wiki/Eusociality" title="Eusociality">eusocial</a>, creating colonies with a single fertile female (or "queen"), many sterile females or "workers", and small proportion of fertile males or "drones". Individual colonies can house tens of thousands of bees. Colony activities are organized by complex communication between individuals, through both odors and the dance language.</p>
<p>The western honey bee was one of the first domesticated insects, and is the primary species maintained by beekeepers to this day for both its honey and <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> services. With human assistance, the western honey bee now occupies every continent except Antarctica. Due to its wide cultivation, this species is the single most important pollinator for agriculture globally. A number of <a href="/wiki/List_of_diseases_of_the_honey_bee" title="List of diseases of the honey bee">pests and diseases</a> threaten the honey bee, especially <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a>.</p>
<p>Western honey bees are an important <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> in scientific studies, particularly in the fields of social evolution, learning and memory; they are also used in studies of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_toxicity_to_bees" title="Pesticide toxicity to bees">pesticide toxicity</a>, to assess non-target impacts of commercial pesticides.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Western Honey Bee can be found on every continent except Antartica. The species is believed to have originated in either Africa,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645-3">[3]</a></sup> or Asia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> from which it spread throughout Africa, the Middle East and Europe. Humans are responsible for its considerable additional range, introducing European subspecies into North America (early 1600s)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> South America, Australia, New Zealand, and eastern Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Western honey bees adapted to the local environments as they spread geographically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645-3">[3]</a></sup> These adaptations include synchronizing colony cycles to the timing of local flower resources, forming a winter cluster in colder climates, migratory swarming in Africa, and enhanced foraging behavior in desert areas. All together, these variations resulted in 28 recognized subspecies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engel99_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Engel99-1">[1]</a></sup> all of which are cross-fertile. The subspecies are divided into four major branches, based on work by Ruttner and confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> analysis. African subspecies belong to branch A, northwestern European subspecies branch M, southwestern European subspecies branch C and Middle-Eastern subspecies branch O.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_life_cycle"><span id="Biology.2C_life_cycle"></span>Biology and life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology and life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the temperate zone <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a> survive winter as a colony, and the queen begins <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a>-laying in mid- to late winter in preparation for spring (probably triggered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_length" title="Day length">day length</a>). The only fertile <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a>, the queen lays the eggs from which all the other bees are produced. Except for a brief period (when she may fly to mate with drones or leave in later life with a <a href="/wiki/Swarming_(honey_bee)" title="Swarming (honey bee)">swarm</a> to establish a new <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colony</a>), the queen rarely leaves the hive after the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> have become bees. She deposits each egg in a cell prepared by worker bees. The egg hatches into a small larva fed by "nurse" bees (worker bees who maintain the interior of the colony). After about a week, the larva is sealed in its cell by the nurse bees and begins its pupal stage. After another week, it emerges as an adult bee.</p>
<p>For the first ten days of their lives, female worker bees clean the hive and feed the larvae. After this, they begin building comb cells. On days 16 through 20, workers receive nectar and pollen from older workers and store it. After the 20th day, a worker leaves the hive and spends the remainder of its life as a forager. The average population of a healthy hive in midsummer may be as high as 40,000 to 80,000 bees. The larvae and <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupae</a> in a frame of honeycomb are known as "frames of <a href="/wiki/Brood_(honey_bee)" title="Brood (honey bee)">brood</a>", and are sold (with adhering bees) to start new beehives.</p>
<p>Workers and queens are fed <a href="/wiki/Royal_jelly" title="Royal jelly">royal jelly</a> during the first three days of their larval stage. Workers are then switched to a diet of pollen and nectar (or diluted honey), while queens will continue to receive royal jelly (which helps large, sexually developed larvae reach the pupal stage more quickly). Queen breeders consider good nutrition during the larval stage critically important for queen quality, with good genetics and sufficient <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a> contributing factors. During the larval and pupal stages, parasites may damage (or destroy) the pupa or larva.</p>
<p>Queens are not raised in the typical horizontal brood cells of the <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb" title="Honeycomb">honeycomb</a>. A queen cell is larger and oriented vertically. If workers sense that an old queen is weakening, they produce emergency cells (known as supersedure cells) made from cells with eggs or young larvae and which protrude from the comb. When the queen finishes her larval feeding and pupates, she moves into a head-downward position and later chews her way out of the cell. At pupation, workers cap (seal) the cell. Shortly before emerging from their cells, young queens may often be heard "piping". The queen makes this sound to evaluate her space, and piping seems to calm worker bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although worker bees are usually infertile females, when some subspecies are stressed <a href="/wiki/Laying_worker_bee" title="Laying worker bee">they may lay</a> fertile eggs. Since workers are not fully sexually developed, they do not mate with drones. Fertile eggs would be haploid (having only the genetic contribution of their mother), and these haploid eggs would always develop into drones. Worker bees secrete the <a href="/wiki/Beeswax" title="Beeswax">wax</a> used to build the hive, clean, maintain and guard it, raise the young and forage for nectar and pollen.</p>
<p>Worker honey bees have a modified <a href="/wiki/Ovipositor" title="Ovipositor">ovipositor</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stinger_(organ)" title="Stinger (organ)">stinger</a>, with which they defend the hive; unlike bees of any other genus and the queens of their own species, this stinger is barbed. Contrary to popular belief, a bee does not always die soon after stinging; this misconception is based on the fact that a bee will usually die after stinging a <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a>. The stinger and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venom_sac" title="Venom sac">venom sac</a>, with musculature and a ganglion allowing them to continue delivering <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> after they are detached, are designed to <a href="/wiki/Autotomy" title="Autotomy">pull free of the body</a> when they lodge. This apparatus (including barbs on the stinger) is thought to have evolved in response to predation by vertebrates, since the barbs do not function (and the stinger apparatus does not detach) unless the stinger is embedded in elastic material. The barbs do not always "catch", so a bee may occasionally pull its stinger free and fly off unharmed (or sting again).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_expectancy">Life expectancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life expectancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">average lifespan</a> of a queen in most subspecies is three to five years, reports from the German-European black bee subspecies previously used for beekeeping indicate that a queen can live up to eight years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Because a queen's store of sperm is depleted near the end of her life, she begins laying more unfertilized eggs; for this reason, beekeepers often replace queens every year or two.</p>
<p>The lifespan of workers varies considerably over the year in regions with long winters. Workers born in spring and summer will work hard, living only a few weeks, but those born in autumn will remain inside for several months as the colony clusters. On average during the year, about one percent of a colony's worker bees die naturally per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Except for the queen, all of a colony's workers are replaced about every four months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_caste">Social caste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social caste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Queens">Queens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Queens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Queen_bee" title="Queen bee">Queen bee</a></div>
<p>Periodically, the colony determines that a new queen is needed. There are three general causes:</p>
<ol>
<li>The hive is filled with honey, leaving little room for new eggs. This will trigger a swarm, where the old queen will take about half the worker bees to found a new colony and leave the new queen with the other half of the workers to continue the old one.</li>
<li>The old queen begins to fail, which is thought to be demonstrated by a decrease in queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honey_bee_pheromones#Types_of_queen_honeybee_pheromones" title="Honey bee pheromones">pheromones</a> throughout the hive. This is known as supersedure, and at the end of the supersedure the old queen is generally killed.</li>
<li>The old queen dies suddenly, a situation known as emergency supersedure. The worker bees find several eggs (or larvae) of the appropriate age range and attempt to develop them into queens. Emergency supersedure can generally be recognized because new queen cells are built out from comb cells, instead of hanging from the bottom of a frame.</li>
</ol>
<p>Regardless of the trigger, workers develop the larvae into queens by continuing to feed them royal jelly (which triggers extended pupal development).</p>

<p>When the virgin queen emerged, she was thought to seek out other queen cells and sting the infant queens within; should two queens emerge simultaneously, they were thought to fight to the death. The queen asserts control over the worker bees by releasing a complex suite of pheromones known as queen scent.</p>
<p>After several days of orientation in and around the hive, the young queen flies to a drone congregation pointÂ â a site near a clearing and generally about 30 feet (9.1Â m) above the groundÂ â where drones from different hives congregate. They detect the presence of a queen in their congregation area by her smell, find her by sight and mate with her in midair; drones can be induced to mate with "dummy" queens with the queen pheromone. A queen will mate multiple times, and may leave to mate several days in a row (weather permitting) until her <a href="/wiki/Spermatheca" title="Spermatheca">spermatheca</a> is full.</p>
<p>The queen lays all the eggs in a healthy colony. The number and pace of egg-laying is controlled by weather, resource availability and specific racial characteristics. Queens generally begin to slow egg-laying in the early fall, and may stop during the winter. Egg-laying generally resumes in late winter when the days lengthen, peaking in the spring. At the height of the season, the queen may lay over 2,500 eggs per day (more than her body mass).</p>
<p>She fertilizes each egg (with stored sperm from the spermatheca) as it is laid in a worker-sized cell. Eggs laid in drone-sized (larger) cells are left unfertilized; these unfertilized eggs, with half as many genes as queen or worker eggs, develop into drones.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Workers">Workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Workers are sterile females produced by the queen that develop from fertilized, diploid eggs. Workers are essential for social structure and proper colony functioning. They carry out the main tasks of the colony, because the queen is occupied with only reproducing. These females will raise their sister workers and future queens that eventually leave the nest to start their own colony. They also forage and return to the nest with nectar and pollen to feed the young.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drone_(bee)" title="Drone (bee)">Drone (bee)</a></div>
<p>Drones are the colony's <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> bees. Since they do not have ovipositors, they do not have stingers. Drone honey bees do not forage for nectar or pollen. The primary purpose of a drone is to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilize</a> a new queen. Many drones will mate with a given queen in flight; each will die immediately after mating, since the process of insemination requires a lethally convulsive effort. Drone honey bees are haploid (single, unpaired chromosomes) in their genetic structure, and are descended only from their mother (the queen). In temperate regions drones are generally expelled from the hive before winter, dying of cold and starvation since they cannot forage, produce honey or care for themselves. There has been research into the role <i>A. mellifera</i> drones play in thermoregulation within the hive. Given their larger size (1.5x), drones may play a significant role. Drones are typically located near the center of hive clusters for unclear reasons. It is postulated that it is to maintain sperm viability, which drops off at cooler temperatures. Another possible explanation is that a more central location allows drones to contribute to warmth, since at temperatures below 25C their ability to contribute declines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen-worker_conflict"><span id="Queen-Worker_Conflict"></span>Queen-worker conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Queen-worker conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Worker_policing" title="Worker policing">Worker policing</a></div>
<p>When a fertile female worker produces drones, a conflict arises between her interests and those of the queen. The worker shares half her genes with the drone and one-quarter with her brothers, favouring her offspring over those of the queen. The queen shares half her genes with her sons and one-quarter with the sons of fertile female workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This pits the worker against the queen and other workers, who try to maximize their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_fitness" title="Reproductive fitness">reproductive fitness</a> by rearing the offspring most related to them. This relationship leads to a phenomenon known as "worker policing". In these rare situations, other worker bees in the hive who are genetically more related to the queen's sons than those of the fertile workers will patrol the hive and remove worker-laid eggs. Another form of worker-based policing is aggression toward fertile females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Some studies have suggested a queen pheromone which may help workers distinguish worker- and queen-laid eggs, but others indicate egg viability as the key factor in eliciting the behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Worker policing is an example of <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">forced altruism</a>, where the benefits of worker reproduction are minimized and that of rearing the queen's offspring maximized.</p>
<p>In very rare instances workers subvert the policing mechanisms of the hive, laying eggs which are removed at a lower rate by other workers; this is known as anarchic syndrome. Anarchic workers can activate their ovaries at a higher rate and contribute a greater proportion of males to the hive. Although an increase in the number of drones would decrease the overall productivity of the hive, the reproductive fitness of the drones' mother would increase. Anarchic syndrome is an example of selection working in opposite directions at the <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_selection" title="Unit of selection">individual and group levels</a> for the stability of the hive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under ordinary circumstances the death (or removal) of a queen increases reproduction in workers, and a significant proportion of workers will have active ovaries in the absence of a queen. The workers of the hive produce a last batch of drones before the hive eventually collapses. Although during this period worker policing is usually absent, in certain groups of bees it continues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the strategy of <a href="/wiki/Kin_selection" title="Kin selection">kin selection</a>, worker policing is not favored if a queen does not mate multiple times. Workers would be related by three-quarters of their genes, and the difference in <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_relationship" title="Coefficient of relationship">relationship</a> between sons of the queen and those of the other workers would decrease. The benefit of policing is negated, and policing is less favored. Experiments confirming this hypothesis have shown a correlation between higher mating rates and increased rates of worker policing in many species of social <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">hymenoptera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoregulation">Thermoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Thermoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1133" data-file-width="1504" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg/220px-Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg/330px-Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg/440px-Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apis_mellifera_Luc_Viatour.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
foraging honey bee</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The honey bee needs an internal body temperature of 35Â Â°C (95Â Â°F) to fly; this temperature is maintained in the nest to develop the brood, and is the optimal temperature for the creation of wax. The temperature on the periphery of the cluster varies with outside air temperature, and the <a href="/wiki/Winter_cluster" title="Winter cluster">winter cluster</a>'s internal temperature may be as low as 20â22Â Â°C (68â72Â Â°F).</p>
<p>Honey bees can forage over a 30Â Â°C (86Â Â°F) air-temperature range because of behavioral and physiological mechanisms for regulating the temperature of their flight muscles. From low to high air temperatures, the mechanisms are: shivering before flight, and stopping flight for additional shivering; passive body-temperature regulation based on work, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a> from regurgitated honey-sac contents. Body temperatures differ, depending on caste and expected foraging rewards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The optimal air temperature for <a href="/wiki/Forage_(honey_bee)" title="Forage (honey bee)">foraging</a> is 22â25Â Â°C (72â77Â Â°F). During flight, the bee's relatively large flight muscles create heat which must dissipate. The honey bee uses evaporative cooling to release heat through its mouth. Under hot conditions, heat from the thorax is dissipated through the head; the bee regurgitates a droplet of warm internal fluidÂ â a "honeycrop droplet"Â â which reduces the temperature of its head by 10Â Â°C (18Â Â°F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Below 7â10Â Â°C (45â50Â Â°F) bees are immobile, and above 38Â Â°C (100Â Â°F) their activity slows. Honey bees can tolerate temperatures up to 50Â Â°C (122Â Â°F) for short periods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication">Communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bee_learning_and_communication" title="Bee learning and communication">Bee learning and communication</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg"><img alt="Bees completely covering the base of a fallen tree" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1067" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg/170px-Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg/255px-Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg/340px-Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bee_swarm_on_fallen_tree03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A large honey-bee swarm on a fallen tree trunk</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Honey-bee behavior has been extensively studied, since bees are widespread and familiar. <a href="/wiki/Karl_von_Frisch" title="Karl von Frisch">Karl von Frisch</a>, who received the 1973 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> for his study of honey-bee communication, noticed that bees communicate with dance. Through these dances, bees communicate information regarding the distance, the situation, and the direction of a food source by the dances of the returning (honey bee) worker bee on the vertical comb of the hive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Capinera2008_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Capinera2008-20">[20]</a></sup> Honey bees direct other bees to food sources with the <a href="/wiki/Round_dance_(honey_bee)" title="Round dance (honey bee)">round dance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Waggle_dance" title="Waggle dance">waggle dance</a>. Although the round dance tells other foragers that food is within 50 metres (160Â ft) of the hive, it provides insufficient information about direction. The waggle dance, which may be vertical or horizontal, provides more detail about the distance and direction of a food source. Foragers are also thought to rely on their <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">olfactory sense</a> to help locate a food source after they are directed by the dances. Unlike <i>A. mellifera</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Apis_florea" title="Apis florea">Apis florea</a></i> do not change the precision of the waggle dance to indicate the type of site that is set as a new goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Therefore, <i>Apis mellifera</i> bees are better at conveying information than its closely related species and this further supports the notion that <i>A. mellifera</i> honeybees are more evolved than <i><a href="/wiki/Apis_florea" title="Apis florea">Apis florea</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another means of communication is the shaking signal, also known as the jerking dance, vibration dance or vibration signal. Although the shaking signal is most common in worker communication, it also appears in reproductive swarming. A worker bee vibrates its body <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Dorsal_and_ventral" title="Anatomical terms of location">dorsoventrally</a> while holding another bee with its front legs. Jacobus Biesmeijer, who examined shaking signals in a forager's life and the conditions leading to its performance, found that experienced foragers executed 92.1 percent of observed shaking signals and 64 percent of these signals were made after the discovery of a food source. About 71 percent of shaking signals occurred before the first five successful foraging flights of the day; other communication signals, such as the waggle dance, were performed more often after the first five successes. Biesmeijer demonstrated that most shakers are foragers and the shaking signal is most often executed by foraging bees on pre-foraging bees, concluding that it is a transfer message for several activities (or activity levels). Sometimes the signal increases activity, as when active bees shake inactive ones. At other times, such as the end of the day, the signal is an inhibitory mechanism. However, the shaking signal is preferentially directed towards inactive bees. All three forms of communication among honey bees are effective in foraging and task management.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pheromones">Pheromones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pheromones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honey_bee_pheromones" title="Honey bee pheromones">Honey bee pheromones</a></div>
<p>Pheromones (substances involved in chemical communication) are essential to honey-bee survival. Honey bees rely on pheromones for nearly all behaviors, including <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>, alarm, <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">defense</a>, orientation, <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a> recognition, food production and integrating colony activities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beekeeping">Beekeeping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Beekeeping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beekeeping" title="Beekeeping">Beekeeping</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apis_mellifera_(queen_and_workers).jpg"><img alt="Larger, solid-brown queen with striped workers" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1108" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg/170px-Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg/255px-Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg/340px-Apis_mellifera_%28queen_and_workers%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apis_mellifera_(queen_and_workers).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Queen bee with workers</div>

<p>The honey bee is a <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonial</a> <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> which is housed, fed and transported by beekeepers. Honey bees do not survive and reproduce individually, but as part of the colony (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Superorganism" title="Superorganism">superorganism</a>).</p>
<p>Honey bees collect flower nectar and convert it to <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, which is stored in the hive. The nectar, transported in the bees' stomachs, is converted with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Digestive_enzyme" title="Digestive enzyme">digestive enzymes</a> and storage in a honey cell for partial dehydration. Nectar and honey provide the energy for the bees' flight muscles and for heating the hive during the winter. Honey bees also collect <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, which supplies protein and fat for the <a href="/wiki/Brood_(honey_bee)" title="Brood (honey bee)">bee brood</a> to grow. Centuries of <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a> by humans have created bees which produce far more honey than the colony needs, and beekeepers (also known as apiarists) harvest the surplus honey.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG"><img alt="Many honey bees on a comb" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG/220px-Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG/330px-Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG/440px-Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Western_Honey_Bees_and_Honeycomb_Closeup.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Honey bees removed from the hive for inspection by a beekeeper</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Beekeepers provide a place for the colony to live and store honey. There are seven basic types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beehive_(beekeeping)" title="Beehive (beekeeping)">beehive</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skep" title="Skep">skeps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Langstroth_hive" title="Langstroth hive">Langstroth hives</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top-bar_hive" title="Top-bar hive">top-bar hives</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Box_hive" title="Box hive">box hives</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log_gum" title="Log gum">log gums</a>, D. E. hive, and miller hives. All U.S. states require beekeepers to use movable frames to allow bee inspectors to check the brood for disease. This allows beekeepers to keep Langstroth, top-bar and D.E. hives without special permission, granted for purposes such as museum use. The type of beehive significantly impacts colony health and wax and honey production. Modern hives also enable beekeepers to transport bees, moving from field to field as crops require pollinating (a source of income for beekeepers).</p>
<p>In cold climates, some beekeepers have kept colonies alive (with varying degrees of success) by moving them indoors for winter. While this can protect the colonies from extremes of temperature and make winter care and feeding more convenient for the beekeeper, it increases the risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases_of_the_honey_bee#Nosema" title="Diseases of the honey bee">dysentery</a> and causes an excessive buildup of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the bees' respiration. Inside wintering has been refined by Canadian beekeepers, who use large barns solely for the wintering of bees; automated ventilation systems assist in carbon-dioxide dispersal.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Honey_bees">Honey bees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Honey bees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A primary product of honey bees is more honey bees. Honey bees are bought as mated queens, in spring packages of a queen with 2 to 5 pounds (0.91 to 2.27Â kg) of bees, as nucleus colonies (which include frames of brood) and as full colonies. Commerce in bees dates to prehistory, and modern methods of producing queens and dividing colonies for increase date to the late 1800s. Bees are typically produced in temperate to tropical regions and sold to colder areas; packages of bees produced in Florida are sold to beekeepers in Michigan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollination">Pollination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pollination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M%C3%A9h_(2).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg/220px-M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg/330px-M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg/440px-M%C3%A9h_%282%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M%C3%A9h_(2).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Honey bee pollinating a flower</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Pollination_management" title="Pollination management">Pollination management</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_crop_plants_pollinated_by_bees" title="List of crop plants pollinated by bees">List of crop plants pollinated by bees</a></div>
<p>The honey bee's primary commercial value is as a <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinator</a> of crops. Although orchards and fields have increased in size, <a href="/wiki/Pollinator_decline" title="Pollinator decline">wild pollinators have dwindled</a>. In a number of regions the pollination shortage is addressed by migratory beekeepers, who supply hives during a crop bloom and move them after the blooming period. Commercial beekeepers plan their movements and wintering locations according to anticipated pollination services. At higher latitudes it is difficult (or impossible) to winter over sufficient bees, or to have them ready for early blooming plants. Much migration is seasonal, with hives wintering in warmer climates and moving to follow the bloom at higher latitudes. In <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a> pollination occurs in February, early in the <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> before local hives have built up their populations.</p>
<p>Almond <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchards</a> require two hives per <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acre</a> (2,000 mÂ² per hive) for maximum yield, and pollination is dependent on the importation of hives from warmer climates. Almond pollination (in February and March in the United States) is the largest managed pollination event in the world, requiring more than one-third of all managed honey bees in the country. Mass movements of bees are also made for apples in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. Despite honey bees' inefficiency as blueberry pollinators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> large numbers are moved to Maine because they are the only pollinators who can be easily moved and concentrated for this and other <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> crops. Bees and other insects maintain <a href="/wiki/Flower_constancy" title="Flower constancy">flower constancy</a> by transferring pollen to other <a href="/wiki/Biological_specificity" title="Biological specificity">biologically specific</a> plants;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harder2001_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harder2001-24">[24]</a></sup> this prevents flower <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigmas</a> from being clogged with pollen from other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chittka1999_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chittka1999-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Honey">Honey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Honey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">Honey</a></div>
<p>Honey is the complex substance made from nectar and sweet deposits from plants and trees which are gathered, modified and stored in the comb by honey bees. Honey is a biological mixture of inverted sugars, primarily glucose and fructose. It has antibacterial and anti fungal properties. Honey from the Western honey bee, along with the bee <i><a href="/wiki/Tetragonisca_angustula" title="Tetragonisca angustula">Tetragonisca angustula</a></i>, has specific antibacterial activity towards an infection causing bacteria, <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">Staphylococcus aureus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miorin-_Honey_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miorin-_Honey-26">[26]</a></sup> Honey will not rot or ferment when stored under normal conditions, however it will crystallize over time. Although crystallized honey is acceptable for human use, bees can only use liquid honey and will remove and discard crystallized honey from the hive.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bee1web.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bee1web.jpg/220px-Bee1web.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bee1web.jpg/330px-Bee1web.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bee1web.jpg/440px-Bee1web.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bee1web.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Honey bee with "tongue" partially extended</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bees produce <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> by collecting <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a>, a clear liquid consisting of nearly 80 percent water and complex sugars. The collecting bees store the nectar in a second <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> and return to the hive, where worker bees remove the nectar. The worker bees digest the raw nectar for about 30 minutes, using <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> to break down the complex sugars into simpler ones. Raw honey is then spread in empty honeycomb cells to dry, reducing its water content to less than 20 percent. When nectar is being processed, honey bees create a draft through the hive by fanning with their <a href="/wiki/Insect_wing" title="Insect wing">wings</a>. When the honey has dried, the honeycomb cells are sealed (capped) with wax to preserve it.</p>
<p>When a hive detects smoke, many bees become nonaggressive; this is thought to be a defense mechanism. Wild colonies generally live in hollow trees; when they detect smoke, they are thought to prepare to evacuate from a forest fire with as much food as they can. To do this, they go to the nearest honey-storage cells and gorge on honey. In this state they are docile, since defending against predation is less important than saving as much food as possible.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beeswax">Beeswax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Beeswax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beeswax" title="Beeswax">Beeswax</a></div>
<p>Mature worker bees secrete beeswax from glands on their abdomen, using it to form the walls and caps of the comb. When honey is harvested, the wax can be collected for use in products like <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seals</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollen">Pollen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Pollen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">Pollen</a></div>
<p>Bees collect pollen in a <a href="/wiki/Pollen_basket" title="Pollen basket">pollen basket</a> and carry it back to the hive, where it is a <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> source for brood-rearing. Excess pollen can be collected from the hive; although it is sometimes consumed as a dietary supplement by humans, bee pollen may cause an allergic reaction in susceptible individuals.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Propolis">Propolis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Propolis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Propolis" title="Propolis">Propolis</a></div>
<p>Propolis is a resinous mixture collected by honey bees from tree buds, sap flows or other botanical sources, which is used as a sealant for unwanted open spaces in the hive. Although propolis is alleged to have health benefits (tincture of Propolis is marketed as a cold and flu remedy), it may cause severe allergic reactions in some individuals. Propolis is also used in wood finishes, and gives a <a href="/wiki/Stradivarius" title="Stradivarius">Stradivarius</a> violin its unique red color.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_jelly">Royal jelly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Royal jelly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Royal_jelly" title="Royal jelly">Royal jelly</a></div>
<p>Royal jelly is a honey-bee secretion used to nourish the larvae. It is marketed for its alleged health benefits. On the other hand, it may cause severe allergic reactions in some individuals.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genome">Genome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Genome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Honey_Bee_Genome_Sequencing_Consortium" title="Honey Bee Genome Sequencing Consortium">Honey Bee Genome Sequencing Consortium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subspecies_of_Apis_mellifera" title="Subspecies of Apis mellifera">Subspecies of Apis mellifera</a></div>
<p>As of October 28, 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Honey_Bee_Genome_Sequencing_Consortium" title="Honey Bee Genome Sequencing Consortium">Honey Bee Genome Sequencing Consortium</a> fully sequenced and analyzed the genome of <i>Apis mellifera</i>. Since 2007, attention has been devoted to <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a>, a decline in European honey bee colonies in a number of regions.</p>
<p>The European honey bee is the third insect, after the <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">fruit fly</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a>, to have its genome mapped. According to scientists who analyzed its genetic code, the honey bee originated in Africa and spread to Europe in two ancient migrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_W._Whitfield.2C_Susanta_K._Behura_.2C_Stewart_H._Berlocher.2C_Andrew_G._Clark.2C_J._Spencer_Johnston.2C_Walter_S._Sheppard.2C_Deborah_R._Smith.2C_Andrew_V._Suarez.2C_Daniel_Weaver_.26_Neil_D._Tsutsui_2006_642.E2.80.93645-3">[3]</a></sup> Scientists have found that genes related to smell outnumber those for taste, and the European honey bee has fewer genes regulating immunity than the fruit fly and the mosquito.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genome_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-genome-27">[27]</a></sup> The genome sequence also revealed that several groups of genes, particularly those related to <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>, resembled those of vertebrates more than other insects. Another significant finding from the honey bee genome study was that honey bee was the first insect to be discovered with a functional DNA methylation system because functional key enzymes (DNA methyl-transferase 1 and 3) were identified in the genome. DNA methylation is one of the important mechanisms in epigenetics to study gene expression and regulation without changing the DNA sequence, but modifications on the DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> DNA methylation later was identified to play an important role in gene regulation and gene alternative splicing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The genome is unusual in having few <a href="/wiki/Transposable_element" title="Transposable element">transposable elements</a>, although they were present in the evolutionary past (inactive remains have been found) and evolved more slowly than those in fly species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genome_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-genome-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards_and_survival">Hazards and survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hazards and survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>European honey-bee populations face threats to their survival, increasing interests in other pollinator species like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_eastern_bumblebee" title="Common eastern bumblebee">B. impatiens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> and European populations were severely depleted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases_of_the_honey_bee#Varroa_mites" title="Diseases of the honey bee">varroa-mite</a> infestations during the early 1990s, and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> beekeepers were further affected by <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a> in 2006 and 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovgren_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lovgren-31">[31]</a></sup> Improved cultural practices and chemical treatments against varroa mites saved most commercial operations; new bee breeds are beginning to reduce beekeeper dependence on <a href="/wiki/Acaricide" title="Acaricide">acaricides</a>. Feral bee populations were greatly reduced during this period; they are slowly recovering, primarily in mild climates, due to <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> for <i>varroa</i> resistance and repopulation by resistant breeds. <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_toxicity_to_bees" title="Pesticide toxicity to bees">Insecticides</a>, particularly when used in excess of label directions, have also depleted bee populations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as bee pests and diseases (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases_of_the_honey_bee#American_foulbrood" title="Diseases of the honey bee">American foulbrood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases_of_the_honey_bee#Acarine_mites_.28Tracheal_mites.29" title="Diseases of the honey bee">tracheal mites</a>) are becoming resistant to medications.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_hazards">Environmental hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Environmental hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Honey_Bee_takes_Nectar.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_honey_bee" title="African honey bee">African honey bee</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> extracts nectar from a flower, as pollen grains stick to its body</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Africanized_bee" title="Africanized bee">Africanized bees</a> have spread across the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where they pose a slight danger to humans (making beekeepingâparticularly hobby beekeepingâdifficult). As an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, feral honey bees have become a significant environmental problem in non-native areas. Imported bees may displace native bees and birds, and may also promote the reproduction of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive plants</a> ignored by native pollinators. Unlike native bees, they do not properly extract or transfer pollen from plants with <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">pore anthers</a> (anthers which only release pollen through tiny apical pores); this requires <a href="/wiki/Buzz_pollination" title="Buzz pollination">buzz pollination</a>, a behavior rarely exhibited by honey bees. Honey bees reduce fruiting in <i><a href="/wiki/Melastoma_affine" title="Melastoma affine">Melastoma affine</a></i>, a plant with pore anthers, by robbing its stigmas of previously deposited pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insect predators of honeybees include the <a href="/wiki/Asian_giant_hornet" title="Asian giant hornet">Asian giant hornet</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asilidae" title="Asilidae">robber flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Green_darner" title="Green darner">green darner</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_beewolf" title="European beewolf">European beewolf</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praying_mantis" title="Praying mantis">Praying mantises</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_strider" title="Water strider">water strider</a>.</p>
<p>Arachnid predators of honeybees include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_spider" title="Fishing spider">fishing spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynx_spider" title="Lynx spider">lynx spiders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misumena" title="Misumena">goldenrod spiders</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_Cross_spider" title="St Andrew's Cross spider">St Andrew's Cross spider</a>.</p>
<p>Reptile and amphibian predators of honeybees include the <a href="/wiki/American_toad" title="American toad">American toad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anolis" title="Anolis">Anole lizards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_bullfrog" title="American bullfrog">American bullfrog</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_Frog" title="Wood Frog">wood Frog</a>.</p>
<p>Specialist bird predators include the <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eaters</a>; other birds that may take bees include <a href="/wiki/Grackle" title="Grackle">grackles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hummingbirds" title="Hummingbirds">hummingbirds</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrant_flycatchers" title="Tyrant flycatchers">tyrant flycatchers</a>.</p>
<p>Mammals that sometimes take bees include <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_shrew" title="Least shrew">least shrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">opossums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratel" title="Ratel">honey badgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Close_Relatives">Close Relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_honey_bee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Close Relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from <i>Apis mellifera,</i> there are 6 other species in the genus <i>Apis</i>. These are <i><a href="/wiki/Apis_andreniformis" title="Apis andreniformis">Apis andreniformis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apis_florea" title="Apis florea">Apis florea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apis_dorsata" title="Apis dorsata">Apis dorsata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apis_cerana" title="Apis cerana">Apis cerana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apis_koschevnikovi" title="Apis koschevnikovi">Apis koschevnikovi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apis_nigrocincta" title="Apis nigrocincta">Apis nigrocincta</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These other species all originated in South and Southeast Asia. Only <i>Apis mellifera</i> is thought to have originated in Europe, Asia, and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
