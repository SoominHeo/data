<p><b>Anarcha-feminism</b>, also called <b>anarchist feminism</b> and <b>anarcho-feminism</b>, combines <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>. It generally views <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a> as a manifestation of involuntary <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercive</a> <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>, that should be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralized</a> <a href="/wiki/Free_association_(communism_and_anarchism)" title="Free association (communism and anarchism)">free association</a>. Anarcha-feminists believe that the struggle against patriarchy is an essential part of <a href="/wiki/Class_conflict" title="Class conflict">class conflict</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> struggle against the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>. In essence, the philosophy sees anarchist struggle as a necessary component of feminist struggle and vice versa. <a href="/wiki/L._Susan_Brown" title="L. Susan Brown">L. Susan Brown</a> claims that, "as anarchism is a political philosophy that opposes all relationships of power, it is inherently feminist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contrary to popular belief and contemporary association with <a href="/wiki/Radical_feminism" title="Radical feminism">radical feminism</a>, anarcha-feminism is not an inherently <a href="/wiki/Militant" title="Militant">militant</a> party. It is described to be an <a href="/wiki/Anti-authoritarianism" title="Anti-authoritarianism">anti-authoritarianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-capitalism" title="Anti-capitalism">anti-capitalism</a>, anti-oppressive philosophy, with the goal of creating an "equal ground" between males and females. The term "anarcha-feminism" suggests the social freedom and liberty of women, without needed dependence upon other groups or parties.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin" title="Mikhail Bakunin">Mikhail Bakunin</a> opposed <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a> and the way the law "[subjected women] to the absolute domination of the man." He argued that "[e]qual rights must belong to men and women" so that women could "become independent and be free to forge their own way of life." Bakunin foresaw the end of "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authoritarian" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juridical" title="Juridical">juridical</a> <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>" and "the full sexual freedom of women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup> (<i>Bakunin on Anarchism</i>, p. 396 and p. 397).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CITESHORT" title="Wikipedia:CITESHORT"><span title="More information is required to link this short citation to its long citation. (January 2014)">incomplete short citation</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon" title="Pierre-Joseph Proudhon">Proudhon</a>, on the other hand, viewed the <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> as the most basic unit of society and of his morality and believed that women had the responsibility of fulfilling a traditional role within the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1860s, anarchism's radical critique of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and the state has been combined with a critique of patriarchy. Anarcha-feminists thus start from the precept that modern society is dominated by men. Authoritarian traits and values—domination, exploitation, aggression, competition, etc.—are integral to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierarchical" title="Hierarchical">hierarchical</a> civilizations and are seen as "masculine." In contrast, non-authoritarian traits and values—cooperation, sharing, compassion, sensitivity—are regarded as "feminine," and devalued. Anarcha-feminists have thus espoused creation of a non-authoritarian, anarchist society. They refer to the creation of a society, based on <a href="/wiki/Cooperation" title="Cooperation">cooperation</a>, sharing, <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Aid:_A_Factor_of_Evolution" title="Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution">mutual aid</a>, etc. as the "<a href="/wiki/Feminization_(sociology)" title="Feminization (sociology)">feminization</a> of society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anarcha-feminism began with late 19th and early 20th century authors and theorists such as anarchist feminists <a href="/wiki/Emma_Goldman" title="Emma Goldman">Emma Goldman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voltairine_de_Cleyre" title="Voltairine de Cleyre">Voltairine de Cleyre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Parsons" title="Lucy Parsons">Lucy Parsons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, an anarcha-feminist group, <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Libres" title="Mujeres Libres">Mujeres Libres</a></span> ("Free Women"), linked to the <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a href="/wiki/Federaci%C3%B3n_Anarquista_Ib%C3%A9rica" title="Federación Anarquista Ibérica">Federación Anarquista Ibérica</a></span>, organized to defend both anarchist and feminist ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stirner" title="Stirner">Stirnerist</a> <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_and_Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Anarchism and Friedrich Nietzsche">Nietzschean</a> feminist <a href="/wiki/Federica_Montseny" title="Federica Montseny">Federica Montseny</a> held that the "emancipation of women would lead to a quicker realization of the social revolution" and that "the revolution against sexism would have to come from intellectual and militant 'future-women.'" According to this Nietzschean concept of Federica Montseny's, women could "realize through art and literature the need to revise their own roles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anarchonietzche_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-anarchonietzche-7">[7]</a></sup> In China, the anarcho-feminist <a href="/wiki/He_Zhen_(anarchist)" title="He Zhen (anarchist)">He Zhen</a> argued that without women's liberation, society could not be liberated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu201353_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu201353-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia_Bolten_and_La_Voz_de_la_Mujer">Virginia Bolten and <i>La Voz de la Mujer</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Virginia Bolten and La Voz de la Mujer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="107" data-file-width="358" height="66" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg/220px-8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg/330px-8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e1/8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:8La_Voz_de_la_Mujer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">La Voz de la Mujer</span></i>, pioneering Argentinian anarcha-feminist publication</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Argentina, <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Bolten" title="Virginia Bolten">Virginia Bolten</a> is responsible for the publication of a newspaper called <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a href="/wiki/La_Voz_de_la_Mujer" title="La Voz de la Mujer">La Voz de la Mujer</a></span></i> (English: The Woman's Voice), which was published nine times in Rosario between January 8, 1896 and January 1, 1897, and was briefly revived in 1901. A similar paper with the same name was reportedly published later in <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>, which suggests that Bolten may also have founded and edited it after her deportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-molyneux_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-molyneux-9">[9]</a></sup> "La Voz de la Mujer" described itself as “dedicated to the advancement of Communist Anarchism.” Its central theme was the multiple natures of women’s oppression. An editorial asserted, “We believe that in present-day society, nothing and nobody has a more wretched situation than unfortunate women.” They said that women were doubly oppressed by both bourgeois society and men. Its beliefs can be seen from its attack on marriage and upon male power over women. Its contributors, like anarchist feminists elsewhere, developed a concept of oppression that focused on gender. They saw marriage as a bourgeois institution which restricted women’s freedom, including their sexual freedom. Marriages entered into without love, fidelity maintained through fear rather than desire, and oppression of women by men they hated were all seen as symptomatic of the coercion implied by the marriage contract. It was this alienation of the individual’s will that the anarchist feminists deplored and sought to remedy, initially through free love, and then more thoroughly through social revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anarcha-feminism.2C_individualist_anarchism_and_the_free_love_movement">Anarcha-feminism, individualist anarchism and the free love movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anarcha-feminism, individualist anarchism and the free love movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism" title="Individualist anarchism">Individualist anarchism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">Free love</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>An important topic within individualist anarchism is <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">free love</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freelove_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-freelove-11">[11]</a></sup> Free love advocates sometimes traced their roots back to <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Warren" title="Josiah Warren">Josiah Warren</a> and to experimental communities, which viewed sexual freedom as a clear, direct expression of an individual's self-ownership. Free love particularly stressed <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a> since most sexual laws discriminated against women, such as marriage laws and anti-birth control measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freelove_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-freelove-11">[11]</a></sup> The most important American free love journal was <i><a href="/wiki/Lucifer_the_Lightbearer" title="Lucifer the Lightbearer">Lucifer the Lightbearer</a></i> (1883–1907), edited by <a href="/wiki/Moses_Harman" title="Moses Harman">Moses Harman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lois_Waisbrooker" title="Lois Waisbrooker">Lois Waisbrooker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Heywood" title="Ezra Heywood">Ezra</a> and Angela Heywood's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Word_(free_love)" title="The Word (free love)"><i>The Word</i></a></i> was also published from 1872–1890 and in 1892–1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freelove_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-freelove-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/M._E._Lazarus" title="M. E. Lazarus">M. E. Lazarus</a> was also an important American individualist anarchist who promoted free love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freelove_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-freelove-11">[11]</a></sup> In Europe, the main propagandist of free love within individualist anarchism was <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Armand" title="Émile Armand">Émile Armand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armandfreelove_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-armandfreelove-13">[13]</a></sup> He proposed the concept of <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">la camaraderie amoureuse</span></i> - to speak of free love as the possibility of voluntary sexual encounter between consenting adults. He was also a consistent proponent of <a href="/wiki/Polyamory" title="Polyamory">polyamory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armandfreelove_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-armandfreelove-13">[13]</a></sup> In France there was also feminist activity inside <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism_in_Europe#France" title="Individualist anarchism in Europe">French individualist anarchism</a> as promoted by individualist feminists Marie Küge, Anna Mahé, <a href="/wiki/Rirette_Ma%C3%AEtrejean" title="Rirette Maîtrejean">Rirette Maîtrejean</a>, and Sophia Zaïkovska.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism" title="Individualist anarchism">individualist anarchist</a> <a href="/wiki/Maria_Lacerda_de_Moura" title="Maria Lacerda de Moura">Maria Lacerda de Moura</a> lectured on topics such as education, <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">free love</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antimilitarism" title="Antimilitarism">antimilitarism</a>. Her writings and essays landed her attention not only in Brazil, but also in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>. In February 1923 she launched <i><span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">Renascença</span></i>, a periodical linked with the anarchist, <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Freethought" title="Freethought">freethinking</a> circles of the period. Her thought was mainly influenced by individualist anarchists such as <a href="/wiki/Han_Ryner" title="Han Ryner">Han Ryner</a> and Émile Armand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voltairine_de_Cleyre">Voltairine de Cleyre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voltairine de Cleyre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Voltairine_de_Cleyre" title="Voltairine de Cleyre">Voltairine de Cleyre</a></div>
<p>Voltairine de Cleyre (November 17, 1866 – June 20, 1912) was an <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_the_United_States" title="Anarchism in the United States">American anarchist</a> writer and feminist. She was a prolific writer and speaker, opposing the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>, marriage, and the domination of religion in sexuality and women's lives. She began her activist career in the <a href="/wiki/Freethought" title="Freethought">freethought movement</a>. De Cleyre was initially drawn to <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism" title="Individualist anarchism">individualist anarchism</a> but evolved through <a href="/wiki/Mutualism_(economic_theory)" title="Mutualism (economic theory)">mutualism</a> to an "<a href="/wiki/Anarchism_without_adjectives" title="Anarchism without adjectives">anarchism without adjectives</a>." She was a colleague of <a href="/wiki/Emma_Goldman" title="Emma Goldman">Emma Goldman</a>, with whom she respectfully disagreed with on many issues. Many of her essays were in the <i>Collected Works of Voltairine de Cleyre</i>, published posthumously by <i><a href="/wiki/Mother_Earth_(magazine)" title="Mother Earth (magazine)">Mother Earth</a></i> in 1914. In her 1895 lecture entitled <i>Sex Slavery,</i> de Cleyre condemns ideals of beauty that encourage women to distort their bodies and child socialization practices that create unnatural <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a>. The title of the essay refers not to traffic in women for purposes of <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>, although that is also mentioned, but rather to marriage laws that allow men to <a href="/wiki/Marital_rape" title="Marital rape">rape</a> their wives without consequences. Such laws make "every married woman what she is, a bonded slave, who takes her master's name, her master's bread, her master's commands, and serves her master's passions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emma_Goldman">Emma Goldman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emma Goldman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Emma_Goldman" title="Emma Goldman">Emma Goldman</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1236" data-file-width="879" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg/180px-Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg/270px-Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg/360px-Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emma_Goldman_seated.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Although she was hostile to <a href="/wiki/First-wave_feminism" title="First-wave feminism">first-wave feminism</a> and its suffragist goals, <a href="/wiki/Emma_Goldman" title="Emma Goldman">Emma Goldman</a> advocated passionately for the rights of women, and is today heralded as a founder of anarcha-feminism. In 1897 she wrote: "I demand the independence of woman, her right to support herself; to live for herself; to love whomever she pleases, or as many as she pleases. I demand freedom for both sexes, freedom of action, freedom in love and freedom in motherhood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A nurse by training, Goldman was an early advocate for educating women about <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a>. Like many contemporary feminists, she saw <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> as a tragic consequence of social conditions, and birth control as a positive alternative. Goldman was also an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">free love</a>, and a strong critic of <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>. She saw early feminists as confined in their scope and bounded by social forces of <a href="/wiki/Religious_fanaticism" title="Religious fanaticism">Puritanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>. She wrote: "We are in need of unhampered growth out of old traditions and habits. The movement for women's emancipation has so far made but the first step in that direction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Sanger" title="Margaret Sanger">Margaret Sanger</a>, an advocate of access to birth control, coined the term "birth control" and disseminated information about various methods in the June 1914 issue of her magazine <i>The Woman Rebel</i>, she received aggressive support from Goldman. Sanger was arrested in August under the <a href="/wiki/Comstock_laws" title="Comstock laws">Comstock laws</a>, which prohibited the dissemination of "obscene, lewd, or lascivious articles"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>—including information relating to birth control. Although they later split from Sanger over charges of insufficient support, Goldman and Reitman distributed copies of Sanger's pamphlet <i>Family Limitation</i> (along with a similar essay of Reitman's). In 1915, Goldman conducted a nationwide speaking tour in part to raise awareness about contraception options. Although the nation's attitude toward the topic seemed to be liberalizing, Goldman was arrested in February 1916 and charged with violation of the Comstock Law. Refusing to pay a $100 fine, she spent two weeks in a prison workhouse, which she saw as an "opportunity" to reconnect with those rejected by society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goldman was also an outspoken critic of <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">prejudice against homosexuals</a>. Her belief that social liberation should extend to gay men and lesbians was virtually unheard of at the time, even among anarchists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Hirschfeld" title="Magnus Hirschfeld">Magnus Hirschfeld</a> wrote, "she was the first and only woman, indeed the first and only American, to take up the defense of homosexual love before the general public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hirschfeld_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-hirschfeld-23">[23]</a></sup> In numerous speeches and letters, she defended the right of gay men and lesbians to love as they pleased and condemned the fear and stigma associated with homosexuality. As Goldman wrote in a letter to Hirschfeld, "It is a tragedy, I feel, that people of a different sexual type are caught in a world which shows so little understanding for homosexuals and is so crassly indifferent to the various gradations and variations of gender and their great significance in life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hirschfeld_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-hirschfeld-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milly_Witkop">Milly Witkop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Milly Witkop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milly_Witkop" title="Milly Witkop">Milly Witkop</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Milly_Witkop" title="Milly Witkop">Milly Witkop</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukrainian</a>-born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-syndicalism" title="Anarcho-syndicalism">anarcho-syndicalist</a><a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">, feminist</a> writer and activist. She was the <a href="/wiki/Common-law_marriage" title="Common-law marriage">common-law wife</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Rocker" title="Rudolf Rocker">Rudolf Rocker</a>. In November 1918, Witkop and Rocker moved to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>; Rocker had been invited by <a href="/wiki/Free_Association_of_German_Trade_Unions" title="Free Association of German Trade Unions">Free Association of German Trade Unions</a> (FVdG) chairman <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kater" title="Fritz Kater">Fritz Kater</a> to join him in building up what would become the <a href="/wiki/Free_Workers%27_Union_of_Germany" title="Free Workers' Union of Germany">Free Workers' Union of Germany</a> (FAUD), an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarcho-syndicalist" title="Anarcho-syndicalist">anarcho-syndicalist</a> trade union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Both Rocker and Witkop became members of the FAUD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolf_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf-25">[25]</a></sup> After its founding in early 1919, a discussion about the role of girls and women in the union started. The male-dominated organization had at first ignored gender issues, but soon women started founding their own unions, which were organized parallel to the regular unions, but still formed part of the FAUD. Witkop was one of the leading founders of the Women's Union in Berlin in 1920. On October 15, 1921, the women's unions held a national congress in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Syndicalist_Women%27s_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Syndicalist Women's Union (page does not exist)">Syndicalist Women's Union</a> (SFB) was founded on a national level. Shortly thereafter, Witkop drafted <i>Was will der Syndikalistische Frauenbund?</i> (<i>What Does the Syndicalist Women's Union Want?</i>) as a platform for the SFB. From 1921, the <i>Frauenbund</i> was published as a supplement to the FAUD organ <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Syndikalist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Der Syndikalist (page does not exist)">Der Syndikalist</a></i>, Witkop was one of its primary writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolf_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Witkop reasoned that proletarian women were exploited not only by capitalism like male workers, but also by their male counterparts. She contended therefore that women must actively fight for their rights, much like workers must fight capitalism for theirs. She also insisted on the necessity of women taking part in class struggle. Housewives could use boycotts to support this struggle. From this, she concluded the necessity of an autonomous women's organization in the FAUD. Witkop also held that domestic work should be deemed equally valuable to wage labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mujeres_Libres"><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Mujeres Libres</span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mujeres Libres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Libres" title="Mujeres Libres">Mujeres Libres</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="336" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg/200px-Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg/300px-Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luic_San_Saorn_1933.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Luc%C3%ADa_S%C3%A1nchez_Saornil" title="Lucía Sánchez Saornil">Lucía Sánchez Saornil</a>, leader of <a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Libres" title="Mujeres Libres">Mujeres Libres</a> in 1933</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Mujeres Libres</span> (English: Free Women) was an <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> women's organization in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> that aimed to empower working class women. It was founded in 1936 by <a href="/wiki/Luc%C3%ADa_S%C3%A1nchez_Saornil" title="Lucía Sánchez Saornil">Lucía Sánchez Saornil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_Comaposada" title="Mercedes Comaposada">Mercedes Comaposada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amparo_Poch_y_Gasc%C3%B3n" title="Amparo Poch y Gascón">Amparo Poch y Gascón</a> and had approximately 30,000 members. The organization was based on the idea of a "double struggle" for <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">women's liberation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolution</a> and argued that the two objectives were equally important and should be pursued in parallel. In order to gain mutual support, they created networks of women anarchists. Flying day-care centres were set up in efforts to involve more women in union activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organization also produced propaganda through radio, traveling libraries and propaganda tours, in order to promote their cause. Organizers and activists traveled through rural parts of Spain to set up rural collectives and support for women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WS54_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-WS54-28">[28]</a></sup> To prepare women for leadership roles in the anarchist movement, they organized schools, women-only social groups and a women-only newspaper to help women gain <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a> and confidence in their abilities and network with one another to develop their <a href="/wiki/Political_consciousness" title="Political consciousness">political consciousness</a>. Many of the female workers in Spain were illiterate and the Mujeres Libres sought to educate them through literacy programs, technically oriented classes, and social studies classes. Schools were also created for train nurses to help injured in emergency medical clinics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WS54_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-WS54-28">[28]</a></sup> Medical classes also provided women with information on sexual health and pre and post-natal care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WS54_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-WS54-28">[28]</a></sup> The Mujeres Libres also created a woman run magazine to keep all of its members informed. The first monthly issue of Mujeres Libres was published on May 20, 1936 (ack 100). However the magazine only had 14 issues. The last issue was still being printed when the civil war battlefront reached Barcelona, and no copies survived. The magazine addressed working class women and focused on “awakening the female conscience toward libertarian ideas.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Luc.C3.ADa_S.C3.A1nchez_Saornil">Lucía Sánchez Saornil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lucía Sánchez Saornil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Lucía Sánchez Saornil (December 13, 1895 – June 2, 1970), was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> poet, militant <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a>. She is best known as one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Libres" title="Mujeres Libres">Mujeres Libres</a>. She served in the <a href="/wiki/Confederaci%C3%B3n_Nacional_del_Trabajo" title="Confederación Nacional del Trabajo">Confederación Nacional del Trabajo</a> (CNT) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solidaridad_Internacional_Antifascista&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solidaridad Internacional Antifascista (page does not exist)">Solidaridad Internacional Antifascista</a> (SIA). By 1919, she had been published in a variety of journals, including <i>Los Quijotes</i>, <i>Tableros</i>, <i>Plural</i>, <i>Manantial</i> and <i>La Gaceta Literaria</i>. Working under a male <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a>, she was able to explore <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> themes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> at a time when homosexuality was criminalized and subject to <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> and punishment. Writing in anarchist publications such as <i>Earth and Freedom</i>, the <i>White Magazine</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Solidaridad_Obrera_(periodical)" title="Solidaridad Obrera (periodical)">Workers' Solidarity</a></i>, Lucía outlined her perspective as a feminist. Although quiet on the subject of <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a>, she attacked the essentialism of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_roles" title="Gender roles">gender roles</a> in Spanish society. In this way, Lucía established herself as one of the most radical of voices among anarchist women, rejecting the ideal of female domesticity which remained largely unquestioned. In a series of articles for <i>Workers' Solidarity</i>, she boldly refuted <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Mara%C3%B1%C3%B3n" title="Gregorio Marañón">Gregorio Marañón</a>'s identification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motherhood" title="Motherhood">motherhood</a> as the nucleus of female identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_developments">Contemporary developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anarcha-feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contemporary developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An important aspect of anarcha-feminism is its opposition to traditional concepts of family, education and <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The institution of marriage is one of the most widely opposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> De Cleyre argued that marriage stifled individual growth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and Goldman argued that it "is primarily an economic arrangement... [woman] pays for it with her name, her privacy, her self-respect, her very life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Anarcha-feminists have also argued for non-hierarchical family and educational structures, and had a prominent role in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_School_(United_States)" title="Modern School (United States)">Modern School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, based on the ideas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesc_Ferrer_i_Gu%C3%A0rdia" title="Francesc Ferrer i Guàrdia">Francesc Ferrer i Guàrdia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>"The Fine Art of Labeling: The Convergence of Anarchism, Feminism, and Bisexuality", by Lucy Friedland and Liz Highleyman, is a piece in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bi_Any_Other_Name:_Bisexual_People_Speak_Out" title="Bi Any Other Name: Bisexual People Speak Out">Bi Any Other Name: Bisexual People Speak Out</a></i> (1991), an <a href="/wiki/Anthology" title="Anthology">anthology</a> edited by <a href="/wiki/Loraine_Hutchins" title="Loraine Hutchins">Loraine Hutchins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lani_Ka%27ahumanu" title="Lani Ka'ahumanu">Lani Ka'ahumanu</a> which is one of the seminal books<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> in the history of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisexual" title="Bisexual">bisexual</a> rights movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanikaahumanu1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-lanikaahumanu1-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In English-speaking anarcha-feminist circles in the United States, the term "manarchist" emerged as a pejorative label for male anarchists who are dismissive of feminist concerns, who are overtly antifeminist, or who behave in ways regarded as patriarchal and misogynistic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The term was used in the 2001 article "Stick it To The Manarchy" by the Rock Bloc Collective in the Onward Newspaper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and later in a 2001 questionnaire, "Are You a Manarchist?".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contemporary anarcha-feminism has been noted for its heavy influence on <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a>."Ecofeminists rightly note that except for anarcha-feminist, no feminist perspective has recognized the importance of healing the nature/culture division."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Current anarcha-feminist groups include <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>'s <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Creando" title="Mujeres Creando">Mujeres Creando</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Radical_cheerleading" title="Radical cheerleading">radical cheerleaders</a>, the Spanish anarcha-feminist <a href="/wiki/Squatting" title="Squatting">squat</a> <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">La <a href="/wiki/Eskalera_Karakola" title="Eskalera Karakola">Eskalera Karakola</a></span>, and the annual <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">La Rivolta!</span> conference in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Contemporary anarcha-feminist writers/theorists include <a href="/wiki/Maria_Mies" title="Maria Mies">Maria Mies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Kornegger" title="Peggy Kornegger">Peggy Kornegger</a>, <a href="/wiki/L._Susan_Brown" title="L. Susan Brown">L. Susan Brown</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">eco-feminist</a> <a href="/wiki/Starhawk" title="Starhawk">Starhawk</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Post-left_anarchy" title="Post-left anarchy">post-left anarchist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">anarcho-primitivist</a> Lilith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the past decades two films have been produced about anarcha-feminism. <i><a href="/wiki/Libertarias" title="Libertarias">Libertarias</a></i> is a historical drama made in 1996 about the Spanish anarcha-feminist organization <a href="/wiki/Mujeres_Libres" title="Mujeres Libres">Mujeres Libres</a>. In 2010 the argentinian film <i>Ni dios, ni patrón, ni marido</i> was released which is centered on the story of anarcha-feminist <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Bolten" title="Virginia Bolten">Virginia Bolten</a> and her publishing of the newspaper <i>La Voz de la Mujer</i> (English: The Woman's Voice).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
