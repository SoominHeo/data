<p><i><b>Diapensia lapponica</b></i> (<b>pincushion plant</b>) is a plant in the family <a href="/wiki/Diapensiaceae" title="Diapensiaceae">Diapensiaceae</a>, the only circumpolar species in the genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diapensia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diapensia (page does not exist)">Diapensia</a></i>, the others being mainly in the Himalaya and on mountains in southwestern China (the evolutionary heartland). This species likely became Circumboreal-circumpolar [Arcticâalpine] after it jumped to arctic habitat from North China and Russia. The most likely candidate for ancestor is a white-flowered D. purpurea (Day R.T. 2003. Diapensia on Cheju Island, South Korea and Musings on Origins. Sarracenia 11(3):29-31.) The plants grow on exposed rocky ridges that are kept free from snow by high winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Diapensia is extremely slow and low-growing and cannot compete with plants that over top it. The plant is very sensitive to higher temperatures (global warming a lethal problem in Sweden) and so is often in misty foggy habitat (R. Day thesis). It usually dies when transplanted to lowland gardens (trials in Newfoundland, by R.Day) and so this is not recommended. Cold-treated or wild and winter-collected seed will germinate indoors. The seed and leaves are high in lipids.</p>
<p>It is a small <a href="/wiki/Cushion_plant" title="Cushion plant">cushion</a>-forming <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a>, up to 15 centimetres (6Â in) in height, and can trap heat in the dome (R. Day thesis). It has oval blunt leathery toothless leaves, up to 1Â cm (0.4Â in) long, which are arranged in dense rosettes. It bears solitary white flowers (rarely pink), on stems up to 3Â cm (1.2Â in) tall.</p>
<p>It could be aged by counting growth-rings or clump diameter, and on this basis, many Canadian plants are thought to live to over a century or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> See also the thesis by R. Day at Memorial University Newfoundland.</p>
<p>In places such as Newfoundland an early June and later, (often) August blooming period occurs on different plants. Day R.T and Scott P.J. 1981. Autecological aspects of Diapensia lapponica in Newfoundland. Rhodora 83: 101-109. It is not known if this is a genetic or environmental affect. Two blooming periods are known for other plants. It often involves flower buds being formed in the present or previous year (overwintering buds)(see R. Day thesis).</p>
<p>Two subspecies are recognised:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Diapensia lapponica</i> subsp. <i>lapponica</i> in eastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, Iceland, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a></li>
<li><i>Diapensia lapponica</i> subsp. <i>obovata</i> in eastern <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> The range of these two subspecies do not meet in north central Canada, and possibly central Siberia, so they are believed to be dispersing east and west from different glacial refugia.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Britain">Status in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diapensia_lapponica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Status in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <i>Diapensia</i> is found only at a single site near <a href="/wiki/Glenfinnan" title="Glenfinnan">Glenfinnan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lochaber" title="Lochaber">Lochaber</a>, the species' most southerly site in Europe. Here, the species occurs on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic_soil" title="Acidic soil">acidic soil</a> among stones on the ridge between the summit of SgÃ¹rr an Utha and the adjoining hill called Fraoch-bheinn, at 760â780 metres (2,490â2,560Â ft) above sea-level. Its total extent at this site is less than 5,000 square metres (1.2 acres). A total of 1,200 clumps or mats have been counted, and monitoring since 1980 has not detected any change in this population.</p>
<p>The discovery of <i>Diapensia</i> took place in July 1951; C. F. Tebbutt, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birdwatcher" title="Birdwatcher">birdwatcher</a>, found the plant, recognising it as "something different".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Diapensia</i> was one of a trio of Arctic plants discovered in Scotland in the early 1950s. Although no new species to Britain had been discovered in Scotland since Victorian times, in 1950, the Arctic plant <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koenigia_islandica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koenigia islandica (page does not exist)">Koenigia islandica</a></i> had been found on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" title="Isle of Skye">Isle of Skye</a>, and in 1952, <i><a href="/wiki/Artemisia_norvegica" title="Artemisia norvegica">Artemisia norvegica</a></i> was found on <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B9l_M%C3%B2r" title="CÃ¹l MÃ²r">CÃ¹l MÃ²r</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A photograph of the plant by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Moyes_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Moyes Adam (page does not exist)">Robert Moyes Adam</a> taken on 14 June 1952 (soon after the initial discovery) is held by the St Andrews University Library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It flowers at this site in May or June, the exact time varying from year to year. Some sources state that the species is found at a second site,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but recent sources state that this is not the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiggington_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiggington-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plant is listed in the 3rd edition of the British vascular plant <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">Red Data Book</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiggington_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiggington-8">[8]</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>. It is also protected under Schedule 8 of the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>.</p>
