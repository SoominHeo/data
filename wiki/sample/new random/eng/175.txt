<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bone rank system</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>bone rank system</b> was the system of <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocratic</a> rank used in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>. It was used to segregate society, and particularly the layers of the aristocracy, on the basis of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a> proximity to the throne and the level of authority they were permitted to wield. The idea of <a href="/wiki/Royal_descent" title="Royal descent">royal blood</a> in other societies is a close analogue to the idea of "sacred bone" in Silla thought.</p>
<p>Bone rank was strictly hereditary, and thus acted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caste_system" title="Caste system">caste system</a>. The scholar, Lee Ki-Baik (1984, p. 43) considers it to have probably been adopted as part of the administrative law introduced from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and promulgated by <a href="/wiki/Beopheung_of_Silla" title="Beopheung of Silla">King Beopheung</a> in 520. However, this likely did nothing but institute in legal fact what was already a society segregated by bloodline and <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineage</a>. Although only two of the five known ranks were referred to as "bone" (골, 骨), the term "bone rank" has become widely used to describe the whole system.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_hierarchy_and_features">The hierarchy and features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bone_rank_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The hierarchy and features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla,_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="891" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg/220px-Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg/330px-Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg/440px-Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla%2C_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Relationship_of_Bone-Rank_Gradations_in_the_kingdom_of_Silla,_to_Office_Rank_and_Post.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>A person's bone rank status governed not only official status and marriage rights, but also the color of one's garments and the maximum dimensions of one's dwelling and carriage. These criteria are described in detail in the 12th century Korean history <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i>, particularly its <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">Monographs</a> (<i>ji</i> 志), book 2 (ranks and offices). The <i>Samguk Sagi'</i>s depiction of Silla life, however, has often been criticized for being excessively static. Unfortunately, since other sources are scarce, it is difficult to judge what sort of changes may have taken place in the bone rank system over the centuries.</p>
<p>The highest level of the bone rank was the "sacred bone", or <i>seonggol</i> (성골, 聖骨), sometimes termed "hallowed bone", which consisted only of a portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_(Korean_name)#Gyeongju" title="Kim (Korean name)">royal Kim family</a>, possibly those considered to have royal blood on both sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kim_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kim-2">[2]</a></sup> Until this rank was abolished with the ascension of <a href="/wiki/Muyeol_of_Silla" title="Muyeol of Silla">King Muyeol</a> in 654, only those holding sacred bone rank were permitted to assume the throne. This led to the crowning of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Seondeok_of_Silla" title="Queen Seondeok of Silla">Queen Seondeok</a> in 632 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Jindeok_of_Silla" title="Queen Jindeok of Silla">Queen Jindeok</a> in 647, because no males of the sacred bone rank were available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kim_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kim-2">[2]</a></sup> This situation may have led to its subsequent abolition.</p>
<p>Below the sacred bone came the "true bone", or <i>jingol</i> (진골, 眞骨), which included the rest of the royal family, as well as the Bak and Seok families of <a href="/wiki/Gyeongju" title="Gyeongju">Gyeongju</a>, which had held the throne in very early times and were involved in most royal marriages, as well as another Kim ['Kimhae' royal clan] lineage descended from the royal house of <a href="/wiki/Geumgwan_Gaya" title="Geumgwan Gaya">Geumgwan Gaya</a>. Members of the true bone could hold any official position, up to the level of full minister. They could also attain office rank up to the highest, <i>ibolchan</i> (이벌찬). After the sacred bone rank was abolished under King Muyol, only those holding the true bone rank could become king.</p>
<p>Below the "true bone" came the head ranks (두품, 頭品), of which only the 6th, 5th and 4th are attested, 6th being the highest. The origins of these lower head rank classes and what defined one‘s status as such are obscure and still debated among scholars. As the <i>jingol</i> were prohibited from marrying into the lower ranks, though they could take concubines from them, it is feasible that one source of the head rank six were the children born from unions between <i>jingol</i> fathers and lower ranking concubines. In any case, members of the head rank six could rise to the position of vice-minister (<i>gyeong</i>, 경, 卿) and as high as the 6th level of office rank, <i>achan</i> (아찬, 阿餐) (two higher ranks of <i>jungachan</i> (중아찬, 重阿餐) and <i>sajung achan</i> (사중아찬, 四重阿餐) were later supplemented to that of <i>achan</i> but these still fell within the 6th level). Members of head ranks four and five could rise only to minor posts. Members of head rank five could reach the 10th level of office rank, <i>daenaema</i> (대내마, 大奈麻), while members of head rank four could attain only the 12th level of office rank, <i>daesa</i> (대사, 大舍). There were 17 levels of office rank altogether. One scholar has proposed that, "head-ranks three, two, and one, if they ever formally existed, must have designated the ... non-privileged general populace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluations">Evaluations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bone_rank_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evaluations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The frustrated ambitions of the head rank six class in particular seem to have played a prominent role in the politics of the late Silla period. Many men of head rank six status, proscribed from rising too high in the Silla administrative system defined by the bone rank system, sought to bypass this by studying <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> (either in Silla or abroad in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_China" title="Tang China">Tang China</a>) or else turned to careers in <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. The most prominent of the head rank six figures was undoubtedly <a href="/wiki/Choe_Chiwon" title="Choe Chiwon">Choe Chiwon</a>, who following an illustrious career in China returned to Silla only to see his attempts at administrative reform rebuffed by an entrenched aristocracy. In the early 10th century the nascent state of <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a>, which succeeded Silla, tapped into the head rank six intellectuals to man its bureaucracy.</p>
<p>The bone rank system's extreme rigidity certainly helped to weaken Silla toward the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Silla" title="Unified Silla">Unified Silla</a> period, although numerous other factors were at play. After the fall of Silla, the bone rank system was abolished entirely, although different and somewhat more flexible caste systems persisted until near the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> in the late 19th century.</p>
