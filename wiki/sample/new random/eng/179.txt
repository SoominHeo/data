<p><b>Frederick Sanger</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Order_of_Merit" title="Member of the Order of Merit">OM</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Companion of the Order of the Companions of Honour">CH</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Australian_Academy_of_Science" title="Fellow of the Australian Academy of Science">FAA</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/Ã¦/ short 'a' in 'bad'">Ã¦</span><span title="/Å/ 'ng' in 'sing'">Å</span><span title="/Ér/ 'er' in 'finger'">Ér</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 13 August 1918Â â 19 November 2013) was a British <a href="/wiki/Biochemist" title="Biochemist">biochemist</a> who won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> twice, one of only two people to have done so in the same category (the other is <a href="/wiki/John_Bardeen" title="John Bardeen">John Bardeen</a> in physics),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelfacts_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelfacts-4">[4]</a></sup> the fourth person overall with <a href="/wiki/Category:Nobel_laureates_with_multiple_Nobel_awards" title="Category:Nobel laureates with multiple Nobel awards">two Nobel Prizes</a>, and the third person overall with two Nobel Prizes in the sciences. In 1958, he was awarded a Nobel Prize in chemistry "for his work on the <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">structure of proteins</a>, especially that of <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>". In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gilbert" title="Walter Gilbert">Walter Gilbert</a> and Sanger shared half of the chemistry prize "for their contributions concerning the determination of base <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">sequences in nucleic acids</a>". The other half was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Paul_Berg" title="Paul Berg">Paul Berg</a> "for his fundamental studies of the biochemistry of nucleic acids, with particular regard to <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant DNA</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel1980_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobel1980-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frederick Sanger was born on 13 August 1918 in <a href="/wiki/Rendcomb" title="Rendcomb">Rendcomb</a>, a small village in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, England, the second son of Frederick Sanger, a <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioner</a>, and his wife, Cicely Sanger (nÃ©e Crewdson).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelbio_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelbio-6">[6]</a></sup> He was one of three children. His brother, Theodore, was only a year older, while his sister May (Mary) was five years younger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup> His father had worked as an Anglican medical missionary in China but returned to England because of ill health. He was 40 in 1916 when he married Cicely who was four years younger. Sanger's father converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quakerism</a> soon after his two sons were born and brought up the children as Quakers. Sanger's mother was the daughter of a wealthy cotton manufacturer and had a Quaker background, but Cicely was not a Quaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Sanger was around five years old the family moved to the small village of <a href="/wiki/Tanworth-in-Arden" title="Tanworth-in-Arden">Tanworth-in-Arden</a> in Warwickshire. The family was reasonably wealthy and employed a governess to teach the children. In 1927, at the age of nine, he was sent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Downs_School_(Herefordshire)" title="The Downs School (Herefordshire)">Downs School</a>, a residential preparatory school run by Quakers near <a href="/wiki/Malvern,_Worcestershire" title="Malvern, Worcestershire">Malvern</a>. His brother Theo was a year ahead of him at the same school. In 1932, at the age of 14, he was sent to the recently established <a href="/wiki/Bryanston_School" title="Bryanston School">Bryanston School</a> in Dorset. This used the <a href="/wiki/Dalton_Plan" title="Dalton Plan">Dalton</a> system and had a more liberal regime which Sanger much preferred. At the school he liked his teachers and particularly enjoyed scientific subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup> Able to complete his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_Certificate_(UK)" title="School Certificate (UK)">School Certificate</a> a year early, for which he was awarded seven credits, Sanger was able to spend most of his last year of school experimenting in the laboratory alongside his chemistry master, Geoffrey Ordish, who had originally studied at Cambridge University and been a researcher in the Cavendish Laboratory. Working with Ordish made a refreshing change from sitting and studying books and awakened Sanger's desire to pursue a scientific career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marksearly_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-marksearly-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1936 Sanger went to <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St John's College, Cambridge">St John's College, Cambridge</a> to study natural sciences. His father had attended the same college. For PartÂ I of his <a href="/wiki/Tripos" title="Tripos">Tripos</a> he took courses in physics, chemistry, biochemistry and mathematics but struggled with physics and mathematics. Many of the other students had studied more mathematics at school. In his second year he replaced physics with physiology. He took three years to obtain his PartÂ I. For his PartÂ II he studied biochemistry and obtained a 1st Class Honours. It was a relatively new department founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gowland_Hopkins" title="Gowland Hopkins">Gowland Hopkins</a> with enthusiastic lecturers who included <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Dixon" title="Malcolm Dixon">Malcolm Dixon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Needham" title="Joseph Needham">Joseph Needham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Baldwin" title="Ernest Baldwin">Ernest Baldwin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both his parents died from cancer during his first two years at Cambridge. His father was 60 and his mother was 58. As an undergraduate Sanger's beliefs were strongly influenced by his Quaker upbringing. He was a pacifist and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Pledge_Union" title="Peace Pledge Union">Peace Pledge Union</a>. It was through his involvement with the Cambridge Scientists' Anti-War Group that he met his future wife, Joan Howe, who was studying economics at <a href="/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge" title="Newnham College, Cambridge">Newnham College</a>. They courted while he was studying for his PartÂ II exams and married after he had graduated in December 1940. Under the <a href="/wiki/Military_Training_Act_1939" title="Military Training Act 1939">Military Training Act 1939</a> he was provisionally registered as a <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objector</a>, and again under the <a href="/wiki/National_Service_(Armed_Forces)_Act_1939" title="National Service (Armed Forces) Act 1939">National Service (Armed Forces) Act 1939</a>, before being granted unconditional exemption from military service by a tribunal. In the meantime he undertook training in social relief work at the Quaker centre, Spicelands, Devon and served briefly as a hospital orderly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sanger began studying for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> in October 1940 under <a href="/wiki/Norman_Pirie" title="Norman Pirie">N.W. "Bill" Pirie</a>. His project was to investigate whether edible protein could be obtained from grass. After little more than a month Pirie left the department and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Neuberger" title="Albert Neuberger">Albert Neuberger</a> became his adviser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup> Sanger changed his research project to study the metabolism of <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sangerphd_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sangerphd-9">[9]</a></sup> and a more practical problem concerning the nitrogen of potatoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His thesis had the title, "The metabolism of the amino acid lysine in the animal body". He was examined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Robert_Harington" title="Charles Robert Harington">Charles Harington</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Charles_Chibnall" title="Albert Charles Chibnall">Albert Charles Chibnall</a> and awarded his doctorate in 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequencing_insulin">Sequencing insulin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sequencing insulin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neuberger moved to the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Medical_Research" title="National Institute for Medical Research">National Institute for Medical Research</a> in London, but Sanger stayed in Cambridge and in 1943 joined the group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Chibnall" title="Charles Chibnall">Charles Chibnall</a>, a protein chemist who had recently taken up the chair in the Department of Biochemistry. Chibnall had already done some work on the amino acid composition of bovine <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and suggested that Sanger look at the amino groups in the protein. Insulin could be purchased from the <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a> chain <a href="/wiki/Boots_UK" title="Boots UK">Boots</a> and was one of the very few proteins that were available in a pure form. Up to this time Sanger had been funding himself. In Chibnall's group he was initially supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_Research_Council_(UK)" title="Medical Research Council (UK)">Medical Research Council</a> and then from 1944 until 1951 by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beit_Memorial_Fellowship_for_Medical_Research" title="Beit Memorial Fellowship for Medical Research">Beit Memorial Fellowship for Medical Research</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelbio_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobelbio-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sanger's first triumph was to determine the complete <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_sequence" title="Protein sequence">sequence</a> of the two polypeptide chains of bovine insulin, A and B, in 1952 and 1951, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture58_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lecture58-13">[13]</a></sup> Prior to this it was widely assumed that proteins were somewhat amorphous. In determining these sequences, Sanger proved that <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> have a defined chemical composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>To get to this point, Sanger refined a partition chromatography method first developed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Laurence_Millington_Synge" title="Richard Laurence Millington Synge">Richard Laurence Millington Synge</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archer_John_Porter_Martin" title="Archer John Porter Martin">Archer John Porter Martin</a> to determine the composition of amino acids in wool. Sanger used a chemical reagent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-fluoro-2,4-dinitrobenzene" title="1-fluoro-2,4-dinitrobenzene">1-fluoro-2,4-dinitrobenzene</a> (now, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanger%27s_reagent" title="Sanger's reagent">Sanger's reagent</a>, fluorodinitrobenzene, FDNB or DNFB), sourced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisonous_gas" title="Poisonous gas">poisonous gas</a> research by Bernhard Charles Saunders at the Chemistry Department at Cambridge University. Sanger's reagent proved effective at labelling the N-terminal amino group at one end of the polypeptide chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marksinsulin_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-marksinsulin-14">[14]</a></sup> He then partially hydrolysed the insulin into short peptides, either with hydrochloric acid or using an enzyme such as <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a>. The mixture of peptides was fractionated in two dimensions on a sheet of filter paper, first by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_electrophoresis" title="Protein electrophoresis">electrophoresis</a> in one dimension and then, perpendicular to that, by <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> in the other. The different peptide fragments of insulin, detected with <a href="/wiki/Ninhydrin" title="Ninhydrin">ninhydrin</a>, moved to different positions on the paper, creating a distinct pattern that Sanger called "fingerprints". The peptide from the N-terminus could be recognised by the yellow colour imparted by the FDNB label and the identity of the labelled amino acid at the end of the peptide determined by complete acid hydrolysis and discovering which dinitrophenyl-amino acid was there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>By repeating this type of procedure Sanger was able to determine the sequences of the many peptides generated using different methods for the initial partial hydrolysis. These could then be assembled into the longer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acid_sequence" title="Amino acid sequence">sequences</a> to deduce the complete structure of insulin. Finally, because the A and B chains are physiologically inactive without the three linking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disulfide_bond" title="Disulfide bond">disulfide bonds</a> (two interchain, one intrachain on A), Sanger and coworkers determined their assignments in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERyleSangerSmithKitai1955_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyleSangerSmithKitai1955-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stretton2002_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stretton2002-16">[16]</a></sup> Sanger's principal conclusion was that the two polypeptide chains of the protein insulin had precise amino acid sequences and, by extension, that every protein had a unique sequence. It was this achievement that earned him his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel prize in Chemistry">Nobel prize in Chemistry</a> in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This discovery was crucial for the later <a href="/wiki/Sequence_hypothesis" title="Sequence hypothesis">sequence hypothesis</a> of Crick for developing ideas of how DNA codes for proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-markspath_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-markspath-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequencing_RNA">Sequencing RNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sequencing RNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1951 Sanger was a member of the external staff of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_Research_Council_(UK)" title="Medical Research Council (UK)">Medical Research Council</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelbio_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-nobelbio-6">[6]</a></sup> and when they opened the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_of_Molecular_Biology" title="Laboratory of Molecular Biology">Laboratory of Molecular Biology</a> in 1962, he moved from his laboratories in the Biochemistry Department of the university to the top floor of the new building. He became head of the Protein Chemistry division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownlee_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-brownlee-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to his move, Sanger began exploring the possibility of sequencing RNA molecules and began developing methods for separating ribonucleotide fragments generated with specific nucleases. This work he did while trying to refine the sequencing techniques he had developed during his work on insulin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-markspath_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-markspath-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The key challenge in the work was finding a pure piece of RNA to sequence. In the course of the work he discovered in 1964, with Kjeld Marcker, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formylmethionine" title="Formylmethionine">formylmethionine tRNA</a> which initiates protein synthesis in bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He was beaten in the race to be the first to sequence a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNA</a> molecule by a group led by <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Holley" title="Robert W. Holley">Robert Holley</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, who published the sequence of the 77 ribonucleotides of <a href="/wiki/Alanine" title="Alanine">alanine</a> tRNA from <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> By 1967 Sanger's group had determined the nucleotide sequence of the <a href="/wiki/5S_ribosomal_RNA" title="5S ribosomal RNA">5S ribosomal RNA</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, a small RNA of 120 nucleotides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequencing_DNA">Sequencing DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sequencing DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">DNA sequencing</a></div>
<p>He then turned to sequencing DNA, which would require an entirely different approach. He looked at different ways of using <a href="/wiki/DNA_polymerase_I" title="DNA polymerase I">DNA polymerase I</a> from <i>E. coli</i> to copy single stranded DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1975, together with Alan Coulson, he published a sequencing procedure using DNA polymerase with radiolabelled nucleotides that he called the "Plus and Minus" technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture80_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-lecture80-24">[24]</a></sup> This involved two closely related methods that generated short oligonucleotides with defined 3' termini. These could be fractionated by <a href="/wiki/Electrophoresis" title="Electrophoresis">electrophoresis</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Polyacrylamide" title="Polyacrylamide">polyacrylamide</a> gel and visualised using autoradiography. The procedure could sequence up to 80 nucleotides in one go and was a big improvement on what had gone before, but was still very laborious. Nevertheless, his group were able to sequence most of the 5,386 nucleotides of the single-stranded <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophage</a> <a href="/wiki/Phi_X_174" title="Phi X 174">ÏX174</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This was the first fully sequenced DNA-based genome. To their surprise they discovered that the <a href="/wiki/Coding_region" title="Coding region">coding regions</a> of some of the genes overlapped with one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsbm_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rsbm-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977 Sanger and colleagues introduced the "dideoxy" chain-termination method for sequencing DNA molecules, also known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanger_method" title="Sanger method">Sanger method</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture80_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-lecture80-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This was a major breakthrough and allowed long stretches of DNA to be rapidly and accurately sequenced. It earned him his second Nobel prize in Chemistry in 1980, which he shared with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gilbert" title="Walter Gilbert">Walter Gilbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Berg" title="Paul Berg">Paul Berg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel1980_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobel1980-5">[5]</a></sup> The new method was used by Sanger and colleagues to sequence human mitochondrial DNA (16,569 base pairs)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and bacteriophage Î» (48,502 base pairs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The dideoxy method was eventually used to sequence the entire <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obituarynature_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-obituarynature-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postgraduate_students">Postgraduate students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Postgraduate students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the course of his career Sanger supervised more than ten PhD students, two of whom went on to also win Nobel Prizes. His first graduate student was <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Robert_Porter" title="Rodney Robert Porter">Rodney Porter</a> who joined the research group in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsbm_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-rsbm-3">[3]</a></sup> Porter later shared the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Edelman" title="Gerald Edelman">Gerald Edelman</a> for his work on the chemical structure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibodies" title="Antibodies">antibodies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Blackburn" title="Elizabeth Blackburn">Elizabeth Blackburn</a> studied for a PhD in Sanger's laboratory between 1971 and 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsbm_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-rsbm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blackphd_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-blackphd-31">[31]</a></sup> She shared the 2009 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with <a href="/wiki/Carol_W._Greider" title="Carol W. Greider">Carol W. Greider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_W._Szostak" title="Jack W. Szostak">Jack W. Szostak</a> for her work on <a href="/wiki/Telomere" title="Telomere">telomeres</a> and the action of <a href="/wiki/Telomerase" title="Telomerase">telomerase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family">Marriage and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marriage and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sanger married Margaret Joan Howe in 1940. They had three children â Robin, born in 1943, Peter born in 1946 and Sally Joan born in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelbio_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-nobelbio-6">[6]</a></sup> He said that his wife had "contributed more to his work than anyone else by providing a peaceful and happy home."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_obit_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_obit-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Sanger retired in 1983, aged 65, to his home, "Far Leys", in <a href="/wiki/Swaffham_Bulbeck" title="Swaffham Bulbeck">Swaffham Bulbeck</a> outside Cambridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsbm_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-rsbm-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust</a> and the Medical Research Council founded the Sanger Centre (now the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanger_Institute" title="Sanger Institute">Sanger Institute</a>), named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_people_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Our_people-34">[34]</a></sup> The Institute is located on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust_Genome_Campus" title="Wellcome Trust Genome Campus">Wellcome Trust Genome Campus</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hinxton" title="Hinxton">Hinxton</a>, only a few miles from Sanger's home. He agreed to having the Centre named after him when asked by <a href="/wiki/John_Sulston" title="John Sulston">John Sulston</a>, the founding director, but warned, "It had better be good."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_people_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Our_people-34">[34]</a></sup> It was opened by Sanger in person on 4 October 1993, with a staff of fewer than 50 people, and went on to take a leading role in the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">sequencing of the human genome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_people_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Our_people-34">[34]</a></sup> The Institute now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> has over 900 people and is one of the world's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genomic" title="Genomic">genomic</a> research centres.</p>
<p>Sanger said he found no evidence for a God so he became an agnostic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hargittai_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-hargittai-35">[35]</a></sup> In an interview published in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">Times</a></i> newspaper in 2000 Sanger is quoted as saying: "My father was a committed Quaker and I was brought up as a Quaker, and for them truth is very important. I drifted away from those beliefs â one is obviously looking for truth, but one needs some evidence for it. Even if I wanted to believe in God I would find it very difficult. I would need to see proof."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>He declined the offer of a <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom#Knighthood" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">knighthood</a>, as he did not wish to be addressed as "Sir". He is quoted as saying, "A knighthood makes you different, doesnât it, and I donât want to be different." In 1986, he accepted the award of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_(Commonwealth)" title="Order of Merit (Commonwealth)">Order of Merit</a>, which can have only 24 living members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_obit_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_obit-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hargittai_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-hargittai-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-times-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 the British <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_Society" title="Biochemical Society">Biochemical Society</a> was given a grant by the Wellcome Trust to catalogue and preserve the 35 laboratory notebooks in which Sanger recorded his research from 1944 to 1983. In reporting this matter, <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> noted that Sanger, "the most self-effacing person you could hope to meet", was spending his time gardening at his <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sanger died in his sleep at <a href="/wiki/Addenbrooke%27s_Hospital" title="Addenbrooke's Hospital">Addenbrooke's Hospital</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> on 19 November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_obit_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_obit-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-death-38">[38]</a></sup> As noted in his obituary, he had described himself as "just a chap who messed about in a lab",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and "academically not brilliant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Sanger&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Sanger is the only person to have been awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry twice, and one of only four two-time Nobel laureates: The other three were <a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Curie</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Physics</a>, 1903 and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Chemistry</a>, 1911), <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Chemistry</a>, 1954 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Peace" title="Nobel Prize for Peace">Peace</a>, 1962) and <a href="/wiki/John_Bardeen" title="John Bardeen">John Bardeen</a> (twice <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Physics</a>, 1956 and 1972).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelfacts_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobelfacts-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
