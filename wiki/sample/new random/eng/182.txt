<p><b>Jñāna</b> (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>: <i>ñāṇa</i>) or <b>gyan</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a term for "knowledge" in Indian philosophy and <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">religion</a>.</p>
<p>The idea of jnana centers on a cognitive event which is recognized when experienced. It is knowledge inseparable from the total experience of reality, especially a total or divine reality (<a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brittanica_on_jnana_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brittanica_on_jnana-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The root <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%9C%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%9E%E0%A4%BE%E0%A4%A8" title="wikt:ज्ञान">jñā-</a> is cognate to English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/know" title="wikt:know">know</a></i>, as well as to the Greek <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%B9%CE%B3%CE%BD%CF%8E%CF%83%CE%BA%CF%89" title="wikt:γιγνώσκω">γνώ-</a> (as in γνῶσις <i><a href="/wiki/Gnosis" title="Gnosis">gnosis</a></i>). Its antonym is <i>ajñāna</i> "ignorance".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Buddhism">In Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jnana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, it refers to pure awareness that is free of conceptual encumbrances, and is contrasted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vijnana" title="Vijnana">vijnana</a>, which is a moment of 'divided knowing'. Entrance to, and progression through the ten stages of Jnana/Bhimis, will lead one to complete enlightenment and <a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">nirvana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Vipassan%C4%81" title="Vipassanā">Vipassanā</a> tradition of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> there are the following ñanas according to <a href="/wiki/Mahasi_Sayadaw" title="Mahasi Sayadaw">Mahasi Sayadaw</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> As a person meditates these ñanas or "knowledges" will be experienced in order. The experience of each may be brief or may last for years and the subjective intensity of each is variable. Each ñana could also be considered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jh%C4%81na" title="Jhāna">jhāna</a> although many are not stable and the mind has no way to remain embedded in the experience. Experiencing all the ñanas will lead to the first of the <a href="/wiki/Four_stages_of_enlightenment" title="Four stages of enlightenment">Four stages of enlightenment</a> then the cycle will start over at a subtler level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Analytical Knowledge of Body and Mind (nama-rupa-pariccheda-ñana) (corresponds to 1st jhana)</li>
<li>Knowledge by Discerning Conditionality (paccaya-pariggaha-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge by Comprehension (sammasana-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Arising and Passing Away (udayabbaya-ñana) (corresponds to 2nd jhana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Dissolution (bhanga-ñana) (corresponds to 3rd jhana)</li>
<li>Awareness of Fearfulness (bhayatupatthana-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Misery (adinava-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Disgust (nibbida-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Desire for Deliverance (muncitu-kamyata-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Re-observation (patisankhanupassana-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Equanimity about Formations (sankhar'upekkha-ñana) (corresponds to 4th jhana)</li>
<li>Insight Leading to emergence (vutthanagamini-vipassana-ñana)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Adaptation (anuloma-ñana) (one-time event)</li>
<li>Maturity Knowledge (gotrabhu-ñana) (one-time event)</li>
<li>Path Knowledge (magga-ñana) (one-time event)</li>
<li>Fruition Knowledge (phala-ñana) (corresponds to <a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">Nibbāna</a>)</li>
<li>Knowledge of Reviewing (paccavekkhana-ñana)</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Hinduism">In Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jnana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sahu explains:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Prajnanam iti Brahman</i> - wisdom is the soul/spirit. <i>Prajnanam</i> refers to the intuitive truth which can be verified/tested by reason. It is a higher function of the intellect that ascertains the <i>Sat</i> or Truth in the <i>Sat-Chit-Ananda</i> or truth-consciousness-bliss, i.e. the <i>Brahman/Atman/Self</i>/person [...] A truly wise person [...] is known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prajna_(Vedic)" title="Prajna (Vedic)">Prajna</a></i> - who has attained <i>Brahman</i>hood itself; thus, testifying to the <i>Vedic</i> <i>Maha Vakya</i> (great saying or words of wisdom): <i>Prajnanam iti Brahman</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESahu200441_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESahu200441-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jnana_yoga" title="Jnana yoga">Jnana yoga</a> (Yoga of Knowledge) is one of the three main paths (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadhana" title="Sadhana">margas</a>), which are supposed to lead towards <a href="/wiki/Moksha" title="Moksha">moksha</a> (liberation) from material miseries. The other two main paths are <a href="/wiki/Karma_yoga" title="Karma yoga">Karma yoga</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhakti_Yoga" title="Bhakti Yoga">Bhakti Yoga</a>. <a href="/wiki/R%C4%81ja_yoga" title="Rāja yoga">Rāja yoga</a> (classical yoga) which includes several yogas, is also said to lead to moksha. It is said that each path is meant for a different temperament of personality.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Jainism">In Jainism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jnana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Jainism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jain_epistemology" title="Jain epistemology">Jain epistemology</a></div>
<p>According to the Jain texts like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tattv%C4%81rths%C5%ABtra" title="Tattvārthsūtra">Tattvārthsūtra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarv%C4%81rthasiddhi" title="Sarvārthasiddhi">Sarvārthasiddhi</a>, knowledge is of five kinds:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kevala_Jnana" title="Kevala Jnana">Kevala Jnana</a> (Omniscience)</li>
<li>Śrutu Jñāna (Scriptural Knowledge)</li>
<li>Mati Jñāna (Sensory Knowledge)</li>
<li>Avadhi Jñāna (Clairvoyance)</li>
<li>Manah prayāya Jñāna (Telepathy)</li>
</ul>
