<p><b>Messier 32</b> (also known as <b>NGC 221</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_elliptical_galaxy" title="Dwarf elliptical galaxy">dwarf elliptical galaxy</a> located about 2.65 million <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from Earth, appearing in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(constellation)" title="Andromeda (constellation)">Andromeda</a>. M32 is a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_galaxy" title="Satellite galaxy">satellite galaxy</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a> (M31) and was discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Gentil" title="Le Gentil">Le Gentil</a> in 1749. M32 measures 6.5 ± 0.2 thousand light-years in diameter at the widest point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Like most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxies" title="Elliptical galaxies">elliptical galaxies</a>, M32 contains mostly older faint red and yellow stars with practically no dust or gas and consequently no current <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSOG_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSOG-8">[8]</a></sup> It does, however, show hints of star formation in the relatively recent past. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RWM09_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-RWM09-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The structure and stellar content of M32 is difficult to explain by traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation" title="Galaxy formation">galaxy formation</a> models. Some simulations suggest a scenario in which the strong <a href="/wiki/Galactic_tide" title="Galactic tide">tidal field</a> of M31 can transform a <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a> into a compact elliptical. As a small spiral galaxy falls into the central parts of M31, most of the outer layers of the smaller spiral are stripped away. The central bulge of the galaxy is much less affected and retains its morphology. Tidal effects trigger a massive star burst in the core, resulting in the high density of M32 observed today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bekkietal2001_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bekkietal2001-10">[10]</a></sup> There is also evidence that M32 has an outer disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlisterGraham2002_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlisterGraham2002-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_measurements">Distance measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distance measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least two techniques have been used to measure distances to M32. The infrared <a href="/wiki/Surface_brightness_fluctuation" title="Surface brightness fluctuation">surface brightness fluctuations</a> distance measurement technique estimates distances to spiral galaxies based on the graininess of the appearance of their bulges. The distance measured to M32 using this technique is 2.46 ± 0.09 million light-years (755 ± 28 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">kpc</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jensenetal2003_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-jensenetal2003-2">[2]</a></sup> However, M32 is close enough that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tip_of_the_red_giant_branch" title="Tip of the red giant branch">tip of the red giant branch</a> (TRGB) method may be used to estimate its distance. The estimated distance to M32 using this technique is 2.51 ± 0.13 million light-years (770 ± 40 kpc).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsevetal2004_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsevetal2004-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karachentsevetal2006_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karachentsevetal2006-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_hole">Black hole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Black hole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>M32 contains a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>. Its mass has been estimated to lie between 1.5 and 5 million solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
