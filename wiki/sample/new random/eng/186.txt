<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Drawing</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Drawing</b> is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_art" title="Visual art">visual art</a> in which a person uses various drawing instruments to mark <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-dimensional" title="Two-dimensional">two-dimensional</a> medium. Instruments include <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">pencils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen_and_ink" title="Pen and ink">pen and ink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inked</a> <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">brushes</a>, wax <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_pencil" title="Color pencil">color pencils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayon" title="Crayon">crayons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charcoal_(art)" title="Charcoal (art)">charcoal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk_chalk" title="Sidewalk chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastels</a>, various kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erasers" title="Erasers">erasers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">markers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">styluses</a>, various metals (such as <a href="/wiki/Silverpoint" title="Silverpoint">silverpoint</a>) and electronic drawing.</p>
<p>A drawing instrument releases small amount of material onto a surface, leaving a visible mark. The most common support for drawing is <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, although other materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">cardboard</a>, plastic, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">board</a>, may be used. Temporary drawings may be made on a <a href="/wiki/Blackboard" title="Blackboard">blackboard</a> or <a href="/wiki/Whiteboard" title="Whiteboard">whiteboard</a> or indeed almost anything. The medium has been a popular and fundamental means of public expression throughout human history. It is one of the simplest and most efficient means of communicating visual ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBTCModule6_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBTCModule6-1">[1]</a></sup> The wide availability of drawing instruments makes drawing one of the most common artistic activities.</p>

<p>In addition to its more artistic forms, drawing is frequently used in commercial <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_animation" title="Traditional animation">animation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architectural_drawing" title="Architectural drawing">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_drawing" title="Engineering drawing">engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">technical drawing</a>. A quick, freehand drawing, usually not intended as a finished work, is sometimes called a <a href="/wiki/Sketch_(drawing)" title="Sketch (drawing)">sketch</a>. An artist who practices or works in technical drawing may be called a <a href="/wiki/Drafter" title="Drafter">drafter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draftsman" title="Draftsman">draftsman</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draughtsman" title="Draughtsman">draughtsman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drawing is one of the major forms of expression within the visual arts. It is generally concerned with the marking of lines and areas of tone onto paper/other material, where the accurate representation of the visual world is expressed upon a plane surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Traditional drawings were <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochrome</a>, or at least had little colour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while modern colored-pencil drawings may approach or cross a boundary between drawing and painting. In Western terminology, drawing is distinct from painting, even though similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_artistic_mediums#Drawing" title="List of artistic mediums">media</a> often are employed in both tasks. Dry media, normally associated with drawing, such as chalk, may be used in <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastel</a> paintings. Drawing may be done with a liquid medium, applied with brushes or pens. Similar supports likewise can serve both: painting generally involves the application of liquid paint onto prepared canvas or panels, but sometimes an <a href="/wiki/Underdrawing" title="Underdrawing">underdrawing</a> is drawn first on that same support.</p>

<p>Drawing is often exploratory, with considerable emphasis on observation, problem-solving and composition. Drawing is also regularly used in preparation for a painting, further obfuscating their distinction. Drawings created for these purposes are called <a href="/wiki/Study_(art)" title="Study (art)">studies</a>.</p>
<p>There are several categories of drawing, including <a href="/wiki/Figure_drawing" title="Figure drawing">figure drawing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartooning" title="Cartooning">cartooning</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doodling" title="Doodling">doodling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_hand" title="Free hand">free hand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shading" title="Shading">shading</a>. There are also many drawing methods, such as line drawing, stippling, shading, the surrealist method of <a href="/wiki/Surrealist_techniques#Entopic_graphomania" title="Surrealist techniques">entopic graphomania</a> (in which dots are made at the sites of impurities in a blank sheet of paper, and lines are then made between the dots), and tracing (drawing on a translucent paper, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tracing_paper" title="Tracing paper">tracing paper</a></i>, around the outline of preexisting shapes that show through the paper).</p>
<p>A quick, unrefined drawing may be called a <i><a href="/wiki/Sketch_(drawing)" title="Sketch (drawing)">sketch</a></i>.</p>
<p>In fields outside art, <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">technical drawings</a> or plans of buildings, machinery, circuitry and other things are often called "drawings" even when they have been transferred to another medium by printing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><b>Drawing as a Form of Communication</b></i> Drawing is one of the oldest forms of human expression, with evidence for its existence preceding that of written communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tversky_2011_499.E2.80.93535_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tversky_2011_499.E2.80.93535-5">[5]</a></sup> It is believed that drawing was used as a specialised form of communication before the invent of the written language,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tversky_2011_499.E2.80.93535_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tversky_2011_499.E2.80.93535-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> demonstrated by the production of cave and rock paintings created by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens sapiens">Homo sapiens sapiens</a></i> around 30,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These drawings, known as pictograms, depicted objects and abstract concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The sketches and paintings produced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times were eventually stylised and simplified, leading to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written language</a> as we know it today.</p>
<p><i><b>Drawing in the Arts</b></i> Drawing is used to express one's creativity, and therefore has been prominent in the world of art. Throughout much of history, drawing was regarded as the foundation for artistic practise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Initially, artists used and reused wooden tablets for the production of their drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following the widespread availability of paper in the 14th century, the use of drawing in the arts increased. At this point, drawing was commonly used as a tool for thought and investigation, acting as a study medium whilst artists were preparing for their final pieces of work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In a period of artistic flourish, the Renaissance brought about drawings exhibiting realistic representational qualities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> where there was a lot of influence from geometry and philosophy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The invention of the first widely available form of <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> led to a shift in the use of drawing in the arts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Photography took over from drawing as a more superior method for accurately representing visual phenomena, and artists began to abandon traditional drawing practises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kovats_2005_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kovats_2005-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a> in the arts encouraged "imaginative originality"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and artists' approach to drawing became more abstract.</p>
<p><i><b>Drawing Outside the Arts</b></i> Although the use of drawing is extensive in the arts, its practice is not confined purely to this field. Before the widespread availability of paper, 12th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monks" title="Monks">monks</a> in European monasteries used intricate drawings to prepare illustrated, illuminated manuscripts on vellum and parchment. Drawing has also been used extensively in the field of science, as a method of discovery, understanding and explanation. In 1616, astronomer <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> explained the changing phases of the moon through his observational telescopic drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kovats_2005_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kovats_2005-16">[16]</a></sup> Additionally, in 1924, geophysicist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Alfred Wegener</a> used illustrations to visually demonstrate the origin of the continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kovats_2005_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kovats_2005-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_draftsmen">Notable draftsmen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable draftsmen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Drawing became significant as an art form around 1500, as artists like <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> came to the fore;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in the early 15th century, engravings began to be <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">made into prints</a> and later came to be used as book illustrations.</p>
<ul>
<li>Notable draftsmen of the 14th, 15th and 16th centuries include <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>.</li>
<li>Notable draftsmen of the 17th century include <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lorrain" title="Claude Lorrain">Claude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Nicolas Poussin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rembrandt_Harmenszoon_van_Rijn" title="Rembrandt Harmenszoon van Rijn">Rembrandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>.</li>
<li>Notable draftsmen of the 18th century include <a href="/wiki/Jean-Honor%C3%A9_Fragonard" title="Jean-Honoré Fragonard">Jean-Honoré Fragonard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Giovanni Battista Tiepolo</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antoine_Watteau" title="Antoine Watteau">Antoine Watteau</a>.</li>
<li>Notable draftsmen of the 19th century include <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Beardsley" title="Aubrey Beardsley">Aubrey Beardsley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Paul_Prud%27hon" title="Pierre-Paul Prud'hon">Pierre-Paul Prud'hon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Degas" title="Edgar Degas">Edgar Degas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_G%C3%A9ricault" title="Théodore Géricault">Théodore Géricault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Ingres" title="Jean Ingres">Jean Ingres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odilon_Redon" title="Odilon Redon">Odilon Redon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier" title="Honoré Daumier">Honoré Daumier</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>.</li>
<li>Notable draftsmen of the 20th century include <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A4the_Kollwitz" title="Käthe Kollwitz">Käthe Kollwitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kubin" title="Alfred Kubin">Alfred Kubin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Beckmann" title="Max Beckmann">Max Beckmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dubuffet" title="Jean Dubuffet">Jean Dubuffet</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Grosz" title="George Grosz">George Grosz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egon_Schiele" title="Egon Schiele">Egon Schiele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arshile_Gorky" title="Arshile Gorky">Arshile Gorky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscar_Kokoschka" title="Oscar Kokoschka">Oscar Kokoschka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Mucha" title="Alphonse Mucha">Alphonse Mucha</a>, <a href="/wiki/M._C._Escher" title="M. C. Escher">M. C. Escher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Masson" title="André Masson">André Masson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Pascin" title="Jules Pascin">Jules Pascin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="598" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg/220px-Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg/330px-Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg/440px-Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Watteau_jeune_fille.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antoine_Watteau" title="Antoine Watteau">Antoine Watteau</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Trois_crayons" title="Trois crayons">trois crayons</a></i> technique</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>medium</i> is the means by which ink, pigment or color are delivered onto the drawing surface. Most drawing media are either dry (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastels" title="Pastels">pastels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cont%C3%A9" title="Conté">Conté</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silverpoint" title="Silverpoint">silverpoint</a>), or use a fluid solvent or carrier (<a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">marker</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen_and_ink" title="Pen and ink">pen and ink</a>). Watercolor pencils can be used dry like ordinary pencils, then moistened with a wet brush to get various painterly effects. Very rarely, artists have drawn with (usually decoded) <a href="/wiki/Invisible_ink" title="Invisible ink">invisible ink</a>. Metalpoint drawing usually employs either of two metals: silver or lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> More rarely used are gold, platinum, copper, brass, bronze, and tinpoint.</p>
<p>Paper comes in a variety of different sizes and qualities, ranging from newspaper grade up to high quality and relatively expensive paper sold as individual sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Papers can vary in texture, hue, acidity, and strength when wet. Smooth paper is good for rendering fine detail, but a more "toothy" paper holds the drawing material better. Thus a coarser material is useful for producing deeper contrast.</p>
<p>Newsprint and typing paper may be useful for practice and rough sketches. Tracing paper is used to experiment over a half-finished drawing, and to transfer a design from one sheet to another. Cartridge paper is the basic type of drawing paper sold in pads. Bristol board and even heavier acid-free boards, frequently with smooth finishes, are used for drawing fine detail and do not distort when wet media (ink, washes) are applied. Vellum is extremely smooth and suitable for very fine detail. Coldpressed watercolor paper may be favored for ink drawing due to its texture.</p>
<p>Acid-free, archival quality paper keeps its color and texture far longer than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a> based paper such as <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">newsprint</a>, which turns yellow and become brittle much sooner.</p>
<p>The basic tools are a <a href="/wiki/Drawing_board" title="Drawing board">drawing board</a> or table, <a href="/wiki/Pencil_sharpener" title="Pencil sharpener">pencil sharpener</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eraser" title="Eraser">eraser</a>, and for ink drawing, <a href="/wiki/Blotting_paper" title="Blotting paper">blotting paper</a>. Other tools used are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circle_compass" title="Circle compass">circle compass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Set_square" title="Set square">set square</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fixative_(drawing)" title="Fixative (drawing)">Fixative</a> is used to prevent pencil and crayon marks from smudging. <a href="/wiki/Drafting_tape" title="Drafting tape">Drafting tape</a> is used to secure paper to drawing surface, and also to mask an area to keep it free of accidental marks sprayed or spattered materials and washes. An easel or slanted table is used to keep the drawing surface in a suitable position, which is generally more horizontal than the position used in painting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2733" data-file-width="1747" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg/220px-Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg/330px-Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg/440px-Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89tude_Madone_d%27Albe_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, study for what became the <a href="/wiki/Alba_Madonna" title="Alba Madonna">Alba Madonna</a>, with other sketches</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Almost all draftsmen use their hands and fingers to apply the media, with the exception of some handicapped individuals who draw with their mouth or feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to working on an image, the artist typically explores how various media work. They may try different drawing implements on practice sheets to determine value and texture, and how to apply the implement to produce various effects.</p>
<p>The artist's choice of drawing strokes affects the appearance of the image. Pen and ink drawings often use <a href="/wiki/Hatching" title="Hatching">hatching</a>—groups of parallel lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Cross-hatching uses hatching in two or more different directions to create a darker tone. Broken hatching, or lines with intermittent breaks, form lighter tones—and controlling the density of the breaks achieves a gradation of tone. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stipple" title="Stipple">Stippling</a>, uses dots to produce tone, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Texture" title="wikt:Texture">texture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shading" title="Shading">shade</a>. Different textures can be achieved depending on the method used to build tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Drawings in dry media often use similar techniques, though pencils and drawing sticks can achieve continuous variations in tone. Typically a drawing is filled in based on which hand the artist favors. A right-handed artist draws from left to right to avoid smearing the image. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erasers" title="Erasers">Erasers</a> can remove unwanted lines, lighten tones, and clean up stray marks. In a sketch or outline drawing, lines drawn often follow the contour of the subject, creating depth by looking like shadows cast from a light in the artist's position.</p>
<p>Sometimes the artist leaves a section of the image untouched while filling in the remainder. The shape of the area to preserve can be painted with <a href="/wiki/Masking_(in_art)" title="Masking (in art)">masking fluid</a> or cut out of a <a href="/wiki/Frisket" title="Frisket">frisket</a> and applied to the drawing surface, protecting the surface from stray marks until the mask is removed.</p>
<p>Another method to preserve a section of the image is to apply a spray-on <i>fixative</i> to the surface. This holds loose material more firmly to the sheet and prevents it from smearing. However the fixative spray typically uses chemicals that can harm the respiratory system, so it should be employed in a well-ventilated area such as outdoors.</p>
<p>Another technique is <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_drawing" title="Subtractive drawing">subtractive drawing</a> in which the drawing surface is covered with graphite or charcoal and then erased to make the image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tone">Tone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1153" data-file-width="740" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/170px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/255px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/340px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Line drawing in <a href="/wiki/Sanguine" title="Sanguine">sanguine</a> by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shading is the technique of varying the tonal values on the paper to represent the shade of the material as well as the placement of the shadows. Careful attention to reflected light, shadows and highlights can result in a very realistic rendition of the image.</p>
<p>Blending uses an implement to soften or spread the original drawing strokes. Blending is most easily done with a medium that does not immediately <a href="/wiki/Fixative_(drawing)" title="Fixative (drawing)">fix</a> itself, such as graphite, chalk, or charcoal, although freshly applied ink can be smudged, wet or dry, for some effects. For shading and blending, the artist can use a <a href="/wiki/Tortillon" title="Tortillon">blending stump</a>, <a href="/wiki/Facial_tissue" title="Facial tissue">tissue</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Kneaded_eraser" title="Kneaded eraser">kneaded eraser</a>, a fingertip, or any combination of them. A piece of <a href="/wiki/Chamois_leather" title="Chamois leather">chamois</a> is useful for creating smooth textures, and for removing material to lighten the tone. Continuous tone can be achieved with graphite on a smooth surface without blending, but the technique is laborious, involving small circular or oval strokes with a somewhat blunt point.</p>
<p>Shading techniques that also introduce texture to the drawing include <a href="/wiki/Hatching" title="Hatching">hatching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stippling" title="Stippling">stippling</a>. A number of other methods produce texture. In addition to the choice of paper, drawing material and technique affect texture. Texture can be made to appear more realistic when it is drawn next to a contrasting texture; a coarse texture is more obvious when placed next to a smoothly blended area. A similar effect can be achieved by drawing different tones close together. A light edge next to a dark background stands out to the eye, and almost appears to float above the surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Form_and_proportion">Form and proportion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Form and proportion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ingres_montagu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="552" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Ingres_montagu.jpg/170px-Ingres_montagu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Ingres_montagu.jpg/255px-Ingres_montagu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Ingres_montagu.jpg/340px-Ingres_montagu.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ingres_montagu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pencil portrait by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingres" title="Ingres">Ingres</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Measuring the dimensions of a subject while blocking in the drawing is an important step in producing a realistic rendition of the subject. Tools such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_(drafting)" title="Compass (drafting)">compass</a> can be used to measure the angles of different sides. These angles can be reproduced on the drawing surface and then rechecked to make sure they are accurate. Another form of measurement is to compare the relative sizes of different parts of the subject with each other. A finger placed at a point along the drawing implement can be used to compare that dimension with other parts of the image. A <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a> can be used both as a <a href="/wiki/Straightedge" title="Straightedge">straightedge</a> and a device to compute proportions.</p>
<p>When attempting to draw a complicated shape such as a human figure, it is helpful at first to represent the form with a set of primitive volumes. Almost any form can be represented by some combination of the cube, sphere, cylinder, and cone. Once these basic volumes have been assembled into a likeness, then the drawing can be refined into a more accurate and polished form. The lines of the primitive volumes are removed and replaced by the final likeness. Drawing the underlying construction is a fundamental skill for representational art, and is taught in many books and schools. Its correct application resolves most uncertainties about smaller details, and makes the final image look consistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drawinghale_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-drawinghale-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more refined art of <a href="/wiki/Figure_drawing" title="Figure drawing">figure drawing</a> relies upon the artist possessing a deep understanding of anatomy and the human proportions. A trained artist is familiar with the skeleton structure, joint location, muscle placement, tendon movement, and how the different parts work together during movement. This allows the artist to render more natural poses that do not appear artificially stiff. The artist is also familiar with how the proportions vary depending on the age of the subject, particularly when drawing a portrait.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perspective">Perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">Linear perspective</a> is a method of portraying objects on a flat surface so that the dimensions shrink with distance. Each set of parallel, straight edges of any object, whether a building or a table, follows lines that eventually converge at a vanishing point. Typically this convergence point is somewhere along the horizon, as buildings are built level with the flat surface. When multiple structures are aligned with each other, such as buildings along a street, the horizontal tops and bottoms of the structures typically converge at a vanishing point.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2-punktperspektive.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="266" data-file-width="400" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/2-punktperspektive.svg/220px-2-punktperspektive.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/2-punktperspektive.svg/330px-2-punktperspektive.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/2-punktperspektive.svg/440px-2-punktperspektive.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2-punktperspektive.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two-point <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_drawing" title="Perspective drawing">perspective drawing</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When both the fronts and sides of a building are drawn, then the parallel lines forming a side converge at a second point along the horizon (which may be off the drawing paper.) This is a two-point perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Converging the vertical lines to a third point above or below the horizon then produces a three-point perspective.</p>
<p>Depth can also be portrayed by several techniques in addition to the perspective approach above. Objects of similar <b>size</b> should appear ever smaller the further they are from the viewer. Thus the back wheel of a cart appears slightly smaller than the front wheel. Depth can be portrayed through the use of <b>texture</b>. As the texture of an object gets further away it becomes more compressed and busy, taking on an entirely different character than if it was close. Depth can also be portrayed by reducing the contrast in more distant objects, and by making their colors less saturated. This reproduces the effect of <b>atmospheric</b> haze, and cause the eye to focus primarily on objects drawn in the foreground.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">composition</a> of the image is an important element in producing an interesting work of <a href="/wiki/Artistic_merit" title="Artistic merit">artistic merit</a>. The artist plans element placement in the art to communicate ideas and feelings with the viewer. The composition can determine the focus of the art, and result in a harmonious whole that is aesthetically appealing and stimulating.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Illumination_(image)" title="Illumination (image)">illumination</a> of the subject is also a key element in creating an artistic piece, and the interplay of <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">light and shadow</a> is a valuable method in the artist's toolbox. The <a href="/wiki/Three-point_lighting" title="Three-point lighting">placement of the light sources</a> can make a considerable difference in the type of message that is being presented. Multiple light sources can wash out any wrinkles in a person's face, for instance, and give a more youthful appearance. In contrast, a single light source, such as harsh daylight, can serve to highlight any texture or interesting features.</p>
<p>When drawing an object or figure, the skilled artist pays attention to both the area within the silhouette and what lies outside. The exterior is termed the <a href="/wiki/Negative_space" title="Negative space">negative space</a>, and can be as important in the representation as the figure. Objects placed in the background of the figure should appear properly placed wherever they can be viewed.</p>

<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Study_(drawing)" title="Study (drawing)">study</a> is a draft drawing that is made in preparation for a planned final image. Studies can be used to determine the appearances of specific parts of the completed image, or for experimenting with the best approach for accomplishing the end goal. However a well-crafted study can be a piece of art in its own right, and many hours of careful work can go into completing a study.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drawing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Individuals display differences in their ability to produce visually accurate drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A visually accurate drawing is described as being "recognized as a particular object at a particular time and in a particular space, rendered with little addition of visual detail that can not be seen in the object represented or with little deletion of visual detail”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Investigative studies have aimed to explain the reasons why some individuals draw better than others. One study posited four key abilities in the drawing process: perception of objects being drawn, ability to make good representational decisions, motor skills required for mark-making and the drawer's own perception of their drawing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621-29">[29]</a></sup> Following this hypothesis, several studies have sought to conclude which of these processes are most significant in affecting the accuracy of drawings.</p>
<p><i><b>Motor function</b></i> Motor function is an important physical component in the 'Production Phase' of the drawing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It has been suggested that motor function plays a role in drawing ability, though its effects are not significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">Perception</a></b></i> It has been suggested that an individual's ability to perceive an object they are drawing is the most important stage in the drawing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_1997_609.E2.80.93621-29">[29]</a></sup> This suggestion is supported by the discovery of a robust relationship between perception and drawing ability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>This evidence acted as the basis of <a href="/wiki/Betty_Edwards" title="Betty Edwards">Betty Edwards</a>' how-to drawing book, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drawing_on_the_Right_Side_of_the_Brain" title="Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain">Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Edwards aimed to teach her readers how to draw, based on the development of the reader's perceptual abilities.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the influential artist and art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> emphasised the importance of perception in the drawing process in his book The Elements of Drawing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He stated that "For I am nearly convinced, that once we see keenly enough, there is very little difficult in drawing what we see".</p>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Visual_memory" title="Visual memory">Visual memory</a></b></i> has also been shown to influence one's ability to create visually accurate drawings. <a href="/wiki/Short-term_memory" title="Short-term memory">Short-term memory</a> plays an important part in drawing as one’s gaze shifts between the object they are drawing and the drawing itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
