<p><b>Robert William "Bob" Haymes</b> (March 29, 1923 – January 27, 1989),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> also known by the <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage names</a> <b>Robert Stanton</b> and <b>Bob Stanton</b>, was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer" title="Singer">singer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Songwriter" title="Songwriter">songwriter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_personality" title="Radio personality">radio</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_host" title="Television host">television host</a>. He is best remembered for co-writing the song "<a href="/wiki/That%27s_All_(1952_song)" title="That's All (1952 song)">That's All</a>", part of the <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Songbook" title="Great American Songbook">Great American Songbook</a>. He was the younger brother of singer and actor <a href="/wiki/Dick_Haymes" title="Dick Haymes">Dick Haymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Haymes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haymes was born in <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>. His mother was Marguerite Wilson, an American of <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> descent. Benjamin Haymes, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires,_Argentina" title="Buenos Aires, Argentina">Buenos Aires, Argentina</a> is listed as Bob's father on his birth certificate. Bob's older brother by seven years was actor, singer <a href="/wiki/Dick_Haymes" title="Dick Haymes">Dick Haymes</a>, who was born in Argentina. Marguerite left Argentina to live in New York and eventually moved to Europe with her sons. Bob spent much of his youth living in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Paris</a> in a townhouse overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Rodin_Museum" title="Rodin Museum">Rodin Museum</a>. As a youth, he attended boarding schools throughout Europe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Haymes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haymes began his career in the early 1940s as a vocalist in the bands of Carl Hoff and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Chester" title="Bob Chester">Bob Chester</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1942 Haymes began work, under the name "Bob Stanton", for the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/Gillette_Cavalcade_of_Sports" title="Gillette Cavalcade of Sports">Gillette Cavalcade of Sports</a></i>. He continued with the program until 1946, when it was turned into a television show on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>. He became the host of the television show until 1949. He served as the host of other NBC shows as well, all under the name "Bob Stanton", including <a href="/wiki/Game_show" title="Game show">game show</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Campus_Hoopla" title="Campus Hoopla">Campus Hoopla</a></i> during the show's run from 1946 to 1947, <i><a href="/wiki/Television_Screen_Magazine" title="Television Screen Magazine">Television Screen Magazine</a></i> in 1948, the country music show <i><a href="/wiki/Village_Barn" title="Village Barn">Village Barn</a></i> in 1949, and the show <i>Around the Town</i> in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this time Haymes began acting in films, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Is_Everybody_Happy%3F_(1943_film)" title="Is Everybody Happy? (1943 film)">Is Everybody Happy?</a></i> (1943) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Winkle_Goes_to_War" title="Mr. Winkle Goes to War">Mr. Winkle Goes to War</a></i> (1944). In several films, including the 1945 <i><a href="/wiki/Abbott_and_Costello_in_Hollywood" title="Abbott and Costello in Hollywood">Abbott and Costello in Hollywood</a></i>, he was credited as "Robert Stanton"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (a screen name that had been used earlier in the 1940s by film actor <a href="/wiki/Kirby_Grant" title="Kirby Grant">Kirby Grant</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup>0</p>
<p>In 1952 he had his only known television acting job, starring in the <a href="/wiki/DuMont_Television_Network" title="DuMont Television Network">DuMont Television Network</a> <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Business" title="It's a Business">It's a Business</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Leo_De_Lyon" title="Leo De Lyon">Leo De Lyon</a>; the two played Broadway song publishers. He appeared under the name "Bob Haymes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1950s, Bob Haymes began work as a songwriter. In 1952, he co-wrote the song "<a href="/wiki/My_Love,_My_Love" title="My Love, My Love">My Love, My Love</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Nick_Acquaviva" title="Nick Acquaviva">Nick Acquaviva</a>, which became a hit when recorded by <a href="/wiki/Joni_James" title="Joni James">Joni James</a> (Acquaviva's eventual sister-in-law) later that year. That same year, Haymes copyrighted the song entitled, "C'est Tout," which was the early incarnation of his most notable song, "<a href="/wiki/That%27s_All_(1952_song)" title="That's All (1952 song)">That's All</a>." In 1953, he refined the song's lyrics with Alan Brandt, who was subsequently credited as co-writer.</p>
<p>The song was first performed by <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a> in 1957, and became a hit when recorded by <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Darin" title="Bobby Darin">Bobby Darin</a> in 1959. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It has since been recorded by dozens of artists including <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mel_Torm%C3%A9" title="Mel Tormé">Mel Tormé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mathis" title="Johnny Mathis">Johnny Mathis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bubl%C3%A9" title="Michael Bublé">Michael Bublé</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rod_Stewart" title="Rod Stewart">Rod Stewart</a>.</p>
<p>In the mid-50s he co-wrote the song "They Say It's Spring" with Marty Clark; the song was made popular by jazz singer <a href="/wiki/Blossom_Dearie" title="Blossom Dearie">Blossom Dearie</a>, who recorded it on her 1957 album <i><a href="/wiki/Give_Him_the_Ooh-La-La_(album)" title="Give Him the Ooh-La-La (album)">Give Him the Ooh-La-La</a></i>. During that same time, he wrote the song "I Never Get Enough of You", which was recorded by his brother, Dick.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1968, Bob Haymes served as the national television director for <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1968" title="United States presidential election, 1968">presidential campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Haymes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>During the 1970s and 1980s, Haymes owned and operated an audio visual production company and continued to write music. In 1984, he recorded his final album, <i>That's All: Bob Haymes Sings &amp; Plays Bob Haymes</i>. The album was recorded in Haymes's studio, That's Not All Studios, in <a href="/wiki/Port_Washington,_New_York" title="Port Washington, New York">Port Washington, New York</a>, and released by Serendipity Recordings, Inc. It included four new songs he wrote during the early 1980s after meeting <a href="/wiki/Dee_Workman_Benedict" title="Dee Workman Benedict">Dee Workman</a>. The album, including the new songs, is still for limited sale by private collectors, on Amazon.com and eBay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Haymes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haymes married Workman on January 26, 1985. His step-daughter from that marriage is film and television actress <a href="/wiki/Eaddy_Mays" title="Eaddy Mays">Eaddy Mays</a>. Haymes and Workman remained married until his death on <a href="/wiki/Hilton_Head_Island,_South_Carolina" title="Hilton Head Island, South Carolina">Hilton Head Island, South Carolina</a> from <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a> (ALS) on January 28, 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solid-biography_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-solid-biography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
