<p><b>Edwin Mattison McMillan</b> (September 18, 1907 – September 7, 1991) was an American physicist and Nobel laureate credited with being the first-ever to produce a <a href="/wiki/Transuranium_element" title="Transuranium element">transuranium element</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>. For this, he shared the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenn_Seaborg" title="Glenn Seaborg">Glenn Seaborg</a> in 1951.</p>
<p>A graduate of <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>, he earned his doctorate from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in 1933, and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Radiation_Laboratory" title="Berkeley Radiation Laboratory">Berkeley Radiation Laboratory</a>, where he discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen-15" title="Oxygen-15">oxygen-15</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-10" title="Beryllium-10">beryllium-10</a>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, he worked on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radar" title="Microwave radar">microwave radar</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT_Radiation_Laboratory" title="MIT Radiation Laboratory">MIT Radiation Laboratory</a>, and on <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Electronics_Laboratory" title="Navy Electronics Laboratory">Navy Radio and Sound Laboratory</a>. In 1942 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, the wartime effort to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bombs</a>, and helped establish the project's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos Laboratory">Los Alamos Laboratory</a> where the bombs were designed. He led teams working on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun-type_nuclear_weapon" title="Gun-type nuclear weapon">gun-type nuclear weapon</a> design, and also participated in the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Implosion-type_nuclear_weapon" title="Implosion-type nuclear weapon">implosion-type nuclear weapon</a>.</p>
<p>McMillan co-invented the <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">synchrotron</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Veksler" title="Vladimir Veksler">Vladimir Veksler</a>. He returned to the Radiation Laboratory after the war, and built them. In 1954 he was appointed associate director of the Radiation Laboratory, being promoted to deputy director in 1958. On the death of Lawrence that year, he became director, and he stayed in that position until his retirement in 1973.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McMillan was born in <a href="/wiki/Redondo_Beach,_California" title="Redondo Beach, California">Redondo Beach, California</a>, on September 18, 1907, the son of Edwin Harbaugh McMillan and his wife Anna Marie McMillan née Mattison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> He had a younger sister, Catherine Helen. His father was a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>, as was his father's twin brother, and three of his mother's brothers. On October 18, 1908, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena, California</a>, where he attended McKinley Elementary School from 1913 to 1918, Grant School from 1918 to 1920, and then <a href="/wiki/Pasadena_High_School_(California)" title="Pasadena High School (California)">Pasadena High School</a>, from which he graduated in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oral1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> (Caltech) was only a mile from his home, and he attended some of the public lectures there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993287_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993287-3">[3]</a></sup> He entered Caltech in 1924. He did a research project with <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a> as an undergraduate and received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">degree</a> in 1928 and his <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in 1929,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> writing an unpublished thesis on "An improved method for the determination of the radium content of rocks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He then took his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in 1933, writing his thesis on the "Deflection of a Beam of HCI Molecules in a Non-Homogeneous Electric Field" under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Condon" title="Edward Condon">Edward Condon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawrence_Berkeley_Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1932, McMillan was awarded a <a href="/wiki/National_Research_Council_(United_States)" title="National Research Council (United States)">National Research Council</a> fellowship, allowing him to attend a university of his choice for postdoctoral study. With his PhD complete, although it was not formally accepted until January 12, 1933,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oral1-2">[2]</a></sup> he accepted an offer from <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Lawrence" title="Ernest Lawrence">Ernest Lawrence</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Radiation_Laboratory" title="Berkeley Radiation Laboratory">Berkeley Radiation Laboratory</a>, which Lawrence had founded the year before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofgrenAbelsonHelmolz1992118.E2.80.93119_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofgrenAbelsonHelmolz1992118.E2.80.93119-6">[6]</a></sup> McMillan's initial work there involved attempting to measure the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Otto_Stern" title="Otto Stern">Otto Stern</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Immanuel_Estermann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Immanuel Estermann (page does not exist)">Immanuel Estermann</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Estermann" title="de:Immanuel Estermann">de</a>)</span> were able to carry out these measurements first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oral1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996217.E2.80.93218_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996217.E2.80.93218-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main focus of the Radiation laboratory at this time was the development of the <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotron</a>, and McMillan, who was appointed to the faculty at Berkeley as an instructor in 1935, soon became involved in the effort. His skill with instrumentation came to the fore, and he contributed improvements to the cyclotron. In particular, he helped develop the process of "<a href="/wiki/Shim_(magnetism)" title="Shim (magnetism)">shimming</a>", adjusting the cyclotron to produce a homogeneous magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288-5">[5]</a></sup> Working with <a href="/wiki/M._Stanley_Livingston" title="M. Stanley Livingston">M. Stanley Livingston</a>, he discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen-15" title="Oxygen-15">oxygen-15</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_oxygen" title="Isotopes of oxygen">isotope of oxygen</a> that emits <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>. To produce it, they bombarded <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> gas with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteron" title="Deuteron">deuterons</a>. This was mixed with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and oxygen to produce water, which was then collected with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>. Radioactivity was found concentrated in it, proving that it was in the oxygen. This was followed by an investigation of the absorption of <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> produced by bombarding <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> with protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996217.E2.80.93218_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996217.E2.80.93218-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1935, McMillan, Lawrence and Robert Thornton carried out cyclotron experiments with deuteron beams that produced a series of unexpected results. Deuterons fused with a target <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>, transmuting the target to a heavier isotope while ejecting a proton. Their experiments indicated a nuclear interaction at lower energies than would be expected from a simple calculation of the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb_barrier" title="Coulomb barrier">Coulomb barrier</a> between a deuteron and a target nucleus. Berkeley theoretical physicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Oppenheimer" title="Robert Oppenheimer">Robert Oppenheimer</a> and his graduate student <a href="/wiki/Melba_Phillips" title="Melba Phillips">Melba Phillips</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Oppenheimer%E2%80%93Phillips_process" title="Oppenheimer–Phillips process">Oppenheimer–Phillips process</a> to explain the phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996218.E2.80.93219_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996218.E2.80.93219-8">[8]</a></sup> McMillan became an <a href="/wiki/Assistant_professor" title="Assistant professor">assistant professor</a> in 1936, and an <a href="/wiki/Associate_professor" title="Associate professor">associate professor</a> in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Ruben" title="Samuel Ruben">Samuel Ruben</a>, he also discovered the isotope <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-10" title="Beryllium-10">beryllium-10</a> in 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993288-5">[5]</a></sup> This was both interesting and difficult to isolate due to its extraordinarily long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a>, about 1.39 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_neptunium">Discovery of neptunium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery of neptunium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> in <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Strassmann" title="Fritz Strassmann">Fritz Strassmann</a> in 1939, McMillan began experimenting with uranium. He bombarded it with <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> produced in the Radiation Laboratory's 37-inch cyclotron through bombarding <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> with deuterons. In addition to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission_product" title="Nuclear fission product">nuclear fission products</a> reported by Hahn and Strassmann, they detected two unusual radioactive isotopes, one with a half-life of about 2.3 days, and the other with one of around 23 minutes. McMillan identified the short-lived isotope as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium-239" title="Uranium-239">uranium-239</a>, which had been reported by Hahn and Strassmann. McMillan suspected that the other was an isotope of a new, undiscovered element, with an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> of 93.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93222_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93222-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time it was believed that element 93 would have similar chemistry to <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>, so he began working with <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio Segrè">Emilio Segrè</a>, an expert on that element from his discovery of its <a href="/wiki/Homology_(chemistry)" title="Homology (chemistry)">homolog</a> <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>. Both scientists began their work using the prevailing theory, but Segrè rapidly determined that McMillan's sample was not at all similar to rhenium. Instead, when he reacted it with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF) with a strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> present, it behaved like members of the <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93223_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93223-11">[11]</a></sup> Since these comprise a large percentage of fission products, Segrè and McMillan decided that the half-life must have been simply another fission product, titling the paper "An Unsuccessful Search for Transuranium Elements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>McMillan realized that his 1939 work with Segrè had failed to test the chemical reactions of the radioactive source with sufficient rigor. In a new experiment, McMillan tried subjecting the unknown substance to HF in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a>, something he had not done before. This reaction resulted in the sample <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitating</a> with the HF, an action that definitively ruled out the possibility that the unknown substance was a rare earth. In May 1940, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Abelson" title="Philip Abelson">Philip Abelson</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institution_for_Science" title="Carnegie Institution for Science">Carnegie Institute</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>, who had independently also attempted to separate the isotope with the 2.3 day half-life, visited Berkeley for a short vacation and they began to collaborate. Abelson observed that the isotope with the 2.3-day half-life did not have chemistry like any known element, but was more similar to uranium than a rare earth. This allowed the source to be isolated and later, in 1945, led to the classification of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actinide_series" title="Actinide series">actinide series</a>. As a final step, McMillan and Abelson prepared a much larger sample of bombarded uranium that had a prominent 23-minute half-life from <sup>239</sup>U and demonstrated conclusively that the unknown 2.3-day half-life increased in strength in concert with a decrease in the 23-minute activity through the following reaction:</p>

<p>This proved that the unknown radioactive source originated from the decay of uranium and, coupled with the previous observation that the source was different chemically from all known elements, proved beyond all doubt that a new element had been discovered. McMillan and Abelson published their results in a paper entitled <i>Radioactive Element 93</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i> on May 27, 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93223_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996221.E2.80.93223-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EL93_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EL93-13">[13]</a></sup> They did not propose a name for the element in the paper, but they soon decided on "neptunium" since uranium had been named after the planet <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> is the next planet beyond in our solar system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993289_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993289-14">[14]</a></sup> McMillan suddenly departed at this point, leaving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenn_Seaborg" title="Glenn Seaborg">Glenn Seaborg</a> to pursue this line of research, which led to the second transuranium element, <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. In 1951, McMillan shared the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> with Seaborg "for their discoveries in the chemistry of the transuranium elements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McMillan's abrupt departure was caused by the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in Europe. In November 1940, he began working at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT_Radiation_Laboratory" title="MIT Radiation Laboratory">MIT Radiation Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, where he participated in the development and testing of airborne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radar" title="Microwave radar">microwave radar</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofgrenAbelsonHelmolz1992118.E2.80.93119_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofgrenAbelsonHelmolz1992118.E2.80.93119-6">[6]</a></sup> He conducted tests in April 1941 with the radar operating from an old <a href="/wiki/Douglas_B-18_Bolo" title="Douglas B-18 Bolo">Douglas B-18 Bolo</a> <a href="/wiki/Medium_bomber" title="Medium bomber">medium bomber</a>. Flying over the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Submarine_Base_New_London" title="Naval Submarine Base New London">Naval Submarine Base New London</a> with <a href="/wiki/Luis_Walter_Alvarez" title="Luis Walter Alvarez">Luis Walter Alvarez</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Chief_Marshal" title="Air Chief Marshal">Air Chief Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Dowding" title="Hugh Dowding">Hugh Dowding</a>, they showed that the radar was able to detect the <a href="/wiki/Conning_tower" title="Conning tower">conning tower</a> of a partly submerged submarine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup> MacMillan married Elsie Walford Blumer in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>, on June 7, 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993291_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993291-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup> Her father was George Blumer, Dean Emeritus of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale_Medical_School" title="Yale Medical School">Yale Medical School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> Her sister Mary was Lawrence's wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996216_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996216-18">[18]</a></sup> The McMillans had three children: Ann Bradford, David Mattison and Stephen Walker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obit-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>McMillan joined the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Electronics_Laboratory" title="Navy Electronics Laboratory">Navy Radio and Sound Laboratory</a> near <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> in August 1941. There he worked on a device called a polyscope. The idea, which came from Lawrence, was to use <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> to build up a visual image of the surrounding water. This proved to be far more difficult that doing so with radar, because of objects in the water and variations in water temperature that caused variations in the speed of sound. The polyscope proved to be impractical, and was abandoned. He also, however, developed a sonar training device for submariners, for which he received a patent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993289_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993289-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oppenheimer recruited McMillan to join the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, the wartime effort to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bombs</a>, in September 1942. Initially, he commuted back and forth between San Diego, where his family was, and Berkeley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup> In November he accompanied Oppenheimer on a trip to <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> on which the <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Ranch_School" title="Los Alamos Ranch School">Los Alamos Ranch School</a> was selected as the site of the project's weapons research laboratory, which became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos Laboratory">Los Alamos Laboratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes1986449.E2.80.93451_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes1986449.E2.80.93451-21">[21]</a></sup> With Oppenheimer and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_H._Manley" title="John H. Manley">John H. Manley</a>, he drew up the specifications for the new laboratory's technical buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199362_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199362-22">[22]</a></sup> He recruited personnel for the laboratory, including <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Wilson" title="Robert R. Wilson">Robert R. Wilson</a>, established the test area known as the Anchor Ranch, and scoured the country for technical equipment from machine tools to a cyclotron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199384_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199384-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the laboratory took shape, McMillan became deputy head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun-type_nuclear_weapon" title="Gun-type nuclear weapon">gun-type nuclear weapon</a> effort under Navy <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States_O-6)" title="Captain (United States O-6)">Captain</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_S._Parsons" title="William S. Parsons">William S. Parsons</a>, an ordnance expert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199384_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall199384-23">[23]</a></sup> The plutonium gun, codenamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_Man_nuclear_bomb" title="Thin Man nuclear bomb">Thin Man</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993114_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993114-24">[24]</a></sup> needed a <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">muzzle velocity</a> of at least 3,000 feet (910 m) per second, which they hoped to achieve with a modified Navy <a href="/wiki/3%E2%80%B3/50_caliber_gun" title="3″/50 caliber gun">3-inch antiaircraft gun</a>. The alternative was to build an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Implosion-type_nuclear_weapon" title="Implosion-type nuclear weapon">implosion-type nuclear weapon</a>. McMillan took an early interest in this, watching tests of this concept conducted by <a href="/wiki/Seth_Neddermeyer" title="Seth Neddermeyer">Seth Neddermeyer</a>. The results were not encouraging. Simple explosions resulted in distorted shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes1986477.E2.80.93479.2C_541_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes1986477.E2.80.93479.2C_541-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a> looked at the implosion program in September 1943, and proposed a radical solution involving <a href="/wiki/Explosive_lens" title="Explosive lens">explosive lenses</a>. This would require expertise in explosives, and McMillan urged Oppenheimer to bring in <a href="/wiki/George_Kistiakowsky" title="George Kistiakowsky">George Kistiakowsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993130.E2.80.93133_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993130.E2.80.93133-26">[26]</a></sup> Kistiakowsky joined the laboratory on February 16, 1944, and Parsons's E (Explosives) Division was divided in two, with McMillan as deputy for the gun and Kistiakowsky as deputy for implosion. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993139_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993139-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>McMillan heard disturbing news in April 1944, and drove out to Pajarito Canyon to confer with Segrè. Segrè's group had tested samples of plutonium bred in the Manhattan Project's nuclear reactors and found that it contained quantities of <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-240" title="Plutonium-240">plutonium-240</a>, an isotope that caused spontaneous fission, making Thin Man impractical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993238.E2.80.93239_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993238.E2.80.93239-28">[28]</a></sup> In July 1944, Oppenheimer reorganised the laboratory to make an all-out effort on implosion. McMillan remained in charge of the gun-type weapon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993245_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993245-29">[29]</a></sup> which would now be used only with <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>. This being the case, Thin Man was replaced by a new, scaled-back design called <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">Little Boy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993256.E2.80.93257_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993256.E2.80.93257-30">[30]</a></sup> McMillan was also involved with the implosion as the head of the G-3 Group within the G (Gadget) Division, which was responsible for obtaining measurements and timings on implosion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993272.E2.80.93273_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoddesonHenriksenMeadeWestfall1993272.E2.80.93273-31">[31]</a></sup> and served as the laboratory's liaison with <a href="/wiki/Project_Camel" title="Project Camel">Project Camel</a>, the aerial test program being carried out by Caltech. On July 16, 1945, he was present at the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_(nuclear_test)" title="Trinity (nuclear test)">Trinity nuclear test</a>, when the first implosion bomb was successfully detonated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996225_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996225-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 1945, McMillan's thoughts began to return to cyclotrons. Over time they had gotten larger and larger. A 184-inch cyclotron was under construction at the Radiation Laboratory, but he realised that a more efficient use could be made of the energy used to accelerate particles. By varying the magnetic field used, the particles could be made to move in stable orbits, and higher energies achieved with the same energy input. He dubbed this the "phase stability principle", and the new design a "<a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">synchrotron</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996226.E2.80.93227_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996226.E2.80.93227-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Unknown to McMillan, the synchrotron principle had already been invented by <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Veksler" title="Vladimir Veksler">Vladimir Veksler</a>, who had published his proposal in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> McMillan became aware of Veksler's paper in October 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup> The two began corresponding, and eventually became friends. In 1963 they shared the <a href="/wiki/Atoms_for_Peace_Award" title="Atoms for Peace Award">Atoms for Peace Award</a> for the invention of the synchrotron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LBL_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-LBL-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phase stability principle was tested with the old 37-inch cyclotron at Berkeley after McMillan returned to the Radiation Laboratory in September 1945. When it was found to work, the 184-inch cyclotron was similarly modified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996226.E2.80.93227_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996226.E2.80.93227-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oral3_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Oral3-16">[16]</a></sup> He became a full professor in 1946. In 1954 he was appointed associate director of the Radiation Laboratory. He was promoted to deputy director in 1958. On the death of Lawrence that year, he became director, and he stayed in that position until his retirement in 1973. The laboratory was renamed the Lawrence Radiation Laboratory in 1958. In 1970, it split into the Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory and the Lawrence Livermore Laboratory, and McMillan became director of the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_bio_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_bio-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LBL_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-LBL-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996230_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacksonPanofsky1996230-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>McMillan was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1947, serving as its chairman from 1968 to 1971. He served on the influential General Advisory Committee (GAC) of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a> from 1954 to 1958, and the Commission on High Energy Physics of the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Physics" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Physics">International Union of Pure and Applied Physics</a> from 1960 to 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993290.E2.80.93291_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaborg1993290.E2.80.93291-38">[38]</a></sup> After his retirement from the faculty at Berkeley in 1974, he spent 1974–75 at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>, where he worked on the g minus 2 experiment to measure the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LBL_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-LBL-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>McMillan suffered the first of a series of strokes in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LBL_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-LBL-36">[36]</a></sup> He died at his home in <a href="/wiki/El_Cerrito,_California" title="El Cerrito, California">El Cerrito, California</a>, from complications from diabetes on September 7, 1991. He was survived by his wife and three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obit_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Obit-19">[19]</a></sup> His gold Nobel Prize medal is in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_American_History" title="National Museum of American History">National Museum of American History</a>, a division of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Smithsonian" title="The Smithsonian">The Smithsonian</a>, in Washington DC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NMAH_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NMAH-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
