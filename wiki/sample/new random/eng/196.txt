<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Stop codon</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>, a <b>stop codon</b> (or <b>termination codon</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a> triplet within <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> that signals a termination of <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a> into proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">Proteins</a> are based on polypeptides, which are unique sequences of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>. Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codon" title="Codon">codons</a> in messenger RNA (from DNA) correspond to the addition of an amino acid to a growing polypeptide chain, which may ultimately become a protein. Stop codons signal the termination of this process by binding <a href="/wiki/Release_factor" title="Release factor">release factors</a>, which cause the ribosomal subunits to disassociate, releasing the amino acid chain. While start codons need nearby sequences or initiation factors to start translation, a stop codon alone is sufficient to initiate termination.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the standard genetic code, there are three different stop codons:</p>
<ul>
<li>in RNA:
<ul>
<li>UAG ("amber")</li>
<li>UAA ("ochre")</li>
<li>UGA ("opal")</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>in DNA:
<ul>
<li>TAG ("amber")</li>
<li>TAA ("ochre")</li>
<li>TGA ("opal" or "umber")</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code#Variations_to_the_standard_genetic_code" title="Genetic code">variations</a></div>
<p>In 2007, the UGA codon has been identified as the codon coding for <a href="/wiki/Selenocysteine" title="Selenocysteine">Selenocysteine</a> (Sec). This amino acid is found in 25 <a href="/wiki/Selenoprotein" title="Selenoprotein">selenoproteins</a> where it is located in the active site of the protein. Transcription of this codon is enabled by the proximity of the <a href="/wiki/SECIS_element" title="SECIS element">SECIS element</a> (SElenoCysteine Incorporation Sequence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The UAG codon can translate into <a href="/wiki/Pyrrolysine" title="Pyrrolysine">pyrrolysine</a> in a similar manner.</p>
<p>Distribution of stop codons within the genome of an organism is non-random and can correlate with <a href="/wiki/GC-content" title="GC-content">GC-content</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, the <i>E. coli</i> K-12 genome contains 2705 TAA (63%), 1257 TGA (29%), and 326 TAG (8%) stop codons (GC content 50.8%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Also the substrates for the stop codons release factor 1 or release factor 2 are strongly correlated to the abundance of stop codons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Large scale study of bacteria with a broad range of GC-contents shows that while the frequency of occurrence of TAA is negatively correlated to the GC-content and the frequency of occurrence of TGA is positively correlated to the GC-content, the frequency of occurrence of the TAG stop codon, which is often the minimally used stop codon in a genome, is not influenced by the GC-content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonsense_mutations" title="Nonsense mutations">Nonsense mutations</a> are changes in DNA sequence that introduce a premature stop codon, causing any resulting protein to be abnormally shortened. This often causes a loss of function in the protein, as critical parts of the amino acid chain are no longer created. Because of this terminology, stop codons have also been referred to as <b>nonsense codons</b>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amber.2C_ochre.2C_and_opal_nomenclature">Amber, ochre, and opal nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amber, ochre, and opal nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stop codons were historically given many different names, as they each corresponded to a distinct class of mutants that all behaved in a similar manner. These mutants were first isolated within <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophages</a> (<a href="/wiki/Enterobacteria_phage_T4" title="Enterobacteria phage T4">T4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lambda_phage" title="Lambda phage">lambda</a>), <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> that infect the bacteria <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>. Mutations in viral genes weakened their infectious ability, sometimes creating viruses that were able to infect and grow within only certain varieties of <i>E coli</i>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>amber mutations (UAG)</dt>
<dd>were the first set of <a href="/wiki/Nonsense_mutation" title="Nonsense mutation">nonsense mutations</a> to be discovered, isolated by Richard Epstein and Charles Steinberg and named after their friend Harris Bernstein (whose last name means "<a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">amber</a>" in German).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Viruses with amber mutations are characterized by their ability to infect only certain strains of bacteria, known as <b>amber suppressors</b>. These bacteria carry their own mutation that allows a recovery of function in the mutant viruses. For example, a mutation in the tRNA that recognizes the amber stop codon allows translation to "read through" the codon and produce a full-length protein, thereby recovering the normal form of the protein and "suppressing" the amber mutation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Thus, amber mutants are an entire class of virus mutants that can grow in bacteria that contain amber suppressor mutations. Similar suppressors are known for ochre and opal stop codons as well.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a> mutation (UAA)</dt>
<dd>was the second stop codon mutation to be discovered. Given a color name to match the name of amber mutants, ochre mutant viruses had a similar property in that they recovered infectious ability within certain suppressor strains of bacteria. The set of ochre suppressors was distinct from amber suppressors, so ochre mutants were inferred to correspond to a different nucleotide triplet. Through a series of mutation experiments comparing these mutants with each other and other known amino acid codons, <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Brenner" title="Sydney Brenner">Sydney Brenner</a> concluded that the amber and ochre mutations corresponded to the nucleotide triplets "UAG" and "UAA".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brenner_1965_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-brenner_1965-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a> mutations or <a href="/wiki/Umber" title="Umber">umber</a> mutations (UGA)</dt>
<dd>the third and last stop codon in the standard genetic code was discovered soon after, corresponding to the nucleotide triplet "UGA".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Nonsense mutations that created this premature stop codon were later called <i>opal mutations</i> or <i>umber mutations</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hidden_stops">Hidden stops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hidden stops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Hidden stops</b> are non-stop codons that would be read as stop codons if they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frameshift" title="Frameshift">frameshifted</a> +1 or -1. These prematurely terminate translation if the corresponding frame-shift (such as due to a ribosomal RNA slip) occurs before the hidden stop. It is hypothesised that this decreases resource waste on nonfunctional proteins and the production of potential <a href="/wiki/Cytotoxicity" title="Cytotoxicity">cytotoxins</a>. Researchers at <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">Louisiana State University</a> propose the <i><a href="/wiki/Ambush_hypothesis" title="Ambush hypothesis">ambush hypothesis</a></i>, that hidden stops are selected for. Codons that can form hidden stops are used in genomes more frequently compared to synonymous codons that would otherwise code for the same amino acid. Unstable <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">rRNA</a> in an organism correlates with a higher frequency of hidden stops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This hypothesis however could not be validated with a larger data set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stop-codons and hidden stops together are collectively referred as stop-signals. Researchers at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Memphis" title="University of Memphis">University of Memphis</a> found that the ratios of the stop-signals on the three reading frames of a genome (referred to as translation stop-signals ratio or TSSR) of genetically related bacteria, despite their great differences in gene contents, are much alike. This nearly identical Genomic-TSSR value of genetically related bacteria may suggest that bacterial genome expansion is limited by their unique stop-signals bias of that bacterial species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translational_readthrough">Translational readthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Translational readthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Stop codon suppression</b> or <b>translational readthrough</b> occurs when in translation a stop codon is interpreted as a sense codon, that is, when a (standard) amino acid is 'encoded' by the stop codon. Mutated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNAs</a> can be the cause of readthrough, but also certain <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a> motifs close to the stop codon. Translational readthrough is very common in viruses and bacteria, and has also been found as a gene regulatory principle in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25247702_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25247702-14">[14]</a></sup> This kind of endogenous translational readthough constitutes a variation of the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>, because a stop codon codes for an amnio acid. In the case of human <a href="/wiki/Malate_dehydrogenase" title="Malate dehydrogenase">malate dehydrogenase</a>, the stop codon is read through with a frequency of about 4% and the amino acids tryptophan and arginine are encoded by the stop codon. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid27881739_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid27881739-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonstop_mutations">Nonstop mutations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nonstop mutations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="nonstop_mutation"></span></p>
<p>A <b>nonstop mutation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Point_mutation" title="Point mutation">point mutation</a> that occurs within a stop codon. Nonstop mutations cause the continued translation of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> strand into an untranslated region. Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptides" title="Polypeptides">polypeptides</a> resulting from a gene with a nonstop mutation are nonfunctional due to their extreme length. Nonstop mutations differ from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonsense_mutations" title="Nonsense mutations">nonsense mutations</a> in that they do not create a stop codon but, instead, delete one.</p>
<p>Nonstop mutations have been linked with several congenital diseases including <a href="/wiki/Congenital_adrenal_hyperplasia" title="Congenital adrenal hyperplasia">congenital adrenal hyperplasia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_anterior_segment_dysgenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Variable anterior segment dysgenesis (page does not exist)">variable anterior segment dysgenesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_neurogastrointestinal_encephalomyopathy" title="Mitochondrial neurogastrointestinal encephalomyopathy">mitochondrial neurogastrointestinal encephalomyopathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_a_watermark">Use as a watermark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_codon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use as a watermark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010 when <a href="/wiki/Craig_Venter" title="Craig Venter">Craig Venter</a> unveiled the first fully functioning, reproducing cell controlled by <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic DNA</a> he described how his team used frequent stop codons to create <a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_laboratorium#Watermarks" title="Mycoplasma laboratorium">watermarks</a> in RNA and DNA to help confirm the results were indeed synthetic (and not contaminated or otherwise), using it to encode authors names and website addresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
