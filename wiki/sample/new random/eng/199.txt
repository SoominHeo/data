<p><b>David Paul Scofield</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Order of the Companions of Honour">CH</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (21 January 1922 – 19 March 2008), known professionally as <b>Paul Scofield</b>, was an English actor of stage and screen who was known for his striking presence, distinctive voice, and for the clarity and effortless intensity of his delivery. Regarded as one of the greatest Shakespearean actors of all time, Scofield preferred the stage over film. This, and his decision to put his family first, has meant that he has never been as well known outside the United Kingdom as some other actors.</p>
<p>Outside his homeland, Scofield is best known for his <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award" title="BAFTA Award">BAFTA Award</a>- winning performance as Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Thomas More</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1966_in_film" title="1966 in film">1966 film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)" title="A Man for All Seasons (1966 film)">A Man for All Seasons</a></i>. Scofield had originally played the same part <a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons" title="A Man for All Seasons">on stage</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a> and in a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a>-winning performance on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a>.</p>
<p>Actress <a href="/wiki/Helen_Mirren" title="Helen Mirren">Helen Mirren</a>, who appeared with Scofield in the 1989 film <i><a href="/wiki/When_the_Whales_Came" title="When the Whales Came">When the Whales Came</a></i>, said of him, "He aspires to the soul rather than the character. He has no sense of personal ambition. He's one of our great, great actors. We're lucky to have him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, actor and filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a>, who played Scofield's son in <a href="/wiki/Franco_Zeffirelli" title="Franco Zeffirelli">Franco Zeffirelli</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(1990_film)" title="Hamlet (1990 film)">Hamlet</a></i>, compared the experience to being "thrown into the ring with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Tyson" title="Mike Tyson">Mike Tyson</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paul Scofield was born in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, the son of Mary and Edward Harry Scofield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When Scofield was a few weeks old, his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Hurstpierpoint" title="Hurstpierpoint">Hurstpierpoint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, where his father served as the headmaster at the Hurstpierpoint Church of England School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-player_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-player-5">[5]</a></sup> Scofield told his biographer, <a href="/wiki/Garry_O%27Connor" title="Garry O'Connor">Garry O'Connor</a>, that his upbringing was divided. His father was an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> and his mother a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>. Baptised into his mother's faith, Scofield said, "some days we were little Protestants and, on others, we were all devout little Catholics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He added, "A lack of direction in spiritual matters is still with me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scofield recalls, "I was a dunce at school. But at the age of twelve I went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varndean_Grammar_School_for_Boys" title="Varndean Grammar School for Boys">Varndean School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a> where I discovered <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>. They did one of his plays every year, and I lived just for that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-screen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-screen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1939, Scofield left school at the age of seventeen and began training at the <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a> Repertory Theatre. Shortly after the outbreak of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Scofield arrived for a physical examination and was ruled unfit for service in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>. He later recalled, "They found I had crossed toes. I was unable to wear boots. I was deeply ashamed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scofield began his stage career in 1940 with a debut performance in American playwright <a href="/wiki/Eugene_O%27Neill" title="Eugene O'Neill">Eugene O'Neill</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Desire_Under_the_Elms" title="Desire Under the Elms">Desire Under the Elms</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Theatre" title="Westminster Theatre">Westminster Theatre</a>, and was soon being compared to <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a>. He played at the Old Rep in Birmingham. From there he went to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare_Memorial_Theatre" title="Shakespeare Memorial Theatre">Shakespeare Memorial Theatre</a> in Stratford, where he starred in <a href="/wiki/Walter_Nugent_Monck" title="Walter Nugent Monck">Walter Nugent Monck</a>'s 1947 revival of <i><a href="/wiki/Pericles,_Prince_of_Tyre" title="Pericles, Prince of Tyre">Pericles, Prince of Tyre</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fref_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-fref-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1947, Scofield appeared as Hamlet at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">Royal National Theatre</a> alongside a then unknown <a href="/wiki/Claire_Bloom" title="Claire Bloom">Claire Bloom</a> as <a href="/wiki/Ophelia" title="Ophelia">Ophelia</a>. In her later book, <i><a href="/wiki/Leaving_a_Doll%27s_House:_A_Memoir" title="Leaving a Doll's House: A Memoir">Leaving a Doll's House: A Memoir</a></i>, Bloom recalls that during the production she had a serious crush on Scofield. As Scofield was happily married and the father of a son, Bloom hoped only "to be flirted with and taken some notice of." She later recalled, "I could never make up my mind which of my two Hamlets I found the more devastating: the openly homosexual, charismatic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Helpmann" title="Robert Helpmann">Helpmann</a>, or the charming, shy young man from Sussex."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked about Claire Bloom decades later, Scofield recalled, "Sixteen years old I think – so very young and necessarily inexperienced, she looked lovely, she acted with a daunting assurance which belied entirely her inexperience of almost timid reticence. She was a very good Ophelia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scofield's versatility at the height of his career is exemplified by his starring roles in theatrical productions as diverse as the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Expresso_Bongo" title="Expresso Bongo">Expresso Bongo</a></i> (1958) and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brook" title="Peter Brook">Peter Brook</a>'s celebrated production of <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i> (1962).</p>
<p>In his memoir <i>Threads of Time</i>, director Peter Brook wrote about Scofield's versatility:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The door at the back of the set opened, and a small man entered. He was wearing a black suit, steel-rimmed glasses and holding a suitcase. For a moment we wondered who this stranger was and why he was wandering onto our stage. Then we realised that it was Paul, transformed. His tall body had shrunk; he had become insignificant. The new character now possessed him entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a career devoted chiefly to the classical theatre, Scofield starred in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> plays and played the title role in <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone</a></i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Hall_(theatre_director)" title="Peter Hall (theatre director)">Peter Hall</a>'s production for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">Royal National Theatre</a> (1977). Highlights of his career in modern theatre include the roles of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Sir Thomas More</a> in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons" title="A Man for All Seasons">A Man for All Seasons</a></i> (1960); Charles Dyer in Dyer's play <i><a href="/wiki/Staircase_(play)" title="Staircase (play)">Staircase</a></i>, staged by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Shakespeare_Company" title="Royal Shakespeare Company">Royal Shakespeare Company</a> in 1966; the definitive Laurie in <a href="/wiki/John_Osborne" title="John Osborne">John Osborne</a>'s <i>A Hotel in Amsterdam</i> (1968); and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salieri" title="Antonio Salieri">Antonio Salieri</a> in the original stage production of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Shaffer" title="Peter Shaffer">Peter Shaffer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Amadeus" title="Amadeus">Amadeus</a></i> (1979).</p>
<p>He was subsequently the voice of the Dragon in another play by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a>, a children's drama <i>The Thwarting of Baron Bolligrew.</i> <i>Expresso Bongo</i>, <i>Staircase</i> and <i>Amadeus</i> were filmed with other actors, but Scofield starred in the screen versions of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)" title="A Man for All Seasons (1966 film)">A Man for All Seasons</a></i> (1966) and <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear_(1971_UK_film)" title="King Lear (1971 UK film)">King Lear</a></i> (1971).</p>
<p>Other major screen roles include the Art-obsessed Nazi colonel Von Waldheim in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Train_(1964_film)" title="The Train (1964 film)">The Train</a></i> (1964), Strether in a 1977 TV adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ambassadors" title="The Ambassadors">The Ambassadors</a></i>, Tobias in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Delicate_Balance_(film)" title="A Delicate Balance (film)">A Delicate Balance</a></i> (1973), Professor Moroi in the film of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Ny%C3%ADri" title="János Nyíri">János Nyíri</a>'s <i>If Winter Comes</i> (1980), for BBC Television; <a href="/wiki/Mark_Van_Doren" title="Mark Van Doren">Mark Van Doren</a> in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Redford" title="Robert Redford">Robert Redford</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Quiz_Show_(film)" title="Quiz Show (film)">Quiz Show</a></i> (1994), and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Danforth" title="Thomas Danforth">Thomas Danforth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Hytner" title="Nicholas Hytner">Nicholas Hytner</a>'s film adaptation (1996) of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Miller" title="Arthur Miller">Arthur Miller</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crucible_(1996_film)" title="The Crucible (1996 film)">The Crucible</a></i>.</p>
<p>According to the DVD extras documentary for the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shooting_Party" title="The Shooting Party">The Shooting Party</a></i> (1985), in the very first shot of the very first day of filming, all the male lead actors, including Paul Scofield, who was playing Sir Randolph Nettleby, were to come into shot on a horse-drawn shooting brake driven by the renowned film horse-master George Mossman. As they turned the first corner, the plank that Mossman was standing on broke in two and he was hurled forward and down, falling between the sets of wheels and taking the reins with him. He was struck by a horse's hoof and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concussed" title="Concussed">concussed</a>. The horses shied and broke into a gallop.</p>
<p>Rupert Frazer admitted that he was the first to jump off, landing safely, but bruised. Out of control, the horses turned to the right when confronted by a stone wall, causing the shooting brake to roll completely, catapulting the actors into a pile of scaffolding that had been stacked next to the wall. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hardy" title="Robert Hardy">Robert Hardy</a> stood up and realised to his amazement that he was unhurt. He looked across to see Edward Fox stand up, "turn completely green and collapse in a heap". He had broken five ribs and his shoulder-blade. He noticed that Paul Scofield was lying very still on the ground "and I saw that his shin-bone was sticking out through his trousers". As the film takes place in October during the partridge-shooting season, the filmmakers had to make a choice whether to delay filming for a year or re-cast. <a href="/wiki/James_Mason" title="James Mason">James Mason</a> had just finished filming <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Fischer_of_Geneva" title="Doctor Fischer of Geneva">Doctor Fischer of Geneva</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>. The <i>Shooting Party</i> schedule was changed to allow him to take over the part of Sir Randolph Nettleby six weeks later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> His broken leg also deprived Scofield of the part of <a href="/wiki/O%27Brien_(Nineteen_Eighty-Four)" title="O'Brien (Nineteen Eighty-Four)">O'Brien</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four_(film)" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four (film)">Nineteen Eighty-Four</a>,</i> in which he was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burton" title="Richard Burton">Richard Burton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scofield was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in the <a href="/wiki/1956_New_Year_Honours" title="1956 New Year Honours">1956 New Year Honours</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He won the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> for <i>A Man for All Seasons</i> and was nominated as <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor">Best Supporting Actor</a> for <i>Quiz Show</i>. Theatrical accolades include a 1962 <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> for <i>A Man for All Seasons</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours_and_awards">Honours and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honours and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1969, Scofield became the sixth performer to win the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_persons_who_have_won_Academy,_Emmy,_Grammy,_and_Tony_Awards#Winners_of_three_out_of_the_four_major_entertainment_awards" title="List of persons who have won Academy, Emmy, Grammy, and Tony Awards">Triple Crown of Acting</a>, winning an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actor_in_a_Miniseries_or_a_Movie" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Miniseries or a Movie">Outstanding Single Performance by an Actor in a Leading Role</a> for <i>Male of the Species</i>.</p>
<p>He was also one of only eight actors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_persons_who_have_won_Academy,_Emmy,_Grammy,_and_Tony_Awards#Winners_of_an_Academy_Award_and_a_Tony_Award_for_the_same_role" title="List of persons who have won Academy, Emmy, Grammy, and Tony Awards">to win both the Tony and the Oscar for the same role on stage and film</a>, for <i>A Man for All Seasons</i>.</p>
<p>Scofield declined the honour of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knighthood" title="Knighthood">knighthood</a> on three occasions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasons_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-seasons-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-19">[19]</a></sup> but was appointed <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> in 1956 and became a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Order of the Companions of Honour">Companion of Honour</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2002 he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary degree</a> of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">D. Litt</a> by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked the reason for his decision to decline the knighthood, Scofield responded, "I have every respect for people who are offered [a knighthood] and accept it gratefully. It is just not an aspect of life that I would want."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Gary O'Connor described being knighted as, "The kind of honour from which [Scofield] instinctively recoiled. Never the actor before the part he plays."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, a poll of actors of the Royal Shakespeare Company, including <a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sinden" title="Donald Sinden">Donald Sinden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janet_Suzman" title="Janet Suzman">Janet Suzman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Richardson" title="Ian Richardson">Ian Richardson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antony_Sher" title="Antony Sher">Antony Sher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corin_Redgrave" title="Corin Redgrave">Corin Redgrave</a>, acclaimed Scofield's Lear as the greatest Shakespearean performance ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Scofield appeared in many radio dramas for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a>, including in later years plays by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Tinniswood" title="Peter Tinniswood">Peter Tinniswood</a>: <i>On the Train to Chemnitz</i> (2001) and <i>Anton in Eastbourne</i> (2002). The latter was Tinniswood's last work and was written especially for Scofield, an admirer of <a href="/wiki/Anton_Chekhov" title="Anton Chekhov">Anton Chekhov</a>. He was awarded the 2002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sam_Wanamaker_Prize" title="Sam Wanamaker Prize">Sam Wanamaker Prize</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4608" data-file-width="3456" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG/220px-Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG/330px-Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG/440px-Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Scofield%27s_gravestone.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Paul and Joy Scofield's gravestone in St Mary's churchyard, Balcombe, West Sussex</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Paul Scofield married actress Joy Parker in October 1942. They had met while he played Hamlet to her Ophelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Scofield later said, "Joy and I simply decided to be married, we were both of age and were determined. Any doubts from our families were overruled and they were the usual ones – too young, etc. We had a week out at the end of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moon_Is_Down" title="The Moon Is Down">The Moon Is Down</a></i> tour, married during that week, and went straight into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitehall_Theatre" title="Whitehall Theatre">Whitehall Theatre</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Paul and Joy Scofield had two children: Martin (born 1945) (who became a Senior Lecturer in English and American literature at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kent" title="University of Kent">University of Kent</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasons_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-seasons-18">[18]</a></sup> and Sarah (born 1951). When asked by Garry O'Connor how he wished to be remembered, Scofield responded, "If you have a family, that is how to be remembered.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Michael_Winner" title="Michael Winner">Michael Winner</a> once described the Scofields as, "one of the few very happily married couples I've ever met."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scofield died from <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> on 19 March 2008 at the age of 86 at a hospital near his home in rural <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> His memorial service was held at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> on 19 March 2009. His wife Joy died four years later on 7 November 2012, age 90.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discography">Discography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Scofield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paul Scofield led the cast in several dramas issued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caedmon_Records" title="Caedmon Records">Caedmon Records</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>King Lear</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Sackler" title="Howard Sackler">Howard Sackler</a> (Text edited by G.B. Harrison), with Pamela Brown (Goneril), Rachel Roberts (Regan), Ann Bell (Cordelia); Wallace Eaton (France), John Rogers (Burgundy), Trevor Martin (Cornwall), Michael Aldridge (Albany), Andrew Keir (Kent), Cyril Cusack (Gloucester), Robert Stephens (Edgar), John Stride (Edmund), Ronnie Stevens (Fool); Arthur Hewlett (Curan, Doctor), Ronald Ibbs (Gentleman, Knight), Willoughby Goddard (Oswald). Eight sides, SRS 233 (first published 1965).</li>
<li><i>Hamlet</i>, directed by Howard Sackler, (Unabridged), with Diana Wynyard (Queen), Roland Culver (Claudius), Donald Houston (Laertes), Zena Walker (Ophelia), Wilfrid Lawson. Eight sides, SRS 232 (first published 1963).</li>
<li><i>A Midsummer Night's Dream</i>, directed by Howard Sackler, with Barbara Jefford, Joy Parker, John Stride, etc. Six sides, SRS 208 (first published 1964).</li>
<li>T.S. Eliot, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Family_Reunion" title="The Family Reunion">The Family Reunion</a></i>, with Flora Robson, Sybil Thorndike, Alan Webb. Six sides, TRS 308.</li>
<li>Charles Dickens, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Christmas_Carol" title="A Christmas Carol">A Christmas Carol</a></i>, with Ralph Richardson as Scooge. Scofield only narrated. (Caedmon),</li>
</ul>
<p>Also:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Homage to <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a></i>, with Laurence Olivier, John Le Mesurier, Cleo Laine, Bernard Cribbins, George Devine, Groucho Marx, Alec McGowen, Anna Quayle, Clive Revill, Ian Richardson, and Nicol Williamson (1965)</li>
<li><i>King Lear</i>, with Harriet Walter (Goneril), Sara Kestelman (Regan), Emilia Fox (Cordelia), Alec McCowen (Gloucester), Kenneth Branagh (Fool), David Burke, Richard A. McCabe, Toby Stephens, etc. Released 2002 to coincide with Scofield's 80th birthday. (Naxos Audiobooks, 3-CD set).</li>
<li>Virgil, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Aeneid" title="The Aeneid">The Aeneid</a></i>, Paul Scofield (narrator), Jill Balcon and Toby Stephens (readers). (Naxos Audiobook CD).</li>
<li>T. S. Eliot, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Waste_Land" title="The Waste Land">The Waste Land</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Quartets" title="Four Quartets">Four Quartets</a></i> (BBC Radiobooks CD).</li>
<li>Sandor Marai, <i><a href="/wiki/Embers" title="Embers">Embers</a></i> (Penguin Audiobooks) – Narrator</li>
<li>With David Suchet and Ron Moody, Scofield led the cast of a radio dramatization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narnia" title="Narnia">Narnia</a> books by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a>, which are available as CD issues. (Tynedale Entertainment) – Narrator</li>
<li>Scofield recorded abridged readings of Dickens's <i>A Christmas Carol</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bleak_House" title="Bleak House">Bleak House</a></i> (Blackstone Audiobooks).</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade_(poem)" title="Façade (poem)">Façade</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Edith_Sitwell" title="Edith Sitwell">Sitwell</a>-<a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">Walton</a>), performed by Paul Scofield and Peggy Ashcroft, with London Sinfonietta conducted by William Walton. (<a href="/wiki/Argo_Records" title="Argo Records">Argo Records</a>, 1972)</li>
<li><i>Don Quixote: The Musical</i>, with Roy Hudd as Sancho Panza. Based on Purcell and D'Urfey's <i>The Comical History of Don Quixote</i>. Later released on CD.(Musica Oscura, 1994)</li>
</ul>
<p>(For a more exhaustive list, see this note:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>)</p>
