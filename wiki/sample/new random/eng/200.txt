<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Whitechapel Vigilance Committee</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>Whitechapel Vigilance Committee</b> was a group of local volunteers who patrolled the streets of London's <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel_District_(Metropolis)" title="Whitechapel District (Metropolis)">Whitechapel District</a> during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel_murders" title="Whitechapel murders">Whitechapel murders</a> of 1888. The volunteers patrolled mainly at night in the search of the murderer. The committee was set up by local businessmen who were concerned that the killings were affecting commerce in the area. It was led by <a href="/wiki/George_Lusk" title="George Lusk">George Lusk</a>, a local builder who was elected chairman during the committee's first meeting on 10 September 1888.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other committee members included Mr. Joseph Aarons, the landlord of The Crown public house on Mile End Road (treasurer), Mr. B. Harris (secretary),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> and Messrs. Cohen, H. A. Harris, Laughton, Houghton, Lord, Isaacs, Rogers, Mitchell, Barnett, Hodgkins, Lindsay, Reeves, and Jacobs. The <i>Daily Telegraph</i> reported on 5 October 1888 that the leading members of the committee were "drawn principally from the trading class, and include a builder, a cigar-manufacturer, a tailor, a picture-frame maker, a licensed victualler, and 'an actor.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The latter was probably the entertainer Charles Reeves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Synthesis_of_published_material" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag may be based upon an improper synthesis of sources. (January 2017)">improper synthesis?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As chairman of the committee, Lusk's name appeared in the national newspapers and on posters in Whitechapel appealing for information concerning the identity of <a href="/wiki/Jack_the_Ripper" title="Jack the Ripper">Jack the Ripper</a> and complaining about the lack of a reward for such information from the government. Due to this publicity, Lusk received threatening letters through the post, allegedly from the murderer. Lusk is also mentioned in a letter dated 17 September 1888, reportedly discovered among archive materials in the late 20th century; however, most experts dismiss this as a modern hoax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vigilancecommittee.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="674" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Vigilancecommittee.jpg/300px-Vigilancecommittee.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Vigilancecommittee.jpg/450px-Vigilancecommittee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Vigilancecommittee.jpg/600px-Vigilancecommittee.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vigilancecommittee.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>On 30 September 1888 the committee members wrote to the government under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Salisbury" title="Lord Salisbury">Lord Salisbury</a> in an attempt to persuade them to offer a reward for information leading to the apprehension of the killer. When the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Matthews,_1st_Viscount_Llandaff" title="Henry Matthews, 1st Viscount Llandaff">Henry Matthews</a> refused, the committee offered its own reward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-1">[1]</a></sup> The committee also employed two private detectives, Mr. Le Grand (or Grand) and Mr. J. H. Batchelor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> to investigate the murders without the involvement of the local police.</p>
<p>The committee was unhappy with the level of protection that the community was receiving from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police" title="Metropolitan Police">police</a>, so it introduced its own system of local patrols, using hand-picked unemployed men to patrol the streets of the East End every evening from midnight to between four and five the next morning. Each of these men received a small wage from the Committee, and patrolled a particular beat, being armed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_whistle" title="Police whistle">police whistle</a>, a pair of <a href="/wiki/Galoshes" title="Galoshes">galoshes</a> and a strong stick. The committee itself met each evening at nine in the The Crown, and once the public house closed at 12.30 am the committee members would inspect and join the patrols. These patrols were shortly to be joined by those of the Working Men's Vigilance Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/From_Hell_letter" title="From Hell letter">"From Hell" letter</a>, which was sent with half of a human kidney, was posted to the committee's chairman George Lusk, who received it on 16 October 1888.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Many scholars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> of the Jack the Ripper murders regard this letter as being the communication most likely to have been sent by the killer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
