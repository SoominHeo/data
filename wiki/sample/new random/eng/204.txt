<p><b>Centaurus A</b> or <b>NGC 5128</b> is a galaxy in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Centaurus" title="Centaurus">Centaurus</a>. It was discovered in 1826 by <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> astronomer <a href="/wiki/James_Dunlop" title="James Dunlop">James Dunlop</a> from his home in Parramatta, in New South Wales, Australia. There is considerable debate in the literature regarding the galaxy's fundamental properties such as its <a href="/wiki/Hubble_sequence" title="Hubble sequence">Hubble type</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy" title="Lenticular galaxy">lenticular galaxy</a> or a giant <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxy</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harris10b_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-harris10b-9">[9]</a></sup> and distance (10–16 million <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tonryetal2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tonryetal2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ned-dist_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ned-dist-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferrarese07_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferrarese07-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess10_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-majaess10-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harris10_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-harris10-6">[6]</a></sup> NGC 5128 is one of the closest <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a> to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, so its <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nucleus</a> has been extensively studied by professional astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israel1998_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-israel1998-12">[12]</a></sup> The galaxy is also the fifth-brightest in the sky,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israel1998_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-israel1998-12">[12]</a></sup> making it an ideal amateur astronomy target,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eicher1988_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-eicher1988-13">[13]</a></sup> although the galaxy is only visible from low northern latitudes and the southern hemisphere.</p>
<p>The center of the galaxy contains a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> with a mass equivalent to 55 million <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which ejects a <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#Relativistic_jet" title="Astrophysical jet">relativistic jet</a> that is responsible for emissions in the <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_spectrum" title="Radio spectrum">radio</a> wavelengths. By taking radio observations of the jet separated by a decade, astronomers have determined that the inner parts of the jet are moving at about one half of the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>. X-rays are produced farther out as the jet collides with surrounding gases resulting in the creation of highly energetic particles. The X-ray jets of Centaurus A are thousands of light-years long, while the radio jets are over a million light-years long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">starburst galaxies</a>, a collision is suspected to be responsible for the intense burst of star formation. Models have suggested that Centaurus A was a large elliptical galaxy which collided and merged with a smaller spiral galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quillen2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quillen2006-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus_A&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NGC 5128 was discovered on 29 April 1826 by <a href="/wiki/James_Dunlop" title="James Dunlop">James Dunlop</a> during a survey at the Parramatta Observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1847 <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a> described the galaxy as "two semi-ovals of elliptically formed nebula appearing to be cut asunder and separated by a broad obscure band parallel to the larger axis of the nebula, in the midst of which a faint streak of light parallel to the sides of the cut appears." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1949 <a href="/wiki/John_Gatenby_Bolton" title="John Gatenby Bolton">John Gatenby Bolton</a>, Bruce Slee and Gordon Stanley localize NGC 5128 as one of the first extragalactical radio sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Five years later, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Baade" title="Walter Baade">Walter Baade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Minkowski" title="Rudolph Minkowski">Rudolph Minkowski</a> suggested that the peculiar structure is the result of a merge event of a giant elliptical galaxy and a small spiral galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The first detection of X-ray emissions, using a sounding rocket, was performed in197o.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1975–76 gamma-ray emissions from Centaurus A were observed through the atmospheric Cerenkov technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">Einstein Observatory</a> detected an X-ray jet emanating from the nucleus in 1979 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Ten years later, young blue stars were found along the central dust band with the Hubble Space Telescope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra X-ray Observatory</a> identified in 1999 more than 200 new point sources .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Another space telescope, the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, found a parallelogram-shaped structure of dust in near infrared images of Centaurus A in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence of gamma emissions with very high energy (more than 100 GeV) was detcted by the <a href="/wiki/High_Energy_Stereoscopic_System" title="High Energy Stereoscopic System">H.E.S.S-Observatorium</a> in Namibia in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following year, Centaurus A was identified as a source of cosmic rays of highest energies, after years of observations by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2016 a review of data from Chandra and <a href="/wiki/XMM-Newton" title="XMM-Newton">XMM-Newton</a>, unusual high flares of energy were found in NGC 5128 and its nearby galaxy <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_4636&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NGC 4636 (page does not exist)">NGC 4636</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jimmy_Erwin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jimmy Erwin (page does not exist)">Jimmy Erwin</a> of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama" title="University of Alabama">University of Alabama</a> hypothesized the discovery as potentially a black hole in a yet unknown process or an <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-mass_black_hole" title="Intermediate-mass black hole">intermediate-mass black hole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus_A&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Centaurus A may be described as having a <a href="/wiki/Peculiar_galaxy" title="Peculiar galaxy">peculiar</a> morphology. As seen from Earth, the galaxy looks like a <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy" title="Lenticular galaxy">lenticular</a> or <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxy</a> with a superimposed dust lane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnegieatlas_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-carnegieatlas-30">[30]</a></sup> The peculiarity of this galaxy was first identified in 1847 by <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a>, and the galaxy was included in <a href="/wiki/Halton_Arp" title="Halton Arp">Halton Arp</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_Peculiar_Galaxies" title="Atlas of Peculiar Galaxies">Atlas of Peculiar Galaxies</a></i> (published in 1966) as one of the best examples of a "disturbed" galaxy with dust absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arp1966_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-arp1966-31">[31]</a></sup> The galaxy's strange morphology is generally recognized as the result of a <a href="/wiki/Interacting_galaxy" title="Interacting galaxy">merger</a> between two smaller galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baademinkowski1954_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-baademinkowski1954-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>The bulge of this galaxy is composed mainly of evolved red stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnegieatlas_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-carnegieatlas-30">[30]</a></sup> The dusty disk, however, has been the site of more recent <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israel1998_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-israel1998-12">[12]</a></sup> over 100 star formation regions have been identified in the disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hodgekennicutt1983_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hodgekennicutt1983-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supernovae">Supernovae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus_A&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Supernovae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> have been detected in Centaurus A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nedsn_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nedsn-34">[34]</a></sup> The first supernova, named <a href="/wiki/SN_1986G" title="SN 1986G">SN 1986G</a>, was discovered within the dark dust lane of the galaxy by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Evans_(astronomer)" title="Robert Evans (astronomer)">R. Evans</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iauc_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-iauc-35">[35]</a></sup> It was later identified as a <a href="/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova" title="Type Ia supernova">Type Ia supernova</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phillipsetal1987_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-phillipsetal1987-36">[36]</a></sup> which forms when a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a>'s mass grows large enough to ignite carbon fusion in its center, touching off a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">runaway</a> thermonuclear reaction, as may happen when a white dwarf in a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system strips gas away from the other star. SN 1986G was used to demonstrate that the spectra of type Ia supernovae are not all identical, and that type Ia supernovae may differ in the way that they change in brightness over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phillipsetal1987_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-phillipsetal1987-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second supernova, dubbed SN2016adj,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> was discovered by Backyard Observatory Supernova Search in February 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus_A&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Distance estimates to NGC 5128 established since the 1980s typically range between 3–5 Mpc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tonryetal2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-tonryetal2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ned-dist_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ned-dist-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferrarese07_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ferrarese07-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess10_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-majaess10-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harris10_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-harris10-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rejkuba04_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-rejkuba04-39">[39]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Cepheids" title="Classical Cepheids">Classical Cepheids</a> discovered in the heavily-obscured dust lane of NGC 5128 yield a distance between ~3–3.5 Mpc, depending on the nature of the extinction law adopted and other considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferrarese07_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ferrarese07-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess10_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-majaess10-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mira_variable" title="Mira variable">Mira variables</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rejkuba04_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-rejkuba04-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Type_II_Cepheid" title="Type II Cepheid">Type II Cepheids</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferrarese07_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-ferrarese07-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess10_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-majaess10-5">[5]</a></sup> were also discovered in NGC 5128, the latter being rarely detected beyond the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess09_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess09-40">[40]</a></sup> The distance to NGC 5128 established from several indicators such as <a href="/wiki/Mira_variable" title="Mira variable">Mira variables</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebulae</a> favour a more distant value of ~3.8 Mpc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harris10b_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-harris10b-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harris10_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-harris10-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nearby_galaxies_and_galaxy_group_information">Nearby galaxies and galaxy group information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus_A&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nearby galaxies and galaxy group information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Centaurus A is at the center of one of two subgroups within the <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A/M83_Group" title="Centaurus A/M83 Group">Centaurus A/M83 Group</a>, a nearby <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_group" title="Galaxy group">group of galaxies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsevetal2002_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsevetal2002-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Messier_83" title="Messier 83">Messier 83</a> (the Southern Pinwheel Galaxy) is at the center of the other subgroup. These two groups are sometimes identified as one group<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbg_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbg-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fouqueetal1992_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-fouqueetal1992-43">[43]</a></sup> and sometimes identified as two groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garcia1993_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-garcia1993-44">[44]</a></sup> However, the galaxies around Centaurus A and the galaxies around M83 are physically close to each other, and both subgroups appear not to be moving relative to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsev2005_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsev2005-45">[45]</a></sup> The Centaurus A/M83 Group is located in the <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster" title="Virgo Supercluster">Virgo Supercluster</a>.</p>

<p>Centaurus A is located approximately 4° north of <a href="/wiki/Omega_Centauri" title="Omega Centauri">Omega Centauri</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> visible with the naked eye).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eicher1988_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-eicher1988-13">[13]</a></sup> Because the galaxy has a high surface brightness and relatively large angular size, it is an ideal target for amateur astronomy observations. The bright central bulge and dark dust lane are visible even in finderscopes and large binoculars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eicher1988_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-eicher1988-13">[13]</a></sup> and additional structure may be seen in larger telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eicher1988_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-eicher1988-13">[13]</a></sup> Centaurus A is visible to the naked eye under exceptionally good conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
