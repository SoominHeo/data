<p>The <b>Twelve <a href="/wiki/Nidana" title="Nidana">Nidānas</a></b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="Pāli">Pali</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>: <i>nidāna</i> "cause, motivation, link") are twelve links doctrine of Buddhism where each link is asserted as a primary causal relationship between the connected links.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peterharvey12links_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-peterharvey12links-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These links present the mechanistic basis of repeated birth, <i><a href="/wiki/Sa%E1%B9%83s%C4%81ra_(Buddhism)" title="Saṃsāra (Buddhism)">Samsara</a></i>, and resultant <i><a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">Dukkha</a></i> (suffering, pain, unsatisfactoriness) starting from <a href="/wiki/Avidy%C4%81_(Buddhism)" title="Avidyā (Buddhism)">avidyā</a> (ignorance, misconceptions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peterharvey12links_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-peterharvey12links-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[a]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Twelve Nidānas</i> doctrine is one application of the Buddhist concept of <a href="/wiki/Prat%C4%ABtyasamutp%C4%81da" title="Pratītyasamutpāda">pratītyasamutpāda</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paticcasamuppada" title="Paticcasamuppada">Paticcasamuppada</a> ("Dependent Arising" or "Dependent Origination").</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions_of_the_twelve_Nidanas">Descriptions of the twelve Nidanas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Descriptions of the twelve Nidanas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pali_literature">Pali literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pali literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several series of Nidānas are described in the suttas.</p>
<p>Dīgha Nikāya Sutta 1, the Brahmajala Sutta, verse 3.71 describes six Nidānas:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[...] [T]hey experience these feelings by repeated contact through the six sense-bases; feeling conditions craving; craving conditions clinging; clinging conditions becoming; becoming conditions birth; birth conditions aging and death, sorrow, lamentation, sadness and distress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[b]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Dīgha Nikāya, Sutta 14 describes ten links, and in Sutta 15 nine links are described, but without the six sense‑bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commentarial_literature">Commentarial literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commentarial literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Twelve Nidānas are explained in detail in the <a href="/wiki/Visuddhimagga" title="Visuddhimagga">Visuddhimagga</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhaghosa" title="Buddhaghosa">Buddhaghosa</a>, the central text of the <a href="/wiki/Mahavihara" title="Mahavihara">Mahāvihāra</a> commentarial tradition. Buddhaghosa recounts four methods to interpret the Twelve Nidanas:</p>
<ol>
<li>Working from "bottom to top",</li>
<li>Working from the "middle to the top",</li>
<li>Working from "top to bottom",</li>
<li>Working from the "middle to the source".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[c]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>The first method begins with ignorance and proceeds to sickness, old age, and death. The second method begins with attachment and proceeds to birth. The third method begins with birth and proceeds back to ignorance. The fourth method begins with attachment and proceeds to ignorance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><br/>
Thus one could observe that:</p>
<p>1. Due to ignorance (of underlying realities of existence) we process/ferment what comes to our mind.</p>
<p>2. This processing/fermentation causes karma to form and mould the status consciousness (vinyana).</p>
<p>3. The functioning/existence of the status consciousness has a close association with regards to sustaining life (one's existence).</p>
<p>4. "Name" and "Form" describes the non-material and material components of one's existence. "Name" are the constituents one's mind, consciousness and ideas... "Form" (material) are the constituents of the body (made of solids, liquids, gasses...).</p>
<p>5. The six sense bases of perception are composed of "Name" (the mind/vinyana...components) and "Form" (the solids, liquids, gasses... components).</p>
<p>6, 7. When the six sense bases of perception comes in contact with entities (ex. eye with external world, nose with fragrances,... mind with thoughts/memories...), they generate feelings (in the mind).</p>
<p>8. Next we generate/get desires for these feelings.</p>
<p>9. These desires makes one "cling onto" them (wanting more...).</p>
<p>10, 11. This clinging causes the generation of causations/factors (karma) that causes/leads/drags one into future births, so that such accumulated karma can take effect, can materialise... (The generation of sankhara /karma due to attachments, desires, longings, cravings... or due to the aversions, angers, hates... generated during the cause of such quests/pursuits... will lead one through eternal samsara resulting in the generation of yet further causations/karma, requiring further... Thus bonding one into this eternal journey...)</p>
<p>12. Then once one gets into a birth, one undergoes/endures all the sufferings associated with such...</p>
<p>1. But again due to our ignorance, we fail to realise the underlying nature/reality of existence. Thus veiled and shadowed by ignorance, we keep on generating the mental fermentations/volitions that keeps one further bonded to samsara...</p>
<p><br/>
Thus the cycle ever so continues for ever and ever unto perpetuity... Thus breaking away from this cycle (by "eradicating"/overcoming ignorance), leads to its cessation (cessation of the perpetual wandering)....</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reversing_the_chain">Reversing the chain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reversing the chain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Understanding the relationships between the phenomena that sustain <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">dukkha</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN12.2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-SN12.2-4">[4]</a></sup> is said to lead to <a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">nibbana</a>, complete freedom from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samsara" title="Samsara">samsara</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Phenomena are sustained only so long as their sustaining factors remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nutriments_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-nutriments-31">[12]</a></sup> This causal relationship is expressed in its most general form as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[t]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When this exists, that comes to be. With the arising of this, that arises. When this does not exist, that does not come to be. With the cessation of this, that ceases.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="external text" href="http://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/sn/sn12/sn12.061.than.html" rel="nofollow">Samyutta Nikaya 12.61</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>This natural law of <i>this/that causality</i> is independent of being discovered, just like the laws of physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[u]</a></sup> In particular, the Buddha applied this law of causality to determine the cause of <i><a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">dukkha</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[v]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reversal of this causal chain shows the way to put an end to stress: "From the remainderless fading and cessation of <i>ignorance</i> comes the cessation of <i>(volitional) fabrications</i>" et cetera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[w]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_lives_and_five_skandhas">Three lives and five skandhas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Three lives and five skandhas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nikayas themselves do not give a systematic explanation of the nidana series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[15]</a></sup> As an expository device, the commentarial tradition presented the factors as a linear sequence spanning over three lives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhikkhu_Bodhi_2005.2C_page_314_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bhikkhu_Bodhi_2005.2C_page_314-39">[16]</a></sup> thus shifting the theme from a single conception (and birth) to a sequence of "incarnations" (roughly speaking).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[x]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Schumann the Nidanas are a later synthesis of Buddhist teachings, meant to make them more comprehensible. Comparison with the five skhandhas shows that the chain contains logical inconsistencies, which can be explained when the chain is considered to be a later elaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchumann1974_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchumann1974-42">[18]</a></sup> This way it is explainable that nama-rupa en consciousness in the 9-fold are the beginning or start, while in the 12-fold chain they are preceded by ignorance and formations. Those can only exist when nama-rupa en consciousness are present. Schumann also proposes that the 12-fold is extended over three existences, and illustrate the succession of rebirths. while Buddhaghosa and Vasubandhu maintain a 2-8-2 schema, Schumann maintains a 3-6-3 scheme, putting the five skandhas aside the twelve nidanas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchumann1974_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchumann1974-42">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception_and_birth">Conception and birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conception and birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The twelve Nidanas may be seen as an explanation of the conception and development of the embryo:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[T]he 12-links formula is unambiguously an ancient tract that was originally written on the subject of the conception and development of the embryo, as a sequence of stages prior to birth; in examining the primary source text, this is as blatant today as it was over two thousand years ago, despite some very interesting misinterpretations that have arisen in the centuries in-between.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unpopular_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-unpopular-40">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[y]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Destiny_after_rebirth">Destiny after rebirth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Destiny after rebirth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dependent origination also describes the process by which <a href="/wiki/Sentient_beings_(Buddhism)" title="Sentient beings (Buddhism)">sentient beings</a> incarnate into any given realm and pursue their various worldly projects and activities with all concomitant suffering. Among these sufferings are aging and death.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daily_life">Daily life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Daily life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contemporary teachers often teach that it can also be seen as a daily cycle occurring from moment to moment throughout each day. There is scriptural support for this as an explanation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abhidharmakosa" title="Abhidharmakosa">Abhidharmakosa</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vasubandhu" title="Vasubandhu">Vasubandhu</a>, insofar as Vasubandu states that on occasion "the twelve parts are realized in one and the same moment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, in the case of avidyā, the first condition, it is necessary to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Three_marks_of_existence" title="Three marks of existence">three marks of existence</a> for a full understanding of its relation to pratityasamutpada. It is also necessary to understand the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_poisons_(Buddhism)" title="Three poisons (Buddhism)">Three Fires</a> and how they fit into the scheme. The Three Fires sit at the very center of the schemata in the <a href="/wiki/Bhavacakra" title="Bhavacakra">Bhavacakra</a> and drive the whole edifice. In Himalayan iconographic representations of the Bhavacakra such as within <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, the Three Fires are known as the Three Poisons which are often represented as the <a href="/wiki/Gankyil" title="Gankyil">Gankyil</a>. The Gankyil is also often represented as the hub of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dharmacakra" title="Dharmacakra">Dharmacakra</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">Nirvana</a> is often conceived of as stopping this cycle. By removing the causes for craving, craving ceases. So, with the ceasing of birth, death ceases. With the ceasing of becoming, birth ceases, and so on, until with the ceasing of ignorance no karma is produced, and the whole process of death and rebirth ceases.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avidy.C4.81">Avidyā</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Avidyā">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avidya_(Buddhism)" title="Avidya (Buddhism)">Avidyā</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>) or <b>Avijjā</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="Pāli">Pāli</a>); <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Tibetan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie transliteration</a>) <i>ma.rig.pa</i> (marikpa), Eng. "ignorance". Ignorance of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Noble_Truths" title="Four Noble Truths">Four Noble Truths</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Three_marks_of_existence" title="Three marks of existence">Three marks of existence</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Five_Skandhas" title="Five Skandhas">Five Skandhas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">Karma</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratitya-samutpada" title="Pratitya-samutpada">Pratītyasamutpāda</a> results in a wrong assessment of reality. This narrowness of experience is the primary cause of <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">duḥkha</span></a> (suffering dissatisfaction, pain, unease, etc.)</p>
<p><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Avidyā</span> may be understood as</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A continuous gradient characterizing not so much a particular state of being, but the quality or direction of situational patterning, experienced as a 'falling away from' the modality of pristine awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992225_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992225-45">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sa.E1.B9.83sk.C4.81ra">Saṃskāra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Saṃskāra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sankhara" title="Sankhara"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Saṃskāra</span></a></b> (Sanskrit) or <b><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Saṅkhāra</span></b> (Pāli); Tibetan <i>du.byed</i> (duche), Eng. "(mental) formations" The impulse accumulations of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">saṃskāra</span> are characterized by the energetic direction of the first motif, manifesting through body, speech, and mind as structuring forces of our being. This relationship forms the basis of our character and our personal karmic patterning.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vij.C3.B1.C4.81na">Vijñāna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Vijñāna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vijnana" title="Vijnana">Vijñāna</a></b> (Sanskrit) or <b>Viññāna</b> (Pāli); Tibetan <i>rnam.par.shes.pa</i> or <i>rnam.shes</i> (namshe), Eng. "consciousness" Vijñāna represents the partially structured consciousness that results from the action of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">saṃskāra</span> and the shaping of that energetic activity into a less flexible and more stagnant form.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is pictured as having a two-fold function: the cognition of objects that arise in our field of awareness and a structured stream that is being continually fed from the reservoir of energetic activity. The interplay between <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">saṃskāra</span> and vijñāna is seen as accounting for all the experiential data associated with the psychological notion of the unconscious, including memory, dreams, and the eruption of emotive complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992225-227_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992225-227-46">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nama-rupa">Nama-rupa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nama-rupa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Namarupa" title="Namarupa">Nāmarūpa</a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli); Tibetan <i>ming.gzugs</i> (mingzuk), Eng. "name and form" Vijñāna has a quick grasping tendency, moving from sensory objects to objects of imagination rapidly. This energy may therefore crystallize and take shape into mental functions, called <i>Nāma</i>, or it may be represented as material forms, called <i>Rūpa</i>.</p>
<p>As a collective idea, the Nāmarūpa motif models the reciprocal relationship of bodily and mental functioning. <i>Nāma</i> is the naming activity of the discursive mind. <i>Rūpa</i> develops an internal representation of external objects, without which mind and body cannot exist.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Nāma</i> refers to three components of mental functioning. There is the sensation or tone-awareness of a mental situation. There is also an ideational or labeling function. And finally there is the component of dispositional orientation, the 'mood-energy' we bring to a situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992227_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992227-47">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Rūpa</i> refers to the four dynamic structuring operations of solidity, cohesion, heat, and motility. They are represented by the elemental symbols of earth, water, fire, and air. The operation of these elemental modes goes to make up what we experience as our physical world, including our body. <i>Rūpa</i> embraces the static aspects of embodiment such as cellular, tissue, and organ structures, as well as the dynamic aspect of body metabolism--electro-physiological pathways, membrane transport, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992227_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992227-47">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Six_sense_bases">Six sense bases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Six sense bases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadayatana" title="Sadayatana"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ṣaḍāyatana</span></a></b> (Sanskrit) or <b><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Saḷāyatana</span></b> (Pāli); Tibetan <i>skye.mched</i> (kyemche), Eng. "six sense gates" or "six sense bases" The close relationship of bodily and mental functioning is differentiated into the six-fold bases of awareness, which contribute to the arising of all sensory experiences that make up our interpretation of reality. The six-fold bases are divided into an internal grouping (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">ādhyātmika</span>) with corollary external (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">bāhya</span>) supports.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The internal grouping refers to the integration of five sensory capabilities (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body) and a sixth capability, termed non-sensuous or mental, which refers to the capability of all acts of memory, imagination, visualization, etc. These internal bases are not to be confused with the corresponding physical organs ... They are simply loci of sensitivity structured such that there arises the experience of seeing, hearing, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228-48">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The six external bases, which always work in conjunction with the corresponding internal base, refer to the six types of possible object awareness. These bases are the means by which the differentiated aspects, which are fleeting stabilizations in the field character of our awareness, stand out long enough to be appropriated as this-or-that specific object. The external and internal bases should be pictured as working together in pairs. In any given moment there is the two-fold working of a particular modality of awareness (eye-sensitivity and color-forms, ear-sensitivity and sounds, etc.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228-48">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact">Contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Spar%C5%9Ba" title="Sparśa">Sparśa</a></b> (Sanskrit) or <b>Phassa</b> (Pāli); Tibetan <i>reg.pa</i> (rekpa), Eng. "contact" The sparśa motif refers to the relationship or <a href="/wiki/Rapport" title="Rapport">rapport</a> between the internal and external <a href="/wiki/Ayatana" title="Ayatana"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">āyatana</span></a>. Impressions of tone arise in conjunction with the specific modality of awareness that is operating.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensation">Sensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vedana" title="Vedana">Vedanā</a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli); Tibetan <i>tshor.ba</i> (tsorwa), Eng. "sensation" There are six types of feeling tone awareness that arise from contact of the <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">āyatana</span>. The feeling tone or sensation of each of the six <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">āyatana</span> is uniquely different. For example, the feeling tone and felt experience of sensations in the body are distinct from the feeling tones generated from experiencing sight or sound.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Each modality is experientially separable on the basis of (a) the <i>place</i> of sensitivity (internal base), (b) the corresponding <i>structure</i> of its field (external base), (c) the manner of articulation or relatedness between (a) and (b), termed rapport, and (d) the resulting distinctive tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992228-48">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Craving">Craving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Craving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanha" title="Tanha"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Tṛṣṇā</span></a></b> (Sanskrit) or <b><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Taṇhā</span></b> (Pāli); Tibetan <i>sred.pa</i> (sepa), Eng. "craving" or "desire" or "thirst": Following the arising of tone-awareness is an unconditioned or habitually patterned experience of craving or attachment. The type of craving or attachment that follows depends upon which of the six <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">āyatanas</span> is involved, and which of the following three "motivations" is present.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The motivation of sensual gratification (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">kāma-tṛṣṇā</span>) is perhaps the most common. It results in simple attachment to whatever arises in one's field of awareness. It is not an overt appropriation, one that we consciously activate. It refers rather to the habitual structuring of experience such that one is compulsively caught up in one situation after another through a process of identification and clinging.<br/></p>
<p>One can also be motivated with regard to the desire for 'eternals' (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">bhava-tṛṣṇā</span>). It is the habitual structuring of any sensory impression, any momentary awareness, such that it might be the occasion for securing an eternal realm of peace and contentment.<br/>
Finally there is the annihilatory motivation (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">vibhava-tṛṣṇā</span>). It is the automatic structuring of experience such that any sensory activation might be the cause of a compulsive thirst to annihilate and destroy. What is commonly regarded as psychopathic behavior might be linked particularly with this type of motivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992229_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992229-49">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attachment">Attachment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Attachment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upadana" title="Upadana">Upādāna</a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli); Tibetan <i>len.pa</i> (lenpa), Eng. "attachment" If the object of one's desires comes to fruition, then these craving desires of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">tṛṣṇā</span> may solidify and manifest as the quality of attachment, or upādāna. This condition of fulfilled desires and attachment is always fleeting and momentary, as new cravings arise once old cravings are satisfied.</p>
<p>Attachment may take many forms, for example, emotional attachment to persons, to life, material comfort, routines, pleasant or unpleasant sensations, beliefs, thoughts, judgements, etc. We may not have attachment to things like wealth or success in society, but we are typically very strongly attached to our feelings and constructed identity of the self.</p>
<p>One may become fixated on a mental "story" or representation of reality, or a mental version of an object or event, preferring and craving for an unrealized internal version of external reality. Once this fixation shapes behavior in a way that internal desires are satiated, then the craving of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">tṛṣṇā</span> may be said to have shifted to the attachment of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">upādāna</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming">Becoming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Becoming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Bhava" title="Bhava">Bhava</a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli), Tibetan <i>srid.pa</i> (sipa), Eng. "becoming" "Once the direction of situational patterning has proceeded to the point of overt clinging, a process of becoming, termed bhava, is initiated. It refers to the new formation of karmic tendencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230-50">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>This creation of new habits and karmic tendencies, called bhava, will come to fruition through future experiences. Bhava, therefore, differs from <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Saṃskāra</span> in temporal nature. "<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Saṃskāra</span> refers to tendencies from past situational patternings (lives) which act on the present situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230-50">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jati_(Buddhism)" title="Jati (Buddhism)">Jāti</a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli); Tibetan <i>skyed.ba</i> (kye wa), Eng. "birth": The <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">jāti</span> motif refers to the process of karmic tendencies of bhava coming to fruition, through the birth of new patternings. That which was desired and conditioned now comes to be.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In a psycho-biological model, <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">jāti</span> refers to the birth or emergence of a newborn being, appearing, according to the specific history of patterning, in one of six 'lifestyles'. These lifestyles indicate the general character of experience. They are symbolized by the terms gods, titans, hungry ghosts, animals, denizens of hell, and human. These embrace all the general ways of being-in-a-situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230-50">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aging.2C_decay_and_death">Aging, decay and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Aging, decay and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaramarana" title="Jaramarana"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Jarā-maraṇa</span></a></b> (Sanskrit and Pāli); Tibetan <i>rga.shi</i> (gashi), Eng. "aging (old age), decay and death"</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Once a new situation or a new being has emerged, it is inevitable that the conditions which brought about its appearance will change. This, the last of the twelve motifs, points to the inevitability of decay and death. Decay affects all structures, which are but fleeting stabilizations fed by the energy flow of habitual patterning. When the cessation of the continuity of experience occurs, we speak of death. It is the total breakdown and dissolution of experience and experiencer.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The process of disintegration, destructuring, and entropic scattering yields a nexus of vibratory murkiness which is the condition of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">avidyā</span>, the first motif. Thus the entire structure of patterning feeds back on itself, and is often pictured as a circle of twelve sections, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wheel_of_Life" title="Wheel of Life">Wheel of Life</a> (bhavacakra, srid-pa'i-'khor-lo).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230-231_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodman1992230-231-51">[26]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Within_Buddhist_literature">Within Buddhist literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Within Buddhist literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Therav.C4.81da">Theravāda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Theravāda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pali_Canon">Pali Canon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Pali Canon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suttapitaka" title="Suttapitaka">Suttapitaka</a> (the most ancient canon of Buddhist writing preserved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therav%C4%81da" title="Theravāda">Theravāda</a> tradition) the first (partial) exposition of the twelve nidānas appears in the Dīgha Nikāya (Long Discourses), Brahmajāla Sutta, verse 3.71.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[27]</a></sup> The reference is partial because it does not cover all twelve links:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[28]</a></sup> " In this same Nikāya, Sutta 14 describes ten links instead of twelve, and in Sutta 15 the links are described, but without the six sense-bases (for a total of nine links in that Sutta).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[29]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...they experience these feelings by repeated contact through the six sense-bases; feeling conditions craving; craving conditions clinging; clinging conditions becoming; becoming conditions birth; birth conditions aging and death, sorrow, lamentation, sadness and distress.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Descriptions of the full sequence of twelve links can be found elsewhere in the Pali canon, for instance in section 12 of the Samyutta Nikaya:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN12.2_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-SN12.2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Now from the remainderless fading and cessation of that very ignorance comes the cessation of fabrications ... From the cessation of birth, then aging and death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, &amp; despair all cease. Such is the cessation of this entire mass of stress and suffering.</p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Therav.C4.81da_commentaries">Theravāda commentaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Theravāda commentaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the commentarial literature of the Theravada tradition (attributed, at least mythically, to the author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhaghosa" title="Buddhaghosa">Buddhaghosa</a>, and written many centuries subsequent to the Suttapitaka passages described above) the same doctrine is instead interpreted as a sequence of three lives, thus shifting the theme from a single conception (and birth) to a sequence of "incarnations" (roughly speaking).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[z]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mahayana">Mahayana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Twelve_Nid%C4%81nas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Mahayana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> tradition, the Buddha taught on the twelve links in detail in the <i>Rice Seedling Sutra</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalai_Lama199236_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalai_Lama199236-56">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeshe_Sonam_Rinchen200626_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeshe_Sonam_Rinchen200626-57">[31]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-32"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-32">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The general formula can be found in the following discourses in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pali_Canon" title="Pali Canon">Pali Canon</a>: <a href="/wiki/Majjhima_Nikaya" title="Majjhima Nikaya">MN</a> 79, MN 115, <a href="/wiki/Samyutta_Nikaya" title="Samyutta Nikaya">SN</a>12.21, SN 12.22, SN 12.37, SN 12.41, SN 12.49, SN 12.50, SN 12.61, SN 12.62, SN 55.28, <a href="/wiki/Anguttara_Nikaya" title="Anguttara Nikaya">AN</a> 10.92, <a href="/wiki/Udana" title="Udana">Ud</a>. 1.1 (first two lines), Ud. 1.2 (last two lines), Ud. 1.3, <a href="/wiki/Niddesa" title="Niddesa">Nd</a>2, <a href="/wiki/Patisambhidamagga" title="Patisambhidamagga">Patis</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-33">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Whether or not there is the arising of Tathagatas, this property stands — this regularity of the Dhamma, this orderliness of the Dhamma, this this/that conditionality." <a class="external text" href="http://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/sn/sn12/sn12.020.than.html" rel="nofollow">SN 12.20</a></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-34"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-34">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Most Suttas follow the order from ignorance to dukkha. But <a class="external text" href="http://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/sn/sn12/sn12.020.than.html" rel="nofollow">SN 12.20</a> views this as a teaching of the requisite conditions for sustaining <i><a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">dukkha</a></i>, which is its main application.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-37"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-37">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Upanisa Sutta in the Samyutta Nikaya describes the reversed order, in which the causes for enlightenment are given. This application of the principle of dependent arising is referred to in Theravada exegetical literature as "transcendental dependent arising".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[13]</a></sup> The chain in this case is:</span>
<ol>
<li><span class="reference-text">suffering (<i><a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">dukkha</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">faith (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddha" title="Saddha">saddhā</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">joy (<i>pāmojja</i>, <i>pāmujja</i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">rapture (<i><a href="/wiki/P%C4%ABti" title="Pīti">pīti</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">tranquillity (<i><a href="/wiki/Passaddhi" title="Passaddhi">passaddhi</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">happiness (<i><a href="/wiki/Sukha" title="Sukha">sukha</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">concentration (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samadhi_(Buddhism)" title="Samadhi (Buddhism)">samādhi</a></i>)</span></li>
<li><span class="reference-text">knowledge and vision of things as they are (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">yathābhūta-ñāna-dassana</span></i>)</span></li>
<li>disenchantment with worldly life (<i>nibbidā</i>)</li>
<li>dispassion (<i>virāga</i>)</li>
<li>freedom, release, emancipation (<i>vimutti</i>, a synonym for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nibbana" title="Nibbana">nibbana</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[14]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>knowledge of destruction of the cankers (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">āsava-khaye-ñāna</span></i>)</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-41"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-41">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Nyanatiloka, for his part in this controversy, sets himself up as the defender of the commentarial tradition that extends the 12-links from a description of a single incarnation into a description of the causes and effects of reincarnation in three separate lifetimes. <li id="cite_note-43"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-43">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the Mahānidāna [sutta]’s brief gloss on the term nāmarūpa [...] we have a very explicit reminder that the subject-matter being described in this sequence of stages is the development of the embryo [...] it is indisputably clear that we are reading about something that may (or may not) enter into (okkamissatha) the mother’s womb (mātukucchismiŋ). ...[T]he passage is wildly incongruent with attempts of many other interpreters to render the whole doctrine in more abstract terms (variously psychological or metaphysical)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unpopular_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-unpopular-40">[17]</a></sup></span></li>
