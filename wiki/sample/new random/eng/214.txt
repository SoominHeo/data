<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sega_CD" title="Sega CD">Sega CD</a>, originally released as the Mega-CD <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">メガCD</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Mega-Shī Dī</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in most regions outside of North America, is an <a href="/wiki/Video_game_accessory" title="Video game accessory">add-on</a> device for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game console</a>, designed and produced by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>. It was released in Japan in 1991, North America in 1992 and in <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">PAL</a> regions in 1993. The device adds a <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> drive to the console, allowing the user to play CD-based games and providing additional hardware functionality. It can also play <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">audio CDs</a> and <a href="/wiki/CD%2BG" title="CD+G">CD+G</a> discs. While the add-on did contain a faster <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing unit</a> than the Genesis, as well as some enhanced graphics capabilities, the main focus of the device was to expand the size of games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-segacd_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-segacd-1">[1]</a></sup> Known for several games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_CD" title="Sonic the Hedgehog CD">Sonic the Hedgehog CD</a></i> and for the controversy of violent video games including <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Trap" title="Night Trap">Night Trap</a></i>, the Sega CD sold 2.24 million units worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sega_Stats_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sega_Stats-2">[2]</a></sup> It was officially discontinued in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RetroGamer_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RetroGamer-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the titles released for the Sega CD were a number of <a href="/wiki/Full_motion_video" title="Full motion video">FMV</a> games, including <i><a href="/wiki/Sewer_Shark" title="Sewer Shark">Sewer Shark</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_(1995_video_game)" title="Fahrenheit (1995 video game)">Fahrenheit</a></i>. Well-known titles include the critically acclaimed <i>Sonic the Hedgehog CD</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lunar:_Eternal_Blue" title="Lunar: Eternal Blue">Lunar: Eternal Blue</a></i>, as well as the controversial <i>Night Trap</i>, which resulted in Congressional hearings on video game violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDFollies_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDFollies-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-5">[5]</a></sup> The Sega CD also received enhanced ports of games from the Genesis, including <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Returns_(video_game)" title="Batman Returns (video game)">Batman Returns</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ecco_the_Dolphin" title="Ecco the Dolphin">Ecco the Dolphin</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RetroGamer_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-RetroGamer-3">[3]</a></sup> Included in this library are six games which, while receiving individual Sega CD releases, also received separate versions that utilized both the Sega CD and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_32X" title="Sega 32X">Sega 32X</a> add-ons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllgameCD32X_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AllgameCD32X-6">[6]</a></sup> In particular, <i>Sonic the Hedgehog CD</i> has been noted for its excellent graphics and new <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a> elements without changing the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)"><i>Sonic</i></a> formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, given the large amount of FMV games and Genesis ports, the Sega CD's game library has been criticized for its lack of depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDFollies_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDFollies-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-failure_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-failure-8">[8]</a></sup> Full motion video quality was poor on the Sega CD due to poor video compression software and the system's limited color palette,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDFollies_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDFollies-4">[4]</a></sup> and the concept never caught on with the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllgameCD_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AllgameCD-9">[9]</a></sup> Likewise, most Genesis ports for the Sega CD added in additional full motion video sequences, extra levels, and enhanced audio, but were otherwise the same game as the Genesis release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllgameCD_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-AllgameCD-9">[9]</a></sup> The video in these sequences have also been criticized, with the quality being considered comparable to an old <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> tape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDFollies_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-CDFollies-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following list contains all of the games released on the Sega CD. Of these games, there are six titles (marked with a <sup><b>†</b></sup> in the title for the game) that were also released in formats that used a combination of the Sega CD and the Sega 32X. All release years given are for the earliest release of the games, which have been sold in several regions around the world, including Japan, North America, and Europe. A total of 209 titles are listed.</p>
