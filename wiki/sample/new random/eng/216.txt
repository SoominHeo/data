<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wearable computer</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Wearable computers</b>, also known as <b>body-borne computers</b> or <b>wearables</b> are miniature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_device" title="Electronic device">electronic devices</a> that are worn under, with or on top of <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interaction-Design_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Interaction-Design-1">[1]</a></sup> This class of <a href="/wiki/Wearable_technology" title="Wearable technology">wearable technology</a> has been developed for general or special purpose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_technologies" title="Information technologies">information technologies</a>. It is also used in media development. Wearable computers are especially useful for applications that require more complex computational support, such as <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">accelerometers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopes</a>, than just hardware coded logic.</p>
<p>Wearable computing devices are variously defined. For example, consumers often refer to wearable computers as computers that can be easily carried on the body, or systems with a heads-up display or speech activated. This contrasts with academics that define wearables as a system that can perform a set of functions without being constrained by the physical hardware of the system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Merchandiser often use the broadest definition, as any computing device worn on the body. This article page will use the broadest definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Smartwatch" title="Smartwatch">Smartwatches</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fitbit" title="Fitbit">Fitbit</a> system are the most common form, worn on the wrist. <a href="/wiki/Google_Glass" title="Google Glass">Google Glass</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Optical_head-mounted_display" title="Optical head-mounted display">optical head-mounted display</a> supplying a <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a> perspective, controlled by novel gestural movements.</p>
<p>One common feature of wearable computers is their persistence of activity. There is constant <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interaction</a> between the wearable and user, so there is no need to turn the device on or off. Another feature is the ability to multi-task. When using a wearable computer, there is no need to stop what one is doing to use the device; its functionality blends seamlessly into all other user actions. These devices can be used by the wearer to act as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosthetic" title="Prosthetic">prosthetic</a>. It may therefore be an extension of the user's mind or body.</p>
<p>Many issues are common to wearables as with <a href="/wiki/Mobile_computing" title="Mobile computing">mobile computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambient_intelligence" title="Ambient intelligence">ambient intelligence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">ubiquitous computing</a> research <a href="/wiki/Scientific_community" title="Scientific community">communities</a>. These include power management and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_management_(electronics)" title="Thermal management (electronics)">heat dissipation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">software architectures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wireless" title="Wireless">wireless</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personal_area_network" title="Personal area network">personal area networks</a>, and data management,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> all of which are essential for overall data quality and trust in the device.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_applications">Areas of applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Areas of applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many applications, the user's body is actively engaged as the device's interface. This usually includes: the skin, hands, voice, eyes, and arms. Wearables are also receptive to any motion or attention.</p>
<p>Wearable computer items have been developed and applied in the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>sensory integration, e.g. to help people see better or understand the world better (whether in task-specific applications like camera-based <a href="/wiki/Welding_helmet" title="Welding helmet">welding helmets</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quantigraphic_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quantigraphic-5">[5]</a></sup> or for everyday use like <a href="/wiki/EyeTap" title="EyeTap">computerized "digital eyeglass"</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interaction-Design_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Interaction-Design-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Behavioral_modeling" title="Behavioral modeling">behavioral modeling</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> <a href="/wiki/Monitoring_(medicine)" title="Monitoring (medicine)">monitoring</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Service_management" title="Service management">service management</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/E-textiles" title="E-textiles">electronic textiles</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion design</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Today "wearable computing" is still a topic of active research, with areas of study including <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> design, <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">pattern recognition</a>. The use of wearables for specific applications, for compensating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disabilities" title="Disabilities">disabilities</a> or supporting elderly people steadily increases. The application of wearable computers in fashion design is evident through Microsoft's prototype of "The Printing Dress" at the <a href="/wiki/International_Symposium_on_Wearable_Computers" title="International Symposium on Wearable Computers">International Symposium on Wearable Computers</a> in June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:511px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wearcompevolution.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="855" data-file-width="1748" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Wearcompevolution.jpg/509px-Wearcompevolution.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Wearcompevolution.jpg/764px-Wearcompevolution.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Wearcompevolution.jpg/1018px-Wearcompevolution.jpg 2x" width="509"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wearcompevolution.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Evolution of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Mann" title="Steve Mann">Steve Mann</a>'s <i>WearComp</i> wearable computer from backpack based systems of the 1980s to his current covert systems</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the varied definitions of "wearable" and "computer", the first wearable computer could be as early as the first abacus on a necklace, a 16th-century abacus ring, the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wristwatch" title="Wristwatch">wristwatch</a> made by Breguet for the Queen of Naples in 1810, or the covert timing devices hidden in shoes to cheat at roulette by Thorp and Shannon in the 1960s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, a computer is not merely a time-keeping or calculating device, but rather a user-programmable item for complex <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interface_(computing)" title="Interface (computing)">interfacing</a>, and data management. By this definition, the wearable computer was invented by Steve Mann, in the late 1970s:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSCC2000_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISSCC2000-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watier_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watier-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Steve Mann, a professor at the University of Toronto, was hailed as the father of the wearable computer and the ISSCC's first virtual panelist, by moderator Woodward Yang of Harvard University (Cambridge Mass.).</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— IEEE ISSCC 8 Feb. 2000</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The development of wearable items has taken several steps of miniaturization from discrete electronics over hybrid designs to fully integrated designs, where just one processor chip, a battery and some interface conditioning items make the whole unit.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1500s">1500s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1500s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Queen Elizabeth I of England received the wristwatch (while not a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a>) from Robert Dudley in 1571, as a new year's present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1600s">1600s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1600s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> saw the introduction of a fully functional abacus on a ring, which could be used while it was being worn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interaction-Design_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Interaction-Design-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbacusRing_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AbacusRing-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1800s">1800s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1800s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first wearable timepiece was made by watchmaker Breguet for the Queen of Naples in 1810. It was a small ladies' pocket watch on a bracelet chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wristlets_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wristlets-13">[13]</a></sup> A wristwatch is a "wearable computer" in the sense that it can be worn and that it also computes time. But it is not a general-purpose computer in the sense of the modern word.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Girard-Perregaux" title="Girard-Perregaux">Girard-Perregaux</a> made wristwatches for the German Imperial Navy after an artillery officer complained that it was not convenient to use both hands to operate a pocket watch while timing his bombardments. The officer had strapped a pocket watch onto his wrist and his superiors liked his solution, and thus asked La Chaux-de-Fonds to travel to Berlin to begin production of small pocket watches attached to wrist bracelets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wristlets_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wristlets-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early acceptance of wristlets by men serving in the military was not widespread, though:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Wristlets, as they were called, were reserved for women, and considered more of a passing fad than a serious timepiece. In fact, they were held in such disdain that many a gentlemen were actually quoted to say they "would sooner wear a skirt as wear a wristwatch".</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— International Watch Magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wristwatch_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wristwatch-14">[14]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1961, mathematicians <a href="/wiki/Edward_O._Thorp" title="Edward O. Thorp">Edward O. Thorp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">Claude Shannon</a> built some computerized timing devices to help them cheat at a game of <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a>. One such timer was concealed in a shoe and another in a pack of cigarettes. Various versions of this apparatus were built in the 1960s and 1970s. Detailed pictures of a shoe-based timing device can be viewed at <a class="external text" href="http://www.eyetap.org/wearcam/eudaemonic/" rel="nofollow">www.eyetap.org</a>.</p>
<p>Thorp refers to himself as the inventor of the first "wearable computer"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In other variations, the system was a concealed cigarette-pack sized <a href="/wiki/Analog_computer" title="Analog computer">analog computer</a> designed to predict the motion of roulette wheels. A data-taker would use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microswitch" title="Microswitch">microswitches</a> hidden in his shoes to indicate the speed of the roulette wheel, and the computer would indicate an <a href="/wiki/Octant_(plane_geometry)" title="Octant (plane geometry)">octant</a> of the roulette wheel to bet on by sending musical tones via radio to a miniature speaker hidden in a collaborator's ear canal. The system was successfully tested in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a> in June 1961, but hardware issues with the speaker wires prevented it from being used beyond test runs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BtD_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-BtD-16">[16]</a></sup> This was not a wearable computer, because it could not be re-purposed during use; rather it was an example of task-specific hardware. This work was kept secret until it was first mentioned in Thorp's book <i>Beat the Dealer</i> (revised ed.) in 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BtD_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-BtD-16">[16]</a></sup> and later published in detail in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1970s saw the rise of special purpose hardware timing devices, similar to the ones from the 1960s, such as roulette prediction devices using next-generation technology. In particular, a group known as <a href="/wiki/Eudaemons" title="Eudaemons">Eudaemonic Enterprises</a> used a <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> 6502 microprocessor with 5K <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">RAM</a> to create a shoe computer with inductive radio communications between a data-taker and bettor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bass_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bass-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Upton_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Upton-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another early wearable system was a camera-to-tactile vest for the blind, published by C.C. Collins in 1977, that converted images into a 1024-point, 10-inch square <a href="/wiki/Haptic_technology" title="Haptic technology">tactile grid</a> on a vest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-collins_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-collins-20">[20]</a></sup> On the consumer end, 1977 also saw the introduction of the HP-01 algebraic <a href="/wiki/Calculator_watch" title="Calculator watch">calculator watch</a> by Hewlett-Packard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marion_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marion-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1980s saw the rise of more general-purpose wearable computers that fit the modern definition of "computer" by going beyond task-specific hardware to more general-purpose (e.g. re-programmable by the user) devices. In 1981, Steve Mann designed and built a backpack-mounted 6502-based wearable multimedia computer with text, graphics, and multimedia capability, as well as video capability (cameras and other photographic systems). Mann went on to be an early and active researcher in the wearables field, especially known for his 1994 creation of the Wearable Wireless <a href="/wiki/Webcam" title="Webcam">Webcam</a>, the first example of <a href="/wiki/Lifelog" title="Lifelog">Lifelogging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FirstStep_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FirstStep-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though perhaps not technically "wearable," in 1986 Steve Roberts built Winnebiko-II, a <a href="/wiki/Recumbent_bicycle" title="Recumbent bicycle">recumbent bicycle</a> with on-board computer and <a href="/wiki/Chorded_keyboard" title="Chorded keyboard">chorded keyboard</a>. Winnebiko II was the first of Steve Roberts' forays into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic_computing" title="Nomadic computing">nomadic computing</a> that allowed him to type while riding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Reflection Technology marketed the Private Eye <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a>, which scans a vertical array of LEDs across the visual field using a vibrating mirror. This display gave rise to several hobbyist and research wearables, including Gerald "Chip" Maguire's <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> / <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> Student Electronic Notebook,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bade_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bade-25">[25]</a></sup> Doug Platt's Hip-PC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>'s VuMan 1 in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platt_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platt-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Student Electronic Notebook consisted of the Private Eye, <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a> diskless AIX <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notebook_computer" title="Notebook computer">notebook computers</a> (prototypes), a stylus based input system and a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_keyboard" title="Virtual keyboard">virtual keyboard</a>. It used <a href="/wiki/Direct-sequence_spread_spectrum" title="Direct-sequence spread spectrum">direct-sequence spread spectrum</a> radio links to provide all the usual TCP/IP based services, including NFS mounted file systems and X11, which all ran in the Andrew Project environment.</p>
<p>The Hip-PC included an Agenda palmtop used as a chording keyboard attached to the belt and a 1.44 megabyte floppy drive. Later versions incorporated additional equipment from Park Engineering. The system debuted at "The Lap and Palmtop Expo" on 16 April 1991.</p>
<p>VuMan 1 was developed as part of a Summer-term course at Carnegie Mellon's Engineering Design Research Center, and was intended for viewing house blueprints. Input was through a three-button unit worn on the belt, and output was through Reflection Tech's Private Eye. The CPU was an 8 MHz 80188 processor with 0.5 MB ROM.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1993, the Private Eye was used in <a href="/wiki/Thad_Starner" title="Thad Starner">Thad Starner</a>'s wearable, based on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doug_Platt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doug Platt (page does not exist)">Doug Platt</a>'s system and built from a kit from Park Enterprises, a Private Eye display on loan from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Devon_Sean_McCullough&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Devon Sean McCullough (page does not exist)">Devon Sean McCullough</a>, and the Twiddler chording keyboard made by Handykey. Many iterations later this system became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> "Tin Lizzy" wearable computer design, and Starner went on to become one of the founders of MIT's wearable computing project. 1993 also saw <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>'s augmented-reality system known as KARMA (Knowledge-based Augmented Reality for Maintenance Assistance). Users would wear a Private Eye display over one eye, giving an overlay effect when the real world was viewed with both eyes open. KARMA would overlay wireframe schematics and maintenance instructions on top of whatever was being repaired. For example, graphical wireframes on top of a laser printer would explain how to change the paper tray. The system used sensors attached to objects in the physical world to determine their locations, and the entire system ran tethered from a desktop computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feiner_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feiner-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feiner2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-feiner2-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edgar_Matias&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edgar Matias (page does not exist)">Edgar Matias</a> and Mike Ruicci of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>, debuted a "wrist computer." Their system presented an alternative approach to the emerging head-up display plus chord keyboard wearable. The system was built from a modified HP 95LX palmtop computer and a Half-QWERTY one-handed keyboard. With the keyboard and display modules strapped to the operator's forearms, text could be entered by bringing the wrists together and typing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matias_94_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matias_94-30">[30]</a></sup> The same technology was used by IBM researchers to create the half-keyboard "belt computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattias_96_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mattias_96-31">[31]</a></sup> Also in 1994, Mik Lamming and Mike Flynn at <a href="/wiki/PARC_(company)" title="PARC (company)">Xerox EuroPARC</a> demonstrated the Forget-Me-Not, a wearable device that would record interactions with people and devices and store this information in a database for later query.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It interacted via wireless transmitters in rooms and with equipment in the area to remember who was there, who was being talked to on the telephone, and what objects were in the room, allowing queries like "Who came by my office while I was on the phone to Mark?". As with the Toronto system, Forget-Me-Not was not based on a head-mounted display.</p>
<p>Also in 1994, <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> started the Smart Modules Program to develop a modular, <i>humionic</i> approach to wearable and carryable computers, with the goal of producing a variety of products including computers, radios, navigation systems and human-computer interfaces that have both military and commercial use. In July 1996, DARPA went on to host the "Wearables in 2005" workshop, bringing together industrial, university, and military visionaries to work on the common theme of delivering computing to the individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DARPA_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-DARPA-33">[33]</a></sup> A follow-up conference was hosted by <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> in August 1996, where plans were finalized to create a new academic conference on wearable computing. In October 1997, Carnegie Mellon University, MIT, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Tech</a> co-hosted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> International Symposium on Wearables Computers (ISWC) in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>. The symposium was a full academic conference with published proceedings and papers ranging from sensors and new hardware to new applications for wearable computers, with 382 people registered for the event.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dr. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_H_Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bruce H Thomas (page does not exist)">Bruce H Thomas</a> and Dr. Wayne Piekarski developed the Tinmith wearable computer system to support <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a>. This work was first published internationally in 2000 at the ISWC conference. The work was carried out at the Wearable Computer Lab in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Australia" title="University of South Australia">University of South Australia</a>.</p>
<p>In 2002, as part of <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Warwick" title="Kevin Warwick">Kevin Warwick</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Warwick#Project_Cyborg" title="Kevin Warwick">Project Cyborg</a>, Warwick's wife, Irena, wore a necklace which was electronically linked to Warwick's nervous system via an implanted <a href="/wiki/Electrode_array" title="Electrode array">electrode array</a> The color of the necklace changed between red and blue dependent on the signals on Warwick's nervous system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/GoPro" title="GoPro">GoPro</a> released their first product, the GoPro HERO 35mm, which began a successful franchise of wearable cameras. The cameras can be worn atop the head or around the wrist and are shock and waterproof. GoPro cameras are used by many athletes and extreme sports enthusiasts, a trend that became very apparent during the early 2010s.</p>
<p>In the late 2000s, various Chinese companies began producing mobile phones in the form of wristwatches, the descendants of which as of 2013 include the i5 and i6, which are GSM phones with 1.8 inch displays, and the <a href="/wiki/ZGPAX_s5" title="ZGPAX s5">ZGPAX s5</a> <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> wristwatch phone.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Standardization with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IETF" title="IETF">IETF</a>, and several industry groups (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a>) led to more various interfacing under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_personal_area_network" title="Wireless personal area network">WPAN</a> (wireless personal area network). It also led the <a href="/wiki/Body_area_network" title="Body area network">WBAN</a> (Wireless body area network) to offer new classification of designs for interfacing and networking. The 6th-generation <a href="/wiki/IPod_Nano" title="IPod Nano">iPod Nano</a>, released in September 2010, has a wristband attachment available to convert it into a wearable wristwatch computer.</p>
<p>The development of wearable computing spread to encompass <a href="/wiki/Rehabilitation_engineering" title="Rehabilitation engineering">rehabilitation engineering</a>, ambulatory intervention treatment, life guard systems, and defense wearable systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> produced a wristwatch called <a href="/wiki/Sony_SmartWatch" title="Sony SmartWatch">Sony SmartWatch</a> that must be paired with an Android phone. Once paired, it becomes an additional remote display and notification tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fitbit" title="Fitbit">Fitbit</a> released several wearable fitness trackers and the Fitbit Surge, a full <a href="/wiki/Smartwatch" title="Smartwatch">smartwatch</a> that is compatible with Android and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a>.</p>
<p>On April 11, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Pebble_(watch)" title="Pebble (watch)">Pebble</a> launched a <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> campaign to raise $100,000 for their initial smartwatch model. The campaign ended on May 18 with $10,266,844, over 100 times the fundraising target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Pebble has released several smartwatches since, including the <a href="/wiki/Pebble_Time" title="Pebble Time">Pebble Time</a> and the Pebble Round.</p>

<p>Google Glass launched their <a href="/wiki/Optical_head-mounted_display" title="Optical head-mounted display">optical head-mounted display</a> (OHMD) to a test group of users in 2013, before it became available to the public on May 15, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Google's mission was to produce a mass-market <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">ubiquitous computer</a> that displays information in a <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a>-like hands-free format<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> that can interact with the Internet via <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language</a> voice commands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2012-02-23_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_2012-02-23-40">[40]</a></sup> Google Glass received criticism over privacy and safety concerns. On January 15, 2015, Google announced that it would stop producing the Google Glass prototype but would continue to develop the product. According to Google, Project Glass was ready to "graduate" from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_X" title="Google X">Google X</a>, the experimental phase of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_15_January_2015_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_15_January_2015-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thync" title="Thync">Thync</a>, a headset launched in 2014, is a wearable that stimulates the brain with mild electrical pulses, causing the wearer to feel energized or calm based on input into a phone app. The device is attached to the temple and to the back of the neck with an adhesive strip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Macrotellect_Ltd" title="Macrotellect Ltd">Macrotellect</a> launched 2 portable brainwave(<a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a>) sensing devices, BrainLink Pro and BrainLink Lite in 2014, which allows families and meditation students to enhance the mental fittness and stress relief with 20+ brain fittness enhancement Apps on Apple and Android App Stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2015, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> announced the sub-miniature Intel Curie for wearable applications, based on its <a href="/wiki/Intel_Quark" title="Intel Quark">Intel Quark</a> platform. As small as a button, it features a 6-axis accelerometer, a DSP sensor hub, a Bluetooth LE unit, and a battery charge controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It was scheduled to ship in the second half of the year.</p>
<p>On April 24, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> released their take on the smartwatch, known as the Apple Watch. The Apple Watch features a touchscreen, many applications, and a heart-rate sensor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<p>The commercialization of general-purpose wearable computers, as led by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Xybernaut" title="Xybernaut">Xybernaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/CDI_Corporation" title="CDI Corporation">CDI</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ViA,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ViA, Inc. (page does not exist)">ViA, Inc.</a> has thus far been met with limited success. Publicly traded Xybernaut tried forging alliances with companies such as <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> in order to make wearable computing widely available, and managed to get their equipment seen on such shows as <a href="/wiki/First_Person_Shooter_(The_X-Files)" title="First Person Shooter (The X-Files)">The X-Files</a>, but in 2005 their stock was delisted and the company filed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11" title="Chapter 11">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy protection amid financial scandal and federal investigation. Xybernaut emerged from bankruptcy protection in January, 2007. ViA, Inc. filed for bankruptcy in 2001 and subsequently ceased operations.</p>
<p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Seiko" title="Seiko">Seiko</a> marketed the <a href="/wiki/Ruputer" title="Ruputer">Ruputer</a>, a computer in a (fairly large) wristwatch, to mediocre returns. In 2001, IBM developed and publicly displayed two prototypes for a wristwatch computer running <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. The last message about them dates to <a class="external text" href="http://gadgets.engadget.com/2004/10/20/watch-this-wednesday-the-linux-watch/" rel="nofollow">2004</a>, saying the device would cost about $250, but it is still under development. In 2002, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil,_Inc." title="Fossil, Inc.">Fossil, Inc.</a> announced the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_Wrist_PDA" title="Fossil Wrist PDA">Fossil Wrist PDA</a>, which ran the <a href="/wiki/Palm_OS" title="Palm OS">Palm OS</a>. Its release date was set for summer of 2003, but was delayed several times and was finally made available on January 5, 2005. <a href="/wiki/Timex_Datalink" title="Timex Datalink">Timex Datalink</a> is another example of a practical wearable computer. <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a> launched a wearable computer called Poma in 2002. <a href="/wiki/Eurotech_(company)" title="Eurotech (company)">Eurotech</a> offers the <a href="/wiki/ZYPAD" title="ZYPAD">ZYPAD</a>, a wrist wearable touch screen computer with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> connectivity and which can run a number of custom applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 2013, a wearable computing device on the wrist to control body temperature was developed at MIT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence of weak market acceptance was demonstrated when <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a> Computer Solutions Company's product failed. Panasonic has specialized in mobile computing with their <a href="/wiki/Toughbook" title="Toughbook">Toughbook</a> line for over 10 years and has extensive market research into the field of portable, wearable computing products. In 2002, Panasonic introduced a wearable <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brick_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brick computer (page does not exist)">brick computer</a> coupled with a handheld or a touchscreen worn on the arm. The "Brick" Computer is the CF-07 Toughbook, dual batteries, screen used same batteries as the base, 800 x 600 resolution, optional GPS and WWAN. Has one M-PCI slot and one PCMCIA slot for expansion. CPU used is a 600 MHz Pentium 3 factory under clocked to 300 MHz so it can stay cool passively as it has no fan. Micro DIM RAM is upgradeable. The screen can be used wirelessly on other computers. The brick would communicate wirelessly to the screen, and concurrently the brick would communicate wirelessly out to the internet or other networks. The wearable brick was quietly pulled from the market in 2005, while the screen evolved to a <a href="/wiki/Thin_client" title="Thin client">thin client</a> touchscreen used with a handstrap.</p>
<p>Google has announced that it has been working on a <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a>-based wearable "<a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a>" device called <a href="/wiki/Google_Glass" title="Google Glass">Google Glass</a>. An early version of the device was available to the US public from April 2013 until January 2015. Despite ending sales of the device through their Explorer Program, Google has stated that they plan to continue developing the technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LG" title="LG">LG</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iriver" title="Iriver">iriver</a> produce earbud wearables measuring heart rate and other biometrics, as well as various activity metrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greater response to commercialization has been found in creating devices with designated purposes rather than all-purpose. One example is the WSS1000. The WSS1000 is a wearable computer designed to make the work of inventory employees easier and more efficient. The device allows workers to scan the barcode of items and immediately enter the information into the company system. This removed the need for carrying a clipboard, removed error and confusion from hand written notes, and allowed workers the freedom of both hands while working; the system improves accuracy as well as efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wearable_computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many technologies for wearable computers derive their ideas from science fiction. There are many examples of ideas from popular movies that have become technologies or are technologies currently being developed.</p>
<ul>
<li>3D User Interface: Devices to display usable interfaces wherever you are. Ex. <a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a>, Gate Workers in Zion (Matrix), etc.</li>
<li>Intelligent Textiles: Clothing that can relay and collect information. Ex. <a href="/wiki/Tron" title="Tron">Tron</a> and also many Sci-Fi Military movies.</li>
<li>Threat Glasses: Scan others in vicinity and assess threat-to-self level. Ex. <a href="/wiki/Terminator_2:_Judgment_Day" title="Terminator 2: Judgment Day">Terminator 2</a> or 'Threep' Technology in Lock-In</li>
<li>Computerized Contact Lenses: Ex. <a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible_%E2%80%93_Ghost_Protocol" title="Mission: Impossible – Ghost Protocol">Mission Impossible 4</a></li>
<li>Combat Suit Armor: Ex. Iron Man Suit</li>
<li>Brain Nano-Bots to Store Memories in the Cloud: Ex. Total Recall</li>
<li>Infrared Headsets: Can help identify suspects and see through walls. Ex. <a href="/wiki/RoboCop" title="RoboCop">Robocop</a>.</li>
<li>Wrist worn Computers: Various abilities such as wearer's stats, area maps, inventory, flashlight, geiger counter (Pip Boy 3000) or communication, laser, detect poison in food, tracking device (Leela's Wrist Device). Ex. Pip Boy 3000 from <a href="/wiki/Fallout_(series)" title="Fallout (series)">Fallout</a> games and Leela's Wrist Device from <a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></li>
</ul>

<p>The wearable computer was introduced to the US Army in 1989, as a small computer that was meant to assist soldiers in battle. Since then, the concept has grown to include the = Land Warrior program and proposal for future systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zieniewicz_2002_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zieniewicz_2002-53">[53]</a></sup> The most extensive military program in the wearables arena is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">US Army</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Land_Warrior" title="Land Warrior">Land Warrior</a> system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> which will eventually be merged into the <a href="/wiki/Future_Force_Warrior" title="Future Force Warrior">Future Force Warrior</a> system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/F-INSAS" title="F-INSAS">F-INSAS</a> is an Indian Military Project, designed largely with wearable computing.</p>
