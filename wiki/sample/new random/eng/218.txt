<p><b>Osaka Castle</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">大坂城 or 大阪城,</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ōsaka-jō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_castle" title="Japanese castle">Japanese castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%AB%C5%8D-ku,_Osaka" title="Chūō-ku, Osaka">Chūō-ku, Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The castle is one of Japan's most famous landmarks and it played a major role in the unification of Japan during the sixteenth century of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azuchi-Momoyama_period" title="Azuchi-Momoyama period">Azuchi-Momoyama period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osaka_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main tower of Osaka Castle is situated on a plot of land roughly one square kilometer. It is built on two raised platforms of landfill supported by sheer walls of cut rock, using a technique called <a href="/wiki/Burdock_piling" title="Burdock piling">Burdock piling</a>, each overlooking a <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a>. The central castle building is five stories on the outside and eight stories on the inside, and built atop a tall stone foundation to protect its occupants from attackers.</p>
<p>The castle grounds, which cover approximately 60,000 square meters (15 acres),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> contain thirteen structures that have been designated as important cultural assets by the Japanese government,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ote-mon Gate</li>
<li>Sakura-mon Gate</li>
<li>Ichiban-yagura Turret</li>
<li>Inui-yagura Turret</li>
<li>Rokuban-yagura Turret</li>
<li>Sengan Turret</li>
<li>Tamon Turret</li>
<li>Kinmeisui Well</li>
<li>Kinzo Storehouse</li>
<li>Enshogura Gunpowder Magazine</li>
<li>Three sections of castle wall all located around Otemon Gate</li>
</ul>

<p>In 1583 <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a> commenced construction on the site of the <a href="/wiki/Ikk%C5%8D-ikki" title="Ikkō-ikki">Ikkō-ikki</a> temple of <a href="/wiki/Ishiyama_Hongan-ji" title="Ishiyama Hongan-ji">Ishiyama Hongan-ji</a>. The basic plan was modeled after <a href="/wiki/Azuchi_Castle" title="Azuchi Castle">Azuchi Castle</a>, the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>. Toyotomi wanted to build a castle that mirrored Oda's, but surpassed it in every way: the plan featured a five-story main tower, with three extra stories underground, and <a href="/wiki/Gold_leaf" title="Gold leaf">gold leaf</a> on the sides of the tower to impress visitors. In 1585 the Inner donjon was completed. Toyotomi continued to extend and expand the castle, making it more and more formidable to attackers. In 1597 construction was completed and Hideyoshi died. Osaka Castle passed to his son, <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyori" title="Toyotomi Hideyori">Toyotomi Hideyori</a>.</p>
<p>In 1600 <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">Tokugawa Ieyasu</a> defeated his opponents at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sekigahara" title="Battle of Sekigahara">Battle of Sekigahara</a>, and started his own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakufu" title="Bakufu">bakufu</a> (i.e., shogunate) in Edo. In 1614 Tokugawa attacked Toyotomi in the winter, starting the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Osaka" title="Siege of Osaka">Siege of Osaka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the Toyotomi forces were outnumbered approximately two to one, they managed to fight off Tokugawa's 200,000-man army and protect the castle's outer walls. Ieyasu had the castle's outer moat filled, negating one of the castle's main outer defenses.</p>
<p>During the summer of 1615, Hideyori began to restore the outer moat. Tokugawa, in outrage, sent his armies to Osaka Castle again, and routed the Toyotomi men inside the outer walls on June 4. Osaka Castle fell to Tokugawa, and the <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_clan" title="Toyotomi clan">Toyotomi clan</a> perished.</p>
<p>In 1620, the new heir to the shogunate, <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Hidetada" title="Tokugawa Hidetada">Tokugawa Hidetada</a>, began to reconstruct and re-arm Osaka Castle. He built a new elevated main tower, five stories on the outside and eight stories on the inside, and assigned the task of constructing new walls to individual samurai clans. The walls built in the 1620s still stand today, and are made out of interlocked granite boulders without mortar. Many of the stones were brought from rock quarries near the <a href="/wiki/Seto_Inland_Sea" title="Seto Inland Sea">Seto Inland Sea</a>, and bear inscribed crests of the various families who contributed them.</p>
<p>In 1660, lightning ignited the gunpowder warehouse and the resulting explosion set the castle on fire. In 1665, lightning struck and burnt down the main tower. In 1843, after decades of neglect, the castle got much-needed repairs when the bakufu collected money from the people of the region to rebuild several of the turrets.</p>
<p>In 1868, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Osaka_castle" title="Fall of Osaka castle">Osaka Castle fell</a> and was surrendered to anti-bakufu imperial loyalists. Much of the castle was burned in the civil conflicts surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>.</p>
<p>Under the Meiji government, Osaka Castle became part of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Osaka_Army_Arsenal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Osaka Army Arsenal (page does not exist)">Osaka Army Arsenal</a> (<i>Osaka Hohei Kosho</i>) manufacturing guns, ammunition, and explosives for Japan's rapidly expanding Western-style military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1928, the main tower was restored after the mayor of Osaka concluded a highly successful fund-raising drive.</p>
<p>During World War II, the arsenal became one of the largest military armories, employing 60,000 workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> Bombing raids targeting the arsenal damaged the reconstructed main castle tower and, on August 14, 1945, destroyed 90% of the arsenal and killed 382 people working there.</p>
<p>In 1995, Osaka's government approved yet another restoration project, with the intent of restoring the main tower to its Edo-era splendor. In 1997, restoration was completed. The castle is a concrete reproduction (including elevators) of the original and the interior is intended as a modern, functioning museum.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessibility">Accessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osaka_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The castle is open to the public and is easily accessible from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osakaj%C5%8Dk%C5%8Den_Station" title="Osakajōkōen Station">Osakajōkōen Station</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JR_West" title="JR West">JR West</a> <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Loop_Line" title="Osaka Loop Line">Osaka Loop Line</a>. It is a popular spot during festival seasons, and especially during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakura" title="Sakura">cherry blossom</a> bloom (<a href="/wiki/Hanami" title="Hanami">hanami</a>), when the sprawling castle grounds are covered with food vendors and <a href="/wiki/Taiko" title="Taiko">taiko</a> drummers. The large indoor arena, <a href="/wiki/Osaka-j%C5%8D_Hall" title="Osaka-jō Hall">Osaka-jō Hall</a>, also is located within the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Castle_Park" title="Osaka Castle Park">castle park</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osaka_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The castle was featured in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Race_20" title="The Amazing Race 20">The Amazing Race 20</a></i>. The castle also appears in the 1955 <a href="/wiki/Toho" title="Toho">Toho</a> <a href="/wiki/Tokusatsu" title="Tokusatsu">tokusatsu</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Godzilla_Raids_Again" title="Godzilla Raids Again">Godzilla Raids Again</a></i>, in which it is destroyed after <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">Godzilla</a> pins <a href="/wiki/Anguirus" title="Anguirus">Anguirus</a> against the castle, causing it to collapse.</p>
