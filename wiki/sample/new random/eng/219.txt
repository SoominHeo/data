<p>The <b>Military Demarcation Line</b> (<b>MDL</b>), sometimes referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armistice_Line" title="Armistice Line">Armistice Line</a>, is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_border" title="Land border">land border</a> or <a href="/wiki/Demarcation_line" title="Demarcation line">demarcation line</a> between <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. On either side of the line is the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Korean Demilitarized Zone">Korean Demilitarized Zone</a> (DMZ). The MDL and DMZ were established by the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Armistice_Agreement" title="Korean Armistice Agreement">Armistice</a> at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Yellow Sea, the two Koreas are divided by a <i>de facto</i> maritime "military demarcation line" and <a href="/wiki/Maritime_boundary#Unresolved_maritime_boundaries" title="Maritime boundary">maritime boundary</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Limit_Line" title="Northern Limit Line">Northern Limit Line</a> (NLL) drawn by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Command" title="United Nations Command">United Nations Command</a> in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The NLL is not described by the Korean Armistice Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demarcation_on_land">Demarcation on land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_Demarcation_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Demarcation on land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The DMZ runs near the 38th parallel, covering roughly 248 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> soldiers patrol this line along the South Korean side while <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> soldiers patrol along the North Korean side.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, the line is called the <i>Hyujeonseon</i> (휴전선), meaning "armistice line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also sometimes called the <i>Gunsa Bungye-seon</i> (군사분계선), which literally means "military demarcation line." However, in colloquial usage, the dividing line is more often called the <i>Sampalseon</i> (삼팔선, "<a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38th parallel</a>"), a name likely coined at the end of World War II, when it would have been an accurate description of the North-South border.</p>
<p>The line itself is marked off by a series of 1,292 identical signs which are placed at intervals across the peninsula. The north facing side of the signs are written in Korean and Chinese, and in Korean and English on the south facing side. The signs are now aging and rusting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_incidents">Military incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_Demarcation_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been <a href="/wiki/List_of_border_incidents_involving_North_Korea" title="List of border incidents involving North Korea">frequent skirmishes</a> along the line since the armistice ended the fighting of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>.</p>

<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Armistice_Agreement" title="Korean Armistice Agreement">Korean Armistice Agreement</a> specifies where the <a href="/wiki/Demarcation_line" title="Demarcation line">demarcation line</a> and demilitarized zone are located on land, the agreement does not mention lines or zones in adjacent <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean waters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Shortly after the signing of the Armistice, a line in the sea was drawn unilaterally by the United Nations Command (UNC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This Northern Limit Line or North Limit Line (NLL) represented the northern limit of the area in which South Korea permits its vessels to navigate, not a demarcation line on which the two Koreas agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hankeoreh_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hankeoreh-11">[11]</a></sup> The Korean Armistice Agreement (KAA) provisions regarding the MDL and DMZ do not extend into the Yellow Sea or Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, North Korea unilaterally asserted its own "North Korean Military Demarcation Line in the West Sea (Yellow Sea)",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> also called the "Inter-Korean MDL in the Yellow Sea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peoplesdaily2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-peoplesdaily2010-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, the UNC-drawn line functions as a <i>de facto</i> or "practical" extension of the 1953 MDL, despite occasional incursions and clashes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_Demarcation_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The MDL was defined and established in the Korean Armistice Agreement (KAA), Article I, paragraphs 1–11. The KAA includes provisions regarding the MDL and DMZ; but those provisions do not extend into the Yellow Sea or the Sea of Japan. The subsequently devised Northern Boundary Line or <a class="external text" href="http://www1.korea-np.co.jp/pk/099th_issue/990616015.htm" rel="nofollow">NLL</a> was neither initially conceived as part of the MDL, nor have the DPRK and UNC agreed to any subsequent extension of the MDL beyond the agreed upon limits of 1953/7/27.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ryoo, Moo Bong. (2009). <a class="external text" href="http://www.dtic.mil/cgi-bin/GetTRDoc?AD=ADA500904&amp;Location=U2&amp;doc=GetTRDoc.pdf" rel="nofollow">"The Korean Armistice and the Islands,"</a> p. 5. Strategy research project at the U.S. Army War College; excerpt, "<i>Strategic Consequences of the Agreement</i>. The agreement to retain the five islands under UNC control has shaped many aspects of the security environment of the Korean Peninsula. The most obvious and prominent consequence is the establishment of the Northern Limit Line (NLL). The NLL has served as a practical maritime borderline and an effective means to separate the forces and thus prevent military clashes between th two Koreas"; compare Kim, Kwang-Tae. <a class="external text" href="http://www.time.com/time/world/article/0,8599,2032789,00.html#ixzz16AytHQUq" rel="nofollow">"After Exchange of Fire, N. Korea Threatens More Strikes on South,"</a> <i>Time</i> (US). November 23, 2010.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Pak, Hŭi-gwŏn. (2000). <i><a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=szcywfgKySAC&amp;pg=PA108" rel="nofollow">The Law of the Sea and Northeast Asia: a Challenge for Cooperation, <i>p. 108</i></a></i>, p. 108, at <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a>; excerpt, "Under the 1953 Armistice Agreement, the Military Demarcation Line was drawn across the Korean peninsula. Since no maritime demarcation line was specified in the Armistice Agreement, however, the United Nations Command drew the NLL...."</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://www.lifeinkorea.com/culture/dmz/dmz.cfm?Subject=History2" rel="nofollow">"Korean War History"</a><span class="reference-accessdate">. Retrieved <span class="nowrap">2006-01-12</span></span>.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AMilitary+Demarcation+Line&amp;rft.btitle=Korean+War+History&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.lifeinkorea.com%2Fculture%2Fdmz%2Fdmz.cfm%3FSubject%3DHistory2&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">See, e.g., <a class="external text" href="http://www.websters-online-dictionary.org/definitions/Armistice?cx=partner-pub-0939450753529744%3Av0qd01-tdlq&amp;cof=FORID%3A9&amp;ie=UTF-8&amp;q=Armistice&amp;sa=Search#906" rel="nofollow">Dictionary – Definition of armistice.</a></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ryoo, p. 13.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-reuters2010-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-reuters2010_7-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://www.reuters.com/article/idUSTRE6AM2O820101123" rel="nofollow">"Factbox: What is the Korean Northern Limit Line?"</a> Reuters (UK). November 23, 2010; retrieved 26 Nov 2010.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Van Dyke, Jon <i>et al.</i> "The North/South Korea Boundary Dispute in the Yellow (West) Sea," <i>Marine Policy</i> 27 (2003), 143–158; note that "Inter-Korean MDL" is cited because it comes from an <a class="external text" href="http://www.law.hawaii.edu/sites/www.law.hawaii.edu/files/webFM/Faculty/N-SKoreaBoundary2003.pdf" rel="nofollow">academic source</a> and the writers were particular enough to include in quotes as we present it. The broader point is that the maritime demarcation line here is NOT a formal extension of the Military Demarcation Line; compare <a class="external text" href="http://english.peopledaily.com.cn/200211/21/eng20021121_107188.shtml" rel="nofollow">"NLL—Controversial Sea Border Between S.Korea, DPRK, "</a> <i>People's Daily</i> (PRC), November 21, 2002; retrieved 22 Dec 2010</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Glionna, John M. <a class="external text" href="http://articles.latimes.com/2011/jan/06/world/la-fg-korea-sea-border-20110107" rel="nofollow">"Sea border a trigger for Korean peninsula tension,"</a> <i>Los Angeles Times</i> (US). January 6, 2011; retrieved 2011-07-28</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Kim Young-koo. <a class="external text" href="https://www.judicialview.com/Law-Review/International/A-Maritime-Demarcation-Dispute-on-the-Yellow-Sea:-Republic-of-Korea/33/8809" rel="nofollow">"A Maritime Demarcation Dispute on the Yellow Sea: Republic of Korea,"</a> <i>Journal of East Asia and International Law</i> (JEAIL), Volume 2, Number 2.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-hankeoreh-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-hankeoreh_11-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://english.hani.co.kr/arti/english_edition/e_national/243031.html" rel="nofollow">"Modern meaning of Korea’s maritime line,"</a> <i>The Hankyoreh</i> (ROK). October 15, 2007.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-12"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-12">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In principle, the UNC and PRK <i>could</i> agree to extend the MDL into the Yellow Sea (IAW para. 62 of the KAA); however, there is no such agreement.</span></li>

(2001). <i><a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=6Rx8Q_cxqvkC&amp;pg=PA191" rel="nofollow">North Korea: a Strange Socialist Fortress, <i>p. 191</i></a></i>, p. 191, at <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a>; <a class="external text" href="http://www.srilankaguardian.org/2010/11/south-korea-is-criminal-without.html" rel="nofollow">"South Korea is a criminal without principle." – Noth Korea",</a> <i>Sir Lanka Guardian,</i> November 25, 2010.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-peoplesdaily2010-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-peoplesdaily2010_14-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://english.peopledaily.com.cn/200211/21/eng20021121_107188.shtml" rel="nofollow">"NLL—Controversial Sea Border Between S.Korea, DPRK, "</a> <i>People's Daily</i> (PRC), 2002-11-21; retrieved 2010-11-26.</span></li>
