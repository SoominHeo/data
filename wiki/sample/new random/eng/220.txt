<p><b>Jefferies LLC</b> is an American global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investment_bank" title="Investment bank">investment bank</a> and institutional securities firm headquartered in New York. The firm provides clients with <a href="/wiki/Capital_market" title="Capital market">capital markets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">financial advisory</a> services, institutional brokerage, securities research, and <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a>. This includes <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restructuring" title="Restructuring">restructuring</a>, and other financial advisory services.</p>
<p>Jefferies was named one of the World's Most Admired Companies by <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine in 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Best Place to Work in the Financial Industry by Here Is The City News in 2010, 2011, 2012 and 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and one of the best companies to work for in the UK by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 12, 2012, Jefferies announced its merger with <a href="/wiki/Leucadia_National" title="Leucadia National">Leucadia</a>, its largest shareholder. At that time, Leucadia common shares were trading at $21.14 per share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As of December 31, 2015, Leucadia shares were trading at $17.39 per share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Jefferies remains independent and is the largest operating company within Leucadia. On March 1, 2013, along with the closing of the merger, Jefferies &amp; Company, Inc. was converted to a <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a> and re-branded Jefferies LLC.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1962.E2.80.931987">1962–1987</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1962–1987">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jefferies was founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boyd_Jefferies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boyd Jefferies (page does not exist)">Boyd Jefferies</a> in 1962. The firm started with $30,000 in borrowed capital, which Boyd Jefferies used to purchase a seat on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Stock_Exchange" title="Pacific Coast Stock Exchange">Pacific Coast Stock Exchange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the early years, the firm was a successful trader and pioneer in what would be called the "<a href="/wiki/Third_market" title="Third market">third market</a>", which allowed for the trading of listed stocks directly between institutional investors in an <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_(finance)" title="Over-the-counter (finance)">over-the-counter</a> style, providing liquidity and anonymity to buyers. In addition to its <a href="/wiki/Third_market" title="Third market">third market</a> niche, Jefferies pioneered use of the split commissions in 1964. By 1965, Jefferies had joined the Detroit, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Stock_Exchange" title="Chicago Stock Exchange">Midwest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Stock_Exchange" title="Boston Stock Exchange">Boston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Stock_Exchange" title="Philadelphia Stock Exchange">Philadelphia</a> stock exchanges. In 1967, the company joined the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (NYSE), opening a five-person office in New York. The growing third market helped Jefferies become the seventh largest firm in size and trading on the NYSE during those years.</p>
<p>Jefferies was acquired in 1969 by Minneapolis-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investors_Diversified_Services" title="Investors Diversified Services">Investors Diversified Services</a> (IDS), the second largest U.S. financial services company at the time, and resigned all its stock exchange memberships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Jefferies saw the acquisition as a means to increase the size of its institutional business with additional capital. However, because IDS did not derive at least 50 percent of its gross income from <a href="/wiki/Broker-dealer" title="Broker-dealer">broker-dealer</a> operations, Jefferies had to quit the New York exchange under Exchange Rule 318. In 1971, IDS and Jefferies filed an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antitrust" title="Antitrust">antitrust</a> lawsuit against the exchange, seeking $6 million in damages. Jefferies and its parent company claimed that the NYSE Big Board was an illegal monopoly and that exclusion had placed the company at a competitive disadvantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1973 the presiding judge informed the NYSE that he planned to rule in Jefferies favour. Membership was opened to brokerage firms owned by other kinds of companies, so long as 80 percent of brokerage was conducted with the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Jefferies rejoined the exchange in March 1973.</p>
<p>The period during which IDS owned Jefferies was tumultuous and ultimately in September 1973 Boyd Jefferies bought back the company, then based in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1977, Jefferies had expanded with offices in Los Angeles, New York, Chicago, Dallas, Boston, and Atlanta.</p>
<p>Jefferies went public on October 13, 1983, with an initial offering of 1.75 million shares at $13 per share. By 1984, according to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_Week" title="Business Week">Business Week</a></i>, Jefferies was among the ten most profitable publicly held brokerages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> International expansion led the company to develop a new overseas office in London, headed by Frank Baxter. In 1986, Baxter became president and chief operating officer, returning to New York to manage the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, Boyd Jefferies was charged by the government and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> with two securities violations: "parking" stock for a customer <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Boesky" title="Ivan Boesky">Ivan Boesky</a> and a customer margin violation. Jefferies, who had also earlier testified against Boesky, pleaded guilty; receiving a fine and a <a href="/wiki/Probation" title="Probation">probation</a> barring him from the securities industry for five years. The company itself was not charged but its brokerage unit was censured by the SEC. Boyd Jefferies resigned from the company in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1988.E2.80.931999">1988–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1988–1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frank Baxter took over as CEO in 1987 and under his leadership the company focused on diversification, moving beyond its third market niche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1990, Jefferies derived approximately 80 percent of its revenues from equity <a href="/wiki/Block_trade" title="Block trade">block trades</a>. In that year, following the collapse of Los Angeles-based <a href="/wiki/Drexel_Burnham_Lambert" title="Drexel Burnham Lambert">Drexel Burnham Lambert</a>, the fifth largest investment bank at the time, Jefferies hired 60 of its bankers and traders, including Jefferies' current chairman and CEO, <a href="/wiki/Richard_B._Handler" title="Richard B. Handler">Richard B. Handler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boyd_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-boyd-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The hires marked the firm's entry into the high yield markets and investment banking. Three years later Jefferies launched its first sector-focused investment banking effort by hiring a group of bankers from Howard Weil, an oil and gas specialty boutique. In March 1994, Jefferies acquired a 25% stake in <a href="/wiki/BBY_Ltd" title="BBY Ltd">BBY Ltd</a>, an Australian stockbroking and corporate advisory firm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Baxter's expansion plans also included global expansion in electronic trading, corporate finance, international convertible sales and derivative sales. Jefferies also moved quickly into the fourth market: off-exchange, computer-based (electronic) trading. In the fourth market the broker's position was eliminated by the Portfolio System for Institutional Trading (POSIT) that traded portfolios and matched buyers and sellers automatically. The company created a wholly owned subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Investment_Technology_Group" title="Investment Technology Group">Investment Technology Group</a> in 1987 to run POSIT. <a href="/wiki/Investment_Technology_Group" title="Investment Technology Group">Investment Technology Group</a> was eventually spun off as a separate public company in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.932010">2000–2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000–2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2000 Frank Baxter stepped down as president of Jefferies and relinquished the CEO title later that year. In January 2001, Handler became Chairman and CEO; and John Shaw became sole president and COO. Handler and Shaw set out to build a fully integrated investment bank and to develop a <a href="/wiki/Merchant_bank" title="Merchant bank">merchant bank</a>. The new leadership proposed to give equity to every employee and diversify the firm's revenue with <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a>, a more aggressive buildup of investment banking and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant_banking" title="Merchant banking">merchant banking</a>. In September 2001, the firm moved its headquarters from Los Angeles to New York. During this period, Jefferies built its investment banking division primarily by acquiring boutique advisory firms with specific sector expertise, most notably Randall &amp; Dewey (energy) and Broadview (technology). Significant acquisitions during this period included:</p>
<ul>
<li>FS Private Investments, renamed Jefferies Capital Partners, 2001</li>
<li>Lawrence Helfant, a NYSE Floor Broker Unit, August 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Quarterdeck Investment Partners, an aerospace and defense advisory firm, December 2002</li>
<li>Broadview International, a technology-focused advisory firm, December 2003</li>
<li>Randall &amp; Dewey, an energy-focused advisory firm, February 2005</li>
<li>Helix, a private equity fund placement firm, May 2005</li>
<li>LongAcre Partners, a media advisory firm, May 2007</li>
<li>Putnam Lovell, a financial services advisory firm, July 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Depfa First Albany Securities, municipal securities, March 2009</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bache_%26_Co." title="Bache &amp; Co.">Bache &amp; Co.</a>, FX, commodities and options trading, July 2011</li>
<li>Hoare Govett, corporate broking, February 2012</li>
</ul>
<p>Beginning in 2008, Jefferies took advantage of the dislocation created during the credit crisis to enter several new business segments, including <a href="/wiki/Mortgage-backed_security" title="Mortgage-backed security">mortgage-backed securities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_bond" title="Municipal bond">municipal bonds</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On June 17, 2009, after several <a href="/wiki/Primary_dealer" title="Primary dealer">primary dealers</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Brothers" title="Lehman Brothers">Lehman Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bear_Stearns" title="Bear Stearns">Bear Stearns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a>, either collapsed or were acquired by other firms, Jefferies was named one of just 17 primary dealers participating in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York" title="Federal Reserve Bank of New York">New York Fed</a>'s open-market buying and selling of securities and Treasury auctions and providing market information to the New York Fed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 2009 the firm expanded its European businesses. Its new European Rates unit, based in London, became an official member of the Federal Republic of Germany's bidding group in October 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilt-Edged_Market_Maker" title="Gilt-Edged Market Maker">Gilt-Edged Market Maker</a> (GEMM) appointed jointly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Debt_Management_Office" title="UK Debt Management Office">UK Debt Management Office</a> and London Stock Exchange,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and was recognized as a Dutch Primary Dealer by the Dutch State Treasury,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> both in December 2009. Additionally, in February 2010 Jefferies was appointed as an EBT (Especialistas em Bilhetes do Tesouro) for Treasury Bills and as an OMP (Operadores de Mercado Primário) for long-term bonds by the Portuguese Treasury and Government Debt Agency (Instituto de Gestão da Tesouraria e do Crédito Público, IGCP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Jefferies is also providing liquidity across the whole spectrum of other European government bonds.</p>
<p>In June 2009 the firm hired more than 35 healthcare-focused investment banking professionals from <a href="/wiki/UBS" title="UBS">UBS</a>. UBS’s health care group, then led by Benjamin Lorello, was a major moneymaker for the firm. The group had closed more than $567 billion in transactions since 2005, generating in excess of $1 billion in revenues for UBS. Since moving to Jefferies, the healthcare group has been ranked the #1 bookrunner in number of healthcare follow-on equity transactions and the #1 ranked financial advisor in number of healthcare M&amp;A transactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_onward">2010 onward</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010 onward">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2011, Jefferies was accused by <a href="/wiki/Egan-Jones_Ratings_Company" title="Egan-Jones Ratings Company">Egan-Jones</a> of having 77% of its shareholder's equity tied up in the same illiquid sovereign debt securities that just toppled <a href="/wiki/MF_Global" title="MF Global">MF Global</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This was accompanied by a concurrent large-scale short seller attack and a campaign of what turned out to be misinformation. Handler and the management team responded with unprecedented immediacy and transparency, collapsed 75% of the position to prove the bonds were hedged and highly liquid, sharply reduced the rest of Jefferies balance sheet and publicly addressed every false accusation. This aggressive and unconventional response resulted in an evenutual 100% increase in Jefferies share price from the November lows. Leucadia, a 29% shareholder, later called this event Jefferies' "finest hour."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 16, 2012 Jefferies CEO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Handler" title="Richard Handler">Richard Handler</a> and Chairman of the Executive Committee Brian Friedman formed the Jefferies Global Senior Advisory Board which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_D_Robinson_III" title="James D Robinson III">James D Robinson III</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Hollick" title="Lord Hollick">Lord Hollick</a>, Michael Goldstein, Bernard Bourigeaud, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Archer" title="Dennis Archer">Dennis Archer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gilles_P%C3%A9lisson" title="Gilles Pélisson">Gilles Pélisson</a> and Sir David Reid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 1, 2012, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_Capital" title="Knight Capital">Knight Capital</a> took a pre-tax loss of $440m due to a trading glitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On Sunday Aug 5 the company managed to raise around $400 million from six investors led by Jefferies in an attempt to stay in business after the trading error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Jefferies CEO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Handler" title="Richard Handler">Richard Handler</a> and Executive Committee Chair Brian Friedman structured and led the rescue and Jefferies purchased $125 million of the $400 million investment and became Knight's largest shareholder.</p>
<p>On November 12, 2012, Jefferies announced its merger with Leucadia, its largest (28%) shareholder. Leucadia is often referred to as a Baby Berkshire because of its similarities to <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a>. Jefferies was valued at $3.8 billion and at the time of the acquisition the newly combined company had $9.4 billion of shareholder's equity, over $5 billion of cash, and $4 billion of net operating loss ("NOL") tax credits. Jefferies remains independent and is the largest operating company within Leucadia. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Handler" title="Richard Handler">Richard Handler</a> became CEO of Leucadia while retaining his position as CEO of Jefferies. Brian Friedman became President of Leucadia while retaining his duties at Jefferies. Ian Cumming retired as CEO of Leucadia and remains a Board Member and Joe Steinberg became Chairman of Leucadia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2014, Jefferies announced a $500 million deal with CircleBack Lending, which was the largest of its kind at the time. They aimed to leverage their experience with <a href="/wiki/Securitization" title="Securitization">securitization markets</a> to structure <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a> upheld by CircleBack Lending assets, which could then be rated and sold to an array of investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In June 2015, the first <a href="/wiki/Securitization" title="Securitization">securitization</a> from this deal took place, totaling $106 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jefferies_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jefferies has coverage groups spanning across all industries including Aerospace &amp; Defense, Business Services, CleanTech, Consumer &amp; Retail, Energy, Financial Institutions Group, Financial Sponsors, Gaming &amp; Leisure, Healthcare, Industrials, Maritime, Media, Public Finance, Real Estate &amp; Lodging, Technology, and Telecommunications.</p>
<p>The firm also provides investors fundamental research and trade execution in equity, <a href="/wiki/Equity-linked_note" title="Equity-linked note">equity-linked</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">fixed income</a> securities, including <a href="/wiki/Corporate_bond" title="Corporate bond">corporate bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Treasury_security" title="United States Treasury security">United States government</a> and agency securities, repo finance, <a href="/wiki/Mortgage-backed_security" title="Mortgage-backed security">mortgage-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asset-backed_security" title="Asset-backed security">asset-backed securities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_bond" title="Municipal bond">municipal bonds</a>, whole loans, and <a href="/wiki/Emerging_market_debt" title="Emerging market debt">emerging market debt</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a>. In addition, Jefferies provides asset management services and products to institutions and other investors.</p>
<p>The Group Global Headquarters located in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, European Headquarters located in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> &amp; Asia Headquarters located in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Jefferies has over 30 offices worldwide including <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> area as well as in leading financial centers around the world that include <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boyd_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-boyd-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
