<p><b>Pad Thai</b>, <b>phat Thai</b> or <b>phad Thai</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">ผัดไทย</span>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration">phat thai</span>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_11940" title="ISO 11940">ISO</a> p̄hạdịthy, <small>pronounced</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[pʰàt tʰāj]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Th-phatthai.ogg" title="File:Th-phatthai.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Th-phatthai.ogg" title="Th-phatthai.ogg">listen</a>)</small>, "fried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_Thailand" title="Cuisine of Thailand">Thai style</a>") is a <a href="/wiki/Stir_frying" title="Stir frying">stir-fried</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_noodle" title="Rice noodle">rice noodle</a> dish commonly served as a <a href="/wiki/Street_food" title="Street food">street food</a> and at casual local eateries in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. It is made with soaked dried rice noodles, which are stir-fried with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a> and chopped firm <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">tofu</a>, and flavored with <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a> pulp, <a href="/wiki/Fish_sauce" title="Fish sauce">fish sauce</a> (<span lang="th" xml:lang="th"><i>nampla</i> น้ำปลา</span>), <a href="/wiki/Dried_shrimp" title="Dried shrimp">dried shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shallots" title="Shallots">shallots</a>, red <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili pepper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jaggery" title="Jaggery">palm sugar</a>, and served with <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">lime</a> wedges and often chopped roast <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It may also contain other vegetables like <a href="/wiki/Bean_sprout" title="Bean sprout">bean sprouts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garlic_chives" title="Garlic chives">garlic chives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a> leaves, pickled <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radishes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turnip" title="Turnip">turnips</a> (<span lang="th" xml:lang="th"><i>hua chaipo</i> หัวไชโป๊</span>), and raw <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a> flowers. It may also contain fresh shrimp, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_(food)" title="Chicken (food)">chicken</a> or other proteins. Vegetarian versions may substitute <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a> for the fish sauce and omit the shrimp.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pad_Thai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A dish of stir-fried <a href="/wiki/Rice_noodles" title="Rice noodles">rice noodles</a> is thought by some to have been introduced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_(city)" title="Ayutthaya (city)">Ayutthaya</a> during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Ayutthaya Kingdom</a> by <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese</a> traders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and subsequently altered to reflect Thai flavor profiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Others believe that the dish is of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese</a> origin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the etymology of the dish's name suggests it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finding_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-finding-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>20th century prime minister <a href="/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram" title="Plaek Phibunsongkhram">Plaek Phibunsongkhram</a> promoted pad Thai in his campaign to establish Thai nationalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Thailand was a main exporter of rice, and the government hoped to increase the amount available for export.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As prime minister of Thailand between 1938 and 1944 and from 1948 to 1957, Phibun hoped to Westernize the country. In 1939, he supported the change of name of the country from Siam to Thailand. At the time, wheat noodles were very popular in Thailand, but Plaek Phibunsongkhram sought to eliminate Chinese influence. His government promoted rice noodles and helped to establish the identity of Thailand. As a result, a new noodle named <i>sen chan</i> was created.</p>
<p>Pad Thai was made popular in Thailand during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Pad Thai has since become one of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_dish" title="National dish">national dishes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Today, some food vendors add <a href="/wiki/Pork_chop" title="Pork chop">pork-chops</a> to enhance the taste (although the original recipe did not contain pork because of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Thailand" title="Government of Thailand">government</a> perception that pork was a Chinese meat). Some food vendors still use the original recipe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_culture">Pop culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pad_Thai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pop culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Pad Thai is listed at number 5 on list of <i>World's 50 most delicious foods</i> readers' poll compiled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNN_Go" title="CNN Go">CNN Go</a> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Thai film <i>Jao saao Pad Thai</i> uses Pad Thai as a plot device as the protagonist claims she will marry whoever eats her pad Thai for 100 days in a row.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2008, in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Throwdown!_with_Bobby_Flay" title="Throwdown! with Bobby Flay">Throwdown! with Bobby Flay</a></i>, Celebrity chef <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Flay" title="Bobby Flay">Bobby Flay</a> was defeated by Chef Nongkran Daks at her restaurant, Thai Basil, in <a href="/wiki/Chantilly,_Virginia" title="Chantilly, Virginia">Chantilly, Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
