<p><b>Fernando Valenzuela Anguamea</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[ferˈnando βalenˈswela]</a></span>; born November 1, 1960), is a <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> former <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> who played seventeen seasons, from <a href="/wiki/1980_in_baseball" title="1980 in baseball">1980</a> to <a href="/wiki/1997_in_baseball" title="1997 in baseball">1997</a>, for six teams, primarily the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>. He batted and threw left-handed. He has a career won-loss record of 173–153 and a 3.54 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">earned run average</a> (ERA). Valenzuela was notable for his unorthodox windup and for being one of a small number of pitchers who threw a <a href="/wiki/Screwball" title="Screwball">screwball</a> regularly. Never a particularly hard thrower, the Dodgers felt he needed another pitch; he was taught the screwball in 1979 by teammate <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Castillo" title="Bobby Castillo">Bobby Castillo</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valenzuela was signed by the Dodgers on July 6, 1979 and debuted late in the 1980 season. In <a href="/wiki/1981_in_baseball" title="1981 in baseball">1981</a>, in what came to be called “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernandomania" title="Fernandomania">Fernandomania</a>”, Valenzuela rose from relative obscurity to achieve superstardom. He won his first eight starts (five of them shutouts). Valenzuela finished with a record of 13-7 and had a 2.48 ERA; the season was shortened by a <a href="/wiki/1981_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1981 Major League Baseball strike">player’s strike</a>. He became the first, and to date, the only player to win both <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young</a> and <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" title="Major League Baseball Rookie of the Year Award">rookie of the year</a> awards in the same season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fernando Valenzuela’s best statistical seasons were 1981 through the <a href="/wiki/1986_in_baseball" title="1986 in baseball">1986</a> seasons. He was named a <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star</a> in each season and won a major league-leading 21 games in 1986, losing a second Cy Young Award to <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scott_(baseball)" title="Mike Scott (baseball)">Mike Scott</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Valenzuela was also known as one of the better hitting pitchers of his era. He had ten career <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> and was occasionally used by Los Angeles Dodgers <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lasorda" title="Tommy Lasorda">Tommy Lasorda</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinch-hitter" title="Pinch-hitter">pinch-hitter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For the remainder of his Dodgers career, however, Valenzuela was less effective due largely to shoulder problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was on the Dodgers’ <a href="/wiki/1988_World_Series" title="1988 World Series">1988</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> championship team, but he did not play in the postseason due to his ailing shoulder. On June 29, 1990, Valenzuela pitched his only major league <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> in a 6-0 win against the visiting <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>. The no-hitter was notable for being the second one pitched that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Earlier, right-hander <a href="/wiki/Dave_Stewart_(baseball)" title="Dave Stewart (baseball)">Dave Stewart</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> no-hit the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in a 5-0 win. It is the only instance in Major League Baseball history of two no-hitters being pitched on the same day. However, after the season, Valenzuela was released by the Dodgers. The remainder of his career was spent with the former <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">California Angels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a>, and the St. Louis Cardinals.</p>
<p>Fernando Valenzuela retired after the 1997 season. In 2003, he returned to the Dodgers as a broadcaster. In 2015, he became a naturalized <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">American</a> citizen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fernando Valenzuela, the youngest of twelve children, was born in <a href="/wiki/Etchohuaquila" title="Etchohuaquila">Etchohuaquila</a>, a small town within the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Navojoa" title="Navojoa">Navojoa</a>, in the state of <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findarticles1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-findarticles1-8">[8]</a></sup> His birth date is officially listed as November 1, 1960, but during his rookie season in 1981, several commentators questioned his age, guessing him to be significantly older than twenty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findarticles1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-findarticles1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career_in_Mexico">Early career in Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career in Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1977, Valenzuela began his professional baseball career when he signed as a professional player with the <a href="/wiki/Mayos_de_Navojoa" title="Mayos de Navojoa">Mayos de Navojoa</a>, next year he was sent to the Guanajuato Tuzos of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Central_League" title="Mexican Central League">Mexican Central League</a>, posting a 5–6 record with a 2.23 ERA. The following year, the Mexican Central League was absorbed into the expanded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liga_Mexicana_de_Beisbol" title="Liga Mexicana de Beisbol">Liga Mexicana de Beisbol</a> (Mexican Baseball League), automatically elevating then 18-year-old Valenzuela to the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_(baseball)" title="Triple-A (baseball)">Triple-A level</a>. Pitching for the Leones de Yucatán (Yucatán Lions) that year, Valenzuela went 10–12 with a 2.49 ERA and 141 strikeouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseball-reference1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-reference1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of MLB teams scouted Valenzuela during this time. Los Angeles Dodgers scout Mike Brito had gone to a game in Mexico to evaluate a shortstop named Ali Uscanga. Valenzuela was pitching against Uscanga when he threw three balls and then three strikes to retire the batter. Brito said that he "forgot all about the shortstop".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-10">[10]</a></sup> The Dodgers finally gambled on the young lefty, buying out his Liga contract on July 6, 1979, for $120,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findarticles1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-findarticles1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_the_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_organization">Move to the Los Angeles Dodgers organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Move to the Los Angeles Dodgers organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After acquiring Valenzuela in the summer of 1979, the Dodgers assigned him to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodi_Dodgers" title="Lodi Dodgers">Lodi Dodgers</a> of the High-A level California League, where he posted a 1–2 record and a 1.13 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">earned run average</a> (ERA) in limited action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BRMinors_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BRMinors-11">[11]</a></sup> The Dodgers felt that Valenzuela needed to learn to throw an <a href="/wiki/Off-speed_pitch" title="Off-speed pitch">off-speed pitch</a>, so they had Dodgers pitcher <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Castillo" title="Bobby Castillo">Bobby Castillo</a> teach him to throw the <a href="/wiki/Screwball" title="Screwball">screwball</a> before the 1980 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crowe_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crowe-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1980 Valenzuela was promoted to the Double-A level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Dodgers" title="San Antonio Dodgers">San Antonio Dodgers</a>. There Valenzuela led the <a href="/wiki/Texas_League" title="Texas League">Texas League</a> with 162 strikeouts, and he earned a 13-9 win-loss record and a 3.10 ERA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TL1980_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TL1980-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valenzuela was called up to the Los Angeles Dodgers bullpen in September 1980. In the last month of the season, Valenzuela helped the Dodgers to a Western Division tie with the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>, pitching 17<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> <a href="/wiki/Shutouts_in_baseball" title="Shutouts in baseball">shutout</a> innings of relief over the course of ten games, during which he earned two wins and a save. Dodgers then lost a one-game playoff – and thus the division championship – to the Astros.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fernandomania">Fernandomania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fernandomania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valenzuela in 1981 was named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opening_day" title="Opening day">opening day</a> starter as a rookie after <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Reuss" title="Jerry Reuss">Jerry Reuss</a> was injured 24 hours before his scheduled start and <a href="/wiki/Burt_Hooton" title="Burt Hooton">Burt Hooton</a> was not ready to fill in. Valenzuela <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shut_out" title="Shut out">shut out</a> the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> 2–0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-14">[14]</a></sup> He started the season 8–0 with five shutouts and an ERA of 0.50. Punctuating this dominance on the mound, Valenzuela had an unusual, flamboyant wind-up (looking skyward just at the apex of every pitch), which drew attention of its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was a habit which he claims to have developed spontaneously, although not until joining the Dodgers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>An instant media icon, Valenzuela drew large crowds from the Los Angeles Latino community every time he pitched and triggering high demand across the country for his rookie <a href="/wiki/Baseball_card" title="Baseball card">baseball cards</a>. The craze surrounding Valenzuela came to be known as "Fernandomania."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-losangelesdodgersonline.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-losangelesdodgersonline.com-17">[17]</a></sup> During his warmup routine at Dodger Stadium, the PA system would play <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a>'s 1976 hit song <i><a href="/wiki/Fernando_(song)" title="Fernando (song)">Fernando</a></i>. He became the first player to win the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" title="Major League Baseball Rookie of the Year Award">Rookie of the Year Award</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a> in the same season. He was also the first rookie to lead the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> in strikeouts. The Dodgers won the <a href="/wiki/1981_World_Series" title="1981 World Series">World Series that season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-brown-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valenzuela was less dominant after the <a href="/wiki/1981_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1981 Major League Baseball strike">1981 player strike</a> wiped out the middle third of the season, but the left-hander still finished with a 13–7 record and a 2.48 ERA. He led all pitchers in complete games (11), shutouts (8), innings pitched (192.1) and strikeouts (180). In the post-season, Valenzuela became the youngest pitcher to start the first game of a series and pitched a complete Game 3 of the <a href="/wiki/1981_World_Series" title="1981 World Series">1981 World Series</a> against the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In total, he went 3–1 in the post-season as he helped the Dodgers to their first World Championship since 1965.</p>
<p>In addition to his skills on the mound, Valenzuela also displayed much better offensive skills than most pitchers. During his rookie season, Valenzuela batted .250 and struck out just nine times in 64 at bats, and was the recipient of the National League's <a href="/wiki/Silver_Slugger_Award" title="Silver Slugger Award">Silver Slugger Award</a> for pitchers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22El_Toro.22">"El Toro"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "El Toro"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the outstanding debut, Valenzuela, nicknamed "El Toro" (the Bull) by fans, settled down into a number of years as a workhorse starter and one of the league's best pitchers. He had one of his best seasons in 1986, when he finished 21–11 with a 3.14 ERA and led the league in wins, complete games and innings pitched. He lost a narrow vote for the Cy Young Award to the Astros' <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scott_(baseball)" title="Mike Scott (baseball)">Mike Scott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Awards86_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Awards86-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1986_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1986 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">1986 All-Star Game</a>, Valenzuela made history by striking out five consecutive American League batters, tying a record set by fellow left-handed screwballer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Hubbell" title="Carl Hubbell">Carl Hubbell</a> in the 1934 contest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubbell_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubbell-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987 his performance declined; he earned a 14–14 win-loss record with a 3.98 ERA. In 1988, a year in which the Dodgers won the <a href="/wiki/1988_World_Series" title="1988 World Series">World Series</a>, he won just five games and missed much of the season. He improved slightly in 1989 and went 10–13, and went 13–13 in 1990. He had one last great moment on June 29, 1990, when he threw a 6–0 <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> against the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> just hours after the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a>' <a href="/wiki/Dave_Stewart_(baseball)" title="Dave Stewart (baseball)">Dave Stewart</a> had thrown one against the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>. According to teammate <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scioscia" title="Mike Scioscia">Mike Scioscia</a>, Fernando and many Dodger players watched Stewart, who was a former Dodger, throw the no-hitter on TV. Afterward, before his game, Fernando said to his teammates, "You just saw a no-hitter on TV, now you will see one in person."</p>
<p>Early in his major league career, Valenzuela had trouble communicating with his catchers because he spoke very little English. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scioscia" title="Mike Scioscia">Mike Scioscia</a>, after being called up as a rookie, made the effort to learn Spanish and eventually became Valenzuela's "personal catcher" with the Dodgers before becoming the full-time catcher.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Dodgers_career">Post-Dodgers career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-Dodgers career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Valenzuela with the Angels, June 12, 1991</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After pitching ineffectively in spring training in 1991, Valenzuela was released by the Dodgers. At the time of Valenzuela's release, several Dodgers leaders, including <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lasorda" title="Tommy Lasorda">Tommy Lasorda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Claire" title="Fred Claire">Fred Claire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Malley" title="Peter O'Malley">Peter O'Malley</a>, praised Valenzuela for creating exciting memories over several seasons and they indicated that it was a difficult decision to release him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNS_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNS-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>An abortive attempt at a comeback with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Angels" title="California Angels">California Angels</a> failed later that summer. He signed with the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> in the spring of 1992, but he never played for the team, and his contract was purchased by Jalisco of the Mexican League that summer. He pitched and played some first base when he wasn't on the mound, before making another brief comeback in 1993 with the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>.</p>
<p>Jumping between the big leagues and Mexico for the next few seasons, he put together one more solid big-league season in 1996 for the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a>, going 13–8 with a 3.62 ERA. He retired a year later with a final record of 173–153 and a 3.54 ERA as a member of the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> invited him to spring training in 1999, but he declined the offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 29, 2004, Valenzuela announced he would return to the mound in the <a href="/wiki/Liga_Mexicana_del_Pac%C3%ADfico" title="Liga Mexicana del Pacífico">Liga Mexicana del Pacífico</a> (the Mexican Pacific Coast League) to play for Los <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aguilas_de_Mexicali" title="Aguilas de Mexicali">Aguilas de Mexicali</a> in October at which time he was nearly 44 years old. He pitched again that Mexican winter league, during the 2005–06 season. On December 20, 2006 in Mexicali, BC, Mexico, Fernando Valenzuela was the starting pitcher for Los Aguilas de Mexicali in the last professional game of his career.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hitting">Hitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valenzuela was considered an atypically good hitter for a pitcher. His best year at the plate was 1990, his last year with the Dodgers, when he <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">hit</a> .304 with five <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">doubles</a>, one home run, and 11 <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">RBI</a> in 69 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/At-bat" title="At-bat">at-bats</a>. That gave him a 101 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OPS%2B" title="OPS+">OPS+</a>, meaning Valenzuela ranked just above average among all National League hitters that year. In 936 career at-bats — roughly two full seasons worth of at-bats for a full-time position player — his career <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a> was .200, with 10 homers, 26 doubles, and 84 RBI. Valenzuela was even used on occasion as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinch-hitter" title="Pinch-hitter">pinch-hitter</a>, batting .368 (7-for-19) in such situations. Twice while with the Dodgers, Valenzuela was called upon to play outfield and first base in marathon extra-inning games in which he did not pitch. He won the Silver Slugger award for pitchers in 1981 and 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-losangelesdodgersonline.com_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-losangelesdodgersonline.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_retirement">After retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: After retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 2003, Valenzuela returned to the Dodgers organization as the Spanish-language radio color commentator for National League West games, joining <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Jarr%C3%ADn" title="Jaime Jarrín">Jaime Jarrín</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepe_%C3%9D%C3%B1iguez" title="Pepe Ýñiguez">Pepe Ýñiguez</a> in the Spanish-language booth. In 2015 he was switched to the color commentator job on the Spanish-language feed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SportsNet_LA" title="SportsNet LA">SportsNet LA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valenzuela also served on the coaching staff of <a href="/wiki/Mexico_national_baseball_team" title="Mexico national baseball team">Team Mexico</a> during the <a href="/wiki/2006_World_Baseball_Classic" title="2006 World Baseball Classic">2006 World Baseball Classic</a>, <a href="/wiki/2009_World_Baseball_Classic" title="2009 World Baseball Classic">2009 World Baseball Classic</a> and <a href="/wiki/2013_World_Baseball_Classic" title="2013 World Baseball Classic">2013 World Baseball Classic</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Valenzuela was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_Heritage_Baseball_Museum" title="Hispanic Heritage Baseball Museum">Hispanic Heritage Baseball Museum</a> Hall of Fame on August 23, 2003, in a pregame on the field ceremony at <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Stadium" title="Dodger Stadium">Dodger Stadium</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2005, he was named one of three starting pitchers on MLB's <a href="/wiki/Latino_Legends_Team" title="Latino Legends Team">Latino Legends Team</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legends_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legends-24">[24]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame#2013" title="Caribbean Baseball Hall of Fame">2013</a>, he was enshrined into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Caribbean Baseball Hall of Fame">Caribbean Baseball Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dodgers clubhouse manager Mitch Poole has unofficially kept Valenzuela's <a href="/wiki/Uniform_number_(Major_League_Baseball)" title="Uniform number (Major League Baseball)">jersey number</a> 34 out of circulation out of respect for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 26, 2010, ESPN broadcast a documentary commemorating Valenzuela's arrival with the Dodgers titled <i>Fernando Nation</i> as part of their <i><a href="/wiki/30_for_30" title="30 for 30">30 for 30</a></i> documentary series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Valenzuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1981, Valenzuela married Linda Burgos, a schoolteacher from Mexico. Early in his career, Valenzuela and his family spent offseasons between the Mexican cities of <a href="/wiki/Etchohuaquila" title="Etchohuaquila">Etchohuaquila</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castro_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castro-27">[27]</a></sup> The couple has four children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broadcasters_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broadcasters-28">[28]</a></sup> One of Valenzuela's sons, Fernando, Jr., played in the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> organizations as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_base" title="First base">first baseman</a>. Since 2006, he has played minor league baseball in Mexico or in independent leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JuniorBR_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-JuniorBR-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valenzuela became a U.S. Citizen on July 22, 2015 at a ceremony in Downtown Los Angeles. Participated in two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tournament_of_Roses_Parade" title="Tournament of Roses Parade">Tournament of Roses Parades</a>, in 1983 aboard the float from the Government of Mexico and in 2008 aboard the Los Angeles Dodgers' float. In 1981, Valenzuela participated in the East Los Angeles Christmas Parade as Grand Marshal.</p>
