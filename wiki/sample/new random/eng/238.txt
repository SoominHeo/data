<p><b>Globalstar</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO) <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">satellite constellation</a> for <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">satellite phone</a> and low-speed data communications, somewhat similar to the <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium satellite constellation">Iridium satellite constellation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbcomm" title="Orbcomm">Orbcomm</a> satellite systems. The Globalstar second-generation constellation will consist of 24 Low Earth Orbiting (LEO) satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-constellation_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-constellation-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Globalstar project was launched in 1991 as a joint venture of <a href="/wiki/Loral_Corporation" title="Loral Corporation">Loral Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qualcomm" title="Qualcomm">Qualcomm</a>. On March 24, 1994, the two sponsors announced formation of Globalstar LP, a <a href="/wiki/Limited_partnership" title="Limited partnership">limited partnership</a> established in the U.S., with financial participation from eight other companies, including <a href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent" title="Alcatel-Lucent">Alcatel</a>, <a href="/wiki/AirTouch" title="AirTouch">AirTouch</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deutsche_Aerospace" title="Deutsche Aerospace">Deutsche Aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyundai" title="Hyundai">Hyundai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone</a>. At that time, the company predicted the system would launch in 1998, based on an investment of $1.8 billion.</p>
<p>Globalstar received its US spectrum allocation from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a> in January 1995, and continued to negotiate with other nations for rights to use the same radio frequencies in their countries.</p>
<p>The first satellites were launched in February 1998, but system deployment was delayed due to a launch failure in September 1998 that resulted in the loss of 12 satellites in a launch by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Agency" title="Russian Space Agency">Russian Space Agency</a>. In February 2000, it launched the last of 52 satellites—48 satellites and four in-orbit spares. Another eight unlaunched satellites were maintained as ground spares.</p>
<p>The first call on the original Globalstar system was placed on November 1, 1998, from Qualcomm chairman <a href="/wiki/Irwin_M._Jacobs" title="Irwin M. Jacobs">Irwin Jacobs</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> to <a href="/wiki/Loral_Space_%26_Communications" title="Loral Space &amp; Communications">Loral Space &amp; Communications</a> CEO and chairman <a href="/wiki/Bernard_L._Schwartz" title="Bernard L. Schwartz">Bernard Schwartz</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>
<p>In October 1999, the system began "friendly user" trials with 44 of 48 planned satellites. In December 1999, the system began limited commercial service for 200 users with the full 48 satellites (no spares in orbit). In February 2000, it began full commercial service with its 48 satellites and 4 spares in North America, Europe and Brazil. Initial prices were $1.79/minute.</p>
<p>On February 15, 2002, the predecessor company Globalstar (old Globalstar) and three of its subsidiaries filed voluntary petitions under Chapter 11 of the United States Bankruptcy Code.</p>
<p>In 2004, restructuring of the old Globalstar was completed. The first stage of the restructuring was completed on December 5, 2003, when Thermo Capital Partners LLC was deemed to obtain operational control of the business, as well as certain ownership rights and risks. Thermo Capital Partners, became the principal owner.</p>
<p>Globalstar LLC was formed as a Delaware limited liability company in November 2003, and was converted into Globalstar, Inc., on March 17, 2006.</p>
<p>In 2007, Globalstar launched eight additional first-generation spare satellites into space to help compensate for the premature failure of their in-orbit satellites. Between 2010 and 2013, Globalstar launched 24 second-generation satellites in an effort to restore their system to full service.</p>
<p>Between 2010 and 2011, Globalstar moved its headquarters from Silicon Valley to <a href="/wiki/Covington,_Louisiana" title="Covington, Louisiana">Covington, Louisiana</a> in part to take advantage of the state's tax breaks and low cost of living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products_and_services">Products and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Globalstar is a provider of mobile satellite voice and data services. Globalstar offers these services to commercial and recreational users in more than 120 countries around the world.</p>
<p>The Company's products include mobile and fixed satellite telephones, simplex and duplex satellite data modems and satellite airtime packages.</p>
<p>In late 2007, Globalstar subsidiary SPOT LLC launched a handheld satellite messaging and tracking personal safety device known as the <a href="/wiki/SPOT_Satellite_Messenger" title="SPOT Satellite Messenger">SPOT Satellite Messenger</a>.</p>
<p>Many land based and maritime industries make use of the various Globalstar products and services from remote areas beyond the reach of cellular and landline telephone service.</p>
<p>Global customer segments include: oil and gas, government, mining, forestry, commercial fishing, utilities, military, transportation, heavy construction, emergency preparedness, and business continuity as well as individual recreational users.</p>
<p>Globalstar data solutions are used for a variety of asset and personal tracking, data monitoring and "Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition" or SCADA applications.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="System_architecture">System architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: System architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gateways">Gateways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gateways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Globalstar satellites are simple "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bent_pipe" title="Bent pipe">bent pipe</a>" analog <a href="/wiki/Radio_repeater" title="Radio repeater">repeaters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> unlike <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation#Satellites" title="Iridium satellite constellation">Iridium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A network of ground <a href="/wiki/Gateway_(telecommunications)" title="Gateway (telecommunications)">gateway</a> stations provides connectivity from the 40 satellites to the <a href="/wiki/Public_switched_telephone_network" title="Public switched telephone network">public switched telephone network</a> and Internet. A satellite must have a Gateway station in view to provide service to any users it may see. Twenty Four Globalstar Gateways are located around the world, including 7 in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Globalstar Gateways are the largest cellular <a href="/wiki/Base_station" title="Base station">base stations</a> in the world with a design capacity for over 10,000 concurrent phone calls over a coverage area that is roughly 50% of the size of the USA. Globalstar supports <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CDMA" title="CDMA">CDMA</a> technology such as the <a href="/wiki/Rake_receiver" title="Rake receiver">rake receiver</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_handoff" title="Soft handoff">soft handoffs</a>, so a handset may be talking via two <a href="/wiki/Spot_beam" title="Spot beam">spot beams</a> to two Gateways for path diversity.</p>
<p>Globalstar users are assigned <a href="/wiki/Telephone_number" title="Telephone number">telephone numbers</a> on the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Numbering_Plan" title="North American Numbering Plan">North American Numbering Plan</a> in North America or the appropriate <a href="/wiki/Telephone_numbering_plan" title="Telephone numbering plan">telephone numbering plan</a> for the country that the overseas gateway is located in, except for Brazil, where the official Globalstar country code (+8818) is used. The use of gateway ground stations provides customers with localized regional phone numbers for their satellite handsets. But if there are no gateway stations to cover certain remote areas (such as areas of the South Pacific and the polar regions), service cannot be provided in these remote areas, even if the satellites may fly over them. As of May 2012, voice and full-duplex data service is currently non-functional over much of Africa, the South Asian subcontinent and most mid-ocean regions due to lack of nearby gateway earth stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Globalstar system uses the Qualcomm CDMA air interface; however, the <a href="/wiki/Ericsson" title="Ericsson">Ericsson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telit" title="Telit">Telit</a> phones accept standard <a href="/wiki/GSM" title="GSM">GSM</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscriber_Identity_Module" title="Subscriber Identity Module">SIM</a> cards, while the <a href="/wiki/Qualcomm" title="Qualcomm">Qualcomm</a> GSP-1600/1700 phones do not have a SIM card interface, but use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CDMA" title="CDMA">CDMA</a>/<a href="/wiki/IS-41" title="IS-41">IS-41</a> based authentication. Therefore, the Globalstar gateways need to support both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CDMA" title="CDMA">CDMA</a>/<a href="/wiki/IS-41" title="IS-41">IS-41</a> and the GSM standards.</p>
<p>Globalstar has roaming agreements with local cellular operators in some regions, enabling the use of a single phone number in satellite and cellular mode on multi-mode Globalstar handsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These cellular roaming agreements are not in place in North America. Because of improvements in cellular phones and networks and the limitations inherent to satellite phones, the newest Globalstar handset (released in 2006) does not include cellular connectivity as Globalstar does not expect subscribers to carry it as their only mobile phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Generation_Satellites">First Generation Satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: First Generation Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Globalstar orbits have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> of 52 degrees. Therefore, Globalstar does not cover polar areas, due to the lower orbital inclination.</p>
<p>Globalstar orbits have an orbital height of approximately 1400 km and latency is still relatively low (approximately 60ms).</p>
<p>A Globalstar satellite has two body-mounted, Earth-facing arrays. First-generation Globalstars weigh approximately 550 kg, However, the second-generation Globalstar design will gain significant mass.</p>
<p>In 2005, some of the satellites began to reach the limit of their operational lifetime of 7.5 years. In December 2005, Globalstar began to move some of its satellites into a <a href="/wiki/Graveyard_orbit" title="Graveyard orbit">graveyard orbit</a> above LEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First-Generation_satellite_problems">First-Generation satellite problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: First-Generation satellite problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to documents filed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="United States Securities and Exchange Commission">SEC</a> on January 30, 2007, Globalstar's previously identified problems with its <a href="/wiki/S_band" title="S band">S-band</a> amplifiers used on its satellites for two-way communications are occurring at a rate faster than expected, possibly eventually leading to reduced levels of two-way voice and duplex data service in 2008. The company's simplex data services used to support the asset tracking products as well as the SPOT Satellite Messenger are not affected by the S-band satellite issue mentioned above. Globalstar also launched eight ground spare satellites in 2007 to help reduce the impact of the issue.</p>
<p>In the filing, Globalstar made the following statements:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Based on data recently collected from satellite operations, the Company has concluded that the degradation of the amplifiers is now occurring at a rate that is faster than previously experienced and faster than the Company had previously anticipated."</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>"Based on its most recent analysis, the Company now believes that, if the degradation of the S-band antenna amplifiers continues at the current rate or further accelerates, and if the Company is unsuccessful in developing additional technical solutions, the quality of two-way communications services will decline, and by some time in 2008 substantially all of the Company’s currently in-orbit satellites will cease to be able to support two-way communications services." <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1366868/000110465907007275/a07-3495_18k.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Industry analysts speculate the problem is caused by radiation exposure the satellites receive when they pass through the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly" title="South Atlantic Anomaly">South Atlantic Anomaly</a> in their 876-mile (1414 km) altitude orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The S-band antenna amplifier degradation does not affect adversely the Company’s one-way “Simplex” data transmission services, which utilize only the L-band uplink from a subscriber’s “Simplex” terminal to the satellites."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sec_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sec-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>"The Company is working on plans, including new products and services and pricing programs, and exploring the feasibility of accelerating procurement and launch of its second-generation satellite constellation, to attempt to reduce the effects of this problem upon its customers and operations. The Company will be able to forecast the duration of service coverage at any particular location in its service area and intends to make this information available without charge to its service providers, including its wholly owned operating subsidiaries, so that they may work with their subscribers to reduce the impact of the degradation in service quality in their respective service areas. The Company is also reviewing its business plan in light of these developments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sec_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-sec-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>"The Company’s liquidity remains strong. At December 31, 2006, in addition to its credit agreement, the Company had unrestricted cash on hand and undrawn amounts under the Thermo Funding Company irrevocable standby stock purchase agreement of approximately $195 million."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sec_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-sec-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Globalstar.27s_interim_solution">Globalstar's interim solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Globalstar's interim solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2007, Globalstar launched eight spare satellites for its existing constellation with a view to reducing the gaps in its two-way voice and data services pending commercial availability of its second-generation satellite constellation, scheduled for initial launch in the second half of 2009. Globalstar will continue to operate its existing satellite constellation to provide and support services until the second-generation Globalstar satellites are available for service.</p>
<p>Until the new second-generation Globalstar satellite constellation is operational, Globalstar is offering its Optimum Satellite Availability Tool (OSAT) on its Internet site, which subscribers may use to predict when one or more unaffected satellites will be overhead at any specific geographic location.</p>
<p>To help customers deal with the S-band communication issues, Globalstar has provided a <a class="external text" href="http://calltimes.globalstar.com/" rel="nofollow">call times tool</a> which calculates optimum calling times for a given location.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-generation_satellites">Second-generation satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Second-generation satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2006, Globalstar announced that Alcatel Alenia Space, now <a href="/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space" title="Thales Alenia Space">Thales Alenia Space</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Mandelieu_Space_Center" title="Cannes Mandelieu Space Center">Cannes headquarters</a>, has been awarded a €661 million contract for the second-generation constellation. The satellites were designed with a life expectancy of 15 years, significantly longer than the design life of Globalstar’s first-generation constellation. The second generation constellation will consist of 24 satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-constellation_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-constellation-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, Globalstar announced on April 3, 2007 that it has signed a €9 million agreement with Thales Alenia Space to upgrade the Globalstar satellite constellation, including necessary hardware and software upgrades to Globalstar’s satellite control network facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2008, Thales Alenia Space began production assembly, integration and testing of the second-generation flight model satellites, in its <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> factory, for launch as early as Q3 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2009, Globalstar, Inc. announced that it has received complete financing for its second-generation satellite constellation and signed an amendment to the initial contract, specifying in particular the adjusted conditions for production and the new satellite delivery timetable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first six second-generation satellites were launched on October 19, 2010, from the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> in Kazakhstan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The launch used a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz-2</a> launch vehicle with a <a href="/wiki/Fregat" title="Fregat">Fregat</a> upper stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These second-generation satellites are expected to provide Globalstar customers with satellite voice and data services until at least 2025. Six more second-generation satellites were launched in July 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> followed by another six satellites in December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As of July 16, 2012, 18 of 24 new satellites had been launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Launch of the second generation constellation was completed February 6, 2013, with the launch of the final six satellites using a Soyuz 2-1a launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The 24 second-generation spacecraft weighed approximately 700 kg (1,500 lb) each at launch, and are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-axis_stabilized_spacecraft" title="3-axis stabilized spacecraft">3-axis stabilized</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_operations">Business operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Business operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_structure_and_financing">Corporate structure and financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Corporate structure and financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Predecessor Company - Globalstar LP</b>. In February 1995, Globalstar Telecommunications Ltd. raised $200 million from its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> market. The IPO price of $20 per share was equivalent to $5 per share after two stock splits. The stock price peaked at (post split) $50 per share in January 2000, but institutional investors began predicting bankruptcy as early as June 2000. The stock price eventually fell below $1 per share, and the stock was delisted by NASDAQ in June 2001.</p>
<p>After the IPO, the publicly traded Globalstar Telecommunications (NASDAQ symbol GSTRF) owned part of system operator Globalstar LP. From that point on, the primary financing for Globalstar LP was vendor financing from its suppliers (including Loral and Qualcomm), supplemented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junk_bonds" title="Junk bonds">junk bonds</a>.</p>
<p>After a total debt and equity investment of $4.3 billion, on February 15, 2002, Globalstar Telecommunications filed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11" title="Chapter 11">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy protection, listing assets of $570 million and liabilities of $3.3 billion. The assets were later bought for $43 million by Thermo Capital Partners LLC.</p>
<p>Globalstar LLC and Globalstar, Inc. When the new Globalstar emerged from bankruptcy in April 2004, it was owned by Thermo Capital Partners (81.25%) and the original creditors of Globalstar L.P. (18.75%). Globalstar LLC was incorporated in April 2006 to become Globalstar, Inc.</p>
<p>Globalstar, Inc. completed an IPO in November 2006. The stock currently trades on the NASDAQ Global Select Market under the symbol GSAT.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SPOT_LLC">SPOT LLC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: SPOT LLC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2007, Globalstar announced the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/SPOT_Satellite_Messenger" title="SPOT Satellite Messenger">SPOT Satellite Messenger</a> product, to be marketed through its latest subsidiary SPOT, Inc., later named SPOT LLC. The SPOT Messenger is manufactured by Globalstar partner Axonn LLC and combines the company's simplex data technology with a Nemerix GPS chipset. SPOT is intended to leverage Globalstar's still adequate L-Band uplink, which is used by simplex modems. The product was launched early November 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Employees">Employees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globalstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Employees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first five employees of Globalstar were transferred from the founding companies in 1991. Although few figures were publicly disclosed, the company apparently reached a peak of about 350 employees until layoffs in March 2001. However, this figure was misleading, as most of the development, operations and sales employees were employed by the company’s strategic partners.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Loral's <a href="/wiki/Chairman" title="Chairman">chairman</a> Bernard Schwartz served as Globalstar’s chairman and CEO until May 2001.</p>
<p>The company then appointed satellite telecommunications veteran Olof Lundberg to lead a turnaround at the company to serve as chairman and CEO. After beginning his career with Swedish Telecom, Lundberg had been founding Director General (later CEO) of <a href="/wiki/Inmarsat" title="Inmarsat">Inmarsat</a> from 1979 to 1995. He served as founding CEO and later CEO and Chairman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICO_Global_Communications" title="ICO Global Communications">ICO Global Communications</a> from 1995 to 1999.</p>
<p>Lundberg resigned from the company (then in bankruptcy) on June 30, 2003.</p>
