<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Five-star rank</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>five-star rank</b> is a very senior military rank, first established in the United States in 1944, with a five-star insignia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and corresponding ranks in other countries. The rank is that of the most senior operational military commanders, and within <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>'s "standard rank scale" it is designated by <a href="/wiki/Ranks_and_insignia_of_NATO" title="Ranks and insignia of NATO">the code OF-10</a>.</p>
<p>Not all armed forces have such a rank, and in those that do the actual insignia of the "five-star ranks" may not contain five stars. For example: the insignia for the French OF-10 rank <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" title="Marshal of France">maréchal de France</a></i> contains 7 stars; the insignia for the Portuguese <i>marechal</i> contains four gold stars; and many of the insignia of the ranks in <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> contain no stars at all.</p>
<p>Typically, five-star officers hold the rank of <a href="/wiki/General_of_the_army" title="General of the army">general of the army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_fleet" title="Admiral of the fleet">admiral of the fleet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_marshal" title="Field marshal">field marshal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_the_air_force" title="Marshal of the air force">marshal of the air force</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_air_force" title="General of the air force">general of the air force</a>, and several other similarly named ranks. Five-star ranks are extremely senior - usually the highest ranks. As an active rank, the position exists only in a minority of countries and is usually held by only a very few officers during <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">wartime</a>. In times of peace, it is usually held only as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_rank" title="Honorary rank">honorary rank</a>.</p>
<p>Despite the rarity and seniority of five-star officers, even more senior ranks have been adopted in the United States, namely, <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Navy_(United_States)" title="Admiral of the Navy (United States)">admiral of the navy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_armies" title="General of the armies">general of the armies</a>. Other names for highly senior ranks from the twentieth century include <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo"><i>généralissime</i> (France)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo"><i>generalisimo</i> (Spain)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Generalissimus_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Generalissimus of the Soviet Union"><i>generalissimus</i> (USSR)</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_five-star_ranks">Australian five-star ranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five-star_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Australian five-star ranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Fleet_(Royal_Australian_Navy)" title="Admiral of the Fleet (Royal Australian Navy)">Admiral of the fleet</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oz5starInsig_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oz5starInsig-2">[nb 1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal_(Australia)" title="Field Marshal (Australia)">Field marshal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oz5starInsig_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oz5starInsig-2">[nb 1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_the_Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Marshal of the Royal Australian Air Force">Marshal of the Royal Australian Air Force</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oz5starInsig_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oz5starInsig-2">[nb 1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Only one Australian born officer (<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Blamey" title="Thomas Blamey">Sir Thomas Blamey</a>) has held a substantive Australian five-star rank (field marshal).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">HM King George VI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">HRH Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a> have held all three Australian five-star ranks in an honorary capacity, and have been the only holders of the Australian ranks of admiral of the fleet and marshal of the RAAF.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Thailand" title="Monarchy of Thailand">monarch of Thailand</a> is appointed to the three ranks automatically upon accession as he is the constitutional <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Royal_Thai_Armed_Forces" title="Head of the Royal Thai Armed Forces">Head of the Royal Thai Armed Forces</a>. <p>Promotion to the ranks of admiral of the fleet and marshal of the Royal Air Force is now generally held in abeyance in peacetime with exceptions for special circumstances. Promotion to the rank of field marshal was generally stopped in 1995 as a cost-cutting measure but is still made in some cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heathcote_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-heathcote-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc20120615_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc20120615-7">[5]</a></sup> The most recent appointments to five-star ranks are the promotions in 2012 of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">The Prince of Wales</a> to honorary five-star rank in all three services, and of former Chief of the Defence Staff <a href="/wiki/Charles_Guthrie,_Baron_Guthrie_of_Craigiebank" title="Charles Guthrie, Baron Guthrie of Craigiebank">Lord Guthrie of Craigiebank</a> to the honorary rank of field marshal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc20120615_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc20120615-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monarchy20120616_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-monarchy20120616-8">[6]</a></sup> In 2014 the former Chief of the Defence Staff <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jock_Stirrup,_Baron_Stirrup" title="Jock Stirrup, Baron Stirrup">Lord Stirrup</a> was promoted to the honorary rank of marshal of the RAF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and (later) serving <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, a small number of British five-star commanders have held the additional title <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Allied_Commander" title="Supreme Allied Commander">Supreme Allied Commander</a>, given operational control over all air, land, and sea units led by the <a href="/wiki/Four-star_rank" title="Four-star rank">four-star commanders</a> of multi-national forces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_five-star_ranks">US five-star ranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five-star_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: US five-star ranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_Admiral_(U.S.)" title="Fleet Admiral (U.S.)">Fleet admiral</a> (held by four officers)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/General_of_the_Army_(United_States)" title="General of the Army (United States)">General of the Army</a> (held by five officers)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/General_of_the_Air_Force" title="General of the Air Force">General of the Air Force</a> (held by one officer)</li>
</ul>
<p>Before the five-star ranks were established in 1944, two officers had previously been promoted from their four-star ranks to the superior and unique ranks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Navy" title="Admiral of the Navy">Admiral of the Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_of_the_Armies" title="General of the Armies">General of the Armies</a>: Admiral <a href="/wiki/George_Dewey" title="George Dewey">George Dewey</a> (appointment 1903 retroactive to 1899, died 1917) and General <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">John J. Pershing</a> (appointed 1919, died 1948). In 1944 the Navy and Army specified that these officers were considered senior to any officers promoted to the five-star ranks within their services (but it was not clear if they were senior by rank or by seniority due to an earlier date of rank).</p>
<p>Five-star ranks were created in the US military during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> because of the awkward situation created when some American senior commanders were placed in positions commanding allied officers of higher rank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor2009p283_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-taylor2009p283-10">[8]</a></sup> US officers holding five-star rank never retire; they draw full active duty pay for life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> The five-star ranks were retired in 1981 on the death of General of the Army <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bradley" title="Omar Bradley">Omar Bradley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor2009p283_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-taylor2009p283-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nine Americans have been promoted to five-star rank, one of them, <a href="/wiki/Henry_H._Arnold" title="Henry H. Arnold">Henry H. Arnold</a>, in two services (US Army then later in the US Air Force). As part of the bicentennial celebration, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> was, 177 years after his death, permanently made senior to all other US generals/admirals, with the title <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_armies" title="General of the armies">general of the armies</a></i>, effective on 4 July 1976. The appointment stated he was to have "rank and precedence over all other grades of the Army, past or present".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[nb 3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and (later) serving <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, a small number of American five-star commanders have held the additional title <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Allied_Commander" title="Supreme Allied Commander">Supreme Allied Commander</a>, given operational control over all air, land, and sea units led by the <a href="/wiki/Four-star_rank" title="Four-star rank">four-star commanders</a> of multi-national forces.</p>

<p><span class="reference-text">The timing of the first seven appointments was to establish both a clear order of seniority and a near-equivalence between the Army and Navy services. In 1949, Arnold was honored by being made the first, and to date only, general of the air force. He is the only American to serve in a five-star rank in two of its military services. By a Congressional Act of 24 March 1903, Admiral <a href="/wiki/George_Dewey" title="George Dewey">George Dewey</a>'s rank was established as <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Navy_(United_States)" title="Admiral of the Navy (United States)">admiral of the navy</a>, a rank which was specified to be senior to the four-star rank of admiral and was equal to <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Fleet_(Royal_Navy)" title="Admiral of the Fleet (Royal Navy)">admiral of the fleet</a> in the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. Admiral Dewey was the only individual ever appointed to this rank, which lapsed with his death on 16 January 1917. Admiral of the navy was considered superior to fleet admiral during World War II. On 3 September 1919, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Pershing" title="John Pershing">John Pershing</a> was promoted to the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_armies" title="General of the armies">general of the armies</a> (officially general of the armies of the United States) in recognition of his service during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. He is the only person promoted to this rank during their lifetime.</span></p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="reference-text"><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_a"><b><a href="#ref_a">^a</a></b></span> During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bicentennial" title="United States Bicentennial">United States Bicentennial</a> year, George Washington was posthumously appointed to the grade of General of the Armies of the United States by the congressional joint resolution <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Public_Law_94-479" title="s:Public Law 94-479">Public Law 94-479</a> passed on 19 January 1976, with an effective appointment date of 4 July 1976 but having rank and precedence over all other grades of the Army, past or present. This restored Washington's position as the most senior US military officer. Between the joint resolution concerning Washington's rank, the fact that Omar Bradley was still alive, and thus still considered to be on active duty, and statements made and actions taken during and after World War II about the relationship between General of the Armies and General of the Army, it appears General of the Armies is superior in rank to General of the Army.</span></dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ol>
