<p><b>Wörthersee Stadion</b> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:W%C3%B6rthersee_Stadion.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/W%C3%B6rthersee_Stadion.ogg" title="Wörthersee Stadion.ogg">Wörtherseestadion</a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:W%C3%B6rthersee_Stadion.ogg" title="File:Wörthersee Stadion.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Multi-purpose_stadium" title="Multi-purpose stadium">multi-purpose stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. It is the home ground of <a href="/wiki/SK_Austria_Klagenfurt_(2007)" title="SK Austria Klagenfurt (2007)">Austria Klagenfurt</a>. The stadium is situated within the Sportpark Klagenfurt campus of several other sports venues. Its name refers to the nearby <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rthersee" title="Wörthersee">Wörthersee</a> lake.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%B6rthersee_Stadion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The first Wörtherseestadion was built in 1960 as the home ground of the old SK Austria Klagenfurt (the later defunct <a href="/wiki/FC_K%C3%A4rnten" title="FC Kärnten">FC Kärnten</a>), a track and field stadium with a capacity of 10,900. In 1962 the team was first promoted to the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Football_Bundesliga" title="Austrian Football Bundesliga">Austrian Football Bundesliga</a> (then called <i>A-Liga</i>) and until their final relegation in 1989 regularly attracted thousands of spectators. A second attempt by the FC Kärnten to enter the Bundesliga, backed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">Carinthian</a> governor <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rg_Haider" title="Jörg Haider">Jörg Haider</a>, was successful in 2001. The team was again relegated in 2004, nevertheless in view of the coming <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2008" title="UEFA Euro 2008">UEFA Euro 2008</a> the Carinthian government resolved upon the reconstruction of the stadium. It was demolished from 2005 and replaced by the larger Hypo-Arena, named after the sponsoring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypo_Alpe-Adria-Bank_International" title="Hypo Alpe-Adria-Bank International">Hypo Alpe-Adria-Bank International</a>. Football matches were temporarily staged at the <a href="/wiki/Sportzentrum_Fischl" title="Sportzentrum Fischl">Sportzentrum Fischl</a> grounds.</p>
<p>The Hypo-Arena was one of eight stadiums hosting the 2008 European Football Championship, for which it was built to hold 32,000. It was considered whether the stadium should be reduced to the capacity of 22,000 after the event. The official opening was on 7 September 2007 and hosted a friendly between <a href="/wiki/Austria_national_football_team" title="Austria national football team">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan_national_football_team" title="Japan national football team">Japan</a> in front of 26,500 spectators. To ensure an economical percentage of seats sold, Haider had contrived the relocation of the Bundesliga team <a href="/wiki/ASK%C3%96_Pasching" title="ASKÖ Pasching">ASKÖ Pasching</a> to Klagenfurt, where, from the 2007-08 season, it played under the name <a href="/wiki/SK_Austria_K%C3%A4rnten" title="SK Austria Kärnten">SK Austria Kärnten</a> until its bankruptcy in 2010.</p>
<p>On 16 September 2009 the Carinthian government and Austrian Sport Minister <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Darabos" title="Norbert Darabos">Norbert Darabos</a> agreed to basically maintain the stadium's capacity. After the fitout it accommodates 18,000 visitors watching the Austrian Football League and 30,000 attending international matches and other major events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The lower tier of the South Stand is fitted with rail seats for <a href="/wiki/Safe_standing" title="Safe standing">safe standing</a>. Upon the turbulences around the Hypo Group Alpe Adria, the stadium was renamed Wörtherseestadion in 2010.</p>
<p>From August 2015 till January 2016 the upper stands were closed because of a court decision, which reduced the capacity to 12,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
