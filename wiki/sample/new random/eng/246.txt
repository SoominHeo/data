<p>The <b>Mannaeans</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span> (country name usually <b>Mannea</b>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a>: <i>Mannai</i>, possibly Biblical <i>Minni</i>, מנּי) were an ancient people who lived in the territory of present-day northwestern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Urmia" title="Lake Urmia">lake Urmia</a>, around the 10th to 7th centuries BC. At that time they were neighbors of the empires of <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartu</a>, as well as other small buffer states between the two, such as <a href="/wiki/Musasir" title="Musasir">Musasir</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zikirta" title="Zikirta">Zikirta</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (Jeremiah 51:27) the Mannaeans are called <b>Minni</b>. In the Jewish Encyclopedia (1906), <i>Minni</i> is identified with <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jenc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jenc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosen_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosen-2">[2]</a></sup> but it could refer to one of the provinces in ancient Armenia; Minni, <a href="/wiki/Ararat_Province" title="Ararat Province">Ararat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashkenaz" title="Ashkenaz">Ashkenaz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asiaminor_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-asiaminor-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to examinations of the place and personal names found in Assyrian and <a href="/wiki/Urartian_language" title="Urartian language">Urartian</a> texts, the Mannaeans, or at least their rulers, spoke <a href="/wiki/Hurrian_language" title="Hurrian language">Hurrian</a>, a non-Semitic and non-Indo-European language related to Urartian, with no modern language connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frye_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-frye-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannaeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their kingdom was situated east and south of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Urmia" title="Lake Urmia">Lake Urmia</a>, roughly centered around the Urmia plain in this part of what is today named "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_Azerbaijan" title="Iranian Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan region of Iran</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Excavations that began in 1956 succeeded in uncovering the fortified city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hasanlu" title="Hasanlu">Hasanlu</a>, once thought to be a potential Mannaean site. More recently, the site of <a href="/wiki/Qalaichi" title="Qalaichi">Qalaichi</a> (possibly ancient Izirtu/Zirta) has been linked to the Mannaeans based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stela" title="Stela">stela</a> with this toponym found at the site.</p>
<p>After suffering several defeats at the hands of both <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythians</a> and Assyrians, the remnants of the Mannaean populace were absorbed by an <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranians" title="Indo-Iranians">Iranian</a> people known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matieni" title="Matieni">Matieni</a> and the area became known as <a href="/wiki/Matiene" title="Matiene">Matiene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It was then annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Medes</a> in about 609 BC.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannaeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mannaean kingdom began to flourish around 850 BC. The Mannaeans were mainly a settled people, practicing irrigation and breeding cattle and horses. The capital was another fortified city, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Izirtu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Izirtu (page does not exist)">Izirtu</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zirta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zirta (page does not exist)">Zirta</a>).</p>
<p>By the 820s BC they had expanded to become the first large state to occupy this region since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gutians" title="Gutians">Gutians</a>, later followed by the unrelated <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> peoples, the <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Medes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persians</a>. By this time they had a prominent aristocracy as a ruling class, which somewhat limited the power of the king.</p>
<p>Beginning around 800 BC, the region became contested ground between <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartu</a>, who built several forts on the territory of Mannae, and <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>. During open conflict between the two, ca. 750-730 BC, Mannae seized the opportunity to enlarge its holdings. The Mannaean kingdom reached the pinnacle of its power during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Iranzu" title="Iranzu">Iranzu</a> (ca. 725-720 BC).</p>
<p>In 716 BC, king <a href="/wiki/Sargon_II" title="Sargon II">Sargon II</a> of Assyria moved against Mannae, where the ruler <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aza_of_Mannae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aza of Mannae (page does not exist)">Aza</a>, son of Iranzu, had been deposed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ullusunu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ullusunu (page does not exist)">Ullusunu</a> with the help of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urartians" title="Urartians">Urartians</a>. Sargon took <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Izirtu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Izirtu (page does not exist)">Izirtu</a>, and stationed troops in <a href="/wiki/Parsua" title="Parsua">Parsua</a> (Parsua was distinct from Parsumash located further southeast in what is today known as Fars province in Iran<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>). The Assyrians thereafter used the area to breed, train and trade horses.</p>
<p>According to one Assyrian inscription, the <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">Cimmerians</a> (<i>Gimirru</i>) originally went forth from their homeland of <i>Gamir</i> or <i>Uishdish</i> on the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> in "the midst of Mannai" around this time. The Cimmerians first appear in the annals in the year 714 BC, when they apparently helped the Assyrians to defeat Urartu. Urartu chose to submit to the Assyrians, and together the two defeated the Cimmerians and thus kept them out of the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a>. At any rate, the Cimmerians had again rebelled against Sargon by 705, and he was killed whilst driving them out. By 679 they had instead migrated to the east and west of Mannae.</p>
<p>The Mannaeans are recorded as rebelling against <a href="/wiki/Esarhaddon" title="Esarhaddon">Esarhaddon</a> of Assyria in 676 BC, when they attempted to interrupt the horse trade between <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a> and its colony of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parsuash" title="Parsuash">Parsuash</a>.</p>
<p>The king <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ahsheri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ahsheri (page does not exist)">Ahsheri</a>, who ruled until the 650s BC, continued to enlarge the territory of Mannae, although paying tribute to Assyria. However, Mannae suffered a crushing defeat at the hands of the Assyrians around 660 BC, and subsequently an internal revolt broke out, continuing until Ahsheri's death. Also in the 7th century BC, Mannae was defeated by the advancing <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythians</a>, who had already raided Urartu and been repelled by the Assyrians. Somewhat later (585 BC) destroying Mannae. This defeat contributed to the further break-up of the Mannaean kingdom.</p>
<p>King Ahsheri's successor, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ualli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ualli (page does not exist)">Ualli</a>, as a vassal of Assyria, took the side of the Assyrians against the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Medes</a> (<i>Madai</i>), who were at this point still based to the east along the southwest shore of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> and revolting against Assyrian domination. The Medes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persians</a> were subjugated by Assyria. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo_Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo Assyrian Empire">Neo Assyrian Empire</a> which had dominated the region for three hundred years began to unravel, consumed by civil war after the death of <a href="/wiki/Ashurbanipal" title="Ashurbanipal">Ashurbanipal</a> in 627 BC. The upheavals in Assyria allowed the Medes to free themselves from Assyrian vassalage and make themselves the major power in ancient Iran at the expense of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persians</a>, Mannaeans and the remnants of the indigenous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elamites" title="Elamites">Elamites</a> whose kingdom had been destroyed by the Assyrians. The Mede kingdom conquered the remnants of Mannae in 616 BC and absorbed the populace.</p>
