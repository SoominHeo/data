<p>The <b>Rafflesiaceae</b> are a family of rare parasitic plants found in the tropical forests of east and southeast Asia, including <i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia_arnoldii" title="Rafflesia arnoldii">Rafflesia arnoldii</a></i>, which has the largest flowers of all plants. The plants are <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">endoparasites</a> of vines in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Tetrastigma" title="Tetrastigma">Tetrastigma</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Vitaceae" title="Vitaceae">Vitaceae</a>) and lack stems, leaves, roots, and any photosynthetic tissue. They rely entirely on their host plants for both water and nutrients, and only the emerge as flowers from the roots or lower stems of the host plants.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flowers">Flowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rafflesiaceae flowers mimic rotting carcasses in scent, color, and texture to attract their pollinators, carrion flies. For this reason, some flowers of the family <i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia" title="Rafflesia">Rafflesia</a></i> are nicknamed "corpse flowers". Most members of Rafflesiaceae possess a large, bowl-shaped floral chamber formed by a <a href="/wiki/Perianth" title="Perianth">perianth</a> tube and a diaphragm. This diaphragm is the opening for carrion fly pollinators and is surrounded by attractive sterile organs. Flowers are generally <a href="/wiki/Gonochorism" title="Gonochorism">unisexual</a>, and can range from tens of cm to over a meter large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Past taxonomic works have varied as to the classification of Rafflesiaceae. The classification of Rafflesiaceae has been somewhat problematic due to their highly reduced vegetative parts, modified reproductive structures, and anomalous molecular evolution (Davis 2008). Rafflesiaceae lacks <i>rbcL</i> and other <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastid</a> genes commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> inference in green plants. In fact, Molina et al. (2014) found that a genus of <i>Rafflesia</i> is the first parasitic plant studied containing no recognizable remnants of the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> genome.</p>
<p>Most traditional classifications that were based entirely on morphological features considered Rafflesiaceae <i><a href="/wiki/Sensu" title="Sensu">sensu lato</a></i> (in the broad sense) to include nine genera, but the heterogeneity among these genera caused early workers, such as Harms (1935), to recognize four distinct groups that were then classified as tribes (still within Rafflesiaceae). This tribal system was followed by Takhtajan et al. (1985).</p>
<p>The first molecular phylogenetic study (using DNA sequences) that showed two of these tribes were not related was by Barkman et al. (2004). This study showed three genera (corresponding to tribe Rafflesieae, that is, <i>Rafflesia</i>, <i>Rhizanthes</i>, and <i>Sapria</i>) were components of the eudicot order Malpighiales. The genus <i>Mitrastema</i> (tribe Mitrastemeae) was shown to be unrelated and a member of the order Ericales. Later that year, Nickrent et al. (2004), using additional molecular data, confirmed the placements by Barkman et al. (2004) and also examined the positions of the two other tribes, Cytineae (<i>Bdallophyton</i> and <i>Cytinus</i>) and Apodantheae (<i>Apodanthes</i>, <i>Berlinianche</i>,and <i>Pilostyles</i>). Nickrent et al. (2004) showed Cytineae was related to Malvales and Apodantheae to either Malvales or Cucurbitales. Apodantheae has since been confirmed to be in the Cucurbitales (Filipowicz and Renner 2010).</p>
<p>Thus, the group traditionally classified as a single family, Rafflesiaceae, was actually composed of at least four distinct and very distantly related clades, with their similarities due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a> under their common parasitic lifestyle. A goal of taxonomy is to classify together only plants that all share a common ancestor, i.e., are monophyletic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Families">Families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thus, the original Rafflesiaceae <i>sensu lato</i> is currently split into four families:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APweb_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-APweb-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Rafflesiaceae (<i><a href="/wiki/Sensu" title="Sensu">sensu stricto</a></i>): <i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia" title="Rafflesia">Rafflesia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizanthes" title="Rhizanthes">Rhizanthes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sapria" title="Sapria">Sapria</a></i>Â â order Malpighiales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mitrastemon" title="Mitrastemon">Mitrastemonaceae</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Mitrastemon" title="Mitrastemon">Mitrastema</a></i>Â â order Ericales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cytinaceae" title="Cytinaceae">Cytinaceae</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Bdallophytum" title="Bdallophytum">Bdallophyton</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cytinus" title="Cytinus">Cytinus</a></i>Â â order Malvales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Apodanthaceae" title="Apodanthaceae">Apodanthaceae</a>: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Apodanthes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Apodanthes (page does not exist)">Apodanthes</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Berlinianche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Berlinianche (page does not exist)">Berlinianche</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pilostyles" title="Pilostyles">Pilostyles</a></i>Â â order Cucurbitales</li>
</ul>
<p>These four families can be easily distinguished by floral and inflorescence features:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rafflesiaceae: inferior ovary, large flowers occurring singly</li>
<li>Mitrastemonaceae: superior ovary, flowers occurring singly</li>
<li>Cytinaceae: inferior ovary, flowers in inflorescences</li>
<li>Apodanthaceae: inferior ovary, small flowers occurring singly (but arising in clusters from host bark)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogenetic_analysis">Phylogenetic analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phylogenetic analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="720" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg/200px-Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg/300px-Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg/400px-Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rafflesia_euphorb_tree.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
New hypothesis of Rafflesiaceae derived from within Euphorbiaceae. Rafflesiaceae in red, Euphorbiaceae in black (redrawn from Davis et al., 2007).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early work on higher-level relationships was able to place Rafflesiaceae (in the strict sense) within the order <a href="/wiki/Malpighiales" title="Malpighiales">Malpighiales</a>, but was not able to resolve the closest ancestor within the order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A more recent phylogenetic analysis found strong support for Rafflesiaceae being derived from within <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a>, which is surprising as members of that family typically have very small flowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to their analysis, the rate of flower size evolution was more or less constant throughout the family, except at the origin of RafflesiaceaeÂ â a period of about 46 million years between when the group split from the higher Euphorbiaceae, and when the existing Rafflesiaceae split from each otherÂ â where the flowers rapidly evolved to become much larger before reverting to the slower rate of change. Given that this hypothesis is confirmed, in order to maintain <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a> of Euphorbiaceae, either the basal clade (represented by <i><a href="/wiki/Pogonophora_(plant)" title="Pogonophora (plant)">Pogonophora</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pera_(plant)" title="Pera (plant)">Pera</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Clutia" title="Clutia">Clutia</a></i> in the tree) has to be split off as the separate family <a href="/wiki/Peraceae" title="Peraceae">Peraceae</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm_Phylogeny_Website" title="Angiosperm Phylogeny Website">Angiosperm Phylogeny Website</a> does,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APweb_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-APweb-3">[3]</a></sup> or Rafflesiaceae would have to be included in the Euphorbiaceae.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_gene_transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafflesiaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Horizontal gene transfer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial genes</a> in the Rafflesiaceae appear to have come from their hosts (<a href="/wiki/Tetrastigma" title="Tetrastigma">Tetrastigma</a>). Because the hosts are not closely related to the parasites (as shown by <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">molecular phylogeny</a> results for other parts of the genome), this is believed to be the result of <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Especially high rates of HGT have been found to take place in Rafflesiaceae mitochondrial genes when compared to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_gene" title="Nuclear gene">nuclear genes</a> and to HGT in <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophic</a> plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
