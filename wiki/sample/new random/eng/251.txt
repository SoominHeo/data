<p><b>USS <i>Wasp</i> (CV-7)</b> was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> commissioned in 1940 and lost in action in 1942. She was the eighth ship named <a href="/wiki/USS_Wasp" title="USS Wasp">USS <i>Wasp</i></a>, and the sole ship of a class built to use up the remaining tonnage allowed to the U.S. for aircraft carriers under the treaties of the time. As a reduced-size version of the <a href="/wiki/Yorktown-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Yorktown-class aircraft carrier"><i>Yorktown</i>-class aircraft carrier</a> hull, <i>Wasp</i> was more vulnerable than other United States aircraft carriers available at the opening of hostilities. <i>Wasp</i> was initially employed in the Atlantic campaign, where Axis naval forces were perceived as less capable of inflicting decisive damage. After supporting the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Iceland#United_States_occupation_force" title="Invasion of Iceland">occupation of Iceland</a> in 1941, <i>Wasp</i> joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Home_Fleet" title="British Home Fleet">British Home Fleet</a> in April 1942 and twice ferried British fighter aircraft to <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>. <i>Wasp</i> was then transferred to the Pacific in June 1942 to replace losses at the battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Coral_Sea" title="Battle of the Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Midway</a>. After supporting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Guadalcanal" title="Invasion of Guadalcanal">invasion of Guadalcanal</a>, <i>Wasp</i> was sunk by the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_submarine_I-19" title="Japanese submarine I-19"><i>I-19</i></a> on 15 September 1942.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Wasp</i> was a product of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Treaty" title="Washington Naval Treaty">Washington Naval Treaty</a>. After the construction of the carriers <a href="/wiki/USS_Yorktown_(CV-5)" title="USS Yorktown (CV-5)"><i>Yorktown</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Enterprise_(CV-6)" title="USS Enterprise (CV-6)"><i>Enterprise</i></a>, the U.S. was still permitted 15,000 long tons (15,000 t) to build a carrier.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Wasp_(CV-7)_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1686" data-file-width="2121" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg/220px-USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg/330px-USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg/440px-USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USS_Wasp_(CV-7)_deck_edge_elevator_with_SB2U_1940.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Wasp</i> was the first carrier fitted with a deck edge elevator.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Navy sought to squeeze a large air group onto a ship with nearly 25% less displacement than the <i>Yorktown</i>-class. In order to save weight and space, <i>Wasp</i> was constructed with low-power machinery (compare <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 75,000 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower#Shaft_horsepower" title="Horsepower">shp</a> (56,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>) machinery with <i>Yorktown</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 120,000 shp (89,000 kW), <a href="/wiki/Essex-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Essex-class aircraft carrier"><i>Essex</i>-class</a>'s 150,000 shp (110,000 kW), and the <a href="/wiki/Independence-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Independence-class aircraft carrier"><i>Independence</i>-class</a>'s 100,000 shp (75,000 kW)).</p>
<p>Additionally, <i>Wasp</i> was launched with almost no armor, modest speed and, more significantly, no protection from torpedoes. Absence of side protection of the boilers and internal aviation fuel stores "doomed her to a blazing demise". These were inherent design flaws that were recognized when constructed but could not be remedied within the allowed tonnage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These flaws, combined with a relative lack of damage control experience in the early days of the war, were to prove fatal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> was the first carrier fitted with a deck edge elevator. The elevator consisted of a platform for the front wheels and an outrigger for the tail wheel. The two arms on the sides moved the platform in a half-circle up and down between the flight deck and the hangar deck.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_history">Service history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Service history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inter-war_period">Inter-war period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inter-war period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>She was laid down on 1 April 1936 at the <a href="/wiki/Fore_River_Shipyard" title="Fore River Shipyard">Fore River Shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quincy,_Massachusetts" title="Quincy, Massachusetts">Quincy, Massachusetts</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">launched</a> on 4 April 1939, sponsored by Carolyn Edison (wife of Assistant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_the_Navy" title="Secretary of the Navy">Secretary of the Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edison" title="Charles Edison">Charles Edison</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> on 25 April 1940 at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Quartermaster_Base&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Army Quartermaster Base (page does not exist)">Army Quartermaster Base</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Boston,_Massachusetts" title="South Boston, Massachusetts">South Boston, Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">Captain</a> <a href="/wiki/John_W._Reeves,_Jr." title="John W. Reeves, Jr.">John W. Reeves, Jr.</a> in command.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> remained at <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> through May, fitting out, before she got underway on 5 June 1940 for calibration tests on her <a href="/wiki/Radio_direction_finder" title="Radio direction finder">radio direction finder</a> gear. After further fitting out while anchored in Boston harbor, the new aircraft carrier steamed independently to <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>, Virginia; anchoring there on 24 June. Four days later, she sailed for the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> in company with the <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Morris_(DD-417)" title="USS Morris (DD-417)"><i>Morris</i></a>.</p>
<p><i>En route</i>, she conducted the first of many carrier qualification tests. Among the earliest of the qualifiers was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant,_junior_grade" title="Lieutenant, junior grade">Lieutenant, junior grade</a> <a href="/wiki/David_McCampbell" title="David McCampbell">David McCampbell</a>, who later became the Navy's top-scoring "<a href="/wiki/Flying_ace" title="Flying ace">ace</a>" in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. <i>Wasp</i> arrived at <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a> in time to "dress ship" in honor of <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a>.</p>
<p>A fatal incident marred the carrier's shakedown. On 9 July, one of her <a href="/wiki/Vought" title="Vought">Vought</a> <a href="/wiki/Vought_SB2U_Vindicator" title="Vought SB2U Vindicator">SB2U-2 Vindicator</a> <a href="/wiki/Dive_bomber" title="Dive bomber">dive bombers</a> crashed 2 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> (2.3 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>; 3.7 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>) from the ship. <i>Wasp</i> bent on flank speed to close, as did the <a href="/wiki/Plane_guard" title="Plane guard">plane-guarding</a> destroyer <i>Morris</i>. The latter's boats recovered items from the plane's baggage compartment, but the plane itself had gone down with its crew of two.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> departed Guantanamo Bay on 11 July and returned to Hampton Roads four days later. There, she embarked planes from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=1st_Marine_Air_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="1st Marine Air Group (page does not exist)">1st Marine Air Group</a> and took them to sea for qualification trials. Operating off the southern drill grounds, the ship and her planes honed their skills for a week before the Marines and their planes were disembarked at Norfolk, and the carrier moved north to Boston for post-shakedown repairs.</p>
<p>While at Boston, she fired a <a href="/wiki/21-gun_salute" title="21-gun salute">21-gun salute</a> and rendered honors to President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a>, whose yacht, <a href="/wiki/USS_Potomac_(AG-25)" title="USS Potomac (AG-25)"><i>Potomac</i></a>, stopped briefly at the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Navy_Yard" title="Boston Navy Yard">Boston Navy Yard</a> on 10 August.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> departed the Army Quartermaster Base on 21 August to conduct steering drills and full-power trials. Late the following morning, she got underway for Norfolk, Virginia. For the next few days, while destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Ellis_(DD-154)" title="USS Ellis (DD-154)"><i>Ellis</i></a> operated as plane guard, <i>Wasp</i> launched and recovered her aircraft: fighters from <a href="/wiki/VF-72" title="VF-72">Fighter Squadron 7</a> (VF-7) and scout bombers from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=VS-72&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="VS-72 (page does not exist)">Scouting Squadron 72</a> (VS-72). The carrier put into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norfolk_Navy_Yard" title="Norfolk Navy Yard">Norfolk Navy Yard</a> on 28 August for repair work on her <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">turbines</a> – alterations which kept the ship in dockyard hands into the following month. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drydock" title="Drydock">Drydocked</a> from 12–18 September, <i>Wasp</i> ran her final <a href="/wiki/Sea_trial" title="Sea trial">sea trials</a> in Hampton Roads on 26 September 1940.</p>
<p>Now ready to join the fleet and assigned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Strike_Group_Three" title="Carrier Strike Group Three">Carrier Division 3</a>, Patrol Force, <i>Wasp</i> shifted to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Operating Base, Norfolk</a> (NOB Norfolk) from the Norfolk Navy Yard on 11 October. There she loaded 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curtiss_P-40" title="Curtiss P-40">Curtiss P-40</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighters</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">Army Air Corps'</a> <a href="/wiki/8th_Operations_Group" title="8th Operations Group">8th Pursuit Group</a> and nine <a href="/wiki/North_American_O-47" title="North American O-47">North American O-47</a>A <a href="/wiki/Aerial_reconnaissance" title="Aerial reconnaissance">reconnaissance aircraft</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2d_Observation_Squadron" title="2d Observation Squadron">2d Observation Squadron</a>, as well as her own spares and utility unit Grumman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J2F_Duck" title="J2F Duck">J2F Duck</a> <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boats</a> on the 12th. Proceeding to sea for maneuvering room, <i>Wasp</i> flew off the Army planes in a test designed to compare the take-off runs of standard Navy and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> aircraft. That experiment, the first time that Army planes had flown from a Navy carrier, foreshadowed the use of the ship in the ferry role that she performed so well in World War II.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> then proceeded on toward <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> in company with the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Plunkett_(DD-431)" title="USS Plunkett (DD-431)"><i>Plunkett</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Niblack_(DD-424)" title="USS Niblack (DD-424)"><i>Niblack</i></a>. Over the ensuing four days, the carrier's planes flew routine training flights, including <a href="/wiki/Dive_bomber" title="Dive bomber">dive-bombing</a> and machine gun practices. Upon arrival at <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a>, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s saluting batteries barked out a 13-gun salute to <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral_(United_States)" title="Rear admiral (United States)">Rear Admiral</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hayne_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hayne Ellis (page does not exist)">Hayne Ellis</a>, Commander, Atlantic Squadron, embarked in <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Texas_(BB-35)" title="USS Texas (BB-35)"><i>Texas</i></a> on 19 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the remainder of October and into November, <i>Wasp</i> trained in the Guantanamo Bay area. Her planes flew carrier qualification and refresher training flights, while her gunners sharpened up their skills in short-range battle practices at targets towed by the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_tug" title="Fleet tug">fleet tug</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Seminole_(AT-65)" title="USS Seminole (AT-65)"><i>Seminole</i></a>.</p>

<p>Her work in the Caribbean finished, <i>Wasp</i> sailed for Norfolk and arrived shortly after noon on 26 November. She remained at the Norfolk Navy Yard through Christmas of 1940. Then, after first conducting <a href="/wiki/Degaussing" title="Degaussing">degaussing</a> experiments with the survey ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Hannibal_(AG-1)" title="USS Hannibal (AG-1)"><i>Hannibal</i></a>, she steamed independently to Cuba.</p>
<p>Arriving at Guantanamo Bay on 27 January 1941, <i>Wasp</i> conducted a regular routine of flight operations into February. With destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Walke_(DD-416)" title="USS Walke (DD-416)"><i>Walke</i></a> as her plane guard, <i>Wasp</i> operated out of Guantanamo and <a href="/wiki/Culebra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Culebra, Puerto Rico">Culebra</a>, conducting her maneuvers with an impressive array of warships—battleship <i>Texas</i>, carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_Ranger_(CV-4)" title="USS Ranger (CV-4)"><i>Ranger</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cruiser" title="Heavy cruiser">heavy cruisers</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Tuscaloosa_(CA-37)" title="USS Tuscaloosa (CA-37)"><i>Tuscaloosa</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Wichita_(CA-45)" title="USS Wichita (CA-45)"><i>Wichita</i></a>, and a host of destroyers. <i>Wasp</i> ran gunnery drills and exercises, as well as routine flight training evolutions into March. Underway for Hampton Roads on 4 March, the aircraft carrier conducted a night battle practice into the early morning hours of the 5th.</p>
<p>During the passage to Norfolk, heavy weather sprang up on the evening of 7 March. <i>Wasp</i> was steaming at standard speed, 17 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (20 <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">mph</a>; 31 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>). Off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a>, a lookout spotted a red flare at 22:45, then a second set of flares at 22:59. At 23:29, with the aid of her searchlights, <i>Wasp</i> located the stranger in trouble. She was the lumber <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> <i>George E. Klinck</i>, bound from <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Harbor,_Maine" title="Southwest Harbor, Maine">Southwest Harbor, Maine</a>.</p>
<p>The sea, in the meantime, worsened from a <a href="/wiki/Sea_state" title="Sea state">state 5</a> to a state 7. <i>Wasp</i> lay to, maneuvering alongside at 00:07 on 8 March. At that time, four men from the schooner clambered up a swaying <a href="/wiki/Jacob%27s_ladder_(nautical)" title="Jacob's ladder (nautical)">Jacob's ladder</a> buffeted by gusts of wind. Then, despite the raging tempest, <i>Wasp</i> lowered a boat, at 00:16, and brought the remaining four men aboard from the foundering 152 ft (46 m) schooner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later that day, <i>Wasp</i> disembarked her rescued mariners and immediately went into drydock at the Norfolk Navy Yard. The ship received vital repairs to her turbines. Portholes on the third deck were welded over to provide better watertight integrity, and steel splinter shielding around her 5 in (130 mm) and 1.1 in (28 mm) batteries was added. <i>Wasp</i> was one of 14 ships to receive the early <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">RCA</a> <a href="/wiki/CXAM_radar" title="CXAM radar">CXAM-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RADAR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RADAR-2">[2]</a></sup> After those repairs and alterations were finished, <i>Wasp</i> got underway for the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> on 22 March, arriving at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, United States Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a> three days later. She soon shifted to Guantanamo Bay and loaded maritime supplies for transportation to Norfolk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Returning to Norfolk on 30 March, <i>Wasp</i> conducted routine flight operations out of Hampton Roads over the ensuing days, into April. In company with <a href="/wiki/USS_Sampson_(DD-394)" title="USS Sampson (DD-394)"><i>Sampson</i></a>, the carrier conducted an abortive search for a downed patrol plane in her vicinity on 8 April. For the remainder of the month, <i>Wasp</i> operated off the eastern seaboard between <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, and Norfolk conducting extensive flight and patrol operations with her embarked air group. She shifted to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> in mid-May, anchoring at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grassy_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grassy Bay (page does not exist)">Grassy Bay</a> on the 12th. Eight days later, the ship got underway in company with the heavy cruiser <a href="/wiki/USS_Quincy_(CA-39)" title="USS Quincy (CA-39)"><i>Quincy</i></a> and the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Livermore_(DD-429)" title="USS Livermore (DD-429)"><i>Livermore</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Kearny_(DD-432)" title="USS Kearny (DD-432)"><i>Kearny</i></a> for exercises at sea before returning to Grassy Bay on 3 June. <i>Wasp</i> sailed for Norfolk three days later with the destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Edison_(DD-439)" title="USS Edison (DD-439)"><i>Edison</i></a> as her anti-submarine screen.</p>
<p>After a brief stay in the Tidewater area, <i>Wasp</i> headed back toward Bermuda on 20 June. <i>Wasp</i> and her escorts patrolled the stretch of the Atlantic between Bermuda and Hampton Roads until 5 July, as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fleet_Forces_Command" title="United States Fleet Forces Command">Atlantic Fleet</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Neutrality_Patrol" title="Neutrality Patrol">neutrality patrol</a> zones were extended eastward. Reaching Grassy Bay on that day, she remained in port a week before returning to Norfolk, sailing on 12 July in company with heavy cruiser <i>Tuscaloosa</i> and destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Grayson_(DD-435)" title="USS Grayson (DD-435)"><i>Grayson</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Anderson_(DD-411)" title="USS Anderson (DD-411)"><i>Anderson</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/USS_Rowan_(DD-405)" title="USS Rowan (DD-405)"><i>Rowan</i></a>.</p>
<p>Following her return to Norfolk on 13 July 1941, <i>Wasp</i> and her embarked air group conducted refresher training off the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Capes" title="Virginia Capes">Virginia Capes</a>. Meanwhile, the situation in the Atlantic had taken on a new complexion, with American participation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Atlantic_(1940)" title="Battle of the Atlantic (1940)">Battle of the Atlantic</a> only a matter of time, when the United States took another step toward involvement on the side of the British. To protect American security and to free British forces needed elsewhere, the United States made plans to <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Iceland#United_States_occupation_force" title="Invasion of Iceland">occupy Iceland</a>. <i>Wasp</i> played an important role in the move.</p>
<p>Late on the afternoon of 23 July, while the carrier lay alongside Pier 7, NOB Norfolk, 32 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">Army Air Forces</a> (AAF) pilots reported on board "for temporary duty". At 06:30 the following day, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s crew watched an interesting cargo come on board, hoisted on deck by the ship's cranes: 30 <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_P-40_Warhawk" title="Curtiss P-40 Warhawk">P-40</a>Cs and three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_Stearman" title="Boeing Stearman">PT-17</a> trainers from the AAF <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33rd_Pursuit_Squadron" title="33rd Pursuit Squadron">33rd Pursuit Squadron</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=8th_Air_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="8th Air Group (page does not exist)">8th Air Group</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Combat_Command" title="Air Force Combat Command">Air Force Combat Command</a>, home-based at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchel_Field" title="Mitchel Field">Mitchel Field</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. Three days later, four newspaper correspondents – including the noted journalist <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Pratt" title="Fletcher Pratt">Fletcher Pratt</a> — came on board.</p>
<p>The carrier had drawn the assignment of ferrying those vital army planes to Iceland because of a lack of British aircraft to cover the American landings. The American P-40s would provide the defensive fighter cover necessary to watch over the initial American occupying forces. <i>Wasp</i> slipped out to sea on 28 July, with the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_O%27Brien_(DD-415)" title="USS O'Brien (DD-415)"><i>O'Brien</i></a> and <i>Walke</i> as plane guards. The heavy cruiser <a href="/wiki/USS_Vincennes_(CA-44)" title="USS Vincennes (CA-44)"><i>Vincennes</i></a> later joined the formation at sea.</p>
<p>Within a few days, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s group joined the larger <a href="/wiki/Task_Force_16" title="Task Force 16">Task Force 16</a>—consisting of the battleship <a href="/wiki/USS_Mississippi_(BB-41)" title="USS Mississippi (BB-41)"><i>Mississippi</i></a>, the heavy cruisers <i>Quincy</i> and <i>Wichita</i>, five destroyers, the auxiliary <a href="/wiki/USS_Semmes_(DD-189)" title="USS Semmes (DD-189)"><i>Semmes</i></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Attack_transport" title="Attack transport">attack transport</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_American_Legion_(APA-17)" title="USS American Legion (APA-17)"><i>American Legion</i></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Combat_stores_ship" title="Combat stores ship">stores ship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Mizar_(AF-12)" title="USS Mizar (AF-12)"><i>Mizar</i></a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_cargo_ship" title="Amphibious cargo ship">amphibious cargo ship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Almaack_(AKA-10)" title="USS Almaack (AKA-10)"><i>Almaack</i></a>. Those ships, too, were bound for Iceland with the first occupation troops embarked. On the morning of 6 August, <i>Wasp</i>, <i>Vincennes</i>, <i>Walke</i>, and <i>O'Brien</i> parted company from Task Force 16 (TF 16). Soon thereafter, the carrier turned into the wind and commenced launching the planes from the 33rd Pursuit Squadron. As the P-40s and the trio of trainers droned on to Iceland, <i>Wasp</i> headed home for Norfolk, her three escorts in company. After another week at sea, the group arrived back at Norfolk on 14 August.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> put to sea again on 22 August for carrier qualifications and refresher landings off the Virginia capes. Two days later, Rear Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._Kent_Hewitt" title="H. Kent Hewitt">H. Kent Hewitt</a>, Commander Cruisers, Atlantic Fleet, shifted his flag from the <a href="/wiki/Light_cruiser" title="Light cruiser">light cruiser</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Savannah_(CL-42)" title="USS Savannah (CL-42)"><i>Savannah</i></a> to <i>Wasp</i> while the ships lay anchored in Hampton Roads. Underway on the 25th, in company with <i>Savannah</i> and the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Monssen_(DD-436)" title="USS Monssen (DD-436)"><i>Monssen</i></a> and <i>Kearny</i>, the aircraft carrier conducted flight operations over the ensuing days. Scuttlebutt on board the carrier had her steaming out in search of the <a href="/wiki/Kriegsmarine" title="Kriegsmarine">German</a> heavy cruiser <a href="/wiki/German_cruiser_Admiral_Hipper" title="German cruiser Admiral Hipper"><i>Admiral Hipper</i></a>, which was reportedly roaming the western Atlantic in search of prey. Suspicions were confirmed for many on the 30th when the British battleship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Rodney_(29)" title="HMS Rodney (29)">HMS <i>Rodney</i></a> was sighted some 20 nautical miles (37 km; 23 mi) away, on the same course as the Americans.</p>
<p>In any event, if they had been in search of a German raider, they did not make contact with her. <i>Wasp</i> and her escorts anchored in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Paria" title="Gulf of Paria">Gulf of Paria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> on 2 September, where Admiral Hewitt shifted his flag back to <i>Savannah</i>. The carrier remained in port until 6 September, when she again put to sea on patrol "to enforce the neutrality of the United States in the Atlantic".</p>
<p>While at sea, the ship received the news of a German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> unsuccessfully attempting to attack the destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Greer_(DD-145)" title="USS Greer (DD-145)"><i>Greer</i></a>. The U.S. had been getting more and more involved in the war; American warships were now convoying British merchantmen halfway across the Atlantic to the "mid-ocean meeting point" (MOMP).</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s crew looked forward to returning to Bermuda on 18 September, but the new situation in the Atlantic meant a change in plans. Shifted to the colder climes of <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, the carrier arrived at <a href="/wiki/Placentia_Bay" title="Placentia Bay">Placentia Bay</a> on 22 September and fueled from the oiler <a href="/wiki/USS_Salinas_(AO-19)" title="USS Salinas (AO-19)"><i>Salinas</i></a> the following day. The respite in port was a brief one, however, as the ship got underway again, late on 23 September for Iceland. In company with <i>Wichita</i>, four destroyers, and the repair ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Vulcan_(AR-5)" title="USS Vulcan (AR-5)"><i>Vulcan</i></a>, <i>Wasp</i> arrived at <a href="/wiki/Hvalfj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Hvalfjörður">Hvalfjörður</a>, Iceland, on 28 September. Two days earlier, Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_R._Stark" title="Harold R. Stark">Harold R. Stark</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Naval_Operations" title="Chief of Naval Operations">Chief of Naval Operations</a> had ordered American warships to do their utmost to destroy whatever German or Italian warships they found.</p>
<p>With the accelerated activity entailed in the US Navy's conducting <a href="/wiki/Convoy" title="Convoy">convoy</a> escort missions, <i>Wasp</i> put to sea on 6 October in company with <i>Vincennes</i> and four destroyers. Those ships patrolled the foggy, cold, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> until returning to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Placentia_Bay" title="Little Placentia Bay">Little Placentia Bay</a>, Newfoundland on 11 October, anchoring during a fierce gale that lashed the bay with high winds and stinging spray. On 17 October, <i>Wasp</i> set out for Norfolk, patrolling en route, and arrived at her destination on 20 October. The carrier soon sailed for Bermuda and conducted qualifications and refresher training flights <i>en route</i>. Anchoring in Grassy Bay on 1 November, <i>Wasp</i> operated on patrols out of Bermuda for the remainder of the month.</p>
<p>October had seen the incidents involving American and German warships multiplying on the high seas. <i>Kearny</i> was torpedoed on 17 October, <i>Salinas</i> on 28 October, and in the most tragic incident that autumn, <a href="/wiki/USS_Reuben_James_(DD-245)" title="USS Reuben James (DD-245)"><i>Reuben James</i></a> was torpedoed and sunk with heavy loss of life on 30 October. Meanwhile, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, tension between the U.S. and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> increased almost with each passing day.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> slipped out to sea from Grassy Bay on 3 December and rendezvoused with <a href="/wiki/USS_Wilson_(DD-408)" title="USS Wilson (DD-408)"><i>Wilson</i></a>. While the destroyer operated as plane guard, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s air group flew day and night refresher training missions. In addition, the two ships conducted gunnery drills before returning to Grassy Bay two days later, where she lay at anchor on 7 December 1941 during the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_Fleet">Atlantic Fleet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atlantic Fleet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Naval authorities felt considerable anxiety that <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> warships in the Caribbean and <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> were prepared to make a breakout and attempt to get back to France. Accordingly, <i>Wasp</i>, the light cruiser <a href="/wiki/USS_Brooklyn_(CL-40)" title="USS Brooklyn (CL-40)"><i>Brooklyn</i></a>, and the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Sterett_(DD-407)" title="USS Sterett (DD-407)"><i>Sterett</i></a> and <i>Wilson</i>, departed Grassy Bay and headed for <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>. Faulty intelligence gave American authorities in Washington the impression that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vichy_French" title="Vichy French">Vichy French</a> armed merchant cruiser <i>Barfleur</i> had gotten underway for sea. The French were accordingly warned that the auxiliary cruiser would be sunk or captured unless she returned to port and resumed her internment. As it turned out, <i>Barfleur</i> had not departed after all, but had remained in harbor. The tense situation at Martinique eventually dissipated, and the crisis abated.</p>
<p>With tensions in the West Indies lessened considerably, <i>Wasp</i> departed Grassy Bay and headed for Hampton Roads three days before Christmas, in company with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Long_Island_(AVG-1)" title="USS Long Island (AVG-1)"><i>Long Island</i></a>, and escorted by the destroyers <a href="/wiki/USS_Stack_(DD-406)" title="USS Stack (DD-406)"><i>Stack</i></a> and <i>Sterett</i>. Two days later, the carrier moored at the Norfolk Navy Yard to commence an overhaul that would last into 1942.</p>
<p>After departing Norfolk on 14 January 1942, <i>Wasp</i> headed north and touched at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Argentia" title="Naval Station Argentia">NS Argentia</a>, Newfoundland, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casco_Bay,_Maine" title="Casco Bay, Maine">Casco Bay, Maine</a>. On 16 March, as part of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Task_Group_22.6&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Task Group 22.6 (page does not exist)">Task Group 22.6</a> (TG 22.6), she headed back toward Norfolk. During the morning watch the next day, visibility lessened considerably; and, at 06:50, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s bow plunged into <i>Stack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s starboard side, punching a hole and completely flooding the destroyer's number one fireroom. <i>Stack</i> was detached and proceeded to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Navy_Yard" title="Philadelphia Navy Yard">Philadelphia Navy Yard</a>, where her damage was repaired.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, <i>Wasp</i> made port at Norfolk on the 21st without further incident. Shifting back to Casco Bay three days later, she sailed for the British Isles on 26 March, with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Task_Force_39&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Task Force 39 (page does not exist)">Task Force 39</a> under the command of Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/John_W._Wilcox,_Jr." title="John W. Wilcox, Jr.">John W. Wilcox, Jr.</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/USS_Washington_(BB-56)" title="USS Washington (BB-56)"><i>Washington</i></a>. That force was to reinforce the <a href="/wiki/Home_Fleet" title="Home Fleet">Home Fleet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. While <i>en route</i>, Rear Admiral Wilcox was swept overboard from the battleship and drowned. Although hampered by poor visibility conditions, <i>Wasp</i> planes took part in the search. Wilcox's body was spotted an hour later, face down in the raging seas, but it was not recovered due to the weather and the heavy seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Robert_C._Giffen" title="Robert C. Giffen">Robert C. Giffen</a>, who flew his flag on <i>Wichita</i>, assumed command of TF 39. The American ships were met by a force based around the light cruiser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Edinburgh_(C16)" title="HMS Edinburgh (C16)">HMS <i>Edinburgh</i></a> on 3 April. Those ships escorted them to <a href="/wiki/Scapa_Flow" title="Scapa Flow">Scapa Flow</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a>. While there, a <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Gladiator" title="Gloster Gladiator">Gloster Gladiator</a> flown by Captain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Fancourt" title="Henry Fancourt">Henry Fancourt</a> of the Royal Navy made the first landing of the war by a British plane on an American aircraft carrier when it landed on <i>Wasp</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>While the majority of TF 39 joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Home_Fleet" title="British Home Fleet">British Home Fleet</a> — being renumbered to TF 99 in the process — to cover convoys routed to North Russia, <i>Wasp</i> departed Scapa Flow on 9 April, bound for the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Clyde estuary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. On the following day, the carrier sailed up the Clyde River, past the John Brown Clydebank shipbuilding facilities. There, shipyard workers paused long enough from their labors to accord <i>Wasp</i> a tumultuous reception as she passed. <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s impending mission was an important one – one upon which the fate of the island bastion of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> hung. That <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Malta_(World_War_II)" title="Siege of Malta (World War II)">key isle was then being pounded daily by German and Italian planes</a>. The British, faced with the loss of air superiority over the island, requested the use of a carrier to transport planes that could wrest air superiority from the Axis aircraft. <i>Wasp</i> drew ferry duty once again to participate in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Calendar" title="Operation Calendar">Operation Calendar</a>, one of many <a href="/wiki/Malta_Convoys" title="Malta Convoys">Malta Convoys</a>.</p>

<p>Having landed her <a href="/wiki/Torpedo_bomber" title="Torpedo bomber">torpedo bombers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dive_bomber" title="Dive bomber">dive bombers</a> at <a href="/wiki/RNAS_Hatston" title="RNAS Hatston">Hatston</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, <i>Wasp</i> loaded 47 <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire Mk. V</a> fighters of <a href="/wiki/No._603_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 603 Squadron RAF">No. 603 Squadron RAF</a> at <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> on 13 April, then departed on the 14th, this was the start of "Operation Calendar". Her screen consisted of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Force_W&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Force W (page does not exist)">Force "W"</a> of the Home Fleet – a group that included the <a href="/wiki/Battlecruiser" title="Battlecruiser">battlecruiser</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Renown_(1916)" title="HMS Renown (1916)">HMS <i>Renown</i></a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_cruiser" title="Anti-aircraft cruiser">anti-aircraft cruisers</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Cairo_(D87)" title="HMS Cairo (D87)">HMS <i>Cairo</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Charybdis_(88)" title="HMS Charybdis (88)"><i>Charybdis</i></a>. <a href="/wiki/USS_Madison_(DD-425)" title="USS Madison (DD-425)"><i>Madison</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Lang_(DD-399)" title="USS Lang (DD-399)"><i>Lang</i></a> also served in <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s screen.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> and her consorts passed through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Gibraltar" title="Straits of Gibraltar">Straits of Gibraltar</a> under cover of the pre-dawn darkness on 19 April, avoiding the possibility of being discovered by Spanish or Axis agents. At 04:00 on 20 April, <i>Wasp</i> spotted 11 <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F4F_Wildcat" title="Grumman F4F Wildcat">Grumman F4F Wildcat</a> fighters on her deck and quickly launched them to form a <a href="/wiki/Combat_air_patrol" title="Combat air patrol">combat air patrol</a> (CAP) over Force "W". Meanwhile, the Spitfires were warming up their engines in the hangar deck spaces below. With the Wildcats patrolling overhead, the Spitfires were brought up singly on the after elevator, spotted for launch, and then given the go-ahead to take off. One by one, they roared down the deck and over the forward rounddown, until each Spitfire was aloft and winging toward Malta.</p>

<p>When the launch was complete, <i>Wasp</i> retired toward Gibraltar, having safely delivered her charges. However, those Spitfires, which flew in to augment the dwindling numbers of Gladiator and <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Hurricane" title="Hawker Hurricane">Hurricane</a> fighters, were tracked by efficient Axis intelligence and their arrival pinpointed. Most of the Spitfires were destroyed by heavy German air raids which caught many planes on the ground.</p>
<p>As a result, it looked as if the acute situation required a second ferry run to Malta. Accordingly, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, fearing that Malta would be "pounded to bits", asked President Roosevelt to allow <i>Wasp</i> to have "another good sting." Roosevelt responded in the affirmative. <i>Wasp</i> loaded another contingent of Spitfire Vs at King George V Dock Glasgow and sailed for the Mediterranean on 3 May. Again, <i>Wasp</i> proceeded unmolested. This time, the British carrier <a href="/wiki/HMS_Eagle_(1918)" title="HMS Eagle (1918)">HMS <i>Eagle</i></a> accompanied <i>Wasp</i>, and she, too, carried a contingent of Spitfires bound for Malta. The Spitfires for <i>Eagle</i> had been loaded at Greenock, James Watt Dock, from lighters. This was the start of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bowery" title="Operation Bowery">Operation Bowery</a>.</p>
<p>The two Allied carriers reached their launching points early on Saturday, 9 May, with <i>Wasp</i> steaming in column ahead of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Eagle_(1918)" title="HMS Eagle (1918)"><i>Eagle</i></a> at a distance of 1,000 yards (910 m). At 06:30, <i>Wasp</i> commenced launching planes – 11 Wildcats of <a href="/wiki/VF-71" title="VF-71">VF-71</a> to serve as CAP over the task force. First, <i>Eagle</i> flew off her 17 Spitfires in two waves; then <i>Wasp</i> flew off 47 more. The first Spitfire took off at 06:43, piloted by Sergeant-Pilot Herrington, but lost power soon after takeoff and plunged into the sea, with loss of pilot and aircraft. The other planes flew off safely and formed up to fly to Malta. An auxiliary fuel tank on another aircraft failed to draw; without the additional fuel the pilot could not make Malta, and his only alternatives were to land on board <i>Wasp</i> – with no tailhook – or to ditch and take his chances in the water.</p>
<p>Pilot Officer Jerrold Alpine Smith chose the former. <i>Wasp</i> bent on full speed and recovered the plane at 07:43. The Spitfire came to a stop just 15 feet (4.6 m) from the forward edge of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_deck" title="Flight deck">flight deck</a>, making what one <i>Wasp</i> sailor observed to be a "one wire" landing. With her vital errand completed, <i>Wasp</i> set sail for the British Isles while a German radio station broadcast the startling news that the American carrier had been sunk; on 11 May, Prime Minister Churchill sent a message to <i>Wasp</i>: "Many thanks to you all for the timely help. Who said a wasp couldn't sting twice?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_Fleet">Pacific Fleet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pacific Fleet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Early in May 1942, almost simultaneously with <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s second Malta run—<a href="/wiki/Operation_Bowery" title="Operation Bowery">Operation Bowery</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Coral_Sea" title="Battle of the Coral Sea">Battle of the Coral Sea</a> had been fought, then the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Battle of Midway</a> a month later. These battles reduced the U.S. to three carriers in the Pacific, and it became imperative to transfer <i>Wasp</i>.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> was hurried back to the U.S. for alterations and repairs at the Norfolk Navy Yard. During the carrier's stay in the Tidewater region, Captain Reeves – who had been promoted to flag rank – was relieved by Captain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forrest_P._Sherman" title="Forrest P. Sherman">Forrest P. Sherman</a> on 31 May. Departing Norfolk on 6 June, <i>Wasp</i> sailed with TF 37 which was built around the carrier and the battleship <a href="/wiki/USS_North_Carolina_(BB-55)" title="USS North Carolina (BB-55)"><i>North Carolina</i></a> and escorted by <i>Quincy</i>, <a href="/wiki/USS_San_Juan_(CL-54)" title="USS San Juan (CL-54)"><i>San Juan</i></a> and six destroyers. The group transited the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> on 10 June, at which time <i>Wasp</i> and her consorts became TF 18, the carrier flying the two-star flag of Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Noyes" title="Leigh Noyes">Leigh Noyes</a>.</p>
<p>Arriving at <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> on 19 June, <i>Wasp</i> embarked the remainder of her complement of aircraft, <a href="/wiki/Grumman_TBF_Avenger" title="Grumman TBF Avenger">Grumman TBF-1 Avengers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_SBD_Dauntless" title="Douglas SBD Dauntless">Douglas SBD-3 Dauntlesses</a>, the former replacing the old Vindicators. On 1 July, she sailed for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonga_Islands" title="Tonga Islands">Tonga Islands</a> as part of the convoy for the five transports carrying the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="2nd Marine Regiment (United States)">2nd Marine Regiment</a>.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, preparations to invade the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> were proceeding to disrupt the Japanese offensive to establish a defensive perimeter around the edge of their "<a href="/wiki/Greater_East_Asia_Co-Prosperity_Sphere" title="Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere">Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere</a>".</p>

<p>On 4 July, while <i>Wasp</i> was <i>en route</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">South Pacific</a>, the Japanese landed on <a href="/wiki/Guadalcanal" title="Guadalcanal">Guadalcanal</a>. Allied planners realized Japanese operation of land-based aircraft from that key island would imperil Allied control of the <a href="/wiki/New_Hebrides" title="New Hebrides">New Hebrides</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> area. Plans were made to evict the Japanese before their Guadalcanal airfield became operational. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Admiral" title="Vice Admiral">Vice Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Ghormley" title="Robert L. Ghormley">Robert L. Ghormley</a> — with experience as Special Naval Observer in London— was detailed to take command of the operation; and he established his headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Since the Japanese had a foothold on Guadalcanal, time was of the essence; preparations for an allied invasion proceeded with secrecy and speed.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> — together with the carriers <a href="/wiki/USS_Saratoga_(CV-3)" title="USS Saratoga (CV-3)"><i>Saratoga</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Enterprise_(CV-6)" title="USS Enterprise (CV-6)"><i>Enterprise</i></a> — was assigned to the Support Force under Vice Admiral <a href="/wiki/Frank_Jack_Fletcher" title="Frank Jack Fletcher">Frank Jack Fletcher</a>. Under the tactical command of Rear Admiral Noyes, embarked on <i>Wasp</i>, the carriers were to provide air support for the invasion and initiation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guadalcanal_campaign" title="Guadalcanal campaign">Guadalcanal campaign</a>.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> and her airmen practiced day and night operations to hone their skills until Captain Sherman was confident that his airmen could perform their mission. "<a href="/wiki/D-Day_(military_term)" title="D-Day (military term)">D-day</a>" had originally been set for 1 August, but the late arrival of some of the transports carrying Marines pushed the date to 7 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>En route</i>, <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s engines became a problem, with a 14 July message from CTF 18 to CINCPAC reporting that she had suffered a casualty to her starboard high-pressure turbine that even at lowest speeds was making a loud scraping noise, limiting speed to only fifteen knots under her port engine thus making air operations entirely dependent on favorable wind. The ship's company was undertaking repairs, including lifting the turbine casing. Repairs to the rotor itself were proposed at "BLEACHER" (<a href="/wiki/Tongatapu" title="Tongatapu">Tongatapu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga Islands</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where the <a href="/wiki/Destroyer_tender" title="Destroyer tender">destroyer tender</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Whitney_(AD-4)" title="USS Whitney (AD-4)"><i>Whitney</i></a> was stationed, with four days estimated for the work there. <i>Wasp</i> arrived 18 July for those repairs and on 21 July (21 0802 July) CTF 18 reported <i>Wasp</i> had successfully completed a trial making turns for twenty-seven knots with pre-casualty twenty-five knot operations possible with reduced reliability. Replacement blades available at Pearl Harbor and replacement of all three rows of blading was recommended after the ongoing operations were completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nimitz_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nimitz-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Wasp</i>, screened by the heavy cruiser <a href="/wiki/USS_San_Francisco_(CA-38)" title="USS San Francisco (CA-38)"><i>San Francisco</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Salt_Lake_City_(CA-25)" title="USS Salt Lake City (CA-25)"><i>Salt Lake City</i></a>, and four destroyers, steamed westward toward Guadalcanal on the evening of 6 August until midnight. Then, she changed course to the eastward to reach her launch position 84 nautical miles (97 mi; 156 km) from <a href="/wiki/Tulagi" title="Tulagi">Tulagi</a> one hour before dawn. <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s first combat air patrol fighter took off at 05:57.</p>
<p>The early flights of Wildcats and Dauntlesses were assigned specific targets: Tulagi, <a href="/wiki/Gavutu" title="Gavutu">Gavutu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanambogo" title="Tanambogo">Tanambogo</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Halavo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Halavo (page does not exist)">Halavo</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Purvis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Port Purvis (page does not exist)">Port Purvis</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haleta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haleta (page does not exist)">Haleta</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bungana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bungana (page does not exist)">Bungana</a>, and the radio station dubbed "Asses' Ears".</p>
<p>The Wildcats, led by <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> Shands and his wingman <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">Ensign</a> S. W. Forrer, patrolled the north coast toward Gavatu. The other two headed for the seaplane facilities at Tanambogo. The Grummans, arriving simultaneously at daybreak, surprised the Japanese and strafed patrol planes and fighter-<a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">seaplanes</a> in the area. Fifteen <a href="/wiki/Kawanishi_H8K" title="Kawanishi H8K">Kawanishi H8K</a> "Emily" flying boats and seven <a href="/wiki/Nakajima_A6M2-N" title="Nakajima A6M2-N">Nakajima A6M2-N</a> "Rufe" <a href="/wiki/Floatplane" title="Floatplane">floatplane</a> fighters were destroyed by Shands' fighters during low-level strafing passes. Shands was credited with four "Rufes" and one "Emily", while his wingman, Forrer, was credited with three "Rufes" and an "Emily". Lieutenant Wright and Ensign Kenton were credited with three patrol planes apiece and a motorboat tending the "Emilys"; Ensigns Reeves and Conklin were each credited with two and shared a fifth patrol plane between them. The strafing Wildcats also destroyed an aviation fuel truck and a truck loaded with spare parts.</p>
<p>Post-attack assessment estimated that the antiaircraft and shore battery sites pinpointed by intelligence had been destroyed by the Dauntless dive bombers in their first attack. None of <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s planes was shot down, but Ensign Reeves landed his Wildcat aboard <i>Enterprise</i> after running low on fuel.</p>
<p>At 07:04, <i>Wasp</i> launched 12 Avengers loaded with bombs for use against land targets, and led by Lieutenant H. A. Romberg. The Avengers silenced resistance by bombing Japanese troop concentrations east of the knob of land known as Hill 281, in the Makambo-Sasapi sector, and the prison on Tulagi Island.</p>
<p>Some 10,000 men had been put ashore during the first day's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guadalcanal" title="Battle of Guadalcanal">operations against Guadalcanal</a>, and met only slight resistance. On Tulagi, however, the Japanese resisted stoutly, retaining about <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>5</sub></span> of the island by nightfall. <i>Wasp</i>, <i>Saratoga</i>, and <i>Enterprise</i> — with their screens – retired to the southward at nightfall.</p>

<p><i>Wasp</i> fighters led by Lieutenant C. S. Moffett maintained a continuous CAP over the transport area until noon on 8 August. Meanwhile, a scouting flight of 12 Dauntlesses led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Commander_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant Commander (United States)">Lieutenant Commander</a> E. M. Snowden searched a sector to a radius of 220 nautical miles (250 mi; 410 km) from their carrier, extending it to include all of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Isabel_Island" title="Santa Isabel Island">Santa Isabel Island</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Georgia" title="New Georgia">New Georgia</a> groups.</p>
<p>The Dauntless pilots made no contact with the Japanese during their two hours in the air, but at 08:15, Snowden sighted a "Rufe" some 40 nautical miles (46 mi; 74 km) from <a href="/wiki/Rekata_Bay" title="Rekata Bay">Rekata Bay</a> and shot the plane down with fixed .50 in (13 mm) machine guns.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, a large group of Japanese planes approached from Bougainville to attack the transports off <a href="/wiki/Lunga_Point" title="Lunga Point">Lunga Point</a>. Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Richmond_K._Turner" title="Richmond K. Turner">Richmond K. Turner</a> ordered all transports to get underway and to assume cruising disposition. Eldridge was leading a formation of Dauntlesses from VS-71 against <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mbangi_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mbangi Island (page does not exist)">Mbangi Island</a>, off Tulagi. His rear seat gunner, Aviation Chief Radioman L. A. Powers, assumed the formation of Japanese planes were friendly until six Zeroes bounced the first section with 12 unsuccessful firing passes.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, the leader of the last section of VS-71 – Lieutenant, junior grade Robert L. Howard – unsuccessfully attacked twin-engined <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_G4M" title="Mitsubishi G4M">Mitsubishi G4M</a> "Betty" <a href="/wiki/Medium_bomber" title="Medium bomber">medium bombers</a> heading for the American transports, and was engaged by four Zeroes escorting the bombers. Howard shot down one Zero with his fixed .50 in (13 mm) guns while his rear gunner, Seaman 2nd Class Lawrence P. Lupo, discouraged Japanese fighters attacking from astern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s casualties for the entire action on 7 and 8 August were:</p>
<ul>
<li>One fighter pilot, Ens. Thaddeus J. Capowski, missing in action when he was separated from the formation. His parents (Mr and Mrs Walter Capowski of Yonkers NY) were notified of TJC's MIA status in early September 1942; shortly thereafter TJC was found safe and alive.</li>
<li>One scout bomber shot down; pilot Lieut. Dudley H. Adams wounded by explosive bullets and recovered by <a href="/wiki/USS_Dewey_(DD-349)" title="USS Dewey (DD-349)"><i>Dewey</i></a>; Radioman-gunner Harry E. Elliott, ARM3c, missing, reported to have been killed before the crash.</li>
<li>One fighter landed in the water due to propeller trouble; pilot recovered.</li>
<li>One fighter crashed on deck; pilot injured; plane jettisoned overboard.</li>
<li>One fighter crashed into barrier first day; repaired and flown second day.</li>
</ul>
<p>Total plane losses for <i>Wasp</i> were 3 Wildcat fighters and 1 Dauntless scout bomber. Against these, her planes destroyed 15 enemy flying boats, 8 floatplane fighters, and 1 Zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 18:07 on 8 August, Vice Admiral Frank Jack Fletcher recommended to Ghormley, at Nouméa, that the air support force be withdrawn. Fletcher, concerned by the large numbers of Japanese planes that had attacked on 8 August, reported that he had only 78 fighters left (he had started with 99) and that fuel for the carriers was running low. Ghormley approved the recommendation, and <i>Wasp</i> joined <i>Enterprise</i> and <i>Saratoga</i> in retiring from Guadalcanal. By midnight, the landing had attained the immediate objectives. Japanese resistance – except for a few snipers – on Gavutu and Tanombogo had been overcome. Early on 9 August, a Japanese surface force engaged an American one in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Savo_Island" title="Battle of Savo Island">Battle of Savo Island</a> and retired with minimal damage after sinking four Allied heavy cruisers off <a href="/wiki/Savo_Island" title="Savo Island">Savo Island</a>, including two that had served with <i>Wasp</i> in the Atlantic: <i>Vincennes</i> and <i>Quincy</i>. The early and unexpected withdrawal of the support force, including <i>Wasp</i>, when coupled with Allied losses in the Battle of Savo Island, jeopardized the success of the operation in the Solomons.</p>
<p>After the initial day's action in the Solomons campaign, the carrier spent the next month engaged in patrol and covering operations for convoys and resupply units headed for Guadalcanal. The Japanese began transporting reinforcements to contest the Allied forces.</p>
<p><i>Wasp</i> was ordered south by Vice Admiral Fletcher to refuel and did not participate in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Eastern_Solomons" title="Battle of the Eastern Solomons">Battle of the Eastern Solomons</a> on 24 August. After fueling on 24 August <i>Wasp</i> hurried to the battle zone. Her total aircraft group was 26 <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F4F_Wildcat" title="Grumman F4F Wildcat">Grumman F4F Wildcats</a>, 25 <a href="/wiki/Douglas_SBD_Dauntless" title="Douglas SBD Dauntless">Douglas SBD Dauntlesses</a>, and 11 <a href="/wiki/Grumman_TBF_Avenger" title="Grumman TBF Avenger">Grumman TBF Avengers</a>. (One SBD Dauntless was earlier lost on 24 August by ditching in the sea because of engine trouble).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lundstrom_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lundstrom-8">[8]</a></sup> On the morning of 25 August, <i>Wasp</i> launched a search mission. The Douglas SBD Dauntless of pilot Lieut. Chester V. Zalewski shot down two of <a href="/wiki/Aichi_E13A" title="Aichi E13A">Aichi E13A</a>1 "Jake" <a href="/wiki/Floatplane" title="Floatplane">floatplanes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Atago" title="Japanese cruiser Atago">Japanese cruiser <i>Atago</i></a> (Vice Admiral <a href="/wiki/Nobutake_Kond%C5%8D" title="Nobutake Kondō">Nobutake Kondō</a>'s flagship). But the SBD Dauntlesses sighted no ships. The Japanese fleet had withdrawn out of range. At 13:26 on 25 Augustus, <i>Wasp</i> launched a search/attack mission of 24 SBD Dauntlesses and 10 TBF Avengers against the convoy of Rear Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raizo_Tanaka" title="Raizo Tanaka">Raizo Tanaka</a> that seemed to be still within range. Although the SBD Dauntlesses shot down a flying boat, they could not find the enemy ships anymore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lundstrom_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lundstrom-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the battle on 24 August <i>Enterprise</i> was damaged and had to return to port for repairs. <i>Saratoga</i> was torpedoed a week later and departed the South Pacific war zone for repairs as well. That left only two carriers in the southwest Pacific, the <i>Wasp</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_(CV-8)" title="USS Hornet (CV-8)"><i>Hornet</i></a>, which had been in commission for only a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss">Loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Wasp_(CV-7)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On Tuesday, 15 September 1942, the carriers <i>Wasp</i> and <i>Hornet</i> and battleship <a href="/wiki/USS_North_Carolina_(BB-55)" title="USS North Carolina (BB-55)"><i>North Carolina</i></a> with 10 other warships were escorting the transports carrying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._7th_Marine_Regiment" title="U.S. 7th Marine Regiment">7th Marine Regiment</a> to Guadalcanal as reinforcements. <i>Wasp</i> was operating some 150 nautical miles (170 mi; 280 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Makira" title="Makira">San Cristobal Island</a>. Her aircraft were being refueled and rearmed for antisubmarine patrol missions and <i>Wasp</i> had been at general quarters from an hour before sunrise until the time when the morning search returned to the ship at 10:00. Thereafter, the ship was in condition 2, with the air department at flight quarters. The only contact with the Japanese that day had been a Japanese four-engined <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boat</a> that was downed by one of <i>Wasp</i>s F4F Wildcats at 12:15.</p>
<p>About 14:20, the carrier turned into the wind to launch eight F4F Wildcats and 18 SBD Dauntlesses and to recover eight F4F Wildcats and three SBD Dauntlesses that had been airborne since before noon. Lt. (jg) Roland H. Kenton, USNR, flying a F4F-3 Wildcat of VF-71 was the last aircraft off the deck of <i>Wasp</i>. The ship rapidly completed the recovery of the 11 aircraft before turning to starboard, heeling slightly as it did so. At 14:44 a lookout reported "three torpedoes ... three points forward of the starboard beam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A spread of six <a href="/wiki/Type_95_torpedo" title="Type 95 torpedo">Type 95 torpedoes</a> were fired at <i>Wasp</i> at about 14:44 from the tubes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B1_type_submarine" title="B1 type submarine">B1 Type</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_submarine_I-19" title="Japanese submarine I-19"><i>I-19</i></a>. <i>Wasp</i> put over her rudder hard to starboard to avoid the salvo, but it was too late. Three torpedoes struck in quick succession about 14:45; one actually broached, left the water, and struck the ship slightly above the waterline. All hit in the vicinity of the ship's gasoline tanks and magazines. Two of the spread of torpedoes passed ahead of <i>Wasp</i> and were observed passing astern of <a href="/wiki/USS_Helena_(CL-50)" title="USS Helena (CL-50)"><i>Helena</i></a> before <a href="/wiki/USS_O%27Brien_(DD-415)" title="USS O'Brien (DD-415)"><i>O'Brien</i></a> was hit by one at 14:51 while maneuvering to avoid the other. The sixth torpedo passed either astern or under <i>Wasp</i>, narrowly missed <a href="/wiki/USS_Lansdowne_(DD-486)" title="USS Lansdowne (DD-486)"><i>Lansdowne</i></a> in <i>Wasp</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s screen about 14:48, was seen by <a href="/wiki/USS_Mustin_(DD-413)" title="USS Mustin (DD-413)"><i>Mustin</i></a> in <i>North Carolina</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s screen about 14:50, and struck <i>North Carolina</i> about 14:52.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>There was a rapid succession of explosions in the forward part of the ship. Aircraft on the flight and hangar decks were thrown about and dropped on the deck with such force that landing gears snapped. Aircraft suspended in the hangar overhead fell and landed upon those on the hangar deck; fires broke out in the hangar and below decks. Soon, the heat of the intense gasoline fires detonated the ready ammunition at the forward anti-aircraft guns on the starboard side, and fragments showered the forward part of the ship. The number two 1.1 in (28 mm) mount was blown overboard.</p>
<p>Water mains in the forward part of the ship had been rendered inoperable meaning no water was available to fight the fire forward, and the fires continued to set off ammunition, bombs, and gasoline. As the ship listed 10-15<a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">°</a> to starboard, oil and gasoline, released from the tanks by the torpedo hit, caught fire on the water.</p>
<p>Captain Sherman slowed to 10 knots (12 mph; 19 km/h), ordering the rudder put to port to try to get the wind on the starboard bow; he then went astern with right rudder until the wind was on the starboard quarter, in an attempt to keep the fire forward. At that point, flames made the central station unusable, and communication circuits went dead. Soon, a serious gasoline fire broke out in the forward portion of the hangar; within 24 minutes of the initial attack, there were three additional major gasoline vapor explosions. Ten minutes later Sherman decided to abandon ship as the firefighting was ineffectual. Survivors would have to disembark quickly to minimize loss of life.</p>
<p>After consulting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral" title="Rear Admiral">Rear Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Noyes" title="Leigh Noyes">Leigh Noyes</a>, Captain Sherman ordered "abandon ship" at 15:20. All badly injured men were lowered into rafts or rubber boats. Many unwounded men had to abandon ship from aft because the forward fires were burning with such intensity. The departure, as Sherman observed it, looked "orderly", and there was no panic. The only delays occurred when many men showed reluctance to leave until all the wounded had been taken off. The abandonment took nearly 40 minutes, and at 16:00 Sherman abandoned the ship once he was satisfied that no survivors were left on board.</p>
<p>Although the submarine hazard caused the accompanying destroyers to lie well clear or to shift position, they carried out rescue operations until <a href="/wiki/USS_Laffey_(DD-459)" title="USS Laffey (DD-459)"><i>Laffey</i></a>, <i>Lansdowne</i>, <i>Helena</i>, and <a href="/wiki/USS_Salt_Lake_City_(CA-25)" title="USS Salt Lake City (CA-25)"><i>Salt Lake City</i></a> had 1,946 men embarked. The fires on <i>Wasp</i>, drifting, traveled aft and there were four violent explosions at nightfall. <i>Lansdowne</i> was ordered to torpedo the carrier and stand by until she was sunk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Lansdowne</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Mark_15_torpedo" title="Mark 15 torpedo">Mark 15 torpedoes</a> had the same unrecognized flaws reported for the <a href="/wiki/Mark_14_torpedo" title="Mark 14 torpedo">Mark 14 torpedo</a>. The first two torpedoes were fired perfectly, but did not explode, leaving <i>Lansdowne</i> with only three more. The magnetic influence exploders on these were disabled and the depth set at 10 feet (3.0 m). All three detonated, but <i>Wasp</i> remained afloat for some time, sinking at 21:00.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-p2-10">[10]</a></sup> 193 men had died and 366 were wounded during the attack. All but one of her 26 airborne aircraft made a safe trip to carrier <i>Hornet</i> nearby before <i>Wasp</i> sank, but 45 aircraft went down with the ship. Another Japanese submarine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_submarine_I-15" title="Japanese submarine I-15"><i>I-15</i></a>, duly observed and reported the sinking of <i>Wasp</i>, as other US destroyers kept <i>I-19</i> busy avoiding 80 depth charges. <i>I-19</i> escaped safely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=USS_Wasp_%28CV-7%29&amp;params=12_24_58_S_164_8_0_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">12°24′58″S</span> <span class="longitude">164°8′0″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">12.41611°S 164.13333°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-12.41611; 164.13333</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></p>
