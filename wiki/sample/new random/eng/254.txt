<p><b>Dothideomycetes</b> is the largest and most diverse <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascomycete" title="Ascomycete">ascomycete</a> <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>. It comprises 11 <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">orders</a> 90 <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a>, 1300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a> and over 19,000 known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk2008-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schoch2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schoch2009-2">[2]</a></sup> Traditionally, most of its members were included in the loculoascomycetes, which is not part of the currently accepted classification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hibbett2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hibbett2007-3">[3]</a></sup> This indicates that several traditional morphological features in the class are not unique and DNA sequence comparisons are important to define the class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lumbsch2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lumbsch2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The designation loculoascomycetes was first proposed for all fungi which have ascolocular development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This type of development refers to the way in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascoma" title="Ascoma">sexual structure</a>, bearing the sexual spores (<a href="/wiki/Ascospore" title="Ascospore">ascospores</a>) forms. Dothideomycetes mostly produce flask-like structures referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascoma" title="Ascoma">pseudothecia</a>, although other shape variations do exist (e.g. see structures found in <a href="/wiki/Hysteriales" title="Hysteriales">Hysteriales</a>). During ascolocular development pockets (locules) form first within the vegetative cells of the fungus and then all the subsequent structures form. These include the <a href="/wiki/Ascus" title="Ascus">asci</a> which, superficially, have a thicker outer layer through which a thinner inner layer ‘bursts’, like a jack-in-a-box to release the spores. These asci are therefore referred to as bitunicate (superficially, two layers) or fissitunicate (referring to spore release). After several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">DNA sequence comparisons</a> it is now clear that another group of fungi which share these characteristics are distantly related. These are the <a href="/wiki/Black_yeast" title="Black yeast">"black yeasts"</a> in subclass <a href="/wiki/Chaetothyriomycetidae" title="Chaetothyriomycetidae">Chaetothyriomycetidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Eurotiomycetes" title="Eurotiomycetes">Eurotiomycetes</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This means that loculoascomycetes did not constitute <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">a natural group</a>.</p>
<p>The best known members of this class are several important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytopathology" title="Phytopathology">plant pathogens</a> (like <i><a href="/wiki/Phaeosphaeria_nodorum" title="Phaeosphaeria nodorum">Phaeosphaeria nodorum</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Venturia_inaequalis" title="Venturia inaequalis">Venturia inaequalis</a></i>). However, a majority of described species are either found as <a href="/wiki/Endophyte" title="Endophyte">endophytes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">saprobes</a> growing on woody debris, decaying leaves or <a href="/wiki/Coprophilous_fungi" title="Coprophilous fungi">dung</a>. A smaller number exist as <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelsen2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelsen2009-7">[7]</a></sup> and a single species, <i><a href="/wiki/Cenococcum_geophilum" title="Cenococcum geophilum">Cenococcum geophilum</a></i>, can form <a href="/wiki/Mycorrhiza" title="Mycorrhiza">mycorrhizal</a> associations with plant roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoBuglio1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoBuglio1996-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
