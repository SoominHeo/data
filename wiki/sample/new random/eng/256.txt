<p><b>Marvel Entertainment, LLC</b> (formerly <b>Marvel Enterprises</b> and <b>Toy Biz, Inc.</b> and marketed and stylized as <b>MARVEL</b>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_(law)" title="Company (law)">company</a> founded in June 1998, merging <b>Marvel Entertainment Group, Inc.</b> and <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Toys" title="Marvel Toys">ToyBiz</a>. The company is a wholly owned <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a>, and is mainly known for its <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Animation" title="Marvel Animation">Marvel Animation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Television" title="Marvel Television">Marvel Television</a> units. <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Studios" title="Marvel Studios">Marvel Studios</a>, formerly under the <i>Marvel</i> umbrella, became a subsidiary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Studios_(division)" title="The Walt Disney Studios (division)">The Walt Disney Studios</a>, where it develops and produces <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Cinematic_Universe" title="Marvel Cinematic Universe">a shared universe</a> that shares continuity with <a href="/wiki/List_of_Marvel_Cinematic_Universe_television_series" title="List of Marvel Cinematic Universe television series">shows produced by the television unit</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009, The Walt Disney Company acquired Marvel Entertainment for $4 billion;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMarvel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMarvel-2">[2]</a></sup> it has been a <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a> (LLC) since then. For financial reporting purposes, Marvel is reported as primarily a part of Disney's <a href="/wiki/Disney_Consumer_Products" title="Disney Consumer Products">Consumer Products</a> segment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the years, Marvel Entertainment has entered in several partnerships and negotiations with other companies across a variety of businesses. As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Marvel has film licensing agreements with <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>, and a theme park licensing agreement with <a href="/wiki/Universal_Parks_%26_Resorts" title="Universal Parks &amp; Resorts">Universal Parks &amp; Resorts</a> extant before Disney's acquisition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Aside from their contract with Universal, Marvel's characters and properties have also appeared at <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Parks_and_Resorts" title="Walt Disney Parks and Resorts">Walt Disney Parks and Resorts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Marvel Entertainment Group, Inc. (Marvel or MEG), incorporated on <span class="sortkey" style="display:none;speak:none">000000001986-12-02-0000</span><span style="white-space:nowrap">December 2, 1986</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mrvp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mrvp-6">[6]</a></sup> as the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Productions" title="Marvel Productions">Marvel Productions</a>, was put up for sale as part of the liquidation of its then parent corporation, <a href="/wiki/Cadence_Industries" title="Cadence Industries">Cadence Industries</a>, and sold in 1986 to <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pictures" title="New World Pictures">New World Pictures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-8">[8]</a></sup> On January 6, 1989, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Perelman" title="Ronald Perelman">Ronald Perelman</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacAndrews_%26_Forbes_Holdings" title="MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings">MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings</a> group of companies bought Marvel Entertainment Group from New World for $82.5 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> not including <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Productions" title="Marvel Productions">Marvel Productions</a>, which was folded into New World's TV and movie business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>"It is a mini-Disney in terms of intellectual property," said Perelman. "Disney's got much more highly recognized characters and softer characters, whereas our characters are termed action heroes. But at Marvel we are now in the business of the creation and marketing of characters."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwt_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cwt-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Going_Public_and_acquisition">Going Public and acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Going Public and acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Marvel made an initial public offer of 40% of the stock (ticker symbol NYSE:MRV) on July 15, 1991, giving $40 million from the proceeds to Andrews Group, Marvel's then direct parent corporation within MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fu-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel Entertainment Group then began expanding with acquisitions and forming new divisions. Marvel purchased the <a href="/wiki/Trading_card" title="Trading card">trading card</a> company <a href="/wiki/Fleer" title="Fleer">Fleer</a> on July 24, 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> On April 30, 1993, Marvel acquired 46% of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Toys" title="Marvel Toys">ToyBiz</a>, for the rights to make Marvel toys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> The Andrews Group named <a href="/wiki/Avi_Arad" title="Avi Arad">Avi Arad</a> of ToyBiz as the president and CEO of the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Studios" title="Marvel Studios">Marvel Films</a> division and of New World Family Filmworks, Inc., a <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pictures" title="New World Pictures">New World Entertainment</a> subsidiary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993 and 1994, Marvel's <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding companies</a> â Marvel Holdings, Inc. and Marvel Parent Holdings, Inc. â were formed between Andrews Group and MEG and issued over half a billion dollars in bonds under the direction of Perelman, secured by Marvel's rising stock, which was passed up in dividends to Perlman's group of companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbn-12">[12]</a></sup> Marvel continued making acquisitions with <a href="/wiki/Panini_Comics" title="Panini Comics">Panini</a>, an Italian sticker-maker on August 4, 1994 for $158.4 million, and <a href="/wiki/SkyBox_International" title="SkyBox International">SkyBox International</a> on March 8, 1995 for $150 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel also purchased <a href="/wiki/Heroes_World_Distribution" title="Heroes World Distribution">Heroes World Distribution</a>, a regional distributor to comic-book shops on Dec. 28, 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> Marvel's attempt to distribute its products directly led to a decrease in sales and aggravated the losses which Marvel suffered when the <a href="/wiki/Comic_book_collecting#The_speculator_boom" title="Comic book collecting">comic book bubble</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> popped, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994 Major League Baseball strike">1994 Major League Baseball strike</a> massacred the profits of the Fleer unit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReasonMag_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReasonMag-14">[14]</a></sup> and Panini, whose revenue depended largely on Disney licensing, was hobbled by poor Disney showings at the box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raviv38-39_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raviv38-39-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Studios_and_bankruptcy">Marvel Studios and bankruptcy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marvel Studios and bankruptcy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While licensing revenue reached $50 million in 1995, MEG laid off 275 employees on January 4, 1996,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> as losses for the 1995 year were $48.4 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> On November 12, 1996 Perelman offered to have the Andrews Group purchase additional shares with an issue for $350 million in November 1996 (the "Andrews Plan"), which would have required ToyBiz to become a wholly owned subsidiary of Marvel. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Icahn" title="Carl Icahn">Carl Icahn</a> began buying Marvel's bonds at 20% of their value and moved to block Perelman's plan. The Marvel group of companies filed for bankruptcy on December 27, 1996, but the noteholders, led by Icahn, initially blocked this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwt_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-cwt-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1996, Marvel created <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Studios" title="Marvel Studios">Marvel Studios</a>, an incorporation of Marvel Films, due to the sale of its film and TV sister company, <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pictures" title="New World Pictures">New World Communications Group</a>, to <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a>. Filing with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> to raise money to finance the new corporation, Marvel, Isaac Perlmutter's Zib, Inc. and Avi Arad sold <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Toys" title="Marvel Toys">Toy Biz</a> stocks, which Marvel had started and took public in February 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyse_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyse-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Icahn fought to take control of the company from Perelman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raviv38-39_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raviv38-39-15">[15]</a></sup> The court ruled on February 26, 1997 that Icahn could foreclose on a controlling interest in Marvel shares put up for collateral for the company's bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> On April 28, 1997, ToyBiz and Marvel agreed to a plan supported by Chase Bank that would merge the two companies, grant then lenders a $250 million loan proceeds, a 5-year, $170 million note, 28% of merged entity's equity and all stock in subsidiaries Fleer/SkyBox and Panini subsidiaries with Marvel shareholders receiving two sets of stock warrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Finally Icahn took control of Marvel's board and became Marvel's chairman on June 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> Bankruptcy proceedings continued with multi-way arguments among Perelman, Icahn, Toy Biz, and the banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwt_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-cwt-1">[1]</a></sup> A plan for reorganization agreed to by Icahn and the MEG's secured creditors fell apart on October 8 with the introduction of the better Toy Biz plan. The Bankruptcy Court on December 24 appointed a trustee to oversee the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1997, Marvel formed its Marvel Enterprise division, headed by president and CEO Scott C. Marden, to manage its trading card and sticker businesses, as well as Marvel Interactive, an <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>-entertainment and software-publishing company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tfl_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tfl-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>A lawsuit by bond holders and Marvel's litigation trustees was filed in 1997, accusing Perelman and other Marvel Board Directors of diverting $553.5 million in proceeds from 1993 and 1994 notes to other MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings' companies, prior to Marvel's 1996 bankruptcy. The lawsuit asked for $470.8 million in damages. The appellate court ruled that, âNone of the proceeds went to Marvel, or were used for Marvelâs benefitâ, but instead improperly enriched the directors. While denying any wrongdoing, Perelman agreed in August 2008 to settle for $80 million, which the trustees accepted. The settlement fund, after paying off trustees' and legal fees, administrative expenses and a $2 million loan, had $50 million to distribute to some Marvel Entertainment Group shareholders and unsecured creditors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbn_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbn-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 18, 1998, Marvel and co-owner <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Hollywood" title="Universal Studios Hollywood">Universal Studios Hollywood</a> opened Marvel Mania Restaurant near <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> in California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Planet_Hollywood" title="Planet Hollywood">Planet Hollywood</a>. However, Planet Hollywood had financial problems due to expanding too quickly, and closed Marvel Mania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another Toy Biz reorganization plan supported by the most creditors is submitted to the court on February 13, 1998. New York Stock Exchange stops trading of Marvel stock on April 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> Toy Biz owners <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ike_Perlmutter" title="Ike Perlmutter">Ike Perlmutter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avi_Arad" title="Avi Arad">Avi Arad</a>, with the banks on their side, snatched Marvel from Perelman and Icahn, in order to protect their own financial interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raviv38-39_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Raviv38-39-15">[15]</a></sup> Retailer and columnist <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Rozanski" title="Chuck Rozanski">Chuck Rozanski</a> estimated that Perelman made $200 to $400 million from Marvel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PerelTeam_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-PerelTeam-23">[23]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine believes he made nothing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DontMess_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DontMess-24">[24]</a></sup> and the judge in the Marvel bankruptcy trial estimated that Perelman made $280 million plus various tax advantages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raviv38-39_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Raviv38-39-15">[15]</a></sup> The judge ousted Icahn as Marvel's chairman in December 1997, naming a trustee to run Marvel while discussion continued between the various factions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwt_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-cwt-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Enterprises">Marvel Enterprises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marvel Enterprises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ToyBiz and Marvel Entertainment Group were merged into Marvel Enterprises to bring it out of bankruptcy in June 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwt_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-cwt-1">[1]</a></sup> In February 1999, Fleer/Skybox was sold to a corporation owned by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Grass" title="Alex Grass">Alex</a> and Roger Grass, a father and son, for US$30 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, the rights to names like "Spider-Man" were being challenged. Toy Biz hired an attorney to review its license agreement. Los Angeles patent attorney <a href="/wiki/Carole_E._Handler" title="Carole E. Handler">Carole E. Handler</a> found a legal loophole in the licensing of the Marvel name and was successful in reclaiming Marvel Enterprises' movie rights to its character Spider-Man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel Enterprise organized itself into four major units, Marvel Studios, Toy Biz, Licensing and Publishing, while in November 1999 adding Marvel Characters Group to manage Marvel's IP and oversee marketing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Bill Stine purchased back <a href="/wiki/Quest_Aerospace" title="Quest Aerospace">Quest Aerospace</a>, a 1995 Toy Biz acquisition, from Marvel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In summer 2003, Marvel places an offer for <a href="/wiki/Artisan_Entertainment" title="Artisan Entertainment">Artisan Entertainment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A new unit, Marvel International, was set up in London under a president, Bruno Maglione, to extend the company's operation and presence in major overseas markets in November 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wn_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-wn-32">[32]</a></sup> In December 2003, Marvel Entertainment acquired Cover Concepts from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst Communications, Inc.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awn0_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-awn0-33">[33]</a></sup> In November 2004, Marvel consolidated its children's sleepwear-apparel licensing business with American Marketing Enterprises, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2004, the corporation sued <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>-based NCSoft Corp. and <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>-based Cryptic Studios Inc. over possible trademark infringement in their <i>City of Heroes</i> massive multiplayer online game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Marvel settled a film-royalties lawsuit in April 2005 with its former editor-in-chief and publisher, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, paying him $10 million and negotiating an end to his royalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Entertainment">Marvel Entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marvel Entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In September 2005, Marvel Enterprises changed its name to Marvel Entertainment to reflect the corporation's expansion into financing <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Cinematic_Universe" title="Marvel Cinematic Universe">its own movie slate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, several <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee_Media" title="Stan Lee Media">Stan Lee Media</a> related groups filed lawsuits against Marvel Entertainment for $1 billion and for Lee's Marvel creations in multiple states most of which have been dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hr1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-hr1-38">[38]</a></sup> Additionally, a lawsuit over ownership of the character <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Rider_(comics)" title="Ghost Rider (comics)">Ghost Rider</a> was filed on March 30, 2007, by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Friedrich" title="Gary Friedrich">Gary Friedrich</a> and Gary Friedrich Enterprises, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Disney_subsidiary_.282009-present.29">Disney subsidiary (2009-present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvel_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disney subsidiary (2009-present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On August 31, 2009, <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> announced a deal to acquire Marvel Entertainment for $4.24 billion, with Marvel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shareholders" title="Shareholders">shareholders</a> to receive $30 and approximately 0.745 Disney shares for each share of Marvel they own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Disney_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Disney-40">[40]</a></sup> The voting occurred on December 31, 2009 and the merger was approved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMarvel_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DMarvel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The acquisition of Marvel was finalized hours after the shareholder vote, therefore giving Disney full ownership of Marvel Entertainment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The company was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delisted" title="Delisted">delisted</a> from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> under its ticker symbol (MVL), due to the closing of the deal.</p>
<p>On June 2, 2010 Marvel announced that it promoted <a href="/wiki/Joe_Quesada" title="Joe Quesada">Joe Quesada</a> to Chief Creative Officer of Marvel Entertainment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In June 2010, Marvel set up a <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Television" title="Marvel Television">television division</a> headed by <a href="/wiki/Jeph_Loeb" title="Jeph Loeb">Jeph Loeb</a> as executive vice president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dl_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-dl-44">[44]</a></sup> Three months later, <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Tinker" title="Smith &amp; Tinker">Smith &amp; Tinker</a> licensed from Marvel the character rights for a <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> digital collectible game for Facebook and Apple's mobile platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On October 1, 2010, Marvel moved its offices to a 60,000-square-foot (5,600Â m<sup>2</sup>) suite at 135 W. 50th Street, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, under a nine-year sublease contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee_Media" title="Stan Lee Media">Stan Lee Media</a>'s lawsuit against Marvel was dismissed again in February 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hr1_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-hr1-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2013, <a href="/wiki/Feld_Entertainment" title="Feld Entertainment">Feld Entertainment</a> agreed with Marvel to produce a <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe_Live!" title="Marvel Universe Live!">Marvel Character-based live arena show</a>. Marvel was also launching a new pop culture and lifestyle web show, âEarthâs Mightiest Showâ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-47">[47]</a></sup> On August 22, 2013, Marvel Entertainment announced that it was working with <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Experience#Hero_Ventures" title="Marvel Experience">Hero Ventures</a> on The <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Experience" title="Marvel Experience">Marvel Experience</a>, a traveling production/attraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ut_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-ut-48">[48]</a></sup> In April 2014, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Disneyland" title="Hong Kong Disneyland">Hong Kong Disneyland</a> announced the construction of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_Experience" title="Iron Man Experience">Iron Man Experience</a>, the first Marvel ride at any Disney theme park. It will be built on a location in the park's <a href="/wiki/Tomorrowland" title="Tomorrowland">Tomorrowland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 16, 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-courtdecision_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-courtdecision-50">[50]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> estate served notices of termination to <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Studios_(division)" title="Walt Disney Studios (division)">Walt Disney Studios</a>, <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Marvel sought to invalidate those claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In mid-March 2010 Kirby's estate "sued Marvel to terminate copyrights and gain profits from [Kirby's] comic creations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In July 2011, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">United States District Court for the Southern District of New York</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Summary_judgment" title="Summary judgment">summary judgment</a> in favor of Marvel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-courtdecision_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-courtdecision-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> which was affirmed in August 2013 by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Second_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The Kirby estate filed a petition on March 21, 2014 for a review of the case by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> but a settlement was reached on September 26, 2014 and the family requested that the petition be dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
