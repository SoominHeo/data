<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tail</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>tail</b> is the section at the rear end of an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>'s body; in general, the term refers to a distinct, flexible <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendage</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Torso" title="Torso">torso</a>. It is the part of the body that corresponds roughly to the <a href="/wiki/Sacrum" title="Sacrum">sacrum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coccyx" title="Coccyx">coccyx</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. While tails are primarily a feature of <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> including <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slug" title="Slug">slugs</a>, have tail-like appendages that are sometimes referred to as tails. Tailed objects are sometimes referred to as "caudate" and the part of the body associated with or proximal to the tail are given the adjective "caudal".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3343" data-file-width="5015" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG/220px-Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG/330px-Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG/440px-Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alopex_lagopus_IMG_9019.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulpes_lagopus" title="Vulpes lagopus">Vulpes lagopus</a></i> (Arctic fox) sleeping with its tail wrapped as a blanket.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Animal tails are used in a variety of ways. They provide a source of <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a> for <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and some other forms of <a href="/wiki/Marine_life" title="Marine life">marine life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake1983_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blake1983-1">[1]</a></sup> Many land animals use their tails to brush away <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> and other biting insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WALDBAUERWaldbauer2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WALDBAUERWaldbauer2009-2">[2]</a></sup> Some species, including <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo" title="Kangaroo">kangaroos</a>, use their tails for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">balance</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawson2003_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dawson2003-4">[4]</a></sup> and some, such as New World <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">opossums</a>, have what are known as <a href="/wiki/Prehensile_tail" title="Prehensile tail">prehensile tails</a>, which are adapted to allow them to grasp tree branches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tails are also used for social signaling. Some <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> species flash the white underside of their tails to warn other nearby deer of possible danger,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muller-Schwarze2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muller-Schwarze2012-6">[6]</a></sup> beavers slap the water with their tails to indicate danger,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson2008-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canids" title="Canids">canids</a> (including domestic <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>) indicate <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotions</a> through the positioning and movement of their tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorenHodgson2011_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorenHodgson2011-8">[8]</a></sup> Some species' tails are armored, and some, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a>, contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venom_(poison)" title="Venom (poison)">venom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson2002_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson2002-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some species of <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a> can <a href="/wiki/Autotomy" title="Autotomy">detach</a> ("cast") their tails from their bodies. This can help them to escape <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a>, which are either distracted by the wriggling, detached tail or left with only the tail while the lizard flees. Tails cast in this manner generally grow back over time, though the replacement is typically darker in colour than the original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiversMader2005_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiversMader2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most birds' tails end in long <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectrices" title="Rectrices">rectrices</a>. These feathers are used as a rudder, helping the bird steer and maneuver in <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">flight</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMSTOCK2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-COMSTOCK2010-11">[11]</a></sup> they also help the bird to balance while it is perched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burnie2008_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burnie2008-12">[12]</a></sup> In some species—such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds_of_paradise" title="Birds of paradise">birds of paradise</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyrebirds" title="Lyrebirds">lyrebirds</a>, and most notably <a href="/wiki/Peafowl" title="Peafowl">peafowl</a>—modified tail feathers play an important role in <a href="/wiki/Courtship#Courtship_in_the_animal_kingdom" title="Courtship">courtship</a> displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The extra-stiff tail feathers of other species, including <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpeckers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodcreeper" title="Woodcreeper">woodcreepers</a>, allow them to brace themselves firmly against tree trunks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McFarlane1994_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-McFarlane1994-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tails of grazing animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Tail_(horse)" title="Tail (horse)">horses</a>, are used both to sweep away insects and positioned or moved in ways that indicate the animal's physical or emotional state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OsborneBoyce2014_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-OsborneBoyce2014-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_tails">Human tails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human tails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Human <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryos</a> have a tail that measures about one-sixth of the size of the embryo itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As the embryo develops into a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>, the tail is absorbed by the growing body. Infrequently, a child is born with a ’"soft tail", which contains no vertebrae, but only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels">blood vessels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscles" title="Muscles">muscles</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerves" title="Nerves">nerves</a>, but this is regarded as an abnormality rather than a vestigial true tail, even when such an appendage is located where the tail would be expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Humans have a "tail bone" (the <a href="/wiki/Coccyx" title="Coccyx">coccyx</a>) attached to the pelvis, formed of fused <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a>, usually four, at the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_column" title="Vertebral column">vertebral column</a>. It does not protrude externally.</p>
