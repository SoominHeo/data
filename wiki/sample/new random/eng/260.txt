<p>The <b>Book of Joel</b> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>. Joel is part of a group of twelve prophetic books known as the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Minor_Prophets" title="Twelve Minor Prophets">Twelve Minor Prophets</a>. (The term indicates the short length of the text in relation longer prophetic texts known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_Prophets" title="Major Prophets">Major Prophets</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Book_of_Joel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After a superscription ascribing the prophecy to <a href="/wiki/Joel_(prophet)" title="Joel (prophet)">Joel</a> (son of Pethuel), the book may be broken down into the following sections:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lament over a great <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locust</a> plague and a severe drought (1:1–2:17)
<ul>
<li>The effects of these events on agriculture, farmers, and on the supply of agricultural offerings for the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a>, interspersed with a call to national lament. (1:1-20)</li>
<li>A more <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_literature" title="Apocalyptic literature">apocalyptic</a> passage comparing the locusts to an army, and revealing that they are God’s army. (2:1-11)</li>
<li>A call to national repentance in the face of God's judgment. (2:12-17)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Promise of future blessings (2:18-32 or 2:18–3:5)
<ul>
<li>Banishment of the locusts and restoration of agricultural productivity as a divine response to national penitence. (2:18-27)</li>
<li>Future prophetic gifts to all God’s people, and the safety of God’s people in the face of cosmic cataclysm. (2:28-32 or 3:1-5)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Coming judgment on God’s (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_Israel" title="People of Israel">Israel’s</a>) enemies and the vindication of Israel. (3:1-21 or 4:1-21)</li>
</ul>
<p>(Note: the Book of Joel's division verses differs greatly between editions of the Bible; some editions have three chapters, others four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oyc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oyc-1">[1]</a></sup>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Book_of_Joel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joel_(Michelangelo).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="894" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/220px-Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/330px-Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/440px-Joel_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joel_(Michelangelo).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Prophet Joel as imagined by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">Fresco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a> Ceiling, 1508–1512)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As there are no explicit references in the book to datable persons or events, scholars have assigned a wide range of dates to the book. The main positions are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Ninth century BC, particularly in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Jehoash_of_Judah" title="Jehoash of Judah">Joash</a> – a position especially popular among nineteenth-century scholars (making Joel one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Literary_prophets" title="Literary prophets">writing prophets</a>)</li>
<li>c. 630–587 BC, in the last decades of the kingdom of Judah (contemporary with <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezekiel" title="Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habakkuk" title="Habakkuk">Habakkuk</a>)</li>
<li>c. 520–500 BC, contemporary with the return of the exiles and the careers of <a href="/wiki/Zechariah_(Hebrew_prophet)" title="Zechariah (Hebrew prophet)">Zechariah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haggai" title="Haggai">Haggai</a>.</li>
<li>The decades around 400 BC, during the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> period (making him one of the latest writing prophets)</li>
</ul>
<p>Evidence produced for these positions are allusions in the book to the wider world, similarities with other prophets, and linguistic details. Other commentators, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> attach no great importance to the precise dating.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_interpretation">History of interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Book_of_Joel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The preservation of the book of Joel indicates that it was accorded special status by its contemporaries as “the word of the Lord” (1:1). Its history as part of the Jewish and Christian canons followed that of the entire scroll of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Prophets" title="Minor Prophets">Minor Prophets</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masoretic_text" title="Masoretic text">Masoretic text</a> places Joel between <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Hosea" title="Book of Hosea">Hosea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Amos" title="Book of Amos">Amos</a> (the order inherited by the Tanakh and Old Testament), while the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> order is Hosea–Amos–<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Micah" title="Book of Micah">Micah</a>–Joel–<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Obadiah" title="Book of Obadiah">Obadiah</a>–<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jonah" title="Book of Jonah">Jonah</a>. The Hebrew text of Joel seems to have suffered little from <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism" title="Textual criticism">scribal transmission</a>, but is at a few points supplemented by the Septuagint, <a href="/wiki/Peshitta" title="Peshitta">Syriac</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> versions, or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjectural_emendation" title="Conjectural emendation">conjectural emendation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While the book purports to describe a plague of locusts, some ancient Jewish opinion saw the locusts as <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> interpretations of Israel's enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This allegorical interpretation was applied to the church by many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_fathers" title="Church fathers">church fathers</a>. Calvin took a literal interpretation of ch.1, but allegorical view of chapter 2, a position echoed by some modern interpreters. Most modern interpreters, however, see Joel speaking of a literal locust plague given a prophetic/ <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_literature" title="Apocalyptic literature">apocalyptic</a> interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The traditional ascription of the whole book to the prophet Joel was challenged in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries by a theory of a three-stage process of composition: 1:1–2:27 were from the hand of Joel, and dealt with a contemporary issue; 2:28–3:21/3:1–4:21 were ascribed to a continuator with an apocalyptic outlook. Mentions in the first half of the book to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Lord" title="Day of the Lord">day of the Lord</a> were also ascribed to this continuator. 3:4-8/4:4-8 could be seen as even later. Details of exact ascriptions differed between scholars.</p>
<p>This splitting of the book’s composition began to be challenged in the mid-twentieth century, with scholars defending the unity of the book, the plausibility of the prophet combining a contemporary and apocalyptic outlook, and later additions by the prophet. The authenticity of 3:4–8 has presented more challenges, although a number of scholars still defend it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_quotes_and_allusions">Biblical quotes and allusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Book_of_Joel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biblical quotes and allusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many parallels of language between Joel and other Old Testament prophets. They may represent Joel’s literary use of other prophets, or vice versa.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, his prophecy of the outpouring of God′s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit_(Judaism)" title="Holy Spirit (Judaism)">Holy Spirit</a> upon all people was notably quoted by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a> sermon (<a class="external text" href="http://www.biblica.com/en-us/bible/online-bible/?osis=niv:Acts.2–2" rel="nofollow">Acts 2</a>).</p>
