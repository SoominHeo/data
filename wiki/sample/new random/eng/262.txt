<p><i><b>Tylosaurus</b></i> (Greek <i>τυλος</i>/<i>tylos</i> "protuberance, knob" + Greek <i>σαυρος</i>/<i>sauros</i> "lizard") was a <a href="/wiki/Mosasaur" title="Mosasaur">mosasaur</a>, a large, predatory marine lizard closely related to modern <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizards</a> and to snakes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tylosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1037" data-file-width="2452" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg/220px-Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg/330px-Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg/440px-Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_prorDB15.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>T. proriger</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A distinguishing characteristic of <i>Tylosaurus</i> is its elongated, cylindrical premaxilla (snout) from which it takes its name and which may have been used to ram and stun prey and also in intraspecific combat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Early restorations showing <i>Tylosaurus</i> with a dorsal crest were based on misidentified tracheal cartilage, but when the error was discovered, depicting mosasaurs with such crests was already a trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tylosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="1790" height="51" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tylosaurus.jpg/220px-Tylosaurus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tylosaurus.jpg/330px-Tylosaurus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tylosaurus.jpg/440px-Tylosaurus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>T. proriger</i> erroneously depicted with a dorsal crest and no tail fluke, by <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Knight" title="Charles R. Knight">Charles R. Knight</a>, 1899</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plesiosaur" title="Plesiosaur">plesiosaurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, fish, and other mosasaurs, <i>Tylosaurus</i> was a dominant predator of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a> during the Late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>. The genus was among the largest of the mosasaurs (along with <i><a href="/wiki/Mosasaurus" title="Mosasaurus">Mosasaurus hoffmannii</a></i>), with the possibly conspecific<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JVP2016_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-JVP2016-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Hainosaurus" title="Hainosaurus">Hainosaurus bernardi</a></i> reaching lengths of approximately 12.2 metres (40 ft), and <i>T. pembinensis</i> reaching comparable sizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>T. proriger</i>, the largest species of <i>Tylosaurus</i>, reached lengths in excess of 13 metres.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tylosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="401" data-file-width="1302" height="68" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg/220px-Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg/330px-Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg/440px-Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sharp_osborn_tylosaurus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Complete <i>T. proriger</i> skeleton, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.F._Osborn" title="H.F. Osborn">H.F. Osborn</a>'s 1899 description</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like many other mosasaurs, the early history of this <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> is complex and involves the <a href="/wiki/Bone_Wars" title="Bone Wars">infamous rivalry</a> between two early American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologists" title="Paleontologists">paleontologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">Edward Drinker Cope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Othniel_Charles_Marsh" title="Othniel Charles Marsh">Othniel Charles Marsh</a>. Originally, the name <i>"Macrosaurus" proriger</i> was proposed by Cope <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cope.2C_1869_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cope.2C_1869-5">[5]</a></sup> for a fragmentary skull and thirteen vertebrae collected from near Monument Rocks in western <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> in 1868. It was placed in the collections of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> Museum of Comparative Zoology. Only a year later, Cope redescribed the same material in greater detail and referred it, instead, to the English mosasaur taxon <i>Liodon</i>. Then, in 1872, Marsh named a more complete specimen as a new genus, <i>Rhinosaurus</i> ("nose lizard"), but soon discovered that this name had already been used for a different animal. Cope suggested that <i>Rhinosaurus</i> be replaced by yet another new name, <i>Rhamposaurus</i> which also proved to be preoccupied. Marsh finally erected <i>Tylosaurus</i> later in 1872, to include the original Harvard material as well as additional, more complete specimens which had also been collected from Kansas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh.2C_1872_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh.2C_1872-6">[6]</a></sup> A giant specimen of <i>T. proriger</i>, recovered in 1911 by C. D. Bunker near Wallace, Kansas is one of the largest skeleton of <i>Tylosaurus</i> ever found. It is currently on display at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a> Museum of Natural history.</p>

<p>In 1918, Charles H. Sternberg found a <i>Tylosaurus</i>, with the remains of a plesiosaur in its stomach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oceansofkansas.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-oceansofkansas.com-7">[7]</a></sup> The specimen is currently mounted in the United States National Museum (Smithsonian) and the plesiosaur remains are stored in the collections. Although these important specimens were briefly reported by C. H. Sternberg (1922), the information was lost to science until 2001. This specimen was rediscovered and described by Everhart (2004a). It is the basis for the story line in the 2007 National Geographic IMAX movie <i>Sea Monsters</i>, and a book by the same name (Everhart, 2007).</p>
<p>A photograph of a <i>Tylosaurus skull</i> was taken by <a href="/wiki/George_F._Sternberg" title="George F. Sternberg">George F. Sternberg</a> about 1926 after he collected and prepared the specimen. It was discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Smoky_Hill_Chalk" title="Smoky Hill Chalk">Smoky Hill Chalk</a> of Logan County, Kansas. Sternberg offered the specimen to the Smithsonian and included a photograph in his letter to Charles Gilmore. Copies of the original photos are in the archives of the Sternberg Museum of Natural History (FHSM). The specimen is FHSM VP-3, the exhibit specimen in the same museum.</p>
<p>A 34 feet (10 m) long <i>Tylosaurus</i> found in <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> by Alan Komrosky in 2009 is now on display at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_World_Treasures" title="Museum of World Treasures">Museum of World Treasures</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita</a>, Kansas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Though many species of <i>Tylosaurus</i> have been named over the years, only a few are now recognized by scientists as taxonomically valid. They are as follows: <i>Tylosaurus proriger</i> (Cope, 1869<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cope.2C_1869_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cope.2C_1869-5">[5]</a></sup>), from the <a href="/wiki/Santonian" title="Santonian">Santonian</a> and lower to middle <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">Campanian</a> of North America (Kansas, Alabama, Nebraska, etc.) and <i>Tylosaurus nepaeolicus</i> (Cope, 1874 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cope.2C_1874_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cope.2C_1874-9">[9]</a></sup>), from the Santonian of North America (Kansas). <i>Tylosaurus kansasensis</i>, named by Everhart in 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Everhart2005_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Everhart2005-10">[10]</a></sup> from the late <a href="/wiki/Coniacian" title="Coniacian">Coniacian</a> of Kansas, has been shown to be based on juvenile specimens of <i>T. nepaeolicus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nepaeolicus_char_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nepaeolicus_char-11">[11]</a></sup> It is likely that <i>T. proriger</i> evolved as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paedomorphic" title="Paedomorphic">paedomorphic</a> variety of <i>T. nepaeolicus</i>, retaining juvenile features into adulthood and attaining much larger adult size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nepaeolicus_char_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nepaeolicus_char-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A closely related genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Hainosaurus" title="Hainosaurus">Hainosaurus</a></i>, is known from the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> of North America and Europe. Both <i>Tylosaurus</i> and <i>Hainosaurus</i> members of the group Tylosaurinae <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williston.2C_1898_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williston.2C_1898-12">[12]</a></sup> and are referred to informally as "tylosaurines" or "tylosaurs." Research published in 2016 however indicates that <i>Hainosaurus</i> is likely congeneric with <i>Tylosaurus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JVP2016_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-JVP2016-1">[1]</a></sup> Bell <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_Jr.2C_1997_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell_Jr.2C_1997-13">[13]</a></sup> placed the tylosaurines together with the plioplatecarpine mosasaurs (<i>Platecarpus</i>, <i>Plioplatecarpus</i>, etc.) in an informal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> grouping which he called the "Russellosaurinae."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tylosaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_skin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="884" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/220px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/330px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/440px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_skin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Scales of <i>T. proriger</i> (KUVP-1075)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stomach contents associated with specimens of <i>Tylosaurus proriger</i> indicate that this ferocious mosasaur had a varied diet, including fish, sharks, smaller mosasaurs, plesiosaurs, and flightless diving birds such as <i><a href="/wiki/Hesperornis" title="Hesperornis">Hesperornis</a></i>. In some paleoenvironments, <i>Tylosaurus</i> seems to have preferred shallow, nearshore waters (as with the <a href="/wiki/Eutaw_Formation" title="Eutaw Formation">Eutaw Formation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mooreville_Chalk_Formation" title="Mooreville Chalk Formation">Mooreville Chalk Formation</a> of Alabama), while favoring deeper water farther out from shore in other environments (as with the Niobrara Chalk of the western U.S.).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="794" data-file-width="1192" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg/220px-Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg/330px-Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg/440px-Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knight_Tylosaurus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>T. proriger</i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Knight" title="Charles R. Knight">Charles R. Knight</a>, 1899</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talkeetna_Mountains_Hadrosaur" title="Talkeetna Mountains Hadrosaur">Talkeetna Mountains Hadrosaur</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Hadrosaurid" title="Hadrosaurid">hadrosaurid</a> of indeterminate classification whose carcass appeared to have been deposited in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathyal" title="Bathyal">bathyal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_shelf" title="Outer shelf">outer shelf</a> environment that later became Alaska's <a href="/wiki/Matanuska_Formation" title="Matanuska Formation">Matanuska Formation</a>. Every element of its skeleton not found in a <a href="/wiki/Concretion" title="Concretion">concretion</a> bore many closely spaced ovualar conical depressions ranging in diameter from 2.12–5.81 millimetres (0.083–0.229 in) and 1.64–3.62 millimetres (0.065–0.143 in) deep. These depressions are probably bite marks. The depressions are not symmetrical enough for gastropod drill marks and are not shaped like <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">sponge</a> borings. None of the preserved fish fossils of the Matanuska Formation fit the size or geometry of the borings. The size and spacing and shape by contrast, however, closely resembles the teeth of <i>Tylosaurus proriger</i>. The apparent tooth marks are unlikely to have occurred before the carcass was washed out to sea since that would have punctured the body, preventing the buildup of bloating gases that allowed the carcass to drift out to sea in the first place. The distribution of bite marks corresponds inversely to the presence of flesh in the animal. For instance, lower limb bones sustained the most damage because there was the least amount of flesh shielding the bones at those locations. The concretions formed as the flesh chemically reacted to the seafloor on the largest parts of the animal where the scavenging mosasaur would be unable to fully wrap its jaws around the carcass. Bones pulled free from the carcass were buried in the mud and preserved in mudstone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasche_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pasche-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
