<p><b>Georg Ernst Stahl</b> (22 October 1659<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> – 24 May 1734) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>. He was a supporter of vitalism, and until the late 18th century his works on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phlogiston" title="Phlogiston">phlogiston</a> were accepted as an explanation for chemical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-source_2-2">[2]</a></sup> He died in Berlin. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_Education">Life and Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ernst_Stahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born in St. John's Parish in <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> on October 21, 1659. His father was Johann Lorentz Stahl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-source_1-3">[3]</a></sup> He was raised in <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a>, which influenced his viewpoints on the world. His interests in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> were due to the influence a professor of medicine, Jacob Barner, and a chemist, Johann Kunckel von Löwenstjern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-source_3-4">[4]</a></sup> In the late 1670s, Stahl moved to <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Jena" title="Saxe-Jena">Saxe-Jena</a> to study medicine at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a>. Stahl’s success at Jena earned him a M.D. around 1683 and then he went on to teach at the same university.</p>
<p>Teaching at the university gained him such a good reputation that in 1687 he was hired as the personal physician to Duke Johann Ernst of Sachsen-Weimar. In 1693, he joined his old college friend <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hoffmann" title="Friedrich Hoffmann">Friedrich Hoffmann</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Halle" title="University of Halle">University of Halle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-source_3-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1694, he held the chair of medicine at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Halle" title="University of Halle">University of Halle</a>. From 1715 until his death, he was the physician and counselor to King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_I_of_Prussia" title="Friedrich Wilhelm I of Prussia">Friedrich Wilhelm I of Prussia</a> and in charge of Berlin's Medical Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-source_1-3">[3]</a></sup> He had two wives, who had died from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerperal_fever" title="Puerperal fever">puerperal fever</a> in 1696 and 1706. He also had a son Johnathan and a daughter who died in 1708.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-source_2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ernst_Stahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stahl's focus was on the distinction between the living and nonliving. Although he did not support the views of iatro-mechanists, he believed that all non-living creatures are mechanical and so are living things to a certain degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-source_1-3">[3]</a></sup> His views were that nonliving things are stable throughout time and did not rapidly change. On the other hand, living things are subject to change and have a tendency to decompose, which led Stahl to work with fermentation.</p>
<p>Stahl professed an <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animistic</a> system, in opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Boerhaave" title="Hermann Boerhaave">Hermann Boerhaave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hoffmann" title="Friedrich Hoffmann">Friedrich Hoffmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His main argument on living things was that there is an agent responsible for delaying this decomposition of living things and that agent is the <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">anima</a> or soul of the living organism. The anima controls all of the physical processes that happen in the body. It not only just controls the mechanical aspects of it but the direction and goals of them too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-source_2-2">[2]</a></sup> How the anima controls these processes is through motion. He believed that the three important motions of the body are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circulation_of_blood" title="Circulation of blood">circulation of blood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Excretion" title="Excretion">excretion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secretion</a>.</p>
<p>These beliefs were reflected in his views on medicine. He thought that medicine should deal with the body as a whole and its anima, rather than the specific parts of a body. Having knowledge on the specific mechanical parts of the body is not very useful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-source_2-2">[2]</a></sup> His views had been criticized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>, with whom he exchanged letters, later published in a book titled <i>Negotium otiosum seu σκιαμαχία</i> (1720).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Also, during the first part of the 18th century, Stahl's ideas on the non-physical part of the body were disregarded while his mechanistic ideas on the body were accepted in the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Boerhaave" title="Hermann Boerhaave">Hermann Boerhaave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hoffmann" title="Friedrich Hoffmann">Friedrich Hoffmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-source_4-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonic_Motion">Tonic Motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ernst_Stahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tonic Motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a physician, Stahl worked with patients and focused on the soul, or <i><a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">anima</a></i>, as well as blood circulation and tonic motion. <i>Anima</i> was a vital force that when working properly would allow the subject to be healthy; however, when malfunction of the <i>anima</i> occurred, so did illness. Tonic motion, to Stahl, involved the contracting and relaxing movements of the body tissue in order to serve the three main purposes. Tonic motion helped explain how animals produce <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> and how <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fevers</a> were caused. In Stahl's 1692 dissertation, <i>De motu tonico vitali,</i> Stahl explains his theory of tonic motion and how it is connected to blood flow within a subject, without citing <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>'s blood flow and circulation theories, which lacked an explanation of irregular blood flow. Also within the dissertation, 'practitioners' are mentioned as users of his theory of tonic motion.</p>
<p>Stahl's theory of <i>tonic motion</i> was about the <a href="/wiki/Muscle_tone" title="Muscle tone">muscle tone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>. During his work at Halle, Stahl oversaw patients experiencing <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nosebleed" title="Nosebleed">nosebleeds</a>. Tonic motion explained these phenomena as blood needed a natural or artificial path to flow when a part of the body is obstructed, injured, or swollen. Stahl also experimented with <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstruation</a>, finding that <a href="/wiki/Bloodletting" title="Bloodletting">bloodletting</a> in an upper portion of the body would relieve bleeding during the period. During the next period, the wound would experience pain and swelling, which would only be relieved by an opening in the foot. He also followed this procedure as a treatment for <a href="/wiki/Amenorrhoea" title="Amenorrhoea">amenorrhoea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ernst_Stahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The best of Stahl’s work in chemistry was done while he was a professor at Halle. Just like medicine, he believed that chemistry could not be reduced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanistic" title="Mechanistic">mechanistic</a> views. Although he believed in atoms, he did not believe that atomic theories were enough to describe the chemical processes that go on. He believed that atoms could not be isolated individually and that they join together to form elements. He took an empirical approach when establishing his descriptions of chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_3_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-source_3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stahl used the works of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Joachim_Becher" title="Johann Joachim Becher">Johann Joachim Becher</a> to help him come up with explanations of chemical phenomena. The main theory that Stahl got from J. J. Becher was the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phlogiston" title="Phlogiston">phlogiston</a>. This theory did not have any experimental basis before Stahl. He was able to make the theory applicable to chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_3_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-source_3-4">[4]</a></sup> Becher's theories attempted in explaining chemistry as comprehensively as seemingly possible through classifying different earths according to specific reactions. <i>Terra pinguis</i> was a substance that escaped during combustion reactions, according to Becher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Stahl, influenced by Becher's work, developed his theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phlogiston" title="Phlogiston">phlogiston</a>. Phlogiston theory did not have any experimental basis before Stahl worked with metals and various other substances in order separate phlogiston from them. Stahl proposed that metals were made of calx, or ash, and phlogiston and that once a metal is heated, the phlogiston leaves only the calx within the substance.He was able to make the theory applicable to chemistry as it was one of the first unifying theories in the discipline. Phlogiston provided an explanation of various chemical phenomena and encouraged the chemists of the time to rationally work with the theory to explore more of the subject. This theory was later replaced by Antoine-Laurent Lavoisier’s theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_1_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-source_1-3">[3]</a></sup> He also propounded a view of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation,</a> which in some respects resembles that supported by <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> a century and half later. Although his theory was replaced, Stahl's theory of phlogiston is seen to be the transition between <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> and chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_3_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-source_3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ernst_Stahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
