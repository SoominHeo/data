<p><b>Gearbox Software, LLC</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game development</a> company based in <a href="/wiki/Frisco,_Texas" title="Frisco, Texas">Frisco, Texas</a>. It was established in 1999 by developers from companies such as <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bethesda_Softworks" title="Bethesda Softworks">Bethesda Softworks</a>, with one of the founders, <a href="/wiki/Randy_Pitchford" title="Randy Pitchford">Randy Pitchford</a>, as CEO. The company initially created expansions for the <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Corporation</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(series)" title="Half-Life (series)">Half-Life</a></i>, then ported that game and others to console platforms. In 2005 Gearbox launched its first independent set of games, <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms_(video_game_series)" title="Brothers in Arms (video game series)">Brothers in Arms</a></i>, on console and mobile devices. It became their flagship franchise and spun off a comic book series, television documentary, books, and action figures. Their second original game series <i><a href="/wiki/Borderlands_(series)" title="Borderlands (series)">Borderlands</a></i> was released in 2009, and by 2015 had sold over 26 million copies. The company also owns the intellectual property of <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem" title="Duke Nukem">Duke Nukem</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld" title="Homeworld">Homeworld</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gearbox Software was founded on February 16, 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> by five members of the content team from the defunct developer Rebel Boat Rocker: <a href="/wiki/Randy_Pitchford" title="Randy Pitchford">Randy Pitchford</a>, Brian Martel, Stephen Bahl, Landon Montgomery, and Rob Heironimus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Before Rebel Boat Rocker, Pitchford and Martel previously worked together at <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a>, and Montgomery previously worked at <a href="/wiki/Bethesda_Softworks" title="Bethesda Softworks">Bethesda Softworks</a>.</p>
<p>They started with developing expansions to <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Software</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i>. Porting <i>Half-Life</i> to console platforms (each with new game content) followed, building the company's experience in console game-making, in addition to enhancing and building upon the successful <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike" title="Counter-Strike">Counter-Strike</a></i> branch of the <i>Half-Life</i> franchise. Prior to <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_2" title="Half-Life 2">Half-Life 2</a></i>, they had developed or helped develop every <i>Half-Life</i> expansion game or port, including <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Opposing_Force" title="Half-Life: Opposing Force">Opposing Force</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Blue_Shift" title="Half-Life: Blue Shift">Blue Shift</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Condition_Zero" title="Counter-Strike: Condition Zero">Counter-Strike: Condition Zero</a></i>, <i>Half-Life</i> for the Sony <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> (including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-Life_Decay" title="Half-Life Decay">Half-Life Decay</a></i>), and <i>Half-Life</i> for the Sega <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a> (including <i>Blue Shift</i>). Branching out to other publishers, they pursued additional <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">port</a> work, each game being released with additional content, but this time from console to PC. These projects included their first non-first-person shooter, <i><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hawk%27s_Pro_Skater_3" title="Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 3">Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 3</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Combat_Evolved" title="Halo: Combat Evolved">Halo: Combat Evolved</a></i>, forging new publisher relationships with <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Game_Studios" title="Microsoft Game Studios">Microsoft Game Studios</a> respectively. Additional new development, in the form of a PC game in the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> franchise (<i><a href="/wiki/James_Bond_007:_Nightfire" title="James Bond 007: Nightfire">James Bond 007: Nightfire</a></i>) for <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>, also occurred during the company's initial 5-year period.</p>
<p>In 2005, they launched an original property of their creation, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms_(series)" title="Brothers in Arms (series)">Brothers in Arms</a></i>, with the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Road_to_Hill_30" title="Brothers in Arms: Road to Hill 30">Brothers in Arms: Road to Hill 30</a></i> on the Xbox, PC and PlayStation 2. Later that year a sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Earned_in_Blood" title="Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood">Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood</a></i>, was launched. In 2008 <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Hell%27s_Highway" title="Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway">Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway</a></i> was released.</p>
<p>2007 brought announcements of new projects based on licensed film intellectual properties, including the crime drama <i><a href="/wiki/Heat_(1995_film)" title="Heat (1995 film)">Heat</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the science-fiction classic <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(franchise)" title="Alien (franchise)">Aliens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alien_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-alien-4">[4]</a></sup> In the September 2007 issue of <a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a>, a new game franchise was revealed, the sci-fi shooter <i><a href="/wiki/Borderlands_(video_game)" title="Borderlands (video game)">Borderlands</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> after which Gearbox <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEO" title="CEO">CEO</a> Randy Pitchford mentioned in an online interview that development on the <i>Heat</i> game had not yet begun, as the planned development partner for the project had gone under.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-heat-6">[6]</a></sup> This was followed by an announcement by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> that they would be helming a new version of rhythm game <i><a href="/wiki/Samba_de_Amigo" title="Samba de Amigo">Samba de Amigo</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>, a departure from their signature first-person shooter titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquiring_Duke_Nukem">Acquiring <i>Duke Nukem</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acquiring Duke Nukem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> announced its license of the <i>Aliens</i> franchise and a development deal with Gearbox Software to create <i><a href="/wiki/Aliens:_Colonial_Marines" title="Aliens: Colonial Marines">Aliens: Colonial Marines</a></i>. Also in 2008, Gearbox Software's CEO Randy Pitchford announced that the company was working on yet another major unannounced title, hinting that it was "huge".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On September 3, 2010, Gearbox announced that they are behind <i>Duke Nukem Forever</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2009, Allen Blum, the co-designer of <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_3D" title="Duke Nukem 3D">Duke Nukem 3D</a></i> and his development team are housed at Gearbox Software under the name of Triptych Games. The team worked on the game in their own homes before Gearbox Software decided to collaborate.</p>
<p>In June 2011, Duke Nukem Forever was released and received negative critical reception on release, with most of the criticism directed towards the unfinished, rushed state of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite the criticism the game topped the charts on release and made a profit for its distributor, Take-Two Interactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aliens:_Colonial_Marines_controversy"><i>Aliens: Colonial Marines</i> controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aliens: Colonial Marines controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2013, an anonymous source reported to Destructoid that Gearbox had been taking people and resources off <i>Aliens: Colonial Marines</i> to put them to work on <i><a href="/wiki/Borderlands_(video_game)" title="Borderlands (video game)">Borderlands</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever" title="Duke Nukem Forever">Duke Nukem Forever</a></i>, and yet was still collecting full payments from Sega as if they were working on <i>Aliens: Colonial Marines</i>. When Sega discovered this misconduct they canceled <i>Colonial Marines</i>, which led to the game's protracted development; "At some point in 2008, Sega temporarily pulled the plug on the game [...] They caught wind of Gearbox shifting resources despite still collecting milestone checks as if the team were full size and lying to Sega and 2k about the number of people working on each project. This led to the round of layoffs at Gearbox in late 2008."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game drew additional controversy due to the accusations that much of the game's development was not by Gearbox Software, but was outsourced to other developers in order to compensate for mismanagement on behalf of Gearbox. While Sega initially denied that any such outsourcing occurred, sources claimed that developers <a href="/wiki/Demiurge_Studios" title="Demiurge Studios">Demiurge Studios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nerve_Software" title="Nerve Software">Nerve Software</a> were responsible for the game's <a href="/wiki/Downloadable_content" title="Downloadable content">downloadable content</a>, while <a href="/wiki/TimeGate_Studios" title="TimeGate Studios">TimeGate Studios</a> was responsible for the majority of the game's campaign, and were unable to create the planned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_version" title="Beta version">Beta version</a> on schedule despite several delays. This caused the game to be rushed through redesigns, certification and shipping, despite being in a largely unfinished state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A class action lawsuit filed in April 2013 by Roger Damion Perrine and John Locke alleged that Gearbox and Sega falsely advertised <i>Aliens: Colonial Marines</i> by showing demos at trade shows, such as PAX and E3, that did not accurately represent the final product. Sega and the plaintiffs reached a settlement in late 2014, wherein Sega agreed to pay $1.25 million to the class. A motion for preliminary approval of the class settlement was pending as of January 2015. Gearbox has not agreed to settle, and the plaintiffs continue to litigate claims against the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 5, 2013, Sega confirmed that the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U" title="Wii U">Wii U</a> port of the game was cancelled due to poor reception of the Xbox 360 and PlayStation 3 versions of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Also in April, Gearbox acquired the <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld" title="Homeworld">Homeworld</a></i> franchise from <a href="/wiki/THQ" title="THQ">THQ</a> during its bankruptcy auction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-homeworldauction_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-homeworldauction-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2013, it was reported that TimeGate Studios filed for chapter 11 bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gearbox_Studio_Qu.C3.A9bec"><i>Gearbox Studio Québec</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gearbox Studio Québec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2015, video game news sites are reporting that Gearbox is opening a second development studio in Quebec City, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The studio will be run by former Volta Director <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastien_Caisse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sebastien Caisse (page does not exist)">Sebastien Caisse</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> Art Director <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre-Andre_Dery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre-Andre Dery (page does not exist)">Pierre-Andre Dery</a>. While little details about the studio's project(s) are revealed, it is announced that the team will grow to at least 100 and that it will develop original AAA titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The core team is built around former <i><a href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed" title="Assassin's Creed">Assassin's Creed</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Skylanders" title="Skylanders">Skylanders</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i> developers and <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Laval" title="Université Laval">Université Laval</a> IT staff.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_stories">Other stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2013, <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a> sued Gearbox for unpaid royalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In September 2013, 3DRealms dropped the suit with founder Scott Miller explaining it as a misunderstanding on their part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2013, Gearbox announced plans to rerelease <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld" title="Homeworld">Homeworld</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld_2" title="Homeworld 2">Homeworld 2</a></i> in high definition for modern PC platforms, in addition to making it available through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Distribution" title="Digital Distribution">digital distributors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2014, Gearbox filed a lawsuit against <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a> for attempting to make another <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem" title="Duke Nukem">Duke Nukem</a></i> game without the consent of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2014, Randy Pitchford formally contested the <i>Aliens: Colonial Marines</i> class action lawsuit stating the game had cost them millions of their own money and the advertising was solely the fault of the publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Half-Life"><i>Half-Life</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Half-Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(series)" title="Half-Life (series)">Half-Life (series)</a></div>
<p>Gearbox has developed a total of six games in the <i>Half-Life</i> series: the expansion packs <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Opposing_Force" title="Half-Life: Opposing Force">Opposing Force</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Blue_Shift" title="Half-Life: Blue Shift">Blue Shift</a></i>; ports of <i>Half-Life</i> for Dreamcast (which included <i>Blue Shift</i>) and <i>Half-Life</i> for PlayStation 2 (which included <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Decay" title="Half-Life: Decay">Half-Life: Decay</a></i>); they also did a large amount of work on both the retail release of <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike" title="Counter-Strike">Counter-Strike</a></i> and the main portion of <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Condition_Zero" title="Counter-Strike: Condition Zero">Counter-Strike: Condition Zero</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brothers_in_Arms"><i>Brothers in Arms</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Brothers in Arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms_(series)" title="Brothers in Arms (series)">Brothers in Arms (series)</a></div>
<p>During their fourth year, Gearbox began secretly working on their first internally driven and independently owned game: <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Road_to_Hill_30" title="Brothers in Arms: Road to Hill 30">Brothers in Arms: Road to Hill 30</a></i>. Developed for both <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> and Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> console, and built with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unreal_engine#Unreal_Engine_2" title="Unreal engine">Unreal Engine 2</a>, this game was released in March 2005. The sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Earned_in_Blood" title="Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood">Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood</a></i>, followed seven months later. The series was published by <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a>, who supported both games with <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> versions, and later worked with them to develop <i>Brothers in Arms</i> games for portable systems (<a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> home console.</p>
<p>In 2005, Gearbox licensed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unreal_engine#Unreal_Engine_3" title="Unreal engine">Unreal Engine 3</a> from <a href="/wiki/Epic_Games" title="Epic Games">Epic Games</a>, to replace the Unreal Engine 2 technology used in previous games, and grew its internal development teams to handle the demands of next-generation technology and content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Hell%27s_Highway" title="Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway">Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway</a></i> was the first new title to be announced, continuing the company's flagship franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Hell%27s_Highway" title="Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway">Brothers in Arms: Hell's Highway</a></i> was launched in September 2008. By 2008, the franchise also spun off a comic book series, a two-part television documentary, a line of action figures, and a novelization and non-fiction history book.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Borderlands_series"><i>Borderlands</i> series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Borderlands series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Borderlands_(series)" title="Borderlands (series)">Borderlands (series)</a></div>
<p>After the completion of <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms:_Earned_in_Blood" title="Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood">Brothers in Arms: Earned in Blood</a></i>, Gearbox began working on their second original game, <i><a href="/wiki/Borderlands_(video_game)" title="Borderlands (video game)">Borderlands</a></i>. First revealed in the September 2007 issue of <a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a>, <i>Borderlands</i> was described as "<i>Mad Max</i> meets <i>Diablo</i>", and its FPS-meets-RPG gameplay was revealed, along with screenshots of the early art style and the first three playable characters. The gaming press saw the game next at the European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GamesCon" title="GamesCon">GamesCon</a> in 2007, and again at GamesCon and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">E3</a> in 2008. In early 2009, it was revealed in <a href="/wiki/PC_Gamer" title="PC Gamer">PC Gamer</a> magazine that they had changed the graphical style and added the fourth player character. Released in 2009, <i>Borderlands</i> is billed as a "role playing shooter" (a <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> with <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing</a> elements), and demonstrates Gearbox's continued support for the Unreal Engine 3.</p>
<p>Following the unexpected<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> success of the first <i>Borderlands</i>, which sold between three<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borderlands_sells_3_million_units.3B_Pitchford_discusses_Gearbox_hiring_policy.2C_Gamertag_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borderlands_sells_3_million_units.3B_Pitchford_discusses_Gearbox_hiring_policy.2C_Gamertag-29">[29]</a></sup> to four-and-a-half million copies since release,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Take-Two_sales_sink_12.25_in_June_quarter_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Take-Two_sales_sink_12.25_in_June_quarter-30">[30]</a></sup> creative director Mike Neumann stated that there was a chance of a <i><a href="/wiki/Borderlands_2" title="Borderlands 2">Borderlands 2</a></i> being created, adding that the decision "seems like a no-brainer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On August 2, 2011, the game was officially confirmed and titled as <i>Borderlands 2</i>. The first look at the game was shown at Gamescom 2011, and an extensive preview was included in the September edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a></i> magazine, with <i>Borderlands 2</i> being the cover story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borderlands_2_coming_in_2012_with_new_characters_and_equipment_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borderlands_2_coming_in_2012_with_new_characters_and_equipment-32">[32]</a></sup> Like the first game, <i>Borderlands 2</i> was developed by Gearbox Software and published by 2K Games, running on a heavily modified version of <a href="/wiki/Epic_Games" title="Epic Games">Epic Games</a>' Unreal Engine 3. The game was released on September 18, 2012 in North America and was released on September 21, 2012 internationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duke_Nukem_series"><i>Duke Nukem</i> series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Duke Nukem series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever" title="Duke Nukem Forever">Duke Nukem Forever</a></div>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penny_Arcade_Expo" title="Penny Arcade Expo">Penny Arcade Expo</a> on September 3, 2010, it was announced that development of the long awaited <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever" title="Duke Nukem Forever">Duke Nukem Forever</a></i> will be continued by Gearbox after the project was abandoned by <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a> after 12 years, with Gearbox purchasing the intellectual property of the franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It was released by Take Two Interactive on June 10, 2011 internationally with a North American release on June 14.</p>
<p>In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wired.com" title="Wired.com">Wired.com</a> interview with Randy Pitchford, it was revealed that Allen Blum's development team Triptych Games have been brought into the office of Gearbox, making them a separate internal developer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_3D:_Reloaded" title="Duke Nukem 3D: Reloaded">Duke Nukem 3D: Reloaded</a></div>
<p>In the fall of 2010, Frederik Schreiber had started throwing around the idea of doing a <i>Duke Nukem 3D</i> remake. Schreiber then created a test map to give an idea of what it may look like, which he then took screenshots of and posted on the Gearbox forums. Shortly after posting the screenshots the images and the project made their way to various gaming sites causing a small buzz within the gaming community. He first contacted Gearbox Software, who told him to contact George Broussard and Scott Miller at 3D Realms. Schreiber proceeded to contact 3D Realms. The screenshots for the project were enough to convince Scott Miller to a certain degree about the project, but the game would need Take Two's permission for it to happen.</p>
<p>Schreiber again contacted Gearbox, hoping they would have a better relationship with Take Two than 3D Realms. After following the proper channels within Gearbox, he was able to get in contact with PJ Putnam, Vice President and General Counsel of Gearbox Software. Gearbox was interested in helping the project and Schreiber was eventually granted a "personal non-commercial license" to Duke Nukem.</p>
<p>Having received official permission to proceed, Schreiber officially announced the game on October 13, 2010, under the name <i>Duke Nukem Next-Gen</i>, revealing he had set up a small team to work with. It was also stated the game would be based on the Unreal Engine 3 and would not require any other game for it to run. On November 4, 2010, the game was officially renamed to <i>Duke Nukem 3D: Reloaded</i>.</p>
<p>The game has been put on an indefinite hold as of September 24, 2011, pending the resolution of differences between the Interceptor Entertainment team and Gearbox Software due to ambiguity on whether or not the finished product would actually be allowed to see release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 15, 2015 Gearbox confirmed that a new Duke Nukem was in Development and that concept images have been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duke_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duke-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 4, 2016 Gearbox officially announced Duke Nukem 3D: 20th Anniversary Edition World Tour. The game includes brand new levels developed in conjunction with some of the original developers, re-recorded lines by original Duke voice actor Jon St. John, and new music from original composer Lee Jackson. It released on October 11, 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battleborn"><i>Battleborn</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Battleborn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battleborn_(video_game)" title="Battleborn (video game)">Battleborn (video game)</a></div>
<p>Released in May 2016, <i>Battleborn</i> is a cooperative first-person shooter video game with <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_online_battle_arena" title="Multiplayer online battle arena">multiplayer online battle arena</a> (MOBA) elements. It takes place in a space fantasy setting where multiple races contest possession of the universe's last star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gearbox_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gearbox have developed using a number of existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_engines" title="Game engines">game engines</a> for various projects, including <a href="/wiki/GoldSrc" title="GoldSrc">GoldSrc</a>, <a href="/wiki/RenderWare" title="RenderWare">RenderWare</a>, Bungie's <a href="/wiki/Halo_(series)" title="Halo (series)">Halo</a>, and Unreal Engine. They have completed games on a variety of game platforms, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_Computer" title="Personal Computer">PC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>.</p>
<p>In 2006, they partnered with <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> to provide development computer systems and technology for their studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dell_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-dell-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2007, they purchased a Moven <a href="/wiki/Motion_capture" title="Motion capture">motion capture</a> system that uses non-optical intertia technology, to augment their existing Vicon optical motion capture system becoming one of the few independent developers with two in-house <a href="/wiki/Motion_capture" title="Motion capture">motion capture</a> capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2008, it was announced that they had licensed <a href="/wiki/NaturalMotion" title="NaturalMotion">NaturalMotion</a>'s Morpheme software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
