<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <b>E<sub>6</sub></b> is the name of some closely related <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">Lie groups</a>, linear <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_group" title="Algebraic group">algebraic groups</a> or their <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebras</a> <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<msub>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi mathvariant="fraktur">e</mi>
</mrow>
</mrow>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mn>6</mn>
</mrow>
</msub>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\mathfrak {e}}_{6}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\mathfrak  {e}}_{6}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f478652f321cdcdc6a3a0185a7beac54022adb" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.005ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span>, all of which have dimension 78; the same notation E<sub>6</sub> is used for the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_lattice" title="Root lattice">root lattice</a>, which has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rank_of_a_Lie_group" title="Rank of a Lie group">rank</a> 6. The designation E<sub>6</sub> comes from the Cartan–Killing classification of the complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_Lie_algebra" title="Simple Lie algebra">simple Lie algebras</a> (see <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan#Work" title="Élie Cartan">Élie_Cartan § Work</a>). This classifies Lie algebras into four infinite series labeled A<sub><i>n</i></sub>, B<sub><i>n</i></sub>, C<sub><i>n</i></sub>, D<sub><i>n</i></sub>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exceptional_simple_Lie_group" title="Exceptional simple Lie group">five exceptional cases</a> labeled E<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/E7_(mathematics)" title="E7 (mathematics)">E<sub>7</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/E8_(mathematics)" title="E8 (mathematics)">E<sub>8</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/F4_(mathematics)" title="F4 (mathematics)">F<sub>4</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/G2_(mathematics)" title="G2 (mathematics)">G<sub>2</sub></a>. The E<sub>6</sub> algebra is thus one of the five exceptional cases.</p>
<p>The fundamental group of the complex form, compact real form, or any algebraic version of E<sub>6</sub> is the <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_group" title="Cyclic group">cyclic group</a> <b>Z</b>/3<b>Z</b>, and its <a href="/wiki/Outer_automorphism_group" title="Outer automorphism group">outer automorphism group</a> is the cyclic group <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>. Its <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_representation" title="Fundamental representation">fundamental representation</a> is 27-dimensional (complex), and a basis is given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/27_lines_on_a_cubic_surface" title="27 lines on a cubic surface">27 lines on a cubic surface</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dual_representation" title="Dual representation">dual representation</a>, which is inequivalent, is also 27-dimensional.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>, E<sub>6</sub> plays a role in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unified_theory" title="Grand unified theory">grand unified theories</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_and_complex_forms">Real and complex forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E6_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Real and complex forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a unique complex Lie algebra of type E<sub>6</sub>, corresponding to a complex group of complex dimension 78. The complex adjoint Lie group E<sub>6</sub> of <a href="/wiki/Complex_dimension" title="Complex dimension">complex dimension</a> 78 can be considered as a simple real Lie group of real dimension 156. This has fundamental group <b>Z</b>/3<b>Z</b>, has maximal <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact</a> subgroup the compact form (see below) of E<sub>6</sub>, and has an outer automorphism group non-cyclic of order 4 generated by complex conjugation and by the outer automorphism which already exists as a complex automorphism.</p>
<p>As well as the complex Lie group of type E<sub>6</sub>, there are five real forms of the Lie algebra, and correspondingly five real forms of the group with trivial center (all of which have an algebraic double cover, and three of which have further non-algebraic covers, giving further real forms), all of real dimension 78, as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>The compact form (which is usually the one meant if no other information is given), which has fundamental group <b>Z</b>/3<b>Z</b> and outer automorphism group <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>.</li>
<li>The split form, EI (or E<sub>6(6)</sub>), which has maximal compact subgroup Sp(4)/(±1), fundamental group of order 2 and outer automorphism group of order 2.</li>
<li>The quasi-split form EII (or E<sub>6(2)</sub>), which has maximal compact subgroup SU(2) × SU(6)/(center), fundamental group cyclic of order 6 and outer automorphism group of order 2.</li>
<li>EIII (or E<sub>6(-14)</sub>), which has maximal compact subgroup SO(2) × Spin(10)/(center), fundamental group <b>Z</b> and trivial outer automorphism group.</li>
<li>EIV (or E<sub>6(-26)</sub>), which has maximal compact subgroup F<sub>4</sub>, trivial fundamental group cyclic and outer automorphism group of order 2.</li>
</ul>
<p>The EIV form of E<sub>6</sub> is the group of collineations (line-preserving transformations) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octonionic_projective_plane" title="Octonionic projective plane">octonionic projective plane</a> <b>OP</b><sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is also the group of determinant-preserving linear transformations of the exceptional <a href="/wiki/Jordan_algebra" title="Jordan algebra">Jordan algebra</a>. The exceptional Jordan algebra is 27-dimensional, which explains why the compact real form of E<sub>6</sub> has a 27-dimensional complex representation. The compact real form of E<sub>6</sub> is the <a href="/wiki/Isometry_group" title="Isometry group">isometry group</a> of a 32-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_manifold" title="Riemannian manifold">Riemannian manifold</a> known as the 'bioctonionic projective plane'; similar constructions for E<sub>7</sub> and E<sub>8</sub> are known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosenfeld_projective_plane" title="Rosenfeld projective plane">Rosenfeld projective planes</a>, and are part of the <a href="/wiki/Freudenthal_magic_square" title="Freudenthal magic square">Freudenthal magic square</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="E6_as_an_algebraic_group">E<sub>6</sub> as an algebraic group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E6_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: E6 as an algebraic group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By means of a <a href="/wiki/Chevalley_basis" title="Chevalley basis">Chevalley basis</a> for the Lie algebra, one can define E<sub>6</sub> as a linear algebraic group over the integers and, consequently, over any commutative ring and in particular over any field: this defines the so-called split (sometimes also known as “untwisted”) adjoint form of E<sub>6</sub>. Over an algebraically closed field, this and its triple cover are the only forms; however, over other fields, there are often many other forms, or “twists” of E<sub>6</sub>, which are classified in the general framework of <a href="/wiki/Galois_cohomology" title="Galois cohomology">Galois cohomology</a> (over a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_field" title="Perfect field">perfect field</a> <i>k</i>) by the set <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>k</i>, Aut(E<sub>6</sub>)) which, because the Dynkin diagram of E<sub>6</sub> (see <a href="#Dynkin_diagram">below</a>) has automorphism group <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>, maps to <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>k</i>, <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>) = Hom (Gal(<i>k</i>), <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>) with kernel <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>k</i>, E<sub>6,ad</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the field of real numbers, the real component of the identity of these algebraically twisted forms of E<sub>6</sub> coincide with the three real Lie groups mentioned <a href="#Real_and_complex_forms">above</a>, but with a subtlety concerning the fundamental group: all adjoint forms of E<sub>6</sub> have fundamental group <b>Z</b>/3<b>Z</b> in the sense of algebraic geometry, with Galois action as on the third roots of unity; this means that they admit exactly one triple cover (which may be trivial on the real points); the further non-compact real Lie group forms of E<sub>6</sub> are therefore not algebraic and admit no faithful finite-dimensional representations. The compact real form of E<sub>6</sub> as well as the noncompact forms EI=E<sub>6(6)</sub> and EIV=E<sub>6(-26)</sub> are said to be <i>inner</i> or of type <sup>1</sup>E<sub>6</sub> meaning that their class lies in <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>k</i>, E<sub>6,ad</sub>) or that complex conjugation induces the trivial automorphism on the Dynkin diagram, whereas the other two real forms are said to be <i>outer</i> or of type <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>.</p>
<p>Over finite fields, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lang%E2%80%93Steinberg_theorem" title="Lang–Steinberg theorem">Lang–Steinberg theorem</a> implies that <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>k</i>, E<sub>6</sub>) = 0, meaning that E<sub>6</sub> has exactly one twisted form, known as <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>: see <a href="#Chevalley_and_Steinberg_groups_of_type_E6_and_2E6">below</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_subalgebras_and_representations">Important subalgebras and representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E6_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Important subalgebras and representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lie algebra E<sub>6</sub> has an F<sub>4</sub> subalgebra, which is the fixed subalgebra of an outer automorphism, and an SU(3) × SU(3) × SU(3) subalgebra. Other maximal subalgebras which have an importance in physics (see below) and can be read off the Dynkin diagram, are the algebras of SO(10) × U(1) and SU(6) × SU(2).</p>
<p>In addition to the 78-dimensional adjoint representation, there are two dual <a href="/wiki/E8_(mathematics)#Subgroups" title="E8 (mathematics)">27-dimensional "vector" representations</a>.</p>
<p>The characters of finite dimensional representations of the real and complex Lie algebras and Lie groups are all given by the <a href="/wiki/Weyl_character_formula" title="Weyl character formula">Weyl character formula</a>. The dimensions of the smallest irreducible representations are (sequence <span class="nowrap"><a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A121737" title="oeis:A121737">A121737</a></span> in the <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">OEIS</a>):</p>
<dl>
<dd><u>1</u>, 27 (twice), <u>78</u>, 351 (four times), <u>650</u>, 1728 (twice), <u>2430</u>, <u>2925</u>, <u>3003 (twice)</u>, <u>5824 (twice)</u>, 7371 (twice), 7722 (twice), 17550 (twice), 19305 (four times), 34398 (twice), <u>34749</u>, <u>43758</u>, 46332 (twice), 51975 (twice), 54054 (twice), 61425 (twice), <u>70070</u>, <u>78975 (twice)</u>, <u>85293</u>, 100386 (twice), <u>105600</u>, 112320 (twice), <u>146432 (twice)</u>, <u>252252 (twice)</u>, 314496 (twice), 359424 (four times), <u>371800 (twice)</u>, 386100 (twice), 393822 (twice), 412776 (twice), <u>442442 (twice)</u>…</dd>
</dl>
<p>The underlined terms in the sequence above are the dimensions of those irreducible representations possessed by the adjoint form of E<sub>6</sub> (equivalently, those whose weights belong to the root lattice of E<sub>6</sub>), whereas the full sequence gives the dimensions of the irreducible representations of the simply connected form of E<sub>6</sub>.</p>
<p>The symmetry of the Dynkin diagram of E<sub>6</sub> explains why many dimensions occur twice, the corresponding representations being related by the non-trivial outer automorphism; however, there are sometimes even more representations than this, such as four of dimension 351, two of which are fundamental and two of which are not.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_representation" title="Fundamental representation">fundamental representations</a> have dimensions 27, 351, 2925, 351, 27 and 78 (corresponding to the six nodes in the <a href="#Dynkin_diagram">Dynkin diagram</a> in the order chosen for the <a href="#Cartan_matrix">Cartan matrix</a> above, i.e., the nodes are read in the five-node chain first, with the last node being connected to the middle one).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="E6_polytope">E6 polytope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E6_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: E6 polytope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/E6_polytope" title="E6 polytope">E<sub>6</sub> polytope</a></b> is the <a href="/wiki/Convex_hull" title="Convex hull">convex hull</a> of the roots of E<sub>6</sub>. It therefore exists in 6 dimensions; its <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> contains the <a href="/wiki/Coxeter_group" title="Coxeter group">Coxeter group</a> for E<sub>6</sub> as an <a href="/wiki/Index_of_a_subgroup" title="Index of a subgroup">index</a> 2 subgroup.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chevalley_and_Steinberg_groups_of_type_E6_and_2E6">Chevalley and Steinberg groups of type E<sub>6</sub> and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E6_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chevalley and Steinberg groups of type E6 and 2E6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B2E%E2%82%86" title="²E₆">²E₆</a></div>
<p>The groups of type <i>E</i><sub>6</sub> over arbitrary fields (in particular finite fields) were introduced by Dickson (<a href="#CITEREFDickson1901">1901</a>, <a href="#CITEREFDickson1908">1908</a>).</p>
<p>The points over a <a href="/wiki/Finite_field" title="Finite field">finite field</a> with <i>q</i> elements of the (split) algebraic group E<sub>6</sub> (see <a href="#E6_as_an_algebraic_group">above</a>), whether of the adjoint (centerless) or simply connected form (its algebraic universal cover), give a finite <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Lie_type" title="Group of Lie type">Chevalley group</a>. This is closely connected to the group written E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>), however there is ambiguity in this notation, which can stand for several things:</p>
<ul>
<li>the finite group consisting of the points over <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub> of the simply connected form of E<sub>6</sub> (for clarity, this can be written E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) or more rarely <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<li>(rarely) the finite group consisting of the points over <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub> of the adjoint form of E<sub>6</sub> (for clarity, this can be written E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>), and is known as the "adjoint" Chevalley group of type E<sub>6</sub> over <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub>), or</li>

<p>From the finite group perspective, the relation between these three groups, which is quite analogous to that between SL(<i>n,q</i>), PGL(<i>n,q</i>) and PSL(<i>n,q</i>), can be summarized as follows: E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is simple for any <i>q</i>, E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) is its <a href="/wiki/Schur_multiplier" title="Schur multiplier">Schur cover</a>, and E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) lies in its automorphism group; furthermore, when <i>q</i>−1 is not divisible by 3, all three coincide, and otherwise (when <i>q</i> is congruent to 1 mod 3), the Schur multiplier of E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is 3 and E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is of index 3 in E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>), which explains why E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) and E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) are often written as 3·E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) and E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>)·3. From the algebraic group perspective, it is less common for E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) to refer to the finite simple group, because the latter is not in a natural way the set of points of an algebraic group over <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub> unlike E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) and E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>).</p>
<p>Beyond this “split” (or “untwisted”) form of E<sub>6</sub>, there is also one other form of E<sub>6</sub> over the finite field <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub>, known as <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>, which is obtained by twisting by the non-trivial automorphism of the Dynkin diagram of E<sub>6</sub>. Concretely, <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>), which is known as a Steinberg group, can be seen as the subgroup of E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i><sup>2</sup>) fixed by the composition of the non-trivial diagram automorphism and the non-trivial field automorphism of <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i><sup>2</sup></sub>. Twisting does not change the fact that the algebraic fundamental group of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub> is <b>Z</b>/3<b>Z</b>, but it does change those <i>q</i> for which the covering of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub> by <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub> is non-trivial on the <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub>-points. Precisely: <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) is a covering of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>), and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) lies in its automorphism group; when <i>q</i>+1 is not divisible by 3, all three coincide, and otherwise (when <i>q</i> is congruent to 2 mod 3), the degree of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) over <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is 3 and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is of index 3 in <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>), which explains why <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) are often written as 3·<sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>)·3.</p>
<p>Two notational issues should be raised concerning the groups <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>). One is that this is sometimes written <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i><sup>2</sup>), a notation which has the advantage of transposing more easily to the Suzuki and Ree groups, but the disadvantage of deviating from the notation for the <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub>-points of an algebraic group. Another is that whereas <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) are the <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub>-points of an algebraic group, the group in question also depends on <i>q</i> (e.g., the points over <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i><sup>2</sup></sub> of the same group are the untwisted E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i><sup>2</sup>) and E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i><sup>2</sup>)).</p>
<p>The groups E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) and <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) are simple for any <i>q</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and constitute two of the infinite families in the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classification of finite simple groups</a>. <p>(sequence <span class="nowrap"><a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A008916" title="oeis:A008916">A008916</a></span> in the <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">OEIS</a>). The order of E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) or E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) (both are equal) can be obtained by removing the dividing factor gcd(3,<i>q</i>−1) from the first formula (sequence <span class="nowrap"><a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A008871" title="oeis:A008871">A008871</a></span> in the <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">OEIS</a>), and the order of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) or <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>) (both are equal) can be obtained by removing the dividing factor gcd(3,<i>q</i>+1) from the second (sequence <span class="nowrap"><a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A008915" title="oeis:A008915">A008915</a></span> in the <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">OEIS</a>).</p>
<p>The Schur multiplier of E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is always gcd(3,<i>q</i>−1) (i.e., E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) is its Schur cover). The Schur multiplier of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is gcd(3,<i>q</i>+1) (i.e., <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,sc</sub>(<i>q</i>) is its Schur cover) outside of the exceptional case <i>q</i>=2 where it is 2<sup>2</sup>·3 (i.e., there is an additional 2<sup>2</sup>-fold cover). The outer automorphism group of E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is the product of the diagonal automorphism group <b>Z</b>/gcd(3,<i>q</i>−1)<b>Z</b> (given by the action of E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>)), the group <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b> of diagram automorphisms, and the group of field automorphisms (i.e., cyclic of order <i>f</i> if <i>q</i>=<i>p<sup>f</sup></i> where <i>p</i> is prime). The outer automorphism group of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6</sub>(<i>q</i>) is the product of the diagonal automorphism group <b>Z</b>/gcd(3,<i>q</i>+1)<b>Z</b> (given by the action of <sup>2</sup>E<sub>6,ad</sub>(<i>q</i>)) and the group of field automorphisms (i.e., cyclic of order <i>f</i> if <i>q</i>=<i>p</i><sup><i>f</i></sup> where <i>p</i> is prime).</p>

<p><span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> = 8</span> <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">supergravity</a> in five dimensions, which is a <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_reduction" title="Dimensional reduction">dimensional reduction</a> from <span class="texhtml">11</span> dimensional supergravity, admits an <span class="texhtml">E<sub>6</sub></span> bosonic global symmetry and an <span class="texhtml">Sp(8)</span> bosonic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauge_symmetry" title="Gauge symmetry">local symmetry</a>. The fermions are in representations of <span class="texhtml">Sp(8)</span>, the gauge fields are in a representation of <span class="texhtml">E<sub>6</sub></span>, and the scalars are in a representation of both (Gravitons are <a href="/wiki/Singlet_state" title="Singlet state">singlets</a> with respect to both). Physical states are in representations of the coset <span class="texhtml">E<sub>6</sub>/Sp(8)</span>.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unification_theory" title="Grand unification theory">grand unification theories</a>, <span class="texhtml">E<sub>6</sub></span> appears as a possible gauge group which, after its <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_breaking" title="Symmetry breaking">breaking</a>, gives rise to <span class="texhtml">the SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1)</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauge_group" title="Gauge group">gauge group</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_model" title="Standard model">standard model</a> (also see <a href="/wiki/E8_(mathematics)#Importance_in_physics" title="E8 (mathematics)">Importance in physics of E8</a>). One way of achieving this is through breaking to <span class="texhtml">SO(10) × U(1)</span>. The adjoint <span class="texhtml">78</span> representation breaks, as explained above, into an adjoint <span class="texhtml">45</span>, spinor <span class="texhtml">16</span> and <span class="texhtml"><span style="text-decoration:overline;">16</span></span> as well as a singlet of the <span class="texhtml">SO(10)</span> subalgebra.
