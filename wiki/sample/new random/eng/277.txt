<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blanchard's transsexualism typology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Blanchard's transsexualism typology</b>, also <b>Blanchard autogynephilia theory</b> (<b>BAT</b>) and <b>Blanchard's taxonomy</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological_typology" title="Psychological typology">psychological typology</a> of male-to-female (MtF) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transsexualism" title="Transsexualism">transsexualism</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Blanchard" title="Ray Blanchard">Ray Blanchard</a> through the 1980s and 1990s, building on the work of his colleague, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Freund" title="Kurt Freund">Kurt Freund</a>. Blanchard divided <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans_women" title="Trans women">trans women</a> into two different groups: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual_transsexual" title="Homosexual transsexual">homosexual transsexuals</a>, whom Blanchard says seek <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a> to romantically and sexually attract (ideally heterosexual) men, and "autogynephilic transsexuals" who purportedly are sexually aroused at the idea of having a female body. The typology suggests distinctions between MtF transsexuals, but does not speculate on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Causes_of_transsexualism" title="Causes of transsexualism">causes of transsexualism</a>. The distinction is a recurring theme in scholarly literature on transsexualism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bancroft2009_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bancroft2009-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supporters of the theory include <a href="/wiki/Anne_Lawrence" title="Anne Lawrence">Anne Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Michael_Bailey" title="J. Michael Bailey">J. Michael Bailey</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Cantor" title="James Cantor">James Cantor</a>, and others who say that there are significant differences between the two proposed groups, including sexuality, age of transition, ethnicity, IQ, fetishism, and quality of adjustment. Under the theory, homosexual transsexuals are predicted to begin transitioning earlier in life,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onesixtytwo_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-onesixtytwo-2">[2]</a></sup> generally before turning 30, which accounts for their supposedly better adjustment. They are also more likely to come from poorer, non-white or immigrant backgrounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> have lower IQs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as well as be by definition exclusively attracted to men. Autogynephilic transsexuals are more likely to be attracted to women, exclusively or not, or to be <a href="/wiki/Asexuality" title="Asexuality">asexual</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onesixtytwo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-onesixtytwo-2">[2]</a></sup> They are also said under the theory to display more fetishistic or otherwise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphilic" title="Paraphilic">paraphilic</a> arousal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Criticism of the research and theory has come from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Allen_Moser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Allen Moser (page does not exist)">Charles Allen Moser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julia_Serano" title="Julia Serano">Julia Serano</a>, Jaimie Veale, Larry Nuttbrock, <a href="/wiki/John_Bancroft_(sexologist)" title="John Bancroft (sexologist)">John Bancroft</a>, and others who say that the theory is poorly representative of trans women, and reduces <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> to a matter of attraction. While Nuttbrock criticized it, he and his colleagues conducted research that fully supported the theory and replicated the most important findings of Blanchard's earlier work.</p>
<p>The theory has been the subject of protests in the <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> community, which peaked with the publication of Bailey's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Would_Be_Queen" title="The Man Who Would Be Queen">The Man Who Would Be Queen</a></i> in 2003. Following the publication of Bailey's book, Blanchard distinguished between the value of the theory as a behavioral description, versus as an explanation of transsexualism and that only further scientific research could resolve the latter question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bancroft2009_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bancroft2009-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Association_for_Transgender_Health" title="World Professional Association for Transgender Health">World Professional Association for Transgender Health</a> (WPATH) does not support the theory, citing a need for further research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpath1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wpath1-6">[6]</a></sup> WPATH also criticize the theory as being stigmatizing due to focusing on behavior instead of distress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpath2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wpath2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The early history of the study of transsexualism is sparse; however, the concept of a categorization of transsexual people can be seen as early as 1923 with the work of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Hirschfeld" title="Magnus Hirschfeld">Magnus Hirschfeld</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hirschfeld1923_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hirschfeld1923-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1966, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Benjamin" title="Harry Benjamin">Harry Benjamin</a> wrote that researchers of his day thought that attraction to men, while feeling that oneself is a woman, was the factor that distinguished a transvestite from a transsexual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benjamin1966_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-benjamin1966-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1980 in the DSM-III, a new diagnosis was introduced, that of "302.5 Transsexualism" under "Other Psychosexual Disorders". This was an attempt to provide a diagnostic category for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_identity_disorder" title="Gender identity disorder">gender identity disorders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-martin-10">[10]</a></sup> The diagnostic category, <i>transsexualism</i>, was for <a href="/wiki/Gender_dysphoria" title="Gender dysphoria">gender dysphoric</a> individuals who demonstrated at least two years of continuous interest in transforming their physical and social gender status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The subtypes were asexual, homosexual (same "biological sex"), heterosexual (other "biological sex") and unspecified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-martin-10">[10]</a></sup> This was removed in the DSM-IV, in which gender identity disorder replaced transsexualism. Previous taxonomies, or systems of categorization, used the terms <i>classic transsexual</i> or <i>true transsexual</i>, terms once used in <a href="/wiki/Differential_diagnosis" title="Differential diagnosis">differential diagnoses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benjamin1966_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-benjamin1966-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sexologist <a href="/wiki/Ray_Blanchard" title="Ray Blanchard">Ray Blanchard</a> coined the term <i>autogynephilia</i> in 1989 to describe MtF transsexuals who were sexually aroused by the idea of being women. He stated that while they lacked a specific term to describe the concept, there was evidence for the concept among clinicians of the early 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a> used the terms <i>eonism</i> and <i>sexo-aesthetic inversion</i> to describe similar cross-gender feelings and behaviors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard2005_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The clinical literature of Freund, Blanchard, and others has consistently divided male-to-female transsexuals into two distinct groups - "homosexual transsexuals," people who are sexually attracted to (ideally heterosexual) men and supposedly desire a normative female body to attract them, and "heterosexual fetishistic transvestites" in which the transsexual purportedly finds the idea of having a normative female body sexually arousing but desires a heterosexual female partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bancroft2009_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bancroft2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Kurt Freund first distinguished between these two proposed types in a 1982 research article, theorizing that the "homosexual" transsexuals were qualitatively different from the so-called "heterosexual males" with gender dysphoria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freund1982_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-freund1982-13">[13]</a></sup> Sexologist Ray Blanchard coined the term "autogynephilia" to describe this latter category of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bancroft2009_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bancroft2009-1">[1]</a></sup> In a 2005 article on the concept of Ray Blanchard noted that Freund was probably the first author to distinguish between the erotic arousal due to dressing as a woman (<a href="/wiki/Transvestism" title="Transvestism">transvestism</a>) versus erotic arousal due to physically transforming into a more typically female form (autogynephilia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard2005_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blanchard's observations at the Clarke Institute began by categorizing male-to-female transsexuals into four groups based off their supposed sexual orientations: "homosexual," "heterosexual," bisexual, and <a href="/wiki/Analloerotic" title="Analloerotic">analloerotics</a> (i.e. trans women attracted to men, women, both, or neither, respectively.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1985_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1985-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1989_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1989-15">[15]</a></sup> Blanchard then conducted a series of studies on people <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assigned_male_at_birth" title="Assigned male at birth">assigned male at birth</a> with gender dysphoria, including male-to-female transsexual people, and concluded that there existed only two distinct types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1985_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1985-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1989_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1989-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchardetal1987_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchardetal1987-16">[16]</a></sup> Blanchard said that one type of gender dysphoria/transsexualism manifests itself in individuals who are exclusively attracted to men (Homosexual transsexuals averaged a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinsey_Scale" title="Kinsey Scale">Kinsey Scale</a> measurement of 5-6 and six is the maximum, or a 9.86±2.37 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modified_Androphilia_Scale" title="Modified Androphilia Scale">Modified Androphilia Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawrence2005_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawrence2005-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leavitt1990_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-leavitt1990-18">[18]</a></sup>), whom he referred to as <i>homosexual transsexuals</i>, adopting Freund's terminology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchardetal1987_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchardetal1987-16">[16]</a></sup> The other type he defined as including those who are attracted to females (gynephilic), attracted to both males and females (bisexual), and attracted to neither males nor females (<i>analloerotic</i> or asexual); Blanchard referred to this latter set collectively as the <i>non-homosexual transsexuals</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1989class_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1989class-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1988_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1988-20">[20]</a></sup> Blanchard says that the "non-homosexual" transsexuals (but not the "homosexual" transsexuals) exhibit <i>autogynephilia</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchardetal1987_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchardetal1987-16">[16]</a></sup> which he defined as a <a href="/wiki/Paraphilia" title="Paraphilia">paraphilic</a> interest in having female anatomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1989_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1989-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1991_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1991-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey2003_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bailey2003-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autogynephilia_and_autoandrophilia">Autogynephilia and autoandrophilia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Autogynephilia and autoandrophilia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><b>Autogynephilia</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> αὐτό- ("self"), γυνή ("woman") and φιλία ("love") — "love of oneself as a woman") is a term coined in 1989 by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Blanchard" title="Ray Blanchard">Ray Blanchard</a>, to refer to "a man's paraphilic tendency to be <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">sexually aroused</a> by the thought or image of himself as a woman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1989_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1989-15">[15]</a></sup> Alternative terms proposed for this notion include <i>automonosexuality</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eonism" title="Eonism">eonism</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexo-aesthetic_inversion" title="Sexo-aesthetic inversion">sexo-aesthetic inversion</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LawsO.27Donohue2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-LawsO.27Donohue2008-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSM-IV-TR" title="DSM-IV-TR">DSM-IV-TR</a> includes an essentially equivalent definition, and recognizes autogynephilia as a common occurrence in the <a href="/wiki/Transvestic_fetishism" title="Transvestic fetishism">transvestic fetishism</a> disorder, but does not classify autogynephilia as a disorder by itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diagnostical_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders:_DSM-IV-TR:_4th_Edition_Text_Revision_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diagnostical_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders:_DSM-IV-TR:_4th_Edition_Text_Revision-25">[25]</a></sup> The paraphilias working group on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSM_5" title="DSM 5">DSM 5</a> included autogynephilia and autoandrophilia as subtypes of transvestic disorder, a proposal that was opposed by the <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Association_for_Transgender_Health" title="World Professional Association for Transgender Health">World Professional Association for Transgender Health</a> (WPATH), stating the lack of empirical evidence for the theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpath1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wpath1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpath2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wpath2-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Autogynephilia is most notable for its use in Blanchard's taxonomy to explain the presence of <a href="/wiki/Gender_dysphoria" title="Gender dysphoria">gender dysphoria</a> in "non-homosexual" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gynephilia" title="Gynephilia">gynephilic</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male-to-female_transsexual" title="Male-to-female transsexual">male-to-female transsexuals</a>, in contrast to the gender dysphoria observed in "homosexual" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Androphilia" title="Androphilia">androphilic</a>) transsexuals. Autogynephilia has also been suggested to pertain to romantic love as well as to sexual arousal patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LawrenceBecoming_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-LawrenceBecoming-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blanchard provides case examples to illustrate the autogynephilic sexual fantasies that people reported:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard2005_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Philip was a 38-year-old professional man referred to the author's clinic for assessment....Philip began masturbating at puberty, which occurred at age 12 or 13. The earliest sexual fantasy he could recall was that of having a woman's body. When he masturbated, he would imagine that he was a nude woman lying alone in her bed. His mental imagery would focus on his breasts, his vagina, the softness of his skin, and so on—all the characteristic features of the female physique. This remained his favorite sexual fantasy throughout his life.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Blanchard, "An autogynephile does not necessarily become sexually aroused every time he pictures himself as female or engages in feminine behavior, any more than a heterosexual man automatically gets an erection whenever he sees an attractive woman. Thus, the concept of autogynephilia—like that of heterosexuality, homosexuality, or pedophilia—refers to a <i>potential</i> for sexual excitation"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1991_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1991-21">[21]</a></sup> [emphasis in original].</p>
<p>Blanchard classified four subtypes of autogynephilic sexual fantasies, but noted that "All four types of autogynephilia tend to occur in combination with other types rather than alone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1991_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1991-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blanchard1993_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-blanchard1993-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Transvestic autogynephilia: arousal to the act or fantasy of wearing typically feminine clothing</li>
<li>Behavioral autogynephilia: arousal to the act or fantasy of doing something regarded as feminine</li>
<li>Physiologic autogynephilia: arousal to fantasies of body functions specific to people regarded as female</li>
<li>Anatomic autogynephilia: arousal to the fantasy of having a normative woman's body, or parts of one</li>
</ul>
<p>There also exist male assigned at birth people who report being sexually aroused by the image or idea of having <i>some</i> but not <i>all</i> normative female anatomy, such as having breasts but retaining their penis and testicles; Blanchard referred to this phenomenon as <i>partial autogynephilia.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>To test the possibility that natal females can also experience autogynephilia, Moser (2009) created an <i>Autogynephilia Scale for Women</i> based on items used to categorize MtF transsexuals as autogynephilic in other studies. A questionnaire that included the ASW was distributed to a sample of 51 professional women employed at an urban hospital; 29 completed questionnaires were returned for analysis. By the common definition of ever having erotic arousal to the thought or image of oneself as a woman, 93% of the respondents would be classified as autogynephilic. Using a more rigorous definition of "frequent" arousal to multiple items, 28% would be classified as autogynephilic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19591032_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19591032-31">[31]</a></sup> However, in a response to the paper, Lawrence (2009) criticized Moser's methodology and conclusions, and asserted that genuine autogynephilia occurs very rarely, if ever, in natal women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20069491_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20069491-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DSM-5">DSM-5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: DSM-5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/DSM-5" title="DSM-5">DSM-5</a>, published in 2013, <i>With autogynephilia</i> (sexual arousal by thoughts, images of self as a female) is a specifier to <b>302.3 <a href="/wiki/Transvestism" title="Transvestism">Transvestic disorder</a></b> (intense sexual arousal from cross-dressing fantasies, urges or behaviors); the other specifier is <i>With <a href="/wiki/Fetishism" title="Fetishism">fetishism</a></i> (sexual arousal to fabrics, materials or garments).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DSM-5-paraphillic_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-DSM-5-paraphillic-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autoandrophilia_and_autoandrophobia">Autoandrophilia and autoandrophobia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Autoandrophilia and autoandrophobia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The analogous term <i><b>autoandrophilia</b></i> refers to a person <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assigned_female_at_birth" title="Assigned female at birth">assigned female at birth</a> who is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">sexually aroused</a> by the thought or image of being a <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">man</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBSnews_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBSnews-34">[34]</a></sup> It was classified as a type of <a href="/wiki/Transvestic_fetishism" title="Transvestic fetishism">transvestic fetishism</a> in a proposed revision to the <a href="/wiki/DSM-5" title="DSM-5">DSM-5</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dsm5_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-dsm5-35">[35]</a></sup> but not included in the final version. Less work has been done on autoandrophilia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Vice_(magazine)" title="Vice (magazine)">Vice</a></i>, Blanchard stated "I proposed it simply in order not to be accused of sexism, because there are all these women who want to say women can rape too, women can be pedophiles too, women can be exhibitionists too. It’s a perverse expression of feminism, and so, I thought, let me jump the gun on this. I don’t think the phenomenon even exists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><b>Autoandrophobia</b></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> αὐτό- ("self"), ἀνήρ ("man") and φόβος ("fear") — "fear of oneself as a man") is a related but different term to autogynephilia which was coined by Moser (2010). Some MtF transsexuals in whom <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a> has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraindicated" title="Contraindicated">contraindicated</a> (e.g., due to <a href="/wiki/Deep_vein_thrombosis" title="Deep vein thrombosis">deep vein thrombosis</a>) have found that <a href="/wiki/Antiandrogen" title="Antiandrogen">antiandrogens</a> alone were sufficient to relieve their gender dysphoria. This suggests that MtF transsexuals are motivated to transition not merely by an interest in possessing feminine characteristics but also by a desire to block masculine characteristics. According to Moser, "the desire to block other sexual interests is not characteristic of individuals with a paraphilia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moser2010_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-moser2010-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Criticism of the theory of "homosexual transsexuals" is generally focused in two categories: the use of the terms "homosexual" and "non-homosexual" to refer to MtF transsexuals by their assigned sex<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bagemihl_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-bagemihl-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin-40">[40]</a></sup> and the data underlying the theory itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19591032_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19591032-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moser2010_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-moser2010-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veale2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-veale2008-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuttbrock2010_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuttbrock2010-42">[42]</a></sup> Harry Benjamin, in his seminal work <i>The Transsexual Phenomenon</i>, opined that the question "is a transsexual homosexual?" had both "yes" and "no" answers depending on whether sexual anatomy or <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> was prioritised, and in cases of post-operative male-to-female transsexuals, describing them as "homosexual men" was against "common sense and reason".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin-40">[40]</a></sup> The terminology has also been described as confusing and controversial among transsexuals seeking sexual reassignment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leavitt1990_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-leavitt1990-18">[18]</a></sup> archaic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wahng_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-wahng-43">[43]</a></sup> and demeaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiblum2000_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-leiblum2000-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2008 sexologist <a href="/wiki/John_Bancroft_(sexologist)" title="John Bancroft (sexologist)">John Bancroft</a> expressed regret for having used this terminology, which was standard when he used it, to refer to transsexual women, and that he now tries to use words more sensitively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bancroftcomment_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bancroftcomment-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles Allen Moser, a physician, criticized Blanchard's theory, stating that it uses an overly-broad definition of autogynephilia, is not sufficiently relevant to MtF transsexual patients, fails to account for all information on sexual and romantic interests of homosexual and transsexual people, relies too heavily on <a href="/wiki/Penile_plethysmograph" title="Penile plethysmograph">phallometry</a>, and lacks supporting data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moser2010_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-moser2010-38">[38]</a></sup> Jaimie Veale, a psychologist, published a modified version of the typology in 2010 which proposes that variances in gender identity are driven by biological and childhood socialization factors but personality and environment determine whether psychological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_mechanism" title="Defense mechanism">defense mechanisms</a> are employed to repress these variances. In the presence of defense mechanisms, the typology predicts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossdressing" title="Crossdressing">crossdressing</a> or "atypical transsexuality," while their absence results in <a href="/wiki/Drag_queen" title="Drag queen">drag artists</a> or "classic transsexuality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veale2010_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-veale2010-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Julia Serano, a male-to-female transgender biochemist and trans activist, wrote that there were flaws in Blanchard's original papers, including that they were conducted among overlapping populations primarily at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarke_Institute" title="Clarke Institute">Clarke Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> without nontranssexual controls, that the subtypes were not empirically derived but instead were "<a href="/wiki/Begging_the_question" title="Begging the question">begging the question</a> that transsexuals fall into subtypes based on their sexual orientation," and that further research had found a non-deterministic correlation between cross-gender arousal and sexual orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup> She said that Blanchard did not discuss the idea that cross-gender arousal may be an effect, rather than a cause, of gender dysphoria, and that Blanchard assumed that <a href="/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation" title="Correlation does not imply causation">correlation implied causation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup> Serano also stated that the wider idea of cross-gender arousal was affected by the prominence of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_objectification" title="Sexual objectification">sexual objectification</a> of women, accounting for both a relative lack of cross-gender arousal in transsexual men and similar patterns of autogynephilic arousal in non-transsexual women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup> Serano criticised proponents of the theory, claiming that they dismiss non-autogynephilic, non-androphilic transsexuals as misreporting or lying while not questioning androphilic transsexuals, describing it as "tantamount to hand-picking which evidence counts and which does not based upon how well it conforms to the model",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup> either making the theory unscientific due to its unfalsifiability, or invalid due to the nondeterministic correlation that later studies found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup> Further criticisms alleged that the theory undermined <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lived_experience&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lived experience (page does not exist)">lived experience</a> of transsexual women, contributed to pathologisation and sexualisation of transsexual women, and the literature itself fed into the stereotype of transsexuals as "purposefully deceptive", which could be used to justify discrimination and violence against transsexuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serano_47-5"><a href="#cite_note-serano-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Philosopher T. M. Bettcher, also drawing from her own experience as a trans woman, has critiqued the notion of "autogynephilia", and "target errors" generally, within a framework of "erotic structuralism", arguing that the notion conflates essential distinctions between "source of attraction" and "erotic content", and "(erotic) interest" and "(erotic) attraction", thus misinterpreting what she prefers to call, following Serano, "female embodiment eroticism". She maintains that not only is "an erotic interest in oneself as a gendered being", as she puts it, a non-pathological and indeed necessary component of regular sexual attraction to others, but within the framework of erotic structuralism, a "misdirected" attraction to oneself as postulated by Blanchard is outright nonsensical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bettcher2014_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bettcher2014-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transsexual_community_reaction">Transsexual community reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transsexual community reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept received attention when <a href="/wiki/Anne_Lawrence" title="Anne Lawrence">Anne Lawrence</a> described it on her website in the late 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawrence1998_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawrence1998-49">[49]</a></sup> When Bailey published <i>The Man Who Would Be Queen</i> in 2003, which based its portrayal of male-to-female transsexual people on Blanchard's taxonomy, an enormous controversy resulted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreger2008_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dreger2008-50">[50]</a></sup> Transsexual activist and engineer <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Conway" title="Lynn Conway">Lynn Conway</a> investigated the publication of Bailey's book by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a> and along with other activists accused Bailey of misconduct. <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a> investigated Bailey, but did not reveal the findings of that investigation and did not comment on whether or not Bailey had been punished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> According to a summary of the controversy written by Northwestern University <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioethicist" title="Bioethicist">bioethicist</a> and historian <a href="/wiki/Alice_Dreger" title="Alice Dreger">Alice Dreger</a>, the accusations were unfounded and comprised an attempt by some trans activists to silence Bailey for expressing views that contrasted with the public image they wanted. The accusations themselves did not hold up to scrutiny, according to Dreger's analysis. For example, two of the four transsexual women who accused Bailey of misusing their stories in the book were not actually mentioned in the book at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreger2008_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dreger2008-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Leavitt and Berger, "transsexuals, as a group, vehemently oppose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual_transsexual" title="Homosexual transsexual">homosexual transsexual</a> label and its pejorative baggage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leavitt1990_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-leavitt1990-18">[18]</a></sup> Trans man <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Devor" title="Aaron Devor">Aaron Devor</a> wrote, "If what we really mean to say is attracted to males, then say 'attracted to males' or androphilic... I see absolutely no reason to continue with language that people find offensive when there is perfectly serviceable, in fact better, language that is not offensive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lane2008_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-lane2008-52">[52]</a></sup> Still other transsexual people are opposed to any and all models of diagnosis which allow medical professionals to prevent anyone from changing their sex, and seek their removal from the DSM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shefer_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-shefer-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alice Dreger studied the reactions of trans activists and other controversies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2015_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2015-54">[54]</a></sup> She argued that although the science appears correct that eroticism is behind the typology of transgenderism, activists in the trans community preferred the simpler narrative of literally being one sex trapped in the body of the other. Dreger says, "Autogynephilia is perhaps best understood as a love that would really rather we didn’t speak its name",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2015_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2015-54">[54]</a></sup> in reference to the famous expression <i><a href="/wiki/The_love_that_dare_not_speak_its_name" title="The love that dare not speak its name">the love that dare not speak its name</a></i> formerly used to refer to homosexuality or <a href="/wiki/Pederasty" title="Pederasty">pederasty</a>.</p>
<p>Jaimie Veale, Dave Clark and Terri Lomax studied trans women's impressions of and opinions on Blanchard's typology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> They found that over half had negative views. Common criticisms included that the typology was too narrow and restrictive, it did not apply to their experiences, or that the typology was driven by questionable motives.</p>
