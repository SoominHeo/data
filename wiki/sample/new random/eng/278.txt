<p><b>Mimamsa</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAST" title="IAST">IAST</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṃsā</span></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word that means "reflection" or "critical investigation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britenc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-britenc-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also the name of one of six orthodox (<a href="/wiki/%C4%80stika_and_n%C4%81stika" title="Āstika and nāstika">astika</a>) schools of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>. The school is known for its philosophical theories on the nature of <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Hermeneutics" title="Hermeneutics">hermeneutics</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-olil-3">[3]</a></sup> The school was influential and foundational to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ved%C4%81nta" title="Vedānta">vedāntic</a> schools of Hinduism, with the difference that the Mīmāṃsā school (also known as <i>Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā</i> or <i>Karma-Mīmāṃsā</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup>) developed and emphasized <i>karma-kāṇḍa</i> (study of ritual actions, early four <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a>), while the Vedānta schools (also known as <i>Uttara-Mīmāṃsā</i>) developed and emphasized <i>jñana-kāṇḍa</i> (study of knowledge and spirituality, later parts of Vedas, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upani%E1%B9%A3ads" title="Upaniṣads">Upaniṣads</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-olil-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mīmāṃsā has several sub-schools, each defined by its <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>. The Prābhākara sub-school (from Prabhākara Miśra) considered five epistemically reliable means to gaining knowledge - <i><a href="/wiki/Pratyaksha" title="Pratyaksha">Pratyakṣha</a></i> (perception), <i>Anumāna</i> (inference), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upam%C4%81na" title="Upamāna">Upamāna</a></i> (comparison and analogy), <i>Arthāpatti</i> (postulation, derivation from circumstances), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9Aabda" title="Śabda">Śabda</a></i> (word, testimony of past or present reliable experts).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gflood_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gflood-6">[6]</a></sup> The Bhāṭṭa sub-school (from Kumārila Bhaṭṭa) added a sixth to its canon of reliable epistemology - <i>Anupalabdhi</i> (non-perception, negative/cognitive proof).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jag_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-jag-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The school of Mīmāṃsā consists of both <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theism" title="Theism">theistic</a> doctrines and the school showed little interest in systematic examination of the existence of God. Rather, it held that the soul is an eternal, omnipresent, inherently active spiritual essence, then focussed on the epistemology and metaphysics of <i>dharma</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To them, <i><a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a></i> meant rituals and social duties, not <i><a href="/wiki/Deva_(Hinduism)" title="Deva (Hinduism)">devas</a></i> (gods) because gods existed only in name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup> The Mīmāṃsakas held that Vedas are "eternal authorless infallible", that Vedic <i>vidhi</i> (injunctions) and <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">mantras</a> in rituals are prescriptive <i>kārya</i> (actions), and the rituals are of primary importance and merit. They considered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upani%E1%B9%A3ad" title="Upaniṣad">Upaniṣads</a> (later portions of Vedas) and other self-knowledge, spirituality-related texts as subsidiary, a philosophical view that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ved%C4%81nta" title="Vedānta">Vedānta</a> school of Hinduism disagreed with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-olil-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mīmāṃsā gave rise to the study of <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While their deep analysis of language and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic" title="Linguistic">linguistics</a> influenced other schools of Hinduism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> their views were not shared by others. Mīmāṃsakas considered the purpose and power of language was to clearly <i>prescribe</i> the proper, correct and right. In contrast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ved%C4%81nta" title="Vedānta">Vedāntins</a> extended the scope and value of language as a tool to also <i>describe</i>, <i>develop</i> and <i>derive</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup> Mīmāṃsakas considered orderly, law driven, procedural life as central purpose and noblest necessity of <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a> and society, and divine (theistic) sustenance means to that end.</p>
<p>The Mīmāṃsā school of Hinduism is a form of <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">realism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A key text of the Mīmāṃsā school is the <a href="/wiki/Purva_Mimamsa_Sutras" title="Purva Mimamsa Sutras">Mīmāṃsā Sūtra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jaimini" title="Jaimini">Jaimini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil2_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-olil2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mhiri_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mhiri-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mīmāṃsā (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a>: <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">मीमांसा</span>, also spelled Mimamsa) means "reflection, consideration, profound thought, investigation, examination, discussion" in Sanskrit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliamsm_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliamsm-14">[14]</a></sup> It also refers to the "examination of the Vedic text"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliamsm_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliamsm-14">[14]</a></sup> and to a school of Hindu philosophy that is also known as <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>Pūrva Mīmāṃsā</i></span> ("prior" inquiry, also <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>Karma-Mīmāṃsā</i></span>), in contrast to <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>Uttara Mīmāṃsā</i></span> ("posterior" inquiry, also <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>Jñana-Mīmāṃsā</i></span>) – the opposing school of <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">Vedanta</a>. This division is based on classification of the Vedic texts into <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">karmakāṇḍa</span></i>, the early sections of the Veda treating of mantras and rituals (<a href="/wiki/Samhita" title="Samhita">Samhitas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brahmana" title="Brahmana">Brahmanas</a>), and the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">jñānakāṇḍa</span></i> dealing with the meditation, reflection and knowledge of Self, Oneness, <a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olil_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-olil-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mhiri_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-mhiri-13">[13]</a></sup> Between the <i>Samhitas</i> and <i>Brahmanas</i>, the Mimamsa school places greater emphasis to the <a href="/wiki/Brahmana" title="Brahmana">Brahmanas</a> - the part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda">Vedas</a> that is a commentary on Vedic rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Donald Davis translates Mimamsa as the "desire to think", and in colloquial historical context as "how to think and interpret things".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DonaldRDavidJr2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldRDavidJr2010-16">[16]</a></sup> In the last centuries of the 1st-millennium BCE, the word Mīmāṃsā began to denote the thoughts on and interpretation of the Vedas, first as <i>Purva-Mimamsa</i> for rituals portions in the earlier layers of texts in the Vedas, and as <i>Uttara-Mimamsa</i> for the philosophical portions in the last layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DonaldRDavidJr2010_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldRDavidJr2010-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fxclooneyp25_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fxclooneyp25-17">[17]</a></sup> Over time, Purva-Mimamsa was just known as the Mimamsa school, and the Uttara-Mimamsa as the <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">Vedanta</a> school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fxclooneyp25_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-fxclooneyp25-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemology">Epistemology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Epistemology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, later Mimāṃsākas made some notable contributions. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Nyaya" title="Nyaya">Nyaya</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Vaisheshika" title="Vaisheshika">Vaisheshika</a> systems, the <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Prābhākara</span> sub-school of Mīmāṃsā recognizes five means of valid knowledge (Skt. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pram%C4%81%E1%B9%87a" title="Pramāṇa">pramāṇa</a></i>). The <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bhāṭṭa</span> sub-school of Mīmāṃsā recognizes one additional sixth, namely <i>anuapalabdhi</i>, just like <a href="/wiki/Advaita_Vedanta" title="Advaita Vedanta">Advaita Vedanta</a> school of Hinduism. These six epistemically reliable means of gaining knowledge are:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pratyaksa">Pratyaksa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pratyaksa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Main article : <a href="/wiki/Pratyaksha" title="Pratyaksha">Pratyaksha</a></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Pratyaksha" title="Pratyaksha">Pratyakṣa</a></i> (प्रत्यक्ष्य) means perception. It is of two types in Mimamsa and other schools of Hinduism: external and internal. External perception is described as that arising from the interaction of five senses and worldly objects, while internal perception is described by this school as that of inner sense, the mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kamal_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kamal-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The ancient and medieval Indian texts identify four requirements for correct perception:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kpmat_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-kpmat-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>Indriyarthasannikarsa</i> (direct experience by one's sensory organ(s) with the object, whatever is being studied), <i>Avyapadesya</i> (non-verbal; correct perception is not through <a href="/wiki/Hearsay" title="Hearsay">hearsay</a>, according to ancient Indian scholars, where one's sensory organ relies on accepting or rejecting someone else's perception), <i>Avyabhicara</i> (does not wander; correct perception does not change, nor is it the result of deception because one's sensory organ or means of observation is drifting, defective, suspect) and <i>Vyavasayatmaka</i> (definite; correct perception excludes judgments of doubt, either because of one's failure to observe all the details, or because one is mixing inference with observation and observing what one wants to observe, or not observing what one does not want to observe).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kpmat_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-kpmat-20">[20]</a></sup> Some ancient scholars proposed "unusual perception" as <i>pramana</i> and called it internal perception, a proposal contested by other Indian scholars. The internal perception concepts included <i>pratibha</i> (intuition), <i>samanyalaksanapratyaksa</i> (a form of induction from perceived specifics to a universal), and <i>jnanalaksanapratyaksa</i> (a form of perception of prior processes and previous states of a 'topic of study' by observing its current state).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Further, some schools of Hinduism considered and refined rules of accepting uncertain knowledge from <i>Pratyakṣa-pramana</i>, so as to contrast <i>nirnaya</i> (definite judgment, conclusion) from <i>anadhyavasaya</i> (indefinite judgment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anumana">Anumana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anumana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Main article : <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anumana" title="Anumana">Anumana</a></p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anum%C4%81%E1%B9%87a" title="Anumāṇa">Anumāṇa</a></i> (अनुमान) means inference. It is described as reaching a new conclusion and truth from one or more observations and previous truths by applying reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Observing smoke and inferring fire is an example of <i>Anumana</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kamal_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-kamal-18">[18]</a></sup> In all except one Hindu philosophies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> this is a valid and useful means to knowledge. The method of inference is explained by Indian texts as consisting of three parts: <i>pratijna</i> (hypothesis), <i>hetu</i> (a reason), and <i>drshtanta</i> (examples).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jl4647_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-jl4647-25">[25]</a></sup> The hypothesis must further be broken down into two parts, state the ancient Indian scholars: <i>sadhya</i> (that idea which needs to proven or disproven) and <i>paksha</i> (the object on which the <i>sadhya</i> is predicated). The inference is conditionally true if <i>sapaksha</i> (positive examples as evidence) are present, and if <i>vipaksha</i> (negative examples as counter-evidence) are absent. For rigor, the Indian philosophies also state further epistemic steps. For example, they demand <i>Vyapti</i> - the requirement that the <i>hetu</i> (reason) must necessarily and separately account for the inference in "all" cases, in both <i>sapaksha</i> and <i>vipaksha</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jl4647_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-jl4647-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A conditionally proven hypothesis is called a <i>nigamana</i> (conclusion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upamana">Upamana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Upamana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Main article : <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upamana" title="Upamana">Upamana</a></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Upam%C4%81%E1%B9%87a" title="Upamāṇa">Upamāṇa</a></i> (उपमान) means comparison and analogy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gflood_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-gflood-6">[6]</a></sup> Some Hindu schools consider it as a proper means of knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <i>Upamana</i>, states Lochtefeld,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jl721_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-jl721-29">[29]</a></sup> may be explained with the example of a traveller who has never visited lands or islands with endemic population of wildlife. He or she is told, by someone who has been there, that in those lands you see an animal that sort of looks like a cow, grazes like cow but is different from a cow in such and such way. Such use of analogy and comparison is, state the Indian epistemologists, a valid means of conditional knowledge, as it helps the traveller identify the new animal later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jl721_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-jl721-29">[29]</a></sup> The subject of comparison is formally called <i>upameyam</i>, the object of comparison is called <i>upamanam</i>, while the attribute(s) are identified as <i>samanya</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mw457_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-mw457-30">[30]</a></sup> Thus, explains <a href="/wiki/Monier_Monier-Williams" title="Monier Monier-Williams">Monier Williams</a>, if a boy says "her face is like the moon in charmingness", "her face" is <i>upameyam</i>, the moon is <i>upamanam</i>, and charmingness is <i>samanya</i>. The 7th century text <a href="/wiki/Bha%E1%B9%AD%E1%B9%ADik%C4%81vya" title="Bhaṭṭikāvya">Bhaṭṭikāvya</a> in verses 10.28 through 10.63 discusses many types of comparisons and analogies, identifying when this epistemic method is more useful and reliable, and when it is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mw457_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-mw457-30">[30]</a></sup> In various ancient and medieval texts of Hinduism, 32 types of <i>Upanama</i> and their value in epistemology are debated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthapatti">Arthapatti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arthapatti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthapatti" title="Arthapatti">Arthapatti</a></div>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arth%C4%81patti" title="Arthāpatti">Arthāpatti</a></i> (अर्थापत्ति) means postulation, derivation from circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gflood_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-gflood-6">[6]</a></sup> In contemporary logic, this <i>pramana</i> is similar to circumstantial <a href="/wiki/Modus_ponens" title="Modus ponens">implication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> As example, if a person left in a boat on river earlier, and the time is now past the expected time of arrival, then the circumstances support the truth postulate that the person has arrived. Many Indian scholars considered this <i>pramana</i> as invalid or at best weak, because the boat may have gotten delayed or diverted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, in cases such as deriving the time of a future sunrise or sunset, this method was asserted by the proponents to be reliable. Another common example for <i>arthapatti</i> found in the texts of Mīmāṃsā and other schools of Hinduism is, that if "Devadatta is fat" and "Devadatta does not eat in day", then the following must be true: "Devadatta eats in the night". This form of postulation and deriving from circumstances is, claim the Indian scholars, a means to discovery, proper insight and knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The Hindu schools that accept this means of knowledge state that this method is a valid means to conditional knowledge and truths about a subject and object in original premises or different premises. The schools that do not accept this method, state that postulation, extrapolation and circumstantial implication is either derivable from other <i>pramanas</i> or flawed means to correct knowledge, instead one must rely on direct perception or proper inference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anupalabdi">Anupalabdi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anupalabdi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Main article : <a href="/wiki/Anupalabdhi" title="Anupalabdhi">Anupalabdhi</a>, See also: <a href="/wiki/Abhava" title="Abhava">Abhava</a></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Anupalabdhi" title="Anupalabdhi">Anupalabdi</a></i> (अनुपलब्धि), accepted only by Kumarila Bhatta sub-school of Mīmāṃsā, means non-perception, negative/cognitive proof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eliottjag_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-eliottjag-35">[35]</a></sup> <i>Anupalabdhi pramana</i> suggests that knowing a negative, such as "there is no jug in this room" is a form of valid knowledge. If something can be observed or inferred or proven as non-existent or impossible, then one knows more than what one did without such means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In the two schools of Hinduism that consider <i>Anupalabdhi</i> as epistemically valuable, a valid conclusion is either <i>sadrupa</i> (positive) or <i>asadrupa</i> (negative) relation - both correct and valuable. Like other <i>pramana</i>, Indian scholars refined <i>Anupalabdi</i> to four types: non-perception of the cause, non-perception of the effect, non-perception of object, and non-perception of contradiction. Only two schools of Hinduism accepted and developed the concept "non-perception" as a <i>pramana</i>. The schools that endorsed <i>Anupalabdi</i> affirmed that it as valid and useful when the other five <i>pramanas</i> fail in one's pursuit of knowledge and truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ds_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ds-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Abhava" title="Abhava">Abhava</a></i> (अभाव) means non-existence. Some scholars consider <i>Anupalabdi</i> to be same as <i>Abhava</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup> while others consider <i>Anupalabdi</i> and <i>Abhava</i> as different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ds_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-ds-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <i>Abhava-pramana</i> has been discussed in ancient Hindu texts in the context of <i>Padartha</i> (पदार्थ, referent of a term). A <i>Padartha</i> is defined as that which is simultaneously <i>Astitva</i> (existent), <i>Jneyatva</i> (knowable) and <i>Abhidheyatva</i> (nameable).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chba_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-chba-39">[39]</a></sup> Specific examples of <i>padartha</i>, states Bartley, include <i>dravya</i> (substance), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guna" title="Guna">guna</a></i> (quality), <i>karma</i> (activity/motion), <i>samanya/jati</i> (universal/class property), <i>samavaya</i> (inherence) and <i>vishesha</i> (individuality). <i>Abhava</i> is then explained as "referents of negative expression" in contrast to "<a href="/wiki/Sense_and_reference" title="Sense and reference">referents</a> of positive expression" in <i>Padartha</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chba_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-chba-39">[39]</a></sup> An absence, state the ancient scholars, is also "existent, knowable and nameable", giving the example of negative numbers, silence as a form of testimony, <i>asatkaryavada</i> theory of causation, and analysis of deficit as real and valuable. <i>Abhava</i> was further refined in four types, by the schools of Hinduism that accepted it as a useful method of epistemology: <i>dhvamsa</i> (termination of what existed), <i>atyanta-abhava</i> (impossibility, absolute non-existence, contradiction), <i>anyonya-abhava</i> (mutual negation, reciprocal absence) and <i>pragavasa</i> (prior, antecedent non-existence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chba_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-chba-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sabda">Sabda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sabda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabda" title="Sabda">Sabda</a></div>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9Aabda" title="Śabda">Śabda</a></i> (शब्द) means relying on word, testimony of past or present reliable experts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpsb_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-dpsb-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eliottjag_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-eliottjag-35">[35]</a></sup> Hiriyanna explains <i>Sabda-pramana</i> as a concept which means reliable expert testimony. The schools of Hinduism which consider it epistemically valid suggest that a human being needs to know numerous facts, and with the limited time and energy available, he can learn only a fraction of those facts and truths directly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mhir_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-mhir-41">[41]</a></sup> He must rely on others, his parent, family, friends, teachers, ancestors and kindred members of society to rapidly acquire and share knowledge and thereby enrich each other's lives. This means of gaining proper knowledge is either spoken or written, but through <i>Sabda</i> (words).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mhir_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-mhir-41">[41]</a></sup> The reliability of the source is important, and legitimate knowledge can only come from the <i>Sabda</i> of reliable sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eliottjag_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-eliottjag-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mhir_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-mhir-41">[41]</a></sup> The disagreement between the schools of Hinduism has been on how to establish reliability. Some schools, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carvaka" title="Carvaka">Carvaka</a>, state that this is never possible, and therefore <i>Sabda</i> is not a proper pramana. Other schools debate means to establish reliability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_Vedanta_school">Relation to Vedanta school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relation to Vedanta school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An interesting feature of the Mimāṃsā school of philosophy is its unique epistemological theory of the intrinsic validity of all cognition as such. It is held that all knowledge is <i>ipso facto</i> true (Skt. <i>svataḥprāmāṇyavāda</i>). Thus, what is to be proven is not the truth of a cognition, but its falsity. The Mimāṃsākas advocate the self-validity of knowledge both in respect of its origin (<i>utpatti</i>) and ascertainment (<i>jñapti</i>). Not only did the Mimāṃsākas make the very great use of this theory to establish the unchallengeable validity of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda">Vedas</a>, but later Vedantists also drew freely upon this particular Mimāṃsā contribution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaphysics_and_beliefs">Metaphysics and beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Metaphysics and beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The core tenets of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>Pūrva Mīmāṃsā</i></span> are <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritualism</a> (<a href="/wiki/Orthopraxy" title="Orthopraxy">orthopraxy</a>), anti-asceticism and anti-mysticism. The central aim of the school is elucidation of the nature of <i><a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a></i>, understood as a set ritual obligations and prerogatives to be performed properly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atheism">Atheism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Atheism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mīmāṃsā theorists decided that the evidence allegedly proving the <a href="/wiki/Existence_of_God" title="Existence of God">existence of God</a> was insufficient. They argue that there was no need to postulate a maker for the world, just as there was no need for an author to compose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda">Vedas</a> or a God to validate the rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Mīmāṃsā argues that the Gods named in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda">Vedas</a> have no existence apart from the <i><a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">mantras</a></i> that speak their names. To that regard, the power of the mantras is what is seen as the power of Gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dharma"><i>Dharma</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dharma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a> as understood by Pūrva Mīmāṃsā can be loosely translated into English as "virtue", "morality" or "duty". The Pūrva Mīmāṃsā school traces the source of the knowledge of dharma neither to sense-experience nor inference, but to verbal <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a> (i.e. knowledge of words and meanings) according to Vedas. In this respect it is related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ny%C4%81ya" title="Nyāya">Nyāya</a> school, the latter, however, accepts only four sources of knowledge (<i>pramāṇa</i>) as valid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Pūrva Mīmāṃsā school held <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a> to be equivalent to following the prescriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Samhita" title="Samhita">Saṃhitās</a> and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hma%E1%B9%87a" title="Brāhmaṇa">Brāhmaṇa</a> commentaries relating the correct performance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaj%C3%B1a" title="Yajña">Vedic rituals</a>. Seen in this light, Pūrva Mīmāṃsā is essentially ritualist (<a href="/wiki/Orthopraxy" title="Orthopraxy">orthopraxy</a>), placing great weight on the performance of <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a> or action as enjoined by the Vedas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_Ved.C4.81nta">Relation to Vedānta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relation to Vedānta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emphasis of Yajnic Karmakāṇḍas in Pūrva Mīmāṃsā is erroneously interpreted by some to be an opposition to Jñānakāṇḍa of Vedānta and Upaniṣads. Pūrva Mīmāṃsā does not discuss topics related to Jñānakāṇḍa, such as salvation (<i>mokṣa</i>), but it never speaks against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mok%E1%B9%A3a" title="Mokṣa">mokṣa</a>. Vedānta quotes Jaimini's belief in Brahman as well as in mokṣa:</p>
<p>In Uttara-Mīmāṃsā or Vedānta (4.4.5-7), Bāḍarāyaṇa cites Jaimini as saying (ब्राह्मेण जैमिनिरूपन्यासादिभ्यः) "<i>(The mukta Puruṣa is united with the Brahman) as if it were like the Brahman, because descriptions (in Śruti etc) prove so</i>".</p>
<p>In Vedānta (1.2.28), Bāḍarāyaṇa cites Jaimini as saying that "There is no contradiction in taking Vaishvānara as the supreme Brahman".</p>
<p>In 1.2.31, Jaimini is again quoted by Bāḍarāyana as saying that the nirguna (attribute-less) Brahman can manifest itself as having a form.</p>
<p>In 4.3.12, Bādarāyana again cites Jaimini as saying that the mukta Purusha attains Brahman.</p>
<p>In Pūrva Mīmāṃsā too, Jaimini emphasises the importance of faith in and attachment to the Omnipotent Supreme Being Whom Jaimini calls "The Omnipotent Pradhaana" (The Main):</p>
<p>Pūrva Mīmāṃsā 6.3.1: "sarvaśaktau pravṛttiḥ syāt tathābhūtopadeśāt" (सर्वशक्तौ प्रवृत्तिः स्यात् तथाभूतोपदेशात्). The term <i>upadeśa</i> here means instructions of the śāstras as taught. We should tend towards the omnipotent supreme being. In the context of Pūrva Mīmāṃsā 6.3.1 shown above, next two sutras becomes significant, in which this Omnipotent Being is termed as "<a href="/wiki/Pradhana" title="Pradhana">pradhāna</a>", and keeping away from Him is said to be a "doṣa", hence all beings are asked to get related ("abhisambandhāt" in tadakarmaṇi ca doṣas tasmāt tato viśeṣaḥ syāt pradhānenābhisambandhāt; Jaimini 6, 3.3) to the "Omnipotent Main Being" (api vāpy ekadeśe syāt pradhāne hy arthanirvṛttir guṇamātram itarat tadarthatvāt; Jaimini 6, 3.2). Karma-Mīmāṃsā supports the Vedas, and Rgveda says that one Truth is variously named by the sages. It is irrelevant whether we call Him as Pradhāna or Brahman or Vaishvānara or Shiva or God.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school's origins lie in the scholarly traditions of the final centuries BCE, when the priestly ritualism of Vedic sacrifice was being marginalized by <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">Vedanta</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> To counteract this challenge, several groups emerged dedicated to demonstrating the validity of the Vedic texts by rigid formulation of rules for their interpretation. The school gathers momentum in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gupta_period" title="Gupta period">Gupta period</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9A%C4%81bara" title="Śābara">Śābara</a>, and reaches its apex in the 7th to 8th centuries with <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><a href="/wiki/Kum%C4%81rila_Bha%E1%B9%AD%E1%B9%ADa" title="Kumārila Bhaṭṭa">Kumārila Bhaṭṭa</a></span> and <a href="/wiki/Prabh%C4%81kara" title="Prabhākara">Prabhākara</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The school for some time in the Early Middle Ages exerted near-dominant influence on learned Hindu thought, and is credited as a major force contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Decline_of_Buddhism_in_India" title="Decline of Buddhism in India">decline of Buddhism in India</a>, but it has fallen into decline in the High Middle Ages and today is all but eclipsed by Vedanta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimamsa_texts">Mimamsa texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mimamsa texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foundational text for the Mīmāṃsā school is the <a href="/wiki/Purva_Mimamsa_Sutras" title="Purva Mimamsa Sutras">Purva Mīmāṃsā Sutras</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jaimini" title="Jaimini">Jaimini</a> (ca. 3rd to 1st century BCE). A major commentary was composed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9A%C4%81bara" title="Śābara">Śābara</a> in ca. the 5th or 6th century CE. The school reaches its height with <a href="/wiki/Kum%C4%81rila_Bha%E1%B9%AD%E1%B9%ADa" title="Kumārila Bhaṭṭa"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Kumārila Bhaṭṭa</span></a> and <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><a href="/wiki/Prabh%C4%81kara" title="Prabhākara">Prabhākara</a></span> (fl. ca. 700 CE). Both Kumarila Bhatta and Prabhākara (along with <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Murāri</span>, whose work is no more extant) have written extensive commentaries on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9A%C4%81bara" title="Śābara">Śābara</a>'s Mīmāṃsāsūtrabhāṣyam. Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, Mandana Miśra, Pārthasārathi Miśra, Sucarita Miśra, Ramakrishna Bhatta, Madhava Subhodini, Sankara Bhatta, Krsnayajvan, Anantadeva, Gaga Bhatta, Ragavendra Tirtha, VijayIndhra Tirtha, Appayya Dikshitar, Paruthiyur Krishna Sastri, Mahomahapadyaya Sri Ramsubba Sastri, Sri Venkatsubba Sastri, Sri A. Chinnaswami Sastri, Sengalipuram Vaidhyanatha Dikshitar were some of the Mimamsa Scholars.</p>
<p>The <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā Sūtra</span></i> of Jaimini (c. 3rd century BCE) has summed up the general rules of <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">nyāya</span></i> for Vedic interpretation. The text has 12 chapters, of which the first chapter is of philosophical value. The commentaries on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā Sūtra</span></i> by <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bhartṛmitra</span>, <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bhavadāsa</span>, Hari and <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Upavarṣa</span> are no more extant. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śabara</span> (c. 1st century BCE) is the first commentator of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā Sūtra</span></i>, whose work is available to us. His <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">bhāṣya</span></i> is the basis of all later works of <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> . <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Kumārila Bhaṭṭa</span> (7th century CE), the founder of the first school of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> commented on both the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Sūtra</span></i> and its <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śabara Bhāṣya</span></i>. His treatise consists of 3 parts, the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ślokavārttika</span></i>, the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Tantravārttika</span></i> and the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ṭupṭīkā</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Manḍana Miśra</span> (8th century CE) was a follower of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Kumārila</span>, who wrote <i>Vidhiviveka</i> and <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsānukramaṇī</span></i>. There are several commentaries on the works of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Kumārila</span>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Sucarita Miśra</span> wrote a <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Kāśikā</span></i> (commentary) on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ślokavārttika</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Someśvara Bhatta</span> wrote <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Nyāyasudhā</span></i>, also known as <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Rāṇaka</span></i>, a commentary on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Tantravārttika</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Pārthasarathi Miśra</span> wrote <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Nyāyaratnākara</span></i> (1300 CE), another commentary on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ślokavārttika</span></i>. He also wrote <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śāstradīpikā</span></i>, an independent work on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> and <i>Tantraratna</i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Venkaṭa Dīkṣita</span>’s <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vārttikabharaṇya</span></i> is a commentary on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ṭupṭīkā</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Prabhākara</span> (8th century CE), the originator of the second school of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> wrote his commentary <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bṛhatī</span></i> on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śabara Bhāṣya</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śālikanātha</span>’s <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ṛjuvimalā</span></i> (9th century CE) is a commentary on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bṛhatī</span></i>. His <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Prakaraṇapañcikā</span></i> is an independent work of this school and the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Pariśiṣṭa</span></i> is a brief explanation of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śabara Bhāṣya</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Bhavanātha</span>’s <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Nyāyaviveka</span></i> deals with the views of this school in details. The founder of the third school of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> was <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Murāri</span>, whose works have not reached us.</p>
<p><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Āpadeva</span> (17th century) wrote an elementary work on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i>, known as <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsānyāyaprakaśa</span></i> or <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Āpadevī</span></i>. <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Arthasaṁgraha</span></i> of <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Laugākṣi Bhāskara</span> is based on the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Āpadevī</span></i>. <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vedānta Deśika</span>’s <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śeśvara Mīmāṁsā</span></i> was an attempt to combine the views of the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Mīmāṁsā</span></i> and the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vedānta</span></i> schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
