<p><b>James William "Jimmy" Somerville</b> (born 22 June 1961) is a Scottish pop singer and songwriter. He sang in the 1980s with the pop groups <a href="/wiki/Bronski_Beat" title="Bronski Beat">Bronski Beat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communards_(band)" title="Communards (band)">The Communards</a>, and has also had a solo career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advocate_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-advocate-1">[1]</a></sup> He is known in particular for his <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> singing voice.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jimmy_Somerville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1983, Somerville co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Synthpop" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Bronski_Beat" title="Bronski Beat">Bronski Beat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cofounder_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cofounder-2">[2]</a></sup> which proceeded to have several hits in the British charts. Their biggest hit was "<a href="/wiki/Smalltown_Boy" title="Smalltown Boy">Smalltown Boy</a>" which reached No. 3 in the UK charts. In the music video Somerville played the song's titular character who leaves his hostile hometown for the friendlier city. This was a reflection of Somerville's own experience when he came to London in about 1980, and was able to enjoy the gay scene and also attend the London Gay Teenage Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Somerville left Bronski Beat in 1985 and formed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communards_(band)" title="Communards (band)">The Communards</a> with classically trained pianist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Coles" title="Richard Coles">Richard Coles</a> (now a <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> vicar and broadcaster).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vicar_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-vicar-4">[4]</a></sup> They had several hits, including a cover version of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Melvin_%26_the_Blue_Notes" title="Harold Melvin &amp; the Blue Notes">Harold Melvin &amp; the Blue Notes</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Leave_Me_This_Way" title="Don't Leave Me This Way">Don't Leave Me This Way</a>," which spent four weeks at No. 1 in the UK charts and became the biggest-selling single of 1986 in the UK. He also sang backing vocals on the <a href="/wiki/Fine_Young_Cannibals" title="Fine Young Cannibals">Fine Young Cannibals</a>' version of "<a href="/wiki/Suspicious_Minds" title="Suspicious Minds">Suspicious Minds</a>", which was a UK Top 10 hit.</p>
<p>The Communards split in 1988 and Somerville began a solo career the following year. He released his debut solo album <i><a href="/wiki/Read_My_Lips_(Jimmy_Somerville_album)" title="Read My Lips (Jimmy Somerville album)">Read My Lips</a></i> in November 1989, which contained three UK Top 30 hits, including a hit cover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sylvester_James" title="Sylvester James">Sylvester</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hi-NRG" title="Hi-NRG">hi-NRG</a> classic "<a href="/wiki/You_Make_Me_Feel_(Mighty_Real)" title="You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)">You Make Me Feel</a>" and a cover of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comment_te_dire_adieu%3F_(song)" title="Comment te dire adieu? (song)">Comment te dire adieu?</a>", a duet with <a href="/wiki/June_Miles-Kingston" title="June Miles-Kingston">June Miles-Kingston</a>, which reached number 14 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Singles_chart" title="UK Singles chart">UK Singles chart</a>. He also sang on the second <a href="/wiki/Band_Aid_(band)" title="Band Aid (band)">Band Aid</a> project at the end of 1989.</p>
<p>In November 1990, Somerville's "greatest hits" album <i>The Singles Collection 1984–1990</i> (which featured his hits with Bronski Beat and The Communards in addition to his own material) was released and reached number 4 on the UK Album Chart. It included a cover of the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>' hit song "<a href="/wiki/To_Love_Somebody_(song)" title="To Love Somebody (song)">To Love Somebody</a>", which also reached the UK Top 10. Also in 1990, Somerville contributed the song "<a href="/wiki/From_This_Moment_On_(Cole_Porter_song)" title="From This Moment On (Cole Porter song)">From This Moment On</a>" to the <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a> tribute album <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_%2B_Blue" title="Red Hot + Blue">Red Hot + Blue</a></i> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Organization" title="Red Hot Organization">Red Hot Organization</a>, the proceeds from which benefited AIDS research.</p>
<p>In 1991 Somerville provided backing vocals to a track called "Why Aren't You in Love With Me?" from the album "Ripe" by Communards offshoot band <a href="/wiki/Banderas_(duo)" title="Banderas (duo)">Banderas</a>. The Banderas duo, Caroline Buckley and Sally Herbert, had previously been part of Somerville's backing band. After this, he disappeared from the limelight for several years. He returned in 1995 with the album <i>Dare to Love,</i> which included "<a href="/wiki/Heartbeat_(Jimmy_Somerville_song)" title="Heartbeat (Jimmy Somerville song)">Heartbeat</a>" (a UK Top 30 hit and a No. 1 hit on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Play" title="Hot Dance Club Play">US dance chart</a>), "<a href="/wiki/Hurts_So_Good" title="Hurts So Good">Hurts So Good</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/By_Your_Side_(Jimmy_Somerville_song)" title="By Your Side (Jimmy Somerville song)">By Your Side</a>", though commercial success was now beginning to elude him and his contract with London Records to which he had been signed for over a decade came to an end.</p>
<p>A new single, "Dark Sky", was released in 1997 and peaked at No. 66 in the UK. His third album, entitled <i>Manage The Damage</i>, was released in 1999 via <a href="/wiki/Gut_Records" title="Gut Records">Gut Records</a>, though failed to chart. A companion remix album, <i>Root Beer</i>, came out in 2000. His dance-orientated fourth solo album, <i>Home Again</i>, was released in 2004, though again failed to chart.</p>
<p>May 2009 saw the release of Somerville's <i>Suddenly Last Summer</i> album, which contained acoustic interpretations of other people's songs. The album was initially only available as a digital download but in May 2010 was made available in a limited edition (3,000 copies) CD/DVD in the UK. In late 2010, Somerville released a dance EP called <i>Bright Thing</i>. He has also had an acting career, appearing in <a href="/wiki/Sally_Potter" title="Sally Potter">Sally Potter</a>'s 1992 film of <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando_(movie)" title="Orlando (movie)">Orlando</a></i>, in <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Julien" title="Isaac Julien">Isaac Julien</a>'s 1989 <i><a href="/wiki/Looking_for_Langston" title="Looking for Langston">Looking for Langston</a></i>, and in an episode of the cult science fiction television series <i><a href="/wiki/Lexx" title="Lexx">Lexx</a></i> ("Girltown").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>2010's EP <i>Bright Thing</i> was the first of a series of three, with Somerville releasing <i>Momentum</i> in 2011 and <i>Solent</i> in 2012, with long term collaborator John Winfield.</p>
<p>September 2014 saw the release of new single "Back to Me" followed by "Travesty", both from Somerville's new disco-inspired album 'Homage'. The emphasis on the recording of the new album has been on achieving the musical authenticity of original disco which Somerville grew up listening to. He stated 'I've finally made the disco album I always wanted to and never thought I could'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
