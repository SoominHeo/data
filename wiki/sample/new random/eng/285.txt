<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hydrogen line</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>hydrogen line</b>, <b>21-centimeter line</b> or <b>H I line</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral line</a> that is created by a change in the energy state of neutral <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms. This electromagnetic radiation is at the precise frequency of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009142040575176670♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">420</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">405</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">751</span>.7667<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.009 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength#in_non-vacuum_media" title="Wavelength">vacuum wavelength</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999211061140541999♠</span>21.106<span style="margin-left:.25em;">114</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">0542</span> <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">cm</a></span> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">free space</a>. This wavelength falls within the <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, and it is observed frequently in <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, since those <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> can penetrate the large clouds of interstellar <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> that are <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>.</p>
<p>The microwaves of the hydrogen line come from the atomic transition of an electron between the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperfine_levels" title="Hyperfine levels">hyperfine levels</a> of the hydrogen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principle_quantum_number" title="Principle quantum number">1</a><a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_quantum_number" title="Azimuthal quantum number">s</a> <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> that have an energy difference of ~ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000587433000000000♠</span>5.874<span style="margin-left:.25em;">33</span> µ<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyperphysics_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hyperphysics-3">[3]</a></sup> It is called the <i>spin-flip transition</i>. The frequency, <span class="texhtml"><i>ν</i></span>, of the <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a> that are emitted by this transition between two different energy levels is given by the <a href="/wiki/Planck%E2%80%93Einstein_relation" title="Planck–Einstein relation">Planck–Einstein relation</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>E</i> = <i>hν</i></span>. According to that relation, the <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a> of a 1,420,405,751.7667 Hz <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is ~ 5.87433 µeV. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constant_of_proportionality" title="Constant of proportionality">constant of proportionality</a>, <span class="texhtml"><i>h</i></span>, is known as the <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck constant</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">Hyperfine structure</a></div>
<p>The ground state of neutral hydrogen consists of an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> bound to a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>. Both the electron and the proton have intrinsic magnetic dipole moments ascribed to their <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>, whose interaction results in a slight increase in energy when the spins are parallel, and a decrease when antiparallel. The fact that only parallel and antiparallel states are allowed is a result of the quantum mechanical discretization of the total angular momentum of the system. When the spins are parallel, the magnetic dipole moments are antiparallel (because the electron and proton have opposite charge), thus one would expect this configuration to actually have <i>lower energy</i> just as two magnets will align so that the north pole of one is closest to the south pole of the other. This logic fails here because the wave functions of the electron and the proton overlap; that is, the electron is not spatially displaced from the proton, but encompasses it. The magnetic dipole moments are therefore best thought of as tiny current loops. As parallel currents attract, the parallel magnetic dipole moments (i.e., antiparallel spins) have lower energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The transition has an energy difference of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6975941171340287870♠</span>5.874<span style="margin-left:.25em;">33</span> µeV</span> that when applied in the Planck equation gives:</p>

<p>where <span class="texhtml"><i>h</i></span>, is the <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck constant</a> and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> is the speed of light.</p>
<p>This transition is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_line" title="Forbidden line">forbidden</a> with an extremely small transition rate of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6985290000000000000♠</span>2.9<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−15</sup> s<sup>−1</sup></span>, and a mean lifetime of the excited state of around 10 million years. A spontaneous occurrence of the transition is unlikely to be seen in a laboratory on Earth, but it can be artificially induced using a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_maser" title="Hydrogen maser">hydrogen maser</a>. It is commonly observed in astronomical settings such as <a href="/wiki/H_I_region" title="H I region">hydrogen clouds</a> in our galaxy and others. Owing to its long lifetime, the line has an extremely small natural width, so most broadening is due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">Doppler shifts</a> caused by bulk motion or nonzero temperature of the emitting regions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 1930s, it was noticed that there was a radio 'hiss' that varied on a daily cycle and appeared to be extraterrestrial in origin. After initial suggestions that this was due to the Sun, it was observed that the radio waves seemed to propagate from the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">centre of the Galaxy</a>. These discoveries were published in 1940 and were noted by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Oort" title="Jan Oort">Jan Oort</a> who knew that significant advances could be made in astronomy if there were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> in the radio part of the spectrum. He referred this to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hendrik_van_de_Hulst" title="Hendrik van de Hulst">Hendrik van de Hulst</a> who, in 1944, predicted that <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">neutral</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> could produce radiation at a <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of 1420.4058 MHz due to two closely spaced energy levels in the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a>.</p>
<p>The 21 cm line (1420.4 MHz) was first detected in 1951 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harold_Irving_Ewen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harold Irving Ewen (page does not exist)">Ewen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mills_Purcell" title="Edward Mills Purcell">Purcell</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and published after their data was corroborated by Dutch astronomers Muller and Oort,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and by Christiansen and Hindman in Australia. After 1952 the first maps of the neutral hydrogen in the Galaxy were made, and revealed for the first time the spiral structure of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_radio_astronomy">In radio astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In radio astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 21 cm spectral line appears within the <a href="/wiki/Radio_spectrum" title="Radio spectrum">radio spectrum</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/L_band" title="L band">L band</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF band</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_window" title="Microwave window">microwave window</a> to be exact). Electromagnetic energy in this range can easily pass through the Earth's atmosphere and be observed from the Earth with little interference.</p>
<p>Assuming that the hydrogen atoms are uniformly distributed throughout the galaxy, each line of sight through the galaxy will reveal a hydrogen line. The only difference between each of these lines is the doppler shift that each of these lines has. Hence, one can calculate the relative speed of each arm of our <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotation_curve" title="Rotation curve">rotation curve</a> of our <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> has been calculated using the 21 cm hydrogen line. It is then possible to use the plot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotation_curve" title="Rotation curve">rotation curve</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> to determine the distance to a certain point within the galaxy.</p>
<p>Hydrogen line observations have also been used indirectly to calculate the mass of galaxies, to put limits on any changes over time of the universal <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a> and to study the dynamics of individual galaxies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_cosmology">In cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In cosmology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The line is of great interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_bang" title="Big bang">big bang</a> <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmology</a> because it is the only known way to probe the "dark ages" from <a href="/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology)" title="Recombination (cosmology)">recombination</a> to <a href="/wiki/Reionization" title="Reionization">reionization</a>. Including the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>, this line will be observed at frequencies from 200 MHz to about 9 MHz on Earth. It potentially has two applications. First, by <a href="/wiki/Intensity_mapping" title="Intensity mapping">mapping the intensity</a> of redshifted 21 centimeter radiation it can, in principle, provide a very precise picture of the <a href="/wiki/Matter_power_spectrum" title="Matter power spectrum">matter power spectrum</a> in the period after recombination. Second, it can provide a picture of how the universe was reionized, as neutral hydrogen which has been ionized by radiation from stars or quasars will appear as holes in the 21 cm background.</p>
<p>However, 21 cm observations are very difficult to make. Ground based experiments to observe the faint signal are plagued by interference from television transmitters and the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, so they must be made from very secluded sites care taken to eliminate interference. Space based experiments, even on the far side of the Moon (where they would be sheltered from interference from terrestrial radio signals), have been proposed to compensate for this. Little is known about other effects, such as <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron emission</a> and free–free emission on the galaxy. Despite these problems, 21 cm observations, along with space-based gravity wave observations, are generally viewed as the next great frontier in observational cosmology, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#Polarization" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background polarization</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_terrestrial_remote_sensing">In terrestrial remote sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In terrestrial remote sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_Moisture_and_Ocean_Salinity_satellite" title="Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity satellite">Soil Moisture &amp; Ocean Salinity</a> (SMOS) satellite's main scientific instrument <a href="/wiki/Microwave_Imaging_Radiometer_with_Aperture_Synthesis" title="Microwave Imaging Radiometer with Aperture Synthesis">Microwave Imaging Radiometer with Aperture Synthesis</a> (MIRAS) uses the 1400–1427 MHz frequencies (including 1420.406 MHz) to monitor the ocean surface salinity and the soil moisture of the Earth. The choice of the H I band results from much better radiative signature of salinity and moisture in microwave than at higher frequencies, and no electromagnetic interference from anthropogenic sources, as H I is reserved for radioastronomy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_uses_for_SETI">Possible uses for SETI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible uses for SETI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_plaque" title="Pioneer plaque">Pioneer plaque</a>, attached to the <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">Pioneer 11</a> <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, portrays the hyperfine transition of neutral hydrogen and used the wavelength as a standard scale of measurement. For example, the height of the woman in the image is displayed as eight times 21 cm, or 168 cm. Similarly the frequency of the hydrogen spin-flip transition was used for a unit of time in a map to Earth included on the Pioneer plaques and also the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> probes. On this map, the position of the Sun is portrayed relative to 14 <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> whose rotation period circa 1977 is given as a multiple of the frequency of the hydrogen spin-flip transition. It is theorized by the plaque's creators that an advanced civilization would then be able to use the locations of these pulsars to locate the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> at the time the spacecraft were launched.</p>
<p>The 21 cm hydrogen line is considered a favorable frequency by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SETI" title="SETI">SETI</a> program in their search for signals from potential extraterrestrial civilizations. In 1959, Italian physicist <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Cocconi" title="Giuseppe Cocconi">Giuseppe Cocconi</a> and American physicist <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morrison" title="Philip Morrison">Philip Morrison</a> published "Searching for Interstellar Communications", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_paper" title="Academic paper">paper</a> proposing the 21 cm hydrogen line and the potential of microwaves in the search for interstellar communications. According to George Basalla, the paper by Cocconi and Morrison "provided a reasonable theoretical basis" for the then nascent SETI program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Similarly, <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9C%D0%B0%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9,_%D0%9F%D1%91%D1%82%D1%80_%D0%92%D0%B0%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" title="ru:Маковецкий, Пётр Васильевич">Pyotr Makovetsky</a> proposed SETI use a frequency which is equal to either:</p>

Such a signal would not be overwhelmed by the H I line itself, or by any of its harmonics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
