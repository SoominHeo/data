<p><b>William Dudley Haywood</b> (February 4, 1869 – May 18, 1928), better known as <b>"Big Bill" Haywood</b>, was a founding member and leader of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">Industrial Workers of the World</a> (IWW) and a member of the executive committee of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">Socialist Party of America</a>. During the first two decades of the 20th century, he was involved in several important labor battles, including the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Labor_Wars" title="Colorado Labor Wars">Colorado Labor Wars</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Textile_Strike" title="Lawrence Textile Strike">Lawrence Textile Strike</a>, and other textile strikes in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.</p>
<p>Haywood was an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">industrial unionism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-1">[1]</a></sup> a labor philosophy that favors organizing all workers in an industry under one union, regardless of the specific trade or skill level; this was in contrast to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Craft_union" title="Craft union">craft unions</a> that were prevalent at the time, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">AFL</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cahn-137-169_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cahn-137-169-2">[2]</a></sup> His belief that workers of all ethnicities should be united also clashed with many unions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-337-339_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-337-339-3">[3]</a></sup> His preference for <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> over political tactics alienated him from the Socialist Party leadership and contributed to his recall from the party's executive committee in 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolgoff_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dolgoff-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Never one to shy from violent conflicts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolgoff_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dolgoff-4">[4]</a></sup> Haywood was frequently the target of prosecutors. His trial for the murder of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Steunenberg" title="Frank Steunenberg">Frank Steunenberg</a> in 1907 (of which he was acquitted) drew national attention; in 1918, he was one of 101 <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">IWW</a> members convicted of violating the <a href="/wiki/Espionage_Act_of_1917" title="Espionage Act of 1917">Espionage Act of 1917</a> during the <a href="/wiki/First_Red_Scare" title="First Red Scare">First Red Scare</a>. In 1921, while out of prison during an appeal of his conviction, Haywood fled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> <a href="/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic" title="Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic">Russia</a>, where he spent the remaining years of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speak_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-speak-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>William D. Haywood was born in 1869 in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah_Territory" title="Utah Territory">Utah Territory</a>. His father, a former <a href="/wiki/Pony_Express" title="Pony Express">Pony Express</a> rider, died of pneumonia when Haywood was three years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> His maternal grandfather was an Afrikaner born in the Orange Free State in 1820. At age nine, he injured his right eye while <a href="/wiki/Whittling" title="Whittling">whittling</a> a slingshot with a knife, permanently blinding his right eye. Haywood never had his damaged eye replaced with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_eye" title="Glass eye">glass eye</a>; when photographed, he would turn his head to show his left profile. That same year, he began working in the <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a>, never having received much formal education. After brief stints as a <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboy</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homestead_Act" title="Homestead Act">homesteader</a>, he returned to mining in 1896.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-1">[1]</a></sup> High-profile events such as the gradual demise of the <a href="/wiki/Molly_Maguires" title="Molly Maguires">Molly Maguires</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haywood_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-haywood-7">[7]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haymarket_Massacre" title="Haymarket Massacre">Haymarket Massacre</a> in 1886 and the <a href="/wiki/Pullman_Strike" title="Pullman Strike">Pullman Strike</a> in 1894 fostered Haywood's interest in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_movement" title="Labor movement">labor movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Federation_of_Miners_involvement">Western Federation of Miners involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Western Federation of Miners involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1896, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Boyce" title="Ed Boyce">Ed Boyce</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Federation_of_Miners" title="Western Federation of Miners">Western Federation of Miners</a>, spoke at the <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> silver mine where Haywood was working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> Inspired by his speech, Haywood signed up as a WFM member, thus formally beginning his involvement in America's labor movement.</p>
<p>Haywood immediately became active in the WFM, and by 1900 he had become a member of the national union's General Executive Board. In 1902, he became secretary-treasurer of the WFM, the number two position after President <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moyer" title="Charles Moyer">Charles Moyer</a>. That year, the WFM became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Labor_Wars" title="Colorado Labor Wars">Colorado Labor Wars</a>, a struggle centered in the Cripple Creek mining district that lasted for several years and took the lives of 33 union and non-union workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> The WFM initiated a series of strikes designed to extend the benefits of the union to other workers, who suffered from brutal<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> working conditions and starvation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> wages. The defeat of these strikes led to Haywood's belief in "<a href="/wiki/One_Big_Union_(concept)" title="One Big Union (concept)">One Big Union</a>" organized along <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">industrial</a> lines to bring broader <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> support for labour struggles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_of_the_Industrial_Workers_of_the_World">Foundation of the Industrial Workers of the World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foundation of the Industrial Workers of the World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Late in 1904, several prominent labor radicals met in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> to lay down plans for a new revolutionary union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stjohn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-stjohn-8">[8]</a></sup> A manifesto was written and sent around the country. Unionists who agreed with the manifesto were invited to attend a convention to found the new union which was to become the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">Industrial Workers of the World</a>.</p>
<p>At 10 a.m. on June 27, 1905, Haywood addressed the crowd assembled at Brand's Hall in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-329-330_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-329-330-9">[9]</a></sup> In the audience were two hundred delegates from organizations all over the country representing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialists" title="Socialists">socialists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarchists" title="Anarchists">anarchists</a>, miners, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_unionists" title="Industrial unionists">industrial unionists</a> and rebel workers. Haywood opened the <a href="/wiki/First_Convention_of_the_Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="First Convention of the Industrial Workers of the World">First Convention of the Industrial Workers of the World</a> with the following speech:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-329-330_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-329-330-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Fellow Workers, this is the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> of the working-class. We are here to confederate the workers of this country into a working-class movement that shall have for its purpose the emancipation of the working-class from the slave bondage of capitalism. The aims and objects of this organization shall be to put the working-class in possession of the economic power, the means of life, in control of the machinery of production and distribution, without regard to capitalist masters.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Other speakers at the convention included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Debs" title="Eugene Debs">Eugene Debs</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">Socialist Party of America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Harris_Jones" title="Mary Harris Jones">Mary Harris "Mother" Jones</a>, an organizer for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Mine_Workers_of_America" title="United Mine Workers of America">United Mine Workers of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-329-330_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-329-330-9">[9]</a></sup> After its foundation, the IWW would become aggressively involved in the labor movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Murder_trial">Murder trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Murder trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haywood,_Moyer,_Pettibone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="301" data-file-width="306" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Haywood%2C_Moyer%2C_Pettibone.jpg/220px-Haywood%2C_Moyer%2C_Pettibone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/Haywood%2C_Moyer%2C_Pettibone.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Haywood,_Moyer,_Pettibone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1907 photo of defendants <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moyer" title="Charles Moyer">Charles Moyer</a>, Bill Haywood, and <a href="/wiki/George_Pettibone" title="George Pettibone">George Pettibone</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On December 30, 1905, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Steunenberg" title="Frank Steunenberg">Frank Steunenberg</a> was killed by an explosion in front of his <a href="/wiki/Caldwell,_Idaho" title="Caldwell, Idaho">Caldwell, Idaho</a> home. A former governor of <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>, Steunenberg had clashed with the WFM in previous strikes. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Horsley" title="Albert Horsley">Harry Orchard</a>, a former WFM member who had once acted as WFM President <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moyer" title="Charles Moyer">Charles Moyer</a>'s bodyguard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haywood.2C_158_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haywood.2C_158-10">[10]</a></sup> was arrested for the crime, and evidence was found in his hotel room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:87</sup> Famed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinkerton_National_Detective_Agency" title="Pinkerton National Detective Agency">Pinkerton</a> detective <a href="/wiki/James_McParland" title="James McParland">James McParland</a>, who had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_spies" title="Labor spies">infiltrated and helped to destroy</a> the <a href="/wiki/Molly_Maguires" title="Molly Maguires">Molly Maguires</a>, was placed in charge of the investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup></p>
<p>Before the trial, McParland ordered that Orchard be placed on death row in the Boise penitentiary, with restricted food rations and under constant surveillance. After McParland had prepared his investigation, he met with Orchard over a "sumptuous lunch" followed by cigars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:89</sup> The Pinkerton detective told Orchard that he could escape immediate hanging only if he implicated the leaders of the WFM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:90</sup> In addition to using the threat of hanging, McParland promised food, cigars, better treatment, possible freedom, and even a possible financial reward if Orchard cooperated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:89–91</sup> The detective obtained a 64-page confession from Orchard in which the suspect took responsibility for a string of crimes and at least seventeen murders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:91</sup></p>
<p>Writer Peter Carlson charged that McParland then used perjured extradition papers, which falsely stated that WFM leaders had been at the scene of the Steunenberg murder,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:93</sup> to cross state lines into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a> and arrest Haywood, Moyer, and <a href="/wiki/George_Pettibone" title="George Pettibone">George Pettibone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> However, under Idaho law, conspirators were considered to be present at the scene of the crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> McParland arrived in Denver on Thursday, February 15, and presented the extradition papers to Colorado Governor McDonald, who, by prior arrangement with the governor of Idaho, accepted them immediately. But they waited until Saturday evening to arrest Haywood, Pettigrew, and Moyer, then held the three overnight in the Denver Jail, and refused requests by the three to contact lawyers and family. Early Sunday morning the prisoners were put on a special train, and guarded by Colorado militia, were sped to Idaho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Writer Peter Carlson described it as a "kidnapping scheme,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:93</sup> to extradite them to Idaho before the courts in Denver could intervene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:93–94</sup> The events were so extraordinary that even <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">American Federation of Labor</a> president <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gompers" title="Samuel Gompers">Samuel Gompers</a>, who had little good to say about the WFM, directed his union to raise funds for the defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:97</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a> denied a <a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">habeas corpus</a> appeal (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Pettibone_v._Jasper_C_Nichols&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Pettibone v. Jasper C Nichols (page does not exist)">George Pettibone v. Jasper C Nichols</a>), ruling that the arrest and extradition were legal, with only Justice <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McKenna" title="Joseph McKenna">Joseph McKenna</a> dissenting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:101</sup></p>
<p>Haywood's trial began on May 9, 1907, with famed Chicago defense attorney <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Darrow" title="Clarence Darrow">Clarence Darrow</a> defending him. The government had only the testimony of Orchard,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-11"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:107</sup> the confessed bomber, to implicate Haywood and the other defendants, and Orchard's checkered past and admitted violent history were skillfully exploited by Darrow during the trial, though Darrow did not lead Orchard's cross-examination./<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> During the trial Orchard admitted that he had acted as a paid informant of the <a href="/wiki/Mine_Owners%27_Association" title="Mine Owners' Association">Mine Owners' Association</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-12"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:119</sup> in effect working for both sides. He admitted to accepting money from Pinkerton detectives, and had caused explosions during mining disputes before he had met Moyer or Haywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-13"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> After Darrow's final summation (which moved many in the courtroom to tears),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> the jury acquitted Haywood. During the subsequent trial of George Pettibone, Darrow conducted a powerful cross-examination against Orchard, before falling ill and withdrawing from the trial, leaving <a href="/wiki/Orrin_N._Hilton" title="Orrin N. Hilton">Judge Hilton</a> of Denver in charge of the defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> After a second jury acquitted Pettibone, the charges against Moyer were dropped.</p>
<p>Despite his radical views, Haywood emerged from the trial with a national reputation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Debs" title="Eugene Debs">Eugene Debs</a> called him "the Lincoln of Labor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Along with his colorful background and appearance, he was known for his blunt statements about capitalism. "The capitalist has no heart," he often said, "but harpoon him in the pocketbook and you will draw blood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Another time, he began a speech by noting, "Tonight I am going to speak on the class struggle and I am going to make it so plain that even a lawyer can understand it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Yet Haywood also had a flair for dangerous hyperbole that, when quoted in newspapers, was used to justify wholesale arrests of IWW strikers. "Confiscate! That's good!" he often said. "I like that word. It suggests stripping the capitalist, taking something away from him. But there has got to be a good deal of force to this thing of taking."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the WFM withdrew from the IWW in 1907, Bill Haywood remained a member of both organizations. His murder trial had made Haywood a celebrity, and he was in demand as a speaker for the WFM. But his increasingly radical speeches became more at odds with the WFM, and in April 1908, the WFM announced that the union had ended Haywood's role as a union representative. Haywood left the WFM, and devoted all his time to organizing for the IWW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawrence_Textile_Strike">Lawrence Textile Strike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lawrence Textile Strike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Textile_Strike" title="Lawrence Textile Strike">Lawrence Textile Strike</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:11-haywood-bohn.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="371" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/11-haywood-bohn.jpg/220px-11-haywood-bohn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/11-haywood-bohn.jpg/330px-11-haywood-bohn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/11-haywood-bohn.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:11-haywood-bohn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Haywood was the co-author of a popular exposition of the principles of industrial unionism published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_H._Kerr_%26_Co." title="Charles H. Kerr &amp; Co.">Charles H. Kerr &amp; Co.</a> in 1911.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bill Haywood had left the WFM and was organizing for the IWW by the time the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Textile_Strike" title="Lawrence Textile Strike">Lawrence Textile Strike</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence, Massachusetts</a> garnered national attention. On January 11, 1912, textile mill workers in Lawrence left their jobs in protest of lowered wages. Within a week, twenty thousand workers were on strike. The IWW already had a presence in Lawrence and assumed leadership of the strike).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Authorities responded by calling out police, and the strike quickly escalated into violence. Local IWW leaders <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Ettor" title="Joseph Ettor">Joseph Ettor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Giovannitti" title="Arturo Giovannitti">Arturo Giovannitti</a> were jailed on charges of murdering <a href="/wiki/Anna_LoPizzo" title="Anna LoPizzo">Anna LoPizzo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> a striker whom nineteen witnesses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> later said was killed by police gunfire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-14"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:166</sup> and martial law was declared. In response, Haywood and other organizers arrived to take charge of the strike. Over the next several weeks, Haywood personally masterminded or approved many of the strike's tactical innovations. Chief among these was his decision to send strikers' hungry children to sympathetic families in New York and Vermont).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After hearing from immigrants how European strikers had used this tactic during prolonged strikes, Haywood decided to take the gamble in Lawrence, a first in American Labor history. He and the IWW used announcements in socialist newspapers to solicit host families, then screened strikers to see who might be willing to send their children into the care of strangers. On February 10, 1912, the first group of "Lawrence Strike Children" bid tearful goodbyes to their parents and, with chaperones to guide them, boarded a train for New York. The children arrived safely in Manhattan that evening where they were taken to a Labor hall. They were soon lavished with food and clothes and would stay in New York another seven weeks. Despite their excellent treatment, officials in Lawrence and elsewhere were shocked by the move. "I could scarcely believe that the strike leaders would do such a thing as this," Lawrence mayor Michael Scanlon said. "Lawrence could have very easily cared for these children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Though also denounced in the press, the children's exodus gained widespread publicity and made the city of Lawrence look bad. While police and the mayor allowed smaller contingents of children to leave on two more occasions, plans were made to crack down).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On February 24, when strikers attempted to send still more children away, police were ready. During a melee, women and children were forcibly separated, police lashed out with clubs, and dozens of strikers and their offspring were jailed. A national outrage resulted. The <i>New York World</i> wrote, "The Lawrence authorities must be blind and the mill owners mad."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson175_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson175-23">[23]</a></sup> The <i>New York Tribune</i> called the police response "as chuckle-headed an exhibition of incompetence to deal with a strike situation as it is possible to recall."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson175_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watson175-23">[23]</a></sup> The incident led to a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">congressional</a> hearing and the attention of President <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>. Nationwide publicity pressured the mill owners into cooperating with the strikers; on March 12, the owners agreed to all the demands of the strikers, officially ending the strike).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, Haywood and the IWW were not yet finished in Lawrence; despite the end of the strike, Ettor and Giovannitti remained in prison. Haywood threatened the authorities with another strike, saying "Open the jail gates or we will close the mill gates." Legal efforts and a one-day strike on September 30 did not prompt the authorities to drop the charges. Haywood was indicted in Lawrence for misuse of strike funds, a move that kept him from returning to the city and eventually led to his arrest on the Boston Common. However, on November 26, Ettor and Giovannitti were acquitted, and upon their release were treated to a massive demonstration of public support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socialist_Party_of_America_involvement">Socialist Party of America involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Socialist Party of America involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For many years, Haywood was an active member of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">Socialist Party of America</a>. Haywood had always been largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> in his political views, and campaigned for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Debs" title="Eugene Debs">Eugene Debs</a> during the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1908" title="United States presidential election, 1908">1908 presidential election</a>, traveling by train with Debs around the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolgoff_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-dolgoff-4">[4]</a></sup> Haywood also represented the Socialist Party as a delegate to the 1910 congress of the <a href="/wiki/Second_International" title="Second International">Second International</a>, an organization working towards international socialism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolgoff_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-dolgoff-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1912, he was elected to the Socialist Party National Executive Committee.</p>
<p>However, the aggressive tactics of Haywood and the IWW, along with their call for abolition of the wage system and the overthrow of capitalism created tension with moderate electorally-oriented leaders of the Socialist Party. Haywood and the IWW focused on <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> and strikes, which often led to violence, and were less concerned with political tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siitonen_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-siitonen-25">[25]</a></sup> When Haywood was quoted speaking at public meetings in New York City to the effect that he had never advocated the use of the ballot by the workers but had instead favored the tactics of direct action, an initiative recalling Haywood from the NEC was launched by the State Executive Committee of the Socialist Party of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Result_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Result-26">[26]</a></sup> In February 1913 the recall of Haywood was approved by a margin of more than 2-to-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Result_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Result-26">[26]</a></sup> Following his defeat, Haywood left the ranks of the Socialist Party, joined by thousands of other IWW members and their sympathizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siitonen_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-siitonen-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_labor_involvement">Other labor involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other labor involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paterson_strike_leaders.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="223" data-file-width="336" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Paterson_strike_leaders.jpg/300px-Paterson_strike_leaders.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/Paterson_strike_leaders.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paterson_strike_leaders.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1913 photo of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paterson_silk_strike_of_1913" title="Paterson silk strike of 1913">Paterson silk strike</a> leaders <a href="/wiki/Patrick_L._Quinlan" title="Patrick L. Quinlan">Patrick L. Quinlan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Tresca" title="Carlo Tresca">Carlo Tresca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Gurley_Flynn" title="Elizabeth Gurley Flynn">Elizabeth Gurley Flynn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adolph_Lessig" title="Adolph Lessig">Adolph Lessig</a>, and Haywood</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1913, Haywood was involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paterson_silk_strike_of_1913" title="Paterson silk strike of 1913">Paterson silk strike</a>. Haywood and approximately 1,850 strikers were arrested during the course of the strike. Despite the long holdout and fundraising efforts, the strike ended in failure on July 28, 1913. Haywood again made headlines, however, when the IWW staged the Paterson Strike Pageant at Madison Square Garden, in which actual strikers appeared onstage as themselves re-enacting the strike even as it was going on in Paterson. Haywood became a celebrity in progressive circles and began frequenting the intellectual salon of <a href="/wiki/Mabel_Dodge_Luhan" title="Mabel Dodge Luhan">Mabel Dodge Luhan</a> where, while often wearing his Stetson hat, he hobnobbed with writers and artists of the avant-garde.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Espionage_trial">Espionage trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Espionage trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Haywood and the IWW frequently clashed with the government during their labor actions. The onset of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> gave the federal government the opportunity to take action against Haywood and the IWW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-372-373_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-372-373-27">[27]</a></sup> Using the newly passed <a href="/wiki/Espionage_Act_of_1917" title="Espionage Act of 1917">Espionage Act of 1917</a> as justification, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice</a> raided forty-eight IWW meeting halls on September 5, 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siitonen_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-siitonen-25">[25]</a></sup> The Department of Justice, with the approval of President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, then proceeded to arrest 165 IWW members for "conspiring to hinder the draft, encourage desertion, and intimidate others in connection with labor disputes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-372-373_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-372-373-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 1918, Haywood and 100 of the arrested IWW members began their trial, presided over by Judge <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>. The trial lasted five months, the longest criminal trial up to that time; Haywood himself testified for three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-372-373_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-372-373-27">[27]</a></sup> All 101 defendants were found guilty, and Haywood (along with fourteen others) was sentenced to twenty years in prison.</p>
<p>Despite the efforts of his supporters, Haywood was unable to overturn the conviction. In 1921, Haywood skipped bail while out on appeal and fled to Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begins_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Begins-28">[28]</a></sup> IWW officials were taken by surprise that Haywood had jumped bail, with his own attorney declaring that "Haywood has committed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hara-kiri" title="Hara-kiri">hara-kiri</a> so far as the labor movement is concerned if he has really run away. He will be disowned by the IWW and all sympathizers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begins_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Begins-28">[28]</a></sup> A bond in the amount of $15,000 posted by millionaire supporter <a href="/wiki/William_Bross_Lloyd" title="William Bross Lloyd">William Bross Lloyd</a> was forfeited as a result of Haywood's flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begins_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Begins-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_Soviet_Russia">Life in Soviet Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Life in Soviet Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
<p>In Soviet Russia, Haywood became a labor advisor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenin" title="Lenin">Lenin</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> government,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup> and served in that position until 1923. Haywood also participated in the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Kuzbass_Autonomous_Industrial_Colony" title="Kuzbass Autonomous Industrial Colony">Kuzbass Autonomous Industrial Colony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Various visitors to Haywood's small Moscow apartment in later years recalled that he felt lonely and depressed, and expressed a desire to return to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1926 he took a Russian wife, though the two had to communicate in sign language, as neither spoke the other's language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speak_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-speak-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On May 18, 1928, Haywood died in a <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> hospital from a stroke brought on by alcoholism and diabetes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Half of his ashes were buried in the <a href="/wiki/Kremlin_Wall_Necropolis" title="Kremlin Wall Necropolis">Kremlin wall</a>; an urn containing the other half of his ashes was sent to Chicago and buried near the <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_Martyrs%27_Monument" title="Haymarket Martyrs' Monument">Haymarket Martyrs' Monument</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linder_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-linder-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Haywood.27s_labor_philosophy">Haywood's labor philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Haywood's labor philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_unionism">Industrial unionism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Industrial unionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even before Haywood first became an official with the Western Federation of Miners, he was convinced that the system under which working people toiled was unjust. He described the execution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haymarket_Riot" title="Haymarket Riot">Haymarket</a> leaders in 1887 as a turning point in his life, predisposing him toward membership in the largest organization of the day, the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Labor" title="Knights of Labor">Knights of Labor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-15"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36</sup> Haywood had watched men die in unsafe mine tunnels, and had marched with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelly%27s_Army" title="Kelly's Army">Kelly's Army</a>. He had suffered a serious hand injury in the mines, and found that his only support came from other miners. When Haywood listened to <a href="/wiki/Ed_Boyce" title="Ed Boyce">Ed Boyce</a> of the WFM addressing a group of miners in 1896, he discovered radical unionism and welcomed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-16"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:50–52</sup></p>
<p>Haywood also shared Boyce's skepticism of the role played by the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">American Federation of Labor</a> (AFL). Haywood criticized labor officials who were, in his view, insufficiently supportive of Labor militants. For example, he recalled with disdain the opening remarks of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gompers" title="Samuel Gompers">Samuel Gompers</a> when the AFL leader appeared before Illinois Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_James_Oglesby" title="Richard James Oglesby">Richard Oglesby</a> on behalf of the Haymarket prisoners:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I have differed all my life with the principles and methods of the condemned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Gompers was an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Craft_unionism" title="Craft unionism">craft unionism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cahn-137-169_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cahn-137-169-2">[2]</a></sup> the idea that workers should be separated into unions according to their skills. The AFL disdained to organize workers who were not skilled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-17"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:80</sup> Furthermore, in 1900 Gompers became the first vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/National_Civic_Federation" title="National Civic Federation">National Civic Federation</a>, which was "dedicated to the fostering of harmony and collaboration between capital and organized labor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> But Haywood had become convinced by the experiences of striking railroad workers that a different union philosophy, some form of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">industrial unionism</a>, was necessary for workers to obtain justice. This had become apparent in 1888 when the craft-organized locomotive firemen kept their engines running, helping their employers to break a strike called by the railroad engineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-18"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bill_haywood_headshot_side.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="370" data-file-width="280" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Bill_haywood_headshot_side.jpg/220px-Bill_haywood_headshot_side.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Bill_haywood_headshot_side.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bill_haywood_headshot_side.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bill Haywood, unknown date</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eugene_V._Debs" title="Eugene V. Debs">Eugene V. Debs</a> had been head of the locomotive firemen's union, but he resigned to create the <a href="/wiki/American_Railway_Union" title="American Railway Union">American Railway Union</a> (ARU), organized industrially to include all railroad workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-19"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:45</sup> In June 1894, the ARU voted to join in solidarity with the ongoing <a href="/wiki/Pullman_Strike" title="Pullman Strike">Pullman strike</a>. Railroad traffic throughout the nation was "largely paralyzed. The effectiveness of the industrial form of unionism was evident from the start."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The strike was eventually crushed by massive government intervention that included 2600 Deputy U.S. Marshals, and 14,000 state and federal troops in Chicago alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Debs attempted to seek help from the American Federation of Labor. He asked that AFL railroad brotherhood affiliates present the following proposition to the Railway Managers' Association:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...that the strikers return to work at once as a body, upon the condition that they be restored to their former positions, or, in the event of failure, to call a general strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Big_Bill_Haywood_1929.2C_pp._77-78_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Bill_Haywood_1929.2C_pp._77-78-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Observing that the ARU was defenseless, AFL officials viewed the plight of the rival organization as an opportunity to bolster the railway brotherhoods, which the AFL was courting, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_federation_competition_in_the_U.S.#Industrial_unionism_in_the_Pullman_Strike" title="Labor federation competition in the U.S.">instructed all AFL affiliates to withhold help</a>. In spite of what Haywood perceived as "treachery" and "double-cross"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Big_Bill_Haywood_1929.2C_pp._77-78_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Bill_Haywood_1929.2C_pp._77-78-39">[39]</a></sup> by the AFL leadership — the ARU members had put their own organization at risk for others, but the AFL refused to even help them try to end the strike in a draw — the power of workers crossing their trade lines and jurisdictional boundaries to join together in a fight against capital greatly impressed him. He described the revelation of such power as "a great rift of light."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-20"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup></p>
<p>For Haywood, industrial union principles were later confirmed by the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Federation_of_Miners" title="Western Federation of Miners">Western Federation of Miners</a> in the 1903–05 Cripple Creek strike due — he believed — to insufficient labor solidarity. The WFM miners had sought to extend the benefits of union to the mill workers who processed their ore. Since the government had crushed the ARU, the railroad workers were again organized along craft lines, similar to the AFL. Those same railroad unions continued to haul the ore <i>from mines</i> that were run by strike breakers, <i>to mills</i> that were run by strike breakers. "The railroaders form the connecting link in the proposition that is scabby at both ends," Haywood complained. "This fight, which is entering its third year, could have been won in three weeks if it were not for the fact that the trade unions are lending assistance to the mine operators."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-21"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:80</sup> The obvious solution, it seemed to Haywood, was for all of the workers to join the same union, and to take collective action in concert against the employers. The militants of the WFM referred to the AFL as the "American <i>Separation</i> of Labor,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-22"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:81</sup> a criticism that was later echoed by the Industrial Workers of the World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haywood.27s_revolutionary_imperative">Haywood's revolutionary imperative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Haywood's revolutionary imperative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"[The mine owners] did not find the gold, they did not mine the gold, they did not mill the gold, but by some weird alchemy all the gold belonged to them!"</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">--Haywood, William D. <i>The Autobiography of Big Bill Haywood</i>. New York: International Publishers, 1929, p. 171</cite></div>
<p>Haywood's industrial unionism was much broader than formulating a more effective method of conducting strikes. Haywood grew up a part of the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-23"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26–30</sup> and his respect for working people was genuine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-24"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:49</sup> He was quickly angered by the arrogance of employers "who had never ... spoken to a workingman except to give orders."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-25"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:60</sup></p>
<p>Having met Debs during his WFM days, Haywood had also become interested in the former railway leader's new passion, socialism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-26"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:58</sup> Haywood subscribed to the belief, and with Boyce, formulated as a new motto for the WFM, that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Labor produces all wealth; all wealth belongs to the producer thereof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-27"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Haywood observed how the government frequently took the side of business to defeat the tactics and the aspirations of the miners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-28"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:65</sup> During an <a href="/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene,_Idaho_labor_confrontation_of_1899" title="Coeur d'Alene, Idaho labor confrontation of 1899">1899 organizing drive</a> in <a href="/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene,_Idaho" title="Coeur d'Alene, Idaho">Coeur d'Alene</a>, with pay cuts as a motivating issue, the company hired spies and then fired organizers and pro-union miners. Some frustrated miners responded with violence and when two men were killed, martial law was declared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-29"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup> As they had done in a strike in Coeur d'Alene seven years earlier, soldiers acted as strike breakers. They rounded up hundreds of union members without formal charges and put them in a filthy, vermin-infested warehouse without sanitation services for a year. They were so crowded that the soldiers locked the overflow of prisoners in boxcars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-30"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55</sup> One local union leader was imprisoned for 17 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-31"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup></p>
<p>Haywood considered the brutal conditions in Coeur d'Alene a manifestation of class warfare. In 1901 the miners agreed at the WFM convention that a "complete revolution of social and economic conditions" was "the only salvation of the working classes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the WFM's 1903–04 struggle in Colorado, with martial law once again in force, two declarations uttered by the National Guard and recorded for posterity further clarified the relationship of the mine operator's enforcement army — provided courtesy of the Colorado governor — to the workers. When union attorneys asked the courts to free illegally imprisoned strikers, Adjutant General <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Bell" title="Sherman Bell">Sherman Bell</a> declared, "<a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">Habeas corpus</a> be damned, we'll give 'em <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">post mortems</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-32"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62</sup> Reminded of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a>, one of Bell's junior officers declared coolly, "To hell with the Constitution. We're not going by the Constitution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-33"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:63</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="401" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg/220px-Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg/330px-Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bill_haywood_iww_convention.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Haywood at a convention in Chicago (1917)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>General Bell had been the manager of one of the coal mines in Cripple Creek where the strike was taking place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It wasn't any surprise to Haywood that soldiers seemed to be working in the interests of the employers; he had seen that situation before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-34"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:65</sup> But when the Colorado legislature acknowledged the complaints of organized labor and passed an <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">eight hour</a> law, the Colorado supreme court declared it unconstitutional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-35"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup> So the WFM took the issue to the voters, and 72 percent of the state's voters approved the referendum. But the Colorado government ignored the results of the referendum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-36"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:65</sup></p>
<p>To members of the WFM, it became clear that government favored the companies, and only direct action by organized workers could secure the eight-hour day for themselves. When miners in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idaho_Springs" title="Idaho Springs">Idaho Springs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telluride,_Colorado" title="Telluride, Colorado">Telluride</a> decided to strike for the eight-hour day, they were rounded up at gunpoint by vigilante groups and expelled from their communities. Warrants were issued for the arrest of the law-breaking vigilantes, but they were not acted upon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-37"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:64–65</sup></p>
<p>Haywood complained that <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a> was "wielding more power with his golf sticks than could the people of Colorado with their ballots."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> It appeared to Haywood that the deck was stacked, and no enduring gains could be won for the workers short of changing the rules of the game. Increasingly, his industrial unionism took on a revolutionary flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In 1905 Haywood joined the more left-leaning socialists, labor anarchists in the Haymarket tradition, and other militant unionists to formulate the concept of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">revolutionary industrial unionism</a> that animated the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">Industrial Workers of the World</a>. Haywood called this philosophy "socialism with its working clothes on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haywood.2C_158_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haywood.2C_158-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haywood favored direct action. The socialist philosophy — which WFM supporter the Rev. Fr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_J._Hagerty" title="Thomas J. Hagerty">Thomas J. Hagerty</a> called <i>"slowcialism"</i> — did not seem hard-nosed enough for Haywood's labor instincts. After the Boise murder trial, he had come to believe,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is to the ignominy of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">Socialist Party</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Labor_Party" title="Socialist Labor Party">Socialist Labor Party</a> that they have so seldom joined forces with the I.W.W. in these desperate political struggles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While Haywood continued to champion direct action, he advocated the political action favored by the socialists as just one more mechanism for change, and only when it seemed relevant. At an October 1913 meeting of the Socialist Party, Haywood stated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I advocate the industrial ballot alone when I address the workers in the textile industries of the East where a great majority are foreigners without political representation. But when I speak to American workingmen in the West I advocate both the industrial and the political ballot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marot_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-marot-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The "industrial ballot" referred to the direct action methods (strikes, slowdowns, etc.) of the IWW.</p>
<p>Haywood seemed most comfortable with a philosophy arrived at through the hard-scrabble experiences of the workers. He had the ability to translate complex economic theories into simple ideas that resonated with working people. He distilled the voluminous work of Karl Marx into a simple observation, "If one man has a dollar he didn't work for, some other man worked for a dollar he didn't get."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlson_11-38"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:146</sup> While Haywood respected the work of Marx, he referred to it with irreverent humor. Acknowledging his scars from dangerous mining work, and from numerous fistfights with police and militia, he liked to say, "I've never read Marx's Capital, but I have the marks of capital all over me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haywood demonstrated his Marxist roots when, confronted by the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Industrial_Relations" title="Commission on Industrial Relations">Commission on Industrial Relations</a> with an argument about the sanctity of private property, he responded that a capitalist's property merely represented "unpaid labor, <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">surplus value</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> But the forum also gave Haywood an opportunity to compare the philosophy of the IWW with that of Marx and the socialist parties. Reminded by the Commission that socialists advocated ownership of the industries by the state, Haywood remembered in his autobiography that he had drawn a clear distinction. All of industry should be owned "by the workers," he observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_unity_in_the_labor_movement">Racial unity in the labor movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Haywood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Racial unity in the labor movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of Haywood's philosophy relating to socialism, to the idea that <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">industrial unionism</a> was preferable to <a href="/wiki/Craft_unionism" title="Craft unionism">craft unionism</a>, what he saw as the evils of the wage system, his attitude about corporations, militia, and politicians, seem to have been held in common with his mentor at the WFM, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Boyce" title="Ed Boyce">Ed Boyce</a>. Boyce also called for legislation to forbid employment of aliens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Unlike Boyce and many other labor leaders and organizations of the time, Haywood believed that workers of all ethnicities should organize into the same union. According to Haywood, the IWW was "big enough to take in the black man, the white man; big enough to take in all nationalities – an organization that will be strong enough to obliterate state boundaries; to obliterate national boundaries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orth_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-orth-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1912, Haywood spoke at a convention for <a href="/wiki/The_Brotherhood_of_Timber_Workers" title="The Brotherhood of Timber Workers">The Brotherhood of Timber Workers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>; at the time, interracial meetings in the state were illegal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-337-339_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-337-339-3">[3]</a></sup> Haywood insisted that the white workers invite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> workers to their convention, declaring:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>You work in the same mills together. Sometimes a black man and a white man chop down the same tree together. You are meeting in a convention now to discuss the conditions under which you labor. Why not be sensible about this and call the Negroes into the Convention? If it is against the law, this is one time when the law should be broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-337-339_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-337-339-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ignoring the law against interracial meetings, the convention invited the African American workers. The convention would eventually vote to affiliate with the IWW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zinn-337-339_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-zinn-337-339-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
