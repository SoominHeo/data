<p><b>Sawdust</b> or <b>wood dust</b> is a by-product of cutting, grinding, drilling, sanding, or otherwise pulverizing wood or any other material with a <a href="/wiki/Saw" title="Saw">saw</a> or other tool; it is composed of fine particles of wood. It is also the byproduct of certain animals, birds and insects which live in wood, such as the <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpecker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carpenter_ant" title="Carpenter ant">carpenter ant</a>. It can present a hazard in manufacturing industries, especially in terms of its flammability. Sawdust is the main component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particleboard" title="Particleboard">particleboard</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sawdust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A major use of sawdust is for particleboard; coarse sawdust may be used for <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">wood pulp</a>. Sawdust has a variety of other practical uses, including serving as a <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a>, as an alternative to clay <a href="/wiki/Litter_box" title="Litter box">cat litter</a>, or as a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>. Until the advent of refrigeration, it was often used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icehouse_(building)" title="Icehouse (building)">icehouses</a> to keep ice frozen during the summer. It has been used in <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">artistic displays</a>, and as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scatter_(modeling)" title="Scatter (modeling)">scatter</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_railroading" title="Model railroading">miniature railroad</a> and other models. It is also sometimes used to soak up liquid spills, allowing the spill to be easily collected or swept aside. As such, it was formerly common on barroom floors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is used to make <a href="/wiki/Cutler%27s_resin" title="Cutler's resin">Cutler's resin</a>. Mixed with water and frozen, it forms <a href="/wiki/Pykrete" title="Pykrete">pykrete</a>, a slow-melting, much stronger form of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>.</p>
<p>Sawdust is used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Briquette" title="Briquette">charcoal briquettes</a>. The claim for invention of the first commercial charcoal briquettes goes to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> who created them from the wood scraps and sawdust produced by his automobile factory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_food">Use in food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sawdust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cellulose, fibre starch that is indigestible to humans, and a filler in some low calorie foods, can be and is made from sawdust, as well as from other plant sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> While there is no documentation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> for the persistent rumor, based upon <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">Upton Sinclair</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">The Jungle</a></i>, that sawdust was used as a filler in sausage, cellulose derived from sawdust was and is used for sausage casings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Sawdust-derived cellulose has also been used as a filler in bread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a> survivor, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_Nyiszli" title="Miklós Nyiszli">Miklós Nyiszli</a>, reports in <i>Auschwitz: A Doctor's Eyewitness Account</i> that the subaltern medical staff, who served <a href="/wiki/Josef_Mengele" title="Josef Mengele">Dr. Josef Mengele</a>, subsisted on "bread made from wild <a href="/wiki/Chestnut#Nutrition" title="Chestnut">chestnuts</a> sprinkled with sawdust."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety_hazards">Health and safety hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sawdust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Health and safety hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Airborne sawdust and sawdust accumulations present a number of health and safety hazards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Wood dust becomes a potential health problem when, for example, the wood particles, from processes such as sanding, become airborne and are inhaled. Wood dust is a known human <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Certain woods and their dust contain toxins that can produce severe allergic reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Water-borne <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> digest organic material in leachate, but use up much of the available oxygen. This high "biological oxygen demand" can suffocate fish and other organisms. There is an equally detrimental effect on beneficial bacteria, so it is not at all advisable to use sawdust within home aquariums, as was once done by hobbyists seeking to save some expense on activated charcoal.</p>
<p>People can be exposed to wood dust in the workplace by breathing it in, skin contact, or eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for wood dust exposure in the workplace as 15 mg/m<sup>3</sup> total exposure and 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> respiratory exposure over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosions_and_fire">Explosions and fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sawdust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Explosions and fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Combustibility#Combustible_dust" title="Combustibility">Combustibility § Combustible dust</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">Dust explosion</a></div>
<p>Sawdust is flammable and accumulations provide a ready source of fuel. Airborne sawdust can be ignited by sparks or even heat accumulation and result in explosions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sawdust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, unless reprocessed into particleboard, burned in a sawdust burner or used to make heat for other milling operations, sawdust may collect in piles and add harmful <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">leachates</a> into local water systems, creating an environmental hazard. This has placed small sawyers and environmental agencies in a deadlock.</p>
<p>Questions about the science behind the determination of sawdust being an environmental hazard remain for sawmill operators (though this is mainly with finer particles), who compare wood residuals to dead trees in a forest. Technical advisors have reviewed some of the environmental studies, but say most lack standardized methodology or evidence of a direct impact on <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>. They don’t take into account large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drainage_area" title="Drainage area">drainage areas</a>, so the amount of material that is getting into the water from the site in relation to the total drainage area is minuscule.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Other scientists have a different view, saying the "dilution is the solution to pollution" argument is no longer accepted in environmental science. The decomposition of a tree in a forest is similar to the impact of sawdust, but the difference is of scale. Sawmills may be storing thousands of cubic metres of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_residues&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wood residues (page does not exist)">wood residues</a> in one place, so the issue becomes one of concentration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>But of larger concern are substances such as <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a> that protect trees from predators while they are alive, but can leach into water and poison wildlife. Those types of things remain in the tree and, as the tree decays, they slowly are broken down. But when sawyers are processing a large volume of wood and large concentrations of these materials permeate into the runoff, the <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> they cause is harmful to a broad range of organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
