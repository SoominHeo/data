<p>The <b>exclamation mark</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="English in the Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth English</a>) or <b>exclamation point</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a> mark usually used after an <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjection</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">exclamation</a> to indicate strong feelings or high volume (shouting), and often marks the end of a sentence. Example: "Watch out!" Similarly, a bare exclamation mark (with nothing before or after) is often used in <a href="/wiki/Warning_sign" title="Warning sign">warning signs</a>.</p>
<p>Other uses include:</p>
<ul>
<li>In mathematics it denotes the <a href="/wiki/Factorial" title="Factorial">factorial</a> operation.</li>
<li>Several computer languages use <b>"!"</b> at the beginning of an expression to denote logical negation: e.g. "!A" means "the logical negation of A", also called "not A".</li>
<li>Some languages use "!" to denote a <a href="/wiki/Click_consonant" title="Click consonant">click consonant</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Graphically the exclamation mark is represented as a <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">full stop</a> point with a vertical line above. One theory of its origin is that it is derived from a Latin exclamation of joy (<i>io</i>). The modern graphical representation is believed to have been born in the Middle Ages. Medieval copyists wrote the Latin word <i>io</i> at the end of a sentence to indicate joy. The word <i>io</i> meant "hurray". Over time, the <i>i</i> moved above the <i>o</i>, and the <i>o</i> became smaller, becoming a point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exclamation mark was first introduced into English printing in the 15th century to show emphasis, and was called the "sign of admiration or exclamation"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or the "note of admiration" until the mid-17th century;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> admiration referred to its <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> sense of wonderment.</p>
<p>The exclamation mark did not have its own dedicated key on standard manual typewriters before the 1970s. Instead, one typed a <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">period</a>, backspaced, and typed an <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">apostrophe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the 1950s, secretarial dictation and typesetting manuals in America referred to the mark as "bang",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> perhaps from comic books where the ! appeared in dialogue balloons to represent a gun being fired,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> although the nickname probably emerged from letterpress printing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This bang usage is behind the names of the <a href="/wiki/Interrobang" title="Interrobang">interrobang</a>, an unconventional typographic character, and a <a href="/wiki/Shebang_(Unix)" title="Shebang (Unix)">shebang line</a>, a feature of <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> computer systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slang_and_other_names_for_the_exclamation_mark">Slang and other names for the exclamation mark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Slang and other names for the exclamation mark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the printing world, the exclamation mark can be called a screamer, a gasper, a slammer, or a startler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(programmer_subculture)" title="Hacker (programmer subculture)">hacker culture</a>, the exclamation mark is called "bang", "shriek", or, in the British slang known as Commonwealth Hackish, "pling". For example, the password communicated in the spoken phrase "Your password is em-nought-pee-aitch-pling-en-three" is <tt>m0ph!n3</tt>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambox_current_red.svg"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(January 2015)</i></small></span></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The exclamation mark is common to languages using the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, although usage varies slightly between languages. The exclamation mark was also adopted in languages written in other scripts, such as <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a>, but it has never been found in <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A sentence ending in an exclamation mark may be an <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">exclamation</a> (such as "Wow!", "Boo!"), or an <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a> ("Stop!"), or may indicate astonishment or surprise: "They were the footprints of a gigantic hound!" Exclamation marks are occasionally placed mid-sentence with a function similar to a comma, for dramatic effect, although this usage is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obsolescent" title="Obsolescent">obsolescent</a>: "On the walk, oh! there was a frightful noise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Informally, exclamation marks may be repeated for additional emphasis ("That's great!!!"), but this practice is generally considered unacceptable in formal prose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exclamation mark is sometimes used in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">question mark</a>. This can be in protest or astonishment ("Out of all places, the squatter-camp?!"); a few writers replace this with a single, nonstandard punctuation mark, the <a href="/wiki/Interrobang" title="Interrobang">interrobang</a>, which is the combination of a question mark and an exclamation mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overly frequent use of the exclamation mark is generally considered poor writing, for it distracts the reader and devalues the mark's significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Cut out all these exclamation points...An exclamation point is like laughing at your own joke.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/F._Scott_Fitzgerald" title="F. Scott Fitzgerald">F. Scott Fitzgerald</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Some authors, however, most notably <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a> and Madison Acampora, are known for unashamedly liberal use of the exclamation mark. In <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>, the very frequent use of exclamation mark is common—see <a href="#Comics">Comics</a>, below.</p>
<p>For information on the use of spaces after an exclamation mark, see the discussion of <a href="/wiki/Full_stop#Spacing_after_a_full_stop" title="Full stop">spacing after a full stop</a>.</p>
<p>Several studies have shown that women use exclamation marks more than men do, and one study suggests that, in addition to other usage, exclamation marks may also function as markers of friendly interaction, for example, by making "Hi!" or "Good luck!" seem friendlier than simply "Hi." or "Good luck." (with periods).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In English writing and often <a href="/wiki/Subtitle_(captioning)" title="Subtitle (captioning)">subtitles</a>, a <b>(!)</b> symbol (an exclamation mark within <a href="/wiki/Bracket#Parentheses_.28_.29" title="Bracket">parentheses</a>) implies that a character has made an obviously <a href="/wiki/Sarcasm" title="Sarcasm">sarcastic</a> comment e.g.: "<i>Ooh, a sarcasm detector. That's a</i> really useful <i>invention(!)</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It also is used to indicate surprise at one's own experience or statement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French">French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In French, next to marking exclamations or indicating astonishment, the exclamation mark is also commonly used to mark orders or requests: <i>Viens ici !</i> (English: "Come here!"). A space (« espace fine ») is used between the last word and the exclamation mark in European French, but not in Canadian French. One can also combine an exclamation mark with a question mark at the end of a sentence where appropriate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German">German</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> uses the exclamation mark for several things that English conveys with other <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>In the salutation line of a letter, for which English uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comma_(punctuation)" title="Comma (punctuation)">comma</a>: <i>Lieber Hans!</i> (English: "Dear Hans,") In this case, the first word of the following sentence begins with a capital letter. However the use of a comma, as in English, is both acceptable and far more common.</li>
<li>On signs, not just those warning of danger as discussed below, the exclamation mark is used to emphasize the sign's content: <i>Betreten verboten!</i> (English: "No trespassing")</li>
<li>At the end of an <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a> sentence: <i>Ruf mich morgen an!</i> (English: "Call me tomorrow.")</li>
</ul>
<p><span id="Greek_exclamation_mark"></span><span id="Thavmastiko"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cantonese">Cantonese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cantonese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cantonese has not historically used exclamation marks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Usage of exclamation marks is common in written Mandarin and in some Yue speaking regions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Canton and Hong Kong regions, however, refused to accept the exclamation mark as it was seen as carrying with it unnecessary and confusing Western connotations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An exclamation mark in Cantonese can indicate strong feeling. For example, the sentence 「X你老母!」</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">Modern Greek</a>, the exclamation mark (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Θαυμαστικό</span>, <i>thavmastikó</i>) has been introduced from <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin scripts</a> and is used identically, although without the reluctance seen in <a href="#English">English usage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicky_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicky-19">[19]</a></sup> A minor grammatical difference is that, while a series of interjections each employ an exclamation mark (e.g., <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">«Ωχ! Αχ!»</span>, <i>Ōch! Ach!</i>, "Oops! Oh!"), an interjection should only be separated from an extended exclamation by a comma (e.g., <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">«Ωχ, ξέχασα το μάτι της κουζίνας ανοιχτό!»</span>, <i>Ōch, xéchasa to máti tīs kouzínas anoichtó!</i>, "Oops! I left the stove on.").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">Spanish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spanish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc,_Barcelona_(detail).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="816" data-file-width="2469" height="73" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg/220px-Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg/330px-Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg/440px-Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc%2C_Barcelona_%28detail%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Billboard_at_Montju%C3%AFc,_Barcelona_(detail).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Trilingual billboard in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> (detail), showing the initial exclamation mark for Spanish, but not for <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> (top line) and English.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Spanish, a sentence or clause ending in an exclamation mark must also begin with an <a href="/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks" title="Inverted question and exclamation marks">inverted exclamation mark</a> (the same also applies to the question mark):</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>¿Estás loco? ¡Casi la mataste!</i> – "Are you crazy? You almost killed her!"</dd>
</dl>
<p>In informal written online communications, however, inverted question and exclamation marks have become less common.</p>
<p>As in British English, a bracketed exclamation mark may be used to indicate irony or surprise at a statement:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Dice que esta noche no va a salir de fiesta (!). – "He said that he's not going to a party tonight(!)."</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Such use is not matched by an inverted opening exclamation mark.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish">Turkish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Turkish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, an exclamation mark is used after a sentence or phrase for <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/emphasis" title="wikt:emphasis">emphasis</a>, and is common following both commands and the addressees of such commands. For example, in <i>Ordular! İlk hedefiniz Akdenizdir, ileri!</i> ("Armies! Your first target is the Mediterranean") order by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Atatürk">Atatürk</a>, <i>ordular</i> (the armies) constitute the addressee. It is further used in parentheses "(!)" after a sentence or phrase to indicate <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sarcasm" title="Sarcasm">sarcasm</a>: <i>Çok iyi bir iş yaptın (!)</i> ("You've done a very good job – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Not!" title="Not!">Not!</a>").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics">Phonetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Phonetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan languages</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, the exclamation mark is used as a letter to indicate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postalveolar_click" title="Postalveolar click">postalveolar click</a> sound (represented as <i>q</i> in <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> orthography). In <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>, this letter is properly coded as <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+01C3</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">ǃ</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LATIN LETTER RETROFLEX CLICK</span> and distinguished from the common punctuation symbol <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+0021</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">!</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">EXCLAMATION MARK</span> to allow software to deal properly with word breaks.</p>
<p>The exclamation mark has sometimes been used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic_notation" title="Phonetic notation">phonetic symbol</a> to indicate that a consonant is <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective</a>. More commonly this is represented by an <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">apostrophe</a>, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superscript" title="Superscript">superscript</a> <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> symbol (<span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+02C0</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">ˀ</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">MODIFIER LETTER GLOTTAL STOP</span>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interrobang">Interrobang</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Interrobang">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interrobang" title="Interrobang">Interrobang</a></div>
<p>There is a non–standard punctuation mark intended to combine the functions of a question mark and an exclamation mark in English called <a href="/wiki/Interrobang" title="Interrobang">interrobang</a>, which resembles those marks superimposed over one another ("‽") but it is seldom seen outside Unicode documentation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> - the sequence of "?!" or "!?" is used almost exclusively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proper_names">Proper names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Proper names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although exclamation marks are, as a standard, part of a complete sentence and not the spelling of individual words, they appear in many proper names, especially in commercial advertising. Prominent examples include the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">Web</a> service <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joomla!" title="Joomla!">Joomla!</a> the <a href="/wiki/Game_show" title="Game show">game show</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Jeopardy!" title="Jeopardy!">Jeopardy!</a></i> and the '60s musical TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Shindig!" title="Shindig!">Shindig!</a></i>. The titles of the <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musicals</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma!" title="Oklahoma!">Oklahoma!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver!" title="Oliver!">Oliver!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Oh!_Calcutta!" title="Oh! Calcutta!">Oh! Calcutta!</a></i> and the movies <i><a href="/wiki/Airplane!" title="Airplane!">Airplane!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Moulin_Rouge!" title="Moulin Rouge!">Moulin Rouge!</a></i> also contain exclamation marks. Writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliot_S!_Maggin" title="Elliot S! Maggin">Elliot S! Maggin</a> and cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Scott_Shaw_(artist)" title="Scott Shaw (artist)">Scott Shaw!</a> include exclamation marks in their names. In the 2016 United States presidential campaign, Republican candidate Jeb Bush used "Jeb!" as his campaign logo.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Place_names">Place names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Place names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Westward_Ho!" title="Westward Ho!">Westward Ho!</a>, named after <a href="/wiki/Westward_Ho!_(novel)" title="Westward Ho! (novel)">the novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kingsley" title="Charles Kingsley">Charles Kingsley</a>, is the only place name in the United Kingdom that officially contains an exclamation mark. There is a town in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Louis-du-Ha!_Ha!,_Quebec" title="Saint-Louis-du-Ha! Ha!, Quebec">Saint-Louis-du-Ha! Ha!</a>, which is spelled with two exclamation marks. The city of <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ohio" title="Hamilton, Ohio">Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, changed its name to Hamilton! in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Ostrava" title="Ostrava">Ostrava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, changed its logotype to Ostrava!!! in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>On <a href="/wiki/Warning_sign" title="Warning sign">warning signs</a>, an exclamation mark is often used to draw attention to a warning of danger, hazards, and the unexpected. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode_and_HTML">Unicode and HTML</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Unicode and HTML">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mark is encoded as <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+0021</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">!</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">EXCLAMATION MARK</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#33;</code>).</p>
<p>Related forms are encoded:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+01C3</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%"><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ǃ</span></span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LATIN LETTER RETROFLEX CLICK</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#451;</code>) (In IPA: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_click" title="Alveolar click">alveolar click</a>)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+203C</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">‼</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">DOUBLE EXCLAMATION MARK</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#8252;</code>) <i>(for use in vertical text)</i></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_various_fields">Use in various fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Use in various fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the symbol represents the <a href="/wiki/Factorial" title="Factorial">factorial</a> operation. The expression <i>n</i>! means "the product of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integers" title="Integers">integers</a> from 1 to <i>n</i>". For example, 4! (read <i>four factorial</i>) is 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24. (0! is defined as 1, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral_element" title="Neutral element">neutral element</a> in <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a>, not multiplied by anything.) Additionally, it can also represent <a href="/wiki/Uniqueness_quantification" title="Uniqueness quantification">uniqueness</a> or, if used in front of a number, it can represent a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subfactorial" title="Subfactorial">subfactorial</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computers">Computers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Computers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, the exclamation mark (sometimes called a "bang")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> corresponds to <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">character</a> 33 (21 in <a href="/wiki/Hexadecimal" title="Hexadecimal">hexadecimal</a>). It is therefore found in Unicode at <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+0021</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">!</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">EXCLAMATION MARK</span>. The inverted exclamation mark is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO-8859" title="ISO-8859">ISO-8859</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO-8859-1" title="ISO-8859-1">1</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO-8859-9" title="ISO-8859-9">9</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO-8859-15" title="ISO-8859-15">15</a> at position 161 (A1<sub>HEX</sub>) and in Unicode at <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+00A1</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">¡</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">INVERTED EXCLAMATION MARK</span>.</p>
<p>The name given to "!" by programmers varies according to their background. In the UK the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pling" title="Pling">pling</a> was popular in the earlier days of computing, whilst in the United States the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vociferation" title="Vociferation">shriek</a> was used. It is claimed that these word usages were invented in the US and <i>shriek</i> is from Stanford or MIT; however, <i>shriek</i> for the ! sign is found in the <i>Oxford English Dictionary</i> dating from the 1860s.</p>
<p>Several computer languages use "!" for various meanings, most importantly for logical negation; e.g. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/!%3D" title="!=">!=</a> B means "A is <a href="/wiki/Inequation" title="Inequation">not equal</a> to B", and !A means "the logical <a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">negation</a> of A" (also called "not A"). In the UK, <a href="/wiki/BBC_BASIC" title="BBC BASIC">BBC BASIC</a> used pling as an indirection operator, equivalent to <a href="/wiki/PEEK_and_POKE" title="PEEK and POKE">PEEK and POKE</a> of four <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">bytes</a> at once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BCPL" title="BCPL">BCPL</a>, the precursor of <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>, used "!" for pointer and array indirection: "!P" was equivalent to C's "*P" and "P!3" is equivalent to "P[3]" in C.</p>
<p>Plings are also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acorn_Computers_Ltd" title="Acorn Computers Ltd">Acorn</a> <a href="/wiki/RISC_OS" title="RISC OS">RISC OS</a> to denote an <a href="/wiki/Application_directory" title="Application directory">application directory</a>: a folder that when double clicked executes a program file inside called <i>!Run</i>. Other files in the appfolder generally contain resources the application needs to run. The appfolder can be viewed as a normal folder by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_click" title="Double click">double-clicking</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Shift_key" title="Shift key">shift key</a> held down. In addition, other special resource files such as <i>!Boot</i> (executed the first time the application containing it comes into view of the filer), <i>!Sprites</i> (an icon file containing icon definitions loaded if <i>!Boot</i> cannot be found) and <i>!Help</i> (a text, HTML or other executable file listed in the filer menu for the application) also start with a pling.</p>
<p>Early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> systems also used the exclamation mark as a separator character between hostnames for routing information, usually referred to as "<a href="/wiki/UUCP#UUCP_for_mail_routing" title="UUCP">bang path</a>" notation.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" title="Internet Relay Chat">IRC</a> protocol, a user's nickname and <a href="/wiki/Identifier" title="Identifier">ident</a> are separated by an exclamation mark in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hostmask" title="Hostmask">hostmask</a> assigned to him or her by the server.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Geek_Code" title="Geek Code">Geek Code</a> version 3, "!" is used before a letter to denote that the geek refuses to participate in the topic at hand. In some cases, it has an alternate meaning, such as <i>G!</i> denoting a geek of no qualifications, <i>!d</i> denoting not wearing any clothes, <i>P!</i> denoting not being allowed to use Perl, and so on. They all share some negative connotations however.</p>
<p>When computer programs display messages that alert the user, an exclamation mark may be shown alongside it to indicate that the message is important and should be read. This often happens when an error is made, or to obtain user consent for hazardous operations such as deleting data.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNIX" title="UNIX">UNIX</a> scripting (typically for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNIX_shell" title="UNIX shell">UNIX shell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a>), "!" is usually used after a "#" in the first line of a script, the <a href="/wiki/Interpreter_directive" title="Interpreter directive">interpreter directive</a>, to tell the OS what program to use to run the script. The "#!" is usually called a "hash-bang" or <a href="/wiki/Shebang_(Unix)" title="Shebang (Unix)">shebang</a>. A similar convention for <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a> files calls for the first line to begin with "%!", called "percent-bang".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>An exclamation mark starts <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=History_expansion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="History expansion (page does not exist)">history expansions</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Unix_shell" title="Unix shell">Unix shells</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bash_(Unix_shell)" title="Bash (Unix shell)">bash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tcsh" title="Tcsh">tcsh</a> where <tt>!!</tt> executes the previous command and <tt>!*</tt> refers to all of the arguments from the previous command.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/ML_(programming_language)" title="ML (programming language)">ML</a> programming language (including <a href="/wiki/Standard_ML" title="Standard ML">Standard ML</a> and <a href="/wiki/OCaml" title="OCaml">OCaml</a>), "!" is the operator to get the value out of a "reference" data structure.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Haskell_(programming_language)" title="Haskell (programming language)">Haskell</a> programming language, "!" is used to express strictness.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language)" title="Scheme (programming language)">Scheme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language)" title="Ruby (programming language)">Ruby</a> programming languages, "!" is conventionally the suffix for functions and special forms that mutate their input.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Swift_(programming_language)" title="Swift (programming language)">Swift</a> programming language, a type followed by "!" denotes an "implicitly unwrapped optional", an <a href="/wiki/Option_type" title="Option type">option type</a> where the compiler does not enforce safe unwrapping. The "!" operator "force unwraps" an option type, causing an error if it is nil.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The exclamation mark can be used in video games to signify that a character is startled or alarmed. In the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_(series)" title="Metal Gear (series)">Metal Gear</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paper_Mario" title="Paper Mario">Paper Mario</a></i> series, an exclamation mark appears over enemies' heads when they notice the player.</p>

<p>In recent <a href="/wiki/Internet#Social_impact" title="Internet">Internet culture</a>, especially where <a href="/wiki/Leet" title="Leet">leet</a> is used, multiple exclamation marks may be affixed with the numeral "1" as in <i>!!!!!!111</i>. The notation originates from a common error: when typing multiple exclamation points quickly, the typist may fail to hold the <kbd class="keyboard-key nowrap" style="border: 1px solid #aaa; -moz-border-radius: 0.2em; -webkit-border-radius: 0.2em; border-radius: 0.2em; -moz-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); -webkit-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); background-color: #f9f9f9; background-image: -moz-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -o-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -webkit-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: linear-gradient(to bottom, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); padding: 0.1em 0.3em; font-family: inherit; font-size: 0.85em;">⇧ <a href="/wiki/Shift_key" title="Shift key">Shift</a></kbd><kbd class="keyboard-key nowrap" style="border: 1px solid #aaa; -moz-border-radius: 0.2em; -webkit-border-radius: 0.2em; border-radius: 0.2em; -moz-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); -webkit-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); background-color: #f9f9f9; background-image: -moz-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -o-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -webkit-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: linear-gradient(to bottom, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); padding: 0.1em 0.3em; font-family: inherit; font-size: 0.85em;">1</kbd> combination that produces the exclamation mark on many <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_layout" title="Keyboard layout">keyboard layouts</a>. This error, first used intentionally as a joke in the leet linguistic community, is now an accepted form of exclamation in leet and derivative dialects such as <a href="/wiki/Lolcat" title="Lolcat">Lolspeak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Some utterances include further substitutions, for example "!!!111oneeleven".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fandom" title="Fandom">fandom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fanfiction" title="Fanfiction">fanfiction</a>, ! is used to signify a defining quality in a character, usually signifying an alternative interpretation of a character from a <a href="/wiki/Canon_(fiction)" title="Canon (fiction)">canonical</a> work. Examples of this would be "Romantic!Draco" or "Vampire!Harry" from <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> fandom. It is also used to clarify the current persona of a character with multiple identities or appearances, such as to distinguish "Armor!Al" from "Human!Al" in a work based on <a href="/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist" title="Fullmetal Alchemist">Fullmetal Alchemist</a>. The origin of this usage is unknown, although it is hypothesized to have originated with certain <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles">Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles</a> action figures, for example, "Football Player! Leonardo", "Rockstar! Raphael", and "Breakdancer! Michelangelo".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comics">Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

This <i>Action Comics</i> cover from 1959 ends every sentence with an exclamation point or <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">question mark</a>. Often, few or no <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">periods</a> would be used in the entire book.</div>

<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> comics of the mid-20th century, routinely use the exclamation point instead of the period, which means <i>the character has just realized something</i>; unlike when the question mark appears instead, which means the character is confused, surprised or he does not know what is happening. This tends to lead to exaggerated speech, in line with the other <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperboles</a> common in comic books. A portion of the motivation, however, was simply that a period might disappear in the printing process used at the time, whereas an exclamation point would likely remain recognizable even if there was a printing glitch. For a short period <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Editor-in-Chief" title="Editor-in-Chief">Editor-in-Chief</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, attempted to curb their overuse by a short-lived ban on exclamation points altogether, which led to an inadvertent lack of ending punctuation on many sentences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Comic book writer <a href="/wiki/Elliot_S._Maggin" title="Elliot S. Maggin">Elliot S! Maggin</a> once accidentally signed his name with an exclamation due to the habit of using them when writing comic scripts; it became his professional name from then on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Similarly, comic artist <a href="/wiki/Scott_Shaw_(artist)" title="Scott Shaw (artist)">Scott Shaw!</a> has used the exclamation point after his name throughout his career.</p>
<p>In comic books and <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a> in general, a large exclamation point is often used near or over a character's head to indicate surprise. A <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">question mark</a> can similarly be used to indicate confusion. This practice also appears in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chess">Chess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punctuation_(chess)" title="Punctuation (chess)">Punctuation (chess)</a></div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebraic_chess_notation" title="Algebraic chess notation">chess notation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punctuation_(chess)#.21:_Good_move" title="Punctuation (chess)">"!"</a> denotes a good move, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punctuation_(chess)#.E2.80.BC:_Brilliant_move" title="Punctuation (chess)">"!!"</a> denotes an excellent move, "?!" denotes a dubious move, and "!?" denotes an interesting, risky move. In some chess variants such as large-board <a href="/wiki/Shogi_variant" title="Shogi variant">Shogi variants</a>, "!" is used to record pieces capturing by stationary feeding or burning.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scrabble">Scrabble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Scrabble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a>, an exclamation mark written after a word is used to indicate its presence in the <a href="/wiki/Official_Tournament_and_Club_Word_List" title="Official Tournament and Club Word List">Official Tournament and Club Word List</a> but its absence from the <a href="/wiki/Official_Scrabble_Players_Dictionary" title="Official Scrabble Players Dictionary">Official Scrabble Players Dictionary</a>, usually because the word has been judged offensive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball">Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Exclamation points or <a href="/wiki/Asterisk" title="Asterisk">asterisks</a> can be used on scorecards to denote a "great defensive play".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_music">Popular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Popular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band "<i><a href="/wiki/!!!" title="!!!">!!!</a></i>" (pronounced "Chk Chk Chk") uses exclamation points as its name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPRatc07_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPRatc07-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the pop punk band <a href="/wiki/Panic!_at_the_Disco" title="Panic! at the Disco">Panic! at the Disco</a> dropped the exclamation point in its name; this became the "most-discussed topic on [fan] message boards around the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2009, the exclamation mark was re-inserted following the band's split.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band <a href="/wiki/Bomb_the_Music_Industry!" title="Bomb the Music Industry!">Bomb the Music Industry!</a> utilizes an exclamation mark in its name, as well as several album and song titles and promotional material. Examples include their songs "(Shut) Up The Punx!!!" and the album <i><a href="/wiki/Adults!!!:_Smart!!!_Shithammered!!!_And_Excited_by_Nothing!!!!!!!" title="Adults!!!: Smart!!! Shithammered!!! And Excited by Nothing!!!!!!!">Adults!!!: Smart!!! Shithammered!!! And Excited by Nothing!!!!!!!</a></i>.</p>
<p>American musician <a href="/wiki/Pink_(singer)" title="Pink (singer)">Pink</a> is known to use an exclamation point in her stage name, (P!nk),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> as well as three in the subtitle of her Greatest Hits album, <i>So Far!!!</i>, which was released in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The exclamation mark was the subject of a bitter argument between <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Benes" title="Elaine Benes">Elaine Benes</a> and her boyfriend, Jake Jarmel, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Seinfeld" title="Seinfeld">Seinfeld</a></i> episode, “<a href="/wiki/The_Sniffing_Accountant" title="The Sniffing Accountant">The Sniffing Accountant</a>”. Elaine got upset with Jake for not putting an exclamation mark at the end of a message about a friend having a baby. Jake took extreme exception to the trivial criticism and broke up with Elaine, putting an exclamation mark after his parting words: <b>“I’M LEAVING!”</b></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exclamation_mark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In musicals, an exclamation mark is usually used when the title of the show has the same title of a song within the act. Examples of this are shows like <i><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma!" title="Oklahoma!">Oklahoma!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Mia!" title="Mamma Mia!">Mamma Mia!</a></i>.</p>
