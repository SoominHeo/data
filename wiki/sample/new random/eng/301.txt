<p>The <i><b>Decretum Gratiani</b></i>, also known as the <i><b>Concordia discordantium canonum</b></i> or <i><b>Concordantia discordantium canonum</b></i>, is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">Canon law</a> compiled and written in the 12th century as a legal textbook by the <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a> known as <b>Gratian</b>. It forms the first part of the collection of six legal texts, which together became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Canonici" title="Corpus Juris Canonici">Corpus Juris Canonici</a></i>. It was used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonist" title="Canonist">canonists</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> until Pentecost (May 19) 1918, when a revised <i><a href="/wiki/1917_Code_of_Canon_Law" title="1917 Code of Canon Law">Code of Canon Law</a></i> (<i>Codex Iuris Canonici</i>) promulgated by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XV" title="Pope Benedict XV">Pope Benedict XV</a> on 27 May 1917 obtained legal force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decretum_Gratiani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was about 1150 that <a href="#Author">Gratian</a>, teacher of theology at the monastery of Saints Nabor and Felix and sometimes believed to have been a <a href="/wiki/Camaldolese" title="Camaldolese">Camaldolese</a> monk,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catenc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-catenc-2">[2]</a></sup> composed the work entitled by himself, <i>Concordia discordantium canonum</i>, but called by others <i>Nova collectio, Decreta, Corpus juris canonici</i>, also <i>Decretum Gratiani</i>, the latter being now the commonly accepted name. He did this to obviate the difficulties which beset the study of practical, external theology (theologia practica externa), i. e. the study of canon law. In spite of its great reputation and wide diffusion, the Decretum has never been recognized by the Church as an official collection.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is divided into three parts (<i>ministeria, negotia, sacramenta</i>). The first part is divided into 101 distinctions (<i>distinctiones</i>), the first 20 of which form an introduction to the general principles of canon Law (tractatus decretalium); the remainder constitutes a tractatus ordinandorum, relative to ecclesiastical persons and function. The second part contains 36 causes (<i>causœ</i>), divided into questions (quœstiones), and treat of ecclesiastical administration and marriage; the third question of the 33rd causa treats of the Sacrament of Penance and is divided into 7 distinctions. The third part, entitled "De consecratione", treats of the sacraments and other sacred things and contains 5 distinctions. Each distinction or question contains <i>dicta Gratiani</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Maxim_(philosophy)" title="Maxim (philosophy)">maxims</a> of Gratian, and canones. Gratian himself raises questions and brings forward difficulties, which he answers by quoting auctoritates, i. e. canons of councils, decretals of the popes, texts of the Scripture or of the Fathers. These are the canones; the entire remaining portion, even the summaries of the canons and the chronological indications, are called the maxims or dicta Gratiani. It is to be noted that many auctoritates have been inserted in the "Decretum" by authors of a later date. These are the <i>Paleœ</i>, so called from <a href="/wiki/Paucapalea" title="Paucapalea">Paucapalea</a>, the name of the principal commentator on the "Decretum". The Roman revisers of the 16th century (1566–82) corrected the text of the "Decree" and added many critical notes designated by the words Correctores Romani.</p>
<p>The <i>Decretum</i> is quoted by indicating the number of the canon and that of the distinction or of the cause and the question. To differentiate the distinctions of the first part from those of the third, question of the 33rd cause of the second part and those of the third part, the words de Pœn., i. e. <i>de Pœnitentiâ</i>, and de Cons., i. e. <i>de Consecratione</i> are added to the latter. For instance, "c. 1. d. XI" indicates the first part of the "Decree". distinction XI, canon 1; "c. 1., de Pœn., d. VI," refers to the second part, 33rd cause, question 3, distinction VI, canon 1; "c. 8, de Cons., d. II" refers to the third part, distinction II, canon 8; "c. 8, C. XII, q. 3" refers to the second part, cause XII, question 3, canon 8. Sometimes, especially in the case of well-known and much-quoted canons, the first words are also indicated, e. g., c. Si quis suadente diabolo, C. XVII, q. 4, i. e. the 29th canon of the second part, cause XVII, question 4. Occasionally the first words alone are quoted. In both cases, to find the canon it is necessary to consult the alphabetical tables (printed in all editions of Gratian) that contain the first words of every canon.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Author">Author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decretum_Gratiani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gratian (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin_language" title="Medieval Latin language">Medieval Latin</a>: <span lang="lat-med" xml:lang="lat-med"><i>Gratianus</i></span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon lawyer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>. He flourished in the mid 12th century. Little else is known about his biography.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graciano.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="269" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Graciano.jpg/180px-Graciano.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Graciano.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graciano.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gratian</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He is sometimes incorrectly referred to as Franciscus Gratianus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Johannes Gratian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catenc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-catenc-2">[2]</a></sup> or Giovanni Graziano. For a long time he was believed to have been born at the end of the 11th century, at <a href="/wiki/Chiusi" title="Chiusi">Chiusi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>. He was said to have become a monk at <a href="/wiki/Camaldoli" title="Camaldoli">Camaldoli</a> and then he taught at the <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Felix" title="St. Felix">St. Felix</a> in Bologna and devoted his life to studying canon law, but contemporary scholarship does not attach credibility to these traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Since the 11th century, Bologna had been the centre of the study of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>, as well as of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, after the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a></i> was rediscovered in western Europe. Gratian's work was an attempt, using early <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic</a> method, to solve seemingly contradictory canons from previous centuries. Gratian quoted a great number of authorities, including the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, papal and conciliar legislation, church fathers such as <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>, and secular law in his efforts to reconcile the canons. Gratian found a place in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a>'s <i>Paradise</i> among the doctors of the Church:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">

<p>He has long been acclaimed as <i>Pater Juris Canonici</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Latin" title="Ecclesiastical Latin">Latin</a>, "Father of Canon Law"), a title he shares with his successor <a href="/wiki/Raymond_of_Penyafort" title="Raymond of Penyafort">St. Raymond of Penyafort</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Textual_history">Textual history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decretum_Gratiani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Textual history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vulgate version of Gratian's collection was completed at some point after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Lateran_Council" title="Second Lateran Council">Second Lateran Council</a>, which it quotes. Research by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Winroth" title="Anders Winroth">Anders Winroth</a> established that some manuscripts of an early version of Gratian's text, which differs considerably from the mainstream textual tradition, have survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> With later commentaries and supplements, the work was incorporated into the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Canonici" title="Corpus Juris Canonici">Corpus Juris Canonici</a></i>. The <i>Decretum</i> quickly became the standard textbook for students of canon law throughout Europe, but it never received any formal official recognition by the papacy. Only the <a href="/wiki/1917_Code_of_Canon_Law" title="1917 Code of Canon Law"><i>Codex Juris Canonici</i> of 1917</a> put it out of use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crompton_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crompton-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Winroth" title="Anders Winroth">Anders Winroth</a> shows that the <i>Decretum</i> existed in two published <a href="/wiki/Recension" title="Recension">recensions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The first dates to sometime after 1139, while the second dates to 1150 at the latest. There are several major differences between the two recensions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first recension is a more coherent and analytical work.</li>
<li>The second recension places a much greater emphasis on papal primacy and power.</li>
<li>The second recension includes Roman law extracts taken directly from the <i>Corpus Juris Civilis</i>, whereas the first recension does not demonstrate substantial familiarity with Roman jurisprudence.</li>
</ul>
<p>These differences led Winroth to conclude that Roman law was not as far developed by 1140 as scholars had previously thought. He has also argued that the second recension was due not to the original author of the first recension (whom he calls Gratian 1), but rather another jurist versed in Roman law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, Winroth's thesis of two Gratians remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>This field of inquiry is hampered by ignorance of the compiler's identity and the existence of manuscripts with abbreviated versions of the text or variant versions not represented by Winroth's two recensions. One of these is the manuscript St. Gall, Stiftsbibliothek, 673 (=Sg), which some have argued contains the earliest known version (borrador) of the <i>Decretum</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but which other scholars have argued contains an abbreviation of the first recension expanded with texts taken from the second recension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gratian.27s_sources">Gratian's sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decretum_Gratiani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gratian's sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gratian's sources were Roman law, the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, the writings of (or attributed to) the <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Fathers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_decretal" title="Papal decretal">papal decretals</a>, the acts of <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">church councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synods</a>. In most cases, Gratian did not obtain this material from a direct reading of the sources, but rather through intermediate collections. Thanks to the research of modern scholars - in particular, Charles Munier, Titus Lenherr, and Peter Landau - we now know that Gratian made use of a relatively small number of collections in the composition of most of the <i>Decretum</i>, these being:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Lucca" title="Anselm of Lucca">Anselm (II) of Lucca</a>’s canonical collection, originally compiled around 1083 and existing in four main recensions: A, B, Bb, and C. Peter Landau suggests that Gratian probably employed a manuscript containing an expanded form of recension A which he calls recension A’;</li>
<li>the <i>Collectio tripartita</i> attributed to <a href="/wiki/Ivo_of_Chartres" title="Ivo of Chartres">Ivo of Chartres</a>, usually thought to date to 1095;</li>
<li>the <i>Panormia</i> of <a href="/wiki/Ivo_of_Chartres" title="Ivo of Chartres">Ivo of Chartres</a>, also usually dated to 1095, although several scholars have argued for a later date and some even question Ivo's authorship;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_of_St._Grisogono" title="Gregory of St. Grisogono">Gregory of St. Grisogono</a>'s <i>Polycarpus</i>, completed some time after 1111;</li>
<li>the <i>Collection in Three Books</i>, composed some time between 1111 and 1139, though dated by some to around 1123;</li>
<li>the <i>Glossa ordinaria</i> to the Bible.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other sources are known to have been used in the composition of particular sections of the <i>Decretum</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>'s <i>Etymologies</i> for DD. 1-9 (the so-called <i>Treatise on Laws</i>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alger_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Alger of Liège">Alger of Liège</a>'s <i>Liber de misericordia et iustitia</i> for C. 1;</li>
<li>the <i>Sententiae magistri A.</i> for the <i>De penitentia</i> and some other sections.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect">Effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decretum_Gratiani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gratian himself named his work <i>Concordia Discordantium Canonum</i> - "Concord of Discordancies of Canons." The name is fitting: Gratian tried to harmonize apparently contradictory canons with each other, by discussing different interpretations and deciding on a solution. This <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectical</a> approach allowed for other law professors to work with the <i>Decretum</i> and to develop their own solutions and commentaries. These legalists are known as the <a href="/wiki/Decretist" title="Decretist">decretists</a>.</p>
<p>These commentaries were called <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">glosses</a>. Editions printed in the 15th, 16th or 17th centuries frequently included the glosses along with the text. Collections of glosses were called "gloss apparatus" or <i>Lectura in Decretum</i> (see also <a href="/wiki/Glossator" title="Glossator">glossator</a>). Systematic commentaries were called <i>Summae</i>. Some of these <i>Summae</i> were soon in circulation as well and obtained the same level of fame as the <i>Decretum</i> itself. Early commentators included <a href="/wiki/Paucapalea" title="Paucapalea">Paucapalea</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magister_Rolandus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magister Rolandus (page does not exist)">Magister Rolandus</a>. The most important commentators were probably <a href="/wiki/Rufinus_(decretist)" title="Rufinus (decretist)">Rufin of Bologna</a> (died before 1192) and <a href="/wiki/Huguccio" title="Huguccio">Huguccio</a> (died 1210). Less well-known was the commentary of <a href="/wiki/Simon_of_Bisignano" title="Simon of Bisignano">Simon of Bisignano</a>, which consisted of the <i>Glosses on the Decretum</i> and the <i>Summa Simonis</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Lombard" title="Peter Lombard">Peter Lombard</a> borrowed and adapted from the <i>Decretum</i> when discussing <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penance</a> in his <i>Sentences</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
