<p>The current <b>coat of arms of Austria</b> has been in use, in various forms, by the <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Republic of Austria</a> since 1919. Between 1934 and the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">German annexation in 1938</a> Austria used a different coat of arms, which consisted of a double-headed eagle. The establishment of the Second Republic in 1945 saw the return of the original (First Republic) arms, with broken chains added to symbolise Austria's liberation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In translation, the <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazon</a> of the Federal Arms of the Republic of Austria reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Gules a fess Argent, escutcheon on the breast of an eagle displayed Sable, langued Gules, beaked Or, crowned with a mural crown of three visible merlons Or, armed Or, dexter talon holding sickle, sinister talon holding hammer, both talons shackled with chain broken Argent.</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>There are two different versions of the arms: One version in accordance with the Federal Constitution, in which the eagle is represented plain black, and another more artistic version in which the eagle's feathers are detailed. Both versions are used in parallel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The symbols and emblems used in the Austrian arms are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The Eagle:</i> Austria's sovereignty (introduced 1919)</li>
<li><i>The <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">escutcheon</a></i> Emblem of Austria (late Middle Ages, reintroduced 1915)</li>
<li><i>The <a href="/wiki/Mural_crown" title="Mural crown">mural crown</a>:</i> Austria as a republic (introduced 1919)</li>
<li><i>The sickle:</i> Agriculture (introduced 1919)</li>
<li><i>The Hammer:</i> Industry (introduced 1919)</li>
<li><i>The broken chains:</i> Liberation from <a href="/wiki/Austria_in_the_time_of_National_Socialism" title="Austria in the time of National Socialism">National Socialist dictatorship</a> (added 1945)</li>
</ul>
<p>Discussions about the arms have been triggered in the past by differing political interpretations, especially by the use of the hammer and the sickle and the broken chains, since the crossed <a href="/wiki/Hammer_and_sickle" title="Hammer and sickle">hammer and sickle</a> are a widespread symbol of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, as is the breaking of chains. Surveys have however confirmed, that understanding of the actual symbolism of the arms is widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diem.2C_Symbole_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diem.2C_Symbole-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the one hand the arms serve as a new republican symbol, on the other as a modified version of the historical Habsburg arms. The current version of the arms is often regarded as being reminiscent of the double-headed eagle of the Habsburg monarchy. According to this interpretation, the single headed eagle alludes, in the sense of the removal of the <a href="/wiki/Dexter_and_sinister" title="Dexter and sinister">left hand</a>, "Hungarian" head, to the removal of the eastern part of the Habsburg Empire. However, Addendum 202 to the 1919 <i><a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gesetz_%C3%BCber_das_Staatswappen_und_das_Staatssiegel_der_Republik_Deutsch%C3%B6sterreich" title="de:Gesetz über das Staatswappen und das Staatssiegel der Republik Deutschösterreich">Law on the State Arms and the State Seal of the Republic of German Austria</a></i> states expressly that the "new" single headed Austrian eagle is based not on the double headed eagle (symbol of the Habsburgs since 1804, and previously of the Holy Roman Empire), but rather on the "symbol of the legions of the Roman Republic", the <a href="/wiki/Aquila_(Roman)" title="Aquila (Roman)">Aquila</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diem.2C_Entwicklung.2C_1919_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diem.2C_Entwicklung.2C_1919-2">[2]</a></sup> The Austrian federal states have however retained pre-republican heraldic traditions (mostly heraldic images from the Middle Ages, but also diverse accoutrements such as archducal and ducal hats, and knights' helmets).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_tower_arms">The tower arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The tower arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AustriaCoA-1918.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="180" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/AustriaCoA-1918.svg/220px-AustriaCoA-1918.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/AustriaCoA-1918.svg/330px-AustriaCoA-1918.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/AustriaCoA-1918.svg/440px-AustriaCoA-1918.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AustriaCoA-1918.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reconstruction of the tower arms</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 31 October 1918, the council of state agreed upon the colours of the republic (red-white-red) as well as the new coat of arms composed personally by chancellor <a href="/wiki/Karl_Renner" title="Karl Renner">Karl Renner</a>. The coat of arms was composed quickly due to the need for having a seal at the peace talks after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The coat of arms consisted of a black tower representing the bourgeoisie, two crossed red hammers representing workers and a golden wreath of ears of corn representing farmers. The colours black, red and gold was deliberately chosen because they were the <a href="/wiki/National_colours_of_Germany" title="National colours of Germany">national colours of Germany</a>, the three colours associated with republican Germany (as opposed to red, white and black).</p>
<p>The new coat of arms was however withdrawn as it was met with heavy criticism from, for example, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Loos" title="Adolf Loos">Adolf Loos</a> and heraldists; the proposal was seen as too similar to commercial advertising. A decision by the council of ministers on 8 May 1919 replaced the tower arms with the eagle.</p>
