<p><b>Albert Szent-Györgyi von <a href="/wiki/Rapoltu_Mare" title="Rapoltu Mare">Nagyrápolt</a></b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>nagyrápolti Szent-Györgyi Albert</i></span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Hungarian" title="Help:IPA for Hungarian">[ˈnɒɟraːpolti ˈsɛnɟørɟi ˈɒlbɛrt]</a></span>; September 16, 1893 – October 22, 1986) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_American" title="Hungarian American">Hungarian American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiologist" title="Physiologist">physiologist</a> who won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is credited with discovering <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> and the components and reactions of the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>. He was also active in the Hungarian Resistance during World War II and entered Hungarian politics after the war.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Szent-Györgyi was born in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, Austro-Hungarian Empire, in 1893. His father, Miklós Szent-Györgyi, was a landowner, born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%A2rgu_Mure%C5%9F" title="Târgu Mureş">Marosvásárhely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> (today Târgu Mureş, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a>, and could trace his ancestry back to 1608 when Sámuel, a Calvinist predicant, was ennobled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At the time of Szent-Györgyi's birth, being of the nobility was considered important and created opportunities that otherwise were not available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaylord_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaylord-4">[4]</a></sup> (Miklós Szent-Györgyi's parents were Imre Szent-Györgyi and Mária Csiky).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His mother, Jozefina, a Roman Catholic, was a daughter of József Lenhossék and Anna Bossányi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Jozefina was a sister of <a href="/wiki/Mih%C3%A1ly_Lenhoss%C3%A9k" title="Mihály Lenhossék">Mihály Lenhossék</a>; both of these men were Professors of <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">Anatomy</a> at the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s_Lor%C3%A1nd_University" title="Eötvös Loránd University">Eötvös Loránd University</a>. His family included three generations of scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-7">[7]</a></sup> Music was important in the Lenhossék family. His mother Jozefina prepared to become an opera singer and auditioned for Gustav Mahler, then a conductor at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budapest_Opera" title="Budapest Opera">Budapest Opera</a>. He advised her to marry instead, since her voice was not enough. Albert himself was good at the piano, while his brother Pál became a professional violinist.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_research">Medical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2981" data-file-width="2002" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg/220px-WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg/330px-WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg/440px-WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WGBBBC_Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Szent-Györgyi in 1917 Italy</div>

</div>
<p>Szent-Györgyi began his studies at the <a href="/wiki/Semmelweis_University" title="Semmelweis University">Semmelweis University</a> in 1911,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-7">[7]</a></sup> then began research in his uncle's anatomy lab. His studies were interrupted in 1914 to serve as an army medic in World War I. In 1916, disgusted with the war, Szent-Györgyi shot himself in the arm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> claimed to be wounded from enemy fire, and was sent home on medical leave. He was then able to finish his medical education and received his MD in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-7">[7]</a></sup> He married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korn%C3%A9lia_Dem%C3%A9ny" title="Kornélia Demény">Kornélia Demény</a>, the daughter of the Hungarian Postmaster General that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, Szent-Györgyi began his research career in <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>. He switched universities several times over the next few years, finally ending up at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Groningen" title="University of Groningen">University of Groningen</a>, where his work focused on the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>. This work landed him a position as a <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> fellow at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a>. He received his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> from <a href="/wiki/Fitzwilliam_College,_Cambridge" title="Fitzwilliam College, Cambridge">Fitzwilliam College, Cambridge</a> in 1927 for work on isolating an <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid" title="Organic acid">organic acid</a>, which he then called "hexuronic acid", from adrenal gland tissue.</p>
<p>He accepted a position at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Szeged" title="University of Szeged">University of Szeged</a> in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-7">[7]</a></sup> There, Szent-Györgyi and his research fellow Joseph Svirbely found that "hexuronic acid" was actually the thus far unidentified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiscorbutic" title="Antiscorbutic">antiscorbutic</a> factor, known as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>. After <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Norman_Haworth" title="Walter Norman Haworth">Walter Norman Haworth</a> had determined the structure of vitamin C, and in honour of its antiscorbutic properties, it was given the formal chemical name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-ascorbic_acid" title="L-ascorbic acid">L-ascorbic acid</a>. In some experiments they used <a href="/wiki/Paprika" title="Paprika">paprika</a> as the source for their vitamin C. Also during this time, Szent-Györgyi continued his work on cellular respiration, identifying <a href="/wiki/Fumaric_acid" title="Fumaric acid">fumaric acid</a> and other steps in what would become known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krebs_cycle" title="Krebs cycle">Krebs cycle</a>. In Szeged he also met <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zolt%C3%A1n_Bay" title="Zoltán Bay">Zoltán Bay</a>, physicist, who became his personal friend and partner in research on matters of bio-physics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1937, he received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> "for his discoveries in connection with the biological combustion process with special reference to vitamin C and the <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fumaric_acid" title="Fumaric acid">fumaric acid</a>". Albert Szent-Györgyi offered all of his Nobel prize money to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> in 1940. (The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_Volunteers_in_the_Winter_War" title="Hungarian Volunteers in the Winter War">Hungarian Volunteers in the Winter War</a> travelled to fight for the Finns after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Finland" title="Soviet invasion of Finland">Soviet invasion of Finland</a> in 1939.)</p>
<p>In 1938, he began work on the <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> movement. He found that muscles contain <a href="/wiki/Actin" title="Actin">actin</a>, which when combined with the protein <a href="/wiki/Myosin" title="Myosin">myosin</a> and the energy source <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, contract muscle fibers.</p>
<p>In 1947, Szent-Györgyi established the Institute for Muscle Research at the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole,_Massachusetts" title="Woods Hole, Massachusetts">Woods Hole, Massachusetts</a> with financial support from Hungarian businessman Stephen Rath. However, Szent-Györgyi still faced funding difficulties for several years, due to his foreign status and former association with the government of a Communist nation. In 1948, he received a research position with the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> (NIH) in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a> and began dividing his time between there and Woods Hole. In 1950, grants from the <a href="/wiki/Armour_and_Company" title="Armour and Company">Armour Meat Company</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a> allowed him to establish the Institute for Muscle Research.</p>
<p>During the 1950s, Szent-Györgyi began using <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopes</a> to study muscles at the subunit level. He received the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Lasker_Award_for_Basic_Medical_Research" title="Albert Lasker Award for Basic Medical Research">Lasker Award</a> in 1954. In 1955, he became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalized_citizen" title="Naturalized citizen">naturalized citizen</a> of the United States. He became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1956.</p>
<p>In the late 1950s, Szent-Györgyi developed a research interest in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and developed ideas on applying the theories of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> to the biochemistry (<a href="/wiki/Quantum_biology" title="Quantum biology">quantum biology</a>) of cancer. The death of Rath, who had acted as the financial administrator of the Institute for Muscle Research, left Szent-Györgyi in a financial mess. Szent-Györgyi refused to submit government grants which required him to provide minute details on exactly how he intended to spend the research dollars and what he expected to find. After Szent-Györgyi commented on his financial hardships in a 1971 newspaper interview, attorney Franklin Salisbury contacted him and later helped him establish a private nonprofit organization, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Foundation_for_Cancer_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Foundation for Cancer Research (page does not exist)">National Foundation for Cancer Research</a>. Late in life, Szent-Györgyi began to pursue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a> as a potential cause of cancer. He came to see cancer as being ultimately an electronic problem at the molecular level. In 1974, reflecting his interests in quantum physics, he proposed the term "<a href="/wiki/Negentropy" title="Negentropy">syntropy</a>" replace the term "<a href="/wiki/Negentropy" title="Negentropy">negentropy</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ralph Moss, a protégé of his in the years he performed his cancer research, wrote a biography entitled: "Free Radical: Albert Szent-Gyorgyi and the Battle over Vitamin C", <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0913729787">ISBN 0-913729-78-7</a>, (1988), Paragon House Publishers, New York. Aspects of this work are an important precursor to what is now dubbed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redox_signaling" title="Redox signaling">redox signaling</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statement_on_scientific_discovery">Statement on scientific discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Statement on scientific discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Albert Szent-Györgyi, who realized that "a discovery must be, by definition, at variance with existing knowledge,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> divided scientists into two categories: the <a href="/wiki/Apollonian_and_Dionysian" title="Apollonian and Dionysian">Apollonians and the Dionysians</a>. He called scientific dissenters, who explored "the fringes of knowledge," Dionysians. He wrote, "In science the Apollonian tends to develop established lines to perfection, while the Dionysian rather relies on intuition and is more likely to open new, unexpected alleys for research...The future of mankind depends on the progress of science, and the progress of science depends on the support it can find. Support mostly takes the form of grants, and the present methods of distributing grants unduly favor the Apollonian."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement_in_politics">Involvement in politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Involvement in politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the government of <a href="/wiki/Gyula_G%C3%B6mb%C3%B6s" title="Gyula Gömbös">Gyula Gömbös</a> and the associated <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_National_Defence_Association" title="Hungarian National Defence Association">Hungarian National Defence Association</a> gained control of politics in Hungary, Szent-Györgyi helped his <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> friends escape from the country. During World War II, he joined the Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Resistance_during_World_War_II" title="Resistance during World War II">resistance movement</a>. Although Hungary was allied with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">Axis Powers</a>, the Hungarian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_K%C3%A1llay" title="Miklós Kállay">Miklós Kállay</a> sent Szent-Györgyi to Cairo in 1944 under the guise of a scientific lecture to begin secret negotiations with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>. The Germans learned of this plot and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> himself issued a warrant for the arrest of Szent-Györgyi. He escaped from house arrest and spent 1944 to 1945 as a fugitive from the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a>.</p>
<p>After the war, Szent-Györgyi had become well-recognized as a public figure and there was some speculation that he might become President of Hungary, should the Soviets permit it. Szent-Györgyi established a laboratory at the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s_Lor%C3%A1nd_University" title="Eötvös Loránd University">University of Budapest</a> and became head of the biochemistry department there. He was elected a member of Parliament and helped re-establish the Academy of Sciences. Dissatisfied with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> rule of Hungary, he emigrated to the United States in 1947.</p>
<p>In 1967, Szent-Györgyi signed a letter declaring his intention to <a href="/wiki/Tax_resistance" title="Tax resistance">refuse to pay taxes</a> as a means of protesting the U.S. war against Vietnam, and urging other people to take a similar stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He married Cornelia Demény, daughter of the Hungarian Postmaster-General, in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-first-9">[9]</a></sup> Their daughter, Cornelia Szent-Györgyi, was born in 1918. He and Cornelia divorced in 1941.</p>
<p>In 1941, he wed Marta Borbiro Miskolczy. She died of cancer in 1963.</p>
<p>Szent-Györgyi married June Susan Wichterman, the 25-year-old daughter of Woods Hole biologist Ralph Wichterman, in 1965. They were divorced in 1968.</p>
<p>He married his fourth wife, Marcia Houston, in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> They adopted a daughter, Lola von Szent-Györgyi.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Szent-Gy%C3%B6rgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Szent-Györgyi died in <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole,_Massachusetts" title="Woods Hole, Massachusetts">Woods Hole, Massachusetts</a> on October 22, 1986. He was honored with a <a href="/wiki/Google_Doodle" title="Google Doodle">Google Doodle</a> September 16, 2011, 118 years after his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2004, nine interviews were conducted with family, colleagues, and others to create a Szent-Györgyi oral history collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
