<p><b>Overtime</b> is the amount of time someone works beyond <i>normal working hours</i>. The term is also used for the pay received for this time. Normal hours may be determined in several ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>by custom (what is considered healthy or reasonable by society),</li>
<li>by practices of a given trade or profession,</li>
<li>by legislation,</li>
<li>by agreement between employers and workers or their representatives.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most nations have overtime <a href="/wiki/Labour_law" title="Labour law">labour laws</a> designed to dissuade or prevent employers from forcing their employees to work excessively long hours. These laws may take into account other considerations than the humanitarian, such as preserving the health of workers so that they may continue to be productive, or increasing the overall level of employment in the economy. One common approach to regulating overtime is to require employers to pay workers at a higher hourly rate for overtime work. Companies may choose to pay workers higher overtime pay even if not obliged to do so by law, particularly if they believe that they face a <a href="/wiki/Backward_bending_supply_curve_of_labour" title="Backward bending supply curve of labour">backward bending supply curve of labour</a>.</p>
<p>Overtime pay rates can cause workers to work longer hours than they would at a flat hourly rate. Overtime laws, attitudes toward overtime and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hours_of_work" title="Hours of work">hours of work</a> vary greatly from country to country and between different economic sectors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_off_in_lieu">Time off in lieu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Time off in lieu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Time off in lieu</i>; <i>compensatory time</i>; or <i>comp time</i> refers to a type of work schedule arrangement that allows (or requires) workers to take time off instead of, or in addition to, receiving overtime pay. A worker may receive overtime pay plus equal time off for each hour worked on certain agreed days, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_holidays" title="Bank holidays">bank holidays</a>.</p>
<p>In the United States, such arrangements are currently legal in the public sector but not in the private sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, non-exempt workers must receive at least one and one half times their normal hourly wage for every hour worked beyond 40 hours in a work week. For example, workers who clock 48 hours in one week would receive the pay equivalent to 52 hours of work (40 hours + 8 hours at 1.5 times the normal hourly wage). With comp time, the worker could (or would have to) forgo the 12 hours of overtime pay and instead take 8 paid hours off at some future date.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In some other jurisdictions, such as Canada, employers might be required to pay the overtime at the higher rate (e.g. 1.5 times the normal rate), but also be allowed to require time off in lieu at the <b>normal</b> rate. Thus, an employee might work 48 hours in one week, and 32 hours the next week (assuming over 40 hours is overtime), and be paid an extra amount equivalent to 4 hours work (8 multiplied by 0.5).</p>
<p>In Australia, such arrangements both in the private and public sector are common.</p>
<p>In some cases, particularly when employees are represented by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_union" title="Labour union">labour union</a>, overtime may be paid at a higher rate than 1.5 times the hourly pay. In some factories, for example, if workers are required to work on a Sunday, they may be paid twice their regular rate, i.e., double time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overtime_laws_by_jurisdiction">Overtime laws by jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overtime laws by jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Directive_(European_Union)" title="Directive (European Union)">Directives</a> issued by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> must be incorporated into law by member states.</p>
<p>Directives 93/104/EC (1993), 2000/34/EC (2000), which limited working hours, were consolidated into 2003/88/EC (2003). Employers and employees can agree to opt out, under certain circumstances.</p>
<p>The directives require:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>maximum average working week (including overtime) of 48 hours over a 17-week reference period</li>
<li>minimum daily rest period of 11 consecutive hours in every 24</li>
<li>breaks when the working day exceeds 6 hours</li>
<li>minimum weekly rest period of 24 hours plus the 11 hours daily rest period in every 7-day period</li>
<li>minimum of 4 weeks paid annual leave</li>
<li>night work restricted to an average of 8 hours in any 24-hour period</li>
</ul>
<p>The directives apply to:</p>
<ul>
<li>all sectors of activity, both public and private</li>
<li>Doctors in training used to work a maximum week of 58 hours until 2009. From 1 August 2009 their maximum working week fell to 48 hours.</li>
</ul>
<p>Exemptions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Member States of the EC may exempt: managing executives or other persons with autonomous decision-making power; family workers; and workers officiating at religious ceremonies. These are workers whose working time is not measured or pre-determined or can be determined by the workers.</li>
<li>Other categories can be exempted from the directive's key provisions provided compensatory rest or appropriate protection is granted. These include employees who work a long way from home, or whose activities require a permanent presence or continuity of service or production, or who work in sectors which have peaks of activity. Examples include off-shore workers, security guards, journalists, emergency workers, agricultural workers, tour guides, etc.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Japan the <a href="/wiki/Labor_Standards_Act_of_1947" title="Labor Standards Act of 1947">Labour Standards Act</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">労働基準法</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> of 1947 provides for an eight-hour work day and 40-hour workweek with at least one day off per week. The act requires a premium of at least 25% over the ordinary hourly wage for any overtime work, 35% for any work on prescribed off days, and an additional 25% for any work between 10 pm and 5 am.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Employers must enter into an overtime agreement with a labour representative prior to any overtime work by employees, and this agreement must stipulate to the maximum number of overtime hours that an employee may work, which may be no more than 15 hours per week, 45 hours per month and 360 hours per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_overtime_law">Federal overtime law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Federal overtime law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the United States the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Labor_Standards_Act" title="Fair Labor Standards Act">Fair Labor Standards Act</a> of 1938 applies to employees in industries engaged in or producing goods for interstate commerce. The FLSA establishes a standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work_week" title="Work week">work week</a> of 40 hours for certain kinds of workers, and mandates payment for overtime hours to those workers of one and one-half times the workers' normal rate of pay for any time worked above 40 hours. The law creates two broad categories of employees, those who are "exempt" from the regulation and those who are "non-exempt". Under the law, employers are not required to pay exempt employees overtime but must do so for non-exempt employees. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_contractors" title="Independent contractors">Independent contractors</a> are not considered employees and therefore are not protected by the FLSA. Several factors determine whether a worker is an employee, who might be entitled to overtime compensation, or an independent contractor, who would not be so entitled. The employment agreement stating that a party is an independent contractor does not make it necessarily so. The nature of a job determines whether an employee is entitled to overtime pay, not employment status or the field of work. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Classes of workers who are exempt from the regulation include certain types of administrative, professional, and executive employees. To qualify as an administrative, professional, or executive employee and therefore not be entitled to overtime, three tests must be passed based on salary basis, duties, and salary level. The tests vary between administrative, professional, and executive employees based on their different duties and salary levels. There are many other classes of workers who may be exempt including outside salespeople, certain agricultural employees, certain live-in employees, and certain transportation employees. Employees can neither waive their FLSA protections nor abridge them by contract.</p>
<p>An employer may not retaliate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> against an employee for filing a complaint or instituting a proceeding based on the FLSA. An employer that engages in any form of verifiable retaliation would be liable under the <a class="external text" href="http://finduslaw.com/fair_labor_standards_act_flsa_29_uscode_chapter_8#16" rel="nofollow">Fair Labor Standards Act Section 216(b)</a> for equitable relief including reinstatement, promotion, payment of lost wages, and payment of liquidated damages. Acts of retaliation include terminating employment, disrupting the workplace, threats, acts of physical violence, and constructive discharge.</p>
<p>Out of approximately 120 million American workers, nearly 50 million are exempt from overtime laws (US Department of Labor, <a href="/wiki/Wage_and_Hour_Division" title="Wage and Hour Division">Wage and Hour Division</a>, 1998). In 2004, the United States was 7th out of 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> countries in terms of annual working hours per worker. (See <a href="/wiki/Working_time" title="Working time">Working time</a> for a complete listing.) In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">United States Department of Labor</a> proposed dramatic changes to certain exemptions from federal minimum wage and overtime requirements. These changes are anticipated to take effect in July 2016, but as of January 2016, still are pending final approval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Proposed changes include: setting the minimum salary level required for exemption for full-time salaried workers at $970 per week, or $50,440 annually (an increase from the current $455 per week, or $23,660 annually) Increasing the total annual compensation required to exempt highly compensated employees to $122,148 annually (from the current $100,000 annually).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 23, 2004, President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">Department of Labor</a> proposed changes to regulations governing implementation of the law. According to one study, the changes would have had significant impact on the number of workers covered by overtime laws and have exempted several million additional workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Bush administration maintained that the practical impact on working Americans would be minimal and that the changes would help clarify an outdated regulation. In particular, the new rules would have allowed more companies to offer <a href="/wiki/Flextime" title="Flextime">flextime</a> to their workers in lieu of overtime.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="California_overtime_law">California overtime law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overtime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: California overtime law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s overtime laws involve overlapping <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statutes" title="Statutes">statutes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regulations" title="Regulations">regulations</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precedents" title="Precedents">precedents</a> that govern the compensation of employees in California. While the governing federal law is the <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/fair_labor_standards_act_flsa_29_u_s_code_chapter_8" rel="nofollow">Fair Labor Standards Act</a> (29 USC 201-219), California overtime law is codified in provisions of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/california_labor_code" rel="nofollow">California Labor Code</a> and in <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/industrial_welfare_commission_wage_orders" rel="nofollow">Wage orders</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Welfare_Commission" title="Industrial Welfare Commission">Industrial Welfare Commission</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Because there are two sources of applicable law (federal and state), a California employer must comply with both.</p>
<p>In California, based on <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/node/6/#55" rel="nofollow">California Labor Code 1171</a>, only an <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/node/6/#55" rel="nofollow">employment relationship</a> is required for overtime rules to apply. Under the California <a class="external text" href="http://finduslaw.com/industrial_welfare_commission_wage_orders" rel="nofollow">Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Orders</a>, an "employer" is "any person ... who directly or indirectly, or through an agent or any other person, employs or exercises control over wages, hours, or working conditions of any person." Under the <a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/california_labor_code" rel="nofollow">California Labor Code</a>, an "<a class="external text" href="http://www.finduslaw.com/node/4/#16" rel="nofollow">employee</a>" is "[any] person, including aliens and minors, rendering actual service in any business for an employer, whether gratuitously or for wages or pay, whether the wages or pay are measured by the standard of time, piece, task, commission, or other method of calculation, and whether the service is rendered on a commission, concessionaire, or other basis." Independent contractors are not employees covered by overtime laws, so it is important to determine if a worker is an independent contractor or an employee.</p>
<p>California overtime laws differ from federal overtime laws in many respects. Foremost, pursuant to California Labor Code Section 510, non-exempt employees must be compensated at one and a half times the regular rate of pay for all hours worked in excess of eight hours in a workday, 40 hours in a workweek and the first eight hours of a seventh consecutive workday. Employees in California are entitled to double-time for working more than twelve hour workdays or more than eight hours on the seventh consecutive workday of a single workweek. Under federal law there are only 40 hour weekly overtime limits. This eight hour overtime limit in California frequently gives rise to wage-and-hour litigation for violations of state, but not federal, labour laws.</p>
<p>For example, "comp time" schemes, where employers tell employees that since they worked 10 hours on Monday they can work 6 hours on Tuesday, are illegal because even though the employees are not working more than 40 hours for the purposes of overtime compensation under federal law, they are working more than 8 hours for purposes of California overtime law and rounding the 6- and 10-hour workdays to two 8-hour workdays would cheat the employee out of two hours of overtime pay.</p>
<p>Perhaps the biggest difference between California and federal overtime law relates to the <a class="external text" href="http://www.dir.ca.gov/glossary.asp?letter=A" rel="nofollow">administrative exemption's</a> "primarily engaged" in duties that meet the test for the exemption requirement, such as duties that involve exercising independent discretion and judgment as set forth in the controversial <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110629143550/http://www.dir.ca.gov/iwc/WageOrders2006/iwcarticle4.htmlWage" rel="nofollow">Order No. 4</a>. Whereas under the Fair Labor Standards Act "primarily engaged" does not necessarily mean at least half, under California wage-and-hour laws, less than half of exempt duties automatically eliminates the overtime exemption.</p>
