<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Physiocracy</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<a href="/wiki/Pierre_Samuel_du_Pont_de_Nemours" title="Pierre Samuel du Pont de Nemours">Pierre Samuel du Pont de Nemours</a>, a prominent physiocrat. In his book <i>la Physiocratie</i>, du Pont advocated low tariffs and free trade.</div>

<p><b>Physiocracy</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> for "government of nature") is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_theory" title="Economic theory">economic theory</a> developed by a group of 18th century <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economists" title="Economists">economists</a> who believed that the wealth of nations was derived solely from the value of "land <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>" or "land development" and that agricultural products should be highly priced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Their theories originated in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and were most popular during the second half of the 18th century. Physiocracy is perhaps the first well-developed theory of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>.</p>
<p>The movement was particularly dominated by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a> (1694–1774) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne-Robert-Jacques_Turgot" title="Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot">Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot</a> (1727–1781).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_2003_p62_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner_2003_p62-2">[2]</a></sup> It immediately preceded the first modern school, <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economics</a>, which began with the publication of <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i> in 1776.</p>
<p>The most significant contribution of the physiocrats was their emphasis on productive work as the source of national wealth. This is in contrast to earlier schools, in particular <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a>, which often focused on the ruler's wealth, accumulation of gold, or the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_trade" title="Balance of trade">balance of trade</a>. Whereas, the mercantilist school of economics said that value in the products of society was created at the point of sale,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by the seller exchanging his products for more money than the products had "previously" been worth, the physiocratic school of economics was the first to see labor as the sole source of value. However, for the physiocrats, only agricultural labor created this value in the products of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> All "industrial" and non-agricultural labor was "unproductive appendages" to agricultural labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time the physiocrats were formulating their ideas, economies were almost entirely agrarian. That is presumably why the theory considered only agricultural labor to be valuable. Physiocrats viewed the production of goods and services as consumption of the agricultural surplus, since the main source of power was from human or animal muscle and all energy was derived from the surplus from agricultural production. Profit in capitalist production was really only the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_Rent" title="Economic Rent">rent</a>" obtained by the owner of the land on which the agricultural production was taking place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historian <a href="/wiki/David_B._Danbom" title="David B. Danbom">David B. Danbom</a> explains, "The physiocrats damned cities for their artificiality and praised more natural styles of living. They celebrated farmers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They called themselves <i><a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">Les Économistes</a></i>, but are generally referred to as physiocrats to distinguish them from the many schools of economic thought that followed them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">Precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Physiocracy is an <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarianist</a> philosophy. In the late <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, the dominant <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">senatorial class</a> was not allowed to engage in banking or commerce<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byrd.2C_34_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Byrd.2C_34-9">[9]</a></sup> but relied on their <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latifundia" title="Latifundia">latifundia</a></i>, large plantations, for income. They circumvented this rule through freedmen proxies who sold surplus agricultural goods.</p>
<p>Another inspiration came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_China_(pre-1911)" title="Economic history of China (pre-1911)">China's economic system</a>, then the largest in the world. Chinese society broadly distinguished <a href="/wiki/Four_occupations" title="Four occupations">four occupations</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholar-bureaucrats" title="Scholar-bureaucrats">scholar-bureaucrats</a> (who were also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gentry_(China)" title="Gentry (China)">agrarian landlords</a>) at the top and merchants at the bottom (because they did not produce but only distributed goods made by others). Leading physiocrats like <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a> were avid <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianists</a> who advocated China's agrarian policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some scholars have advocated connections with the school of <a href="/wiki/Agriculturalism" title="Agriculturalism">agriculturalism</a>, which promoted utopian communalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One of the integral part of physiocracy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laissez_faire" title="Laissez faire">laissez faire</a>, was adopted from Quesnay's writings on China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baghdiantz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baghdiantz-12">[12]</a></sup> being a translation of the Chinese term <a href="/wiki/Wu_wei" title="Wu wei">wu wei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The concept natural order of physiocracy originated from "Way of Nature" of Chinese Taoism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derk_page6_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Derk_page6-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pierre_Le_Pesant,_sieur_de_Boisguilbert" title="Pierre Le Pesant, sieur de Boisguilbert">Pierre Le Pesant, sieur de Boisguilbert</a> served as a member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a>'s local administration of Paris, and wrote pamphlets and booklets on subjects related to his work: taxation, grain trade, and money. Le Pesant asserted that wealth came from <a href="/wiki/Homo_economicus" title="Homo economicus">self-interest</a> and markets are connected by money flows (i.e. an expense for the buyer is revenue for the producer). Thus he realized that lowering prices in times of shortage – common at the time – is dangerous economically as it acted as a disincentive to production. Generally, Le Pesant advocated less government interference in the grain market, as any such interference would generate "anticipations" which would prevent the policy from working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_2003_p._61_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner_2003_p._61-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>For instance, if the government bought corn abroad, some people would speculate that there is likely to be a shortage and would buy more corn, leading to higher prices and more of a shortage. This was an early example of advocacy of free trade. In anonymously published tracts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vauban" title="Vauban">Vauban</a> proposed a system known as <i>La dîme royale</i>: this involved major simplification of the French tax code by switching to a relatively <a href="/wiki/Flat_tax" title="Flat tax">flat tax</a> on property and trade. Vauban's use of statistics contrasted with earlier empirical methods in economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_2003_p._61_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner_2003_p._61-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around the time of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> between France and England (1756–63), the physiocracy movement grew. Several journals appeared, signaling an increasing audience in France for new economic ideas. Among the most important were the <i>Journal Œconomique</i> (1721–72), which promoted <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomy</a> and rational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Husbandry" title="Husbandry">husbandry</a> and the <i>Journal du commerce</i> (1759–62), which was heavily influenced by the Irishman <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cantillon" title="Richard Cantillon">Richard Cantillon</a> (1680–1734), both dominated by physiocrats; the <i>Journal de l'agriculture, du commerce et des finances</i> (1765–74) and the <i>Ephémérides du citoyen</i> (1767–72 and 1774–76).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_2003_p62_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner_2003_p62-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Claude_Marie_Vincent_de_Gournay" title="Jean Claude Marie Vincent de Gournay">Vincent de Gournay</a> (1712–59), the <a href="/wiki/Intendant" title="Intendant">Intendant du commerce</a>, brought together a group of young researchers including <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_V%C3%A9ron_Duverger_de_Forbonnais" title="François Véron Duverger de Forbonnais">François Véron Duverger de Forbonnais</a> (1722–1800) and one of the two most famous physiocrats, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne-Robert-Jacques_Turgot" title="Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot">Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot</a> (1727–81). The other, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a> (1694–1774), was among those writing prolifically in contemporaneous journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_2003_p62_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner_2003_p62-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Henry_George" title="Henry George">Henry George</a> in the United States advocated the collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_rent" title="Land rent">land rent</a> as the primary if not the sole source of public revenue.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tableau_.C3.A9conomique"><i>Tableau économique</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tableau économique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Tableau_%C3%A9conomique" title="Tableau économique">Tableau économique</a></i> or <i>Economic Table</i> is an economic model first described by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a> in 1759, which laid the foundation of the physiocrats’ economic theories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_1983_p.189_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel_1983_p.189-16">[16]</a></sup> It also contains the origins of modern ideas on the circulation of wealth and the nature of interrelationships in the economy. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The model Quesnay created consisted of three economic agents: the "proprietary" class consisted only of landowners; the "productive" class consisted of agricultural laborers; the "sterile" class was made up of artisans and merchants. The flow of production and cash between the three classes originated with the proprietary class because they owned the land and bought from both of the other classes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_order">Natural order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The physiocrats thought there was a "<a href="/wiki/Natural_order_(philosophy)" title="Natural order (philosophy)">natural order</a>" that allowed human beings to live together. Men did not come together via a somewhat arbitrary "<a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a>". Rather, they have to discover the laws of the natural order that will allow individuals to live in society without losing significant freedoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The concept of natural order originated in China. The Chinese had believed that there can be good government only when a perfect harmony exists between the "Way of Man" (governmental institutions) and the "Way of Nature" (Quesnay's natural order).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derk_page6_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Derk_page6-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individualism_and_laissez-faire">Individualism and laissez-faire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Individualism and laissez-faire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">Individualism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">Laissez-faire</a></div>
<p>The physiocrats, especially Turgot, believed that self-interest is the motivation for each segment of the economy to play its role. Each individual is best suited to determine what goods they want and what work would provide them with what they want out of life. While a person might labor for the benefit of others, they will work harder for their own benefit; however, each person's needs are being supplied by many other people. The system works best when there is a complementary relationship between one person's needs and another person's desires, and trade restrictions place an unnatural barrier to achieving one's goals. Laissez faire was popularized by physiocrat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vincent_de_Gournay" title="Vincent de Gournay">Vincent de Gournay</a> who is said to have adopted the term from <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a>'s writings on China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baghdiantz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baghdiantz-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_property">Private property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Private property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>None of the theories concerning the value of land could work without strong legal support for the ownership of private property. Combined with the strong sense of individualism, private property becomes a critical component of the Tableau's functioning. The physiocrats believed in the institution of private property, they saw property as a tree and its branches, as social institutions. They actually stated that landlords must enjoy 2/5 on the land surpluses. They also advocated that landlords should be given dues, otherwise they will take the land away from the cultivators.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diminishing_returns">Diminishing returns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diminishing returns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diminishing_returns" title="Diminishing returns">Diminishing returns</a></div>
<p>Turgot was one of the first to recognize that "successive applications of the variable input will cause the product to grow, first at an increasing rate, later at a diminishing rate until it reaches a maximum."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This was a recognition that the productivity gains required to increase national wealth had an ultimate limit, and, therefore, wealth was not infinite.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_capital">Investment capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Investment capital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both Quesnay and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne_Robert_Jacques_Turgot,_Baron_de_Laune" title="Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Baron de Laune">Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Baron de Laune</a> recognized that capital was needed by farmers to start the production process, and both were proponents of using some of each year’s profits to increase productivity. Capital was also needed to sustain the laborers while they produced their product. Turgot recognizes that there is opportunity cost and risk involved in using capital for something other than land ownership, and he promotes interest as serving a “strategic function in the economy.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_developments">Subsequent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiocracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Subsequent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ideas of the Physiocrats had an influence on <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>, and above all, <a href="/wiki/Henry_George" title="Henry George">Henry George</a>, who appears at first to have come to similar conclusions independently. George was the driving force behind what became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_Tax" title="Single Tax">Single Tax</a> movement, not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_Tax" title="Flat Tax">Flat Tax</a>. The Single Tax is a proposal for the use of the annual rental value of land (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_value_taxation" title="Land value taxation">land value taxation</a>) as the principal or sole source of public revenue.</p>
