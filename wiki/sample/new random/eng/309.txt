<p>The <b>1932 Summer Olympics</b>, officially known as the <b>Games of the X Olympiad</b>, was a major worldwide multi-athletic event which was celebrated in 1932 in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States. No other cities made a bid to host these Olympics. Held during the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, many nations and athletes were unable to pay for the trip to Los Angeles. Fewer than half the participants of the <a href="/wiki/1928_Summer_Olympics" title="1928 Summer Olympics">1928 Summer Olympics</a> in Amsterdam returned to compete in 1932. Even U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> skipped the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zarnowski_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zarnowski-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organizing committee put no record of the finances of the Games in their report, though contemporary newspapers reported that the Games had made a <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a> of US$1,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zarnowski_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zarnowski-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Host_city_selection">Host city selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1932_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Host city selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The selection process for the 1932 Summer Olympics consisted of one bid, from Los Angeles, which ultimately hosted the games. The selection was made at the 23rd IOC Session in Rome, Italy, in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-votes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-votes-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Highlights">Highlights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1932_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Highlights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium,_Los_Angeles,_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="462" data-file-width="586" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg/220px-The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg/330px-The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg/440px-The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium%2C_Los_Angeles%2C_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Australian_Olympic_Team_at_the_Olympic_Stadium,_Los_Angeles,_1932_-_photographer_unknown.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<ul>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Village" title="Olympic Village">Olympic Village</a> was built for the first time, in affluent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin_Hills" title="Baldwin Hills">Baldwin Hills</a>, occupied by the male athletes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Female athletes were housed at the Chapman Park Hotel on <a href="/wiki/Wilshire_Boulevard" title="Wilshire Boulevard">Wilshire Boulevard</a>.</li>
<li>The victory podium was used for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS-LANDMARK_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-LANDMARK-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Coliseum" title="Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum">Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum</a> was known in 1932 as <i>Olympic Stadium</i>.</li>
<li>Tenth Street, a major thoroughfare in Los Angeles, was renamed <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Boulevard_(Los_Angeles)" title="Olympic Boulevard (Los Angeles)">Olympic Boulevard</a> in honor of the Games of the Tenth Olympiad.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babe_Didrikson" title="Babe Didrikson">Babe Didrikson</a> won two gold medals in the <a href="/wiki/Javelin_throw" title="Javelin throw">javelin</a> and the hurdles event. She also competed in a jump-off for a silver in the high jump. Her technique in the jump-off was ruled illegal, leaving Didrikson with second place.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a>, only three nations took part. The host nation lost both matches, 1-24 to India and 2-9 to Japan, but still won a bronze medal.</li>
<li>Poland's <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82awa_Walasiewicz" title="Stanisława Walasiewicz">Stanisława Walasiewicz</a> won the gold medal in the women's 100 m; she would also win the silver medal in the event four years later. After her death in 1980, it was discovered that she was <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a> and would have been ineligible to participate.</li>
<li>The executive council of the IAAF suspended <a href="/wiki/Paavo_Nurmi" title="Paavo Nurmi">Paavo Nurmi</a> from international amateur athletics four months before the Games, pending an investigation into his amateur status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Less than three days before the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1932_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_10,000_metres" title="Athletics at the 1932 Summer Olympics – Men's 10,000 metres">10,000 m</a>, the seven members of the council formed a special commission and rejected his entries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eddie_Tolan" title="Eddie Tolan">Eddie Tolan</a> won both the 100 m and 200 m sprint events.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Romeo_Neri" title="Romeo Neri">Romeo Neri</a> won three gold medals in gymnastics.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helene_Madison" title="Helene Madison">Helene Madison</a> won three gold medals in swimming, while the Japanese upset the men's events and took all but one title.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takeichi_Nishi" title="Takeichi Nishi">Takeichi Nishi</a> (Baron Nishi) was the gold medalist with his horse Uranus in the equestrian <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_at_the_1932_Summer_Olympics" title="Equestrian at the 1932 Summer Olympics">show jumping individual event</a>. Nishi's gold medal is Japan's only gold medal in the equestrian event to this day. Nishi would later die in 1945 as an officer stationed in the defense of the island of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Iwo_Jima" title="Battle of Iwo Jima">Iwo Jima</a>, and as such is a main character in <a href="/wiki/Clint_Eastwood" title="Clint Eastwood">Clint Eastwood</a>'s film, <i><a href="/wiki/Letters_from_Iwo_Jima" title="Letters from Iwo Jima">Letters from Iwo Jima</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kusuo_Kitamura" title="Kusuo Kitamura">Kusuo Kitamura</a> won the gold medal in the men's 1500 meter freestyle swimming race. He was and continues to be the youngest ever male swimmer to win a gold medal at the Olympic Games.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dunc_Gray" title="Dunc Gray">Dunc Gray</a> won Australia's first cycling gold medal; he set a world record of 1m 13s in the 1000 <a href="/wiki/Track_time_trial" title="Track time trial">time trial</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dunc_Gray_Velodrome" title="Dunc Gray Velodrome">Dunc Gray Velodrome</a>, built for the <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics" title="2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Sydney Olympic Games</a>, was named after him.</li>
<li>Due to an official's error, the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1932_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_3000_metres_steeplechase" title="Athletics at the 1932 Summer Olympics – Men's 3000 metres steeplechase">3,000 m steeplechase</a> went for 3,460 m, or one extra lap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Venues">Venues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1932_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Venues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rosebowl.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rosebowl.JPG/220px-Rosebowl.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rosebowl.JPG/330px-Rosebowl.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rosebowl.JPG/440px-Rosebowl.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rosebowl.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Rose Bowl hosted the track cycling events for the 1932 Summer Olympics</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fifteen sports venues were used for there 1932 Summer Olympics. In order to control cost in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_States" title="Great Depression in the United States">Great Depression</a>, existing venues were used. They included two <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">golf courses</a>, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_park" title="City park">city parks</a>, three public highways, and a city road. The Swimming Stadium was the only new venue constructed for these games; it was built with temporary wooden seating. The Rose Bowl, constructed in 1921, was made into a temporary <a href="/wiki/Velodrome" title="Velodrome">velodrome</a> for <a href="/wiki/Track_cycling" title="Track cycling">track cycling</a> events with the help of a French engineer under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Union_Cycliste_Internationale" title="Union Cycliste Internationale">Union Cycliste Internationale</a> (UCI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rose-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-rose-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Coliseum" title="Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum">Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum</a>, constructed in 1923, was used as the Olympic Stadium;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stadium_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-stadium-10">[10]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamchist_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lamchist-11">[11]</a></sup> The Olympic Auditorium was constructed in 1924 in preparation for Los Angeles being awarded the Games; it was modified to meet the specifications of the boxing, weightlifiting, and wrestling federations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aud_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aud-12">[12]</a></sup> Long Beach Marine Stadium was created in 1925 when <a href="/wiki/Alamitos_Bay" title="Alamitos Bay">Alamitos Bay</a> was dredged, then further dredged seven years later in time for the 1932 Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elysian_Park,_Los_Angeles" title="Elysian Park, Los Angeles">Elysian Park</a>, the oldest city park in Los Angeles, was founded in 1886, and has been part of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">Los Angeles Police Department</a> (LAPD) training academy since 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Riviera Country Club opened in 1926 as the Los Angeles Athletic Club and was renamed Riviera by the time of the 1932 Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The swimming stadium, constructed adjacent to the Coliseum in 1932, was intended to be a temporary structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swim_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-swim-17">[17]</a></sup> Riverside Drive, Los Angeles Avenue, Vineyard Avenue, and the Pacific Coast Highway were common driving routes in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> at the time of the 1932 Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-road-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walk_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-walk-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Coliseum was the first home for the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Dodgers</a> <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) team when it moved from <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn, New York</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1958_in_baseball" title="1958 in baseball">1958 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The following year, it hosted the <a href="/wiki/1959_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game_(second_game)" title="1959 Major League Baseball All-Star Game (second game)">MLB All-Star Game</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1959_World_Series" title="1959 World Series">World Series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Once <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Stadium" title="Dodger Stadium">Dodger Stadium</a> was completed in <a href="/wiki/1962_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1962 Los Angeles Dodgers season">1962</a>, the Dodgers moved there where they have been since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Rams_(1946%E2%80%9394)" title="Los Angeles Rams (1946–94)">Los Angeles Rams</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) team used the Coliseum as its host stadium from <a href="/wiki/1946_Los_Angeles_Rams_season" title="1946 Los Angeles Rams season">1946</a> to <a href="/wiki/1980_Los_Angeles_Rams_season" title="1980 Los Angeles Rams season">1980</a> when it moved to <a href="/wiki/Anaheim,_California" title="Anaheim, California">Anaheim</a>, located southeast of Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It also hosted what would become known as <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_I" title="Super Bowl I">Super Bowl I</a> in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Even the <a href="/wiki/American_Football_League" title="American Football League">American Football League</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Chargers" title="San Diego Chargers">Chargers</a> used the Coliseum as a venue in 1960 until their move to <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The Coliseum continues to host <a href="/wiki/USC_Trojans_football" title="USC Trojans football">USC Trojans football</a> games to this day, and also hosted <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins_football" title="UCLA Bruins football">UCLA Bruins football</a> for a number of years. The Rams return to the Coliseum in 2016.</p>
<p>The track constructed in the Rose Bowl was given to the Tournament of Roses Association upon completion of the 1932 Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-rose-8">[8]</a></sup> The Bowl was expanded between 1932 and the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> three times, increasing its capacity from 83,000 in 1931 to 104,594 in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbsfact_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbsfact-28">[28]</a></sup> It hosted <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XI" title="Super Bowl XI">Super Bowl XI</a> in 1977, where the <a href="/wiki/1976_Oakland_Raiders_season" title="1976 Oakland Raiders season">Oakland Raiders</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/1976_Minnesota_Vikings_season" title="1976 Minnesota Vikings season">Minnesota Vikings</a> 32-14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbsfact_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-rbsfact-28">[28]</a></sup> It is the current home of <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins_football" title="UCLA Bruins football">UCLA Bruins football</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_Game" title="Rose Bowl Game">Rose Bowl Game</a>, and was the home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.A._Galaxy" title="L.A. Galaxy">L.A. Galaxy</a> soccer team for a number of years.</p>
<p>Elysian Park's <a href="/wiki/Shooting_range" title="Shooting range">shooting range</a> was left intact for the LAPD to use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-rose-8">[8]</a></sup> Sunset Fields Golf Club was renamed Brentwood Country Club in 1941 and is still in use as of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> All of the road courses were returned to public usage after the Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-road-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walk_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-walk-19">[19]</a></sup> The Olympic Auditorium continued to be of use for boxing and <a href="/wiki/Roller_derby" title="Roller derby">roller derby</a> events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> until June 2005 when it was bought to be used as a megachurch. Los Angeles Harbor continues to be a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_port" title="Sea port">sea port</a> in the Western United States, employing 919,000 people and generating <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a> 39.1 billion in annual wages and tax revenues as of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Riveria Country Club continues to host <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> events, hosting the <a href="/wiki/1948_U.S._Open_(golf)" title="1948 U.S. Open (golf)">1948 U.S. Open</a> and the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Championship" title="PGA Championship">PGA Championship</a> in <a href="/wiki/1983_PGA_Championship" title="1983 PGA Championship">1983</a> and <a href="/wiki/1995_PGA_Championship" title="1995 PGA Championship">1995</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Swim Stadium was renovated in 2003 and continues to be in use as of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a>, the Coliseum and the Rose Bowl were used as <a href="/wiki/Venues_of_the_1984_Summer_Olympics" title="Venues of the 1984 Summer Olympics">venues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>A total of 37 nations were represented at the 1932 Games. Colombia made its first appearance at the Olympic Games, and the Republic of China (with a single athlete) competed for the first time after its failed appearance at the <a href="/wiki/1924_Summer_Olympics" title="1924 Summer Olympics">1924 Games</a>.</p>
