<p><b>1. Fußball-Club Köln 01/07 e. V.</b>, commonly known as simply <b>1. FC Köln</b> or <b>FC Cologne</b> in English (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ʔɛf ˈtseː ˈkœln]</a></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Germany" title="Football in Germany">German association football club</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. It was formed in 1948 as a merger of the clubs <i>Kölner Ballspiel-Club 1901</i> and <i>SpVgg Sülz 07</i>. Köln play in the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a>, the highest league of German football.</p>
<p>The club's nickname <i>Die Geißböcke</i> ("The <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">Billy Goats</a>") refers to the club's <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a>, a male goat named <i>Hennes</i> after the veteran FC player and (later) manager <a href="/wiki/Hennes_Weisweiler" title="Hennes Weisweiler">Hennes Weisweiler</a>. The first <i>Hennes</i> was donated by a circus entrepreneur as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cologne_carnival" title="Cologne carnival">Cologne carnival</a> joke. The current mascot is <i>Hennes</i> VIII, and has been since 24 July 2006. Another nickname for the club, more common locally due to its ambiguity, is <i>FC</i> (often written as <i>Effzeh</i>), a common <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> abbreviation for football clubs. Characteristic for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colognian_language" title="Colognian language">dialect spoken around Cologne</a>, this is pronounced "EF-tsay",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup (October 2015)">Colognian IPA needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in contrast to the Standard German pronunciation of the abbreviation where the second syllable is emphasized (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ʔɛf ˈtseː]</a></span>).</p>
<p>Like many of Germany's other professional football clubs, 1. FC Köln is part of a larger sports club with teams in other sports like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_handball" title="Team handball">handball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">table tennis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a>. 1. FC Köln had around 78.500 members as of May 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, making it the fourth largest club in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predecessor_sides">Predecessor sides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Predecessor sides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kolner_BC.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="775" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Kolner_BC.png/250px-Kolner_BC.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Kolner_BC.png/375px-Kolner_BC.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Kolner_BC.png/500px-Kolner_BC.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kolner_BC.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Kölner BC was formed on 6 June 1901 by a group of young men who were unhappy as part of the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a> club FC Borussia Köln and far more interested in football. BC was a competitive side in the Zehnerliga West in the years before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> who took the <a href="/wiki/Western_German_football_championship" title="Western German football championship">Westdeutsche championship</a> in 1912 and advanced to the preliminary rounds of the <a href="/wiki/1912_German_football_championship" title="1912 German football championship">national finals</a>. Their next best result was a losing appearance in the 1920 league final, where they lost a 1–3 to <a href="/wiki/Borussia_M%C3%B6nchengladbach" title="Borussia Mönchengladbach">Borussia Mönchengladbach</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpVgg_Sulz.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/SpVgg_Sulz.png/100px-SpVgg_Sulz.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/SpVgg_Sulz.png/150px-SpVgg_Sulz.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/SpVgg_Sulz.png/200px-SpVgg_Sulz.png 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SpVgg_Sulz.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Historical logo of predecessor side <i>SpVgg Sülz</i></div>

<p>Spielvereinigung 1907 Köln-Sülz was established in 1907 as Sülzer Sportverein and on 1 January 1919 merged with Fußball Club 1908 Hertha Sülz to form SpVgg. They won the Westdeutscher title in 1928 and they too went out in the early rounds of the <a href="/wiki/1928_German_football_championship" title="1928 German football championship">national finals</a> in their turn on that stage. They went on to play as a top flight club in the <a href="/wiki/Gauliga_Mittelrhein" title="Gauliga Mittelrhein">Gauliga Mittelrhein</a>, one of sixteen premier level divisions established in 1933 in the re-organization of German football under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>. The side earned generally good results through the 1930s – including a divisional championship in 1939 – but then faltered in the early 1940s. After the 1941 season the Gauliga Mittlerhein was split into two new divisions: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_K%C3%B6ln-Aachen" title="Gauliga Köln-Aachen">Gauliga Köln-Aachen</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_Moselland" title="Gauliga Moselland">Gauliga Moselland</a>, which included clubs from occupied <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. Sülz struggled until they were united with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VfL_K%C3%B6ln_1899" title="VfL Köln 1899">VfL Köln 1899</a> for the 1943–44 season to form the combined wartime side Kriegspielgemeinschaft VfL 99/Sülz 07 which promptly won the Gauliga Köln-Aachen title by a single point over SG Düren 99 in a close race. The club did not play the next campaign as war overtook the region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_successful_new_club">A successful new club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A successful new club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the union of these two predecessor sides, 1. FC Köln began play in the tough <a href="/wiki/Oberliga_West_(1947%E2%80%9363)" title="Oberliga West (1947–63)">Oberliga West</a> in the 1949–50 season and by 1954 had won their first divisional championship. That same year they lost the <a href="/wiki/1953%E2%80%9354_DFB-Pokal" title="1953–54 DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokal</a> final 1–0 to <a href="/wiki/VfB_Stuttgart" title="VfB Stuttgart">VfB Stuttgart</a>. <i>Die Geißböcke</i> won their second divisional championship in 1960 and appeared in the <a href="/wiki/1960_German_football_championship" title="1960 German football championship">national final</a> against <a href="/wiki/Hamburger_SV" title="Hamburger SV">Hamburger SV</a>, where they went down to a 2–3 defeat. They went on to finish first in the Oberliga West in each of the next three seasons and again played their way to the national final in <a href="/wiki/1962_German_football_championship" title="1962 German football championship">1962</a> and <a href="/wiki/1963_German_football_championship" title="1963 German football championship">1963</a>. They won the '62 match 4–0 over <a href="/wiki/1._FC_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="1. FC Nürnberg">1. FC Nürnberg</a> resulting in entry to the <a href="/wiki/1962%E2%80%9363_European_Cup" title="1962–63 European Cup">1962–63 European Cup</a> where they were one of the favourites to win the trophy. In the first round Köln visited <a href="/wiki/Dundee_F.C." title="Dundee F.C.">Dundee F.C.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and lost 1–8, and despite winning the second leg back in Germany by 4–0 they were out of the tournament. In the following year's national final they lost 1–3 to <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuing_success">Continuing success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Continuing success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_(26842261950).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="5472" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg/220px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg/330px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg/440px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_Mannschaftsfoto_%2826842261950%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1963, FC Köln was selected as one of the original 16 teams to play in the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a>, Germany's new professional football league. Köln continued their winning ways by becoming the first ever <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_football_champions" title="List of German football champions">Bundesliga champion</a> in the league's inaugural <a href="/wiki/1963%E2%80%9364_Bundesliga" title="1963–64 Bundesliga">1963–64 season</a>. As German champions, Köln entered the <a href="/wiki/1964%E2%80%9365_European_Cup" title="1964–65 European Cup">1964–65 European Cup</a> where it met England's <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> at the quarter-final stage. After two 0–0 draws, a third game was played which was also a stalemate, this time 2–2. As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kicks_from_the_penalty_mark" title="Kicks from the penalty mark">penalty shootout</a> had not yet been introduced as the means of deciding a tie, Köln went out of the competition on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coin_toss" title="Coin toss">toss of a coin</a>. Ironically enough, there was the need for a second coin toss because the first time the coin stuck vertically in the ground. The club also became the first Bundesliga side to field a Brazilian player when it signed <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%A9z%C3%A9" title="Zézé">Zézé</a> for a then club record fee of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">DM</a> 150,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schnee_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schnee-2">[2]</a></sup> Domestically, Köln recorded a second-place finish in the <a href="/wiki/1964%E2%80%9365_Bundesliga" title="1964–65 Bundesliga">1964–65 Bundesliga</a> season and won its first <a href="/wiki/DFB-Pokal" title="DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokal</a> in <a href="/wiki/1967%E2%80%9368_DFB-Pokal" title="1967–68 DFB-Pokal">1967–68</a>.</p>
<p>At the start of the 1970s, Köln reached three DFB-Pokal finals in four seasons, losing all three; to <a href="/wiki/Kickers_Offenbach" title="Kickers Offenbach">Kickers Offenbach</a> in <a href="/wiki/1969%E2%80%9370_DFB-Pokal" title="1969–70 DFB-Pokal">1970</a>, <a href="/wiki/FC_Bayern_Munich" title="FC Bayern Munich">Bayern Munich</a> in <a href="/wiki/1970%E2%80%9371_DFB-Pokal" title="1970–71 DFB-Pokal">1971</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borussia_M%C3%B6nchengladbach" title="Borussia Mönchengladbach">Borussia Mönchengladbach</a> in <a href="/wiki/1972%E2%80%9373_DFB-Pokal" title="1972–73 DFB-Pokal">1973</a>. The team also achieved another second place Bundesliga finish in <a href="/wiki/1972%E2%80%9373_Bundesliga" title="1972–73 Bundesliga">1973</a> before reaching another DFB-Pokal final in <a href="/wiki/1976%E2%80%9377_DFB-Pokal" title="1976–77 DFB-Pokal">1977</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/Hertha_BSC" title="Hertha BSC">Hertha BSC</a> over two legs to win the trophy for the second time.</p>
<p>In 1977–78, FC Köln enjoyed its most successful season, winning the <a href="/wiki/1977%E2%80%9378_Bundesliga" title="1977–78 Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> title, its third national title overall, and retaining the <a href="/wiki/1977%E2%80%9378_DFB-Pokal" title="1977–78 DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokal</a>. This makes Köln one of only four clubs to have won <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)#Germany" title="Double (association football)">the double</a> in <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)#Bundesliga_era" title="Double (association football)">the Bundesliga era</a>.</p>
<p>Köln had another losing DFB-Pokal final appearance in <a href="/wiki/1979%E2%80%9380_DFB-Pokal" title="1979–80 DFB-Pokal">1980</a>, before winning the competition for a fourth time in <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_DFB-Pokal" title="1982–83 DFB-Pokal">1983</a>. In 1986, the club appeared in its first European final, losing 5–3 on aggregate to <a href="/wiki/Real_Madrid_C.F." title="Real Madrid C.F.">Real Madrid</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1986_UEFA_Cup_Final" title="1986 UEFA Cup Final">UEFA Cup Final</a>. Two second place Bundesliga finishes, in <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Bundesliga" title="1988–89 Bundesliga">1988–89</a> and <a href="/wiki/1989%E2%80%9390_Bundesliga" title="1989–90 Bundesliga">1989–90</a>, and another DFB-Pokal final loss in <a href="/wiki/1990%E2%80%9391_DFB-Pokal" title="1990–91 DFB-Pokal">1991</a>, marked the end of a glorious thirty-year period for FC Köln.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century:_ups_and_downs">21st century: ups and downs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1._FC_K%C3%B6ln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century: ups and downs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, the club's performance has been mixed. The FC holds the dubious distinction of the worst goal drought in Bundesliga history: in <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Bundesliga" title="2001–02 Bundesliga">2002</a>, the supporters had to wait 1034 excruciating minutes (equivalent to 11-and-a-half games) until <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cichon" title="Thomas Cichon">Thomas Cichon</a> found the back of the net again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_(27116427705).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2371" data-file-width="3557" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg/220px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg/330px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg/440px-1._FC_K%C3%B6ln_beim_Training_%2827116427705%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

1. FC Köln during training</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the early years of the Bundesliga, 1. FC Köln was the most successful club in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> in terms of total points won. Beginning in the early 1990s, however, the club's performance fell, and in <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Bundesliga" title="1997–98 Bundesliga">1998</a> it was relegated for the first time. Since about 2000, the side has been a "<a href="/wiki/Yo-yo_club" title="Yo-yo club">yo-yo team</a>", moving between the first and second divisions. It has returned to the Bundesliga at the end of the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_2._Bundesliga" title="2004–05 2. Bundesliga">2004–05 season</a> as <a href="/wiki/2._Bundesliga" title="2. Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a> champions after having been relegated the <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Bundesliga" title="2003–04 Bundesliga">season before</a>. There was little optimism about their return to the top flight as they were picked by German football magazine <i>kicker</i> as one of the clubs most likely to be relegated.</p>
<p>This prediction came true when Köln lost to <a href="/wiki/Hamburger_SV" title="Hamburger SV">Hamburger SV</a> 1–0 in the third-to-last match of <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Bundesliga" title="2005–06 Bundesliga">the season</a>. The club finished the season in second-last place and was relegated after conceding a league-worst 71 goals. The team's most prolific goal scorer was <a href="/wiki/Lukas_Podolski" title="Lukas Podolski">Lukas Podolski</a> with a total of 12 goals, who transferred to Bayern Munich after the end of the season. He also appeared with the <a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">Germany national team</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 FIFA World Cup</a>.</p>
<p>In late 2006, former coach <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Daum" title="Christoph Daum">Christoph Daum</a> was convinced to once again take the helm of the 2. Bundesliga club and succeeded in leading the club back to the Bundesliga in 2008. After a successful Bundesliga campaign in 2008–09, Daum left Köln for his former club <a href="/wiki/Fenerbah%C3%A7e_S.K._(football)" title="Fenerbahçe S.K. (football)">Fenerbahçe</a>. Köln's former star-striker Lukas Podolski returned for the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Bundesliga" title="2009–10 Bundesliga">2009–10 season</a>.</p>
<p>After a poor run of form in the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Bundesliga" title="2010–11 Bundesliga">2010–11 season</a>, recording only one win from its opening nine Bundesliga fixtures, Köln replaced coach <a href="/wiki/Zvonimir_Soldo" title="Zvonimir Soldo">Zvonimir Soldo</a> with <a href="/wiki/Frank_Schaefer" title="Frank Schaefer">Frank Schaefer</a>. Schaefer, who was originally in charge of the under-23 team of Köln, decided after the season that he would rather spend more time with his family than be a coach in the Bundesliga. Former <a href="/wiki/Norway_national_football_team" title="Norway national football team">Norwegian international</a> and recent <a href="/wiki/F.C._Copenhagen" title="F.C. Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> coach <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A5le_Solbakken" title="Ståle Solbakken">Ståle Solbakken</a> replaced him. After earning just eight points in the first 13 matches of the second half of the season, Schaefer and former Köln player <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Lottner" title="Dirk Lottner">Dirk Lottner</a> replaced Solbakken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The club, however, was relegated at the end of the season, finishing in 17th place.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_2._Bundesliga" title="2012–13 2. Bundesliga">2012–13 season</a>, under new trainer <a href="/wiki/Holger_Stanislawski" title="Holger Stanislawski">Holger Stanislawski</a>, Köln finished in fifth place in the <a href="/wiki/2._Bundesliga" title="2. Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a>, missing out on promotion back to the top division. In <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_2._Bundesliga" title="2013–14 2. Bundesliga">2013–14</a>, under new trainer <a href="/wiki/Peter_St%C3%B6ger" title="Peter Stöger">Peter Stöger</a>, Köln finished first in the 2. Bundesliga, and has been promoted back to the top division for the 2014–15 season.</p>
<p>FC Köln finished in 12th place in the Bundesliga at the end of the <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9315_Bundesliga" title="2014–15 Bundesliga">2014–15 season</a>. In the second succeeding season since the promotion, the club finished under <a href="/wiki/Peter_St%C3%B6ger" title="Peter Stöger">Peter Stöger</a> in ninth position in <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_Bundesliga" title="2015–16 Bundesliga">2015–16</a>, the best club finish in the Bundesliga since <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_Bundesliga" title="1991–92 Bundesliga">1991–92</a>. On 16 September 2016, after its third match of the season, Köln reached the top of the Bundesliga table for the first time in 20 years.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The team plays its home matches in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RheinEnergie_Stadion" title="RheinEnergie Stadion">Sportpark Müngersdorf</a> (official name: RheinEnergie Stadion), with a capacity of 50,000.<br/>
The official name <i>RheinEnergie Stadion</i> comes from a contract with the local power supplier RheinEnergie AG that will last until 2018.<br/>
However, most fans still call the stadium "Müngersdorfer Stadion", named after the suburb of Müngersdorf, where it is located.<br/>
The average attendance in the 2015–2016 season was 48.676.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
