<p><b>Shota Rustaveli</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka" xml:lang="ka">შოთა რუსთაველი</span>), <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">mononymously</a> known simply as <b>Rustaveli</b>, was a 12th-century <a href="/wiki/Georgians" title="Georgians">Georgian</a> poet. He is considered to be the preeminent poet of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_Golden_Age_(11th-13th_centuries)" title="Georgian Golden Age (11th-13th centuries)">Georgian Golden Age</a> and one of the greatest contributors to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_literature" title="Georgian literature">Georgian literature</a>. Rustaveli is the author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Knight_in_the_Panther%27s_Skin" title="The Knight in the Panther's Skin">The Knight in the Panther's Skin</a></i> (ვეფხისტყაოსანი, <i>Vepkhist'q'aosani</i>), a Georgian national <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shota_Rustaveli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little, if anything, is known about Rustaveli from contemporary sources. His poem itself, namely the prologue, provides a clue to his identity: the poet identifies himself as "a certain Rustveli." "Rustveli" is not a surname, but a territorial epithet which can be interpreted as "of/from/holder of <a href="/wiki/Rustavi" title="Rustavi">Rustavi</a>". Later Georgian authors from the 15th through 18th centuries are more informative: they are almost unanimous in identifying him as Shota Rustaveli, a name which is preserved on a fresco and a document from the formerly Georgian <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_the_Cross" title="Monastery of the Cross">Monastery of the Holy Cross</a> at <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The fresco was described by the Georgian pilgrim Timote Gabashvili in 1757/58, and rediscovered by a team of Georgian scholars in 1960. The same Jerusalem document speaks of Shota as a sponsor of the monastery and a "high treasurer", thus echoing a popular legend that Rustaveli was a minister at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Tamar" title="Queen Tamar">Queen Tamar</a>’s court and retired to the monastery at an advanced age. Both a folk tradition and the 17th-century royal poet <a href="/wiki/Archil_of_Imereti" title="Archil of Imereti">Archil</a> identify Rustaveli as a native of the southern Georgian region of <a href="/wiki/Meskheti" title="Meskheti">Meskheti</a>, where his home village Rustavi was located (not to be confused with the modern-day city of <a href="/wiki/Rustavi" title="Rustavi">Rustavi</a> near <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>). He is assumed to have been born between 1160 and 1165. A legend states that Rustaveli was educated at the medieval Georgian academies of <a href="/wiki/Gelati_Monastery" title="Gelati Monastery">Gelati</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ikalto" title="Ikalto">Ikalto</a>, and then in "<a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>" (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>). He must have produced his major work no earlier than the 1180s and no later than the first decade of the 13th century, most probably 1205-1207.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Knight_in_the_Panther.27s_Skin"><i>The Knight in the Panther's Skin</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shota_Rustaveli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Knight in the Panther's Skin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Knight_in_the_Panther%27s_Skin" title="The Knight in the Panther's Skin">The Knight in the Panther's Skin</a></i> has been translated into many languages. It was first printed in 1712 in the Georgian capital <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>. The manuscripts of <i>The Knight in the Panther's Skin</i> occupy an important place among the works produced in Georgia.</p>
<p>Two folios of this text, dating from the 16th century, are located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Manuscripts_of_Georgia" title="Institute of Manuscripts of Georgia">Institute of Manuscripts of Georgia</a> in Tbilisi, and some lines of the poem from the 14th century are also held there. All other copies of the poem date from the 17th century.</p>
<p>The highest Georgian state prize in the fields of art and literature is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shota_Rustaveli_State_Prize" title="Shota Rustaveli State Prize">Shota Rustaveli State Prize</a>. Tbilisi's main thoroughfare is Rustaveli Avenue. In Tbilisi, one can also find the <a href="/wiki/Rustaveli_Theatre" title="Rustaveli Theatre">Rustaveli Theatre</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shota_Rustaveli_Institute_of_Georgian_Literature&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shota Rustaveli Institute of Georgian Literature (page does not exist)">Shota Rustaveli Institute of Georgian Literature</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Georgian Academy of Sciences">Georgian Academy of Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rustaveli_(Tbilisi_Metro)" title="Rustaveli (Tbilisi Metro)">Rustaveli (Tbilisi Metro)</a>, among many other landmarks bearing his name.</p>

<p>The fresco representing the legendary Georgian poet Shota Rustaveli was vandalized in June 2004 in the 11th-century Georgian-built <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_the_Cross" title="Monastery of the Cross">Monastery of the Holy Cross</a>. An unknown vandal scratched out the face of Shota Rustaveli and part of the accompanying Georgian inscription with his name on the south-west pier. Georgia officially complained to Israel after the priceless fresco was defaced in Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shota_Rustaveli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 3, 2001, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and Georgia jointly issued <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a> to honor Shota Rustaveli. Designed by Yitzhak Granot, the Israeli stamp (3.40 NIS) showed the author with Hebrew text in the background.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mih.C3.A1ly_Zichy">Mihály Zichy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shota_Rustaveli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mihály Zichy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mih%C3%A1ly_Zichy" title="Mihály Zichy">Mihály Zichy</a>, a 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungarian</a> painter, rose to the rank of "national painter" in Georgia as he produced the classic illustrations that have been frequently used in editions of Rustaveli's poetry. A sculpture and a street commemorate Zichy's work in <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>.</p>
