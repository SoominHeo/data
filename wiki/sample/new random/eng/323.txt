<p><b>General Dynamics Corporation</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporation</a>. Formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merger" title="Merger">mergers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divestiture" title="Divestiture">divestitures</a>, it is the world's fifth-largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" title="Defense contractor">defense contractor</a> based on 2012 revenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DefenseNews_2013_ranking_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DefenseNews_2013_ranking-7">[7]</a></sup> General Dynamics is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/West_Falls_Church,_Virginia" title="West Falls Church, Virginia">West Falls Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company has changed markedly in the <a href="/wiki/Post%E2%80%93Cold_War_era" title="Post–Cold War era">post–Cold War era</a> of defense consolidation. It has four main business segments: Marine Systems, Combat Systems, Information Systems Technology, and Aerospace. General Dynamics' former Fort Worth Division manufactured one of the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>'s most-produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_fighter" title="Jet fighter">jet fighters</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>—until 1993, when production was sold to <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed</a>. In 1999, the company re-entered the <a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">airframe</a> business with its purchase of <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_Boat">Electric Boat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electric Boat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">General Dynamics Electric Boat</a></div>
<p>General Dynamics traces its ancestry to <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Holland" title="John Philip Holland">John Philip Holland</a>'s <b>Holland Torpedo Boat Company</b>. This company was responsible for developing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>'s first <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a> built at <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Nixon_(naval_architect)" title="Lewis Nixon (naval architect)">Lewis Nixon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Shipyard" title="Crescent Shipyard">Crescent Shipyard</a>, located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabethport,_New_Jersey" title="Elizabethport, New Jersey">Elizabethport, New Jersey</a>. The revolutionary submarine boat <i>Holland VI</i> was built there, its keel being laid down in 1896. Crescent's superintendent and naval architect, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Leopold_Busch" title="Arthur Leopold Busch">Arthur Leopold Busch</a>, supervised the construction of this submarine. After being launched on 17 May 1897, it was eventually purchased by the Navy and renamed <a href="/wiki/USS_Holland_(SS-1)" title="USS Holland (SS-1)">USS <i>Holland</i> (SS-1)</a>. The <i>Holland</i> was officially commissioned on 12 October 1900 and became the United States Navy's first submarine, later known as SS-1. The Navy placed an order for more submarines, which were developed in rapid succession and were assembled at two different locations on both coasts. These submarines were known as the A-Class or <i>Adder</i> Class, and became America's first fleet of underwater craft at the beginning of the 20th century.</p>
<p>Holland grew short on funds, due to the lengthy and expensive process of introducing the world's first practical submarines, and he had to part with his company and sell his interest to financier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isaac_Leopold_Rice" title="Isaac Leopold Rice">Isaac Leopold Rice</a>, renaming the new firm as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_Boat" title="Electric Boat">Electric Boat Company</a> on 7 February 1899. Holland effectively lost control of the company and found himself earning a salary of $90 a week as chief engineer, while the company that he founded was selling submarines for $300,000 each.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Holland resigned from the company effective April 1904. Rice became Electric Boat's first President, remaining there from that time until 1915 when he stepped down just prior to his death on 2 November 1915.</p>
<p>Electric Boat gained a reputation for unscrupulous arms dealing in 1904-05, when it sold submarines to Japan's <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a> and Russia's <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Navy" title="Imperial Russian Navy">Imperial Russian Navy</a>, who were then at war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salinger2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salinger2013-11">[11]</a></sup> Holland submarines were also sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Royal_Navy" title="British Royal Navy">British Royal Navy</a> through the English armaments company <a href="/wiki/Vickers" title="Vickers">Vickers</a>, and to the Dutch to serve in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Navy" title="Royal Netherlands Navy">Royal Netherlands Navy</a>. The new pioneering craft originally developed by the company was now legitimized as genuine naval weapons by the world's most powerful navies.</p>
<p>In the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> wind-down, Electric Boat was cash-flush but lacking in work, with its workforce shrinking from 13,000 to 4,000 by 1946.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Hoping to diversify, president and chief executive officer <a href="/wiki/John_Jay_Hopkins" title="John Jay Hopkins">John Jay Hopkins</a> started looking for companies that would fit into Electric Boat's market.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadair_purchase">Canadair purchase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canadair purchase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hopkins quickly found that <a href="/wiki/Canadair" title="Canadair">Canadair</a>, owned by the Canadian government, was suffering from similar post-war malaise and was up for sale. Hopkins bought the company for $10 million in 1946. Even by the Canadian government's calculations, the factory alone was worth more than $22 million,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> excluding the value of the remaining contracts for planes or spare parts.</p>
<p>When Electric Boat purchased Canadair, its production line and inventory systems were in disorder. Hopkins hired Canadian-born mass-production specialist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H._Oliver_West&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="H. Oliver West (page does not exist)">H. Oliver West</a> to take over the president's role and return Canadair to profitability. Shortly after the takeover, Canadair began delivering its new <a href="/wiki/Canadair_North_Star" title="Canadair North Star">Canadair North Star</a> (a version of the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">Douglas DC-4</a>), and was able to deliver aircraft to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-Canada_Airlines" title="Trans-Canada Airlines">Trans-Canada Airlines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Airlines" title="Canadian Pacific Airlines">Canadian Pacific Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> (BOAC) well in advance of their contracted delivery times.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As defense spending increased with the onset of the Cold War, Canadair went on to win many Canadian military contracts for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a>, and became a major aerospace company. These included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadair_T-33" title="Canadair T-33">Canadair T-33</a> trainer, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadair_Argus" title="Canadair Argus">Canadair Argus</a> long-range maritime reconnaissance and <a href="/wiki/Military_transport_aircraft" title="Military transport aircraft">transport aircraft</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadair_F-86" title="Canadair F-86">Canadair F-86</a> <i>Sabre</i>. Between 1950 and 1958, 1,815 Sabres were built. Canadair also produced 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CF-104_Starfighter" title="CF-104 Starfighter">CF-104 Starfighter</a> supersonic fighter aircraft, a license-built version of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter" title="Lockheed F-104 Starfighter">Lockheed F-104</a>.</p>
<p>In 1976, Canadair was sold back to the Canadian Government, which sold it to <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier Inc.</a> in 1986.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Dynamics_emerges">General Dynamics emerges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General Dynamics emerges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the aircraft production at Canadair became increasingly important to the company, Hopkins argued that the name "Electric Boat" was no longer appropriate. On 24 April 1952, Electric Boat was reorganized as General Dynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>General Dynamics was still flush with cash after the Canadair purchase, and, given the success of that company, it continued to look for new aviation purchases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In March 1953, it purchased <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> from the Atlas Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-12">[12]</a></sup> The sale was approved by government oversight with the proviso that General Dynamics would continue to operate out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Plant_4" title="Air Force Plant 4">Air Force Plant 4</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>. This factory was set up in order to spread out strategic aircraft production and rented to Convair during the war to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator" title="B-24 Liberator">B-24 Liberator</a> bombers. Over time, the Fort Worth plant became Convair's major production center.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As was the case with Canadair, Convair worked as an independent division under the General Dynamics umbrella. Over the next decade, the company introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-106_Delta_Dart" title="F-106 Delta Dart">F-106 Delta Dart</a> <a href="/wiki/Interceptor_aircraft" title="Interceptor aircraft">Interceptor</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-58_Hustler" title="B-58 Hustler">B-58 Hustler</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Convair_880" title="Convair 880">Convair 880</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convair_990" title="Convair 990">990</a> <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a>. Convair also introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_missile" title="Atlas missile">Atlas</a> missile platform, the first U.S. operational <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_churn">Management churn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Management churn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hopkins fell seriously ill during 1957, and was eventually replaced by Frank Pace later that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-12">[12]</a></sup> Meanwhile, John Naish succeeded Joseph McNarney as president of Convair. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Crown" title="Henry Crown">Henry Crown</a> became the company's largest shareholder, and merged his <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Material_Service_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Material Service Corporation (page does not exist)">Material Service Corporation</a> with GD in 1959.</p>
<p>Naish left in May 1961, taking most of Convair's top people with him.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> GD subsequently reorganized into Eastern Group in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and Western Group in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, with the latter taking over all of the aerospace activities and dropping the Convair brand name from its aircraft in the process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Frank Pace retired under pressure in 1962 and Roger Lewis, former Assistant Secretary of the Air Force and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_American_Airways" title="Pan American Airways">Pan American Airways</a> CEO, was brought in as CEO. The company recovered, then fell back into the same struggles. In 1971, the board brought in <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_S._Lewis,_Jr." title="David S. Lewis, Jr.">Dave Lewis</a> (no relation) as chairman and CEO, who served until retiring in 1985.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_powerhouse">Aviation powerhouse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aviation powerhouse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the early 1960s the company bid on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s TFX (Tactical Fighter, Experimental) project for a new low-level "penetrator". <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>, newly installed as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>, forced a merger of the TFX with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> plans for a new long-range "fleet defender" aircraft. In order to bid on a naval version successfully, GD partnered with <a href="/wiki/Grumman" title="Grumman">Grumman</a>, who would build a customized version for <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> duties. After four rounds of bids and changes, the GD/Grumman team finally won the contract over a <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> submission.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-111" title="General Dynamics F-111">F-111</a> first flew in December 1964. The F-111B flew in May 1965, but the Navy said that it was too heavy for use on aircraft carriers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With an unacceptable Navy version, estimates for 2,400 F-111s, including exports, were sharply reduced, but GD still managed to make a $300-million profit on the project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Grumman went on to use many of the innovations of the F-111 in the highly successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-14_Tomcat" title="F-14 Tomcat">F-14 Tomcat</a>, an aircraft designed solely as a carrier-borne fighter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reorganization">Reorganization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reorganization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 1965, GD reorganized into 12 operating divisions based on product lines. The board decided to build all future planes in Fort Worth, ending plane production at Convair's original plant in San Diego but continuing with space and missile development there. In October 1970, Roger Lewis left and David S. Lewis from <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> was named CEO. Lewis required that the company headquarters move to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, which occurred in February 1971.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="F-16_success">F-16 success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: F-16 success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1972, GD bid on the USAF's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightweight_Fighter" title="Lightweight Fighter">Lightweight Fighter</a> (LWF) project. GD and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Corporation" title="Northrop Corporation">Northrop</a> were awarded prototype contracts. GD's F-111 program was winding down, and the company desperately needed a new aircraft contract. It organized its own version of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a>'s famed "<a href="/wiki/Skunk_Works" title="Skunk Works">Skunk Works</a>", the Advanced Concepts Laboratory, and responded with a new aircraft design incorporating modern equipment.</p>
<p>GD's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="F-16 Fighting Falcon">YF-16</a> first flew in January 1974, and proved to have slightly better performance than the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_YF-17" title="Northrop YF-17">YF-17</a> in head-to-head testing. It entered production as the F-16 in January 1975 with an initial order of 650 and a total order of 1,388. The F-16 also won contracts worldwide, beating the F-17 in foreign competition as well. GD built an aircraft production factory in Fort Worth, Texas. F-16 orders eventually totaled more than 4,000, making it the largest and most successful program for the company, and one of the most successful western military projects since World War II.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_Systems_focus">Land Systems focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Land Systems focus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976, General Dynamics sold the struggling Canadair back to the Canadian government for $38 million. By 1984, General Dynamics had four divisions: Convair in San Diego, General Dynamics-Fort Worth, General Dynamics-Pomona, and General Dynamics-Electronics. In 1985 a further reorganization created the Space Systems Division from the Convair Space division. In 1985, GD also acquired <a href="/wiki/Cessna" title="Cessna">Cessna</a>. In 1986 the Pomona division (which mainly produced the Standard Missile and the Phalanx CIWS for the Navy) was split up, creating the Valley Systems Division. Valley Systems produced the Stinger surface-to-air missile and the Rolling Airframe Missile (RAM). Both units were recombined into one entity in 1992.</p>
<p>Henry Crown, still GD's largest shareholder, died on 15 August 1990. Following this, the company started to rapidly divest its under-performing divisions under CEO <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>. Cessna was re-sold to <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a> in January 1992, the San Diego and Pomona missile production units to <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_Aerospace" title="Hughes Aerospace">Hughes Aerospace</a> in May 1992, the Fort Worth aircraft production to <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a> in March 1993 (a nearby electronics production facility was separately sold to Israeli-based <a href="/wiki/Elbit_Systems" title="Elbit Systems">Elbit Systems</a>, marking that company's entry into the United States market), and its Space Systems Division to <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> in 1994. The remaining Convair Aircraft Structure unit was sold to McDonnell Douglas in 1994. The remains of the Convair Division were simply closed in 1996. GD's exit from the aviation world was short-lived, and in 1999 the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a>. The Pomona operation was closed shortly after its sale to Hughes Aircraft.</p>
<p>Having divested itself of its aviation holdings, GD concentrated on land and sea products. GD purchased <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a>'s defense divisions in 1982, renaming them General Dynamics Land Systems. In 2003, it purchased the defense divisions of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> as well. It is now a major supplier of armored vehicles of all types, including the <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LAV_25" title="LAV 25">LAV 25</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stryker" title="Stryker">Stryker</a>, and a wide variety of vehicles based on these chassis. <a href="/wiki/Force_Protection_Inc" title="Force Protection Inc">Force Protection, Inc.</a> was acquired by General Dynamics Land Systems in November 2011 for $350 mil.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Land_Systems" title="General Dynamics Land Systems">General Dynamics Land Systems</a> was hurt by the cancellation of the US Army's Future Combat Systems program and the loss in the MRAP-All Terrain Vehicle competition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_lawsuit_and_settlement">Government lawsuit and settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Government lawsuit and settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 19, 2008, GD agreed to pay $4 million to settle a lawsuit brought by the US Government claiming that a GD unit fraudulently billed the government for defectively manufactured parts used in US military aircraft and submarines. The US alleged that GD defectively manufactured or failed to test parts used in US military aircraft from September 2001 to August 2003, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-141_Starlifter" title="C-141 Starlifter">C-141 Starlifter</a> transport plane. The GD unit involved, based in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Cove,_New_York" title="Glen Cove, New York">Glen Cove, New York</a>, closed in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_acquisitions">20th-century acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 20th-century acquisitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1946 - <a href="/wiki/Canadair" title="Canadair">Canadair</a> purchased from the Canadian government.</li>
<li>1952 - <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">Electric Boat</a> became General Dynamics.</li>
<li>1953 - <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> merged with General Dynamics.</li>
<li>1959 - Henry Crown acquires company and becomes majority shareholder.</li>
<li>1962-1963 - Convair-produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket)" title="Atlas (rocket)">Mercury-Atlas</a> rockets launch four manned <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a> missions into low Earth orbit, including <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a>.</li>
<li>1971-1985 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_S._Lewis,_Jr." title="David S. Lewis, Jr.">David S. Lewis, Jr.</a>, was chairman and chief executive officer. During his tenure, General Dynamics’ revenues and earnings quadrupled.</li>
<li>1982 - Formed General Dynamics Land Systems after the acquisition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" title="Chrysler Corporation">Chrysler</a>'s combat systems.</li>
<li>1995 - Acquired <a href="/wiki/Bath_Iron_Works" title="Bath Iron Works">Bath Iron Works</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prudential_Insurance" title="Prudential Insurance">Prudential Insurance</a>.</li>
<li>1996 - Acquired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teledyne_Vehicle_Systems" title="Teledyne Vehicle Systems">Teledyne Vehicle Systems</a>.</li>
<li>1997 - Acquired Lockheed Martin Defense Systems and Lockheed Martin Armament Systems.</li>
<li>1997 - Acquired Advanced Technology Systems, formerly an operating unit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucent_Technologies" title="Lucent Technologies">Lucent Technologies</a>.</li>
<li>1997 - Acquired Computing Devices International, formerly a division of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceridian_Corporation" title="Ceridian Corporation">Ceridian Corporation</a>.</li>
<li>1998 - Acquired <a href="/wiki/National_Steel_and_Shipbuilding_Company" title="National Steel and Shipbuilding Company">National Steel and Shipbuilding Company</a>.</li>
<li>1999 - Acquired <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a> from <a href="/wiki/Forstmann_Little_%26_Company" title="Forstmann Little &amp; Company">Forstmann Little</a>.</li>
<li>1999 - Acquired <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GTE_Government_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GTE Government Systems (page does not exist)">GTE Government Systems</a>, Communication Systems, Electronic Systems and Worldwide Telecommunication Systems Divisions.</li>
<li>2000 - Acquired Saco Defense from New Colt Holding Corp. which owned it since 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>

<li>1992 - <a href="/wiki/Cessna" title="Cessna">Cessna</a> to <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a>.</li>
<li>1992 - Electronics Division sold to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlyle_Group" title="Carlyle Group">Carlyle Group</a> of Washington, D.C. and renamed GDE Systems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-articles.latimes.com_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-articles.latimes.com-24">[24]</a></sup></li>

<li>1993 - Space Systems Division to <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a>.</li>
<li>1994 - Convair's <a href="/wiki/Aerostructure" title="Aerostructure">aerostructures</a> unit to <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a>, (Convair closed in 1996).</li>
<li>2006 - Material Service to <a href="/wiki/Hanson_plc" title="Hanson plc">Hanson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>2007 - Freeman United Coal Mining Co. sold to Springfield Coal Co. for an undisclosed amount<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>2010 - GD Advanced Information Systems Spacecraft division to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences" title="Orbital Sciences">Orbital Sciences</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_governance">Corporate governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Corporate governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current members of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of General Dynamics are: Mary Barra, Nicholas Chabraja, James Crown, William Fricks, Paul Kaminski, John Keane, Lester Lyles, James N. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financials">Financials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Financials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>General Dynamics has about $30 billion in sales,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> primarily military, but also civilian with its Gulfstream Aerospace unit and conventional shipbuilding and repair with its National Steel and Shipbuilding subsidiary.</p>
<p>In 2004 General Dynamics bid for the UK company <a href="/wiki/Alvis_plc" title="Alvis plc">Alvis plc</a>, the leading British manufacturer of armored vehicles. In March the board of Alvis Vickers voted in favor of the £309m takeover. However at the last minute <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> offered £355m for the company. This deal was finalized in June 2004.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
