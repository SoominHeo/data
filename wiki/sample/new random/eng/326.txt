<p><i><b>Higher Superstition: The Academic Left and Its Quarrels with Science</b></i> is a 1994 book by <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Gross" title="Paul R. Gross">Paul R. Gross</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a href="/wiki/Norman_Levitt" title="Norman Levitt">Norman Levitt</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_Superstition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Levitt states he is a leftist trying to save the "academic left" from itself by exposing misuses and abuses of science to advance political goals.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_influence">Reception and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_Superstition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reception and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book inspired the <a href="/wiki/Sokal_affair" title="Sokal affair">Sokal hoax</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sokal" title="Alan Sokal">Alan Sokal</a> published a bogus paper to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodernist" title="Postmodernist">postmodernist</a> journal that did not <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-review</a> submissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postmodernism_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-postmodernism-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="Â Dead link since January 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> Sokal stated in an interview that while he was initially skeptical about <i>Higher Superstition</i>, he concluded after reading the works Gross and Levit criticized that they were describing them fairly in "about 80 percent of the cases".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
