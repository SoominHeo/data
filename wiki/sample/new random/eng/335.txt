<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Stoning</h1>

<p><b>Stoning</b>, or <b>lapidation</b>, is a method of <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> whereby a group throws <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stones" title="Stones">stones</a> at a person until they die. No individual among the group can be identified as the one who kills the subject. This is in contrast to the case of a judicial <a href="/wiki/Executioner" title="Executioner">executioner</a>. Slower than other forms of execution, stoning within the context of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> is considered a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torturous_death" title="Torturous death">execution by torture</a>.</p>
<p>Stoning is called <i><a href="/wiki/Rajm" title="Rajm">rajm</a></i> (Arabic: <span style="font-size:large;">رجم</span>) in Islamic literature, and is a practice found in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, and parts<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2016)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebtrf_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebtrf-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_phone_stoning_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cell_phone_stoning-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In some countries, such as Afghanistan and Iraq, stoning has been declared illegal by the state, but is practiced extrajudicially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_phone_stoning_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cell_phone_stoning-2">[2]</a></sup> In several others, people have been sentenced to death by stoning, but the sentence has not been carried out. In modern times, allegations of stoning are politically sensitive; the government of Iran, for example, describes allegations of stoning as political propaganda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranprop_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iranprop-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_history">In history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Judaism">In Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Religion_and_capital_punishment#Judaism" title="Religion and capital punishment">Religion and capital punishment § Judaism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judaism_and_violence" title="Judaism and violence">Judaism and violence</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Torah">Torah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Torah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Jewish <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> (the first five books of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>: <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leviticus" title="Book of Leviticus">Leviticus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a>) serves as a common religious reference for <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>. Stoning is the method of execution mentioned most frequently in the Torah. (Murder is not mentioned as an offense punishable by stoning, but it seems that a member of the victim's family was allowed to kill the murderer; see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avenger_of_blood" title="Avenger of blood">Avenger of blood</a>.) The crimes punishable by stoning were the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Touching <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai" title="Mount Sinai">Mount Sinai</a> while God was giving <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">Ten Commandments</a>, Exodus 19:13</li>
<li>An ox that gores someone to death should be stoned, Exodus 21:28</li>
<li>Breaking <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Sabbath" title="Biblical Sabbath">Sabbath</a>, Numbers 15:32–36</li>
<li>Homosexual practices, Leviticus 20:13; both parties should be stoned</li>
<li>Having a "<a href="/wiki/Familiar_spirit" title="Familiar spirit">familiar spirit</a>" (or being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necromancer" title="Necromancer">necromancer</a>) or being a "<a href="/wiki/Magician_(paranormal)" title="Magician (paranormal)">wizard</a>", Leviticus 20:27</li>
<li>Enticing others to polytheism, Deuteronomy 13:7–11</li>
<li>Cursing God, Leviticus 24:10–16</li>
<li>Engaging in <a href="/wiki/Idolatry" title="Idolatry">idolatry</a>, Deuteronomy 17:2–7; or seducing others to do so, Deuteronomy 13:7–12</li>
<li>"Rebellion" against parents, after repeated warnings, Deuteronomy 21:18–21</li>
<li>Getting married as though a virgin, when not a virgin, Deuteronomy 22:13–21</li>
<li>Sexual intercourse between a man and a woman engaged to another man in a town, together, since she did not cry out (<a href="/wiki/Extramarital_sex" title="Extramarital sex">extramarital sex</a>), Deuteronomy 22:23–24; both parties should be stoned to death</li>
<li>Sexual intercourse between a man and a woman engaged to another man in a field, forced, where no one could hear her cries and save her (<a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>), Deuteronomy 22:25–27; the man should be stoned</li>
</ul>
<p>Describing the stoning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostasy_from_Judaism" title="Apostasy from Judaism">apostates from Judaism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> states:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If thy brother, the son of thy mother, or thy son, or thy daughter, or the wife of thy bosom, or thy friend, which [is] as thine own soul, entice thee secretly, saying, <i>Let us go and serve other gods</i>, which thou hast not known, thou, nor thy fathers; [Namely], of the gods of the people which [are] round about you, nigh unto thee, or far off from thee, from the [one] end of the earth even unto the [other] end of the earth; Thou shalt not consent unto him, nor hearken unto him; <i>neither shall thine eye pity him</i>, neither shalt thou spare, <i>neither shalt thou conceal him: But thou shalt surely kill him</i>; thine hand shall be first upon him to put him to death, and afterwards the hand of all the people. And thou shalt stone him with stones, that he die; because he hath sought to thrust thee away from the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land of Egypt, from the house of bondage.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><i><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 13:6–10<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></i></div>
</blockquote>
<p>A conspicuous concrete case noted in the Bible was that of <a href="/wiki/Achan_(biblical_figure)" title="Achan (biblical figure)">Achan</a>, stoned to death <a href="/wiki/Collective_responsibility" title="Collective responsibility">together with</a> his sheep, other livestock and his children for having pillaged valuables from <a href="/wiki/Jericho" title="Jericho">Jericho</a> during <a href="/wiki/Joshua" title="Joshua">Joshua</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Canaan" title="Conquest of Canaan">Conquest of Canaan</a>. The inclusion of the children in the punishment was troubling to later Jewish commentators, who wrote much about the case<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mishna">Mishna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mishna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> describes four methods of execution: stoning, pouring molten lead down the throat of the condemned person, beheading, and strangulation (see <a href="/wiki/Capital_and_corporal_punishment_in_Judaism" title="Capital and corporal punishment in Judaism">Capital and corporal punishment in Judaism</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mishna" title="Mishna">Mishna</a> gives the following list of persons who should be stoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia.com-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>"To the following sinners stoning applies – אלו הן הנסקלין</p>
<ul>
<li>one who has had <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">relations with his mother</a> – הבא על האם</li>
<li>with his father's wife – ועל אשת האב</li>
<li>with his daughter-in-law – ועל הכלה</li>
<li>a human male <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">with a human male</a> – ועל הזכור</li>
<li>or <a href="/wiki/Zoophilia" title="Zoophilia">with cattle</a> – ועל הבהמה</li>
<li>and the same is the case with a woman who uncovers herself before cattle – והאשה המביאה את הבהמה</li>
<li>with a blasphemer – והמגדף</li>
<li>an idolater – והעובד עבודת כוכבים</li>
<li>he who sacrifices one of his children to Molech – והנותן מזרעו למולך</li>
<li>one that occupies himself with familiar spirits – ובעל אוב</li>
<li>a wizard – וידעוני</li>
<li>one who violates Sabbath – והמחלל את השבת</li>
<li>one who curses his father or mother – והמקלל אביו ואמו</li>
<li>one who has assaulted a betrothed damsel – והבא על נערה המאורסה</li>
<li>a seducer who has seduced men to worship idols – והמסית</li>
<li>and the one who misleads a whole town – והמדיח</li>
<li>a witch (male or female) – והמכשף</li>
<li>a stubborn and rebellious son – ובן סורר ומורה"</li>
</ul>
<p>As God alone was deemed to be the only arbiter in the use of capital punishment, not fallible people, the <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin" title="Sanhedrin">Sanhedrin</a> made stoning a hypothetical upper limit on the severity of punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Prior to early Christianity, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a>, doubts were growing in Jewish society about the effectiveness of capital punishment in general (and stoning in particular) in acting as a useful deterrent. Subsequently its use was dissuaded by the central legislators. The Mishnah states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A Sanhedrin that puts a man to death once in seven years is called destructive. Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah says that this extends to a Sanhedrin that puts a man to death even once in seventy years. Rabbi Akiba and Rabbi Tarfon say: Had we been in the Sanhedrin none would ever have been put to death. Rabban Simeon ben Gamaliel says: they would have multiplied shedders of blood in Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tractate_makkos_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tractate_makkos-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the following centuries the leading Jewish sages imposed so many restrictions on the implementation of capital punishment as to make it <i>de facto</i> illegal. The restrictions were to prevent execution of the innocent, and included many conditions for a testimony to be admissible that were difficult to fulfill.</p>
<p>Philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moses_Maimonides" title="Moses Maimonides">Moses Maimonides</a> wrote, "It is better and more satisfactory to acquit a thousand guilty persons than to put a single innocent one to death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He was concerned that the law guard its public perception, to preserve its majesty and retain the people's respect. He saw errors of commission as much more threatening to the integrity of law than errors of omission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mode_of_Judgment">Mode of Judgment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mode of Judgment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In rabbinic law, capital punishment may only be inflicted by the verdict of a regularly constituted court of twenty-three qualified members. There must be the most trustworthy and convincing testimony of at least two qualified eyewitnesses to the crime, who must also depose that the culprit had been forewarned of the criminality and the consequences of his project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia.com-7">[7]</a></sup> The culprit must be a person of legal age and of sound mind, and the crime must be proved to have been committed of the culprit's free will and without the aid of others.</p>
<p>On the day the verdict is pronounced, the convict is led forth to execution. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torah_Laws" title="Torah Laws">Torah law</a> (Leviticus 19:18) prescribes, "Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself"; and the Rabbis maintain that this love must be extended beyond the limits of social intercourse in life, and applied even to the convicted criminal who, "though a sinner, is still thy brother" (Mak. 3:15; Sanh. 44a): "The spirit of love must be manifested by according him a decent death" (Sanh. 45a, 52a). Torah law provides (Deut. 24:16), "The parents shall not be put to death for the children, neither shall the children be put to death for the parents; every man shall be put to death for his own sins", and rabbinic jurisprudence follows this principle both to the letter and in spirit. A sentence is not attended by confiscation of the convict's goods; the person's possessions descend to their legal heirs.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> limits the use of the death penalty to Jewish criminals who:</p>
<ul>
<li>(A) while about to do the crime were warned not to commit the crime while in the presence of two witnesses (and only individuals who meet a strict list of standards are considered acceptable witnesses); and</li>
<li>(B) having been warned, committed the crime in front of the same two witnesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In theory, the Talmudic method of how stoning is to be carried out differs from mob stoning. According to the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Oral_law" title="Oral law">oral law</a>, after the Jewish criminal has been determined as guilty before the Great <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin" title="Sanhedrin">Sanhedrin</a>, the two valid witnesses and the sentenced criminal go to the edge of a two story building. From there the two witnesses are to push the criminal off the roof of a two story building. The two-story height is chosen as this height is estimated by the Talmud to effect a quick and painless demise but is not so high that the body will become dismembered. After the criminal has fallen, the two witnesses are to drop a large boulder onto the criminal – requiring both of the witnesses to lift the boulder together. If the criminal did not die from the fall or from the crushing of the large boulder, then any people in the surrounding area are to quickly cause him to die by stoning with whatever rocks they can find.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Islam">In Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Islamic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharia_law" title="Sharia law">sharia law</a> is based on the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a> as primary sources. Stoning in the <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">Sunnah</a> mainly follows on the Jewish stoning rules of the Torah. A few hadiths refer to Muhammad ordering the stoning of a married Jewish man and a married woman committing an illegal sexual act after <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In a few others, a Bedouin man is lashed, while a woman is stoned to death, for having sex outside marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Qur'an forbids all sexual intercourse outside the marital bond between a man and a woman as <a href="/wiki/Islamic_views_on_sin" title="Islamic views on sin">sinful</a>, but makes no distinction between illegal sex outside marriage and illegal sex between an unmarried man and a woman. Verse 24:2 of the Quran declares that the punishment for consensual but illegal sex is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flogging" title="Flogging">flogging</a> with 100 strokes, but it makes no mention of stoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some modern Muslim scholars suggest that stoning to death should not be part of <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sharia</a>, because <a href="/wiki/Quranism" title="Quranism">only Quran</a> should be the basis of sharia, and hadiths should not be considered a source of sharia. The vast majority of Muslims and most Islamic scholars, however, consider <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadiths</a>, which describe the words, conduct and example set by Muhammad during his life, as a source of law and religious authority second only to the Quran. They consider <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahih" title="Sahih">sahih</a> hadiths to be a valid source of Sharia, justifying their belief on <a href="/wiki/Al-Ahzab" title="Al-Ahzab">Quranic verse 33</a>.21,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and other verses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mqz_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-mqz-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>When one carefully compares Qur'an Surah 4 Ayat 25, and, Surah 24 Ayat 2, it seems illogical for the interpretation of Hadith which permits stoning for Muslims to be correct, within the context that it is not possible to have half of death. Furthermore, the Ayats at Surah 5 Ayat 51 could be considered. If any particular Hadith is being contradicted by Qur'an, then Muslims need to reassess their interpretation of Hadith. We also have Surah 4 Ayat 92.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hadiths">Hadiths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hadiths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Stoning is described as punishment in multiple hadiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nisrine_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nisrine-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kalu_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-kalu-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> hadith collections differ because scholars from the two traditions differ as to the reliability of the narrators and transmitters and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imamah_(Shi%27a_doctrine)" title="Imamah (Shi'a doctrine)">Imamah</a>. Shi'a sayings related to stoning can be found in <a href="/wiki/Kitab_al-Kafi" title="Kitab al-Kafi">Kitab al-Kafi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and Sunni sayings related to stoning can be found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahih_Bukhari" title="Sahih Bukhari">Sahih Bukhari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sahih_Muslim" title="Sahih Muslim">Sahih Muslim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Based on these hadiths, in some Muslim countries, married adulterers are sentenced to death, while consensual sex between unmarried people results in 100 lashes.</p>
<p>Crimes in the Qur'an are divided into three categories based on the prescribed punishment for the offence. The first category is <a href="/wiki/Hudud" title="Hudud">Hudud</a>, which is considered a religious crime against God and the punishment is execution. <a href="/wiki/Zina" title="Zina">Zina</a> is one of the Hudud crimes, stated to deserve the stoning punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msawwa_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-msawwa-26">[26]</a></sup> Zina includes <a href="/wiki/Extramarital_sex" title="Extramarital sex">extramarital sex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Premarital_sex" title="Premarital sex">premarital sex</a> and homosexuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msawwa_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-msawwa-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">Hadiths</a> describe stoning as punishment under <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sharia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nisrine_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-nisrine-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kalu_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-kalu-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In others stoning is prescribed as punishment for illegal sex between man and woman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> illegal sex by a slave girl, as well as anyone involved in any homosexual relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nisrine_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-nisrine-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In some <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">sunnah</a>, the method of stoning, by first digging a pit and partly burying the person's lower half in it, is described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Narrated <a href="/wiki/Abu_Hurairah" title="Abu Hurairah">Abu Huraira</a> and Zaid bin Khalid Al-Juhani: A bedouin came to Allah's Apostle and said, "O Allah's apostle! I ask you by Allah to judge My case according to Allah's Laws." His opponent, who was more learned than he, said, "Yes, judge between us according to Allah's Laws, and allow me to speak." Allah's Apostle said, "Speak." He (i .e. the bedouin or the other man) said, "My son was working as a laborer for this (man) and he committed illegal sexual intercourse with his wife. The people told me that it was obligatory that my son should be stoned to death, so in lieu of that I ransomed my son by paying one hundred sheep and a slave girl. Then I asked the religious scholars about it, and they informed me that my son must be lashed one hundred lashes, and be exiled for one year, and the wife of this (man) must be <b>stoned to death</b>." Allah's Apostle said, "By Him in Whose Hands my soul is, I will judge between you according to Allah's Laws. The slave-girl and the sheep are to be returned to you, your son is to receive a hundred lashes and be exiled for one year. You, Unais, go to the wife of this (man) and if she confesses her guilt, stone her to death." Unais went to that woman next morning and she confessed. Allah's Apostle ordered that she be stoned to death.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_commentary_in_fiqhs">Legal commentary in fiqhs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legal commentary in fiqhs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to some traditionalist middle-age jurists like the <a href="/wiki/Hanbali" title="Hanbali">Hanbali</a> jurist <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Qudamah" title="Ibn Qudamah">Ibn Qudamah</a>, "Muslim jurists are unanimous on the fact that stoning to death is a specified punishment for the married adulterer and adulteress. The punishment is recorded in number of traditions and the practice of Muhammad stands as an authentic source supporting it. This is the view held by all Companions, Successors and other Muslim scholars with the exception of Kharijites."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IslamOnline.net_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-IslamOnline.net-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> school of jurisprudence (<a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">fiqh</a>) holds that stoning is the required punishment for illegal sex by a married person, as well as for any form of homosexual relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nisrine_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-nisrine-21">[21]</a></sup> In <i>Al-Muwatta</i>, the 8th century Sunni Islamic scholar <a href="/wiki/Malik_ibn_Anas" title="Malik ibn Anas">Malik ibn Anas</a> states that pregnancy in a free Muslim woman is one form of sufficient proof of adultery and she must be stoned to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>All <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> fiqhs, as well as Volume 7 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shi%27ite" title="Shi'ite">Shi'ite</a> hadith, <i>The Book of Legal Penalties</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kitab_al-Kafi" title="Kitab al-Kafi">Kitab al-Kafi</a>, declare stoning as the required punishment for sex that is not allowed under <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sharia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aztecs">Aztecs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aztecs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_today">Usage today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Usage today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of September 2010, stoning is a punishment that is included in the laws in some countries including Saudi Arabia, Sudan, Iran, Somalia, Yemen and some predominantly Muslim states in northern Nigeria as punishment for <a href="/wiki/Zina" title="Zina">Zina</a> ("adultery by married persons").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Afghanistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Farkhunda" title="Murder of Farkhunda">Murder of Farkhunda</a></div>
<p>Stoning is illegal in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, but is sometimes carried out by tribal leaders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_phone_stoning_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-cell_phone_stoning-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wunrnAfghan_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-wunrnAfghan-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indstoningvid_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indstoningvid-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianRokhsana_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianRokhsana-43">[43]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Taliban_insurgency" title="Taliban insurgency">Taliban insurgents</a> extrajudicially in certain parts of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indstoningvid_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Indstoningvid-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianRokhsana_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianRokhsana-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakimi_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hakimi-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes2010_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes2010-45">[45]</a></sup> Before the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Emirate_of_Afghanistan" title="Islamic Emirate of Afghanistan">Taliban government</a>, most areas of Afghanistan, aside from the capital, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>, were controlled by warlords or tribal leaders. The Afghan legal system depended highly on an individual community's local culture and the political or religious ideology of its leaders. Stoning also occurred in lawless areas, where vigilantes committed the act for political purposes. Once the Taliban took over, it became a form of punishment for certain serious crimes or <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a>. After the fall of the Taliban government, the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Hamid_Karzai" title="Presidency of Hamid Karzai">Karzai administration</a> re-enforced the 1976 penal code which made no provision for the use of stoning as a punishment. In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(Afghanistan)" title="Ministry of Justice (Afghanistan)">Ministry of Justice</a> proposed public stoning as punishment for adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrw2013_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrw2013-46">[46]</a></sup> However, the government had to back down from the proposal after it was leaked and triggered international outcry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianRokhsana_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianRokhsana-43">[43]</a></sup> While stoning is officially banned in Afghanistan, it has continued to be reported occasionally as a crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakimi_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hakimi-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brunei">Brunei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Brunei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Brunei" title="Sultan of Brunei">Sultan</a> <a href="/wiki/Hassanal_Bolkiah" title="Hassanal Bolkiah">Hassanal Bolkiah</a> announced that stoning, along with flogging and amputations, would be added to the country's laws in accordance with sharia law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 September 2009, the outgoing Aceh Legislative Council passed a bylaw that called for the stoning of married adulterers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> However, then governor <a href="/wiki/Irwandi_Yusuf" title="Irwandi Yusuf">Irwandi Yusuf</a> refused to sign the bylaw, thereby keeping it a law without legal force and, in some views, therefore still a law <i>draft</i>, rather than <b>actual</b> law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In March 2013, the Aceh government removed the stoning provision from its own draft of a new criminal code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Iran" title="Capital punishment in Iran">Capital punishment in Iran</a></div>
<p>The Iranian judiciary officially placed a moratorium on stoning in 2002; however, in 2007, the Iranian judiciary confirmed that a man who had been convicted of adultery 10 years earlier, was stoned to death in <a href="/wiki/Qazvin_Province" title="Qazvin Province">Qazvin</a> province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC312_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC312-54">[54]</a></sup> In 2008, the judiciary tried to eliminate the punishment from the books in legislation submitted to parliament for approval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp.google.com_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp.google.com-55">[55]</a></sup> In 2009, two people were stoned to death in Mashhad, <a href="/wiki/Razavi_Khorasan_Province" title="Razavi Khorasan Province">Razavi Khorasan Province</a> as punishment for the crime of adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In early 2013, a spokesman for judicial committee of Iran's parliament stated that stoning is no longer mentioned in Iran's legislation, but that punishment will remain the same as it is in Islamic law. He questioned Western enmity against Iran, and termed the campaign to remove <i><a href="/wiki/Rajm" title="Rajm">rajm</a></i> as noise against the implementation of Islamic law in Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Legal scholars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nayyeri_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nayyeri-58">[58]</a></sup> concur that while certain stoning-related passages have been removed from Iran's new penal code, other passages in the new code refer to stoning, and stoning remains as a possible form of punishment under the new Iranian penal code.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Methods</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the 2008 version of the <a href="/wiki/Hudud" title="Hudud">Islamic Penal Code</a> of Iran detailed how stoning punishments are to be carried out for adultery, and even hints in some contexts that the punishment may allow for its victims to avoid death:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmnestyEndExecutions_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmnestyEndExecutions-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Article 102 – An adulterous man shall be buried in a ditch up to near his waist and an adulterous woman up to near her chest and then stoned to death.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Article 103 – In case the person sentenced to stoning escapes the ditch in which they are buried, then if the adultery is proven by testimony then they will be returned for the punishment but if it is proven by their own confession then they will not be returned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmnestyEndExecutions_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-AmnestyEndExecutions-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Article 104 – The size of the stone used in stoning shall not be too large to kill the convict by one or two throws and at the same time shall not be too small to be called a stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmnestyEndExecutions_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-AmnestyEndExecutions-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Depending upon the details of the case, the stoning may be initiated by the judge overseeing the matter or by one of the original witnesses to the adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmnestyEndExecutions_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-AmnestyEndExecutions-59">[59]</a></sup> Certain religious procedures may also need to be followed both before and after the implementation of a stoning execution, such as wrapping the person being stoned in traditional burial dress before the procedure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><br/></p>
<p>The method of stoning set out in the 2008 code was similar to that in a 1999 version of Iran's penal code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Iran revised its penal code in 2013. The new code does not include the above passages, but does include stoning as a <a href="/wiki/Hudud" title="Hudud">hadd</a> punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nayyeri_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nayyeri-58">[58]</a></sup> For example, Book I, Part III, Chapter 5, Article 132 of the new Islamic Penal Code (IPC) of 2013 in the Islamic Republic of Iran states, "If a man and a woman commit <a href="/wiki/Zina" title="Zina">zina</a> together more than one time, if the death penalty and flogging or stoning and flogging are imposed, only the death penalty or stoning, whichever is applicable, shall be executed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipciri2014_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipciri2014-62">[62]</a></sup> Book 2, Part II, Chapter 1, Article 225 of the Iran's IPC released in 2013 states, "the hadd punishment for zina of a man and a woman who meet the conditions of ihsan shall be stoning to death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipciri2014_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipciri2014-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoning_of_Du%27a_Khalil_Aswad" title="Stoning of Du'a Khalil Aswad">Du'a Khalil Aswad</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yazidi" title="Yazidi">Yazidi</a> girl, was stoned by her fellow tribesmen in northern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> for dating a Muslim boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 at least 14 youths were stoned to death in Baghdad, apparently as part of a Shi'ite militant campaign against Western-style "<a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">emo</a>" fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>An Iraqi man was stoned to death, in August 2014, in the northern city of Mosul after one Sunni Islamic court sentenced him to die for the crime of adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the sharia legal system was introduced in the predominantly Muslim north of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> in 2000, more than a dozen Nigerian Muslims have been sentenced to death by stoning for sexual offences ranging from adultery to homosexuality. However, none of these sentences has actually been carried out. They have either been thrown out on appeal or commuted to prison terms as a result of pressure from human rights groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Zia-ul-Haq%27s_Islamization" title="Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's Islamization">Zia-ul-Haq's Islamization</a> measures, stoning to death (<i><a href="/wiki/Rajm" title="Rajm">rajm</a></i>) at a public place was introduced into law via the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Hudood_Ordinances" title="Hudood Ordinances">Hudood Ordinances</a> as punishment for adultery (<i>zina</i>) and rape (<i>zina-bil-jabr</i>) when committed by a married person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> However, stoning has never been officially utilized since the law came into effect and all judicial executions occur by hanging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GallowPak_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-GallowPak-71">[71]</a></sup> The first conviction and sentence of stoning (of Fehmida and Allah-Bakhsh) in September 1981 was overturned under national and international pressure. A conviction for adultery of Safia Bibi, a 13-year-old blind girl who alleged that she was raped by her employer and his son, was reversed and the conviction was set aside on appeal after bitter public criticism. Another conviction for adultery and sentence of stoning (of Shahida Parveen and Muhammad Sarwar) in early 1988 sparked outrage and led to a retrial and acquittal by the Federal Sharia Court. In this case the trial court took the view that notice of divorce by Shahida's former husband, Khushi Muhammad, should have been given to the Chairman of the local council, as stipulated under Section-7(3) of the <i>Muslim Family Laws Ordinance</i>, 1961. This section states that any man who divorces his wife must register it with the Union Council. Otherwise, the court concluded that the divorce stood invalidated and the couple became liable to conviction under the Adultery ordinance. In 2006, the ordinances providing for stoning in the case of adultery or rape were legislatively demoted from overriding status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Extrajudicial stonings in Pakistan have been known to happen in recent times. In March 2013, Pakistani soldier Anwar Din, stationed in <a href="/wiki/Parachinar" title="Parachinar">Parachinar</a>, was publicly stoned to death for allegedly having a love affair with a girl from a village in the country's north western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurram_Agency" title="Kurram Agency">Kurram Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> On 11 July 2013, Arifa Bibi, a young mother of two, was sentenced by a tribal court in <a href="/wiki/Dera_Ghazi_Khan_District" title="Dera Ghazi Khan District">Dera Ghazi Khan District</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_Pakistan" title="Punjab, Pakistan">Punjab</a>, to be stoned to death for possessing a cell phone. Members of her family were ordered to execute her sentence and her body was buried in the desert far away from her village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_phone_stoning_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-cell_phone_stoning-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2014, a couple in a remote area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baluchistan,_Pakistan" title="Baluchistan, Pakistan">Baluchistan</a> province was stoned to death after being accused of an adulterous relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> On 27 May 2014, Farzana Parveen, a 25-year-old married woman who was three months pregnant, was killed by being attacked with batons and bricks by nearly 20 members of her family outside the high court of <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a> in front of "a crowd of onlookers" according to a statement by a police investigator. The assailants, who allegedly included her father and brothers, attacked Farzana and her husband Mohammad Iqbal with batons and bricks. Her father Mohammad Azeem, who was arrested for murder, reportedly called the murder an "honor killing" and said "I killed my daughter as she had insulted all of our family by marrying a man without our consent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The man whose second wife Farzana had become, Iqbal, told a news agency that he had strangled his previous wife in order to marry Farzana, and police said that he had been released for killing his first wife because a "compromise" had been reached with his family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saudi_Arabia">Saudi Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Saudi Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="Capital punishment in Saudi Arabia">Capital punishment in Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p>Legal stoning sentences have been reported in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISHR_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISHR-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aroundtheworld_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-aroundtheworld-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sudan">Sudan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sudan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2012, a Sudanese court convicted Intisar Sharif Abdallah of adultery and sentenced her to death; the charges were appealed and dropped two months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-ST-80">[80]</a></sup> In July 2012, a criminal court in <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>, Sudan, sentenced 23-year-old Layla Ibrahim Issa Jumul to death by stoning for adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> reported that she was denied legal counsel during the trial and was convicted only on the basis of her confession. The organization designated her a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_conscience" title="Prisoner of conscience">prisoner of conscience</a>, "held in detention solely for consensual sexual relations", and lobbied for her release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-ST-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Somalia">Somalia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Somalia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2008, a girl, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aisha_Ibrahim_Duhulow" title="Aisha Ibrahim Duhulow">Aisha Ibrahim Duhulow</a>, was buried up to her neck at a <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalian</a> football stadium, then stoned to death in front of more than 1,000 people. The stoning occurred after she had allegedly pleaded guilty to adultery in a sharia court in <a href="/wiki/Kismayo" title="Kismayo">Kismayo</a>, a city controlled by <a href="/wiki/Islamism" title="Islamism">Islamist</a> insurgents. According to the insurgents she had stated that she wanted sharia law to apply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> However, other sources state that the victim had been crying, had begged for mercy and had to be forced into the hole before being buried up to her neck in the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> later learned that the girl was in fact 13 years old and had been arrested by <a href="/wiki/Al-Shabaab_(militant_group)" title="Al-Shabaab (militant group)">al-Shabab</a> militia after she had reported being gang-raped by three men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2009, another instance of stoning was publicised after Mohamed Abukar Ibrahim was accused of adultery by the <a href="/wiki/Hizbul_Islam" title="Hizbul Islam">Hizbul Islam</a> militant group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2014, Somali al Shabaab militants stoned a woman to death, after she was declared guilty of adultery by an informal court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Arab_Emirates">United Arab Emirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United Arab Emirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stoning is a legal form of judicial punishment in UAE. In 2006, an <a href="/wiki/Expatriates_in_the_United_Arab_Emirates" title="Expatriates in the United Arab Emirates">expatriate</a> was sentenced to death by stoning for committing adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Between 2009 and 2013, several people were sentenced to death by stoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> In May 2014, an Asian housemaid was sentenced to death by stoning in Abu Dhabi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ISIL">ISIL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: ISIL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant</a></div>
<p>Several adultery executions by stoning committed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISIL" title="ISIL">IS</a> have been reported in the autumn of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.com_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.com-96">[96]</a></sup> The Islamic State's magazine, <a href="/wiki/Dabiq_(magazine)" title="Dabiq (magazine)">Dabiq</a>, documented the stoning of a woman in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raqqa" title="Raqqa">Raqqa</a> as a punishment for adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2014, IS released a video appearing to show a Syrian man stone his daughter to death for alleged adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.com_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.com-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support_for_stoning">Support for stoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Support for stoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A survey conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> in 2013 found varying support in the global Muslim population for stoning as a punishment for adultery. Highest support for stoning is found among Muslims of the Middle East, North Africa, and South Asian countries while generally less support is found among Muslims living in Mediterranean and Central Asian countries. In half of the surveyed countries with adequate sample sizes, at least half of Muslims who favor making sharia the law of the land also support stoning unfaithful spouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pewsharia_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pewsharia-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups_against_stoning">Groups against stoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Groups against stoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stoning has been condemned by several human rights organizations. Some groups, such as <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amnstoning_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-amnstoning-100">[100]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a>, oppose all capital punishment, including stoning. Other groups, such as RAWA (<a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Association_of_the_Women_of_Afghanistan" title="Revolutionary Association of the Women of Afghanistan">Revolutionary Association of the Women of Afghanistan</a>), or the International Committee against Stoning (ICAS), oppose stoning <i>per se</i> as an especially cruel practice.</p>
<p>Specific sentences of stoning, such as the <a href="/wiki/Amina_Lawal" title="Amina Lawal">Amina Lawal</a> case, have often generated international protest. Groups such as Human Rights Watch,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrwstoning_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrwstoning-101">[101]</a></sup> while in sympathy with these protests, have raised a concern that the Western focus on stoning as an especially "exotic" or "barbaric" act distracts from what they view as the larger problems of capital punishment. They argue that the "more fundamental human rights issue in Nigeria is the dysfunctional justice system."</p>
<p>In Iran, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_Stoning_Forever_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stop Stoning Forever Campaign (page does not exist)">Stop Stoning Forever Campaign</a> was formed by various women's rights activists after a man and a woman were stoned to death in <a href="/wiki/Mashhad" title="Mashhad">Mashhad</a> in May 2006. The campaign's main goal is to legally abolish stoning as a form of punishment for adultery in Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meydaan_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-meydaan-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights">Human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stoning is condemned by human rights groups as a form of <a href="/wiki/Cruel_and_unusual_punishment" title="Cruel and unusual punishment">cruel and unusual punishment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a>, and a serious violation of human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amnesty.org_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-amnesty.org-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_rights">Women's rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Women's rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stoning has been condemned as a violation of <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a> and a form of <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> against women. Although stoning is also applied to men, the vast majority of the victims are reported to be women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> According to the international group Women Living Under Muslim Laws stoning "is one of the most brutal forms of violence perpetrated against women in order to control and punish their sexuality and basic freedoms."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amnesty International has argued that the reasons for which women suffer disproportionately from stoning include the fact that women are not treated equally and fairly by the courts; the fact that, being more likely to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illiterate" title="Illiterate">illiterate</a> than men, women are more likely to sign confessions to crimes which they did not commit; and the fact that general discrimination against women in other life aspects leaves them at higher risk of convictions for adultery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT_rights">LGBT rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: LGBT rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stoning also targets homosexuals and others who have same-sex relations in certain jurisdictions. In Mauritania,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebtrf_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ebtrf-1">[1]</a></sup> northern Nigeria,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Somalia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebtrf_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ebtrf-1">[1]</a></sup> and Yemen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebtrf_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ebtrf-1">[1]</a></sup> the legal punishment for <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a> is death by stoning.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Right_to_private_life">Right to private life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Right to private life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Human rights organizations argue that many acts targeted by stoning should not be illegal in the first place, as outlawing them interferes with people's right to a private life. Amnesty International said that stoning deals with "acts which should never be criminalized in the first place, including consensual sexual relations between adults, and choosing one’s religion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amnesty.org_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-amnesty.org-103">[103]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soraya_Manutchehri" title="Soraya Manutchehri">Soraya Manutchehri</a>, 1986, stoned to death in Iran after unconfirmed accusations of adultery</li>
<li>Mahboubeh M. and Abbas H., at Behest-e Zahra cemetery, southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teheran" title="Teheran">Teheran</a>, Iran, 2006. The public was not invited to the stoning, and the incident was not reported to the media. However it was spread by word of mouth to a journalist and women's rights activist. The activist gathered information and further exposed the happening to the world. In response to this, several women's rights activists, lawyers and members of the Networks of Volunteers went on to form the Stop Stoning Forever campaign to stop stoning in Iran.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Du%E2%80%99a_Khalil_Aswad" title="Du’a Khalil Aswad">Du’a Khalil Aswad</a>, 2007, a 17-year-old stoned to death in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a></li>
<li>Jafar Kiani, in Agche – kand, a small village near <a href="/wiki/Takestan" title="Takestan">Takestan</a>, Iran, 2007.</li>
<li>Sara Jaffar Nimat, aged 11, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Khanaqin" title="Khanaqin">Khanaqin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Kurdistan" title="Iraqi Kurdistan">Iraqi Kurdistan</a>, 2007. She had been hit by bricks and stones, and burnt.</li>
<li>Aisha Ibrahim Duhulow, aged 13 in <a href="/wiki/Kismayo" title="Kismayo">Kismayo</a>, Somalia, 2008.</li>
<li>Kurdistan Aziz, aged 16, <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Kurdistan" title="Iraqi Kurdistan">Iraqi Kurdistan</a>, 2008. She had been stoned in an act of "Honour" – killing.</li>
<li>Shano and Daulat Khan Malikdeenkhe, in Khwezai – Baezai area, Pakistan, 2008</li>
<li>Solange Medina, 2009, a 20-year-old stoned to death in <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Juárez">Ciudad Juárez, Chihuahua</a>, Mexico<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></li>
<li>Vali Azad, 30, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilan" title="Gilan">Gilan</a> province, Iran, 2009.</li>
<li>Gustavo Santoro, 2010, a small town mayor in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> believed to have been murdered by stoning<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></li>
<li>Murray Seidman, 2011, a 70-year-old senior in <a href="/wiki/Lansdowne,_Pennsylvania" title="Lansdowne, Pennsylvania">Lansdowne, Pennsylvania</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, stoned to death by 28-year-old John Thomas after allegedly making sexual advances towards the younger man. Thomas' defence is that he did it because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Old_Testament" title="The Old Testament">the Old Testament</a> says to kill homosexuals in certain situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shirley_Jackson" title="Shirley Jackson">Shirley Jackson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Lottery" title="The Lottery">The Lottery</a>" depicts an annual lottery in which one member of a small, isolated American community is ritually stoned to death as a sacrifice. It explores themes of <a href="/wiki/Scapegoating" title="Scapegoating">scapegoating</a>, man's inherent evil and the destructive nature of observing ancient, outdated rituals. The music video for "<a href="/wiki/Man_That_You_Fear" title="Man That You Fear">Man That You Fear</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Manson" title="Marilyn Manson">Marilyn Manson</a> is based on the story.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Stranger_in_a_Strange_Land" title="Stranger in a Strange Land">Stranger in a Strange Land</a></i> reaches its climax with a stoning execution.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freidoune_Sahebjam" title="Freidoune Sahebjam">Freidoune Sahebjam</a>'s 1990 book <i>La Femme Lapidée</i> is based on the story of a woman who was stoned to death in Iran in 1986. The book was the basis of the 2008 film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stoning_of_Soraya_M." title="The Stoning of Soraya M.">The Stoning of Soraya M.</a></i>.</li>
<li>Simon Perry's <i>All Who Came Before</i> climaxes with a stoning as Barabbas enters Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Princess: A true story of life behind the veil in Saudi Arabia</i> by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sasson" title="Jean Sasson">Jean Sasson</a> describes a girl sentenced to death by stoning.</li>
<li>In the 2003 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kite_Runner" title="The Kite Runner">The Kite Runner</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Khaled_Hosseini" title="Khaled Hosseini">Khaled Hosseini</a>, a couple are stoned to death at a soccer stadium in Afghanistan.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film_and_television">In film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stoning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: In film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Seven Sleepers</i>, 2005 – A series running on Iranian TV, in which medieval (300–400 AD) Jews stone Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IranTVstone_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-IranTVstone-119">[119]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/A_Stoning_in_Fulham_County" title="A Stoning in Fulham County">A Stoning in Fulham County</a></i>, 1988 – A made-for-TV movie surrounding the vigilante stoning in an American <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a> community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmishMovieStone_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmishMovieStone-120">[120]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python%27s_Life_of_Brian" title="Monty Python's Life of Brian">Monty Python's Life of Brian</a></i> presents a Jesus of Nazareth-era stoning in a humorous context, ending with a massive boulder being dropped on the Jewish official, not the victim. The film mentions that women are not allowed at stonings, yet almost all of the stone-throwers turn out to be women disguised as men.</li>
<li>Shirley Jackson's "<a href="/wiki/The_Lottery" title="The Lottery">The Lottery</a>" was made into a short (20 minute) film by Larry Yust in 1969 as part of an educational release for Encyclopædia Britannica's "Short Story Showcase".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></li>
<li>The film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kite_Runner_(film)" title="The Kite Runner (film)">The Kite Runner</a></i> depicts the stoning of an adulteress by the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> in a public stadium during a football match.</li>
<li>The film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Istanbul" title="Mission Istanbul">Mission Istanbul</a></i> depicts the stoning of an adulteress in <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>, by the fictional terrorist group Abu Nazir until it is interrupted by the protagonist Vikas Sagar.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Stoning_of_Soraya_M." title="The Stoning of Soraya M.">The Stoning of Soraya M.</a></i>, 2008 film</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zorba_The_Greek" title="Zorba The Greek">Zorba The Greek</a></i>, a 1946 Novel by Nikos Kazantzakis and 1964 movie with Anthony Quinn, has a grim stoning scene where the woman is rescued only to be stabbed at the scene.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Osama_(film)" title="Osama (film)">Osama</a></i> (2003) by director <a href="/wiki/Siddiq_Barmak" title="Siddiq Barmak">Siddiq Barmak</a> depicts a woman being buried in preparation for stoning.</li>
<li>In one CSI: Miami 2011 episode a female college bully is murdered by lapidation.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>'s music video for her song Judas, a scene depicts Gaga being stoned to death.</li>
<li>Although Islamic law prescribes stoning for married adulterers, the television series Sleeper Cell, about an underground radical Islamist group, depicts a scene where a member is stoned for treason.</li>
<li>In Spartacus: War of the Damned (2010–13), Season 3, Episode 2, a slave is stoned by the Roman public.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu_(2014_film)" title="Timbuktu (2014 film)"><i>Timbuktu</i></a> (2014), a film about Islamist insurgents in <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>, Mali, a man and woman are depicted buried up to the neck and stoned to death.</li>
</ul>
