<p>The <b>feminist sex wars</b>, also known as the <b>lesbian sex wars</b>, or simply the <b>sex wars</b> or <b>porn wars</b>, are terms used to refer to collective debates amongst <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminists</a> regarding a number of issues broadly relating to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexuality" title="Sexuality">sexuality</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_activity" title="Sexual activity">sexual activity</a>. Differences of opinion on matters of sexuality deeply polarized the feminist movement, particularly leading feminist thinkers, in the late 1970s and early 1980s and continue to influence debate amongst feminists to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The sides were characterized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist_views_on_pornography#Anti-pornography_feminism" title="Feminist views on pornography">anti-porn feminist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex-positive_feminism" title="Sex-positive feminism">sex-positive feminist</a> groups with disagreements regarding sexuality, including <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erotica" title="Erotica">erotica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_sexual_practices" title="Lesbian sexual practices">lesbian sexual practices</a>, the role of <a href="/wiki/Trans_woman" title="Trans woman">trans women</a> in the lesbian community, <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">sadomasochism</a> and other sexual issues. The feminist movement was deeply divided as a result of these debates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duggan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duggan-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerhard_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerhard-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leidholdt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leidholdt-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vance_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vance-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_opposing_views">Two opposing views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Two opposing views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two sides became labelled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist_views_on_pornography#Anti-pornography_feminism" title="Feminist views on pornography">anti-pornography feminists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex-positive_feminism" title="Sex-positive feminism">sex-positive feminists</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-pornography_feminists">Anti-pornography feminists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anti-pornography feminists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976 <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Dworkin" title="Andrea Dworkin">Andrea Dworkin</a> organized demonstrations against the film <i><a href="/wiki/Snuff_(film)" title="Snuff (film)">Snuff</a></i> in New York, but attempts to start an organization to continue the feminist anti-pornography campaign failed. Efforts were more successful in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Women_Against_Pornography#Similar_groups" title="Women Against Pornography">Women Against Violence Against Women</a> was founded in response to <i>Snuff</i> in 1976; they campaigned against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" title="Rolling Stones">Rolling Stones</a>' 1976 album <i><a href="/wiki/Black_and_Blue" title="Black and Blue">Black and Blue</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The U.S. anti-pornography movement gained ground with the founding of <a href="/wiki/Women_Against_Violence_in_Pornography_and_Media" title="Women Against Violence in Pornography and Media">Women Against Violence in Pornography and Media</a> in 1977 in San Francisco, following a 1976 conference on <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_women" title="Violence against women">violence against women</a> held by local women's centers. Early members included <a href="/wiki/Susan_Griffin" title="Susan Griffin">Susan Griffin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Barry" title="Kathleen Barry">Kathleen Barry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laura_Lederer" title="Laura Lederer">Laura Lederer</a>. WAVPM organised the first national conference on pornography in San Francisco in 1978 which included the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Take_Back_the_Night_(protest)" title="Take Back the Night (protest)">Take Back the Night</a> march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The conference led to anti-pornography feminists organizing in New York in 1979 under the banner of <a href="/wiki/Women_Against_Pornography" title="Women Against Pornography">Women Against Pornography</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and to similar organizations and efforts being created across the United States. In 1983, Page Mellish, a one-time member of WAVPM and of WAP, founded <a href="/wiki/Feminists_Fighting_Pornography" title="Feminists Fighting Pornography">Feminists Fighting Pornography</a> to focus on political activism seeking legal changes to limit the porn industry. Andrea Dworkin and <a href="/wiki/Catharine_MacKinnon" title="Catharine MacKinnon">Catharine MacKinnon</a> wanted civil laws restricting pornography and to this end drafted the <a href="/wiki/Antipornography_Civil_Rights_Ordinance" title="Antipornography Civil Rights Ordinance">Antipornography Civil Rights Ordinance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-sex_feminists">Pro-sex feminists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pro-sex feminists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1979 feminist journalist <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Willis" title="Ellen Willis">Ellen Willis</a> was one of the early voices criticizing anti-pornography feminists for what she saw as sexual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritanism" title="Puritanism">puritanism</a>, moral <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarianism</a> and a threat to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_speech" title="Free speech">free speech</a>. Her 1981 essay, <i>Lust Horizons: Is the Women's Movement Pro-Sex?</i> is the origin of the term, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-sex_feminism" title="Pro-sex feminism">pro-sex feminism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The response to the anti-pornography strand of feminism by the sex-positive feminists was one that promoted sex as an avenue of pleasure for women, seeing anti-pornography positions as aligned to the political right-wing's war on recreational sex and pornography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Early sex positive groups included <a href="/wiki/Samois" title="Samois">Samois</a>, founded in San Francisco in 1978, whose early members included <a href="/wiki/Gayle_Rubin" title="Gayle Rubin">Gayle Rubin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pat_Califia" title="Pat Califia">Pat Califia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_Sex_Mafia" title="Lesbian Sex Mafia">Lesbian Sex Mafia</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Allison" title="Dorothy Allison">Dorothy Allison</a> and Jo Arnone in New York in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_Anti-Censorship_Taskforce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Feminist Anti-Censorship Taskforce (page does not exist)">Feminist Anti-Censorship Taskforce</a> (FACT) was set up in 1984 by Ellen Willis in response to the <a href="/wiki/Antipornography_Civil_Rights_Ordinance" title="Antipornography Civil Rights Ordinance">Dworkin-MacKinnon Ordinance</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boffin_1996_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boffin_1996-14">[14]</a></sup> in 1989 <a href="/wiki/Feminists_Against_Censorship" title="Feminists Against Censorship">Feminists Against Censorship</a> formed in the UK, its members including <a href="/wiki/Avedon_Carol" title="Avedon Carol">Avedon Carol</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Feminists_for_Free_Expression&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Feminists for Free Expression (page does not exist)">Feminists for Free Expression</a> formed in the USA in 1992 with founding members including <a href="/wiki/Veronica_Vera" title="Veronica Vera">Veronica Vera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Candida_Royalle" title="Candida Royalle">Candida Royalle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_events">Key events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Key events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1980 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Organisation_for_Women" title="National Organisation for Women">National Organization for Women</a> identified what became known as the "Big Four" through declaring that "Pederasty, pornography, sadomasochism and public sex" were about "exploitation, violence or invasion of privacy" and not "sexual preference or orientation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> One of the more memorable clashes between the pro-sex and anti-porn feminists occurred at the <a href="/wiki/1982_Barnard_Conference_on_Sexuality" title="1982 Barnard Conference on Sexuality">1982 Barnard Conference on Sexuality</a>. Anti-pornography feminists were excluded from the events’ planning committee, so they staged rallies outside the conference to show their disdain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBride_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBride-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_debates">The debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two sides of the feminist sex wars clashed over a number of issues, resulting in intense debates held both in person and in various media.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pornography_debate">Pornography debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pornography debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist_views_on_pornography" title="Feminist views on pornography">Feminist views on pornography</a></div>
<p>Toward the end of the 1970s, much of the discourse in the feminist movement shifted from the discussion of <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_feminism" title="Lesbian feminism">lesbian feminism</a> to focus on the new topic of <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexuality</a>. One of the primary concerns with sexuality was the issue of <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a>, which caused a great divide among feminists. The two recognized sides of the debate were anti-pornography feminism and "pro-sex" feminism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitemaker.umich_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sitemaker.umich-17">[17]</a></sup> One of the major influences of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-pornography_feminism" title="Anti-pornography feminism">anti-pornography feminism</a> was its predecessor, lesbian feminism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Anti-pornography movements developed from fundamental arguments displayed by lesbianism, such as the notion of patriarchal sexual relations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Willis" title="Ellen Willis">Ellen Willis</a> described these relations as being "based on male power backed by force."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> From this perspective, pornography is created exclusively for men by men and is a direct reflection of the man-dominant paradigm surrounding sexual relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitemaker.umich_a_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-sitemaker.umich_a-19">[19]</a></sup> Another idea taken from lesbian feminism by anti-pornography groups was that sexuality is about creating a compassionate bond and a lasting relation with another person, contrary to the belief of the purely physical nature of sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her book, <i>Pornography: Men Possessing Women</i>, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Dworkin" title="Andrea Dworkin">Andrea Dworkin</a> argued that the theme of pornography is male dominance and as a result it is intrinsically harmful to women and their well-being. Dworkin believed that pornography is not only damaging in its production but also in its consumption, since the viewer will mentally internalize pornography's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misogynistic" title="Misogynistic">misogynistic</a> portrayal of women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitemaker.umich_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-sitemaker.umich-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robin_Morgan" title="Robin Morgan">Robin Morgan</a> summarized the view of anti-pornography feminists that pornography and <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_women" title="Violence against women">violence against women</a> are linked in her statement, "pornography is the theory, rape is the practice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The anti-pornography movement has been criticised by <a href="/wiki/Sex-positive_feminism" title="Sex-positive feminism">sex-positive feminists</a> as a repression of sexuality and a move towards censorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitemaker.umich_a_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-sitemaker.umich_a-19">[19]</a></sup> In her article,<i>Thinking Sex: Notes for a Radical Theory of the Politics of Sexuality</i>, <a href="/wiki/Gayle_Rubin" title="Gayle Rubin">Gayle Rubin</a> characterizes sex liberation as a feminist goal and denounces the idea that anti-pornography feminists speak collectively for all of feminism. She offers the notion that what is needed is a theory of sexuality separate from feminism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In <i>XXX: A Woman's Right to Pornography</i>, <a href="/wiki/Wendy_McElroy" title="Wendy McElroy">Wendy McElroy</a> summarizes the sex-positive perspective as "the benefits pornography provides to women far outweigh any of its disadvantages".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pornography debate among <a href="/wiki/Radical_feminism" title="Radical feminism">radical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Individualist_feminism" title="Individualist feminism">libertarian feminists</a> has focused on the depictions of female sexuality in relation to male sexuality in this type of media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-24">[24]</a></sup> Radical feminists emphasize that pornography illustrates objectification and normalization of sexual violence through presentation of specific acts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-24">[24]</a></sup> In contrast, libertarian feminists are concerned with the stigmatization of sexual minorities and the limited right to practice sexual choice that would be hindered without pornography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sadomasochism_debate">Sadomasochism debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sadomasochism debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Feminist_views_on_BDSM" title="Feminist views on BDSM">Feminist views on BDSM</a></div>
<p>The main locus of the sex wars' debate on <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">sadomasochism</a> and other <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">BDSM</a> practices was San Francisco. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAVPM" title="WAVPM">Women Against Violence in Pornography and Media</a> was founded there in 1977. Its first political action was to picket a live show at a strip club featuring women performing sadomasochistic acts on each other, in line with its stated aim to end all portrayals of women being "bound, raped, tortured, killed or degraded for sexual stimulation or pleasure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> As well as campaigning against pornography, WAVPM were also strongly opposed to <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">BDSM</a>, seeing it as ritualized violence against women and opposed its practice within the lesbian community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1978 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAMOIS" title="SAMOIS">SAMOIS</a> was formed, an organization for women in the BDSM community who saw their sexual practices as consistent with feminist principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prostitution_debate">Prostitution debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prostitution debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Feminist_views_on_prostitution" title="Feminist views on prostitution">Feminist views on prostitution</a></div>
<p>Another debate of the feminist sex wars centered on <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>. The women in the anti-pornography camp argued against prostitution, claiming it is forced on women who have no other alternatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view"><span title="This statement is possibly biased. (November 2015)">neutrality</span></a> is <a href="/wiki/Talk:Feminist_sex_wars" title="Talk:Feminist sex wars">disputed</a></i>]</sup> Meanwhile, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex-positive_feminists" title="Sex-positive feminists">sex-positive feminists</a> argued that this position ignored the self-agency of women who chose <a href="/wiki/Sex_work" title="Sex work">sex work</a>, viewing prostitution as not inherently based on the exploitation of women. <a href="/wiki/Carol_Leigh" title="Carol Leigh">Carol Leigh</a> notes that "The Prostitutes rights movement of the early 1970s evolved directly from the women's movement", but adds: "The women's movement in the U.S. has always been ambivalent about prostitutes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The polarized views of feminists on prostitution have affected their positions on the related issue of <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a>, which is frequently for the purpose of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_exploitation" title="Sexual exploitation">sexual exploitation</a>, with anti-prostitution feminists taking up the position of <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">abolitionists</a> and sex-positive feminists that of regulationists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Min_Liu_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Min_Liu-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>The polarization of feminist ideology during the sex wars has had wide ranging effects. Examples include, according to Liu (2011), "The confusion in the interpretation of the definition of human trafficking is a consequence of opposing feminist views on prostitution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Min_Liu_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Min_Liu-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Third-wave_feminists.27_view_on_sex_wars">Third-wave feminists' view on sex wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feminist_sex_wars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Third-wave feminists' view on sex wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Third-wave_feminism" title="Third-wave feminism">Third-wave feminist</a> writings promote personal, individualized views on the gender-related issues focused on during the feminist sex wars, such as prostitution, pornography and sadomasochism. In particular, the third-wave view of pornography is that there is no greater meaning other than which the actor or consumer gives it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Items such as sex objects and porn, identified by some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_wave_feminism" title="Second wave feminism">second-wave feminists</a> as instruments of oppression are now no longer being exclusively used by men but also by women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Feminist critic <a href="/wiki/Teresa_de_Lauretis" title="Teresa de Lauretis">Teresa de Lauretis</a> sees the sex wars not in terms of polarized sides but as reflecting a <a href="/wiki/Third-wave_feminism" title="Third-wave feminism">third wave feminism</a> inherently embodying difference, which may include conflicting and competing drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Code_2003_445_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Code_2003_445-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Meanwhile, critic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jana_Sawicki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jana Sawicki (page does not exist)">Jana Sawicki</a> rejects both the polarised positions, seeking a third way that is neither morally dogmatic or uncritically libertarian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Code_2003_445_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Code_2003_445-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
