<p>The <b>Angels of Mons</b> is a popular <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a> about a group of <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angels</a> who supposedly protected members of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons" title="Battle of Mons">Battle of Mons</a> at the outset of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 22–23 August 1914, the first major engagement of the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_I)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War I)">British Expeditionary Force</a> in the First World War occurred at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons" title="Battle of Mons">Battle of Mons</a>. Advancing <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> forces were thrown back by heavily outnumbered British troops, who suffered heavy casualties and, being outflanked, were forced into rapid retreat the next day. The retreat and the battle were rapidly perceived by the British public as being a key moment in the war. Despite the censorship going on in Britain at the time, this battle was the first indication the British public had that defeating Germany would not be as easy as some had thought.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthur_Machen_and_.22The_Bowmen.22">Arthur Machen and "The Bowmen"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: Arthur Machen and "The Bowmen"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 29 September 1914 Welsh author <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Machen" title="Arthur Machen">Arthur Machen</a> published a short story entitled "The Bowmen" in the London newspaper <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evening_News_(London)" title="Evening News (London)">The Evening News</a></i>, inspired by accounts that he had read of the fighting at Mons and an idea he had had soon after the battle.</p>
<p>Machen, who had already written a number of factual articles on the conflict for the paper, set his story at the time of the retreat from the Battle of Mons in August 1914. The story described phantom bowmen from the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Battle of Agincourt</a> summoned by a soldier calling on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._George" title="St. George">St. George</a>, destroying a German host.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup> Machen's story was not, however, labelled as fiction and the same edition of the <i>Evening News</i> ran a story by a different author under the heading "Our Short Story". Additionally, Machen's story was written from a first-hand perspective and was a kind of <a href="/wiki/False_document" title="False document">false document</a>, a technique Machen knew well. The unintended result was that Machen had a number of requests to provide evidence for his sources for the story soon after its publication, from readers who thought it was true, to which he responded that it was completely imaginary, as he had no desire to create a <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a>.</p>
<p>A month or two later Machen received requests from the editors of <a href="/wiki/Parish_magazine" title="Parish magazine">parish magazines</a> to reprint the story, which were granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup> In the introduction to <i>The Bowmen and Other Legends of the War</i> (1915) Machen relates that an unnamed priest, the editor of one of these magazines, subsequently wrote to him asking if he would allow the story to be reprinted in <a href="/wiki/Pamphlet" title="Pamphlet">pamphlet</a> form, and if he would write a short preface giving sources for the story. Machen replied that they were welcome to reprint but he could not give any sources for the story since he had none. The priest replied that Machen must be mistaken, that the "facts" of the story must be true, and that Machen had just elaborated on a true account. As Machen later said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It seemed that my light fiction had been accepted by the congregation of this particular church as the solidest of facts; and it was then that it began to dawn on me that if I had failed in the art of letters, I had succeeded, unwittingly, in the art of deceit. This happened, I should think, some time in April, and the snowball of rumour that was then set rolling has been rolling ever since, growing bigger and bigger, till it is now swollen to a monstrous size.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Arthur Machen, Introduction to <i>The Bowmen and Other Legends of the War</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Around that time variations of the story began to appear, told as authentic histories, including an account that told how the corpses of German soldiers had been found on the battlefield with arrow wounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In "The Bowmen" Machen's soldier saw "a long line of shapes, with a shining about them." A Mr. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Percy_Sinnett" title="Alfred Percy Sinnett">A.P. Sinnett</a>, writing in the <i>Occult Review</i>, stated that "those who could see said they saw 'a row of shining beings' between the two armies." This led Machen to suggest that the bowmen of his story had become the Angels of Mons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup> This last point was challenged by Harold Begbie in his book: <i>On the Side of the Angels: A Reply to Arthur Machen</i>, London 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angels">Angels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Angels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 24 April 1915, an account was published in the British <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiritualist" title="Spiritualist">Spiritualist</a></i> magazine telling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_(religion)" title="Vision (religion)">visions</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> force that <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miraculously</a> intervened to help the British at the decisive moment of the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_rum_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_rum-3">[3]</a></sup> This rapidly resulted in a flurry of similar accounts and the spread of wild rumours. Descriptions of this force varied from it being medieval <a href="/wiki/Longbow" title="Longbow">longbow</a> archers alongside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._George" title="St. George">St. George</a> to a strange luminous cloud, though eventually the most popular version came to be <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angelic warriors</a>. Similar tales of such battlefield visions occurred in medieval and ancient warfare. Atrocity reports like the <a href="/wiki/Rape_of_Belgium" title="Rape of Belgium">Rape of Belgium</a> and that of <a href="/wiki/The_Crucified_Soldier" title="The Crucified Soldier">the Crucified Soldier</a> paved the way for a belief that the Christian God would intervene directly against such an evil enemy. However, there are strong similarities between many of these accounts of visions and Machen's story published six months earlier.</p>
<p>In May 1915 a full-blown controversy was erupting, with the angels being used as proof of the action of <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">divine providence</a> on the side of the Allies in <a href="/wiki/Sermon" title="Sermon">sermons</a> across Britain, and then spreading into newspaper reports published widely across the world. Machen, bemused by all this, attempted to end the rumours by republishing the story in August in book form, with a long <a href="/wiki/Preface" title="Preface">preface</a> stating the rumours were false and originated in his story. It became a <a href="/wiki/Bestseller" title="Bestseller">bestseller</a>, and resulted in a vast series of other publications claiming to provide <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> of the Angels' existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowmen_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bowmen-1">[1]</a></sup> Machen tried to set the record straight, but any attempt to lessen the impact of such an inspiring story was seen as bordering on <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> by some. These new publications included popular songs and artists' renderings of the angels. There were more reports of angels and <a href="/wiki/Vision_(spirituality)" title="Vision (spirituality)">apparitions</a> from the front including <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Arc" title="Joan of Arc">Joan of Arc</a>.</p>
<p>Kevin McClure's study describes two types of accounts circulating, some more clearly based on Machen, others with different details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In a time of intense media interest all these reports allegedly confirming sightings of supernatural activity were second-hand and some of them were <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoaxes</a> created by soldiers who were not even at Mons. A careful investigation by the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Psychical_Research" title="Society for Psychical Research">Society for Psychical Research</a> in 1915 said of the first-hand testimony, "We have received none at all, and of testimony at second-hand we have none that would justify us in assuming the occurrence of any <a href="/wiki/Supernormal" title="Supernormal">supernormal</a> phenomenon". The SPR went on to say the stories relating to battlefield "visions" which circulated during the spring and summer of 1915, "prove on investigation to be founded on mere rumour, and cannot be traced to any authoritative source.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_rum_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_rum-3">[3]</a></sup> Given that the Society for Psychical Research believed in the existence of supernatural forces, the conclusions of this report are highly significant.</p>
<p>The sudden spread of the rumours in the spring of 1915, six months after the events and Machen's story was published, is also puzzling. The stories published then often attribute their sources to anonymous British officers. The latest and most detailed examination of the Mons story by <a href="/wiki/David_Clarke_(lecturer)" title="David Clarke (lecturer)">David Clarke</a> suggests these men may have been part of a covert attempt by military intelligence to spread morale-boosting <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a>. As it was a time of allied problems with the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Lusitania" title="RMS Lusitania"><i>Lusitania</i></a> sinking, <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> attacks and failure to achieve a breakthrough on the Western Front, the timing would make military sense. Some of the stories conveniently claimed that sources could not be revealed for security reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_book_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_book-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The only real evidence of visions from actual named serving soldiers provided during the debate stated that they saw visions of phantom cavalrymen, not angels or bowmen, and this occurred during the retreat rather than at the battle itself. Furthermore, these visions did not intervene to attack or deter German forces, a crucial element in Machen's story and in the later tales of angels. Since during the retreat many troops were exhausted and had not slept properly for days, such visions could be hallucinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_rum_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_rum-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the conclusion of the most detailed study of the event it seems that Machen's story provided the genesis for the vast majority of the tales. The stories themselves certainly boosted <a href="/wiki/Morale" title="Morale">morale</a> on the home front, as popular enthusiasm was dying down in 1915 and they demonstrate the importance of religion in wartime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_book_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_book-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_developments">Postwar developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Postwar developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">

<p>After the war the story continued to be frequently repeated but no evidence to support the claim that the Angels existed was ever given by those who were there. The best evidence provided was in Brigadier-General <a href="/wiki/John_Charteris" title="John Charteris">John Charteris</a>' memoirs <i>At G.H.Q.</i>, published in 1931, which said the story of the Angels of Mons was a popular rumour amongst the troops in September 1914; this was the earliest any account said the rumour was in circulation. However it appears from examination of his original letters he wrote those entries after the war and falsified the dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Given his association with pieces of allied propaganda like the story of the “German Corpse-Rendering Works” (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kadaververwertungsanstalt" title="Kadaververwertungsanstalt">Kadaververwertungsanstalt</a></i>) this might indicate Charteris had been behind an attempt to use the Angels for propaganda purposes.</p>
<p>Machen was associated with the story for the rest of his life and grew sick of the connection, as he regarded “The Bowmen” as a poor piece of work. He made little money from the story then or later.</p>
<p>The sudden revival of interest in appearances of angels from the 1980s onwards, especially in the United States, not only amongst Christians, but those interested in the <a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">New Age</a>, has caused uncritical accounts of the story of the angels who saved the British army to be regularly published in books and magazines. Similarly, the story is also often used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sceptic" title="Sceptic">sceptics</a> as a good example of how believers in the supernatural can become convinced of fantastic things by slender evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> References to the story can be found in World War I set novels and films like <i><a href="/wiki/FairyTale:_A_True_Story" title="FairyTale: A True Story">FairyTale: A True Story</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Friends_of_Arthur_Machen" title="The Friends of Arthur Machen">The Friends of Arthur Machen</a> frequently publish articles on developments in the case.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Doidge_hoax">William Doidge hoax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: William Doidge hoax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>

<p>In 2001, an article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i> claimed that a diary, film and photographic evidence proving the existence of the Angels of Mons from a World War I soldier named William Doidge had been found. The article discussed a long involved story in which Doidge was involved with an American GI and an angel seen years later in <a href="/wiki/Woodchester_Mansion" title="Woodchester Mansion">Woodchester Mansion</a>. It was claimed <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Kaye_(director)" title="Tony Kaye (director)">Tony Kaye</a> were going to spend £350,000 to buy the evidence to make a film. Other papers like <i>Variety</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> and television programmes soon followed up the story and a website connected to the mystery became very popular. The footage was supposedly found in a trunk in an <a href="/wiki/Antique_shop" title="Antique shop">antique shop</a> by Danny Sullivan in <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, close to Machen's birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Caerleon" title="Caerleon">Caerleon</a>. In 2002, in a BBC Radio documentary <i>The Making of an Urban Myth</i>, Sullivan admitted the story was a complete hoax to drum up interest in Woodchester Mansion; the footage and soldier never existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angels_of_Mons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_Tale:_A_True_Story" title="Fairy Tale: A True Story">Fairy Tale: A True Story</a></i> based on the <a href="/wiki/Cottingley_Fairies" title="Cottingley Fairies">Cottingley Fairies</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theosophic_Society" title="Theosophic Society">Theosophic Society</a> organises a seminar, where a participant speaks to the audience claiming to be an <a href="/wiki/Witness" title="Witness">eyewitness</a> of the apparition.</li>
<li>The Angels of Mons were depicted as phantom bowmen from the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Battle of Agincourt</a> sighted by the immortal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando_(comics)" title="Orlando (comics)">Orlando</a> during the Battle of Mons in <i><a href="/wiki/The_League_of_Extraordinary_Gentlemen:_Black_Dossier" title="The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen: Black Dossier">The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen: Black Dossier</a></i>.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Promethea" title="Promethea">Promethea</a></i>, Margaret, a previous incarnation of Promethea, refers to imaginary things like herself and the Angels of Mons as the only comfort the young soldiers in the trenches had. She is seen earlier guiding a lone, wounded soldier home.</li>
<li>The Angels of Mons are referenced in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Library" title="Black Library">Black Library</a> novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghostmaker" title="Ghostmaker">Ghostmaker</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Dan_Abnett" title="Dan Abnett">Dan Abnett</a>, where a sniper is advised by the statue of an Imperial Saint.</li>
<li>In the 2013 published historical novel, <i>Raiffe and the Angels of Mons</i> by Mark Hadley, there is reference to the sightings during the Battle of Mons.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Explosions_in_the_Sky" title="Explosions in the Sky">Explosions in the Sky</a> album artwork for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Those_Who_Tell_The_Truth_Shall_Die,_Those_Who_Tell_The_Truth_Shall_Live_Forever" title="Those Who Tell The Truth Shall Die, Those Who Tell The Truth Shall Live Forever">Those Who Tell The Truth Shall Die, Those Who Tell The Truth Shall Live Forever</a></i> is said to be inspired by the Angels of Mons.</li>
<li>At the start of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Ed_Wood_(film)" title="Ed Wood (film)">Ed Wood</a></i>, Wood is shown producing a play based upon the legend.</li>
<li>"The Whole Enchilada", the second episode of the first series of <i><a href="/wiki/Demons_(TV_series)" title="Demons (TV series)">Demons</a></i> makes reference to hundreds of troops on the western front simultaneously sighting the Angels of Mons.</li>
<li>The Angels of Mons are featured in and partly inspire the short story <a href="/wiki/The_Hound_of_Death" title="The Hound of Death">The Hound of Death</a> by <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>.</li>
<li>In 1979, former <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> guitarist, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hackett" title="Steve Hackett">Steve Hackett</a> released the instrumental, "Clocks - The Angel of Mons", on his album: <i><a href="/wiki/Spectral_Mornings" title="Spectral Mornings">Spectral Mornings</a></i>. The album also includes another <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> ghost narrative in a song entitled "Tigermoth".</li>
<li>In the 2013 novel <i>Angel of Mons</i> by Robin Bennett, a teenage boy on a school trip to the World War I battlefields experiences mysterious flashbacks to the Battle of Mons.</li>
<li>Is referenced in the 2010 Harper Teen novel <i>Unearthly</i> by debut author Cynthia Hand</li>
<li>In the ITV series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eternal_Law_(TV_series)" title="Eternal Law (TV series)">Eternal Law</a></i>, the protagonist, an angel going by the name Zak Gist, claims to have been an Angel of Mons. He says that he couldn't bear the idea that all the soldiers were going to die, so he showed them an escape route.</li>
<li>In David Mitchell's 2004 novel <i>Cloud Atlas</i> the character Robert Frobisher composed a piece called "Angel of Mons".</li>
<li>In the 2015 Novel <i>The Long Utopia</i> by Terry Pratchett and Stephen Baxter, the Angels of Mons were identified as a secret unit of 'Steppers' (people with the natural ability to shift themselves and others to parallel universes) who were using their abilities to bring the wounded to safety.</li>
</ul>
