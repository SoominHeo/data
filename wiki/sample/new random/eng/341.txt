<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Isotopes of potassium</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b><a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">Potassium</a></b> (<b>K</b>) has 24 known <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> from <sup>32</sup>K to <sup>56</sup>K. Three isotopes occur naturally: stable <sup>39</sup>K (93.3%) and <sup>41</sup>K (6.7%), and the long-lived radioisotope <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40"><sup>40</sup>K</a> (0.012%).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Relative_atomic_mass" title="Relative atomic mass">relative atomic mass</a> is 39.0983(1).</p>
<p>Naturally occurring radioactive <sup>40</sup>K decays to stable <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon"><sup>40</sup>Ar</a> (10.72% of decays) by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a> (giving it the longest known positron-emitter nuclide half-life). Alternately, and most of the time (89.28%), it decays to stable <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium"><sup>40</sup>Ca</a> by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. <sup>40</sup>K has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 1.248×10<sup>9</sup> years. The long half life of this primordial radioisotope is caused by a highly spin-forbidden transition: <sup>40</sup>K has a nuclear spin of 4, while both of its decay daughters are even-even isotopes with spins of 0.</p>
<p><sup>40</sup>K occurs in natural potassium (and thus in some commercial salt substitutes) in sufficient quantity that large bags of those substitutes can be used as a radioactive source for classroom demonstrations. In healthy animals and people, <sup>40</sup>K represents the largest source of radioactivity, greater even than <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14"><sup>14</sup>C</a>. In a human body of 70 kg mass, about 4,400 nuclei of <sup>40</sup>K decay per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The decay of <sup>40</sup>K to <sup>40</sup>Ar enables a commonly used method for dating rocks. The conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potassium-argon_dating" title="Potassium-argon dating">K-Ar dating method</a> depends on the assumption that the rocks contained no argon at the time of formation and that all the subsequent radiogenic argon (i.e., <sup>40</sup>Ar) was quantitatively retained. <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">Minerals</a> are dated by measurement of the concentration of potassium and the amount of radiogenic <sup>40</sup>Ar that has accumulated.</p>
<p>All other potassium isotopes have half-lives under a day, most under a minute. The least stable are <sup>33</sup>K and <sup>34</sup>K, both with half-lives shorter than 25 nanoseconds. The half-life of <sup>32</sup>K is unknown.</p>
<p>Outside of its use in dating <sup>40</sup>K has been used extensively as <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_tracer" title="Radioactive tracer">tracers</a> in studies of <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. Various potassium isotopes also been used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a> studies because potassium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macronutrient_(ecology)" title="Macronutrient (ecology)">macronutrient</a> required for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_potassium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Values marked # are not purely derived from experimental data, but at least partly from systematic trends. Spins with weak assignment arguments are enclosed in parentheses.</li>
<li>Uncertainties are given in concise form in parentheses after the corresponding last digits. Uncertainty values denote one standard deviation, except isotopic composition and standard atomic mass from IUPAC, which use expanded uncertainties.</li>
<li>Nuclide masses are given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAP" title="IUPAP">IUPAP</a> Commission on Symbols, Units, Nomenclature, Atomic Masses and Fundamental Constants (SUNAMCO).</li>
<li>Isotope abundances are given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Isotopic_Abundances_and_Atomic_Weights" title="Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights">Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights</a>.</li>
</ul>
