<p><b>George Norman Douglas</b> (8 December 1868 – 7 February 1952) was a British writer, now best known for his 1917 novel <i><a href="/wiki/South_Wind_(novel)" title="South Wind (novel)">South Wind</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k.26h_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-k.26h-2">[2]</a></sup> His travel books such as his 1915 <i>Old Calabria</i> were also appreciated for the quality of their writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Norman Douglas was born in <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%BCringen,_Austria" title="Thüringen, Austria">Thüringen, Austria</a> (his surname was registered at birth as <i>Douglass</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His mother was Vanda von Poellnitz. His father was John Sholto Douglas (1845–1874), manager of a cotton mill, who died in a climbing accident when Douglas was about six. He spent the first years of his life on the family estate, Villa Falkenhorst, in Thüringen.</p>
<p>Douglas was brought up mainly in Scotland at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tilquhillie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tilquhillie (page does not exist)">Tilquhillie</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Aberdeenshire" title="River Dee, Aberdeenshire">Deeside</a>, his paternal home. He was educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarlet_Hall" title="Yarlet Hall">Yarlet Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uppingham_School" title="Uppingham School">Uppingham School</a> in England, and then at a grammar school in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k.26h_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-k.26h-2">[2]</a></sup> Douglas's paternal grandfather was the 14th <a href="/wiki/Laird" title="Laird">Laird</a> of Tilquhillie. Douglas's maternal great-grandfather was General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Ochoncar_Forbes" title="James Ochoncar Forbes">James Ochoncar Forbes</a>, 17th <a href="/wiki/Lord_Forbes" title="Lord Forbes">Lord Forbes</a>.</p>
<p>He started in the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Diplomatic_Service" title="Her Majesty's Diplomatic Service">diplomatic service</a> in 1894 and from then until 1896 was based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>, but was placed on leave following a sexual scandal. In 1897 he bought a villa (Villa Maya) in <a href="/wiki/Posillipo" title="Posillipo">Posillipo</a>, a maritime suburb of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. The next year he <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">married a cousin</a> Elizabeth Louisa Theobaldina FitzGibbon (their mothers were sisters, daughters of Baron Ernst von Poellnitz). They had two children, Louis Archibald (Archie) and Robert Sholto (Robin),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndch_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ndch-5">[5]</a></sup> but divorced in 1903 on grounds of Elizabeth's infidelity. Norman's first book publication, (<i>Unprofessional Tales</i> (1901)) was written under the pseudonym <i>Normyx</i>, in collaboration with Elizabeth.</p>
<p>He moved to <a href="/wiki/Capri" title="Capri">Capri</a>, spending time there (at the Villa Daphne) and in London, and became a more committed writer. Nepenthe, the fictional island setting of <i><a href="/wiki/South_Wind_(novel)" title="South Wind (novel)">South Wind</a></i>, is Capri in light disguise. There he was friend with the eccentric community and opium addict Baron <a href="/wiki/Jacques_d%27Adelsw%C3%A4rd-Fersen" title="Jacques d'Adelswärd-Fersen">Jacques d'Adelswärd-Fersen</a>. In 1912–1914 he worked for <i>The English Review</i>. He met <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a> through this connection. This led to a feud, after Lawrence in his 1922 novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Aaron%27s_Rod_(novel)" title="Aaron's Rod (novel)">Aaron's Rod</a></i> based a character on Douglas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In late 1916 he jumped bail in London on a charge of <a href="/wiki/Indecent_assault" title="Indecent assault">indecent assault</a> on a sixteen-year-old boy, and effectively then lived in exile. He himself wrote of this in self-exculpation: 'Norman Douglas of Capri, and of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, was formerly of England, which he fled during the war to avoid persecution for kissing a boy and giving him some cakes and a shilling'. (The boy in fact complained to the police).</p>
<p>In the book <i>Twentieth Century Authors</i>, Douglas stated he disliked <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritanism" title="Puritanism">Puritanism</a>, and "all kinds of set forms, including official <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k.26h_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-k.26h-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During Douglas's years in Florence, he was associated with the publisher and bookseller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pino_Orioli" title="Pino Orioli">Pino Orioli</a>, who published in Italy in his 'Lungarno' series a number of Douglas's books and also works by other English authors, many of which (such as the first edition of Lawrence's <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley's Lover">Lady Chatterley's Lover</a></i>), would have been prosecuted for obscenity if published in London. Douglas probably had a major hand in writing Orioli's autobiography, <i>Adventures of a Bookseller</i>. Douglas' 1920 novel <i>They Went</i> is a fantasy based on <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Breton</a> <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k.26h_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-k.26h-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further scandals led to Douglas leaving Italy for the south of France in 1937. Following the collapse of France in 1940 Douglas left the Riviera, and on a circuitous journey to London, where he lived from 1942 to 1946, he published the first edition of his 'Almanac' in a tiny edition in Lisbon. He returned to Capri in 1946 and was made a citizen of the island. His circle of acquaintances included the writer <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>, the composer <a href="/wiki/Kaikhosru_Shapurji_Sorabji" title="Kaikhosru Shapurji Sorabji">Kaikhosru Shapurji Sorabji</a> and the food writer <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_David" title="Elizabeth David">Elizabeth David</a>.</p>
<p>He died in Capri, apparently deliberately overdosing himself on drugs after a long illness. (see <i>Impossible Woman: Memoirs of Dottoressa Moore</i>, ed. by Greene). His last words are reputed to have been: "Get those fucking nuns away from me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Latin inscription on his tombstone, from an ode by <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, reads: <i>Omnes eodem cogimur</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndch_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ndch-5">[5]</a></sup> "We are all driven to the same end" (i.e., death).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.M._Tomlinson" title="H.M. Tomlinson">H.M. Tomlinson</a>, a contemporary of Douglas's, concluded his 1931 biography by saying that Douglas's kind of prose "is at present out of fashion". He compared the writing to that of great English essayists and novelists: to <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>'s irony and <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Sterne" title="Laurence Sterne">Laurence Sterne</a>'s warmth. Douglas is, writes Tomlinson, "a sad and lonely man", confined to a distant and sterile "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultima_Thule" title="Ultima Thule">Ultima Thule</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Ackroyd" title="Peter Ackroyd">Peter Ackroyd</a> describes Douglas's <i>London Street Games</i> as "a vivid memorial to the inventiveness and energy of London children, and an implicit testimony to the streets which harboured and protected their play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Sutherland_(author)" title="John Sutherland (author)">John Sutherland</a> reports that "Douglas's Mediterranean travel writing chimed with the public taste", and that "there was a time when, in smart literary conversations, Norman Douglas was regarded as one of the smartest things going. Part of that smartness was his keeping, for the whole of his long depraved life, one jump ahead of the law."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In "The Grand Tour and Beyond: British and American Travellers in Southern Italy, 1545-1960", <a href="/wiki/Edward_Chaney" title="Edward Chaney">Edward Chaney</a>, wrote that "the true heir to the great tradition of the 'pedestrian tour' in our own [20th] century has been 'pagan-to-the-core' Norman Douglas. Having first visited the south of Italy with his brother in 1888, before he was 30 he had abandoned his pregnant Russian mistress and his job at the British Embassy in St Petersburg and purchased a villa at Posillipo. By then he had also published his first piece on the subject of southern Italy.." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Douglas's most famous work <i><a href="/wiki/South_Wind_(novel)" title="South Wind (novel)">South Wind</a></i> is a fictionalized account of life in Capri, with controversial references to moral and sexual issues. It has been frequently reprinted.</p>
<p>His travel books also combine erudition, insight, whimsicality, and some fine prose. These works include <i>Siren Land</i> (1911), <i>Fountains in the Sand</i>, described as 'rambles amongst the oases of <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>' (1912), <i>Old Calabria</i> (1915), <i>Together</i> (Austria) (1923) and <i>Alone</i> (Italy) (1921).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russo-3">[3]</a></sup> Reviewing Douglas's work in <i>Italian Americana</i>, John Paul Russo wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Douglas .. published three travel books of his walking tours of Italy: <i>Siren Land</i>, ... <i>Old Calabria</i> ... and <i>Alone</i> ... Scholars prefer the first; Douglas and his afficionados, the third; but the common reader has decided upon the middle work as the masterpiece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Russo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Douglas's early pamphlets on Capri were revised in <i>Capri</i> (privately published, 1930). His last published work was <i>A Footnote on Capri</i> (1952).</p>
<p>In 1928, Douglas published <i>Some Limericks</i>, an anthology of more-or-less obscene <a href="/wiki/Limerick_(poetry)" title="Limerick (poetry)">limericks</a> with a mock-scholarly critical apparatus. This classic (of its kind) has been frequently republished, often without acknowledgment in pirate editions. A definitive edition has now been published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_Douglas_in_fiction">Norman Douglas in fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Norman Douglas in fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Finnegans_Wake" title="Finnegans Wake">Finnegans Wake</a></i> (1939) makes several dozen references to <i>London Street Games</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>'s character Sebastian Knight in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Real_Life_of_Sebastian_Knight" title="The Real Life of Sebastian Knight">The Real Life of Sebastian Knight</a></i> (1941) owns a copy of <i>South Wind</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anthony_Burgess" title="Anthony Burgess">Anthony Burgess</a>'s <i>Earthly Powers</i> (1980) makes occasional reference to Norman Douglas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robertson_Davies" title="Robertson Davies">Robertson Davies</a>' character John Parlabane makes reference to Douglas in the Cornish Trilogy novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rebel_Angels" title="The Rebel Angels">The Rebel Angels</a></i> (1980).</li>
<li>Roger Williams's <i>Lunch With Elizabeth David</i> (Little, Brown, 1999) features Douglas as a major character.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alex_Preston" title="Alex Preston">Alex Preston</a>'s <i>In Love and War</i> (2014) features Douglas as a character.</li>
</ul>
