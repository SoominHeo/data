<p><b>Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ニンテンドーWi-Fiコネクション</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Nintendō Wi-Fi Konekushon</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (commonly abbreviated <b>WFC</b>) was an online <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_game" title="Multiplayer game">multiplayer gaming</a> service run by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> to provide free online play in compatible <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> games. The service included the company's <a href="/wiki/Wii_Shop_Channel" title="Wii Shop Channel">Wii Shop Channel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSi_Shop" title="DSi Shop">DSi Shop</a> game download services, which still work as of 2016. It also ran features for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> systems.</p>
<p>Games designed to take advantage of Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection offered Internet play integrated into the game. When promoting this service, Nintendo emphasized the simplicity and speed of starting an online game. For example, in <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_DS" title="Mario Kart DS">Mario Kart DS</a></i>, an online game was initiated by selecting the online multiplayer option from the main menu, then choosing whether to play with friends, or to play with other gamers (either in the local region or worldwide) at about the same skill level. After a selection was made, the game started searching for an available player.</p>
<p>On January 26, 2012, it was announced by Nintendo during an investors' meeting that the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection service will be succeeded by and absorbed into <b><a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Network" title="Nintendo Network">Nintendo Network</a></b>. This new online system will eventually unify the 3DS and Wii U platforms and replace Friend Codes, while providing paid downloadable content, an online community style multiplayer system, and personal accounts. Nintendo Network is fully supported on the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U" title="Wii U">Wii U</a>, whilst still continuing providing partial legacy support for both Wii and Nintendo DS under the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection brand. Specifically, the Wii U can boot to Wii mode and then access the <a href="/wiki/List_of_messaging_applications_for_Nintendo_game_consoles#Wii_Message_Board" title="List of messaging applications for Nintendo game consoles">Wii Message Board</a> messages which have been recorded by the gameplay progress of compatible local games, but it cannot send Wii Message Board messages remotely between different machines.</p>
<p>The Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection service was terminated on May 20, 2014 at 10.30 PM EST for all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_Wii" title="Nintendo Wii">Wii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">DS</a> games; online play features incorporated into these games are no longer available. The shutdown is connected to the shutdown of multiplayer services by <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a>, who was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Glu_Mobile" title="Glu Mobile">Glu Mobile</a> in 2012. 3DS and Wii U games are not affected by the shutdown because their multiplayer platform uses Nintendo's own infrastructure instead of a third-party service. The Wii Shop Channel, DSiWare shop, third-party video services, and the Pay &amp; Play variants will remain active for the moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-nintendoterminate_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-nintendoterminate-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-gamespyshutdown_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-gamespyshutdown-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shutdown had an immediate effect on all Nintendo-published Wii and Nintendo DS titles, however it may not necessarily apply to certain third-party titles, which could have separate servers running their own games' online functions. For example, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> revealed that some of the games they published on the Wii and Nintendo DS had their online support terminated on June 30, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NL-EAterminate_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NL-EAterminate-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A small selection of online-enabled Wii games, such as newer <i>FIFA</i> games as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i> games (except <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_World_at_War" title="Call of Duty: World at War">World at War</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Quest_X" title="Dragon Quest X">Dragon Quest X</a></i>, which by happenstance are not branded under the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection, may continue using their online functions normally. In 2015 and onwards, various <a href="/wiki/List_of_Wii_games_on_Wii_U_eShop" title="List of Wii games on Wii U eShop">Wii</a> and Nintendo DS titles were digitally re-released, including titles which formerly supported Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection. However, like their physical counterparts post shutdown date, the digital versions are only playable offline, despite faithfully retaining online features.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On November 14, 2005, the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection was deployed, having been developed under the direct supervision of company president Satoru Iwata. Nintendo's Takao Ohara lamented that Nintendo's long history of online strategies had each quit due to unexpectedly insufficient userbases, but that the Wi-Fi Connection had in four months garnered 2.9 million connections from over one million unique users. To achieve the goal of a truly sustainable online userbase with the most-used network service in the world, Ohara described a new strategy of identifying and relieving four main barriers. The proposed four barriers are difficult setup procedures, the psychological barrier preventing newcomers from joining in games, the unpleasantness of receiving abuse from other players, and the cost barrier. The company's proposed online strategy at this point was called "simple, safe, free". Nintendo believed that the online platform's success directly propelled the commercial success of the entire Nintendo DS platform. The Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection then served as part of the basis of what would become the Wii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Famitsu_March_2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Famitsu_March_2006-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection was developed to be easy to connect to, safe for everyone to use, and free. Games designed to take advantage of Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection offered Internet play integrated into the game. The Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection supported up to sixteen players on the Nintendo DS and thirty-two players on the Wii. Basic features of the Wi-Fi Connection included worldwide matchmaking, leaderboards, tournaments. Additional features were available between friends who exchanged Friend Codes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Friend_Codes">Friend Codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Friend Codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each game that used the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection generated a unique twelve-digit Friend Code that could be exchanged with friends and be used to maintain individual friend lists in each game. Though certain games could be played online without a Friend Code, a Friend Code was required to play with a specific person. Friend Codes were generated from an identifier unique to a copy of a game and the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection ID of a DS or Wii system. Using a different copy of a game or loading the same copy in a different system generated a new Friend Code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friend_codes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-friend_codes-9">[9]</a></sup> If users wanted to become "Friends", they had to mutually add Friend Codes and be authenticated as Friends once both of them were online. Nintendo introduced these features as conscious steps to preserve users' privacy. If a DS or Wii game was sold, but not the system, there was no risk of the purchaser impersonating the seller. If a user needed to replace his or her DS system, then the old system's Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection ID could be transferred wirelessly, in order to maintain the user's original Friend Codes on the new machine. Some games required that the user had to use Friend Codes to use any online functionality.</p>
<p>Many games have additional features that are enabled between registered friends. These may include customized matchmaking options, cooperative play, friend lists, text chat, and voice chat. <a href="/wiki/List_of_Wii_games_using_WiiConnect24" title="List of Wii games using WiiConnect24">Certain Wii games</a> use the 16-digit Wii Number to share some data passively between mutually registered users via <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a> instead of using independent Friend Codes. Although, some of these games may use both the 16-digit Wii Number and its own 12-digit Friend Code, depending on whether the online connectivity requires either passive data-sharing or active multiplaying respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pay_.26_Play">Pay &amp; Play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pay &amp; Play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, Nintendo announced a new feature for the Wi-Fi Connection called Pay &amp; Play. Games that use the Pay &amp; Play feature may have additional <a href="/wiki/Downloadable_content" title="Downloadable content">downloadable content</a> (DLC) or services that require extra fees. These fees will be paid for using <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Points" title="Nintendo Points">Nintendo Points</a>. A special red Wi-Fi Connection logo with the words "Pay &amp; Play" is used to distinguish these games from the regular free Wi-Fi games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first games to feature Pay &amp; Play were released in Japan as part of <a href="/wiki/WiiWare" title="WiiWare">WiiWare</a> on the March 25, 2008. <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_Crystal_Chronicles:_My_Life_as_a_King" title="Final Fantasy Crystal Chronicles: My Life as a King">Final Fantasy Crystal Chronicles: My Life as a King</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kotoba_no_Puzzle:_Mojipittan" title="Kotoba no Puzzle: Mojipittan">Kotoba no Puzzle Mojipittan Wii</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lonpos" title="Lonpos">Lonpos</a></i> each had downloadable content available for 100 to 800 Wii Points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first retail Wii titles to feature Pay &amp; Play functionality are <i><a href="/wiki/Samba_de_Amigo" title="Samba de Amigo">Samba de Amigo</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_Hero_World_Tour" title="Guitar Hero World Tour">Guitar Hero World Tour</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Band_2" title="Rock Band 2">Rock Band 2</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: WiiConnect24">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Wii_games_using_WiiConnect24" title="List of Wii games using WiiConnect24">List of Wii games using WiiConnect24</a></div>
<p>WiiConnect24 was shut down on June 28, 2013. WiiConnect24 was a feature of Wii that allowed the system to be connected to the Internet even when the console was in standby mode. Games and channels that utilized WiiConnect24 could send and receive data even while the game was not being played. Players who wished to send data to friends only needed to register each other's Wii System Code and not individual Friend Codes. Players could also send messages to their friends using WiiConnect24 from the Wii Message Board. When a message was received, the Wii's slot light glowed blue. WiiConnect24 is succeeded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StreetPass_and_SpotPass#SpotPass" title="StreetPass and SpotPass">SpotPass</a> for newer Nintendo consoles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BBC_iPlayer">BBC iPlayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: BBC iPlayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 9, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> announced that its online <a href="/wiki/BBC_iPlayer" title="BBC iPlayer">BBC iPlayer</a> would be available on the Wii via the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Channel" title="Internet Channel">Internet Channel</a> browser. Some users experienced difficulties with the service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On November 18, 2009, <a href="/wiki/BBC_iPlayer" title="BBC iPlayer">BBC iPlayer</a> on the Wii was relaunched as the BBC iPlayer Channel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which is free to download from the <a href="/wiki/Wii_Shop_Channel" title="Wii Shop Channel">Wii Shop Channel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The service was only available to users in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. On 10 February 2015, the BBC announced on their website that they had removed BBC iPlayer from the Wii Shop Channel and terminated the service on the Wii due to resource limitations and infrastructure changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wii_Shop_Channel">Wii Shop Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wii Shop Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wii_Shop_Channel" title="Wii Shop Channel">Wii Shop Channel</a></div>
<p>The <i>Wii Shop Channel</i> allows users to download games and other software by redeeming <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Points" title="Nintendo Points">Wii Points</a>, which can be obtained by purchasing Nintendo Points cards from retail outlets or directly through the <i>Wii Shop Channel</i> using <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> or <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa</a> debit/credit cards online. Users can browse in the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a>, <a href="/wiki/WiiWare" title="WiiWare">WiiWare</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wii_Menu" title="Wii Menu">Wii Channels</a> sections for downloads. A feature to purchase downloaded software as gifts for others became available worldwide on December 10, 2007. Additional channels that were not released at the console's launch are available for purchase in the Wii Shop Channel. These include: <a href="/wiki/Internet_Channel" title="Internet Channel">Internet Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Everybody_Votes_Channel" title="Everybody Votes Channel">Everybody Votes Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Check_Mii_Out_Channel" title="Check Mii Out Channel">Check Mii Out Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Channel" title="Nintendo Channel">Nintendo Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hulu" title="Hulu">Hulu Plus Channel</a> and the Japan-only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_Friend_Channel" title="Television Friend Channel">Television Friend Channel</a>. Currently all downloadable channels are free of charge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_Console_Channels">Virtual Console Channels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Virtual Console Channels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a></div>
<p><i>Virtual Console</i> channels are channels that allow users to play their downloaded <i>Virtual Console</i> games obtained from the Wii Shop Channel. The <i>Virtual Console</i> portion of the Wii Shop Channel specializes in older software originally designed and released for home entertainment platforms that are now defunct. These games are played on the Wii through the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console_emulator" title="Video game console emulator">emulation</a> of the older hardware. The prices are generally the same in almost every region and are determined primarily by the software's original platform.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WiiWare_Channels">WiiWare Channels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: WiiWare Channels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/WiiWare" title="WiiWare">WiiWare</a></div>
<p>Functioning similarly to the <i>Virtual Console</i> channels, <i>WiiWare</i> channels allow users to use their WiiWare games obtained from the Wii Shop Channel. The WiiWare section specializes in downloadable software specifically designed for the Wii. The first WiiWare games were made available on March 25, 2008 in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> WiiWare games launched in North America on May 12, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and launched in Europe and Australia on May 20, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The WiiWare section is being touted as a forum to provide developers with small budgets to release smaller-scale games without the investment and risk of creating a title to be sold at retail (somewhat similar to the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a> and the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">PlayStation Store</a>). While actual games have been planned to appear in this section since its inception, there had been no official word on when any would be appearing until June 27, 2007, when Nintendo made an official confirmation in a press release which revealed the first titles would surface sometime in 2008. According to Nintendo, "The remarkable motion controls will give birth to fresh takes on established genres, as well as original ideas that currently exist only in developers' minds."</p>
<p>Like Virtual Console games, WiiWare games are purchased using Wii Points. Nintendo handles all pricing options for the downloadable games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecast_Channel">Forecast Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Forecast Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Forecast Channel</i> first became available on December 19, 2006, and was shut down on June 28, 2013 along with its foundational <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a>. The <i>Forecast Channel</i> allowed weather reports and forecasts to be shown on the console from the Internet via the WiiConnect24 service. The <i>Forecast Channel</i> displayed a view of the Earth as a user-spinnable globe (courtesy of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>), with which users could view weather in other regions. When fully zoomed out, an accurate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-star_map_accuracy_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-star_map_accuracy-25">[25]</a></sup> star map was visible in the background. The <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper" title="Big Dipper">Big Dipper</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a> are easily recognizable, for example. The <i>Forecast Channel</i> features included the current forecast, the UV index, today's overall forecast, tomorrow's forecast, a 5-day forecast (only for the selected country you live in), and a laundry check (Japan only). Certain games like <i><a href="/wiki/Madden_NFL_07" title="Madden NFL 07">Madden NFL 07</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nights:_Journey_of_Dreams" title="Nights: Journey of Dreams">Nights: Journey of Dreams</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_%26_Sonic_at_the_Olympic_Winter_Games" title="Mario &amp; Sonic at the Olympic Winter Games">Mario &amp; Sonic at the Olympic Winter Games</a></i> could use the <i>Forecast Channel</i> to simulate weather conditions depending on the player's region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maddenweather_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-maddenweather-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="News_Channel">News Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: News Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>News Channel</i> allows users to access news headlines and current news events obtained from the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newschannel_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-newschannel-27">[27]</a></sup> News articles are available on a globe view, similar to the <i>Forecast Channel</i>, and as a slide show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wii_gamers_to_get_online_news_service_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wii_gamers_to_get_online_news_service-28">[28]</a></sup> The content was automatically updated and viewable via <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a> with clickable news images supported.</p>
<p>The <i>News Channel</i> became available in North America, Europe, and Australia on January 26, 2007. Content is in a variety of languages provided by the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>, which currently has a two-year contract to provide news and photos to Nintendo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Canadian news is submitted by <a href="/wiki/The_Canadian_Press" title="The Canadian Press">The Canadian Press</a> for publication. Japanese news is provided by <a href="/wiki/Goo_(search_engine)" title="Goo (search engine)">Goo</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> European news is provided by <a href="/wiki/Agence_France-Presse" title="Agence France-Presse">Agence France-Presse</a>.</p>
<p>Starting with the August 6, 2007 update, the <i>News Channel</i> shows a <a href="/wiki/News_ticker" title="News ticker">news ticker</a> in the Wii Menu. However, not visiting the channel for a period of time will result in the ticker not appearing, until the channel is viewed. A December 20, 2007 PAL region update increased the number of news feeds to the channel, sourced from a larger number of news resources and agencies, providing more news that is available per country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As with the Forecast Channel, the <i>News Channel</i> is not available in South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SK_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-SK-31">[31]</a></sup> The <i>News Channel</i> was shut down on June 28, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gameinformer.com_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-gameinformer.com-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Everybody_Votes_Channel">Everybody Votes Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Everybody Votes Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Everybody_Votes_Channel" title="Everybody Votes Channel">Everybody Votes Channel</a></div>
<p><i>Everybody Votes Channel</i> allowed users to vote in simple <a href="/wiki/Opinion_poll" title="Opinion poll">opinion polls</a> and compare and contrast opinions with those of friends, family, and people across the globe.</p>
<p><i>Everybody Votes Channel</i> was launched on February 13, 2007, and was available in the Wii Channels section of the Wii Shop Channel. The application allowed Wii owners to vote on various questions using their <a href="/wiki/Mii" title="Mii">Mii</a> as a registered voter. Additionally, voters could also make predictions for the choice that would be the most popular overall after their own vote had been cast. Each Mii's voting and prediction record was tracked and voters could also view how their opinions compared to others. Whether the Mii was correct in its predictions or not, it was displayed on a statistics page, along with a counter of how many times that Mii voted. Up to six Miis could be registered to vote on the console. The channel was free to download. Each player could make a suggestion for a poll a day. <i>Everybody Votes Channel</i> was shut down on June 28, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gameinformer.com_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-gameinformer.com-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="YouTube_Channel">YouTube Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: YouTube Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The YouTube channel allows the user to view YouTube videos on the television screen and has the ability to sign into an existing YouTube account. The YouTube channel, which became available without warning, is currently only available in the North American and Australian versions of the Wii system, with the North American release on November 15, 2012, only three days before the Wii U was released in North America. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Check_Mii_Out_Channel">Check Mii Out Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Check Mii Out Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Check_Mii_Out_Channel" title="Check Mii Out Channel">Check Mii Out Channel</a></div>
<p>The <i>Check Mii Out Channel</i> (also known as the <i>Mii Contest Channel</i> and the <i>WatchMii Channel</i>), is a channel that allows players to share their Miis and enter them into popularity contests. It was first available on November 11, 2007. It is available free to download from the <a href="/wiki/Wii_Menu" title="Wii Menu">Wii Channels</a> section of the <a href="/wiki/Wii_Shop_Channel" title="Wii Shop Channel">Wii Shop Channel</a>.</p>
<p>Users can post their own Miis in the Posting Plaza, or import other user-submitted Miis to their own personal Mii Parade. Each submitted Mii is assigned a 12-digit entry number to aid in searching. Submitted Miis are given 2 initials by their creator and a notable skill/talent to aid in sorting.</p>
<p>In the Contests section, players can submit their own Miis to compete in contests to best fit a certain description (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mario" title="Mario">Mario</a> without his cap). After the time period for sending a Mii has expired, the user has the choice of voting for three Miis featured on the judging panel, with ten random Miis being shown at a time. Once the judging period is over, the results of the contest may be viewed. Their selection and/or submission's popularity in comparison to others is displayed, as well as the winning Mii and user.</p>
<p>The Check Mii Out Channel sends messages to the Wii Message Board concerning recent contests. Participants in certain contests can add their user and submitted Mii to a photo with a background related to the contest theme. This picture can then be sent to the Wii Message Board. Check Mii Out Channel was shut down on June 28, 2013.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_Friend_Channel">Television Friend Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Television Friend Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Television Friend Channel</i> allows Wii users to check what programs are on the television. Content is provided by <a href="/wiki/Guide_Plus" title="Guide Plus">Guide Plus</a>. The channel is said to be "very fun and Nintendo-esque".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> A "stamp" feature allows users to mark programs of interest with a Mii-themed stamp. If an e-mail address or mobile phone number has been registered in the address book, the channel can send out an alert 30 minutes prior to the start of the selected program. The channel tracks the stamps of all Wii users and allows users to rate programs on a five-star scale. Additionally, when the channel is active the Wii Remote can be used to change the TV's volume and channel so that users can tune into their shows by way of the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign-tv_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-tv-36">[36]</a></sup> The Television Friend Channel launched in Japan on March 4, 2008. It is not going to be launched outside Japan, as most countries, unlike Japan, have a guide built into set-top boxes and/or TVs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digicam_Print_Channel">Digicam Print Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Digicam Print Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Digicam Print Channel</i> is a channel developed in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fujifilm</a> that allow users to import their digital photos from an SD card and place them into templates for printable photo books and business cards through a software wizard. The user is also able to place their <a href="/wiki/Mii" title="Mii">Mii</a> on a business card. The completed design is then sent online to Fujifilm who print and deliver the completed product to the user. The processing of individual photos is also available.</p>
<p>The Digicam Print Channel became available from July 23, 2008 in Japan. It was planned to be available in Europe, Australia, and North America, but was shut down on June 28, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today_and_Tomorrow_Channel">Today and Tomorrow Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Today and Tomorrow Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Today and Tomorrow Channel</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> became available in Japan on December 2, 2008, and in Europe, Australia, and South Korea<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> on September 9, 2009. The channel was developed in collaboration with Media Kobo and allowed users to view fortunes for up to six Miis across five categories: love, work, study, communications, and money. The channel also featured a compatibility test that compared two Miis, and it also gave out "lucky words" that had to be interpreted by the user. The channel used Mii birthdate data, but users had to input a birth year whenever they were loaded onto the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wii_My_Room_Channel">Wii My Room Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Wii My Room Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wii_no_Ma" title="Wii no Ma">Wii no Ma</a></div>
<p>A video on-demand service channel was released in Japan on May 1, 2009, it was planned to be released in North America.The channel is a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> between Nintendo and Japanese advertising agency <a href="/wiki/Dentsu" title="Dentsu">Dentsu</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demae_Channel">Demae Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Demae Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A food delivery service channel was released in Japan on May 26, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The channel is a joint venture between Nintendo and Japanese on-line food delivery portal service Demae-can. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nintendo_Channel">Nintendo Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Nintendo Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Nintendo Channel</i> (also known as the <b>Everybody's Nintendo Channel</b> in Japan), was launched in Japan on November 27, 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EverybodysNintendo_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-EverybodysNintendo-43">[43]</a></sup> in North America on May 7, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and in Europe and Australia on May 30, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and was shut down on June 28, 2013. It allowed <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> users to watch videos such as interviews, trailers, commercials, and even download <a href="/wiki/Game_demo" title="Game demo">demos</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>. In this capacity, the channel worked in a similar way to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DS_Download_Station" title="DS Download Station">DS Download Station</a>. The channel provided games info pages and users could rate games that they had played. A search feature was also available to assist users in finding new games to try or buy. The channel had the ability to take the user directly into the Wii Shop Channel for buying the wanted game immediately. The Nintendo Channel is updated with different Nintendo DS demos and new videos every week; the actual day of the week varies across different international regions.</p>
<p>An updated version of the Nintendo Channel was released in Japan on July 15, 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> North America on September 14, 2009, and in Europe on December 15, 2009. The update introduces a new interface and additional features, options, and statistics for users to view. However, the European version is missing some of these new additional features, such as options for choosing video quality. In addition, a weekly show known as <i><a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Week" title="Nintendo Week">Nintendo Week</a></i> began airing exclusively on the North American edition of the channel, while another weekly show <i>Nintendo TV</i>, is available on the UK version of the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mario_Kart_Channel">Mario Kart Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Mario Kart Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_Wii" title="Mario Kart Wii">Mario Kart Wii</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_Wii" title="Mario Kart Wii">Mario Kart Wii</a></i> allows players to install the <i>Mario Kart Channel</i> on their <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> console. The channel can work without inserting the <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_Wii" title="Mario Kart Wii">Mario Kart Wii</a></i> disc into the console, but to compete in races and time trials the disc is required. The use of the Mario Kart Channel allows for a number of options. A ranking option lets players see their best Time Trial scores for each track and compare their results to those of their friends and other players worldwide, represented by their Miis. Players will have the option of racing against the random or selective ghosts, or improving their results gradually by taking on the ghosts of rivals, those with similar race times. Users have the option to submit these times for others around the world to view. Players can also manage and register friends using the channel and see if any of them are currently online.</p>
<p>Another feature of the channel are Tournaments, where Nintendo will, on a regular basis, invite players to challenges similar to the missions on <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_DS" title="Mario Kart DS">Mario Kart DS</a></i>. Players are also able to compare their competition rankings with other players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wii_Message_Board">Wii Message Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Wii Message Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Message Board's online connectivity is now defunct, along with the shutdown of its foundational <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a> service. It allowed users to leave messages for friends, family members, or other users on a calendar-based message board. Users could also use WiiConnect24 to trade messages and pictures with other Wii owners, conventional email accounts (email pictures to console, but not pictures to email), and mobile phones (through text messages). Each Wii had an individual wii.com email account containing the Wii Number. Prior to trading messages, it was necessary to add and approve contacts in the address book, although the person added would not get a notification of the request. The service also alerted all users of incoming game-related information.</p>
<p>Message Board was available for users to post messages that are available to other Wii users by usage of Wii Numbers with the now defunct <a href="/wiki/WiiConnect24" title="WiiConnect24">WiiConnect24</a>. In addition to writing text, players could also include images from an SD card in the body of messages, as well as attaching a <a href="/wiki/Mii" title="Mii">Mii</a> to the message. Announcements of software updates and video game news were posted by Nintendo. The Message Board could be used for posting memos for oneself or for family members without going online. These messages could then be put on any day of the calendar. The Wii Message Board could also be updated automatically by a real-time game like <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_Crossing:_City_Folk" title="Animal Crossing: City Folk">Animal Crossing: City Folk</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wii_Speak">Wii Speak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Wii Speak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Users with the <a href="/wiki/Wii_Speak" title="Wii Speak">Wii Speak</a> peripheral were able to access the <i>Wii Speak Channel</i>. Users could join one of four rooms (with no limit to the number of people in each room) to chat with others online. Each user is represented by their own <a href="/wiki/Mii" title="Mii">Mii</a>, which lip-syncs to their words. In addition, users could also leave audio messages for other users by sending a message to their Wii Message Board. Users could also share photo <a href="/wiki/Slide_show" title="Slide show">slide shows</a> and comment on them. The Wii Speak Channel became available in North America and Europe on December 5, 2008.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nintendo_DS_Wi-Fi_Connection_games" title="List of Nintendo DS Wi-Fi Connection games">List of Nintendo DS Wi-Fi Connection games</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Wii_Wi-Fi_Connection_games" title="List of Wii Wi-Fi Connection games">List of Wii Wi-Fi Connection games</a></div>
<p>The first Wi-Fi Connection games were <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Kart_DS" title="Mario Kart DS">Mario Kart DS</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hawk%27s_American_Sk8land" title="Tony Hawk's American Sk8land">Tony Hawk's American Sk8land</a></i> for the Nintendo DS, both released on November 15, 2005, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_Crossing:_Wild_World" title="Animal Crossing: Wild World">Animal Crossing: Wild World</a></i> in Japan on November 23 and in North America on December 5. The first Wii Wi-Fi Connection games were released in 2006 in Japan and in 2007 overseas. In Japan and North America the first game was <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Battle_Revolution" title="Pokémon Battle Revolution">Pokémon Battle Revolution</a></i>. In Europe, Australia, and New Zealand the first game was <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Strikers_Charged" title="Mario Strikers Charged">Mario Strikers Charged</a></i>.</p>
<p>Currently, there are over 100 <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> games, 25 <a href="/wiki/WiiWare" title="WiiWare">WiiWare</a> games, 93 <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> games, and 4 <a href="/wiki/List_of_DSiWare_games_and_applications" title="List of DSiWare games and applications">Nintendo DSiWare</a> games available worldwide that supported Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection.</p>
<p>Since May 20, 2014 10:30 EST it is no longer possible to download additional content for games, as the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection service was terminated on that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_options">Connection options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Connection options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nintendo_DS">Nintendo DS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Nintendo DS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> has an <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11_(legacy_mode)" title="IEEE 802.11 (legacy mode)">802.11</a> wireless adapter built in allowing the DS to access the service via a compatible wireless network. Support for 802.11b clients must be offered by an access point for a DS to be able to connect to it. Public hotspots that use a <a href="/wiki/Captive_portal" title="Captive portal">captive portal</a> can be accessed after login using the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS_%26_DSi_Browser" title="Nintendo DS &amp; DSi Browser">Nintendo DS Browser</a>.</p>
<p>Prior to the release of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DSi" title="Nintendo DSi">Nintendo DSi</a>, neither consoles nor games could support security protocols more advanced than <a href="/wiki/Wired_Equivalent_Privacy" title="Wired Equivalent Privacy">WEP</a>. Even after the Nintendo DSi's release, only online-enabled games made exclusively or enhanced for usage on this console iteration were able to support the Nintendo DSi's native online connection. This extended to the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> as well, conditional the console's native online connection setting is the same as the Nintendo DSi, as other advanced settings were also unsupported.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wii">Wii</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Wii">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Wii has an 802.11b/g wireless adapter built-in. Due to flaws in its implementation, however, it is only capable of working when the 802.11 (legacy mode) basic rates of 1 Mbit/s and 2 Mbit/s are advertised by an access point. This means that 802.11b support must be offered on an access point for a Wii to be able to connect to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It is compatible with <a href="/wiki/Wired_Equivalent_Privacy" title="Wired Equivalent Privacy">WEP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_Protected_Access" title="Wi-Fi Protected Access">WPA</a> with TKIP or CCMP, and <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11i-2004" title="IEEE 802.11i-2004">WPA2</a> with CCMP. The Wii is also <a href="/wiki/AOSS" title="AOSS">AOSS</a> compatible as of the 3.0 systemupdate. The connection settings allows players to configure access to and save settings for up to three different networks. Connection settings can be detected automatically or entered manually. The Wii does not have an Ethernet port built in, but can be connected via wired LAN with a USB Ethernet adapter available from Nintendo and third parties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nintendo_Wi-Fi_USB_Connector">Nintendo Wi-Fi USB Connector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Nintendo Wi-Fi USB Connector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>If a compatible wireless network is not available, the Nintendo DS and Wii can also connect through the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Wi-Fi_USB_Connector" title="Nintendo Wi-Fi USB Connector">Nintendo Wi-Fi USB Connector</a>. Broadband Internet access is not required to make use of this connectivity, though it is recommended to reduce network <a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">latency</a>. It was priced at the Nintendo Online Store,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> however it has since been discontinued due to legal issues.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its replacement, the Nintendo Wi-Fi Network Adapter, and many third party products, provide similar functionality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hotspots">Hotspots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Hotspots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nintendo is working with hotspot providers to allow free access in public for Nintendo DS users. In 2005, Nintendo made an agreement with Texas-based firm <a href="/wiki/Wayport,_Inc." title="Wayport, Inc.">Wayport, Inc.</a> to provide access in <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> Restaurants in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> However, the deal was not renewed and has since expired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A similar partnership with FatPort to create free hotspots in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> was announced by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo of Canada</a> on October 19, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006 former Director of Marketing for Nintendo of Europe Jim Merrick announced that Nintendo was planning total of 25,000 hotspots in Europe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> with 7,500 in UK alone thanks to a partnership with <a href="/wiki/The_Cloud_(company)" title="The Cloud (company)">The Cloud</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BT_Openzone" title="BT Openzone">BT Openzone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nintendo of Australia initially announced on November 17, 2005 that they would roll out only 26 hotspots across the country, in partnership with selected <a href="/wiki/EB_Games" title="EB Games">EB Games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myer" title="Myer">Myer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dick_Smith_(retailer)" title="Dick Smith (retailer)">Dick Smith Powerhouse</a> stores. On April 14, 2007, Nintendo announced that over one thousand additional free hotspots had been added in a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Telstra" title="Telstra">Telstra</a> Wireless, providing access in selected hotels, airports, <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a> cafes, and <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> restaurants.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_website">Official website</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Official website">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nintendo created the official <a class="external text" href="http://www.nintendowifi.com" rel="nofollow">Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection</a> web site as a portal for gamers looking to access the service or whom were in need of troubleshooting assistance. The website had live statistics and data from the service's servers and recorded high scores and service status. It also allowed a user to link his or her Nintendo DS Wi-Fi Connection ID to a My Nintendo account. As of November 2008, the site has closed in North America and has now moved into a <a class="external text" href="https://www.nintendo.com/games/wifi" rel="nofollow">subsection</a> of the Games section on Nintendo.com. On May 20, 2014, the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection service was shut down, and because of that, Nintendo has deleted the subsection on its website.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closing">Closing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nintendo_Wi-Fi_Connection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Closing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 20, 2014, 10.30 PM EST Nintendo had ended Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection (WFC) for all games (except for Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection pay and play branded games, Nintendo DSi Shop services and Nintendo Wii Shop Channel services, Nintendo Wii/DSi update servers, and the Connection test server). Despite the closing of Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection many fan efforts to bring back support for Nintendo WFC have been successful with the release of a 3rd party server called Wiimmfi which allows Wii games to be played online similar to how they did before the shutdown with few restrictions.</p>
