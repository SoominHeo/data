<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sokoban</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Sokoban</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">倉庫番</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>sōkoban</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, warehouse keeper)</span> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Transport_puzzle" title="Transport puzzle">transport puzzle</a>, in which the player pushes <a href="/wiki/Wooden_box" title="Wooden box">boxes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crate" title="Crate">crates</a> around in a <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouse</a>, trying to get them to storage locations. The puzzle is usually implemented as a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a>.</p>
<p>Sokoban was created in 1981 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hiroyuki_Imabayashi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hiroyuki Imabayashi (page does not exist)">Hiroyuki Imabayashi</a>, and published in 1982 by <a href="/wiki/Thinking_Rabbit" title="Thinking Rabbit">Thinking Rabbit</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Software_house" title="Software house">software house</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Takarazuka,_Hy%C5%8Dgo" title="Takarazuka, Hyōgo">Takarazuka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sokoban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The game is played on a <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board</a> of <a href="/wiki/Square" title="Square">squares</a>, where each square is a floor or a wall. Some floor squares contain boxes, and some floor squares are marked as storage locations.</p>
<p>The player is confined to the board, and may move horizontally or vertically onto empty squares (never through walls or boxes). The player can also move into a box, which pushes it into the square beyond. Boxes may not be pushed into other boxes or walls, and they cannot be pulled. The puzzle is solved when all boxes are at storage locations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sokoban_published_by_Spectrum_HoloByte"><i>Sokoban</i> published by Spectrum HoloByte</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sokoban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sokoban published by Spectrum HoloByte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Soko-Ban</i> (1988) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM-PC" title="IBM-PC">IBM-PC</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">compatibles</a>) with 50 levels.</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1988 <i>Sokoban</i> was published in US by <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_HoloByte" title="Spectrum HoloByte">Spectrum HoloByte</a> as <i>Soko-Ban</i> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM-PC" title="IBM-PC">IBM-PC</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">compatible</a> computers, <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_series" title="Apple II series">Apple II series</a>. A review in <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Gaming_World" title="Computer Gaming World">Computer Gaming World</a></i> praised the game for being "pure and simple, very playable and mentally challenging", citing its addictive qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGW_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CGW-1">[1]</a></sup> It was also reviewed in <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_(magazine)" title="Dragon (magazine)">Dragon</a></i> giving it 4½ out of 5 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragon132_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon132-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations_of_Sokoban">Implementations of Sokoban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sokoban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Implementations of Sokoban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Implementations of Sokoban have been written for numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platform_(computing)" title="Platform (computing)">computer platforms</a>, including almost all <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">home computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a> systems. Versions also exist for <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game consoles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_phones" title="Mobile phones">mobile phones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_calculators" title="Graphic calculators">graphic calculators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">digital cameras</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_research_on_Sokoban">Scientific research on Sokoban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sokoban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific research on Sokoban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sokoban can be studied using the theory of <a href="/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory" title="Computational complexity theory">computational complexity</a>. The problem of solving Sokoban puzzles was proven to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NP-hard" title="NP-hard">NP-hard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Further work showed that it was significantly more difficult than <a href="/wiki/NP_(complexity)" title="NP (complexity)">NP</a> problems; it is <a href="/wiki/PSPACE-complete" title="PSPACE-complete">PSPACE-complete</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This is also interesting for <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> researchers, because solving Sokoban can be compared to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_planning" title="Automated planning">automated planning</a> that needs to be done by a robot that moves boxes in a warehouse.</p>
<p>Sokoban is difficult not only due to its <a href="/wiki/Branching_factor" title="Branching factor">branching factor</a> (which is comparable to <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>), but also its enormous <a href="/wiki/Search_tree" title="Search tree">search tree</a> depth; some levels can be extended indefinitely, with each iteration requiring an <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponentially growing</a> number of moves and pushes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Skilled human players rely mostly on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heuristics" title="Heuristics">heuristics</a>; they are usually able to quickly discard futile or redundant lines of play, and recognize patterns and subgoals, drastically cutting down on the amount of search.</p>
<p>Some Sokoban puzzles can be solved automatically by using a <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent#Single-agent_.26_multiple_agent" title="Intelligent agent">single-agent search</a> algorithm, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDA*" title="IDA*">IDA*</a>, enhanced by several techniques which make use of domain-specific knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This is the method used by <i>Rolling Stone</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> a Sokoban solver developed by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">University of Alberta</a> GAMES Group. The more complex Sokoban levels are, however, out of reach even for the best automated solvers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sokoban_variants">Sokoban variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sokoban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sokoban variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several puzzles can be considered variants of the original Sokoban game, in the sense that they all make use of a controllable character who pushes boxes around a <a href="/wiki/Maze" title="Maze">maze</a>.</p>
<p><b>Alternative tilings</b>: In the standard game, the mazes are laid out on a <a href="/wiki/Square_tiling" title="Square tiling">square grid</a>. Several variants apply the rules of Sokoban to mazes laid out on other tilings. <i>Hexoban</i> uses <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_tiling" title="Hexagonal tiling">regular hexagons</a> and <i>Trioban</i> uses <a href="/wiki/Triangular_tiling" title="Triangular tiling">equilateral triangles</a>.</p>
<p><b>Alternative pushers</b>: In the variants <i>Multiban</i> and <i>Interlock</i> the player can control multiple characters. In some games the pusher is a small bulldozer.</p>
<p><b>Alternative goals</b>: Several variants adjust the requirements for completing a level. For example, in <i>Block-o-Mania</i> the boxes have different colours, and the goal is to push them onto squares with matching colours. <i>Sokomind Plus</i> implements a similar idea, with boxes and target squares uniquely numbered. In <i>Interlock</i> and <i>Sokolor</i>, the boxes also have different colours, but the goal is to move them so that similarly coloured boxes are adjacent. In <i>CyberBox</i>, each level has a designated exit square, and the goal is to reach that exit. In a variant called <i>Beanstalk</i>, the elements of the level must be pushed onto the goal in a fixed sequence.</p>
<p><b>Additional game elements</b>: <i>Push Crate</i>, <i>Sokonex</i>, <i>Xsok</i>, <i>Cyberbox</i> and <i>Block-o-Mania</i> all add new elements to the basic puzzle. Examples include holes, teleports, moving blocks and one-way passages.</p>
<p><b>Character actions</b>: In <i>Pukoban</i>, the character can pull boxes in addition to pushing them.</p>
<p><b>Reverse mode</b>: The player solves the puzzle backwards, from the end to the initial position by pulling instead of pushing boxes. Standard Sokoban puzzles can be played in reverse mode, and the reverse-mode solutions can be converted to solutions for the standard-mode puzzle. Therefore, reverse mode can also be considered a tool for solving standard Sokoban puzzles. Reverse mode is available in <i>Sokoban YASC</i> and <i>Sokofan</i>.</p>
