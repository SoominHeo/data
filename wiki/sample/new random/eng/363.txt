<p>The <b>Second Spanish Republic</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i>Segunda República Española</i>) was the republican regime that existed in Spain from 1931 to 1939, preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(Spain)" title="Restoration (Spain)">Restoration</a> and followed by <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francoist Spain</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Provisional Government of the Second Spanish Republic">Provisional Government</a> between April and December 1931, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1931" title="Spanish Constitution of 1931">1931 Constitution</a> effectively established the Republic. The Spanish Republic is generally divided in three eras, the <a href="/wiki/First_Biennium" title="First Biennium">First Biennium</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Biennium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dark Biennium (page does not exist)">Dark Biennium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_(Spain)#History" title="Popular Front (Spain)">the Popular Front government</a>.</p>
<p>The Second Spanish Republic was proclaimed when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XIII of Spain">Alfonso XIII</a> left the country following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_election" title="Municipal election">municipal elections</a> in which anti-monarchist candidates won the majority of votes, and the Spanish population celebrated the beginning of the Republic and social pressure made the political regime change. Its government went into exile on <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1939-04-01">1 April 1939</span>, when the last of the Republican forces surrendered to the rebel <i>nacionales</i>, led in part by <i>Generalísimo</i> <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>, ending the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Republican_government_in_exile" title="Spanish Republican government in exile">government in exile of the Second Spanish Republic</a> had an embassy in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> until 1976. After the restoration of democracy in Spain, the government was formally dissolved the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1931_Constitution">1931 Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Second_Spanish_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1931 Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1931" title="Spanish Constitution of 1931">Spanish Constitution of 1931</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Background_of_the_Spanish_Civil_War" title="Background of the Spanish Civil War">Background of the Spanish Civil War</a></div>
<p>On <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1930-01-28">28 January 1930</span> the military dictatorship of General <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Primo_de_Rivera" title="Miguel Primo de Rivera">Miguel Primo de Rivera</a> (who had been in power since September 1923) was overthrown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This led various republican factions from a wide variety of backgrounds, including old conservatives and socialists, to join forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pact_of_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="Pact of San Sebastián">Pact of San Sebastián</a> was the key to the transition from monarchy to republic. Republicans of all tendencies were committed to the Pact of San Sebastian in overthrowing the monarchy and establishing a republic. The restoration of the royal Bourbons was rejected by large sectors of the populace who vehemently opposed the King. The pact, signed by representatives of the main Republican forces, allowed a joint anti-monarchy political campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elecciones_municipales_del_12_de_abril_de_1931" title="es:Elecciones municipales del 12 de abril de 1931">12 April 1931 municipal elections</a> led to a landslide victory for republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Two days later, the Second Republic was proclaimed, and King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII" title="Alfonso XIII">Alfonso XIII</a> went into exile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The king's departure led to a provisional government of the young republic under <a href="/wiki/Niceto_Alcal%C3%A1-Zamora" title="Niceto Alcalá-Zamora">Niceto Alcalá-Zamora</a>. Catholic churches and establishments in cities like <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sevilla" title="Sevilla">Sevilla</a> were set ablaze on 11 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queabc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-queabc-8">[8]</a></sup> In June 1931 a <a href="/wiki/Constituent_Cortes" title="Constituent Cortes">Constituent Cortes</a> was elected to draft a new constitution, which came into force in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new constitution established <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_association" title="Freedom of association">freedom of association</a>, extended <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">suffrage</a> to women in 1933, allowed divorce and stripped the Spanish nobility of any special legal status. Initially it also largely disestablished the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, a trend that was somewhat reversed in 1933. Its controversial articles 26 and 27 imposed stringent controls on Church property and barred religious orders from the ranks of educators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Scholars have described the constitution as hostile to religion, with one scholar characterising it as one of the most hostile of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arguing_Comparative_Politics_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arguing_Comparative_Politics-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ortega_y_Gasset" title="José Ortega y Gasset">José Ortega y Gasset</a> stated, "the article in which the Constitution legislates the actions of the Church seems highly improper to me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">Pope Pius XI</a> condemned the Spanish government's deprivation of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> of Catholics in the <a href="/wiki/Encyclical" title="Encyclical">encyclical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dilectissima_Nobis" title="Dilectissima Nobis">Dilectissima Nobis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>The legislative branch was changed to a single chamber called the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Deputies_(Spain)" title="Congress of Deputies (Spain)">Congress of Deputies</a>. The constitution established legal procedures for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalisation" title="Nationalisation">nationalisation</a> of public services and land, banks, and railways. The constitution provided generally accorded civil liberties and representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne_632_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-payne_632-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Catholic churches in major cities were again subject to arson in 1932, and a revolutionary strike action was seen in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a> the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queabc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-queabc-8">[8]</a></sup> A Catholic church in <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a> saw arson in 1933, and the cathedral in <a href="/wiki/Oviedo" title="Oviedo">Oviedo</a> was destroyed by flames in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queabc_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-queabc-8">[8]</a></sup> The church of San Lorenzo in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gijon" title="Gijon">Gijon</a> was set ablaze in this year as well. The church of San Juan in <a href="/wiki/Albacete" title="Albacete">Albacete</a> was torched three months prior to the onset of the civil war, in March 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queabc_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-queabc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1931 Constitution was formally effective from 1931 until 1939. In the summer of 1936, after the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, it became largely irrelevant after the authority of the Republic was superseded in many places by revolutionary socialists and anarchists on one side and fascists on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne_646_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-payne_646-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Republican Constitution also changed the symbols of the country. The <i><a href="/wiki/Himno_de_Riego" title="Himno de Riego">Himno de Riego</a></i> was established as the national anthem, and the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Flag of the Second Spanish Republic">Tricolor</a>, with three horizontal red-yellow-purple fields, became the new flag of Spain. Under the new Constitution, all of Spain's regions had the right to <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> (1932), the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a> (1936) and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> exercised this right, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andaluc%C3%ADa" title="Andalucía">Andalucía</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arag%C3%B3n" title="Aragón">Aragón</a>, engaged in negotiations with the government before the outbreak of the Civil War. The Constitution guaranteed a wide range of civil liberties, but it failed to agree on key points with the convictions of the conservative right, which was very rooted in rural areas, and with the desires of the hierarchy of the Roman Catholic Church, which was stripped of schools and public subsidies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1934-1935_period_and_miners.27_uprising">1934-1935 period and miners' uprising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Second_Spanish_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1934-1935 period and miners' uprising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Asturian_miners%27_strike_of_1934" title="Asturian miners' strike of 1934">Asturian miners' strike of 1934</a></div>
<p>The majority vote in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_1933" title="Spanish general election, 1933">1933 elections</a> was won by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Confederation_of_the_Autonomous_Right" title="Spanish Confederation of the Autonomous Right">Spanish Confederation of the Autonomous Right</a> (CEDA), led by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Gil-Robles_y_Qui%C3%B1ones" title="José María Gil-Robles y Quiñones">José María Gil Robles</a>, a coalition of centre-right and far-right parties. CEDA set up a coalition with the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Republican_Party" title="Radical Republican Party">Radical Republican Party</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Lerroux" title="Alejandro Lerroux">Alejandro Lerroux</a>, which had come second in the elections. The Socialists came third. With Lerroux as head of Government, the new coalition Executive suspended most of the reforms of the previous government.</p>
<p>The inclusion of three CEDA ministers in the government that took office on <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1934-10-01">October 1, 1934</span> led to a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> and a rebellion by socialists and anarchists in <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a> on <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="10-06">October 6</span>. Miners in Asturias occupied the capital, <a href="/wiki/Oviedo" title="Oviedo">Oviedo</a>, killing officials and clergymen and burning theatres and the university. This rebellion lasted for two weeks until it was crushed by the army, led by General <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>, who in the process destroyed large parts of the city. This operation earned Franco the nickname "Butcher of Asturias". Another rebellion by autonomists in Catalonia was also suppressed, and was followed by mass arrests and trials.</p>
<p>The suspension of the land reforms that had been attempted by the previous government, and the failure of the Asturias miners' uprising, led to a more radical turn by the parties of the left, especially in the PSOE (Socialist Party), where the moderate <a href="/wiki/Indalecio_Prieto" title="Indalecio Prieto">Indalecio Prieto</a> lost ground to <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Largo_Caballero" title="Francisco Largo Caballero">Francisco Largo Caballero</a>, who advocated a socialist revolution. At the same time the involvement of the Centrist government party in the <a href="/wiki/Straperlo" title="Straperlo">Straperlo</a> scandal deeply weakened it, further polarising political differences between right and left. These differences became evident in the 1936 elections.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1936_elections">1936 elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Second_Spanish_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1936 elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1936-01-07">7 January 1936</span>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_1936" title="Spanish general election, 1936">new elections</a> were called. Despite significant rivalries and disagreements the socialists, Communists, and the Catalan and Madrid-based left-wing Republicans decided to work together under the name <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_(Spain)" title="Popular Front (Spain)">Popular Front</a>. The Popular Front won the election on <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="02-16">16 February</span> with 263 MPs against 156 right-wing MPs, grouped within a coalition of the <i>National Front</i> with CEDA, <a href="/wiki/Carlism" title="Carlism">Carlists</a>, and Monarchists. The moderate centre parties virtually disappeared; between the elections, Lerroux's group fell from the 104 representatives it had in 1934 to just 9.</p>
<p>In the following months there was increasing violence between left and right. This helped the development of the fascist-inspired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falange" title="Falange">Falange Española</a>, a National party led by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Primo_de_Rivera" title="José Antonio Primo de Rivera">José Antonio Primo de Rivera</a>, the son of the former dictator, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Primo_de_Rivera" title="Miguel Primo de Rivera">Miguel Primo de Rivera</a>. Although it only received 0.7 percent of the votes in the election, by July 1936 the Falange had 40,000 members.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassinations_of_political_leaders_and_beginning_of_the_war">Assassinations of political leaders and beginning of the war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Second_Spanish_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assassinations of political leaders and beginning of the war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1936-07-12">12 July 1936</span>, Lieutenant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Castillo_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="José Castillo (Spanish Civil War)">José Castillo</a>, an important member of the anti-fascist military organisation <a href="/wiki/Uni%C3%B3n_Militar_Republicana_Antifascista" title="Unión Militar Republicana Antifascista">Unión Militar Republicana Antifascista</a> (UMRA), was shot by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falange" title="Falange">Falangist</a> gunmen. In retaliation the following day, <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="07-13">13 July</span>, UMRA members assassinated <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Calvo_Sotelo" title="José Calvo Sotelo">José Calvo Sotelo</a>, leader of the right-wing opposition and the most prominent Spanish monarchist who, describing the government's actions as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevism" title="Bolshevism">Bolshevist</a> and anarchist, had been exhorting the army to intervene, declaring that Spanish soldiers would save the country from communism if "there are no politicians capable of doing so".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Right blamed the government for Calvo Sotelo's assassination. Although this event is sometimes considered the catalyst for the further political polarisation that ensued, the Falange and other right-wing individuals, including <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_la_Cierva" title="Juan de la Cierva">Juan de la Cierva</a>, had already been conspiring to launch a military coup d'état against the government, to be led by senior army officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> When the antifascist Castillo and the anti-socialist Calvo Sotelo were buried on the same day in the same Madrid cemetery, fighting between the <a href="/wiki/Guardia_de_Asalto" title="Guardia de Asalto">Police Assault Guard</a> and fascist militias broke out in the surrounding streets, resulting in four more deaths.</p>
<p>Three days later (<span class="mw-formatted-date" title="07-17">17 July</span>), the coup d'état began more or less as it had been planned, with an army uprising in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">Spanish Morocco</a>, which then spread to several regions of the country. Franco's move was intended to seize power immediately, but his army uprising met with serious resistance, and great swathes of Spain, including most of the main cities, remained loyal to the Republic of Spain. The leaders of the treason (Franco was not commander-in-chief yet) did not lose heart with the stalemate and apparent failure of the coup. Instead, they initiated a slow and determined war of attrition against the Republican government in Madrid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwm.org.uk_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-iwm.org.uk-18">[18]</a></sup> As a result, an estimated total of perhaps half a million people would lose their lives in the war that followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Second_Spanish_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Background_of_the_Spanish_Civil_War" title="Background of the Spanish Civil War">Background of the Spanish Civil War</a></div>
<p>The Second Republic was proclaimed during a period of worldwide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_depression" title="Economic depression">economic depression</a>. In spite of the high hopes, the Republican authorities had to struggle with rising unemployment and poverty. In the ensuing civil unrest, violence in the form of assassination, revolutionary <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strikes</a>, and mob actions increased to dangerous levels in the eyes of the traditional centres of power, such as the landowners, the Church, and the nobility. Thus, it was easy for them to whip up dissatisfaction with the republican government.</p>
<p>Right-wing political discourse became increasingly polarized, often as a form to check the threat of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> that was perceived to be expanding from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Rather than working towards consensus between political forces, politicians on the right and the left leaned towards polarization and called openly for violence.</p>

<p>The murders of the leftist military leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Castillo_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="José Castillo (Spanish Civil War)">José Castillo</a> and the rightist politician <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Calvo_Sotelo" title="José Calvo Sotelo">José Calvo Sotelo</a> opened the way to a rapidly increasing flood of violence between the political left and right.</p>
<p>Rightist elements in Spain justified their military coup against the Republic claiming that it was ungovernable and failed to respond adequately to the threats of communism, anarchism, anti-clericalism, and acts of random violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As well as this growth in extreme-left violence, the perceived permissive attitude of the Republican elite to secessionist politics in the wealthy industrial regions of <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, was felt by Spanish nationalists to pose a threat to the very existence of Spain as a nation-state.</p>

<p>On <span class="mw-formatted-date" title="1936-07-17">July 17, 1936</span>, General Franco led the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Army_of_Africa" title="Spanish Army of Africa">Spanish Army of Africa</a> from Morocco to attack the mainland, while another force from the north under General <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Mola" title="Emilio Mola">Emilio Mola</a> moved south from Navarre. Military units were also mobilised elsewhere to take over government institutions. Before long the professional Army of Africa had much of the south and west under the control of the rebels. Bloody purges followed in each piece of captured "Nationalist" territory in order to consolidate Franco's future regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwm.org.uk_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-iwm.org.uk-18">[18]</a></sup> Although both sides received foreign military aid, the help that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Fascist Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> (as part of <a href="/wiki/German_involvement_in_the_Spanish_Civil_War" title="German involvement in the Spanish Civil War">German involvement in the Spanish Civil War</a>), and neighbouring Portugal gave the rebels was much greater and more effective than the assistance that the Republicans received from the USSR, Mexico, and volunteers of the <a href="/wiki/International_Brigades" title="International Brigades">International Brigades</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a> wholeheartedly assisted General Franco's military campaign, the governments of France, Britain, and other European powers looked the other way and let the Republican forces die, as the actions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-Intervention_Committee" title="Non-Intervention Committee">Non-Intervention Committee</a> would show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Imposed in the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_neutrality_(international_relations)" title="Country neutrality (international relations)">neutrality</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_isolation" title="International isolation">international isolation</a> of the Spanish Republic ended up favouring the interests of the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">Axis Powers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_the_Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Siege of the Alcázar">Siege of the Alcázar</a> at Toledo early in the war was a turning point, with the rebels winning after a long siege. The Republicans managed to hold out in Madrid, despite a National assault in November 1936, and frustrated subsequent offensives against the capital at <a href="/wiki/Jarama" title="Jarama">Jarama</a> and Guadalajara in 1937. Soon, though, the rebels began to erode their territory, starving Madrid and making inroads into the east. The north, including the Basque country, fell in late 1937, and the Aragon front collapsed shortly afterwards. The <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Guernica" title="Bombing of Guernica">bombing of Guernica</a> was probably the most infamous event of the war and inspired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guernica_(painting)" title="Guernica (painting)">Picasso's painting</a>. It was used as a testing ground for the German Luftwaffe's <a href="/wiki/Condor_Legion" title="Condor Legion">Condor Legion</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ebro" title="Battle of the Ebro">Battle of the Ebro</a> in July–November 1938 was the final desperate attempt by the Republicans to turn the tide. When this failed and Barcelona fell to the rebels in early 1939, it was clear the war was over. The remaining Republican fronts collapsed, and Madrid fell in March 1939.</p>
