<p><b>Louis</b> (also <b>Ludwig</b> or <b>Lewis</b>) "<b>the German</b>" (c. 802-876), also known as <b>Louis II</b>, was the first king of <a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">East Francia</a>. Grandson of emperor <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> and the third son of emperor of <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Francia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> and his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Ermengarde_of_Hesbaye" title="Ermengarde of Hesbaye">Ermengarde of Hesbaye</a>, he received the appellation <i>Germanicus</i> shortly after his death in recognition of the fact that the bulk of his kingdom was in the former <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His early years were partly spent at the court of his grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>, whose special affection he is said to have won. When the emperor Louis the Pious divided his dominions between his sons in 817, Louis was made the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Duchy of Bavaria</a>, following the practice of emperor Charlemagne of bestowing a local kingdom to a close family member who then would serve as his lieutenant and local governor. Louis ruled from <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>, the old capital of the <a href="/wiki/Bavarians#History" title="Bavarians">Bavarii</a>. In 825 he became involved in wars with the <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wends</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbs</a> on his eastern frontier. In 827 he married <a href="/wiki/Hemma" title="Hemma">Hemma</a>, sister of his stepmother <a href="/wiki/Judith_of_Bavaria_(died_843)" title="Judith of Bavaria (died 843)">Judith of Bavaria</a>, both daughters of <a href="/wiki/Welf_(father_of_Judith)" title="Welf (father of Judith)">Welf</a>, whose possessions ranged from <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> to Bavaria. Louis soon began to interfere in the quarrels arising from Judith's efforts to secure a kingdom for her son <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a> and the consequent struggles of his brothers with their father.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebellious_son">Rebellious son</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rebellious son">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His involvement in the first civil war against his father's reign was limited, but in the second his elder brothers, <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a>, then <a href="/wiki/King_of_Italy" title="King of Italy">King of Italy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pepin_I_of_Aquitaine" title="Pepin I of Aquitaine">Pepin I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Aquitaine" title="Duke of Aquitaine">Duke of Aquitaine</a>, persuaded him to invade <a href="/wiki/Alamannia" title="Alamannia">Alamannia</a> which their father had given to their young half-brother <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a>; by promising to give him the land in the new partition they would make after a victory. In 832 he led an army of <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a> into <a href="/wiki/Alamannia" title="Alamannia">Alamannia</a> and completely subjugated it. Louis the Pious disinherited him, but to no effect; the emperor was soon captured by his own rebellious sons and deposed. Upon his swift reinstatement, however, the emperor Louis made peace with his son Louis and legally restored Bavaria (never actually lost) to him in 836.</p>
<p>Louis was the instigator of the third civil war, which began in 839. A strip of his land having been given to the young haf-brother Charles, Louis invaded Alamannia again. This time emperor Louis responded quickly, and soon the younger Louis was forced into the far southeastern corner of his realm, the <a href="/wiki/March_of_Pannonia" title="March of Pannonia">March of Pannonia</a>. Peace was then made by force of arms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_war.2C_840-843">Civil war, 840-843</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Civil war, 840-843">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the civil war which followed the death of emperor <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a>, the empire was divided in 843 by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> in three parts, with Louis becoming the King of <a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">East Francia</a>, a region that spanned the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> drainage basin from <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> southeasterly through the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Forest" title="Thuringian Forest">Thuringian Forest</a> into modern Bavaria.</p>
<p>When the emperor Louis died in 840, and <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a> claimed the whole Empire, Louis allied with <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a>, and defeated Lothair I and their nephew <a href="/wiki/Pepin_II_of_Aquitaine" title="Pepin II of Aquitaine">Pepin II of Aquitaine</a>, son of Pepin, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fontenoy_(841)" title="Battle of Fontenoy (841)">Battle of Fontenoy</a> in June 841.</p>
<p>In June 842 the three brothers met on an island in the river <a href="/wiki/Sa%C3%B4ne" title="Saône">Saône</a> to negotiate a peace, and each appointed forty representatives to arrange the boundaries of their respective kingdoms. This developed into the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a>, concluded in August 843, by which Louis received the bulk of the lands lying east of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> (<a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">East Francia</a>), together with a district around <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, on the left bank of the river (see also <a href="/wiki/Oaths_of_Strasbourg" title="Oaths of Strasbourg">Oaths of Strasbourg</a> 842). His territories included Bavaria (where he made <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> the centre of his government), <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>.</p>
<p>Louis may be called the founder of the German kingdom, though his attempts to maintain the unity of the Empire proved futile. Having in 842 crushed the <i><a href="/wiki/Stellinga" title="Stellinga">Stellinga</a></i> rising in Saxony, in 844 he compelled the <a href="/wiki/Obotrites" title="Obotrites">Obotrites</a> to own his authority and put their prince, Gozzmovil, to death. <a href="/wiki/Thachulf,_Duke_of_Thuringia" title="Thachulf, Duke of Thuringia">Thachulf, Duke of Thuringia</a>, then undertook campaigns against the <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Moravia" title="Great Moravia">Moravians</a>, and other tribes, but was not very successful in resisting the ravaging <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts_with_Charles_the_Bald">Conflicts with Charles the Bald</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conflicts with Charles the Bald">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 852 Louis sent his son <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Younger" title="Louis the Younger">Louis the Younger</a> to <a href="/wiki/Aquitaine" title="Aquitaine">Aquitaine</a>, where nobles had grown resentful of Charles the Bald's rule. The younger Louis did not set out until 854, and returned the following year.</p>
<p>Starting from 853 Louis made repeated attempts to gain the throne of <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a>, which, according to the <i>Annals of <a href="/wiki/Fulda" title="Fulda">Fulda</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Fuldenses" title="Annales Fuldenses">Annales Fuldenses</a></i>), the people of that country offered him in their disgust with the cruel misrule of Charles the Bald. Encouraged by his nephews <a href="/wiki/Pepin_of_Herstal" title="Pepin of Herstal">Pepin of Herstal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_of_Provence" title="Charles of Provence">Charles of Provence</a>, Louis invaded in West Francia in 858. Charles the Bald could not even raise an army to resist the invasion and fled to <a href="/wiki/Burgundy" title="Burgundy">Burgundy</a>. Later that year Louis issued a charter dated "the first year of the reign in West Francia." However, treachery and desertion in his army, and the continued loyalty of the Aquitanian bishops to Charles the Bald, brought about the failure of the whole enterprise, which Louis renounced by a treaty signed at <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> on June 7, 860.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the emperor <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a> died in 855, Louis and Charles for a time cooperated in plans to divide Lothair's possessions among themselves, the only impediments to this being Lothair's sons and heirs - <a href="/wiki/Lothair_II" title="Lothair II">Lothair II</a> (who received <a href="/wiki/Lotharingia" title="Lotharingia">Lotharingia</a>), <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_of_Italy" title="Louis II of Italy">Louis II of Italy</a> (who held the imperial title and the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_of_Lombardy" title="Iron Crown of Lombardy">Iron Crown of Lombardy</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Charles_of_Provence" title="Charles of Provence">Charles of Provence</a>. In 868 at <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> Louis and Charles agreed to partition <a href="/wiki/Lotharingia" title="Lotharingia">Lotharingia</a>. When Lothair II died in 869, Louis was lying seriously ill, and his armies were engaged in a war with the Moravians. Charles the Bald quickly seized Lothair's lands; but Louis, having recovered, compelled him by a threat of war to agree to the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Meerssen" title="Treaty of Meerssen">Treaty of Meerssen</a>, which divided Lothair's lands between all the claimants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisio_regni_among_the_sons"><i>Divisio regni</i> among the sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Divisio regni among the sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The later years of Louis the German were troubled by rebellions of his sons. The eldest, <a href="/wiki/Carloman_of_Bavaria" title="Carloman of Bavaria">Carloman of Bavaria</a>, revolted in 861 and again two years later. This was followed by the second son <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Younger" title="Louis the Younger">Louis the Younger</a>, who was joined by his brother <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Fat" title="Charles the Fat">Charles the Fat</a>. In 864 Louis was forced to grant Carloman the kingdom of Bavaria, which he himself had once held under his father. In 865 he divided the remainder of his lands - <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> with <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> went to Louis the Younger and <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabia</a> with <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Raetia</a> to Charles the Fat.</p>
<p>A report that the emperor <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_of_Italy" title="Louis II of Italy">Louis II of Italy</a> had died led to a peace between father and sons and attempts by Louis the German to gain the imperial crown for his oldest son Carloman. These efforts were thwarted by Louis II of Italy who was in fact not dead, and Louis' old adversary, Charles the Bald.</p>
<p>Louis was preparing for a new war when he died on August 28, 876 in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>. He was buried at the <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lorsch" title="Lorsch">Lorsch</a>, leaving three sons and three daughters. His sons, unusually for their earlier behaviour, respected the divisions made a decade earlier and each contented himself with his own kingdom.</p>
<p>Louis is considered by many to be the most competent of the grandsons of Charlemagne. He obtained for his kingdom a certain degree of security in face of the attacks of <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarians</a>, Slavs, and others. He lived in close alliance with the Church, to which he was very generous, and entered eagerly into schemes for the conversion of his <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">heathen</a> neighbours.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_the_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Louis was married to <a href="/wiki/Hemma" title="Hemma">Hemma</a> (died 31 January 876).,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and they had seven children:</p>
<ul>
<li>Hildegard, (828–856)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carloman_of_Bavaria" title="Carloman of Bavaria">Carloman of Bavaria</a>, (829–880),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> King of Bavaria</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Irmgard_of_Chiemsee" title="Irmgard of Chiemsee">Irmgard of Chiemsee</a> also known as Ermengard, (died 866) (Louis, having established two of his other daughters as abbesses of convents, appointed Irmgard (also known as Ermengard) to govern first the <a href="/wiki/Buchau_Abbey" title="Buchau Abbey">monastery of Buchau</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Frauenchiemsee" title="Frauenchiemsee">royal abbey of Chiemsee</a> in Bavaria. She is commemorated as a saint on 16 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>Gisela, married to Berthold I, Count Palatine of Swabia, and mother of <a href="/wiki/Cunigunde_of_Swabia" title="Cunigunde of Swabia">Cunigunde of Swabia</a>, wife of <a href="/wiki/Conrad_I_of_Germany" title="Conrad I of Germany">Conrad I of Germany</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Younger" title="Louis the Younger">Louis the Younger</a>, (835–882)</li>
<li>Bertha, (died 877)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Fat" title="Charles the Fat">Charles the Fat</a>, (839–888)</li>
</ul>
