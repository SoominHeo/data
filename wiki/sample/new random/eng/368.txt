<p><b>Flanders</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl"><i>Vlaanderen</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[ˈvlaːndərə(n)]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Nl-Vlaanderen.ogg" title="File:Nl-Vlaanderen.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/Nl-Vlaanderen.ogg" title="Nl-Vlaanderen.ogg">listen</a>)</small>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>Flandre</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[flɑ̃dʁ]</a></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>-speaking northern portion of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, although there are several overlapping definitions, including ones related to culture, language, politics and history. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Communities,_regions_and_language_areas_of_Belgium" title="Communities, regions and language areas of Belgium">communities, regions and language areas of Belgium</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> associated with Flanders is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemings" title="Flemings">Fleming</a>, while the corresponding adjective is <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a>. The official capital of Flanders is <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, although Brussels itself has an independent regional government, and the government of Flanders only oversees some cultural aspects of Brussels life.</p>
<p>In historical contexts, Flanders originally refers to the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a> (Flandria), which around AD 1000 stretched from the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Dover" title="Strait of Dover">Strait of Dover</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a>. The core of historical Flanders is situated within modern-day Flanders and corresponds to the provinces <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Flanders" title="East Flanders">East Flanders</a>, but it sometimes stretched into what is now <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. Nevertheless, during the 19th and 20th centuries it became increasingly commonplace in English and some other languages to use the term "Flanders" to refer to the entire Dutch-speaking part of Belgium, stretching all the way to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Maas" title="River Maas">River Maas</a>, as well as cultural movements such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_art" title="Flemish art">Flemish art</a>. In accordance with late 20th century <a href="/wiki/State_reform_in_Belgium" title="State reform in Belgium">Belgian state reforms</a> the area was made into two political entities: the "<a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl"><i>Vlaamse Gemeenschap</i></span>) and the "<a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl"><i>Vlaams Gewest</i></span>). These entities were merged, although geographically the Flemish Community, which has a broader cultural mandate, covers Brussels, whereas the Flemish Region does not.</p>
<p>Flanders has figured prominently in European history. During the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, cities such as <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and Brussels made it one of the richest and most urbanized parts of Europe, weaving the <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> of neighbouring lands into cloth for both domestic use and export. As a consequence, a very sophisticated culture developed, with impressive achievements in the arts and architecture, rivaling those of northern Italy. Belgium was one of the centres of the 19th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> but Flanders was at first overtaken by French-speaking <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>. In the second half of the 20th century, however, Flanders' economy modernised rapidly, and today Flanders is significantly more wealthy than its southern counterpart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geographically, Flanders is generally flat, and has a small section of coast on the North Sea. Much of Flanders is agriculturally fertile and densely populated, with a population density of almost 500 people per square kilometer (1,200 per square mile). It touches France to the west near the coast, and borders the Netherlands to the north and east, and Wallonia to the south. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_Capital_Region" title="Brussels Capital Region">Brussels Capital Region</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclave" title="Enclave">enclave</a> within the Flemish Region. Flanders has exclaves of its own: <a href="/wiki/Voeren" title="Voeren">Voeren</a> in the east is between Wallonia and the Netherlands and <a href="/wiki/Baarle-Hertog" title="Baarle-Hertog">Baarle-Hertog</a> in the north consists of 22 exclaves surrounded by the Netherlands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Belgium">In Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "Flanders" has several main meanings:</p>
<ul>
<li>The "Flemish community" or "Flemish nation", i.e. the social, cultural and linguistic, scientific and educational, economical and political community of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemings" title="Flemings">Flemings</a>. It comprises 6.5 million Belgians (60%) who consider Dutch to be their mother tongue.</li>
<li>The political subdivisions of Belgium: the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a> (competent in mainly economic matters) and the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a> (competent in mainly cultural matters). The first does not comprise Brussels (which forms a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels-Capital_Region" title="Brussels-Capital Region">Region on itself</a>), whereas the latter does comprise the Dutch-speaking inhabitants of Brussels.</li>
<li>The political institutions that govern both subdivisions: the operative body "<a href="/wiki/Flemish_Government" title="Flemish Government">Flemish Government</a>" and the legislative organ "<a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">Flemish Parliament</a>".</li>
<li>The two westernmost provinces of the Flemish Region, <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Flanders" title="East Flanders">East Flanders</a>, forming the central portion of the historic <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Belgium_and_neighbouring_countries">In Belgium and neighbouring countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Belgium and neighbouring countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> territory that existed from the 8th century (<i>Flandria</i>) until its absorption by the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">French First Republic</a>. Until the 1600s, this county also extended over parts of France and the Netherlands.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a></div>
</li>
<li>One of the regions conquered by the French in Flanders, namely <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nord_(French_department)" title="Nord (French department)">Nord department</a>. French Flanders can be divided into two smaller regions: <a href="/wiki/Walloon_Flanders" title="Walloon Flanders">Walloon Flanders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Flanders" title="Maritime Flanders">Maritime Flanders</a>. The first region was predominantly French-speaking already in the 1600s, the latter became so in the 20th century. The <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">city of Lille</a> identifies itself as "Flemish", and this is reflected, for instance, in the name of its local railway station <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Lille_Flandres" title="Gare de Lille Flandres">Lille Flandres</a></i>.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a></div>
</li>
<li>The region conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> in Flanders, now part of the Dutch province of <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a>.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeelandic Flanders</a></div>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch-speaking_part_of_Belgium">Dutch-speaking part of Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dutch-speaking part of Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The significance of the County of Flanders and its <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Flanders" title="Count of Flanders">counts</a> eroded through time, but the designation remained in a very broad sense. In the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">Early modern period</a>, the term Flanders was associated with the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Netherlands" title="Southern Netherlands">Southern Netherlands</a>. During the 19th and 20th centuries, it became increasingly commonplace to refer to the Dutch-speaking part of Belgium as "Flanders". The linguistic limit between French and Dutch was recorded in the early '60's, from <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a> to <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">Maastricht</a>. Now, Flanders extends over the northern part of Belgium, including <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Belgium)" title="Limburg (Belgium)">Belgian Limburg</a> (corresponding closely to the medieval <a href="/wiki/County_of_Loon" title="County of Loon">County of Loon</a>), and the Belgian parts of the medieval <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brabant" title="Duchy of Brabant">Duchy of Brabant</a>.</p>
<p>The ambiguity between this wider area and that of the County (or the Belgian parts thereof), still remains. In most present-day contexts however, in general the term Flanders is taken to refer to either the political, social, cultural, and linguistic community (and the corresponding official institution, the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a>), or the geographical area, one of the three institutional regions in Belgium, namely the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/History_of_art" title="History of art">history of art</a> and other fields, the adjectives Flemish and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Netherlandish" title="Netherlandish">Netherlandish</a> are commonly used to designate all the artistic production in this area before about 1580, after which it refers specifically to the southern Netherlands. For example, the term "Flemish Primitives", now outdated in English but used in French, Flemish and other languages, is a synonym for "<a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Early Netherlandish painting</a>", and it is not uncommon to see <a href="/wiki/Mosan_art" title="Mosan art">Mosan art</a> categorized as Flemish art. In music the <i><a href="/wiki/Franco-Flemish_School" title="Franco-Flemish School">Franco-Flemish School</a></i> is also known as the <i>Dutch School</i>.</p>
<p>Within this Dutch-speaking part of Belgium, French has never ceased to be spoken by some citizens and Jewish groups have been speaking <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a> in Antwerp for centuries. Today, Flanders' minority residents include 170 nationalities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education-2">[2]</a></sup> — the largest groups speaking <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, English, <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, Spanish, Italian and Polish.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Flanders" title="History of Flanders">History of Flanders</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Belgae" title="Belgae">Belgae</a></div>
<p>The area, roughly encompassing the later geographical meanings of Flanders, was considered to be in the northern and less economically developed part of <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Belgica" title="Gallia Belgica">Gallia Belgica</a>. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Roman empire</a> this became an administrative province, but much of modern Belgium eventually became part of <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Germania Inferior</a>. These were the most northerly continental provinces of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Roman empire</a>. Linguistically, the tribes in this area were under <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> influence in the south, and <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> influence in the east, but there is disagreement about what language was spoken locally, which may even have been an intermediate "<a href="/wiki/Nordwestblock" title="Nordwestblock">Nordwestblock</a>" language related to both. By the first century BC <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> had become prevalent. In the future county of Flanders, the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgic" title="Belgic">Belgic</a> tribe in Roman times was the <a href="/wiki/Menapii" title="Menapii">Menapii</a>, but also on the coast were the <a href="/wiki/Marsacii" title="Marsacii">Marsacii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morini" title="Morini">Morini</a>. In the central part of modern Belgium were the <a href="/wiki/Nervii" title="Nervii">Nervii</a> and in the east were the <a href="/wiki/Tungri" title="Tungri">Tungri</a>. The Tungri especially were thought to have links to Germanic tribes east of the Rhine. Another notable group were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxandrians" title="Toxandrians">Toxandrians</a> who appear to have lived in the <a href="/wiki/Campine" title="Campine">Kempen</a> region, in the northern parts of both the Nervian and Tungrian provinces. The Roman provinces of the Menapii, Nervii and Tungri therefore correspond roughly with the medieval counties of Flanders, Brabant and <a href="/wiki/County_of_Loon" title="County of Loon">Loon</a>, and the modern Flemish provinces of East and West Flanders (Menapii), Brabant and Antwerp (Nervii), and Belgian Limburg (Tungri).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Flanders">Historical Flanders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical Flanders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a></div>
<p>Created in the year 862 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal" title="Feudal">feudal</a> <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a>, the County of Flanders was divided when its western districts fell under French rule in the late 12th century. The remaining parts of Flanders came under the rule of the counts of neighbouring <a href="/wiki/County_of_Hainaut" title="County of Hainaut">Hainaut</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin_V_of_Hainaut" title="Baldwin V of Hainaut">1191</a>. The entire area passed in <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Bold" title="Philip the Bold">1384</a> to the dukes of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Burgundy</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Burgundy" title="Mary of Burgundy">1477</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> dynasty, and in <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">1556</a> to the kings of Spain. The western districts of Flanders came finally under French rule under successive treaties of <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_the_Pyrenees" title="Treaty of the Pyrenees">1659</a> (Artois), <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1668)" title="Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1668)">1668</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Treaties_of_Nijmegen" title="Treaties of Nijmegen">1678</a>.</p>
<p>During the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> Flanders' trading towns (notably <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ypres" title="Ypres">Ypres</a>) made it one of the richest and most urbanized parts of Europe, weaving the <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> of neighbouring lands into cloth for both domestic use and export. As a consequence, a very sophisticated culture developed, with impressive achievements in the arts and architecture, rivaling those of northern Italy. Ghent, Bruges, Ypres and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franc_of_Bruges" title="Franc of Bruges">Franc of Bruges</a> formed the <a href="/wiki/Four_Members" title="Four Members">Four Members</a>, a form of parliament that exercised considerable power in Flanders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Increasingly powerful from the 12th century, the territory's autonomous urban <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">communes</a> were instrumental in defeating a French attempt at annexation (1300–1302), finally defeating the French in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Golden_Spurs" title="Battle of the Golden Spurs">Battle of the Golden Spurs</a> (11 July 1302), near <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a>. Two years later, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-Flemish_War_(1297-1305)" title="Franco-Flemish War (1297-1305)">uprising was defeated</a> and Flanders remained part of the French Crown. Flemish prosperity waned in the following century, however, owing to widespread European population decline following the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> of 1348, the disruption of trade during the Anglo-French <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a> (1337–1453), and increased English cloth production. Flemish weavers had gone over to <a href="/wiki/Worstead" title="Worstead">Worstead</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Walsham" title="North Walsham">North Walsham</a> in Norfolk in the 12th century and established the woolen industry.</p>
<p>The County of Flanders started to take control of the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/County_of_Brabant" title="County of Brabant">County of Brabant</a> during the life of <a href="/wiki/Louis_II,_Count_of_Flanders" title="Louis II, Count of Flanders">Louis II, Count of Flanders</a> (1330-1384), who fought his sister-in-law <a href="/wiki/Joanna,_Duchess_of_Brabant" title="Joanna, Duchess of Brabant">Joanna, Duchess of Brabant</a> for control of it. The titles were eventually more clearly united under <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good" title="Philip the Good">Philip the Good</a> (1396 – 1467), Duke of Burgundy. The County of Loon, approximately the modern Flemish province of Limburg, remained independent under the lordship of the Archbishop of Liège until the French Revolution, but surrounded by the Burgundians.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_Countries">Low Countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Low Countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a></div>
<dl>
<dt>Beeldenstorm</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 1500, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> was born in <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>. He inherited the <a href="/wiki/Seventeen_Provinces" title="Seventeen Provinces">Seventeen Provinces</a> (1506), Spain (1516) with its colonies and in 1519 was elected <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pragmatic_Sanction_of_1549" title="Pragmatic Sanction of 1549">Pragmatic Sanction of 1549</a>, issued by Charles V, established the Low Countries as the Seventeen Provinces (or <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a> in its broad sense) as an entity separate from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and from France. In 1556 Charles V abdicated due to ill health (he suffered from crippling <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Spain and the Seventeen Provinces went to his son, king <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a>.</p>
<p>Over the first half of the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> grew to become the second-largest European city north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> by 1560. Antwerp was the richest city in Europe at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to Luc-Normand Tellier "It is estimated that the port of Antwerp was earning the Spanish crown seven times more revenues than the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Americas</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1444" data-file-width="1905" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg/220px-Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg/330px-Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg/440px-Incendio_Ayuntamiento_Amberes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Antwerp" title="Sack of Antwerp">Sack of Antwerp</a> in 1576, in which about 7,000 people died.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Meanwhile, Protestantism had reached the Low Countries. Among the wealthy traders of Antwerp, the <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> beliefs of the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanseatic_(class)" title="Hanseatic (class)">Hanseatic</a> traders found appeal, perhaps partly for economic reasons. The spread of Protestantism in this city was aided by the presence of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustinian" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> cloister (founded 1514) in the St. Andries quarter. Luther, an Augustinian himself, had taught some of the monks, and his works were in print by 1518. The first Lutheran martyrs came from Antwerp. The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> resulted in consecutive but overlapping waves of reform: a Lutheran, followed by a militant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptist" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptist</a>, then a <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">Mennonite</a>, and finally a <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinistic</a> movement. These movements existed independently of each other.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a>, a devout Catholic and self-proclaimed protector of the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suppression_of_dissent" title="Suppression of dissent">suppressed</a> Calvinism in Flanders, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brabant" title="Duchy of Brabant">Brabant</a> and Holland (what is now approximately <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Belgium)" title="Limburg (Belgium)">Belgian Limburg</a> was part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Bishopric of Liège">Bishopric of Liège</a> and was Catholic <i>de facto</i>). In 1566, the wave of <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">iconoclasm</a> known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Beeldenstorm" title="Beeldenstorm">Beeldenstorm</a></i> was a prelude to religious war between Catholics and Protestants, especially the Anabaptists. The <i>Beeldenstorm</i> started in what is now <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a>, with open-air sermons (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl"><i>hagepreken</i></span>) that spread through the Low Countries, first to Antwerp and Ghent, and from there further east and north. In total it lasted not even a month.</p>
<dl>
<dt>The Eighty Years' War and its consequences</dt>
</dl>
<p>Subsequently, Philip II sent <a href="/wiki/Fernando_%C3%81lvarez_de_Toledo,_3rd_Duke_of_Alba" title="Fernando Álvarez de Toledo, 3rd Duke of Alba">the Duke</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Alba" title="Duchy of Alba">Alba</a> to the Provinces to repress the revolt. Alba recaptured the southern part of the Provinces, who signed the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Arras" title="Union of Arras">Union of Atrecht</a>, which meant that they would accept the Spanish government on condition of more freedom. But the northern part of the provinces signed the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Utrecht" title="Union of Utrecht">Union of Utrecht</a> and settled in 1581 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Seven_United_Netherlands" title="Republic of the Seven United Netherlands">Republic of the Seven United Netherlands</a>. Spanish troops quickly started fighting the rebels, but before the revolt could be completely defeated, a war between England and Spain had broken out, forcing Philip's Spanish troops to halt their advance. Meanwhile, the Spanish armies had already conquered the important trading cities of Bruges and Ghent. Antwerp, which was then the most important port in the world, also had to be conquered. On 17 August 1585, Antwerp fell. This ended the Eighty Years' War for the (from now on) <a href="/wiki/Southern_Netherlands" title="Southern Netherlands">Southern Netherlands</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">United Provinces</a> (the Northern Netherlands) fought on until 1648 – the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>.</p>

<p>While Spain was at war with England, the rebels from the north, strengthened by refugees from the south, started a campaign to reclaim areas lost to <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a>'s Spanish troops. They managed to conquer a considerable part of Brabant (the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noord-Brabant" title="Noord-Brabant">Noord-Brabant</a> of the Netherlands), and the south bank of the Scheldt estuary (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeeuws-Vlaanderen" title="Zeeuws-Vlaanderen">Zeeuws-Vlaanderen</a>), before being stopped by Spanish troops. The front line at the end of this war stabilized and became the current border between present-day Belgium and the Netherlands. The Dutch (as they later became known) had managed to reclaim enough of Spanish-controlled Flanders to close off the river <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a>, effectively cutting Antwerp off from its trade routes.</p>
<p>First the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Antwerp" title="Fall of Antwerp">fall of Antwerp</a> to the Spanish and later also the closing of the <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> were causes of a considerable emigration of Antverpians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Many of the Calvinist merchants of Antwerp and also of other Flemish cities left Flanders and emigrated to the north. A large number of them settled in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, which was at the time a smaller port, of significance only in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baltic trade (page does not exist)">Baltic trade</a>. In the following years Amsterdam was rapidly transformed into one of the world's most important ports. Because of the contribution of the Flemish exiles to this transformation, the exodus is sometimes described as "<i>creating a new Antwerp</i>".</p>
<p>Flanders and Brabant, due to these events, went into a period of relative decline from the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" title="Thirty Years War">Thirty Years War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FiifAntwHist_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FiifAntwHist-9">[9]</a></sup> In the Northern Netherlands however, the mass emigration from Flanders and Brabant became an important driving force behind the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age">Dutch Golden Age</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Southern Netherlands (1581–1795)</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quad_Flandria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2498" data-file-width="3345" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/220px-Quad_Flandria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/330px-Quad_Flandria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/440px-Quad_Flandria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quad_Flandria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1609 map of the county of Flanders</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although arts remained at a relatively impressive level for another century with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a> (1577–1640) and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_van_Dyck" title="Anthony van Dyck">Anthony van Dyck</a>, Flanders experienced a loss of its former economic and intellectual power under Spanish, Austrian, and French rule, with heavy taxation and rigid imperial political control compounding the effects of industrial stagnation and Spanish-Dutch and Franco-Austrian conflict. The Southern Netherlands suffered severely under the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>, but under the reign of empress Maria-Theresia these lands economically flourished again. Influenced by the Enlightenment, the Austrian emperor Joseph II was the first sovereign who has been in the Southern Netherlands since king Philip II of Spain left them in 1559.</p>
<dl>
<dt>French Revolution and Napoleonic France (1795–1815)</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 1794 the <a href="/wiki/History_of_France#Revolution" title="History of France">French Republican Army</a> started using Antwerp as the northernmost naval port of France,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FiifAntwHist_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FiifAntwHist-9">[9]</a></sup> which country officially annexed Flanders the following year as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_130_d%C3%A9partements" title="The 130 départements">départements</a></i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lys_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Lys (département)">Lys</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escaut_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Escaut (département)">Escaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deux-N%C3%A8thes" title="Deux-Nèthes">Deux-Nèthes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meuse-Inf%C3%A9rieure" title="Meuse-Inférieure">Meuse-Inférieure</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyle_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Dyle (département)">Dyle</a>. Obligatory (French) army service for all men aged 16–25 was one of the main reasons for the people's uprising against the French in 1798, known as the <i>Boerenkrijg</i> (<i>Peasants' War</i>), with the heaviest fighting in the <a href="/wiki/Campine" title="Campine">Campine</a> area.</p>
<dl>
<dt>United Kingdom of the Netherlands (1815–1830)</dt>
</dl>
<p>After the defeat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> at the 1815 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Belgium" title="Waterloo, Belgium">Waterloo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Brabant" title="Province of Brabant">Brabant</a>, sovereignty over the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Netherlands" title="Austrian Netherlands">Austrian Netherlands</a> – Belgium minus the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Cantons" title="East Cantons">East Cantons</a> and Luxembourg – was given by the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> (1815) to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Netherlands" title="United Netherlands">United Netherlands</a> (Dutch: <i>Verenigde Nederlanden</i>), the state that briefly existed under Sovereign Prince William I of Orange Nassau, the latter King <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_the_Netherlands" title="William I of the Netherlands">William I of the United Kingdom of the Netherlands</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French Empire</a> was driven out of the Dutch territories. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="United Kingdom of the Netherlands">United Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> was born. The Protestant King of the Netherlands, William I rapidly started the industrialisation of the southern parts of the Kingdom. The political system that was set up however, slowly but surely failed to forge a true union between the northern and the southern parts of the Kingdom. The southern <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> mainly was Roman Catholic, in contrast to the mainly Protestant north; large parts of the southern bourgeoisie also primarily spoke French rather than Dutch.</p>
<p>In 1815 the Dutch Senate was reinstated (Dutch: <i>Eerste Kamer der Staaten Generaal</i>). The nobility, mainly coming from the south, became more and more estranged from their northern colleagues. Resentment grew both between the Roman Catholics from the south and the Protestants from the north and among the powerful liberal bourgeoisie from the south and their more moderate colleagues from the north. On 25 August 1830 (after the showing of the opera '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Muette_de_Portici" title="La Muette de Portici">La Muette de Portici</a>' of <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Auber" title="Daniel Auber">Daniel Auber</a> in Brussels) the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Revolution" title="Belgian Revolution">Belgian Revolution</a> sparked off and became a fact. On 4 October 1830, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_Belgium" title="Provisional Government of Belgium">Provisional Government</a> (Dutch: <i>Voorlopig Bewind</i>) proclaimed the independence, which was later confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_Belgium" title="National Congress of Belgium">National Congress</a> that issued a new Liberal Constitution and declared the new state a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Monarchy" title="Constitutional Monarchy">Constitutional Monarchy</a>, under the House of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg" title="Saxe-Coburg">Saxe-Coburg</a>. Flanders now became part of the Kingdom of Belgium, which was recognized by the major European Powers on 20 January 1831. The de facto dissidence was finally recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="United Kingdom of the Netherlands">United Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> on 19 April 1839.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdom_of_Belgium">Kingdom of Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kingdom of Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Belgium" title="History of Belgium">History of Belgium</a></div>
<p>In 1830, the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Revolution" title="Belgian Revolution">Belgian Revolution</a> led to the splitting up of the two countries. Belgium was confirmed as an independent state by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London,_1839" title="Treaty of London, 1839">Treaty of London</a> of 1839, but deprived of the eastern half of Limburg (now Dutch Limburg), and the Eastern half of Luxembourg (now the Grand-Duchy of Luxembourg). Sovereignty over <a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeeuws Vlaanderen</a>, south of the Westerscheldt river delta, was left with the Kingdom of the Netherlands, which was allowed to levy a toll on all traffic to Antwerp harbour until 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FiifAntwHist_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-FiifAntwHist-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Rise of the Flemish Movement</dt>
</dl>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Movement" title="Flemish Movement">Flemish Movement</a></div>
<p>The Belgian Revolution was not well supported in Flanders and even on 4 October 1830, when the Belgian independence was eventually declared, Flemish authorities refused to take orders from the new Belgian government in Brussels. Only after Flanders was subdued with the aid of a large French military force one month later, under the leadership of the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Gustave_le_Doulcet,_comte_de_Pont%C3%A9coulant" title="Louis Gustave le Doulcet, comte de Pontécoulant">Count de Pontécoulant</a>, did Flanders become a true part of Belgium.</p>
<p>The French-speaking bourgeoisie showed very little respect for the Dutch-speaking part of the population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> French became the only official language in Belgium and all secondary and higher education in the Dutch language was abolished.</p>
<p>In 1834, all people even remotely suspected of being "Flemish minded" or calling for the reunification of the Netherlands were prosecuted and their houses looted and burnt. Flanders, until then a very prosperous European region,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was not considered worthwhile for investment and scholarship. A study in 1918 demonstrated that in the first 88 years of its existence, 80% of the Belgian GNP was invested in Wallonia. This led to a widespread poverty in Flanders, forcing roughly 300.000 <a href="/wiki/Flemish_immigration_to_Wallonia" title="Flemish immigration to Wallonia">Flemish to emigrate to Wallonia</a> to start working there in the heavy industry.</p>
<p>All of these events led to a silent uprising in Flanders against the French-speaking domination. But it was not until 1878 that Dutch was allowed to be used for official purposes in Flanders (see <a href="/wiki/Language_legislation_in_Belgium" title="Language legislation in Belgium">language legislation in Belgium</a>), although French remained the only official language in Belgium.</p>
<p>In 1873, Dutch became the official language in public secondary schools. In 1898 Dutch and French were declared equal languages in laws and Royal orders. In 1930 the first Flemish university was opened.</p>
<p>The first official translation of the Belgian constitution in Dutch was not published until 1967.</p>

<dl>
<dt>World War I and its consequences</dt>
</dl>
<p>Flanders (and Belgium as a whole) saw some of the greatest loss of life on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, in particular from the three battles of <a href="/wiki/Ypres" title="Ypres">Ypres</a>.</p>
<p>Flemish feeling of identity and consciousness grew through the events and experiences of war. The occupying German authorities took several Flemish-friendly measures. More importantly, the experiences of many Dutch-speaking soldiers on the front led by French-speaking officers catalysed Flemish emancipation. The French-speaking officers often gave orders in French only, followed by "et pour les Flamands, la même chose!", meaning "and for the Flemish, the same thing!" (which did not help the Flemish conscripts, who were mostly uneducated farmers and workers unable to have understood what had been said in French).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mm-chs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mm-chs-10">[10]</a></sup> The resulting suffering is still remembered by Flemish organizations during the yearly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ijzerbedevaart" title="Ijzerbedevaart">Yser pilgrimage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Diksmuide" title="Diksmuide">Diksmuide</a> at the monument of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IJzertoren" title="IJzertoren">Yser Tower</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Right-Wing Nationalism in the interbellum and World War II</dt>
</dl>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_National_Union" title="Flemish National Union">Flemish National Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verdinaso" title="Verdinaso">Verdinaso</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietsland" title="Dietsland">Dietsland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyriel_Verschaeve" title="Cyriel Verschaeve">Cyriel Verschaeve</a></div>
<p>During the interbellum and World War II, several right-wing fascist and/or national-socialistic parties emerged in Belgium, the Flemish ones being energized by the anti-Flemish discrimination of the Wallonians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Since these parties were promised more rights for the Flemings by the German government during World War II, many of them collaborated with the Nazi regime. After the war, collaborators (or people who were <i>Zwart</i>, "Black" during the war) were prosecuted and punished, among them many Flemish Nationalists whose main political goal had been the emancipation of Flanders. As a result, up until this day Flemish Nationalism is often associated with right-wing and sometimes fascist ideologies.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Flemish autonomy</dt>
</dl>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/State_reform_in_Belgium" title="State reform in Belgium">State reform in Belgium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932011_Belgian_political_crisis" title="2007–2011 Belgian political crisis">2007–2011 Belgian political crisis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Belgium" title="Partition of Belgium">Partition of Belgium</a></div>
<p>After World War II, the differences between Dutch-speaking and French-speaking Belgians became clear in a number of conflicts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Question" title="Royal Question">Royal Question</a>, the question whether King Leopold III should return (which most Flemings supported but not the Walloons) and the use of Dutch in the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_University_of_Leuven_(1834%E2%80%931968)" title="Catholic University of Leuven (1834–1968)">Catholic University of Leuven</a>. As a result, several <a href="/wiki/State_reform_in_Belgium" title="State reform in Belgium">state reforms</a> took place in the second half of the 20th century, which transformed the unitary Belgium into a federal state with <a href="/wiki/Communities,_regions_and_language_areas_of_Belgium" title="Communities, regions and language areas of Belgium">communities, regions and language areas</a>. This resulted also in the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">Flemish Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Government" title="Flemish Government">Government</a>. During the 1970s, all major political parties split into a Flemish and French-speaking party.</p>
<p>Several Flemish parties still advocate for more Flemish autonomy, some even for Flemish independence (see <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Belgium" title="Partition of Belgium">Partition of Belgium</a>), whereas the French-speakers would like to keep the current state as it is. Recent governments (such as <a href="/wiki/Verhofstadt_I_Government" title="Verhofstadt I Government">Verhofstadt I Government</a>) have transferred certain federal competences to the regional governments.</p>
<p>On 13 December 2006, a <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Secession_hoax" title="Flemish Secession hoax">spoof news broadcast</a> by the Belgian Francophone public broadcasting station <a href="/wiki/RTBF" title="RTBF">RTBF</a> declared that Flanders had decided to declare independence from Belgium.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_election,_2007" title="Belgian federal election, 2007">2007 federal elections</a> showed more support for Flemish autonomy, marking the start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932011_Belgian_political_crisis" title="2007–2011 Belgian political crisis">2007–2011 Belgian political crisis</a>. All the political parties that advocated a significant increase of Flemish autonomy gained votes as well as seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_parliament" title="Belgian federal parliament">Belgian federal parliament</a>. This was especially the case for <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">Christian Democratic and Flemish</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Flemish_Alliance" title="New Flemish Alliance">New Flemish Alliance</a> (N-VA) (who had participated on a shared <a href="/wiki/Electoral_list" title="Electoral list">electoral list</a>). The trend continued during the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_regional_elections,_2009" title="Belgian regional elections, 2009">2009 regional elections</a>, where CD&amp;V and N-VA were the clear winners in Flanders, and N-VA became even the largest party in Flanders and Belgium during the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_election,_2010" title="Belgian federal election, 2010">2010 federal elections</a>, followed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%932011_Belgian_government_formation" title="2010–2011 Belgian government formation">longest-ever government formation</a> after which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Di_Rupo_I_Government" title="Di Rupo I Government">Di Rupo I Government</a> was formed excluding N-VA. Eight parties agreed on a <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Belgian_state_reform" title="Sixth Belgian state reform">sixth state reform</a> which aim to solve the disputes between Flemings and French-speakers. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_provincial_and_municipal_elections,_2012" title="Belgian provincial and municipal elections, 2012">2012 provincial and municipal elections</a> however continued the trend of N-VA becoming the biggest party in Flanders.</p>
<p>However, sociological studies show no parallel between the rise of nationalist parties and popular support for their agenda. Instead, a recent study revealed a majority in favour of returning regional competences to the federal level <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>These victories for the advocates of much more Flemish autonomy are very much in parallel with opinion polls that show a structural increase in . Since 2006, certain polls have started showing Flemish independence. Those polls are not yet representative, but they point to a significant long-term trend.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Both the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a> are constitutional institutions of the Kingdom of Belgium, exercising certain powers within their jurisdiction, granted following a series of <a href="/wiki/State_reform_in_Belgium" title="State reform in Belgium">state reforms</a>. In practice, the Flemish Community and Region together form a single body, with its own <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Government" title="Flemish Government">government</a>, as the Community legally absorbed the competences of the Region. The parliament is a directly elected legislative body composed of 124 representatives. The government consists of up to a maximum of eleven members and is presided by a <a href="/wiki/Minister-President_of_Flanders" title="Minister-President of Flanders">Minister-President</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Geert_Bourgeois" title="Geert Bourgeois">Geert Bourgeois</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_Flemish_Alliance" title="New Flemish Alliance">New Flemish Alliance</a>) leading a coalition of his party (N-VA) with <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams</a> (CD&amp;V) and <a href="/wiki/Open_Vlaamse_Liberalen_en_Democraten" title="Open Vlaamse Liberalen en Democraten">Open Vlaamse Liberalen en Democraten</a> (Open VLD).</p>
<p>The area of the Flemish Community is represented on the maps above, including the area of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels-Capital_Region" title="Brussels-Capital Region">Brussels-Capital Region</a> (hatched on the relevant map). Roughly, the Flemish Community exercises competences originally oriented towards the individuals of the Community's language: culture (including audiovisual media), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_education" title="Flemish education">education</a>, and the use of the language. Extensions to personal matters less directly associated with language comprise sports, health policy (curative and preventive medicine), and assistance to individuals (protection of youth, social welfare, aid to families, immigrant assistance services, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fedgov2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-fedgov2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area of the Flemish Region is represented on the maps above. It has a population of more than 6 million (excluding the Dutch-speaking community in the Brussels Region, grey on the map for it is not a part of the Flemish Region). Roughly, the Flemish Region is responsible for territorial issues in a broad sense, including economy, employment, agriculture, water policy, housing, public works, energy, transport, the environment, town and country planning, nature conservation, credit, and foreign trade. It supervises the provinces, municipalities, and intercommunal utility companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fedgov3_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-fedgov3-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of Dutch-speaking <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish people</a> in the Capital Region is estimated to be between 11% and 15% (official figures do not exist as there is no language census and no official subnationality). According to a survey conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_catholique_de_Louvain" title="Université catholique de Louvain">Université catholique de Louvain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louvain-la-Neuve" title="Louvain-la-Neuve">Louvain-la-Neuve</a> and published in June 2006, 51% of respondents from Brussels claimed to be bilingual, even if they do not have Dutch as their first language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> They are governed by the Brussels Region for economics affairs and by the Flemish Community for educational and cultural issues.</p>

<p>As mentioned above, Flemish institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">Flemish Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Government" title="Flemish Government">Government</a>, represent the Flemish Community and the Flemish Region. The region and the community thus <i>de facto</i> share the same parliament and the same government. All these institutions are based in Brussels. Nevertheless, both types of subdivisions (the Community and the Region) still exist legally and the distinction between both is important for the people living in Brussels. Members of the Flemish Parliament who were elected in the Brussels Region cannot vote on affairs belonging to the competences of the Flemish Region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> for all Flemish institutions is <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>. French enjoys a limited official recognition in <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_with_language_facilities" title="Municipalities with language facilities">a dozen municipalities</a> along the borders with French-speaking <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, and a large recognition in the bilingual Brussels Region. French is widely known in Flanders, with 59% claiming to know French according to a survey conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_catholique_de_Louvain" title="Université catholique de Louvain">Université catholique de Louvain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louvain-la-Neuve" title="Louvain-la-Neuve">Louvain-la-Neuve</a> and published in June 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Flanders" title="Politics of Flanders">Politics of Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_parties_in_Flanders" title="Political parties in Flanders">Political parties in Flanders</a></div>
<p>Historically, the political parties reflected the <a href="/wiki/Pillarisation" title="Pillarisation">pillarisation</a> (<i>verzuiling</i>) in Flemish society. The traditional political parties of the three pillars are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian-Democratic_and_Flemish" title="Christian-Democratic and Flemish">Christian-Democratic and Flemish</a> (CD&amp;V), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Flemish_Liberals_and_Democrats" title="Open Flemish Liberals and Democrats">Open Flemish Liberals and Democrats</a> (Open Vld) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_%E2%80%93_Differently" title="Socialist Party – Differently">Socialist Party – Differently</a> (sp.a).</p>
<p>However, during the last half century, many new political parties were founded in Flanders. One of the first was the nationalist <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Union_(Belgium)" title="People's Union (Belgium)">People's Union</a>, of which the right nationalist <a href="/wiki/Vlaams_Blok" title="Vlaams Blok">Flemish Block</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Vlaams_Belang" title="Vlaams Belang">Flemish Interest</a>) split off, and which later dissolved into the now-defunct Spirit or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Liberal_Party_(Belgium)" title="Social Liberal Party (Belgium)">Social Liberal Party</a>, moderate nationalism rather left of the spectrum, on the one hand, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Flemish_Alliance" title="New Flemish Alliance">New Flemish Alliance</a> (N-VA), more conservative but independentist, on the other hand. Other parties are the leftist alternative/ecological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groen!" title="Groen!">Green</a> party; the short-lived anarchistic libertarian spark <a href="/wiki/ROSSEM" title="ROSSEM">ROSSEM</a> and more recently the conservative-right liberal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lijst_Dedecker" title="Lijst Dedecker">List Dedecker</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Dedecker" title="Jean-Marie Dedecker">Jean-Marie Dedecker</a>, and the socialist <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Belgium" title="Workers' Party of Belgium">Workers' Party</a>.</p>
<p>Particularly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_Block" title="Flemish Block">Flemish Block</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_Interest" title="Flemish Interest">Flemish Interest</a> has seen electoral success roughly around the turn of the century, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Flemish_Alliance" title="New Flemish Alliance">New Flemish Alliance</a> during the last few elections, becoming even the largest party in the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_election,_2010" title="Belgian federal election, 2010">2010 federal elections</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flemish_independence">Flemish independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Flemish independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Movement" title="Flemish Movement">Flemish Movement</a></div>
<p>For some inhabitants, Flanders is more than just a geographical area or the federal institutions (Flemish Community and Region). Supporters of the Flemish Movement even call it a nation and pursue Flemish independence, but most people (approximately 75%) living in Flanders say they are proud to be Belgian and opposed to the dissolution of Belgium. 20% is even <i>very proud</i>, while some 25% are not proud and 8% is <i>very not proud</i>. Mostly students claim to be proud of their nationality, with 90% of them staying so. Of the people older than 55, 31% claim to be proud of being a Belgian. Particular opposition to secession comes from women, people employed in <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, the highest social classes and people from big families. Strongest of all opposing the notion are housekeepers - both housewives and house husbands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the Flemish government drafted a "Charter for Flanders" (<i>Handvest voor Vlaanderen</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> of which the first article says <i>"Vlaanderen is een deelstaat van de federale Staat België en maakt deel uit van de Europese Unie."</i> ("Flanders is a <a href="/wiki/Federated_state" title="Federated state">component state</a> of the federal State of Belgium and is part of the European Union"). Though interpreted by many Flemish nationalists as a statement, this phrase is merely a quotation from the Belgian constitution and has no further legal value whatsoever.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Flanders shares its borders with Wallonia in the south, Brussels being an enclave within the Flemish Region. The rest of the border is shared with the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeelandic Flanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Brabant" title="North Brabant">North Brabant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Netherlands)" title="Limburg (Netherlands)">Limburg</a>) in the north and east, and with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> in the west. <a href="/wiki/Voeren" title="Voeren">Voeren</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> of Flanders between Wallonia and the Netherlands, while <a href="/wiki/Baarle-Hertog" title="Baarle-Hertog">Baarle-Hertog</a> in Flanders forms a complicated series of enclaves and exclaves with <a href="/wiki/Baarle-Nassau" title="Baarle-Nassau">Baarle-Nassau</a> in the Netherlands. <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, although bordering Wallonia and close to <a href="/wiki/Voeren" title="Voeren">Voeren</a> in Limburg, does not share a border with Flanders. The <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Community_of_Belgium" title="German-speaking Community of Belgium">German-speaking Community of Belgium</a>, also close to Voeren, does not border Flanders either. (The commune of <a href="/wiki/Plombi%C3%A8res" title="Plombières">Plombières</a>, majority French speaking, lies between them.)</p>
<p>Flanders is a highly urbanised area, lying completely within the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Banana" title="Blue Banana">Blue Banana</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a> are the largest cities of the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> has a population of more than 500,000 citizens and is the largest city, <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> has a population of 250,000 citizens, followed by <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> with 120,000 citizens and <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a> counts almost 100,000 citizens. <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> is a part of Flanders as far as community matters are concerned, but does not belong to the Flemish Region.</p>
<p>Flanders has two main geographical regions: the coastal <a href="/wiki/Yser" title="Yser">Yser</a> basin plain in the north-west and a central plain. The first consists mainly of sand dunes and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clayey</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> soils in the <a href="/wiki/Polder" title="Polder">polders</a>. Polders are areas of land, close to or below sea level that have been reclaimed from the sea, from which they are protected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" title="Dike (construction)">dikes</a> or, a little further inland, by fields that have been drained with canals. With similar soils along the lowermost <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> basin starts the central plain, a smooth, slowly rising fertile area irrigated by many waterways that reaches an average height of about five metres (16.4 ft) above sea level with wide valleys of its rivers upstream as well as the <a href="/wiki/Campine" title="Campine">Campine</a> region to the east having sandy soils at altitudes around thirty metres<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Near its southern edges close to <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> one can find slightly rougher land richer of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> with low hills reaching up to 150 m (490 ft) and small valleys, and at the eastern border with the Netherlands, in the <a href="/wiki/Meuse" title="Meuse">Meuse</a> basin, there are <a href="/wiki/Marl" title="Marl">marl</a> caves (<i>mergelgrotten</i>). Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> around <a href="/wiki/Voeren" title="Voeren">Voeren</a> between the Dutch border and the <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Walloon</a> <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(province)" title="Liège (province)">province of Liège</a> attains a maximum altitude of 288 m (945 ft) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inbo_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-inbo-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The province of Flemish Brabant is the most recent one, being formed in 1995 after the splitting of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Brabant" title="Province of Brabant">province of Brabant</a>.</p>
<p>Most municipalities are made up of several former municipalities, now called <i><a href="/wiki/Deelgemeente" title="Deelgemeente">deelgemeenten</a></i>. The largest municipality (both in terms of population and area) is <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, having more than half a million inhabitants. Its nine <i>deelgemeenten</i> have a special status and are called <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Antwerp" title="Districts of Antwerp">districts</a>, which have an elected council and a college. While any municipality with more than 100,000 inhabitants can establish districts, only Antwerp did this so far. The smallest municipality (also both in terms of population and area) is <a href="/wiki/Herstappe" title="Herstappe">Herstappe</a> (Limburg).</p>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a></b> covers both the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a> and, together with the French Community, the <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels-Capital Region</a>. Brussels, an enclave within the province of Flemish Brabant, is not divided into any province nor is it part of any. It coincides with the <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Brussels-Capital" title="Arrondissement of Brussels-Capital">Arrondissement of Brussels-Capital</a> and includes <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_of_the_Brussels-Capital_Region" title="List of municipalities of the Brussels-Capital Region">19 municipalities</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Government" title="Flemish Government">Flemish Government</a> has its own local institutions in the Brussels-Capital Region, being the <i><a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community_Commission" title="Flemish Community Commission">Vlaamse Gemeenschapscommissie</a></i> (VGC), and its municipal antennae (<i>Gemeenschapscentra</i>, community centres for the Flemish community in Brussels). These institutions are independent from the educational, cultural and social institutions that depend directly on the Flemish Government. They exert, among others, all those cultural competences that outside Brussels fall under the provinces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate is maritime <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a>, with significant precipitation in all seasons (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>: <i>Cfb</i>; the average temperature is 3 °C (37 °F) in January, and 21 °C (70 °F) in July; the average precipitation is 65 millimetres (2.6 in) in January, and 78 millimetres (3.1 in) in July).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Belgium" title="Economy of Belgium">Economy of Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Diamond" title="Flemish Diamond">Flemish Diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_in_Flanders" title="Science and technology in Flanders">Science and technology in Flanders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Flanders" title="Agriculture in Flanders">Agriculture in Flanders</a></div>

<p>Total GDP of the Flemish Region in 2004 was €165,847 billion (<a href="/wiki/Eurostat" title="Eurostat">Eurostat</a> figures). Per capita GDP at <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a> was 23% above the EU average. Flemish productivity per capita is about 13% higher than that in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, and wages are about 7% higher than in Wallonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flanders was one of the first continental European areas to undergo the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, in the 19th century. Initially, the modernization relied heavily on food processing and textile. However, by the 1840s the textile industry of Flanders was in severe crisis and there was famine in Flanders (1846–50). After World War II, <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> experienced a fast expansion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> and petroleum industries. Flanders also attracted a large majority of foreign investments in Belgium. The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1979_oil_crisis" title="1979 oil crisis">1979 oil crises</a> sent the economy into a recession. The steel industry remained in relatively good shape. In the 1980s and 90s, the economic centre of Belgium continued to shift further to Flanders and is now concentrated in the populous <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Diamond" title="Flemish Diamond">Flemish Diamond</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Nowadays, the Flemish economy is mainly service-oriented.</p>
<p>Belgium is a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Coal_and_Steel_Community" title="European Coal and Steel Community">European Coal and Steel Community</a> in 1951, which evolved into the present-day <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>, the single European currency, was introduced in Flanders. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_franc" title="Belgian franc">Belgian franc</a> in 2002.</p>
<p>The Flemish economy is strongly export-oriented, in particular of high value-added goods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The main imports are food products, machinery, rough diamonds, petroleum and petroleum products, chemicals, clothing and accessories, and textiles. The main exports are automobiles, food and food products, iron and steel, finished diamonds, textiles, plastics, petroleum products, and non-ferrous metals. Since 1922, Belgium and Luxembourg have been a single trade market within a <a href="/wiki/Customs_union" title="Customs union">customs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Currency_union" title="Currency union">currency union</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Belgium%E2%80%93Luxembourg_Economic_Union" title="Belgium–Luxembourg Economic Union">Belgium–Luxembourg Economic Union</a>. Its main trading partners are Germany, the Netherlands, France, the United Kingdom, Italy, the United States, and Spain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Antwerp is the number one diamond market in the world, diamond exports account for roughly 1/10 of Belgian exports. The Antwerp-based <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> plant is the largest BASF-base outside Germany, and accounts on its own for about 2% of Belgian exports. Other industrial and service activities in Antwerp include car manufacturing, telecommunications, photographic products.</p>
<p>Flanders is home to several science and technology institutes, such as <a href="/wiki/IMEC" title="IMEC">IMEC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Institute_for_Technological_Research" title="Flemish Institute for Technological Research">VITO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flanders_DC" title="Flanders DC">Flanders DC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flanders_DRIVE" title="Flanders DRIVE">Flanders DRIVE</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Belgium" title="Transport in Belgium">Transport in Belgium</a></div>
<p>Flanders has developed an extensive transportation infrastructure of ports, canals, railways and highways. The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Antwerp" title="Port of Antwerp">Port of Antwerp</a> is the second-largest in Europe, after <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Rotterdam" title="Port of Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Other ports are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_of_Bruges-Zeebrugge" title="Port of Bruges-Zeebrugge">Bruges-Zeebrugge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Ghent" title="Port of Ghent">Ghent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Ostend" title="Port of Ostend">Ostend</a>, of which Bruges and Ostend are located at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Belgian_coast&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Belgian coast (page does not exist)">Belgian coast</a>.</p>
<p>Whereas railways are managed by the federal <a href="/wiki/National_Railway_Company_of_Belgium" title="National Railway Company of Belgium">National Railway Company of Belgium</a>, other public transport (<a href="/wiki/De_Lijn" title="De Lijn">De Lijn</a>) and roads are managed by the Flemish region.</p>
<p>The main airport is <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airport" title="Brussels Airport">Brussels Airport</a>, the only other civilian airport with scheduled services in Flanders is <a href="/wiki/Antwerp_International_Airport" title="Antwerp International Airport">Antwerp International Airport</a>, but there are two other ones with cargo or charter flights: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostend-Bruges_International_Airport" title="Ostend-Bruges International Airport">Ostend-Bruges International Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kortrijk-Wevelgem_International_Airport" title="Kortrijk-Wevelgem International Airport">Kortrijk-Wevelgem International Airport</a>, both in West Flanders.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Belgium" title="Demographics of Belgium">Demographics of Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish people</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Flanders" title="List of cities in Flanders">List of cities in Flanders</a></div>
<p>The highest population density is found in the area circumscribed by the <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>-<a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>-<a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a> agglomerations that surround Mechelen and is known as the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Diamond" title="Flemish Diamond">Flemish Diamond</a>, in other important urban centres as <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a> to the west, and notable centres <a href="/wiki/Turnhout" title="Turnhout">Turnhout</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hasselt" title="Hasselt">Hasselt</a> to the east. On 1 January 2015, the Flemish Region had a population of 6,444,127 and about 15% of the 1,175,173 people in the Brussels Region are also considered Flemish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statbel_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-statbel-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Belgium" title="Religion in Belgium">Religion in Belgium</a></div>
<p>The (Belgian) <i><a href="/wiki/La%C3%AFcit%C3%A9" title="Laïcité">laicist</a></i>, or secularist, constitution provides for freedom of religion, and the various governments in general respect this right in practice. Since independence, Catholicism, counterbalanced by strong <a href="/wiki/Freethought" title="Freethought">freethought</a> movements, has had an important role in Belgium's politics, since the 20th century in Flanders mainly via the Christian trade union <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_Christian_Trade_Unions" title="Confederation of Christian Trade Unions">ACV</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">Christian Democratic and Flemish</a> party (CD&amp;V). According to the <i>2001 Survey and Study of Religion</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> about 47 percent of the Belgian population identify themselves as belonging to the Catholic Church, while Islam is the second-largest religion at 3.5 percent. A 2006 inquiry in Flanders, considered more religious than Wallonia, showed that 55% considered themselves religious, and 36% believed that God created the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jews <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Belgium" title="History of the Jews in Belgium">have been present</a> in Flanders for a long time, in particular <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Antwerp" title="History of the Jews in Antwerp">in Antwerp</a>. More recently, Muslims have immigrated to Flanders, now forming the largest minority religion with about 3.9% in the Flemish Region and 25% in Brussels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The largest Muslim group is the Moroccans, while the second largest is the Turks.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Flanders" title="Education in Flanders">Education in Flanders</a></div>
<p>Education is compulsory from the ages of six to 18, but most <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemings</a> continue to study until around 23. Among the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> countries in 1999, Flanders had the third-highest proportion of 18- to 21-year-olds enrolled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postsecondary_education" title="Postsecondary education">postsecondary education</a>. Flanders also scores very high in international comparative studies on education. Its secondary school students consistently rank among the top three for mathematics and science. However, the success is not evenly spread: ethnic minority youth score consistently lower, and the difference is larger than in most comparable countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Note_Nationality-Minority-Education-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mirroring the historical political conflicts between the freethought and Catholic segments of the population, the Flemish educational system is split into a secular branch controlled by the communities, the provinces, or the municipalities, and a <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidised</a> religious—mostly Catholic—branch. For the subsidised schools, the main costs such as the teacher's wages and building maintenance completely borne by the Flemish government. Subsidised schools are also free to determine their own teaching and examination methods, but in exchange, they must be able to prove that certain minimal terms are achieved by keeping records of the given lessons and exams. It should however be noted that—at least for the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_school" title="Catholic school">Catholic schools</a>—the religious authorities have very limited power over these schools, neither do the schools have a lot of power on their own. Instead, the Catholic schools are a member of the Catholic umbrella organisation <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=VSKO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="VSKO (page does not exist)">VSKO</a>. The VSKO determines most practicalities for schools, like the advised schedules per study field. However, there's freedom of education in Flanders, which doesn't only mean that every pupil can choose his/her preferred school, but also that every organisation can found a school, and even be subsidised when abiding the different rules. This resulted also in some smaller school systems follow 'methodical pedagogies' (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Waldorf_education" title="Waldorf education">Steiner</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montessori" title="Montessori">Montessori</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freinet" title="Freinet">Freinet</a>) or serve the Jewish and Protestant minorities.</p>
<p>During the school year 2003–2004, 68.30% of the total population of children between the ages of six and 18 went to subsidized private schools (both religious schools or 'methodical pedagogies' schools).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The big freedom given to schools results in a constant competition to be the "best" school. The schools get certain reputations amongst parents and employers. So it's important for schools to be the best school since the subsidies depend on the number of pupils. This competition has been pinpointed as one of the main reasons for the high overall quality of the Flemish education. However, the importance of a school's reputation also makes schools more eager to expel pupils that don't perform well. Resulting in the ethnic differences and the well-known waterfall system: pupils start high in the perceived hierarchy, and then drop towards more professional oriented directions or "easier" schools when they can't handle the pressure any longer.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish people</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Movement" title="Flemish Movement">Flemish Movement</a></div>
<p>At first sight, <i>Flemish culture</i> is defined by its <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and its gourmandic mentality, as compared to the more Calvinistic Dutch culture. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_paintings" title="Dutch paintings">Dutch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_painting" title="Flemish painting">Flemish paintings</a> enjoyed more equal international admiration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_and_literature">Language and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Language and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The standard language in Flanders is <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>; spelling and grammar are regulated by a single authority, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Language_Union" title="Dutch Language Union">Dutch Language Union</a> (<i>Nederlandse Taalunie</i>), comprising a committee of ministers of the Flemish and Dutch governments, their advisory council of appointed experts, a controlling commission of 22 parliamentarians, and a secretariate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taalunie1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taalunie1-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taalunie2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taalunie2-32">[32]</a></sup> The term <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> can be applied to the Dutch spoken in Flanders; it shows many regional and local variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biggest difference between Belgian Dutch and Dutch used in the Netherlands is in the pronunciation of words. The Dutch spoken in the north of the Netherlands is typically described as being "sharper", while Belgian Dutch is "softer". In Belgian Dutch, there are also less vowels pronounced as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diphthongs" title="Diphthongs">diphthongs</a>. When it comes to spelling, Belgian Dutch language purists historically avoided to write words using a French spelling, or search for specific translations of words derived from French. While the Dutch prefer to stick with French spelling, as it differentiates Dutch more from the neighbouring German. For example, the Dutch word "punaise" (English: <i><a href="/wiki/Drawing_pin" title="Drawing pin">Drawing pin</a></i>) is derived directly from the French language. Belgian Dutch language purists have lobbied to accept the word "duimspijker" (literally: <i>thumb spike</i>) as official Dutch, though the Dutch Language Union never accepted it as standard Dutch. Other proposals by purists were sometimes accepted, and sometimes reverted again in later spelling revisions. As language purists were quite often professionally involved in language (f.e. as a teacher), these unofficial purist translations are found more often in Belgian Dutch texts.</p>
<p>Literature in non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standardized_dialect" title="Standardized dialect">standardized dialects</a> of the current area of Flanders originated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hendrik_van_Veldeke" title="Hendrik van Veldeke">Hendrik van Veldeke</a>'s <i>Eneas Romance</i>, the first courtly romance in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic language</a> (12th century). With a writer of <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Conscience" title="Hendrik Conscience">Hendrik Conscience</a>'s stature, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_literature" title="Flemish literature">Flemish literature</a> rose ahead of French literature in Belgium's early history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letterkundig-Museum_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letterkundig-Museum-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couttenier_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Couttenier-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guido_Gezelle" title="Guido Gezelle">Guido Gezelle</a> not only explicitly referred to his writings as Flemish but actually used it in many of his poems, and strongly defended it:</p>

<p>The distinction between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_literature" title="Dutch literature">Dutch</a> and Flemish literature, often perceived politically, is also made on intrinsic grounds by some experts such as Kris Humbeeck, professor of Literature at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Antwerp" title="University of Antwerp">University of Antwerp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de-Ridder_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-de-Ridder-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humbeeck_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humbeeck-39">[39]</a></sup> Nevertheless, nearly all<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch-language</a> literature read (and appreciated to varying degrees) in Flanders is the same as that in the Netherlands.</p>
<p>Influential Flemish writers include <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Claes" title="Ernest Claes">Ernest Claes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stijn_Streuvels" title="Stijn Streuvels">Stijn Streuvels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Timmermans" title="Felix Timmermans">Felix Timmermans</a>. Their novels mostly describe rural life in Flanders in the 19th century and at beginning of the 20th. Widely read by the older generations, they are considered somewhat old-fashioned by present-day critics. Some famous Flemish writers of the early 20th century wrote in French, including Nobel Prize winners (1911) <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Maeterlinck" title="Maurice Maeterlinck">Maurice Maeterlinck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emile_Verhaeren" title="Emile Verhaeren">Emile Verhaeren</a>. They were followed by a younger generation, including <a href="/wiki/Paul_van_Ostaijen" title="Paul van Ostaijen">Paul van Ostaijen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Burssens" title="Gaston Burssens">Gaston Burssens</a>, who <i><a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activated</a></i> the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Movement" title="Flemish Movement">Flemish Movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de-Ridder_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-de-Ridder-38">[38]</a></sup> Still widely read and translated into other languages (including English) are the novels of authors such as <a href="/wiki/Willem_Elsschot" title="Willem Elsschot">Willem Elsschot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Paul_Boon" title="Louis Paul Boon">Louis Paul Boon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Claus" title="Hugo Claus">Hugo Claus</a>. The recent crop of writers includes the novelists <a href="/wiki/Tom_Lanoye" title="Tom Lanoye">Tom Lanoye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Brusselmans" title="Herman Brusselmans">Herman Brusselmans</a>, and poets such as the married couple <a href="/wiki/Herman_de_Coninck" title="Herman de Coninck">Herman de Coninck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kristien_Hemmerechts" title="Kristien Hemmerechts">Kristien Hemmerechts</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages_in_Flanders">Other languages in Flanders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Other languages in Flanders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the creation of the Belgian state, French was the only official language. French was during a long period used as a <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">second language</a> in Flanders and, like elsewhere in Europe, commonly spoken among the aristocracy. There is still a French-speaking minority in Flanders, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_with_language_facilities" title="Municipalities with language facilities">municipalities with language facilities</a>, along the language border and the <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_with_language_facilities#Rim_municipalities" title="Municipalities with language facilities">Brussels periphery</a> (Vlaamse Rand), though many of them are French-speakers that migrated to Flanders in recent decades. French is the primary language in the officially bilingual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_Capital_Region" title="Brussels Capital Region">Brussels Capital Region</a>, (see <a href="/wiki/Francization_of_Brussels" title="Francization of Brussels">Francization of Brussels</a>). In <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a>, French is now the native language of the majority of the population and the only official language. Historically it was a Dutch-speaking region and there is still a minority of Dutch-speakers living there.</p>
<p>Many Flemings are also able to speak French, children in Flanders generally get their first French lessons in the 5th primary year (normally around 10 years). But the current lack of French outside the educational context makes it hard to maintain a decent level of French. As such, the proficiency of French is declining. Flemish pupils are also obligated to follow English lessons as their third language. Normally from the second secondary year (around 14 years old), but the ubiquity of English in movies, music, IT and even advertisements makes it easier to learn and maintain the English language. This makes the Flemish people very proficient in English (in Europe, only Sweden and Malta have a better knowledge of English as a second language).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Television_in_Belgium" title="Television in Belgium">Television in Belgium</a></div>
<p>The public radio and television broadcaster in Flanders is <a href="/wiki/Vlaamse_Radio-_en_Televisieomroeporganisatie" title="Vlaamse Radio- en Televisieomroeporganisatie">VRT</a>, which operates the TV channels <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89%C3%A9n" title="Één">één</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canvas_(Belgium)" title="Canvas (Belgium)">Canvas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketnet" title="Ketnet">Ketnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/OP12" title="OP12">OP12</a> and (together with the Netherlands) <a href="/wiki/BVN" title="BVN">BVN</a>. Flemish provinces each have up to two TV channels as well. Commercial television broadcasters include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vtm" title="Vtm">vtm</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vier" title="Vier">Vier</a> (VT4). Popular TV series are for example <i><a href="/wiki/Thuis" title="Thuis">Thuis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/F.C._De_Kampioenen" title="F.C. De Kampioenen">F.C. De Kampioenen</a></i>.</p>
<p>The five most successful Flemish films were <i><a href="/wiki/Loft_(2008_film)" title="Loft (2008 film)">Loft</a></i> (2008; 1,186,071 visitors), <i><a href="/wiki/Koko_Flanel" title="Koko Flanel">Koko Flanel</a></i> (1990; 1,082,000 tickets sold), <i><a href="/wiki/Hector_(1987_film)" title="Hector (1987 film)">Hector</a></i> (1987; 933,000 tickets sold), <i><a href="/wiki/Daens_(film)" title="Daens (film)">Daens</a></i> (1993; 848,000 tickets sold) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alzheimer_Case" title="The Alzheimer Case">De Zaak Alzheimer</a></i> (2003; 750,000 tickets sold). The first and last ones were directed by <a href="/wiki/Erik_Van_Looy" title="Erik Van Looy">Erik Van Looy</a>, and an American remake is being made of both of them, respectively <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Loft_(2012_film)" title="The Loft (2012 film)">The Loft</a></i> (2012) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Memory_of_a_Killer" title="The Memory of a Killer">The Memory of a Killer</a></i>. The other three ones were directed by <a href="/wiki/Stijn_Coninx" title="Stijn Coninx">Stijn Coninx</a>.</p>
<p>Newspapers are grouped under three main publishers: <a href="/wiki/De_Persgroep" title="De Persgroep">De Persgroep</a> with <a href="/wiki/Het_Laatste_Nieuws" title="Het Laatste Nieuws">Het Laatste Nieuws</a>, the most popular newspaper in Flanders, <a href="/wiki/De_Morgen" title="De Morgen">De Morgen</a> and <a href="/wiki/De_Tijd" title="De Tijd">De Tijd</a>. Then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corelio" title="Corelio">Corelio</a> with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=De_Gentenaar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="De Gentenaar (page does not exist)">De Gentenaar</a>, the oldest extant Flemish newspaper, <a href="/wiki/Het_Nieuwsblad" title="Het Nieuwsblad">Het Nieuwsblad</a> and <a href="/wiki/De_Standaard" title="De Standaard">De Standaard</a>. Lastly, <a href="/wiki/Concentra" title="Concentra">Concentra</a> publishes <a href="/wiki/Gazet_van_Antwerpen" title="Gazet van Antwerpen">Gazet van Antwerpen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Het_Belang_van_Limburg" title="Het Belang van Limburg">Het Belang van Limburg</a>.</p>
<p>Magazines include <a href="/wiki/Knack_(magazine)" title="Knack (magazine)">Knack</a> and <a href="/wiki/HUMO" title="HUMO">HUMO</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_Belgium" title="Sport in Belgium">Sport in Belgium</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Association football</a> (soccer) is one of the most popular sports in both parts of Belgium, together with cycling, tennis, swimming and judo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In cycling, the <a href="/wiki/Tour_of_Flanders" title="Tour of Flanders">Tour of Flanders</a> is considered one of the five "<a href="/wiki/Classic_cycle_races" title="Classic cycle races">Monuments</a>". Other "<a href="/wiki/Flanders_Classics" title="Flanders Classics">Flanders Classics</a>" races include <i><a href="/wiki/Dwars_door_Vlaanderen" title="Dwars door Vlaanderen">Dwars door Vlaanderen</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Gent%E2%80%93Wevelgem" title="Gent–Wevelgem">Gent–Wevelgem</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eddy_Merckx" title="Eddy Merckx">Eddy Merckx</a> is regarded as one of the greatest cyclists of all time, with five victories in the <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> and numerous other cycling records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> His hour speed record (set in 1972) stood for 12 years.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Pfaff" title="Jean-Marie Pfaff">Jean-Marie Pfaff</a>, a former Belgian goalkeeper, is considered one of the greatest in the history of football (soccer).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kim_Clijsters" title="Kim Clijsters">Kim Clijsters</a> (as well as the French-speaking Belgian <a href="/wiki/Justine_Henin" title="Justine Henin">Justine Henin</a>) was <a href="/wiki/WTA_Awards" title="WTA Awards">Player of the Year</a> twice in the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Tennis_Association" title="Women's Tennis Association">Women's Tennis Association</a> as she was ranked the number one female tennis player.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kim_Gevaert" title="Kim Gevaert">Kim Gevaert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tia_Hellebaut" title="Tia Hellebaut">Tia Hellebaut</a> are notable <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> stars from Flanders.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1920_Summer_Olympics" title="1920 Summer Olympics">1920 Summer Olympics</a> were held in Antwerp. <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Rogge" title="Jacques Rogge">Jacques Rogge</a> has been president of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> since 2001.</p>
<p>The Flemish government agency for sports is <a href="/wiki/Bloso" title="Bloso">Bloso</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flanders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Belgium" title="Music of Belgium">Music of Belgium</a></div>
<p>Flanders is known for its <a href="/wiki/Music_festival" title="Music festival">music festivals</a>, like the annual <a href="/wiki/Rock_Werchter" title="Rock Werchter">Rock Werchter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomorrowland_(festival)" title="Tomorrowland (festival)">Tomorrowland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pukkelpop" title="Pukkelpop">Pukkelpop</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gentse_Feesten" title="Gentse Feesten">Gentse Feesten</a> are another very large yearly event.</p>
<p>The best-selling Flemish group or artist is the (Flemish-Dutch) group <a href="/wiki/2_Unlimited" title="2 Unlimited">2 Unlimited</a>, followed by (Italian-born) <a href="/wiki/Rocco_Granata" title="Rocco Granata">Rocco Granata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technotronic" title="Technotronic">Technotronic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Lotti" title="Helmut Lotti">Helmut Lotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaya_Con_Dios_(band)" title="Vaya Con Dios (band)">Vaya Con Dios</a>.</p>
<p>The weekly charts of best-selling singles is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultratop_50" title="Ultratop 50">Ultratop 50</a>. <i>Kvraagetaan</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Fixkes" title="Fixkes">Fixkes</a> holds the current record for longest time at #1 on the chart.</p>
