<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>The Canterbury Tales</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><i><b>The Canterbury Tales</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>: <i>Tales of Caunterbury</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a collection of 24 stories that runs to over 17,000 lines written in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>. In 1386, Chaucer became Controller of Customs and Justice of Peace and, three years later, Clerk of the King's work in 1389. It was during these years that Chaucer began working on his most famous text, <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>. The tales (mostly written in <a href="/wiki/Verse_(poetry)" title="Verse (poetry)">verse</a>, although some are in <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">prose</a>) are presented as part of a story-telling contest by a group of <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim" title="Pilgrim">pilgrims</a> as they travel together on a journey from London to Canterbury to visit the shrine of Saint <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a> at <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a>. The prize for this contest is a free meal at the <a href="/wiki/The_Tabard" title="The Tabard">Tabard Inn</a> at Southwark on their return.</p>
<p>After a long list of works written earlier in his career, including <i><a href="/wiki/Troilus_and_Criseyde" title="Troilus and Criseyde">Troilus and Criseyde</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Fame" title="House of Fame">House of Fame</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Parlement_of_Foules" title="Parlement of Foules">Parliament of Fowls</a></i>, <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is near-unanimously seen as Chaucer's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>. He uses the tales and descriptions of its characters to paint an ironic and critical portrait of English society at the time, and particularly of the Church. Chaucer's use of such a wide range of classes and types of people was without precedent in English. Although the characters are fictional, they still offer a variety of insights into customs and practices of the time. Often, such insight leads to a variety of discussions and disagreements among people in the 14th century. For example, although various social classes are represented in these stories and all of the pilgrims are on a spiritual quest, it is apparent that they are more concerned with worldly things than spiritual. Structurally, the collection resembles <i><a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">The Decameron</a></i>, which Chaucer may have read during his first diplomatic mission to <a href="/wiki/Italy_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="Italy in the Middle Ages">Italy</a> in 1372.</p>
<p>It has been suggested that the greatest contribution of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> to <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> was the popularization of the English <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> in mainstream literature, as opposed to French, Italian or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. English had, however, been used as a literary language centuries before Chaucer's time, and several of Chaucer's contemporaries—<a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Langland" title="William Langland">William Langland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pearl_Poet" title="The Pearl Poet">the Pearl Poet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Julian_of_Norwich" title="Julian of Norwich">Julian of Norwich</a>—also wrote major literary works in English. It is unclear to what extent Chaucer was seminal in this evolution of literary preference.</p>
<p>While Chaucer clearly states the addressees of many of his poems, the intended audience of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is more difficult to determine. Chaucer was a <a href="/wiki/Courtier" title="Courtier">courtier</a>, leading some to believe that he was mainly a court poet who wrote exclusively for nobility.</p>
<p><i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is generally thought to have been incomplete at the end of Chaucer's life. In the <a href="/wiki/General_Prologue" title="General Prologue">General Prologue</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> some thirty pilgrims are introduced. According to the Prologue, Chaucer's intention was to write two stories from the perspective of each pilgrim on the way to and from their ultimate destination, St. Thomas Becket's shrine (making for a total of four stories per pilgrim). Although perhaps incomplete, <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is revered as one of the most important works in English literature. Not only do readers find it entertaining, but it is also open to a wide range of interpretations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Text">Text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The question of whether <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is finished has not yet been answered. There are 83 known manuscripts of the work from the late medieval and early Renaissance periods, more than any other vernacular literary text with the exception of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Prick_of_Conscience" title="The Prick of Conscience">The Prick of Conscience</a></i>. This is taken as evidence of the tales' popularity during the century after Chaucer's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearsall.2C_8_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearsall.2C_8-5">[5]</a></sup> Fifty-five of these manuscripts are thought to have been complete at one time, while 28 are so fragmentary that it is difficult to ascertain whether they were copied individually or as part of a set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <i>Tales</i> vary in both minor and major ways from manuscript to manuscript; many of the minor variations are due to copyists' errors, while others suggest that Chaucer added to and revised his work as it was being copied and (possibly) distributed.</p>
<p>Even the earliest surviving manuscripts are not Chaucer's originals, the oldest being MS Peniarth 392 D (called "<a href="/wiki/Hengwrt_Chaucer" title="Hengwrt Chaucer">Hengwrt</a>"), compiled by a scribe shortly after Chaucer's death. The most beautiful of the manuscripts of the tales is the <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Chaucer" title="Ellesmere Chaucer">Ellesmere Manuscript</a>, and many editors have followed the order of the Ellesmere over the centuries, even down to the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first version of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> to be published in print was <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a>'s 1478 edition. Only 10 copies of this edition are known to exist, including one held by the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> and one held by the <a href="/wiki/Folger_Shakespeare_Library" title="Folger Shakespeare Library">Folger Shakespeare Library</a>. Since this print edition was created from a now-lost manuscript, it is counted as among the 83 manuscripts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearsall.2C_8_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pearsall.2C_8-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2004, Professor Linne Mooney claimed that she was able to identify the <a href="/wiki/Scrivener" title="Scrivener">scrivener</a> who worked for Chaucer as an <a href="/wiki/Adam_Pinkhurst" title="Adam Pinkhurst">Adam Pinkhurst</a>. Mooney, then a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine" title="University of Maine">University of Maine</a> and a visiting fellow at <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_College,_Cambridge" title="Corpus Christi College, Cambridge">Corpus Christi College, Cambridge</a>, said she could match Pinkhurst's signature, on an oath he signed, to his lettering on a copy of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> that was transcribed from Chaucer's working copy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Order">Order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Order_of_The_Canterbury_Tales" title="Order of The Canterbury Tales">Order of The Canterbury Tales</a></div>
<p>No authorial, arguably complete version of the <i>Tales</i> exists and no consensus has been reached regarding the order in which Chaucer intended the stories to be placed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-seven-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Textual and manuscript clues have been adduced to support the two most popular modern methods of ordering the tales. Some scholarly editions divide the <i>Tales</i> into ten "Fragments". The tales that make up a Fragment are closely related and contain internal indications of their order of presentation, usually with one character speaking to and then stepping aside for another character. However, between Fragments, the connection is less obvious. Consequently, there are several possible orders; the one most frequently seen in modern editions follows the numbering of the Fragments (ultimately based on the Ellesmere order).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-seven-10">[10]</a></sup> Victorians frequently used the nine "Groups", which was the order used by <a href="/wiki/Walter_William_Skeat" title="Walter William Skeat">Walter William Skeat</a> whose edition <i>Chaucer: Complete Works</i> was used by Oxford University Press for most of the twentieth century, but this order is now seldom followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-seven-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Chaucer wrote in late Middle English, which has clear differences from Modern English. From philological research, we know certain facts about the pronunciation of English during the time of Chaucer. Chaucer pronounced <i>-e</i> at the end of words, so that <i>care</i> was <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈkaːrə]</a></span>, not <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span></span>/</a></span></span> as in Modern English. Other silent letters were also pronounced, so that the word <i>knight</i> was <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kniçt]</span>, with both the <i>k</i> and the <i>gh</i> pronounced, not <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>. In some cases, vowel letters in Middle English were pronounced very differently from Modern English, because the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a> had not yet happened. For instance, the long <i>e</i> in <i>wepyng</i> "weeping" was pronounced as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[eː]</a></span>, as in modern German or Italian, not as <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>. <p>Although no manuscript exists in Chaucer's own hand, two were copied around the time of his death by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Pinkhurst" title="Adam Pinkhurst">Adam Pinkhurst</a>, a scribe with whom he seems to have worked closely before, giving a high degree of confidence that Chaucer himself wrote the <i>Tales</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because the final <i>-e</i> sound was lost soon after Chaucer's time, scribes did not accurately copy it, and this gave scholars the impression that Chaucer himself was inconsistent in using it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It has now been established, however, that <i>-e</i> was an important part of Chaucer's grammar, and helped to distinguish singular adjectives from plural and subjunctive verbs from indicative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waterhouse_decameron.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="630" data-file-width="1000" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Waterhouse_decameron.jpg/220px-Waterhouse_decameron.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Waterhouse_decameron.jpg/330px-Waterhouse_decameron.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Waterhouse_decameron.jpg/440px-Waterhouse_decameron.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waterhouse_decameron.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>A Tale from the Decameron</i> by <a href="/wiki/John_William_Waterhouse" title="John William Waterhouse">John William Waterhouse</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>No other work prior to Chaucer's is known to have set a collection of tales within the framework of pilgrims on a pilgrimage. It is obvious, however, that Chaucer borrowed portions, sometimes very large portions, of his stories from earlier stories, and that his work was influenced by the general state of the literary world in which he lived. Storytelling was the main entertainment in England at the time, and storytelling contests had been around for hundreds of years. In 14th-century England the English Pui was a group with an appointed leader who would judge the songs of the group. The winner received a crown and, as with the winner of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>, a free dinner. It was common for pilgrims on a pilgrimage to have a chosen "master of ceremonies" to guide them and organise the journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bloom" title="Harold Bloom">Harold Bloom</a> suggests that the structure is mostly original, but inspired by the "pilgrim" figures of Dante and Virgil in <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">The Divine Comedy</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harold_Bloom_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harold_Bloom-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">The Decameron</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a> contains more parallels to <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> than any other work. Like the <i>Tales</i>, it features a number of narrators who tell stories along a journey they have undertaken (to flee from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>). It ends with an apology by Boccaccio, much like <a href="/wiki/Chaucer%27s_Retraction" title="Chaucer's Retraction">Chaucer's Retraction</a> to the <i>Tales</i>. A quarter of the tales in <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> parallel a tale in the <i>Decameron</i>, although most of them have closer parallels in other stories. Some scholars thus find it unlikely that Chaucer had a copy of the work on hand, surmising instead that he must have merely read the <i>Decameron</i> at some point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Each of the tales has its own set of sources that have been suggested by scholars, but a few sources are used frequently over several tales. They include poetry by <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, the Bible in one of the many vulgate versions in which it was available at the time (the exact one is difficult to determine), and the works of <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a>. Chaucer was the first author to utilise the work of these last two, both Italians. <a href="/wiki/Boethius" title="Boethius">Boethius</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consolation_of_Philosophy" title="Consolation of Philosophy">Consolation of Philosophy</a></i> appears in several tales, as the works of <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a> do. Gower was a known friend to Chaucer. A full list is impossible to outline in little space, but Chaucer also, lastly, seems to have borrowed from numerous religious encyclopaedias and liturgical writings, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Bromyard" title="John Bromyard">John Bromyard</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Summa_praedicantium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Summa praedicantium (page does not exist)">Summa praedicantium</a></i>, a preacher's handbook, and <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Against_Jovinianus" title="Against Jovinianus">Adversus Jovinianum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Many scholars say there is a good possibility <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaucer_coming_in_contact_with_Petrarch_or_Boccaccio" title="Chaucer coming in contact with Petrarch or Boccaccio">Chaucer met Petrarch or Boccaccio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewer227_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brewer227-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewer277_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brewer277-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendrickson183-192_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickson183-192-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_and_structure">Genre and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Genre and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="610" data-file-width="820" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg/300px-Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg/450px-Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg/600px-Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canterbury-Cathedral-Church-of-England-1890-1900.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a> from the north west <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">circa</a> 1890–1900 (retouched from a black &amp; white photograph)</div>

<p><i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is a collection of stories built around a frame narrative or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_tale" title="Frame tale">frame tale</a>, a common and already long established genre of its period. Chaucer's <i>Tales</i> differs from most other story "collections" in this genre chiefly in its intense variation. Most story collections focused on a theme, usually a religious one. Even in the <i>Decameron</i>, storytellers are encouraged to stick to the theme decided on for the day. The idea of a <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> to get such a diverse collection of people together for literary purposes was also unprecedented, though "the association of pilgrims and storytelling was a familiar one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Introducing a competition among the tales encourages the reader to compare the tales in all their variety, and allows Chaucer to showcase the breadth of his skill in different genres and literary forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the structure of the <i>Tales</i> is largely linear, with one story following another, it is also much more than that. In the <i>General Prologue</i>, Chaucer describes not the tales to be told, but the people who will tell them, making it clear that structure will depend on the characters rather than a general theme or moral. This idea is reinforced when the Miller interrupts to tell his tale after the Knight has finished his. Having the Knight go first gives one the idea that all will tell their stories by class, with the Monk following the Knight. However, the Miller's interruption makes it clear that this structure will be abandoned in favour of a free and open exchange of stories among all classes present. General themes and points of view arise as the characters tell their tales, which are responded to by other characters in their own tales, sometimes after a long lapse in which the theme has not been addressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lastly, Chaucer does not pay much attention to the progress of the trip, to the time passing as the pilgrims travel, or to specific locations along the way to Canterbury. His writing of the story seems focused primarily on the stories being told, and not on the pilgrimage itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hengwrt_Chaucer_(f.2.r)_title_page.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="1288" height="359" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg/220px-Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg/330px-Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg/440px-Hengwrt_Chaucer_%28f.2.r%29_title_page.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hengwrt_Chaucer_(f.2.r)_title_page.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page of Geoffrey Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales in the hand of his personal scribe Adam Pinkhurst, c.1400</div>
</div>

<p>The variety of Chaucer's tales shows the breadth of his skill and his familiarity with many literary forms, linguistic styles, and rhetorical devices. Medieval schools of rhetoric at the time encouraged such diversity, dividing literature (as <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> suggests) into high, middle, and low styles as measured by the density of rhetorical forms and vocabulary. Another popular method of division came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Augustine" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a>, who focused more on audience response and less on subject matter (a Virgilian concern). Augustine divided literature into "majestic persuades", "temperate pleases", and "subdued teaches". Writers were encouraged to write in a way that kept in mind the speaker, subject, audience, purpose, manner, and occasion. Chaucer moves freely between all of these styles, showing favouritism to none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empathy_article_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-empathy_article-30">[30]</a></sup> He not only considers the readers of his work as an audience, but the other pilgrims within the story as well, creating a multi-layered rhetorical puzzle of ambiguities. Thus Chaucer's work far surpasses the ability of any single medieval theory to uncover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>With this, Chaucer avoids targeting any specific audience or social class of readers, focusing instead on the characters of the story and writing their tales with a skill proportional to their social status and learning. However, even the lowest characters, such as the Miller, show surprising rhetorical ability, although their subject matter is more lowbrow. Vocabulary also plays an important part, as those of the higher classes refer to a woman as a "lady", while the lower classes use the word "wenche", with no exceptions. At times the same word will mean entirely different things between classes. The word "pitee", for example, is a noble concept to the upper classes, while in the <i>Merchant's Tale</i> it refers to sexual intercourse. Again, however, tales such as the <i>Nun's Priest's Tale</i> show surprising skill with words among the lower classes of the group, while the <i>Knight's Tale</i> is at times extremely simple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chaucer uses the same meter throughout almost all of his tales, with the exception of <i>Sir Thopas</i> and his prose tales. It is a <a href="/wiki/Decasyllable" title="Decasyllable">decasyllable</a> line, probably borrowed from French and Italian forms, with <a href="/wiki/Riding_rhyme" title="Riding rhyme">riding rhyme</a> and, occasionally, a <a href="/wiki/Caesura" title="Caesura">caesura</a> in the middle of a line. His meter would later develop into the heroic meter of the 15th and 16th centuries and is an ancestor of <a href="/wiki/Iambic_pentameter" title="Iambic pentameter">iambic pentameter</a>. He avoids allowing couplets to become too prominent in the poem, and four of the tales (the Man of Law's, Clerk's, Prioress', and Second Nun's) use <a href="/wiki/Rhyme_royal" title="Rhyme royal">rhyme royal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context_and_themes">Historical context and themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical context and themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DeathWatTylerFull.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1130" data-file-width="1422" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/DeathWatTylerFull.jpg/300px-DeathWatTylerFull.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/DeathWatTylerFull.jpg/450px-DeathWatTylerFull.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/DeathWatTylerFull.jpg/600px-DeathWatTylerFull.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DeathWatTylerFull.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Peasants%27_Revolt" title="Peasants' Revolt">Peasants' Revolt</a> of 1381 is mentioned in the <i>Tales</i>.</div>

</div>
<p><i>The Canterbury Tales</i> was written during a turbulent time in English history. The Catholic Church was in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Schism" title="Western Schism">Western Schism</a> and, though it was still the only Christian authority in Europe, was the subject of heavy controversy. <a href="/wiki/Lollardy" title="Lollardy">Lollardy</a>, an early English religious movement led by <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a>, is mentioned in the <i>Tales</i>, which also mention a specific incident involving pardoners (sellers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indulgences" title="Indulgences">indulgences</a>, which were believed to relieve the temporal punishment due for sins that were already forgiven in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Confession" title="Sacrament of Confession">Sacrament of Confession</a>) who nefariously claimed to be collecting for St. Mary Rouncesval hospital in England. <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is among the first English literary works to mention paper, a relatively new invention that allowed dissemination of the written word never before seen in England. Political clashes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Peasants%27_Revolt" title="Peasants' Revolt">1381 Peasants' Revolt</a> and clashes ending in the deposing of King <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II</a>, further reveal the complex turmoil surrounding Chaucer in the time of the <i>Tales'</i> writing. Many of his close friends were executed and he himself moved to <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> to get away from events in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>While some readers look to interpret the characters of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> as historical figures, other readers choose to interpret its significance in less literal terms. After analysis of Chaucer's diction and historical context, his work appears to develop a critique of society during his lifetime. Within a number of his descriptions, his comments can appear complimentary in nature, but through clever language, the statements are ultimately critical of the pilgrim's actions. It is unclear whether Chaucer would intend for the reader to link his characters with actual persons. Instead, it appears that Chaucer creates fictional characters to be general representations of people in such fields of work. With an understanding of medieval society, one can detect subtle satire at work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Tales</i> reflect diverse views of the Church in Chaucer's England. After the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>, many Europeans began to question the authority of the established Church. Some turned to <a href="/wiki/Lollardy" title="Lollardy">lollardy</a>, while others chose less extreme paths, starting new monastic orders or smaller movements exposing church corruption in the behaviour of the clergy, false <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">church relics</a> or abuse of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indulgences" title="Indulgences">indulgences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Several characters in the <i>Tales</i> are religious figures, and the very setting of the pilgrimage to Canterbury is religious (although the prologue comments ironically on its merely seasonal attractions), making religion a significant theme of the work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two characters, the Pardoner and the Summoner, whose roles apply the Church's secular power, are both portrayed as deeply corrupt, greedy, and abusive. Pardoners in Chaucer's day were those people from whom one bought Church "indulgences" for forgiveness of sins, who were guilty of abusing their office for their own gain. Chaucer's Pardoner openly admits the corruption of his practice while hawking his wares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Summoners were Church officers who brought sinners to the Church court for possible excommunication and other penalties. Corrupt summoners would write false citations and frighten people into bribing them to protect their interests. Chaucer's Summoner is portrayed as guilty of the very kinds of sins for which he is threatening to bring others to court, and is hinted as having a corrupt relationship with the Pardoner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/The_Friar%27s_Tale" title="The Friar's Tale">The Friar's Tale</a>, one of the characters is a summoner who is shown to be working on the side of the devil, not God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Becket_Murder.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="247" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Thomas_Becket_Murder.JPG/220px-Thomas_Becket_Murder.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Thomas_Becket_Murder.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Becket_Murder.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The murder of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Churchmen of various kinds are represented by the Monk, the Prioress, the Nun's Priest, and the Second Nun. Monastic orders, which originated from a desire to follow an ascetic lifestyle separated from the world, had by Chaucer's time become increasingly entangled in worldly matters. Monasteries frequently controlled huge tracts of land on which they made significant sums of money, while peasants worked in their employ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The Second Nun is an example of what a Nun was expected to be: her tale is about a woman whose chaste example brings people into the church. The Monk and the Prioress, on the other hand, while not as corrupt as the Summoner or Pardoner, fall far short of the ideal for their orders. Both are expensively dressed, show signs of lives of luxury and flirtatiousness and show a lack of spiritual depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Prioress%27s_Tale" title="The Prioress's Tale">The Prioress's Tale</a> is an account of Jews murdering a deeply pious and innocent Christian boy, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_libel_against_Jews" title="Blood libel against Jews">blood libel against Jews</a> that became a part of English literary tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The story did not originate in the works of Chaucer and was well known in the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pilgrimage was a very prominent feature of medieval society. The ultimate pilgrimage destination was Jerusalem,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> but within England Canterbury was a popular destination. Pilgrims would journey to cathedrals that preserved relics of saints, believing that such relics held miraculous powers. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Saint Thomas Becket</a>, Archbishop of Canterbury, had been murdered in <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a> by knights of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> during a disagreement between Church and Crown. Miracle stories connected to his remains sprang up soon after his death, and the cathedral became a popular pilgrimage destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The pilgrimage in the work ties all of the stories together and may be considered a representation of Christians' striving for heaven, despite weaknesses, disagreement, and diversity of opinion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_class_and_convention">Social class and convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social class and convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bors_and_Lionel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="408" data-file-width="624" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bors_and_Lionel.jpg/220px-Bors_and_Lionel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bors_and_Lionel.jpg/330px-Bors_and_Lionel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bors_and_Lionel.jpg/440px-Bors_and_Lionel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bors_and_Lionel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bors" title="Bors">Bors</a>' Dilemma – he chooses to save a maiden rather than his brother <a href="/wiki/Sir_Lionel" title="Sir Lionel">Lionel</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The upper class or nobility, represented chiefly by the Knight and his Squire, was in Chaucer's time steeped in a culture of chivalry and courtliness. Nobles were expected to be powerful warriors who could be ruthless on the battlefield yet mannerly in the King's Court and Christian in their actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Knights were expected to form a strong social bond with the men who fought alongside them, but an even stronger bond with a woman whom they idealised to strengthen their fighting ability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Though the aim of chivalry was to noble action, its conflicting values often degenerated into violence. Church leaders frequently tried to place restrictions on jousts and tournaments, which at times ended in the death of the loser. <a href="/wiki/The_Knight%27s_Tale" title="The Knight's Tale">The Knight's Tale</a> shows how the brotherly love of two fellow knights turns into a deadly feud at the sight of a woman whom both idealise. To win her, both are willing to fight to the death. Chivalry was in Chaucer's day on the decline, and it is possible that The Knight's Tale was intended to show its flaws, although this is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Chaucer himself had fought in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_III" title="Edward III">Edward III</a>, who heavily emphasised chivalry during his reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Two tales, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Topas" title="Sir Topas">Sir Topas</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Melibee" title="The Tale of Melibee">The Tale of Melibee</a></i> are told by Chaucer himself, who is travelling with the pilgrims in his own story. Both tales seem to focus on the ill-effects of chivalry—the first making fun of chivalric rules and the second warning against violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Tales</i> constantly reflect the conflict between classes. For example, the division of <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">the three estates</a>: the characters are all divided into three distinct classes, the classes being "those who pray" (the clergy), "those who fight" (the nobility), and "those who work" (the commoners and peasantry).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Most of the tales are interlinked by common themes, and some "quit" (reply to or retaliate against) other tales. Convention is followed when the Knight begins the game with a tale, as he represents the highest social class in the group. But when he is followed by the Miller, who represents a lower class, it sets the stage for the <i>Tales</i> to reflect both a respect for and a disregard for upper class rules. Helen Cooper, as well as Mikhail Bakhtin and Derek Brewer, call this opposition "the ordered and the grotesque, <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a>, officially approved culture and its riotous, and high-spirited underside."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opp_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-opp-54">[54]</a></sup> Several works of the time contained the same opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opp_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-opp-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativism_versus_realism">Relativism versus realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relativism versus realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chaucer's characters each express different—sometimes vastly different—views of reality, creating an atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Playspace" title="Playspace">testing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empathy" title="Empathy">empathy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Relativism" title="Relativism">relativism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empathy_article_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-empathy_article-30">[30]</a></sup> As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helen_Cooper_(professor)" title="Helen Cooper (professor)">Helen Cooper</a> says, "Different genres give different readings of the world: the <a href="/wiki/Fabliau" title="Fabliau">fabliau</a> scarcely notices the operations of God, the saint's life focuses on those at the expense of physical reality, tracts and sermons insist on prudential or orthodox morality, romances privilege human emotion." The sheer number of varying persons and stories renders the <i>Tales</i> as a set unable to arrive at any definite truth or reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liminality">Liminality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Liminality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of <a href="/wiki/Liminality" title="Liminality">liminality</a> figures prominently within <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empathy_article_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-empathy_article-30">[30]</a></sup> A liminal space, which can be both geographical as well as metaphorical or spiritual, is the transitional or transformational space between a “real” (secure, known, limited) world and an unknown or imaginary space of both risk and possibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The notion of a pilgrimage is itself a liminal experience, because it centers on travel between destinations and because pilgrims undertake it hoping to become more holy in the process. Thus, the structure of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> itself is liminal; it not only covers the distance between London and Canterbury, but the majority of the tales refer to places entirely outside the geography of the pilgrimage. Jean Jost summarises the function of liminality in <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>,</p>
<p>"Both appropriately and ironically in this raucous and subversive liminal space, a ragtag assembly gather together and tell their equally unconventional tales. In this unruly place, the rules of tale telling are established, themselves to be both disordered and broken; here the tales of game and earnest, solas and sentence, will be set and interrupted. Here the sacred and profane adventure begins, but does not end. Here, the condition of peril is as prominent as that of protection. The act of pilgrimaging itself consists of moving from one urban space, through liminal rural space, to the next urban space with an ever fluctuating series of events and narratives punctuating those spaces. The goal of pilgrimage may well be a religious or spiritual space at its conclusion, and reflect a psychological progression of the spirit, in yet another kind of emotional space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Liminality is also evident in the individual tales. An obvious instance of this is the <a href="/wiki/The_Friar%27s_Tale" title="The Friar's Tale">Friar’s Tale</a> in which the yeoman devil is a liminal figure because of his transitory nature and function; it is his purpose to issue souls from their current existence to hell, an entirely different one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/The_Franklin%27s_Tale" title="The Franklin's Tale">Franklin’s Tale</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Breton_lai" title="Breton lai">Breton Lai</a> tale, which takes the tale into a liminal space by invoking not only the interaction of the supernatural and the mortal, but also the relation between the present and the imagined past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_literature">Influence on literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influence on literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is sometimes argued that the greatest contribution that this work made to <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> was in popularising the literary use of the <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> English, rather than French or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. English had, however, been used as a literary language for centuries before Chaucer's life, and several of Chaucer's contemporaries—<a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Langland" title="William Langland">William Langland</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pearl_Poet" title="The Pearl Poet">the Pearl Poet</a>—also wrote major literary works in English. It is unclear to what extent Chaucer was responsible for starting a trend rather than simply being part of it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is interesting to note that, although Chaucer had a powerful influence in poetic and artistic terms, which can be seen in the great number of forgeries and mistaken attributions (such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Floure_and_the_Leafe" title="The Floure and the Leafe">The Floure and the Leafe</a></i>, which was translated by <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>), modern English spelling and orthography owe much more to the innovations made by the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Court of Chancery</a> in the decades during and after his lifetime.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>While Chaucer clearly states the addressees of many of his poems (the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Duchess" title="Book of the Duchess">Book of the Duchess</a></i> is believed to have been written for <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt" title="John of Gaunt">John of Gaunt</a> on the occasion of his wife's death in 1368), the intended audience of <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> is more difficult to determine. Chaucer was a <a href="/wiki/Courtier" title="Courtier">courtier</a>, leading some to believe that he was mainly a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_poet" title="Court poet">court poet</a> who wrote exclusively for the nobility. He is referred to as a noble translator and poet by <a href="/wiki/Eustache_Deschamps" title="Eustache Deschamps">Eustache Deschamps</a> and by his contemporary John Gower. It has been suggested that the poem was intended to be read aloud, which is probable as this was a common activity at the time. However, it also seems to have been intended for private reading as well, since Chaucer frequently refers to himself as the writer, rather than the speaker, of the work. Determining the intended audience directly from the text is even more difficult, since the audience is part of the story. This makes it difficult to tell when Chaucer is writing to the fictional pilgrim audience or the actual reader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chaucer's works may have been distributed in some form during his lifetime in part or in whole. Scholars speculate that manuscripts were circulated among his friends, but likely remained unknown to most people until after his death. However, the speed with which copyists strove to write complete versions of his tale in manuscript form shows that Chaucer was a famous and respected poet in his own day. The Hengwrt and Ellesmere manuscripts are examples of the care taken to distribute the work. More manuscript copies of the poem exist than for any other poem of its day except <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Prick_of_Conscience" title="The Prick of Conscience">The Prick of Conscience</a></i>, causing some scholars to give it the medieval equivalent of bestseller status. Even the most elegant of the illustrated manuscripts, however, is not nearly as highly decorated as the work of authors of more respectable works such as <a href="/wiki/John_Lydgate" title="John Lydgate">John Lydgate</a>'s religious and historical literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="15th_century">15th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 15th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>John Lydgate and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Occleve" title="Thomas Occleve">Thomas Occleve</a> were among the first critics of Chaucer's <i>Tales</i>, praising the poet as the greatest English poet of all time and the first to show what the language was truly capable of poetically. This sentiment was universally agreed upon by later critics into the mid-15th century. Glosses included in <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> manuscripts of the time praised him highly for his skill with "sentence" and rhetoric, the two pillars by which medieval critics judged poetry. The most respected of the tales was at this time the Knight's, as it was full of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_additions_and_supplements">Literary additions and supplements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Literary additions and supplements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The incompleteness of the <i>Tales</i> led several medieval authors to write additions and supplements to the tales to make them more complete. Some of the oldest existing manuscripts of the tales include new or modified tales, showing that even early on, such additions were being created. These emendations included various expansions of the <i>Cook's Tale</i>, which Chaucer never finished, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plowman%27s_Tale" title="The Plowman's Tale">The Plowman's Tale</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Gamelyn" title="The Tale of Gamelyn">The Tale of Gamelyn</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Thebes_(Lydgate)" title="Siege of Thebes (Lydgate)">Siege of Thebes</a></i>, and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tale_of_Beryn" title="Tale of Beryn">Tale of Beryn</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Tale of Beryn</i>, written by an anonymous author in the 15th century, is preceded by a lengthy prologue in which the pilgrims arrive at Canterbury and their activities there are described. While the rest of the pilgrims disperse throughout the town, the Pardoner seeks the affections of Kate the barmaid, but faces problems dealing with the man in her life and the innkeeper Harry Bailey. As the pilgrims turn back home, the Merchant restarts the storytelling with <i>Tale of Beryn</i>. In this tale, a young man named Beryn travels from Rome to Egypt to seek his fortune only to be cheated by other businessmen there. He is then aided by a local man in getting his revenge. The tale comes from the French tale <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A9rinus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bérinus (page does not exist)">Bérinus</a></i> and exists in a single early manuscript of the tales, although it was printed along with the tales in a 1721 edition by <a href="/wiki/John_Urry_(literary_editor)" title="John Urry (literary editor)">John Urry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>John Lydgate wrote <i>The Siege of Thebes</i> in about 1420. Like the <i>Tale of Beryn</i>, it is preceded by a prologue in which the pilgrims arrive in Canterbury. Lydgate places himself among the pilgrims as one of them and describes how he was a part of Chaucer's trip and heard the stories. He characterises himself as a monk and tells a long story about the history of Thebes before the events of the <i>Knight's Tale</i>. John Lydgate's tale was popular early on and exists in old manuscripts both on its own and as part of the <i>Tales</i>. It was first printed as early as 1561 by <a href="/wiki/John_Stow" title="John Stow">John Stow</a>, and several editions for centuries after followed suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are actually two versions of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plowman%27s_Tale" title="The Plowman's Tale">The Plowman's Tale</a></i>, both of which are influenced by the story <i><a href="/wiki/Piers_Plowman" title="Piers Plowman">Piers Plowman</a></i>, a work written during Chaucer's lifetime. Chaucer describes a Plowman in the <i>General Prologue</i> of his tales, but never gives him his own tale. One tale, written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Occleve" title="Thomas Occleve">Thomas Occleve</a>, describes the miracle of the Virgin and the Sleeveless Garment. Another tale features a pelican and a griffin debating church corruption, with the pelican taking a position of protest akin to <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a>'s ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Tale of Gamelyn</i> was included in an early manuscript version of the tales, Harley 7334, which is notorious for being one of the lower-quality early manuscripts in terms of editor error and alteration. It is now widely rejected by scholars as an authentic Chaucerian tale, although some scholars think he may have intended to rewrite the story as a tale for the Yeoman. Dates for its authorship vary from 1340 to 1370.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_adaptations">Literary adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Literary adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Many literary works (both fiction and non-fiction alike) have used a similar frame narrative to <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> as an homage. Science-fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Dan_Simmons" title="Dan Simmons">Dan Simmons</a> wrote his <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo Award</a> winning novel <i><a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(Simmons_novel)" title="Hyperion (Simmons novel)">Hyperion</a></i> based on an extra-planetary group of pilgrims. Evolutionary biologist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> used <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> as a structure for his 2004 non-fiction book about <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ancestor%27s_Tale:_A_Pilgrimage_to_the_Dawn_of_Evolution" title="The Ancestor's Tale: A Pilgrimage to the Dawn of Evolution">The Ancestor's Tale: A Pilgrimage to the Dawn of Evolution</a></i>. His animal pilgrims are on their way to find the common ancestor, each telling a tale about evolution.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_Dudeney" title="Henry Dudeney">Henry Dudeney</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Puzzles" title="The Canterbury Puzzles">The Canterbury Puzzles</a></i> contains a part reputedly lost from what modern readers know as Chaucer's tales.</p>
<p>Historical-mystery novelist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.C._Doherty" title="P.C. Doherty">P.C. Doherty</a> wrote a series of novels based on <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>, making use of both the story frame and Chaucer's characters.</p>
<p>Canadian author <a href="/wiki/Angie_Abdou" title="Angie Abdou">Angie Abdou</a> translates <i>The Canterbury Tales</i> to a cross section of people, all snow-sports enthusiasts but from different social backgrounds, converging on a remote back-country ski cabin in British Columbia in the 2011 novel <i>The Canterbury Trail</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_and_homages">Adaptations and homages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Canterbury_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Adaptations and homages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Two_Noble_Kinsmen" title="The Two Noble Kinsmen">The Two Noble Kinsmen</a></i>, by William Shakespeare and John Fletcher, a retelling of "The Knight's Tale", was first performed in 1613 or 1614 and published in 1634. In 1961, <a href="/wiki/Erik_Chisholm" title="Erik Chisholm">Erik Chisholm</a> completed his opera, <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>. The opera is in three acts: The Wyf of Bath’s Tale, The Pardoner’s Tale and The Nun’s Priest’s Tale. <a href="/wiki/Nevill_Coghill" title="Nevill Coghill">Nevill Coghill</a>'s modern English version formed the basis of a musical <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales_(musical)" title="Canterbury Tales (musical)">version</a> that was first staged in 1964.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/A_Canterbury_Tale" title="A Canterbury Tale">A Canterbury Tale</a></i>, a 1944 film jointly written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Powell" title="Michael Powell">Michael Powell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emeric_Pressburger" title="Emeric Pressburger">Emeric Pressburger</a>, is loosely based on the narrative frame of Chaucer's tales. The movie opens with a group of medieval pilgrims journeying through the Kentish countryside as a narrator speaks the opening lines of the <i>General Prologue</i>. The scene then makes a <a href="/wiki/Match_cut#Notable_examples" title="Match cut">now-famous transition</a> to the time of World War II. From that point on, the film follows a group of strangers, each with his or her own story and in need of some kind of redemption, who are making their way to Canterbury together. The film's main story takes place in an imaginary town in Kent and ends with the main characters arriving at Canterbury Cathedral, bells pealing and Chaucer's words again resounding. <i>A Canterbury Tale</i> is recognised as one of the Powell-Pressburger team's most poetic and artful films. It was produced as wartime propaganda, using Chaucer's poetry, referring to the famous pilgrimage, and offering photography of Kent to remind the public of what made Britain worth fighting for. In one scene a local historian lectures an audience of British soldiers about the pilgrims of Chaucer's time and the vibrant history of England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">Pier Paolo Pasolini</a>'s 1972 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales_(film)" title="The Canterbury Tales (film)">The Canterbury Tales</a></i> features several of the tales, some of which keep close to the original tale and some of which are embellished. The <i>Cook's Tale</i>, for instance, which is incomplete in the original version, is expanded into a full story, and the <i>Friar's Tale</i> extends the scene in which the Summoner is dragged down to hell. The film includes these two tales as well as the <i>Miller's Tale</i>, the <i>Summoner's Tale</i>, the <i>Wife of Bath's Tale</i>, and the <i>Merchant's Tale</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 26, 1986, American radio personality <a href="/wiki/Garrison_Keillor" title="Garrison Keillor">Garrison Keillor</a> opened "The News from Lake Wobegon" portion of the first live TV broadcast of his <i><a href="/wiki/A_Prairie_Home_Companion" title="A Prairie Home Companion">A Prairie Home Companion</a></i> radio show with a reading of the original Middle English text of the General Prologue. He commented, "Although those words were written more than 600 years ago, they still describe spring."</p>
<p>English rock musician <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> paid tribute to Chaucer and the book with his 1993 concept album <i><a href="/wiki/Ten_Summoner%27s_Tales" title="Ten Summoner's Tales">Ten Summoner's Tales</a></i>, which he described as ten songs (plus an epilogue number) with no theme or subject tying them together. Sting's real name is Gordon Sumner, hence the reference to the "Summoner" character in the record's title. In essence, the collection of songs was composed as "a musical Canterbury Tales".</p>
<p>Several more recent films, while they are not based on the tales, do have references to them. For example, in the 1995 film <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_(1995_film)" title="Seven (1995 film)">Se7en</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parson%27s_Tale" title="Parson's Tale">Parson's Tale</a></i> is an important clue to the methods of a serial killer who chooses his victims based on the <a href="/wiki/Seven_deadly_sins" title="Seven deadly sins">seven deadly sins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The 2001 film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Knight%27s_Tale_(film)" title="A Knight's Tale (film)">A Knight's Tale</a></i> took its name from "The Knight's Tale". Although it bears little resemblance to the tale, it does feature what Martha Driver and Sid Ray call an "MTV-generation" Chaucer who is a gambling addict with a way with words. Scattered references to the <i>Tales</i> include Chaucer's declaration that he will use his verse to vilify a summoner and a pardoner who have cheated him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Television adaptations include <a href="/wiki/Alan_Plater" title="Alan Plater">Alan Plater</a>'s 1975 re-telling of the stories in a series of plays for BBC2: <i><a href="/wiki/Trinity_Tales" title="Trinity Tales">Trinity Tales</a></i>. In 2003, BBC again featured <a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales_(TV_series)" title="The Canterbury Tales (TV series)">modern re-tellings of selected tales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2014 young adult short novel <i>Anaheim Tales</i> by M.L. Millard was inspired by and references <i>The Canterbury Tales</i>.</p>

<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Rearden, p. 458. <i>There can be no moral doubt but that Chaucer knew Petrarch personally. They were both in France many times, where they might have met. They were both courtiers. They both had an enthusiasm for scholarship. Whether they met then, or whether Chaucer, when on his visit to Genoa, specially visited the Italian, it does not appear.</i> <i>...but the only reason that such a visit could not have occurred lies in the fact that Petrarch himself does not record it. Still, on the other hand, would he have mentioned the visit of a man who was the servant of a barbarous monarch, and whose only claim to notice, literary-wise, was his cultivation of an unknown and uncouth dialect that was half bastard French?</i></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Skeat (1874), p. xxx. <i>And we know that Petrarch, on his own shewing, was so pleased with the story of Griselda that he learnt it by heart as well as he could, for the express purpose of repeating it to friends, before the idea of turning it into Latin occurred to him. Whence we may conclude that Chaucer and Petrarch met at Padua early in 1373; that Petrarch told Chaucer the story by word of mouth, either in Italian or French; and that Chaucer shortly after obtained a copy of Petrarch's Latin version, which he kept constantly before him whilst making his own translation.</i></span></li>
