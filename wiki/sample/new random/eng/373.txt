<p><b>Balmoral Castle</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɒr/ 'or' in 'moral'">ɒr</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estate_house" title="Estate house">estate house</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Deeside" title="Royal Deeside">Royal Deeside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, Scotland, near the village of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crathie" title="Crathie">Crathie</a>, 6.2 miles (10.0 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Ballater" title="Ballater">Ballater</a> and 6.8 miles (10.9 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Braemar" title="Braemar">Braemar</a>.</p>
<p>Balmoral has been one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_British_royal_residences" title="List of British royal residences">residences</a> for members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Royal_Family" title="British Royal Family">British Royal Family</a> since 1852, when the estate and its original castle were purchased privately by <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Prince_Consort" title="Albert, Prince Consort">Prince Albert</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Consort" title="Prince Consort">consort</a> to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>. They remain as the private property of the royal family and are not the property of <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a>.</p>
<p>Soon after the estate was purchased by the royal family, the existing house was found to be too small and the current Balmoral Castle was commissioned. The architect was <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_(architect)" title="William Smith (architect)">William Smith</a> of Aberdeen, although his designs were amended by Prince Albert.</p>
<p>The castle is an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scots_Baronial" title="Scots Baronial">Scots Baronial</a> architecture, and is classified by <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_A_listed" title="Category A listed">category A listed</a> building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The new castle was completed in 1856 and the old castle demolished shortly thereafter.</p>
<p>The Balmoral <a href="/wiki/Estate_(land)" title="Estate (land)">Estate</a> has been added to by successive members of the royal family, and now covers an area of approximately 50,000 acres (20,000 ha). It is a working estate, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Grouse" title="Red Grouse">grouse moors</a>, forestry, and farmland, as well as managed herds of deer, <a href="/wiki/Highland_cattle" title="Highland cattle">Highland cattle</a>, and ponies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="1147" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg/220px-Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg/330px-Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg/440px-Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Gordon_Litho.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
After 1830, Sir Robert Gordon made major alterations to the original castle - <i>lithograph by Josef Kriehuber, 1846</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_II_of_Scotland" title="Robert II of Scotland">King Robert II</a> of Scotland (1316–1390) had a <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting lodge</a> in the area. Historical records also indicate that a house at Balmoral was built by Sir William Drummond in 1390.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The estate is recorded in 1451 as "Bouchmorale", and later was tenanted by Alexander Gordon, second son of the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gordon,_1st_Earl_of_Huntly" title="Alexander Gordon, 1st Earl of Huntly">1st Earl of Huntly</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Tower_houses_in_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Tower houses in Britain and Ireland">tower house</a> was built on the estate by the Gordons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1662 the estate passed to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Farquharson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Farquharson (page does not exist)">Charles Farquharson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Inverey" title="Inverey">Inverey</a>, brother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Farquharson,_3rd_of_Inverey" title="John Farquharson, 3rd of Inverey">John Farquharson</a>, the "Black Colonel". The Farquharsons were <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> sympathisers, and James Farquharson of Balmoral was involved in both the 1715 and 1745 <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_risings" title="Jacobite risings">rebellions</a>. He was wounded at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Falkirk_(1746)" title="Battle of Falkirk (1746)">Battle of Falkirk</a> in 1746. The Farquharson estates were <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/forfeit" title="wikt:forfeit">forfeit</a>, and passed to the Farquharsons of <a href="/wiki/Braemar" title="Braemar">Auchendryne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gordon_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gordon-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1798, <a href="/wiki/James_Duff,_2nd_Earl_Fife" title="James Duff, 2nd Earl Fife">James Duff, 2nd Earl Fife</a>, acquired Balmoral and leased the castle. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gordon_(diplomat)" title="Robert Gordon (diplomat)">Sir Robert Gordon</a>, a younger brother of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gordon,_4th_Earl_of_Aberdeen" title="George Gordon, 4th Earl of Aberdeen">4th Earl of Aberdeen</a>, acquired the lease in 1830. He made major alterations to the original castle at Balmoral, including baronial-style extensions that were designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(architect)" title="John Smith (architect)">John Smith</a> of Aberdeen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_acquisition">Royal acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Royal acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Queen Victoria and Prince Albert first visited Scotland in 1842, five years after her accession to the throne and two years after their marriage. During this first visit they stayed at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Taymouth_Castle" title="Taymouth Castle">Taymouth Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Perthshire" title="Perthshire">Perthshire</a>, the home of the <a href="/wiki/John_Campbell,_2nd_Marquess_of_Breadalbane" title="John Campbell, 2nd Marquess of Breadalbane">Marquess of Breadalbane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup> They returned in 1844 to stay at <a href="/wiki/Blair_Castle" title="Blair Castle">Blair Castle</a> and, in 1847, when they rented Ardverikie by <a href="/wiki/Loch_Laggan" title="Loch Laggan">Loch Laggan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> During the latter trip they encountered weather that was extremely rainy, which led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Clark_(physician)" title="James Clark (physician)">Sir James Clark</a>, the queen's physician, to recommend <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Aberdeenshire" title="River Dee, Aberdeenshire">Deeside</a> instead, for its more healthy climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sir Robert Gordon died in 1847 and his lease on Balmoral reverted to Lord Aberdeen. In February 1848 an arrangement was made—that Prince Albert would acquire the remaining part of the lease on Balmoral, together with its furniture and staff—without having seen the property first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dronning_victoria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1441" data-file-width="1212" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dronning_victoria.jpg/220px-Dronning_victoria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dronning_victoria.jpg/330px-Dronning_victoria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dronning_victoria.jpg/440px-Dronning_victoria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dronning_victoria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coronation portrait of Victoria - <i>by George Hayter, 1837</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The royal couple arrived for their first visit on 8 September 1848.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Victoria found the house "small but pretty",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and recorded in her diary that: "All seemed to breathe freedom and peace, and to make one forget the world and its sad turmoils".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gordon_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gordon-4">[4]</a></sup> The surrounding hilly landscape reminded them of <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, Albert's homeland in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup></p>
<p>Quickly, the house was confirmed to be too small and, in 1848, John and William Smith were commissioned to design new offices, cottages, and other ancillary buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millar55_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-millar55-10">[10]</a></sup> Improvements to the woodlands, gardens, and estate buildings also were being made, with the assistance of the landscape gardener, James Beattie, and possibly by the painter, <a href="/wiki/James_Giles_(painter)" title="James Giles (painter)">James Giles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Major additions to the old house were considered in 1849,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millar55_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-millar55-10">[10]</a></sup> but by then negotiations were under way to purchase the estate from the trustees of the deceased Earl Fife. After seeing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrugated_iron" title="Corrugated iron">corrugated iron</a> cottage at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Exhibition_of_1851" title="Great Exhibition of 1851">Great Exhibition of 1851</a>, Prince Albert ordered a pre-fabricated iron building for Balmoral from E. T. Bellhouse &amp; Co., to serve as a temporary ballroom and dining room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was in use by 1 October 1851, and would serve as a ballroom until 1856.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millar.2C_p.59_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millar.2C_p.59-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sale was completed in June 1852, the price being £32,000, and Prince Albert formally took possession that autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millar56_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-millar56-13">[13]</a></sup> The neighbouring estate of <a href="/wiki/Birkhall" title="Birkhall">Birkhall</a> was bought at the same time, and the lease on <a href="/wiki/Abergeldie_Castle" title="Abergeldie Castle">Abergeldie Castle</a> secured as well. To mark the occasion, the <i>Purchase Cairn</i> was erected in the hills overlooking the castle, the first of many.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_of_the_new_house">Construction of the new house</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction of the new house">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balmoral_Castle_-_Project_Gutenberg_13103.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="354" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Balmoral_Castle_-_Project_Gutenberg_13103.jpg/250px-Balmoral_Castle_-_Project_Gutenberg_13103.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Balmoral_Castle_-_Project_Gutenberg_13103.jpg 1.5x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balmoral_Castle_-_Project_Gutenberg_13103.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Balmoral Castle</i> - a principal <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keep</a> similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Craigievar_Castle" title="Craigievar Castle">Craigievar Castle</a> is the central feature of the castle, while a large <a href="/wiki/Turret" title="Turret">turreted</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_estate_houses" title="Scottish estate houses">country house</a> is attached</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The growing family of Victoria and Albert, the need for additional staff, and the quarters required for visiting friends and official visitors such as cabinet members, however, meant that extension of the existing structure would not be sufficient and that a larger house needed to be built. In early 1852, this was commissioned from William Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millar56_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-millar56-13">[13]</a></sup> The son of John Smith (who designed the 1830 alterations of the original castle), William Smith was city architect of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> from 1852. On learning of the commission, <a href="/wiki/William_Burn" title="William Burn">William Burn</a> sought an interview with the prince, apparently to complain that Smith previously had plagiarised his work, however, Burn was unsuccessful in depriving Smith of the appointment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> William Smith's designs were amended by Prince Albert, who took a close interest in details such as turrets and windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:179px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balmoral_by_queen.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="182" data-file-width="177" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Balmoral_by_queen.JPG" width="177"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balmoral_by_queen.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Balmoral Castle</i>, painted by Queen Victoria in 1854 during its construction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Construction began during summer 1853, on a site some 100 yards (91 m) northwest of the original building that was considered to have a better vista.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Another reason for consideration was, that whilst construction was ongoing, the family would still be able to use the old house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9</sup> Queen Victoria laid the foundation stone on 28 September 1853, during her annual autumn visit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> By the autumn of 1855, the royal apartments were ready for occupancy, although the tower was still under construction and the servants had to be lodged in the old house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millar.2C_p.59_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Millar.2C_p.59-12">[12]</a></sup> By coincidence, shortly after their arrival at the estate that autumn, news circulated about the fall of <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Sevastopol_(1854%E2%80%9355)" title="Siege of Sevastopol (1854–55)">Sevastopol</a>, ending the Crimean War, resulting in wild celebrations by royalty and locals alike. While visiting the estate shortly thereafter, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_German_Emperor" title="Frederick III, German Emperor">Prince Frederick of Prussia</a> asked for the hand of <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Princess_Royal" title="Victoria, Princess Royal">Princess Victoria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG"><img alt="Photograph of a seated Victoria, dressed in black, holding an infant with her children and Prince Albert standing around her" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="2207" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG/220px-Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG/330px-Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG/440px-Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Queen_Victoria_Prince_Albert_and_their_nine_children.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
With their nine children, 1857 - <i>left to right</i>: Alice, Arthur, Prince Albert, Edward, Leopold, Louise, Queen Victoria holding Beatrice, Alfred, Victoria, and Helena</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The new house was completed in 1856, and the old castle subsequently was demolished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup> By autumn 1857, a new bridge across the Dee, designed by <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a> linking Crathie and Balmoral was finished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup></p>
<p>Balmoral Castle is built from <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> quarried at Invergelder on the estate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It consists of two main blocks, each arranged around a courtyard. The southwestern block contains the main rooms, while the northeastern contains the service wings. At the southeast is an 80-foot (24 m) tall clock tower topped with turrets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glendinning_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-glendinning-19">[19]</a></sup> one of which has a balustrade similar to a feature at <a href="/wiki/Castle_Fraser" title="Castle Fraser">Castle Fraser</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenwick_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-fenwick-20">[20]</a></sup> Being similar in style to the demolished castle of the 1830s, the architecture of the new house is considered to be somewhat dated for its time when contrasted with the richer forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scots_Baronial" title="Scots Baronial">Scots Baronial</a> being developed by William Burn and others during the 1850s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glendinning_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-glendinning-19">[19]</a></sup> As an exercise in Scots Baronial, it sometimes is described as being too ordered, pedantic, and even, <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Germany" title="Architecture of Germany">Germanic</a>—as a consequence of Prince Albert's influence on the design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenwick_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-fenwick-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The purchase of a Scottish estate by Victoria and Albert and their adoption of a Scottish architectural style, however, was very influential for the ongoing revival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_culture" title="Highland culture">Highland culture</a>. They decorated Balmoral with tartans and attended <a href="/wiki/Highland_games" title="Highland games">highland games</a> at Braemar. Queen Victoria expressed an affinity for Scotland, even professing herself to be a <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Added to the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" title="Sir Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>, this became a major factor in promoting the adoption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_culture" title="Highland culture">Highland culture</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland_Scotland" title="Lowland Scotland">Lowland Scots</a>. Historian Michael Lynch comments that "the Scottishness of Balmoral helped to give the monarchy a truly British dimension for the first time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victoria_and_Albert_at_Balmoral">Victoria and Albert at Balmoral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Victoria and Albert at Balmoral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BalmoralCastle1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2524" data-file-width="3520" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BalmoralCastle1900.jpg/220px-BalmoralCastle1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BalmoralCastle1900.jpg/330px-BalmoralCastle1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BalmoralCastle1900.jpg/440px-BalmoralCastle1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BalmoralCastle1900.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Balmoral, c.1890–1900</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Even before the completion of the new house, the pattern of the life of the royal couple in the Highlands was soon established. Victoria took long walks of up to four hours daily and Albert spent many days hunting deer and game. In 1849, diarist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Greville_(diarist)" title="Charles Greville (diarist)">Charles Greville</a> described their life at Balmoral as resembling that of gentry rather than royalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Victoria began a policy of commissioning artists to record Balmoral, its surroundings, and its staff. Over the years, numerous painters were employed at Balmoral, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Landseer" title="Edwin Landseer">Edwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Landseer" title="Charles Landseer">Charles Landseer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Haag" title="Carl Haag">Carl Haag</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wyld" title="William Wyld">William Wyld</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Fisk" title="William Henry Fisk">William Henry Fisk</a>, and many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The royal couple took great interest in their staff. They established a lending library.</p>
<p>During the 1850s, new plantations were established near the house and exotic conifers were planted on the grounds. Prince Albert had an active role in these improvements, overseeing the design of <a href="/wiki/Parterre" title="Parterre">parterres</a>, the diversion of the main road north of the river via a new bridge, and plans for farm buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup> These buildings included a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_farm" title="Model farm">model dairy</a> that he developed during 1861, the year of his death. The dairy was completed by Victoria. Subsequently, she also built several monuments to her husband on the estate. These include a pyramid-shaped cairn built a year after Albert's death, on top of <i>Craig Lurachain</i>. A large statue of Albert with a dog and a gun by <a href="/wiki/William_Theed" title="William Theed">William Theed</a>, was inaugurated on 15 October 1867, the twenty-eighth anniversary of their engagement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:20–21</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="3456" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG/220px-Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG/330px-Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG/440px-Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cairn_for_Prince_Albert_Balmoral.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Memorial cairn for Prince Albert, Balmoral Estate</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following Albert's death, Victoria spent increasing periods at Balmoral, staying for as long as four months a year during early summer and autumn. Few further changes were made to the grounds, with the exception of some alterations to mountain paths, the erection of various cairns and monuments, and the addition of some cottages (<i>Karim Cottage</i> and <i>Baile na Coille</i>) built for senior staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18</sup> It was during this period that Victoria began to depend on her servant, <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(servant)" title="John Brown (servant)">John Brown</a>. He was a local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghillie" title="Ghillie">ghillie</a> from Crathie, who became one of her closest companions during her long mourning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:23</sup></p>
<p>In 1887, Balmoral Castle was the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Eugenie_of_Battenberg" title="Victoria Eugenie of Battenberg">Victoria Eugenie</a>, a granddaughter of Queen Victoria. She was born to <a href="/wiki/Princess_Beatrice_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Princess Beatrice of the United Kingdom">Princess Beatrice</a>, the fifth daughter of Victoria and Albert. Victoria Eugenie would become the queen of Spain.</p>
<p>In September 1896, Victoria welcomed <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Emperor Nicholas II of Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandra_Feodorovna_(Alix_of_Hesse)" title="Alexandra Feodorovna (Alix of Hesse)">Empress Alexandra</a> to Balmoral. Four years later Victoria made her last visit to the estate, three months before her death on 22 January 1901.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Victoria">After Victoria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After Victoria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1036" data-file-width="1256" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg/220px-EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg/330px-EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg/440px-EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EdwardVII_at_Balmoral.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Edward VII</a> relaxing at Balmoral Castle</i> - photograph by his wife, Alexandra, c. 1907-1908</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After Victoria's death, the royal family continued to use Balmoral during annual autumn visits. <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> had substantial improvements made during the 1910s and 1920s, including formal gardens to the south of the castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, royal visits to Balmoral ceased. In addition, due to the enmity with Germany, <i>Danzig Shiel</i>, a lodge built by Victoria in Ballochbuie was renamed <i>Garbh Allt Shiel</i> and the "King of Prussia's Fountain" was removed from the grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:25</sup></p>
<p>Since the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip</a> has added herbaceous borders and a water garden. During the 1980s new staff buildings were built close to the castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-garden-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Balmoral is a private property and, unlike the monarch's <a href="/wiki/Official_residence" title="Official residence">official residences</a>, is not the property of <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a>. It originally was purchased personally by Prince Albert, rather than the queen, meaning that no revenues from the estate go to Parliament or to the public purse, as otherwise in accord with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_List_Act_1760&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Civil List Act 1760 (page does not exist)">Civil List Act 1760</a> would be the case for property owned outright by the monarch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wightman_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wightman-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Sandringham_House" title="Sandringham House">Sandringham House</a> in Norfolk, ownership of Balmoral was inherited by <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">Edward VIII</a> on his accession in 1936. When he <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_abdication_crisis" title="Edward VIII abdication crisis">abdicated</a> later the same year, however, he retained ownership of them. A financial settlement was devised, under which Balmoral and Sandringham were purchased by Edward's brother and successor to the Crown, <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently, the estate is still owned outright by the monarch, but, by Trustees under Deeds of Nomination and Appointment, it is managed by a trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wightman_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wightman-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_estate">The estate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The estate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_extent_and_operation">Current extent and operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current extent and operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Balmoral Estate is within the <a href="/wiki/Cairngorms_National_Park" title="Cairngorms National Park">Cairngorms National Park</a> and is partly within the Deeside and Lochnagar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Scenic_Area_(Scotland)" title="National Scenic Area (Scotland)">National Scenic Area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cons_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cons-28">[28]</a></sup> The 50,000-acre (20,000 ha) estate contains a wide variety of landscapes, from the <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Aberdeenshire" title="River Dee, Aberdeenshire">Dee river valley</a> to open mountains. There are seven <a href="/wiki/Munro" title="Munro">Munros</a> (hills in Scotland over 3,000 feet (910 m)) within the estate, the highest being <a href="/wiki/Lochnagar" title="Lochnagar">Lochnagar</a> at 3,789 feet (1,155 m). This mountain was the setting for a children's story, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Man_of_Lochnagar" title="The Old Man of Lochnagar">The Old Man of Lochnagar</a></i>, told originally by <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> to his younger brothers, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Andrew,_Duke_of_York" title="Prince Andrew, Duke of York">Andrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex">Edward</a>. The story was published in 1980, with royalties accruing to <a href="/wiki/The_Prince%27s_Trust" title="The Prince's Trust">The Prince's Trust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:35–51</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The estate also incorporates the 7,500-acre <a href="/wiki/Delnadamph_Lodge" title="Delnadamph Lodge">Delnadamph Lodge</a> estate, bought by Elizabeth II in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TimesJan78_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-TimesJan78-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The estate extends to <a href="/wiki/Loch_Muick" title="Loch Muick">Loch Muick</a> in the southeast where an old boat house and the Royal <a href="/wiki/Bothy" title="Bothy">Bothy</a> (hunting lodge) now named <i>Glas-allt Shiel</i>, built by Victoria, are located.</p>
<p>The working estate includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Grouse" title="Red Grouse">grouse moors</a>, forestry, and farmland, as well as managed herds of deer, <a href="/wiki/Highland_cattle" title="Highland cattle">Highland cattle</a>, and ponies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:38–47</sup> It also offers access to the public for fishing (paid) and hiking during certain seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36–37</sup></p>
<p>Approximately 8,000 acres of the estate are covered by trees, with almost 3,000 acres used for forestry that yields nearly 10,000 tonnes of wood per year. <i>Ballochbuie Forest</i>, one of the largest remaining areas of old Caledonian pine growth in Scotland, consists of approximately 3,000 acres. It is managed with only minimal or no intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:48,51</sup> The principal mammal on the estate is the <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> with a population of 2,000 to 2,500 head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup></p>
<p>The areas of Lochnagar and Ballochbuie were designated in 1998 by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Protection_Areas" title="Special Protection Areas">Special Protection Areas</a> (SPA) under the European Union (EU) <a href="/wiki/Birds_Directive" title="Birds Directive">Birds Directive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Bird species inhabiting the moorlands include <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_grouse" title="Black grouse">black grouse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptarmigan" title="Ptarmigan">ptarmigan</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capercaillie" title="Capercaillie">capercaillie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:38</sup> Ballochbuie also is protected as a <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> by the EU <a href="/wiki/Habitats_Directive" title="Habitats Directive">Habitats Directive</a>, as "one of the largest remaining continuous areas of native <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Forest" title="Caledonian Forest">Caledonian Forest</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In addition, there are four <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">sites of special scientific interest</a> on the estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cons_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-cons-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The royal family employs approximately 50 full-time and 50–100 part-time staff to maintain the working estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Single_malt_whisky" title="Single malt whisky">malt whisky</a> distillery located on the Balmoral Estate produces the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Lochnagar_Single_Malt" title="Royal Lochnagar Single Malt">Royal Lochnagar Single Malt</a> whisky.</p>
<p>There are approximately 150 buildings on the estate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmoral_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-Balmoral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:35</sup> including <a href="/wiki/Birkhall" title="Birkhall">Birkhall</a>, formerly home to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_The_Queen_Mother" title="Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother">Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother</a>, and used now by Prince Charles and the <a href="/wiki/Camilla,_Duchess_of_Cornwall" title="Camilla, Duchess of Cornwall">Duchess of Cornwall</a> for their summer holidays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Craigowan Lodge is used regularly by the family and friends of the royal family and also has been used while Balmoral Castle was being prepared for a royal visit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mail_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mail-36">[36]</a></sup> Six smaller buildings on the estate are let as <a href="/wiki/Holiday_cottage" title="Holiday cottage">holiday cottages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_access_to_gardens_and_castle_grounds">Public access to gardens and castle grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public access to gardens and castle grounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1931, the castle gardens were opened to the public for the first time and they now are open daily between April and the end of July, after which Queen Elizabeth arrives for her annual stay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mail_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-mail-36">[36]</a></sup> The ballroom is the only room in the castle that may be viewed by the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Craigowan_Lodge">Craigowan Lodge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Craigowan Lodge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Craigowan Lodge is a seven-bedroom<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stv_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-stv-39">[39]</a></sup> stone house approximately a mile from the main castle in Balmoral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> More rustic than the castle, the lodge was often the home of Charles and Diana when they visited. Currently, it is used as quarters for important guests.</p>
<p>In the obituary of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Michael_Andreevich_of_Russia" title="Prince Michael Andreevich of Russia">Michael Andreevich Romanoff</a>, the highest-ranking member of the Russian imperial family at the time of his death in 2008, it was noted that his family spent most of World War II at Craigowan Lodge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lodge has been in the news periodically since 2005, because Queen Elizabeth often spends the first few days of her summer holiday there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stv_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-stv-39">[39]</a></sup> During each weekend of the summer the castle is a lucrative source of income from visiting tourists. Sometimes, the Queen arrives at Balmoral before the tourist visiting season is over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Queen Elizabeth was in residence at Balmoral at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Death of Diana, Princess of Wales">death of Diana, Princess of Wales</a> in 1997. Her private discussions with Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> were dramatised in the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Frears" title="Stephen Frears">Stephen Frears</a> film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_(film)" title="The Queen (film)">The Queen</a></i> (2006). The 1997 film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mrs._Brown_(film)" title="Mrs. Brown (film)">Mrs. Brown</a></i> also was based on events at Balmoral. In both films, however, substitute locations were used: <a href="/wiki/Blairquhan_Castle" title="Blairquhan Castle">Blairquhan Castle</a> in <i>The Queen</i>; and <a href="/wiki/Duns_Castle" title="Duns Castle">Duns Castle</a> in <i>Mrs Brown</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Banknote_illustration">Banknote illustration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balmoral_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Banknote illustration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1987 an illustration of the castle has been featured on the reverse side of <a href="/wiki/Banknotes_of_the_pound_sterling" title="Banknotes of the pound sterling">£100 notes</a> issued by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Scotland" title="Royal Bank of Scotland">Royal Bank of Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotbanks_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-scotbanks-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
