<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alban Berg</h1>

<p><b>Alban Maria Johannes Berg</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɛr/ 'err' in 'merry'">ɛr</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈbɛɐ̯k]</a></span>; February 9, 1885 – December 24, 1935) was an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Viennese_School" title="Second Viennese School">Second Viennese School</a>. His compositional style combined <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> lyricism with <a href="/wiki/Twelve-tone_technique" title="Twelve-tone technique">twelve-tone technique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baron2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baron2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Berg was born in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, the third of four children of Johanna and Conrad Berg. His family lived comfortably until the death of his father in 1900.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was more interested in <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> than <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> as a child and did not begin to compose until he was fifteen, when he started to teach himself music. In late February or early March 1902 he fathered a child with Marie Scheuchl, a servant girl in the Berg family household. His daughter, Albine, was born on December 4, 1902.</p>
<p>Berg had little formal music education before he became a student of <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a> in October 1904. With Schoenberg he studied <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">counterpoint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_theory" title="Music theory">music theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By 1906, he was studying music full-time; by 1907, he began <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">composition</a> lessons. His student compositions included five drafts for <a href="/wiki/Piano_sonata" title="Piano sonata">piano sonatas</a>. He also wrote songs, including his <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Early_Songs_(Berg)" title="Seven Early Songs (Berg)">Seven Early Songs</a></i> (<i>Sieben Frühe Lieder</i>), three of which were Berg's first publicly performed work in a concert that featured the music of Schoenberg's pupils in Vienna that year. The early sonata sketches eventually culminated in Berg's <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_(Berg)" title="Piano Sonata (Berg)">Piano Sonata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opus_number" title="Opus number">Op</a>. 1 (1907–1908); it is one of the most formidable "first" works ever written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Berg studied with Schoenberg for six years until 1911. Berg admired him as a composer and mentor, and they remained close lifelong friends.</p>
<p>Among Schoenberg's teaching was the idea that the unity of a musical composition depends upon all its aspects being derived from a single basic idea; this idea was later known as <i>developing variation</i>. Berg passed this on to his students, one of whom, <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">Theodor W. Adorno</a>, stated: "The main principle he conveyed was that of variation: everything was supposed to develop out of something else and yet be intrinsically different".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Piano Sonata is an example—the whole composition is derived from the work's opening <a href="/wiki/Quartal_and_quintal_harmony" title="Quartal and quintal harmony">quartal</a> gesture and its opening phrase.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovation">Innovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Innovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Berg was a part of Vienna's cultural elite during the heady <i><a href="/wiki/Fin_de_si%C3%A8cle" title="Fin de siècle">fin de siècle</a></i> period. His circle included the musicians <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Zemlinsky" title="Alexander von Zemlinsky">Alexander von Zemlinsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schreker" title="Franz Schreker">Franz Schreker</a>, the painter <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Klimt" title="Gustav Klimt">Gustav Klimt</a>, the writer and satirist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kraus_(writer)" title="Karl Kraus (writer)">Karl Kraus</a>, the architect <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Loos" title="Adolf Loos">Adolf Loos</a>, and the poet <a href="/wiki/Peter_Altenberg" title="Peter Altenberg">Peter Altenberg</a>. In 1906, Berg met the singer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helene_Nahowski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helene Nahowski (page does not exist)">Helene Nahowski</a>, daughter of a wealthy family (said by some to be in fact the illegitimate daughter of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph I of Austria</a> from his liaison with <a href="/wiki/Anna_Nahowski" title="Anna Nahowski">Anna Nahowski</a>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> despite the outward hostility of her family, the two were married on May 3, 1911.</p>

<p>In 1913, two of Berg's <i><a href="/wiki/Altenberg_Lieder" title="Altenberg Lieder">Five Songs on Picture Postcard Texts by Peter Altenberg</a></i> (1912) were premièred in Vienna, <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conducted</a> by Schoenberg in the infamous <i><a href="/wiki/Skandalkonzert" title="Skandalkonzert">Skandalkonzert</a></i>. Settings of aphoristic poetic utterances, the songs are accompanied by a very large orchestra. The performance caused a riot, and had to be halted. This was a crippling blow to Berg's self-confidence: he effectively withdrew the work, which is surely<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (March 2014)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> one of his most innovative and assured first orchestral compositions in the literature, and it was not performed in full until 1952. The full score remained unpublished until 1966.</p>
<p>From 1915 to 1918, Berg served in the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army" title="Austro-Hungarian Army">Austro-Hungarian Army</a> and during a period of leave in 1917 he accelerated work on his first <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wozzeck" title="Wozzeck">Wozzeck</a></i>. After the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, he settled again in Vienna, where he taught private pupils. He also helped Schoenberg run his <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Private_Musical_Performances" title="Society for Private Musical Performances">Society for Private Musical Performances</a>, which sought to create the ideal environment for the exploration and appreciation of unfamiliar new music by means of open rehearsals, repeat performances, and the exclusion of professional critics.</p>
<p>Berg had a particular interest in the number 23, using it to structure several works. Various suggestions have been made as to the reason for this interest: that he took it from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biorhythms" title="Biorhythms">Biorhythms</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Fliess" title="Wilhelm Fliess">Wilhelm Fliess</a>, in which a 23-day cycle is considered significant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or because he first suffered an asthma attack on 23rd of the month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Success_of_Wozzeck_and_inception_of_Lulu_1925.E2.80.9329">Success of <i>Wozzeck</i> and inception of <i>Lulu</i> 1925–29</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Success of Wozzeck and inception of Lulu 1925–29">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three excerpts from <i>Wozzeck</i> were performed in 1924, and this brought Berg his first public success. The opera, which Berg completed in 1922, was first performed on December 14, 1925, when <a href="/wiki/Erich_Kleiber" title="Erich Kleiber">Erich Kleiber</a> conducted the first performance in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. Today <i>Wozzeck</i> is seen as one of the century's most important works. Berg made a start on his second opera, the three-act <i><a href="/wiki/Lulu_(opera)" title="Lulu (opera)">Lulu</a></i>, in 1928 but interrupted the work in 1929 for the concert aria <i>Der Wein</i> which he completed that summer. <i>Der Wein</i> presaged <i>Lulu</i> in a number of ways, including vocal style, orchestration, design and text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElliott2014.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.ca.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DqrkTAwAAQBAJ.26pg.3DPA55_p..C2.A055.5D_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElliott2014.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.ca.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DqrkTAwAAQBAJ.26pg.3DPA55_p..C2.A055.5D-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other well-known Berg compositions include the <i><a href="/wiki/Lyric_Suite_(Berg)" title="Lyric Suite (Berg)">Lyric Suite</a></i> (1926), which was later shown to employ elaborate cyphers to document a secret love affair; the post-Mahlerian <i>Three Pieces for Orchestra</i> (completed in 1915 but not performed until after <i>Wozzeck</i>); and the <a href="/wiki/Kammerkonzert_(Berg)" title="Kammerkonzert (Berg)">Chamber Concerto</a> (<i>Kammerkonzert</i>, 1923–25) for violin, piano, and 13 wind instruments: this latter is written so conscientiously that Pierre Boulez has called it "Berg's strictest composition" and it, too, is permeated by cyphers and posthumously disclosed hidden programs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_1930-35">Final years 1930-35</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Final years 1930-35">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Life for the musical world was becoming increasingly difficult in the 1930s both in Vienna and Germany due to the rising tide of <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitism</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> cultural ideology that denounced <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>. Even to have an association with someone who was Jewish could lead to denunciation, and Berg's "crime" was to have studied with the Jewish composer <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>. Berg found that opportunities for his work to be performed in Germany were becoming rare, and eventually his music was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proscribed" title="Proscribed">proscribed</a> and placed on the list of <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_music" title="Degenerate music">degenerate music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENotley2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENotley2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1932 Berg and his wife acquired an isolated lodge, the <i>Waldhaus</i> on the southern shore of the <i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rthersee" title="Wörthersee">Wörthersee</a></i>, near <a href="/wiki/Schiefling_am_See" title="Schiefling am See">Schiefling am See</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carinthia" title="Carinthia">Carinthia</a>, where he was able to work in seclusion, mainly on <a href="/wiki/Lulu_(opera)" title="Lulu (opera)">Lulu</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Berg)" title="Violin Concerto (Berg)">Violin Concerto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailey2010a_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailey2010a-11">[11]</a></sup> At the end of 1934 Berg became involved in the political intrigues around finding a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Krauss" title="Clemens Krauss">Clemens Krauss</a> as director of the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_State_Opera" title="Vienna State Opera">Vienna State Opera</a>. As more of the performances of his work in Germany were cancelled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazis</a>, who had come to power in early 1933, he needed to ensure the new director would be an advocate for modernist music. Originally the premiere of Lulu had been planned for the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_State_Opera" title="Berlin State Opera">Berlin State Opera</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Erich_Kleiber" title="Erich Kleiber">Erich Kleiber</a> continued to champion his music and had conducted the premiere of <i>Wozzeck</i> in 1925, but now this was looking increasingly uncertain, and Lulu was rejected by the Berlin authorities in the spring of 1934. Kleiber's production of the Lulu symphonic suite on 30 November 1934 in Berlin was also the occasion of his resignation in protest at the extent of conflation of culture with politics. Even in Vienna, the opportunities for the Vienna School of musicians was dwindling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENotley2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENotley2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Berg had interrupted the orchestration of <i>Lulu</i> because of an unexpected (and financially much-needed) commission from the Russian-American violinist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Krasner" title="Louis Krasner">Louis Krasner</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Berg)" title="Violin Concerto (Berg)">Violin Concerto</a> (1935). This profoundly elegiac work, composed at unaccustomed speed and posthumously premièred, has become Berg's best-known and beloved composition. Like much of his mature work, it employs an idiosyncratic adaptation of Schoenberg's "dodecaphonic" or <a href="/wiki/Twelve-tone_technique" title="Twelve-tone technique">twelve-tone technique</a>, that enables the composer to produce passages openly evoking tonality, including quotations from historical tonal music, such as a <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a> chorale and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">Carinthian</a> folk song. The Violin Concerto was dedicated "to the memory of an Angel", <a href="/wiki/Manon_Gropius" title="Manon Gropius">Manon Gropius</a>, the deceased daughter of architect <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alma_Mahler" title="Alma Mahler">Alma Mahler</a>.</p>
<p>Berg died in Vienna, on Christmas Eve 1935, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_poisoning" title="Blood poisoning">blood poisoning</a> apparently caused by an insect-sting-induced <a href="/wiki/Carbuncle" title="Carbuncle">carbuncle</a> on his back that occurred in November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was 50 years old.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Berg completed the orchestration of only the first two acts of <i>Lulu</i> before he died. The first two acts were successfully premièred in Zürich in 1937, but for personal reasons Helene Berg subsequently imposed a ban on any attempt to "complete" the final act, which Berg had in fact completed in particell (short score) format. An orchestration was therefore commissioned in secret from <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Cerha" title="Friedrich Cerha">Friedrich Cerha</a> and premièred in Paris (under <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a>) only in 1979, soon after Helene Berg's own death. The complete opera has rapidly entered the repertoire as one of the landmarks of contemporary music and, like <i>Wozzeck</i>, remains a consistent audience draw.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alban_Berg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Berg is remembered as one of the most important composers of the 20th century and to date is the most widely performed opera composer among the Second Viennese School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jarman.2C_Grove_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jarman.2C_Grove-13">[13]</a></sup> He is considered to have brought more "human values" to the twelve-tone system, his works seen as more "emotional" than Schoenberg's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Critically, he is seen as having preserved the Viennese tradition in his music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (September 2011)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His popularity has been more easily secured than many other Modernists since he plausibly combined both Romantic and Expressionist idioms. Though Berg's Romanticism at one time seemed a drawback for some more modernist composers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (September 2011)">weasel words</span></a></i>]</sup> the Berg scholar Douglas Jarman writes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Grove" title="New Grove">New Grove</a>: "As the 20th century closed, the 'backward-looking' Berg suddenly came as [George] <a href="/wiki/George_Perle" title="George Perle">Perle</a> remarked, to look like its most forward-looking composer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jarman.2C_Grove_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jarman.2C_Grove-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The asteroid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4528_Berg" title="4528 Berg">4528 Berg</a> is named after him.</p>
