<p><b><span class="nowrap">(307261) 2002 MS<sub>4</sub></span></b> is a large <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical Kuiper belt object</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2009-P26_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2009-P26-2">[2]</a></sup> the second-largest known object in the Solar System without a name, after <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(225088)_2007_OR10" title="(225088) 2007 OR10">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></a></span>. It was discovered in 2002 by <a href="/wiki/Chad_Trujillo" title="Chad Trujillo">Chad Trujillo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael Brown</a>.</p>
<p>Brown's website lists it as <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">nearly certain</a> to be a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a> estimated it to have a diameter of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005726000000000000♠</span>726<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>123 km</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitzer2007_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitzer2007-9">[9]</a></sup> The Herschel team estimates it to be <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005934000000000000♠</span>934<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>47 km</span>, which would make it one of the 10 largest TNOs currently known<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool6_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool6-5">[5]</a></sup> and large enough to be considered a dwarf planet under the <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet#Draft_proposal" title="IAU definition of planet">2006 draft proposal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">IAU</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-draft2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU-draft2006-10">[10]</a></sup> It is currently 47.2 AU from the Sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDys_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-AstDys-7">[7]</a></sup> and will come to <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a> in 2123.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been observed 55 times, with <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> images back to 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
