<p><i><b>et cetera</b></i> (in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>; <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>Latin pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[ɛt ˈkeːtɛra]</a></span>) (rare: <i>etceteros</i>) (abbreviation: <i><b>etc.</b></i> or <i><b>&amp;c.</b></i> (US English) or <i><b>etc</b></i> or <i><b>&amp;c</b></i> (UK English)) is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> expression that means "and other things", or "and so forth". It is taken directly from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> expression, which literally means "and the rest (of such things)" and is a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> "καιὶτα τέρα" (<i>kai ta tera</i>: "and the other things"; the more usual Greek form is "και τα λοιπά" <i>kai ta loipa</i>: "and the remainder"). <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/et#Latin" title="wikt:et">Et</a></i> means "and"; <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ceterus#Latin" title="wikt:ceterus">cētera</a></i> means "the rest".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spellings_and_usages">Spellings and usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_cetera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spellings and usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The one-word spelling "etcetera" is commonly used and is accepted as correct by many dictionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-19-861271-0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-19-861271-0-1">[1]</a></sup> It is also sometimes spelled <i><b>et caetera</b></i>, <i><b>et coetera</b></i> or <i><b>et cœtera</b></i> and is usually abbreviated to <i><b>etc.</b></i> or <i><b>&amp;c.</b></i> (US English) or '<b>etc</b>' or '<b>&amp;c</b>' (UK English) Some abbreviations that are still used in the United Kingdom, Australia and India, are considered archaic in the United States and commonly used only in legislation, notations for mathematics or qualifications, include <i><b>&amp;ca</b></i>, <i><b>etca</b></i>, <i><b>&amp;/c.</b></i>, <i><b>&amp;e.</b></i>, <i><b>&amp;ct.</b></i>, <i><b>&amp;cm</b></i>, <i><b>etcm</b></i>, <i><b>&amp;cs</b></i>, and <i><b>etcs</b></i>. (The <a href="/wiki/Ampersand" title="Ampersand">ampersand</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">ligature</a> of "et". This is occasionally formed by knowing "et" to mean "and" but not realising "&amp;" is a ligature of "et".)</p>
<p>The phrase <i>et cetera</i> is often used to denote the logical continuation of some sort of series of descriptions. For example, in the following expression:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>We will need a lot of bread: wheat, granary, wholemeal, etc.</i></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_by_monarchs">Usage by monarchs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_cetera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage by monarchs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a>, who sometimes have lengthy titles due to dynastic claims to territories accumulated over the centuries (and also as a matter of prestige), often shorten their full titles by concluding it with "et cetera"; even then the phrase would often be repeated in order to emphasize the monarchs' grandeur.</p>
<p>A prime example of this usage would be from Tsar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II of Russia</a>, who traditionally began his <a href="/wiki/Proclamation" title="Proclamation">proclamations</a> with his shortened (but still long) title: "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluralis_majestatis" title="Pluralis majestatis">We</a>, Nicholas II, <a href="/wiki/By_the_Grace_of_God" title="By the Grace of God">By the Grace of God</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Russia" title="Emperor of Russia">Emperor and Autocrat of All the Russias</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">King of Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Finland" title="Grand Duke of Finland">Grand Duke of Finland</a>, et cetera, et cetera, et cetera."</p>
<p>In the 1956 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_and_I_(1956_film)" title="The King and I (1956 film)">The King and I</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Yul_Brynner" title="Yul Brynner">Yul Brynner</a>, who played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Rama_IV" title="King Rama IV">King Mongkut of Siam</a>, repeatedly used the phrase, "<i>...et cetera, et cetera, et cetera...</i>", to characterize the King as wanting to impress with his great knowledge of many things and his importance in not having to detail them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wcs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wcs-2">[2]</a></sup> This was based upon the usage in the book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anna_and_the_King_of_Siam_(book)" title="Anna and the King of Siam (book)">Anna and the King of Siam</a></i> which related the real king's playful interest in numerous things, with the phrase, "&amp;c, &amp;c".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wcs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wcs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_Latin_expressions">Similar Latin expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_cetera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Similar Latin expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In lists of people, <b>et alii</b> (abbreviated as <i>et al.</i>, meaning "and others") is used in place of <i>etc.</i></li>
<li>In lists of places, <i><b>et alibi</b></i> may be used, which is also abbreviated <i>et al.</i> <i>et alibi</i> means "and elsewhere."</li>
<li>In references to literature or texts in general, <i><b>et sequentes (versus)</b></i> or <i><b>et sequentia</b></i> 'and the words etc. following' (abbreviated <i>et seq.</i> or <i>et seqq.</i>) are used to indicate that only the first portion of a known reference is given explicitly, with broad reference to the following passages which logically follow in sequence to the explicit reference. Hence "Title VII, Section 4, Subsection A, Paragraph 1, <i>et seq.</i>" might refer to many subsections or paragraphs which follow Paragraph 1. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_brief" title="Legal brief">Legal briefs</a> and legislative documents make heavy use of <i>et seq.</i> Notice that there is a functional difference between <i>et seq.</i> and <i>etc.</i> Et seq. and its variations refer specifically to known text; etc. may do so too, but is more likely to leave the reader to supply the unspecified items for himself. It would not be helpful to say: "Various paragraphs of import similar to those in Title VII, Sections 4, 7, and 2 <i>et seq.</i>" though it might make sense to use "etc." in such a context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>"et cetera" and derivatives such as "etceteras" have long been, and still are, used airily, humorously or dismissively, often as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadigan" title="Cadigan">cadigan</a>, for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>... he still wanted numberless appendages to make him a fine gentleman, such as a fashionable tailor and hairdresser, an unblushing confidence, together with a long train of etceteras. These fashionable introductories being wanting, Mr Whitmore was obliged to find a substitute...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (1823)</li>
<li>The cost of the locomotives and their etceteras, is to be $136000 – their wear and tear $75600. Etceteras $90000...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (1834)</li>
<li>The etceteras: asteroids, comets and interplanetary dust are chemically speaking, "impurities" and are just a minuscule fraction of planetary matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> (1989)</li>
<li>Having tried "to recover myth outside the books," the hidalgo crosses paths with common sense, everyday toils, and the religious dictates of the Counter-Reformation on a journey that tries to rescue chivalric etceteras of old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (2008)</li>
<li>/etc, a folder in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNIX-like" title="UNIX-like">UNIX-like</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, responsible mainly for storing system-wide configurations, preferences, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
