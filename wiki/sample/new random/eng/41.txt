<p>The <b>2008 US beef protest in South Korea</b> was a series of protest demonstrations between 24 May 2008 and about 18 July 2008 in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. At its height, the protest involved tens of thousands of people. The protest began after the South Korean government reversed a ban on US beef imports. The ban had been in place since December 2003, when the <a href="/wiki/Prion" title="Prion">prion</a> responsible for <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">bovine spongiform encephalopathy</a> (BSE), or 'mad cow disease' was detected in US beef cattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_7491482_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_7491482-1">[1]</a></sup> The protests occurred on a background of talks concerning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KORUS_FTA" title="KORUS FTA">US-Korea free trade agreement</a>. Unrest was fanned by local media reports such as the <a href="/wiki/Munhwa_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation">Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation</a> (MBC) <i><a href="/wiki/PD_Notebook" title="PD Notebook">PD Notebook</a></i> program, "Is American Beef Really Safe from Mad Cow Disease?" televised on 27 April 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Controversy in South Korea over U.S. beef isn’t new, but has been largely dormant for the past four years. In 2008, the then new Lee Myung-bak government agreed, after extensive negotiations, to restart imports, which had been halted since cases of mad cow disease were found in the United States in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>That decision set off a firestorm of controversy, leading hundreds of thousands of citizens to come out in the spring and early summer of 2008 in protest over the resumed imports. Sensationalist media reports and online rumors fueled speculation that the new president was recklessly putting South Koreans’ health at risk of contracting bovine spongiform encephalopathy, better known as mad cow disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The deal has sparked public outrage amid widespread perceptions, fanned in part by sensational media reports, MBC PD Notebook, that it exposes the country to a higher risk of mad cow disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_protest">Origin of the protest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of the protest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2003">2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 23 December 2003, a first case of BSE in the US was found in Washington state. The Holstein cow had been imported from Canada in 2001. On 9 December the 6.5-year-old cow was slaughtered. The cow was a "downer" (a cow that is unable to walk). For that reason, the US Department of Agriculture (USDA) was automatically notified; and the cow was examined before and after its death by a government vet. The vet determined that the cow was suffering from complications of calving. Samples of tissue were taken for further testing for BSE. Parts of the cow with a high risk of transmitting the BSE prion were removed but may have been sent for "inedible rendering" into food for non-ruminant animals. The carcass itself was allowed to continue on for further processing into human food at other facilities. On 23 December 2003 when tests proved positive for BSE, a recall was made. The contaminated meat had not entered the commercial market. Offspring of the cow were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> South Korea, Japan, Singapore, Malaysia and Taiwan banned imports of US beef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The head of the US FDA at the time was Ann M. Veneman, a former lobbyist for the food industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006">2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2006, sixty-five nations had full or partial restrictions on the importation of US beef. Export sales of US beef fell from $3.8 billion in 2003 to $1.4 billion in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> An attempt to reopen the South Korean market to US beef imports in 2006 (restricted to boneless meat from cattle less than 30 months old) failed when the South Korean government discovered bone chips in a shipment of 3.2 tons of meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Sporadic attempts made in the following year also failed for similar reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_.E2.80.93_Lee.27s_reversal_of_the_beef_import_ban">2008 – Lee's reversal of the beef import ban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2008 – Lee's reversal of the beef import ban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 January 2008 representatives of the president of South Korea met with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Vershbow" title="Alexander Vershbow">Alexander Vershbow</a>, the U.S. ambassador to South Korea to discuss beef imports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Further talks were held between 11 and 17 April 2008. On 18 April U.S. and South Korean negotiators reached an agreement on the sanitary rules that Korea would require of U.S. beef imports. The agreement allowed imports of all cuts of U.S. beef (both boneless and bone-in) and certain beef products, including those from cattle over 30 months old. Processors had to remove material known to risk BSE prion transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> President Lee visited President George W. Bush at <a href="/wiki/Camp_David" title="Camp David">Camp David</a> on 20 April 2008. The U.S. reported,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"President Bush welcomed the decision of the Korean government to resume the import of U.S. beef, based on international standards and science. The two presidents pledged to make every effort to urge their respective legislatures to approve the KORUS FTA (Korean US Free trade agreement) within this year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Demonstrators accused Lee of reversing the ban on imports of U.S. beef in haste, giving the U.S. unwarranted concessions, so that Korea would receive a favorable reception, particularly with respect to ratifying the proposed free trade agreement. The demonstrators said Lee had abandoned his duty of care to the people of South Korea for political gain by ignoring their concern about BSE prion transmission and by lowering tariffs on imported beef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Korea agreed to remove a 40% tariff on beef muscle meat imported from the U.S. for a period of 15 years. Korea could however, impose temporary tariffs if there was a surge of U.S. beef imports above specified levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MBC_.22PD_Notebook.22_program">MBC "PD Notebook" program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: MBC "PD Notebook" program'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 27 April 2008, MBC televised a program called "Is American Beef Really Safe from Mad Cow Disease?" The program precipitated mass demonstrations. After a complaint was received from the South Korean agriculture ministry, the Seoul central prosecutors' office formed a team of five to investigate the program's content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Downer_cows">Downer cows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Downer cows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Downer</i> cows are animals presented for slaughter that are sick or unable to walk. One of many causes for a downer is BSE. MBC was criticised for broadcasting footage of downer cows with translated subtitles that suggested they suffered from BSE, whereas they were filmed because of animal cruelty concerns, not BSE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-B-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aretha_Vinson">Aretha Vinson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aretha Vinson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aretha Vinson was a 21-year-old student at Virginia state university. She died on 9 April 2008 of <a href="/wiki/Wernicke%27s_encephalopathy" title="Wernicke's encephalopathy">Wernicke's encephalopathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The MBC broadcast showed footage of Vinson's mother speaking about her daughter's illness. Translated subtitles suggested Vinson had vCJD, but that was only one initial diagnostic possibility.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_of_US_beef_to_South_Korea">Risk of US beef to South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Risk of US beef to South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Concerns that some commentators raised about the program included, MBC's statements about a genetic vulnerability of Koreans to CJD;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the US exporting cattle over 30 months old to Korea rather than selling it to the domestic market;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and, the risk of contracting CJD by consuming beef products such as powdered soup base in instant noodles, cosmetics, and gelatin medication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_to_.22PD_Notebook.22">Reaction to "PD Notebook"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: Reaction to "PD Notebook"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Demonstrations involving tens of thousands of people began shortly after the first broadcast, and increased when MBC aired another segment two weeks later.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_communications_commission">Korean communications commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Korean communications commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MBC became the subject of legal action. On 12 August 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Communications_Commission" title="Korea Communications Commission">Korea Communications Commission</a> called for MBC to apologise to the public over misrepresentations made in the PD notebook program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> MBC apologised a two-minute broadcast, saying,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"We would like to offer a sincere apology to our viewers (over the report)."</dd>
</dl>
<p>MBC conceded that six translation errors had been made and that downer cattle had been mistakenly identified as suffering from BSE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MBC_versus_Government">MBC versus Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: MBC versus Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The government of South Korea directed its prosecutors office to investigate alleged actions of the MBC including mis-representations made by the PD Notebook program; aggravation of civil unrest; and, defamation of the minister for agriculture. In June 2009, four producers and one writer were indicted on the charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In January 2010, the MBC staff were exonerated by judges of the Seoul central district court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The supreme court upheld the findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some commentators argue that there is a long held and perpetual "culture of protest" in South Korea involving groups ranging from anarchists to social reformers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Anti US beef protests began on 24 May 2008. After the MBC <i>PD Notebook</i> broadcast, the US beef protests in Seoul increased. A three-day demonstration held in relay took place from 5 June 2008 to 7 June 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Attendance peaked on the evening of 10 June 2008, (80,000 protestors in attendance) before declining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> (Photographs depicting the demonstrations from "Reuters", "Getty Images" and "AFP" are published <a class="external text" href="http://www.boston.com/bigpicture/2008/06/south_korean_protests_over_us.html" rel="nofollow">here.</a>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_social_media">Influence of social media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Influence of social media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Internet and text messaging also assisted in publicising the movement. With ready access to social media, among the first to protest were teenage schoolgirls. A teenage schoolgirl holding a lit candle became the symbol of the anti-US beef protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Comments made by some protestors had little basis in science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_demonstrators">The demonstrators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The demonstrators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the initial demonstration, the area in front of Seoul's city hall, as well as the adjoining streets, were occupied by demonstrators. Early in the protest, a festival like atmosphere prevailed as protesters of all walks of life, built a makeshift tent city on the lawn at <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Plaza" title="Seoul Plaza">Seoul Plaza</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> However, some massive, disruptive and sometimes aggressive demonstrations and street marches were held each night, particularly at weekends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Thus, the protest had two faces: more peaceful during the day and more violent at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> About 200 protestors required hospital treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Police_action">Police action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Police action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 1 June 2008, police took action to control demonstrators. Buses and shipping containers were used to halt the progress of street marches. A barrier of shipping containers were erected across Sejong-Ro to stop marchers from reaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheongwadae" title="Cheongwadae">Cheongwadae</a> (office and residence of the president). The protestors named the barrier, "Myung-bak's Fortress" and decorated it with leaflets and large Korean flags. They photographed themselves standing on the barrier. It was dismantled several days later without conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Water cannons were used to suppress protestors. Some demonstrators were detained for questioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> report on the human rights aspects of the demonstrations is published <a class="external text" href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/library/asset/ASA25/008/2008/en/7866408a-9395-11dd-8293-ff015cefb49a/asa250082008en.pdf" rel="nofollow">here.</a> The report said,</p>
<dl>
<dd>" The protests were for the most part peaceful and given its size and duration, both the protesters and the police showed notable organization and restraint. However, there were sporadic incidents of violence, as riot police and protesters clashed. The two main flashpoints of violence occurred on 31 May/1 June, when the police first used water cannons and fire extinguishers, and 28/29 June, the weekend following the government's announcement that US beef imports would resume. The decision to use water cannons and fire extinguishers contributed to the mass resignation of all 14 members of the Korean National Police Agency's human rights committee."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_government">Korean government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Korean government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approval_rating" title="Approval rating">approval rating</a> of Lee fell below 20%. Prime Minister Han Seung-soo and other cabinet members submitted their resignations to President Lee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On 22 May and then again on 18 June 2008, Lee apologised for ignoring public health concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-43">[43]</a></sup> Lee said,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"I should have paid attention to what people want. Sitting on a hill near Cheongwadae on the night of 10 June, watching the candlelight vigil, I blamed myself for not serving the people better.”</dd>
</dl>
<p>Lee also delayed the announcement of any further relaxation of beef importation rules, for example, importation of meat from cattle over 30 months of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-43">[43]</a></sup> On 2 July 2008, <a href="/wiki/Han_Seung-soo" title="Han Seung-soo">Han Seung-soo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_South_Korea" title="Prime Minister of South Korea">Prime Minister of South Korea</a> bought 260,000 <a href="/wiki/Korean_won" title="Korean won">Korean won</a> (roughly US$230–$260) worth of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Steak" title="Steak">steak</a> to eat with his family at his official residence to alleviate public worries about U.S. beef. The same amount of Korean beef would have cost approximately 800,000 <a href="/wiki/Korean_won" title="Korean won">Korean won</a> (roughly $700–$800, three times more expensive than imported U.S. beef).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US">US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 22 June 2008, an American trade envoy headed by Susan C. Schwab, agreed with the Korean prime minister and agriculture minister to limit the export of meat to that from carcasses of cattle less than 30 months old with government certification of their age and to allow Korea to inspect a sample of U.S. slaughterhouses. The agreement also included the banning of importation to Korea of meat from cattle organs considered high risk for transmission of the BSE prion such as brains, eyes, skulls and spinal cord. Schwab said,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"We look forward to safe, affordable, high-quality American beef – the same beef enjoyed by hundreds of millions of U.S. consumers and people in countries around the world – soon arriving on Korean tables."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Casey_(diplomat)" title="Tom Casey (diplomat)">Tom Casey</a>, deputy spokesman at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State_Department" title="U.S. State Department">U.S. State Department</a> rejected the notion that the U.S. had bullied S. Korea into removing the 2003 ban on U.S. beef imports and said,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"(The U.S. and S. Korea) have a long history of military and security cooperation. I don't think this or any other individual issues are going to change the fundamental relations (between the two countries)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>From December 2009 to December 2010 the US meat export federation (USMEF) used a 3 phase project called, "To trust". It was an image campaign, designed to increase the trust of Koreans in US beef. It depicted three women who were mothers and homemakers. One was a rancher, one a scientist and one a food safety inspector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hi.21_Seoul_festival">Hi! Seoul festival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hi! Seoul festival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, a small number of demonstrators commemorated the anniversary of the 2008 protests. They forcibly occupied the <a href="/wiki/Hi!_Seoul_Festival" title="Hi! Seoul Festival">Hi! Seoul Festival</a> stage in front of the city hall in central Seoul. The police removed the protestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commentators">Commentators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Commentators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kim Dae-joong (not to be confused with former Korean President and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dae-jung" title="Kim Dae-jung">Kim Dae-jung</a>), a columnist at the <i>Korea Times</i>, wrote in his editorial piece: "It amounts to double-crossing to be really fond of America in all substantive matters, while bad-mouthing America in public protests."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_beef_imports_in_South_Korea">US beef imports in South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_US_beef_protest_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: US beef imports in South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 1 July 2008, U.S. beef imports resumed. In 2009, the US exported 141 million lbs of beef to South Korea worth $215 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
