<p><b>DivX</b> is a brand of <a href="/wiki/Video_codec" title="Video codec">video codec</a> products developed by DivX, LLC. The DivX codec is notable for its ability to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_compression" title="Video compression">compress</a> lengthy video segments into small sizes while maintaining relatively high visual quality.</p>
<p>There are three DivX <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codecs</a>; the original <a href="/wiki/MPEG-4_Part_2" title="MPEG-4 Part 2">MPEG-4 Part 2</a> DivX codec, the <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264/MPEG-4 AVC</a> DivX Plus HD codec and the <a href="/wiki/High_Efficiency_Video_Coding" title="High Efficiency Video Coding">High Efficiency Video Coding</a> DivX HEVC Ultra HD codec. It is one of several codecs commonly associated with "<a href="/wiki/Ripping" title="Ripping">ripping</a>", whereby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> multimedia are transferred to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">hard disk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transcoding" title="Transcoding">transcoded</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "<b>DivX</b>" brand is distinct from "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Express" title="Digital Video Express">DIVX</a>", which is an obsolete video rental system developed by <a href="/wiki/Circuit_City" title="Circuit City">Circuit City Stores</a> that used custom DVD-like discs and players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DIVX_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DIVX-1">[1]</a></sup> The winking <a href="/wiki/Emoticon" title="Emoticon">emoticon</a> in the early "<span class="nowrap">DivX ;-)</span>" codec name was a <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">tongue-in-cheek</a> reference to the DIVX system. Although not created by them, the DivX company adopted the name of the popular <span class="nowrap">DivX ;-)</span> codec. The company dropped the <a href="/wiki/Smiley" title="Smiley">smiley</a> and released DivX 4.0, which was actually the first DivX version, <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarking</a> the word, <i>DivX</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trademark1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-trademark1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trademark2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-trademark2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><span class="nowrap">DivX ;-)</span></b> (not <b>DivX</b>) 3.11 Alpha and later 3.xx versions refers to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(hobbyist)" title="Hacker (hobbyist)">hacked</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> <i>MPEG-4 Version 3</i> video codec (not to be confused with MPEG-4 Part 3) from Windows Media Tools 4 codecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The video codec, which was actually not MPEG-4 compliant, was extracted around 1998 by French hacker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerome_Rota" title="Jerome Rota">Jerome Rota</a> (also known as Gej) at Montpellier. The Microsoft codec originally required that the compressed output be put in an <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Systems_Format" title="Advanced Systems Format">ASF</a> file. It was altered to allow other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_format_(digital)" title="Container format (digital)">containers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Audio_Video_Interleave" title="Audio Video Interleave">Audio Video Interleave</a> (AVI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Rota hacked the Microsoft codec because newer versions of the Windows Media Player would not play his video portfolio and résumé that were encoded with it. Instead of re-encoding his portfolio, Rota and German hacker Max Morice decided to <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineering" title="Reverse engineering">reverse engineer</a> the codec, which "took about a week".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-napTrap_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-napTrap-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 2000, Jordan Greenhall recruited Rota to form a company (originally called DivXNetworks, Inc., renamed to DivX, Inc. in 2005) to develop an MPEG-4 codec, from scratch, that would still be backward-compatible with the Microsoft MPEG-4 Version 3 format. This effort resulted first in the release of the "OpenDivX" codec and source code on 15 January 2001. OpenDivX was hosted as an open-source project on the Project Mayo web site hosted at projectmayo.com<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> (the name comes from "<a href="/wiki/Mayonnaise" title="Mayonnaise">mayonnaise</a>", because, according to Rota, DivX and mayonnaise are both "French and very hard to make."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-napTrap_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-napTrap-7">[7]</a></sup>). The company's internal developers and some external developers worked jointly on OpenDivX for the next several months, but the project eventually stagnated.</p>
<p>In early 2001, DivX employee "Sparky" wrote a new and improved version of the codec's encoding algorithm known as "encore2". This code was included in the OpenDivX public source repository for a brief time, but then was abruptly removed. The explanation from DivX at the time was that "the community really wants a Winamp, not a Linux." It was at this point that the project <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fork_(software)" title="Fork (software)">forked</a>. That summer, Rota left the French Riviera and moved to San Diego "with nothing but a pack of cigarettes"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-divxCeo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-divxCeo-9">[9]</a></sup> where he and Greenhall founded what would eventually become DivX, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-napTrap_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-napTrap-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>DivX took the encore2 code and developed it into DivX 4.0, initially released in July 2001. Other developers who had participated in OpenDivX took encore2 and started a new project—<a href="/wiki/Xvid" title="Xvid">Xvid</a>—that started with the same encoding core. DivX, Inc. has since continued to develop the DivX codec, releasing DivX 5.0 in March 2002. By the release of version 5.2.1 on 8 September 2004, the DivX codec was substantially feature-complete. Changes since then have tended to focus on speed, and encouraging wider hardware player support, while the company has also focused its time on the formats and next generation codecs.</p>
<p>In February 2011, DivX was acquired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rovi_Corporation" title="Rovi Corporation">Rovi Corporation</a>, upon completion of its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Solutions" title="Sonic Solutions">Sonic Solutions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-divxRovi_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-divxRovi-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2014, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackstone_Group" title="Blackstone Group">Blackstone Group</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax_Capital&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Parallax Capital (page does not exist)">Parallax Capital</a> acquired DivX from Rovi for $75 million. On January 5, 2015, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/IPTV" title="IPTV">IPTV</a> company <a href="/wiki/NeuLion" title="NeuLion">NeuLion</a> would acquire DivX for $62.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcn-neuliondivx_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcn-neuliondivx-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<th scope="row" style="padding:0.2em 0;line-height:1.2em; padding-right:0.65em;">Type of format</th>

</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row" style="padding:0.2em 0;line-height:1.2em; padding-right:0.65em;"><a href="/wiki/Digital_container_format" title="Digital container format">Container for</a></th>
<td style="line-height:1.35em;">MPEG-4 Part 2–compliant video</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row" style="padding:0.2em 0;line-height:1.2em; padding-right:0.65em;">Extended from</th>
<td style="line-height:1.35em;"><a href="/wiki/Audio_Video_Interleave" title="Audio Video Interleave">AVI</a></td>

<p>DivX 6 expanded the scope of DivX from including just a codec and a player by adding an optional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_format_(digital)" title="Container format (digital)">media container format</a> called "DivX Media Format" ("DMF")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (with a .divx extension) that includes support for the following <a href="/wiki/DVD-Video" title="DVD-Video">DVD-Video</a> and <a href="/wiki/VOB" title="VOB">VOB</a> container like features. This media container format is used for the <a href="/wiki/MPEG-4_Part_2" title="MPEG-4 Part 2">MPEG-4 Part 2</a> codec.</p>
<p>This new DivX Media Format also came with a <b>DivX Ultra Certified</b> profile, and all <b>Ultra</b> certified players must support all DivX Media Format features. While video encoded with the DivX codec is an MPEG-4 video stream, the DivX Media Format is analogous to media container formats such as Apple's QuickTime. In much the same way that media formats such as DVD specify MPEG-2 video as a part of their specification, the DivX Media Format specifies MPEG-4-compatible video as a part of its specification. However, despite the use of the ".divx" extension, this format is an extension to the AVI file format. The methods of including multiple audio and even subtitle tracks involve storing the data in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIFF_(File_format)" title="RIFF (File format)">RIFF</a> headers and other such AVI hacks which have been known for quite a while, such that even <a href="/wiki/VirtualDubMod" title="VirtualDubMod">VirtualDubMod</a> supports them. DivX, Inc. did this on purpose to keep at least partial <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward compatibility</a> with AVI, so that players that do not support the new features available to the .divx container format (like interactive menus, chapter points and XSUB subtitles) can at least play that primary video stream (usually the main movie if the DMF file contains multiple video streams like special features like bonus materials). Of course, the DivX codec and tools like <a href="/wiki/Dr._DivX" title="Dr. DivX">Dr. DivX</a> still support the traditional method of creating standard AVI files.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_Plus_HD">DivX Plus HD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: DivX Plus HD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DivX_Plus_HD" title="DivX Plus HD">DivX Plus HD</a></div>
<p>DivX Plus HD is a marketing name for a file type using the standard <a href="/wiki/Matroska" title="Matroska">Matroska</a> media container format (.mkv), rather than the proprietary DivX Media Format. DivX Plus HD files contain an <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264</a> video bitstream, <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Audio_Coding" title="Advanced Audio Coding">AAC</a> surround sound audio, and a number of XML-based attachments defining chapters, subtitles and meta data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This media container format is used for the <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264/MPEG-4 AVC</a> codec.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_Video_on_Demand">DivX Video on Demand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: DivX Video on Demand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DivX Video on Demand (DivX VOD) is DivX's version of <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management (DRM)</a>, which allows content copyright holders to control distribution. DivX, Inc. has received format approval from major Hollywood studios including Sony, Paramount and Lionsgate, which has allowed content retailers to sell protected videos that will play on current and previous generations of DivX certified devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_software">DivX software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DivX software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>DivX</b> (briefly <b>DivX for Windows</b>) is a suite of software for Microsoft Windows that contains the DivX Codec, a standalone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_player_(application_software)" title="Media player (application software)">media player</a>, a video converter and media player <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_(computing)" title="Plug-in (computing)">Plug-in</a> for web browsers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It was released on 16 March 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web-divx-plus8_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-web-divx-plus8-21">[21]</a></sup> DivX 7, released 6 January 2009, added H.264 video, AAC audio and Matroska container support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>DivX Web Player (formerly known as DivX Web Player and DivX Browser Plug-In) is a web browser plug-in for playing embedded video on web pages. Web Player supports playing DivX (.divx), AVI (.avi), Matroska (.mkv), MP4 (.mp4) and QuickTime (.mov) video formats on <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>, Internet Explorer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Safari_(web_browser)" title="Safari (web browser)">Safari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It also supports multiple audio tracks and muxed UTF-8 subtitles and is able to save video files locally upon completing of full-length buffer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 2, 2010, a public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betaware" title="Betaware">beta</a> of the Web Player, called DivX HiQ, introduced the following features in the DivX Web Player for Windows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Support for replacing the playing embedded web video with DivX HiQ instead of the <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash_Player" title="Adobe Flash Player">Adobe Flash Player</a> on video portals like <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DailyMotion" title="DailyMotion">DailyMotion</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Support for H.264 video in MP4 or QuickTime Video files</li>
<li>A plug-in that parses <a href="/wiki/HTML5_video" title="HTML5 video">HTML5 &lt;video&gt; tag</a> for the web browser, thus enabling it to play .divx, .avi, .mkv, .mp4 or .mov video embedded in this manner with DivX Web Player</li>
<li>Hardware accelerated video decoding of H.264 bitstreams</li>
</ul>
<p>On February 10, 2011, The DivX HiQ Beta experiment is over, and will be uninstalled from any computer system in the latest release of the DivX Web Player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 15, 2011, DivX released the new DivX Web Player 2.2.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> New release information for this version is unknown, and not yet published.</p>

<p>DivX Player for Mac, first introduced with DivX 6.5 on the 25 May 2006, is a discontinued standalone player that plays DivX encoded files and DivX Media Format features such as subtitles, multiple audio tracks, and DivX VOD content. The DivX Player for Mac also can play DivX Plus HD compatible files — namely .mkv files encoded with H.264 video and AAC audio. Unlike the DivX 7 and DivX Plus (version 8) Windows counterparts, DivX Player for Mac does not include a burn engine or a utility for moving videos to USB. Starting with DivX 7.3, it can now play MP4 and MOV files, and now has HTML5 support. The player has since been updated to 9.1.2.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Bundled playback of MPEG-4 ASP video with MP3 audio (.divx/.avi) and DivX files (Matroska with H.264 video and AAC audio)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mouse_gesture" title="Mouse gesture">Mouse gestures</a> to control video</li>
<li>Media manager via a video library window</li>
<li>Play any <a href="/wiki/QuickTime" title="QuickTime">QuickTime</a> supported file</li>
<li>Play video as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desktop_background" title="Desktop background">desktop background</a></li>
<li>Manage certified DivX devices to enable playback of purchased/protected content</li>
<li>Playback of purchased/protected video content</li>
</ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Dr._DivX" title="Dr. DivX">Dr. DivX</a> is an application created by DivX, Inc. that is capable of transcoding many video formats to DivX encoded video. The original closed source Dr. DivX terminated at version 1.06 for DivX 5.21, that was the last version of DivX capable of running under Windows 9x/Me. An open source version has been made, which supports DivX 6. <i>Dr. DivX</i> offers greatly expanded features over the free DivX Converter application, that was bundled with the codec from version 6 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Dr. Divx is not compatible with DivX Plus HD.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_H.264_encoder">DivX H.264 encoder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: DivX H.264 encoder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>DivX has released a command line interface (CLI) for the divx264 encoder used in the DivX Converter as beta, free for non-commercial use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web-divx-labsblog-H264beta_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-web-divx-labsblog-H264beta-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_AAC_encoder">DivX AAC encoder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: DivX AAC encoder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To complement the CLI divx264 encoder released on DivX Labs, DivX has also released a DivX AAC encoder CLI as a Windows beta binary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web-divx-labsblog-H264beta_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-web-divx-labsblog-H264beta-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DivX_MKV_Mux">DivX MKV Mux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: DivX MKV Mux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The DivXMKVMux is a free Windows CLI available through DivX Labs with documentation on the DivX Developer Portal; DivX describes the release as a reference mux to demonstrate DivX Plus MKV extensions like <a href="/wiki/DivX_Plus_HD#World_Fonts" title="DivX Plus HD">World Fonts</a> and <a href="/wiki/DivX_Plus_HD#Smooth_FF.2FRW" title="DivX Plus HD">Smooth FF/RW</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware_support">Hardware support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hardware support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DivX, Inc. markets a certification program to consumer electronics and IC manufacturers for the purpose of guaranteed compatibility and playback of video files that fall within DivX profiles. Devices that have been DivX certified usually brandish one of the following marks:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>DivX Certified</li>
<li>DivX Certified for HD 720p and 1080p</li>
<li>DivX Certified for DivX Plus HD</li>
<li>DivX Connected</li>
<li>DivX Certified for DivX Ultra</li>
</ul>
<p>DivX certified devices have included DVD players, car stereos, mobile phones, televisions, Blu-ray players, and even alarm clocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from verifying proper decoding of files conforming to the DivX profiles the certification also confirms the device can play back DivX Video on Demand content, which includes Hollywood content that can be purchased from Internet retailers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaming_system_compatibility">Gaming system compatibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Gaming system compatibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 4 December 2007, native MPEG-4 ASP playback support was added to the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> allowing it to play video encoded with DivX and other MPEG-4 ASP codecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 December 2007, firmware upgrade 2.10 was released for the Sony <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>, which included official DivX Certification. Firmware version 2.50 (released on 15 October 2008) included support for the DivX Video on Demand (DivX VOD) service, and firmware version 2.60 (released on 20 January 2009) included official DivX Certification and updated Profile support to version 3.11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>With introduction of DivX to Go in the DivX Player for Windows, a PlayStation 3 icon is readily available on the interface, which will invoke a transfer wizard for freely converting and copying video files via USB or optical disc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The output from DivX to Go's PlayStation 3 preset is also playable on the Xbox 360.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitors">Competitors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DivX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Competitors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_video_player_software" title="Comparison of video player software">Comparison of video player software</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Video_conversion_software" title="Category:Video conversion software">Category:Video conversion software</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Video_codecs" title="Category:Video codecs">Category:Video codecs</a></div>
<p>The main competitors of DivX Inc. in the proprietary commercial software market are <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft Corporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Inc." title="Google Inc.">Google Inc.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> Microsoft develops <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Video" title="Windows Media Video">Windows Media Video</a> codec (an implementation of <a href="/wiki/VC-1" title="VC-1">VC-1</a> used on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Discs</a>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Player" title="Windows Media Player">Windows Media Player</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_player_(software)" title="Video player (software)">video player</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Expression_Encoder" title="Microsoft Expression Encoder">Microsoft Expression Encoder</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_converter" title="Video converter">video converter</a>. Google Inc. develops <a href="/wiki/WebM" title="WebM">WebM</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Royalty-free" title="Royalty-free">royalty-free</a> video file format chiefly used for online video. Apple Inc. also develops <a href="/wiki/QuickTime" title="QuickTime">QuickTime</a>, a suite of software that can encode, decode and play digital video (including H.264–compliant video).</p>
<p>Additionally, all software products that create (encode) <a href="/wiki/MPEG-4" title="MPEG-4">MPEG-4</a>–compatible digital video also compete with DivX. One of these products that can produce MPEG-4–compliant digital video is <a href="/wiki/Xvid" title="Xvid">Xvid</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_and_open_source" title="Free and open source">free and open source</a> codec that offers comparable quality. Both DivX Codec and Xvid are compliant with MPEG-4 Part 2 (MPEG-4 ASP); however, the most commonly used DivX encoding profile (<i>Home Theater</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> does not employ the same MPEG-4 ASP features enabled in the most commonly used Xvid encoding profile (<i>home</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In a series of subjective quality tests at Doom9.org between 2003 and 2005, the DivX encoder was beaten by the Xvid encoder every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Similar tests were not undertaken for newer versions.</p>
<p>Another notable codec is <tt><a href="/wiki/Libavcodec" title="Libavcodec">libavcodec</a></tt>, a <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> <a href="/wiki/Library_(computing)" title="Library (computing)">library</a> that can create and play digital video formats that comply with MPEG-4 Part 2 and MPEG-4 Part 10. This library is also used by <tt><a href="/wiki/Ffdshow" title="Ffdshow">ffdshow</a></tt>, which can be used for playback with most Windows video players and video converters.</p>
