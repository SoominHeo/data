<p>The <b>United States Army Installation Management Command</b> supports the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>'s warfighting mission by handling the day-to-day operations of U.S. Army installations around the globe. Army garrisons are communities that provide many of the same types of services expected from any small city. Fire, police, housing, and child-care are just some of the things IMCOM does in Army communities every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> IMCOM's vision statement is: Committed to Service — Enhancing Readiness — Sustaining the Army Culture.</p>
<p>IMCOM is headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_TX" title="San Antonio, TX">San Antonio, TX</a> on <a href="/wiki/Fort_Sam_Houston" title="Fort Sam Houston">Fort Sam Houston</a>. IMCOM's headquarters relocated in October, 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Base_Realignment_and_Closure" title="Base Realignment and Closure">Base Realignment and Closure</a> Act of 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="756" data-file-width="996" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg/220px-United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg/330px-United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg/440px-United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command_HQ_Rendering.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> Installation Management Command (IMCOM),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> was activated on 24 Oct. 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> to reduce bureaucracy, apply a uniform business structure to manage U.S. Army installations, sustain the environment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_Army_Environmental_Command_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_Army_Environmental_Command-6">[6]</a></sup> and enhance the well-being of the military community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It consolidated three organizations under a single command as a direct reporting unit:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The former Installation Management Agency (IMA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The former Community and Family Support Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> now called <a class="external text" href="https://www.imcom.army.mil/organization/g9familymwrprograms.aspx" rel="nofollow">G9 Family and MWR Programs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which was formerly a subordinate command of IMCOM.</li>
<li>The former Army Environmental Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> now called the <a class="external text" href="https://aec.army.mil/" rel="nofollow">Army Environmental Command</a> (AEC), which is a subordinate command of IMCOM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Prior to the Installation Management Command, the Army's 184 installations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> were managed by one of 15 Major Commands. Support services varied – some provided better services, some provided worse. In September 2001, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_Secretary" title="Army Secretary">Army Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._White" title="Thomas E. White">Thomas E. White</a> introduced the Transformation of Installation Management (TIM),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> formerly known as Centralized Installation Management (CIM), pledging the Army would implement better business practices and realign installation management to create a more efficient and effective corporate management structure for Army installations worldwide. On 1 Oct. 2002, the Army formed IMA as a field operating agency of the Assistant Chief of Staff for Installation Management (ACSIM) as part of an ongoing effort to realign installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the issues with the 15 major commands (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Major_Commands_of_the_United_States_Army" title="List of Major Commands of the United States Army">List of Major Commands of the United States Army</a>) holding responsibility for base support was that the structure created many inequities throughout the Army. There were no common standards, consistent services or an acutely managed infrastructure. This created an environment where funding was often diverted from installation support to operations. Additionally, there were too many military personnel conducting garrison support operations rather than mission duties. The creation of IMCOM was a commitment to eliminate these inequities, focus on installation management and enhance the well-being of Soldiers, Families and Civilians.</p>
<p>Centralizing installation management was a culture change in the Army; working through the transfers of personnel and funding issues was difficult. In a large organizational change, IMCOM became the Army’s single agency responsible for worldwide installation management, managing 184 Army installations globally with a staff of 120,000 military, civilian and contract members across seven regions on four continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_Army_Strong.5B18.5D">Total Army Strong<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Installation_Management_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Total Army Strong[18]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally named "<a class="external text" href="https://www.army.mil/article/5641/army-leaders-sign-covenant-with-families" rel="nofollow">The Army Family Covenant</a>" in 2007, Army leaders undertook a long-term commitment to resource and standardize critical support programs for Soldiers, their families and civilians. The covenant was focused on specific programs which commanders couldn't change. The focus was:</p>
<ul>
<li>Standardizing and funding existing family programs and services</li>
<li>Increasing accessibility and quality of healthcare</li>
<li>Improving Soldier and family housing</li>
<li>Ensuring excellence in schools, youth services, and child care</li>
<li>Expanding education and employment opportunities for family members"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 2014, the program was renamed "Total Army Strong" and commanders were given the flexibility of tailoring local programs best suit their communities.</p>
<p>The Army Family Covenant is the Army’s statement of commitment to provide high quality services to Soldiers – Active component or Reserve components, single or married, regardless of where they serve – and their Families.</p>
<p>The Installation Management Command supports the Total Army Strong<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and provides a set of tools Soldiers and Army Families can use to locate and access the facilities and services they need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>IMCOM currently manages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Workforce of 54,000</li>
<li>28 airfields</li>
<li>2,643 miles (4,253 km) of railroad</li>
