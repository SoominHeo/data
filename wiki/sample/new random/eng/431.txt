<p>The <b>plus and minus signs</b> (<b>+</b> and <b>−</b>) are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_symbol" title="Mathematical symbol">mathematical symbols</a> used to represent the notions of <a href="/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)" title="Sign (mathematics)">positive and negative</a> as well as the operations of <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">addition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">subtraction</a>. Their use has been extended to many other meanings, more or less analogous. <i>Plus</i> and <i>minus</i> are <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> terms meaning "more" and "less", respectively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the signs now seem as familiar as the <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu-Arabic_numerals" title="Hindu-Arabic numerals">Hindu-Arabic numerals</a>, they are not of great antiquity. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">Egyptian hieroglyphic</a> sign for addition, for example, resembled a pair of legs walking in the direction in which the text was written (<a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> could be written either from right to left or left to right), with the reverse sign indicating subtraction:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<table align="center">
<tr>
<td>
<table class="mw-hiero-table mw-hiero-outer" dir="ltr">
<tr>
<td>
<table class="mw-hiero-table">
<tr>
<td><img alt="D54" height="18" src="/w/extensions/wikihiero/img/hiero_D54.png?75932" style="margin: 1px;" title="D54"/></td>
<td>or</td>
<td><img alt="D55" height="18" src="/w/extensions/wikihiero/img/hiero_D55.png?34f6c" style="margin: 1px;" title="D55"/></td>
</tr>
</table>

<p><a href="/wiki/Nicole_Oresme" title="Nicole Oresme">Nicole Oresme</a>'s manuscripts from the 14th century show what may be one of the earliest uses of the plus sign "+".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe in the early 15th century the letters "P" and "M" were generally used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The symbols (P with line <i>p̄</i> for <i>più</i>, i.e., plus, and M with line <i>m̄</i> for <i>meno</i>, i.e., minus) appeared for the first time in <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a>’s mathematics compendium, <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_de_arithmetica" title="Summa de arithmetica">Summa de arithmetica, geometria, proportioni et proportionalità</a></i>, first printed and published in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1494.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <b>+</b> is a simplification of the Latin "et" (comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Ampersand" title="Ampersand">ampersand</a> <b>&amp;</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <b>−</b> may be derived from a <a href="/wiki/Tilde" title="Tilde">tilde</a> written over <b>m</b> when used to indicate subtraction; or it may come from a shorthand version of the letter m itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In his 1489 treatise <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Widmann" title="Johannes Widmann">Johannes Widmann</a> referred to the symbols − and + as <i>minus</i> and <i>mer</i> (Modern German <i>mehr</i>; "more"): "was − ist, das ist minus, und das + ist das mer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A book published by <a href="/wiki/Henricus_Grammateus" title="Henricus Grammateus">Henricus Grammateus</a> in 1518 makes another early use of + and − for addition and subtraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Recorde" title="Robert Recorde">Robert Recorde</a>, the designer of the <a href="/wiki/Equals_sign" title="Equals sign">equals sign</a>, introduced plus and minus to Britain in 1557 in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Whetstone_of_Witte" title="The Whetstone of Witte">The Whetstone of Witte</a></i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> "There be other 2 signes in often use of which the first is made thus + and betokeneth more: the other is thus made – and betokeneth lesse."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plus_sign">Plus sign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plus sign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"+" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/%2B_(disambiguation)" title="+ (disambiguation)">+ (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The plus sign (<b>+</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_operator" title="Binary operator">binary operator</a> that indicates <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">addition</a>, as in 2 + 3 = 5. It can also serve as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unary_operator" title="Unary operator">unary operator</a> that leaves its <a href="/wiki/Operand" title="Operand">operand</a> <a href="/wiki/Identity_function" title="Identity function">unchanged</a> (+<i>x</i> means the same as <i>x</i>). This notation may be used when it is desired to emphasize the positiveness of a number, especially when contrasting with the negative (+5 versus −5).</p>
<p>The plus sign can also indicate many other operations, depending on the mathematical system under consideration. Many <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> have some operation which is called, or is equivalent to, addition. It is conventional to use the plus sign to only denote <a href="/wiki/Commutative_property" title="Commutative property">commutative operations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraleigh_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraleigh-10">[10]</a></sup> Moreover, the symbolism has been extended to very different operations; plus can also mean:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Exclusive_or" title="Exclusive or">exclusive or</a> (usually written ⊕): 1 + 1 = 0, 1 + 0 = 1</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Logical_disjunction" title="Logical disjunction">logical disjunction</a> (usually written ∨): 1 + 1 = 1, 1 + 0 = 1</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minus_sign">Minus sign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minus sign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The minus sign (<b>−</b>) has three main uses in mathematics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">subtraction</a> operator: A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_operator" title="Binary operator">binary operator</a> to indicate the operation of subtraction, as in 5 − 3 = 2. Subtraction is the inverse of addition.</li>
<li>Directly <a href="/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)" title="Sign (mathematics)">in front of</a> a number and when it is not a subtraction operator it means a <a href="/wiki/Negative_number" title="Negative number">negative number</a>. For instance −5 is negative 5.</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unary_operator" title="Unary operator">unary operator</a> that acts as an instruction to replace the operand by its <a href="/wiki/Additive_inverse" title="Additive inverse">additive inverse</a>. For example, if <i>x</i> is 3, then −<i>x</i> is −3, but if <i>x</i> is −3, then −<i>x</i> is 3. Similarly, −(−2) is equal to 2. The above is a special case of this.</li>
</ol>
<p>All three uses can be referred to as "minus" in everyday speech. In most English-speaking countries, −5 (for example) is normally pronounced "minus five", but in modern US usage it is instead sometimes pronounced "negative five"; here, "minus" may be used by speakers born before 1950, and is still popular in some contexts, but "negative" is usually taught as the only correct reading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Further, some textbooks in the United States encourage −<i>x</i> to be read as "the opposite of <i>x</i>" or "the additive inverse of <i>x</i>" to avoid giving the impression that −<i>x</i> is necessarily negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some contexts, different glyphs are used for these meanings; for instance in the computer language <a href="/wiki/APL_(programming_language)" title="APL (programming language)">APL</a> and the expression language used by <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a> graphing calculators (definitely at least the early models including the <a href="/wiki/TI-81" title="TI-81">TI-81</a> and <a href="/wiki/TI-82" title="TI-82">TI-82</a>) a raised minus sign is used in negative numbers (as in 2 − 5 shows <sup>−</sup>3), but such usage is uncommon.</p>
<p>In mathematics and most programming languages, the rules for the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_operations" title="Order of operations">order of operations</a> mean that −5<sup>2</sup> is equal to −25: Powers bind more strongly than the unary minus, which binds more strongly than multiplication or division. However, in some programming languages and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Excel" title="Microsoft Excel">Microsoft Excel</a> in particular, unary operators bind strongest, so in those cases −5^2 is 25 but 0−5^2 is −25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_elementary_education">Use in elementary education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use in elementary education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some elementary teachers use raised plus and minus signs before numbers to show they are positive or negative numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> For example, subtracting −5 from 3 might be read as "positive three take away negative 5" and be shown as</p>
<dl>
<dd>3 − <sup>−</sup>5 becomes 3 + 5 = 8,</dd>
</dl>
<p>or even as</p>
<dl>
<dd><sup>+</sup>3 − <sup>−</sup>5 becomes <sup>+</sup>3 + <sup>+</sup>5 = <sup>+</sup>8.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_a_qualifier">Use as a qualifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use as a qualifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In grading systems (such as examination marks), the plus sign indicates a grade one level higher and the minus sign a grade lower. For example, B− ("B minus") is one grade lower than B. Sometimes this is extended to two plus or minus signs; for example A++ is two grades higher than A.</p>
<p>Positive and negative are sometimes abbreviated as +ve and −ve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In mathematics the <a href="/wiki/One-sided_limit" title="One-sided limit">one-sided limit</a> <i>x</i>→<i>a</i><sup>+</sup> means <i>x</i> approaches <i>a</i> from the right, and <i>x</i>→<i>a</i><sup>−</sup> means <i>x</i> approaches <i>a</i> from the left. For example, when calculating what <i>x</i><sup>−1</sup> is when <i>x</i> approaches 0, because <i>x</i><sup>−1</sup>→<a href="/wiki/Extended_real_number_line" title="Extended real number line">+∞</a> when <i>x</i>→0<sup>+</sup> but <i>x</i><sup>−1</sup>→−∞ when <i>x</i>→0<sup>−</sup>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_types" title="Blood types">Blood types</a> are often qualified with a plus or minus to indicate the presence or absence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh_factor" title="Rh factor">Rh factor</a>; for instance, A+ means <a href="/wiki/ABO_blood_group_system" title="ABO blood group system">A-type blood</a> with the Rh factor present, while B− means B-type blood with the Rh factor absent.</p>
<p>In music, <a href="/wiki/Augmented_triad#Augmented_chord_table" title="Augmented triad">augmented chords</a> are symbolized with a plus sign, although this practice is not universal as there are other methods for spelling those chords. For example, "C+" is read "C augmented chord". Also used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superscript" title="Superscript">superscript</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_computing">Uses in computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses in computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As well as the normal mathematical usage plus and minus may be used for a number of other purposes in computing.</p>
<p>Plus and minus signs are often used in <a href="/wiki/Tree_view" title="Tree view">tree view</a> on a computer screen to show if a folder is collapsed or not.</p>
<p>In some programming languages, <a href="/wiki/Concatenation" title="Concatenation">concatenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">strings</a> is written: <code>"a" + "b"</code> which equals <code>"ab"</code>.</p>
<p>In most programming languages, subtraction and negation are indicated with the ASCII <a href="/wiki/Hyphen-minus" title="Hyphen-minus">hyphen-minus</a> character, <code>-</code>. In <a href="/wiki/APL_(programming_language)" title="APL (programming language)">APL</a> a raised minus sign (Unicode U+00AF) is used to denote a negative number, as in ¯3) and in <a href="/wiki/J_(programming_language)" title="J (programming language)">J</a> a negative number is denoted by an <a href="/wiki/Underscore" title="Underscore">underscore</a>, as in _5.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> and some other computer programming languages, two plus signs indicate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Increment_operator" title="Increment operator">increment</a> operator and two minus signs a decrement. For example, <code>x++</code> means "increment the value of x by one" and <code>x--</code> means "decrement the value of x by one". By extension, "++" is sometimes used in computing terminology to signify an improvement, as in the name of the language <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>.</p>
<p>There is no concept of negative zero in mathematics, but in computing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E2%88%920_(number)" title="−0 (number)">−0</a> may have a separate representation from zero. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_floating-point_standard" title="IEEE floating-point standard">IEEE floating-point standard</a> 1/−0 is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_infinity" title="Negative infinity">negative infinity</a> (−∞) whereas 1/0 is <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">positive infinity</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E2%88%9E" title="∞">∞</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In chemistry, the minus sign (rather than an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/En_dash" title="En dash">en dash</a>) is used for a <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">single covalent bond</a> between two atoms, as in the <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula" title="Skeletal formula">skeletal formula</a>.</p>
<p>Subscripted plus and minus signs are used as diacritics in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> to indicate <a href="/wiki/Relative_articulation" title="Relative articulation">advanced or retracted articulations</a> of speech sounds.</p>
<p>The minus sign is also used as tone letter in the orthographies of <a href="/wiki/Dan_language" title="Dan language">Dan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krumen_language" title="Krumen language">Krumen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karaboro_languages" title="Karaboro languages">Karaboro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mwan_language" title="Mwan language">Mwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wan_language" title="Wan language">Wan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaour%C3%A9" title="Yaouré">Yaouré</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W%C3%A8_language" title="Wè language">Wè</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nyabwa_language" title="Nyabwa language">Nyabwa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godie_language" title="Godie language">Godié</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Unicode character used for the tone letter (U+02D7) is different from the mathematical minus sign.</p>

<p>The <b>hyphen-minus sign</b> (-) is the <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> alternative/version of the minus sign, and doubles as a <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphen</a>. It is usually shorter in length than the plus sign and sometimes at a different height. It can be used as a substitute for the true minus sign when the character set is limited to <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a>. Most programming languages and other computer readable languages do this, since ASCII is generally available as a subset of most character encodings, while U+2212 is a Unicode feature only.</p>
<p>There is a <b>commercial minus sign</b> (⁒), which looks somewhat like an <a href="/wiki/Obelus" title="Obelus">obelus</a>, at U+2052 (HTML <tt>&amp;#x2052;</tt>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_plus_sign">Alternative plus sign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plus_and_minus_signs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alternative plus sign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Up_tack" title="Up tack">Up tack</a></div>
<div style="float:right; margin: 0 0 10px 10px; padding:40px; font-size:100%; font-family: Georgia; background-color: #ddddff; border: 1px solid #aaaaff;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altplus.svg"><img alt="Altplus.svg" data-file-height="56" data-file-width="80" height="35" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Altplus.svg/50px-Altplus.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Altplus.svg/75px-Altplus.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Altplus.svg/100px-Altplus.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> tradition that dates from at least the 19th century is to write <i>plus</i> using a symbol like an inverted T. This practice was adopted into <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> schools and is still commonplace today in <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary schools</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular">secular</a> schools) but in fewer <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary schools</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_California_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_California-18">[18]</a></sup> It is also used occasionally in books by religious authors, but most books for adults use the international symbol "+". The usual explanation for this practice is that it avoids the writing of a symbol "+" that looks like a <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">Christian cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_California_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_California-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> has this symbol at position <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+FB29</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">﬩</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">HEBREW LETTER ALTERNATIVE PLUS SIGN</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
