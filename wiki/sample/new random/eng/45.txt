<p>A <b>peplos</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">ὁ πέπλος</span>) is a body-length garment established as typical attire for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women_in_ancient_Greece" title="Women in ancient Greece">women in ancient Greece</a> by 500 BC (the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical period</a>). It was a long, tubular cloth with the top edge folded down about halfway, so that what was the top of the tube was now draped below the waist, and the bottom of the tube was at the ankle. The garment was then gathered about the waist and the folded top edge pinned over the shoulders. The folded-down top of the tube provided the appearance of a second piece of clothing. (The <a href="/wiki/Caryatid" title="Caryatid">Caryatid</a> statues show a typical drapery.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-met-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The peplos was draped and open on one side of the body, like the <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Doric</a> <a href="/wiki/Chiton_(costume)" title="Chiton (costume)">chiton</a>. It should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionic</a> chiton, which was a piece of fabric folded over and sewn together along the longer side to form a tube. The Classical garment is represented in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_vase_painting" title="Greek vase painting">Greek vase painting</a> from the 5th century BC and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metope_(architecture)" title="Metope (architecture)">metopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temples</a> in <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric order</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Women_in_ancient_Sparta" title="Women in ancient Sparta">Spartan women</a> continued to wear the peplos much later in history than other Greek cultures, causing other Greeks to call them phainomērídes (φαινομηρίδες) the "thigh-showers."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rituals">Rituals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peplos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rituals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the last day of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyanepsion" title="Pyanepsion">Pyanepsion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priestess</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athena_Polias" title="Athena Polias">Athena Polias</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arrephoros" title="Arrephoros">Arrephoroi</a>, a group of girls chosen to help in the making of the sacred peplos, set up the loom on which the enormous peplos was to be woven by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ergastinai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ergastinai (page does not exist)">Ergastinai</a>, another group of girls chosen to spend about nine months making the sacred peplos. They had to weave a theme of <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>'s defeat of <a href="/wiki/Enceladus_(mythology)" title="Enceladus (mythology)">Enkelados</a> and the Olympian's defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">Giants</a>. The peplos of the statue was changed each year during the <a href="/wiki/Plynteria" title="Plynteria">Plynteria</a>.</p>
<p>The peplos played a role in the Athenian festival of the Great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panathenaea" title="Panathenaea">Panathenaea</a>. Nine months before the festival, at the arts and crafts festival titled <i>Chalkeia</i>, a special peplos would begin to be woven by young women. This peplos was placed on the statue of Athena during the festival procession. The peplos had myths and stories woven into its material and usually consisted of purple and saffron yellow cloth.</p>
