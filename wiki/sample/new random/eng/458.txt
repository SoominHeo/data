<p><b>MPEG transport stream</b> (<b>MPEG-TS</b>, <b>MTS</b> or <b>TS</b>) is a standard <a href="/wiki/Digital_container_format" title="Digital container format">digital container format</a> for transmission and storage of <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">audio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_video" title="Digital video">video</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Program_and_System_Information_Protocol" title="Program and System Information Protocol">Program and System Information Protocol</a> (PSIP) data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afterdawn-ts_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-afterdawn-ts-3">[3]</a></sup> It is used in broadcast systems such as <a href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcasting" title="Digital Video Broadcasting">Digital Video Broadcasting</a> (DVB), <a href="/wiki/ATSC_standards" title="ATSC standards">ATSC</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPTV" title="IPTV">IPTV</a>.</p>
<p>Transport stream specifies a container format encapsulating <a href="/wiki/Packetized_elementary_stream" title="Packetized elementary stream">packetized elementary streams</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Error_detection_and_correction" title="Error detection and correction">error correction</a> and stream synchronization features for maintaining transmission integrity when the signal is <a href="/wiki/Degradation_(telecommunications)" title="Degradation (telecommunications)">degraded</a>.</p>
<p>Transport streams differ from the similarly named <a href="/wiki/MPEG_program_stream" title="MPEG program stream">MPEG program stream</a> in several important ways: program streams are designed for reasonably reliable media, such as discs (like <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a>), while transport streams are designed for less <a href="/wiki/Reliability_(computer_networking)" title="Reliability (computer networking)">reliable</a> transmission, namely <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">terrestrial</a> or <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite broadcast</a>. Further, a transport stream may carry multiple programs.</p>
<p>Transport stream is specified in <i><a href="/wiki/MPEG-2" title="MPEG-2">MPEG-2</a> Part 1, Systems</i>, formally known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO/IEC" title="ISO/IEC">ISO/IEC</a> standard 13818-1</i> or <i>ITU-T Rec. H.222.0</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itu-spec_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-itu-spec-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers_of_communication">Layers of communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Layers of communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="800" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg/400px-MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg/600px-MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg/800px-MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MPEG_Transport_Stream_HL.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>A transport stream encapsulates a number of other substreams, often <a href="/wiki/Packetized_elementary_stream" title="Packetized elementary stream">packetized elementary streams</a> (PES) which in turn wrap the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_stream" title="Elementary stream">main data stream</a> of an MPEG codec, as well as any number of non-MPEG codecs (such as <a href="/wiki/Dolby_Digital" title="Dolby Digital">AC3</a> or <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS</a> audio, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MJPEG" title="MJPEG">MJPEG</a> or <a href="/wiki/JPEG_2000" title="JPEG 2000">JPEG 2000</a> video), text and pictures for subtitles, tables identifying the streams, and even broadcaster-specific information such as an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_program_guide" title="Electronic program guide">electronic program guide</a>. Many unrelated streams are often mixed together, such as several different television channels, or multiple <a href="/wiki/DVD-Video#Chapters_and_angles" title="DVD-Video">angles</a> of a movie. Each stream is chopped into (at most) 188-byte sections and interleaved together; because of the tiny packet size, streams can be interleaved with less latency and greater error resilience compared to <a href="/wiki/MPEG_program_stream" title="MPEG program stream">program streams</a> and common containers such as <a href="/wiki/Audio_Video_Interleave" title="Audio Video Interleave">AVI</a>, <a href="/wiki/QuickTime_File_Format" title="QuickTime File Format">MOV</a>/<a href="/wiki/MPEG-4_Part_14" title="MPEG-4 Part 14">MP4</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matroska" title="Matroska">MKV</a>, which generally wrap each frame into one packet. This is particularly important for videoconferencing, where even one large frame may introduce unacceptable audio delay.</p>
<p>Transport streams tend to be broadcast as <a href="/wiki/Constant_bitrate" title="Constant bitrate">constant bitrate</a> (CBR) to maintain a consistent broadcast rate, filled with padding bytes when not enough data exists, although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-Ray" title="Blu-Ray">Blu-Ray</a> format does not require CBR.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_elements_of_a_transport_stream">Important elements of a transport stream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Important elements of a transport stream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Packet">Packet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Packet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A packet is the basic unit of data in a transport stream, and a transport stream is merely a sequence of packets, without any global header. Each packet starts with a <a href="/wiki/Syncword" title="Syncword">sync byte</a> and a header, that may be followed with optional additional headers; the rest of the packet consists of payload. All header fields are read as <a href="/wiki/Endianness#Big-endian" title="Endianness">big-endian</a>. Packets are 188 bytes in length, but the communication medium may add additional information: <a href="/wiki/Forward_error_correction" title="Forward error correction">Forward error correction</a> is added by <a href="/wiki/ISDB" title="ISDB">ISDB</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcasting" title="Digital Video Broadcasting">DVB</a> (16 bytes) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Television_Systems_Committee_standards" title="Advanced Television Systems Committee standards">ATSC</a> (20 bytes),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/.m2ts" title=".m2ts">M2TS</a> format prefixes packets with a 4-byte copyright and timestamp tag. The 188-byte packet size was originally chosen for compatibility with <a href="/wiki/Asynchronous_Transfer_Mode" title="Asynchronous Transfer Mode">ATM systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Packet_Identifier_.28PID.29">Packet Identifier (PID)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Packet Identifier (PID)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each table or elementary stream in a transport stream is identified by a 13-bit packet identifier (PID). A <a href="/wiki/Inverse_multiplexer" title="Inverse multiplexer">demultiplexer</a> extracts elementary streams from the transport stream in part by looking for packets identified by the same PID. In most applications, <a href="/wiki/Time-division_multiplexing" title="Time-division multiplexing">time-division multiplexing</a> will be used to decide how often a particular PID appears in the transport stream.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transport stream has a concept of programs. Every single program is described by a Program Map Table (PMT) which has a unique PID, and the elementary streams associated with that program have PIDs listed in the PMT. For instance, a transport stream used in digital television might contain three programs, to represent three television channels. Suppose each channel consists of one video stream, one or two audio streams, and any necessary metadata. A <a href="/wiki/ATSC_tuner" title="ATSC tuner">receiver</a> wishing to decode a particular "channel" merely has to decode the payloads of each PID associated with its program. It can discard the contents of all other PIDs. A transport stream with more than one program is referred to as MPTS - Multi Program Transport Stream. A single program transport stream is referred to as SPTS - Single Program Transport Stream.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Program_Specific_Information_.28PSI.29">Program Specific Information (PSI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Program Specific Information (PSI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Program_Specific_Information" title="Program Specific Information">Program Specific Information</a></div>
<p>There are 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Program_Specific_Information" title="Program Specific Information">PSI</a> tables: Program Association (PAT), Program Map (PMT), Conditional Access (CAT), and Network Information (NIT). The MPEG-2 specification does not specify the format of the CAT and NIT.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PAT">PAT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: PAT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>PAT stands for Program Association Table. It lists all programs available in the transport stream. Each of the listed programs is identified by a 16-bit value called <b>program_number</b>. Each of the programs listed in PAT has an associated value of PID for its Program Map Table (PMT).</p>
<p>The value 0x0000 of <b>program_number</b> is reserved to specify the PID where to look for Network Information Table (NIT). If such a program is not present in PAT the default PID value (0x0010) shall be used for NIT.</p>
<p>TS Packets containing PAT information always have PID 0x0000.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PMT">PMT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: PMT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Program Map Tables (PMTs) contain information about programs. For each program, there is one PMT. While the MPEG-2 standard permits more than one PMT section to be transmitted on a single PID (Single Transport stream PID contains PMT information of more than one program), most MPEG-2 "users" such as ATSC and SCTE require each PMT to be transmitted on a separate PID that is not used for any other packets. The PMTs provide information on each program present in the transport stream, including the program_number, and list the elementary streams that comprise the described MPEG-2 program. There are also locations for optional descriptors that describe the entire MPEG-2 program, as well as an optional descriptor for each elementary stream. Each elementary stream is labeled with a stream_type value.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PCR">PCR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: PCR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To enable a decoder to present synchronized content, such as audio tracks matching the associated video, at least once each 100 ms a <i>Program Clock Reference</i>, or PCR is transmitted in the adaptation field of an MPEG-2 transport stream packet. The PID with the PCR for an MPEG-2 program is identified by the pcr_pid value in the associated Program Map Table. The value of the PCR, when properly used, is employed to generate a system_timing_clock in the decoder. The STC or System Time Clock decoder, when properly implemented, provides a highly accurate time base that is used to synchronize audio and video elementary streams. Timing in MPEG2 references this clock. For example, the presentation time stamp (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presentation_time_stamp" title="Presentation time stamp">PTS</a>) is intended to be relative to the PCR. The first 33 bits are based on a 90 kHz clock. The last 9 are based on a 27 MHz clock. The maximum jitter permitted for the PCR is +/- 500 ns.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Null_packets">Null packets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Null packets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some transmission schemes, such as those in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATSC_Standards" title="ATSC Standards">ATSC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcasting" title="Digital Video Broadcasting">DVB</a>, impose strict constant bitrate requirements on the transport stream. In order to ensure that the stream maintains a constant bitrate, a <a href="/wiki/Multiplexer" title="Multiplexer">Multiplexer</a> may need to insert some additional packets. The PID 0x1FFF is reserved for this purpose. The payload of null packets may not contain any data at all, and the receiver is expected to ignore its contents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_digital_video_cameras">Use in digital video cameras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Use in digital video cameras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transport Stream had been originally designed for broadcast. Later it was adapted for usage with digital video cameras, recorders and players by adding a 4-byte timecode (TC) to standard 188-byte packets, which resulted in a 192-byte packet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-av_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bda-av-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-bdre_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bda-bdre-12">[12]</a></sup> This is what is informally called <a href="/wiki/.m2ts" title=".m2ts">M2TS</a> stream. The <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc_Association" title="Blu-ray Disc Association">Blu-ray Disc Association</a> calls it "<a href="/wiki/.m2ts" title=".m2ts">BDAV MPEG-2 transport stream</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-av_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-bda-av-11">[11]</a></sup> JVC called it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOD_and_TOD_(video_format)" title="MOD and TOD (video format)">TOD</a> (possibly an abbreviation for "Transport stream on disc") when used in HDD-based camcorders like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JVC_GZ-HD7" title="JVC GZ-HD7">GZ-HD7</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The timecode allows quick access to any part of the stream either from a media player, or from a non-linear video editing system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is also used to synchronize video streams from several cameras in a multi-camera shoot.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Blu-ray">Use in Blu-ray</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MPEG_transport_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Use in Blu-ray">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Filename extension <a href="/wiki/.m2ts" title=".m2ts">.m2ts</a> is used on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> Video for files which contain an incompatible BDAV MPEG-2 transport stream due to the four additional octets added to every packet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-av_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-bda-av-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bdav-videohelp_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-bdav-videohelp-16">[16]</a></sup> Blu-ray Disc Video titles authored with menu support are in the BDMV (Blu-ray Disc Movie) format and contain audio, video, and other streams in a BDAV container, which is based on the MPEG-2 transport stream format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bdvm_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bdvm-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bdav_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bdav-18">[18]</a></sup> There is also the BDAV (Blu-ray Disc Audio/Visual) format, the consumer oriented alternative to the BDMV format used for movie releases. The BDAV format is used on BD-REs and BD-Rs for audio/video recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bdav_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-bdav-18">[18]</a></sup> Blu-ray Disc employs the MPEG-2 transport stream recording method. That enables transport streams of a BDAV converted digital broadcast to be recorded as they are with minimal alteration of the packets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-bdre_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-bda-bdre-12">[12]</a></sup> It also enables simple stream cut style editing of a BDAV converted digital broadcast that is recorded as is and where the data can be edited just by discarding unwanted packets from the stream. Although it is quite natural, a function for high-speed and easy-to-use retrieval is built in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda-bdre_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-bda-bdre-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bda_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bda-19">[19]</a></sup> Blu-ray Disc Video uses these modified MPEG-2 transport streams, compared to DVD's program streams that don't have the extra transport overhead.</p>
