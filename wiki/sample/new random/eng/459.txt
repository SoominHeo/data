<p>The <b>.338 Lapua Magnum</b> (8.6×70mm or 8.58×70mm) is a rimless, bottlenecked, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centerfire" title="Centerfire">centerfire</a> rifle <a href="/wiki/Cartridge_(firearms)" title="Cartridge (firearms)">cartridge</a>. It was developed during the 1980s as a high-powered, long-range cartridge for military <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">snipers</a>. The United States used it in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Afghanistan War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>. As a result of this, it is now widely available. The loaded cartridge is 14.93 mm (0.588 in) in diameter (rim) and 93.5 mm (3.68 in) long. It can penetrate better-than-standard military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_armour" title="Body armour">body armour</a> at ranges up to 1,000 metres (1,090 yd) and has a maximum effective range of about 1,750 metres (1,910 yd). Muzzle velocity is dependent on barrel length, seating depth and powder charge, and varies from 880 to 915 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a> (2,890 to 3,000 ft/s) for commercial loads with 16.2-gram (250 gr) bullets, which corresponds to about 6,525 J (4,813 ft·lbf) of muzzle energy.</p>
<p>British military issue overpressure .338 Lapua Magnum cartridges with a 91.4 mm (3.60 in) overall length, loaded with 16.2-gram (250 gr) LockBase B408 <a href="/wiki/Very-low-drag_bullet" title="Very-low-drag bullet">very-low-drag bullets</a> fired at 936 m/s (3,071 ft/s) muzzle velocity from a <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_International_AWM#British_Armed_Forces" title="Accuracy International AWM">L115A3 Long Range Rifle</a> were used in November 2009 by British sniper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporal_of_Horse" title="Corporal of Horse">Corporal of Horse</a> (CoH) <a href="/wiki/Craig_Harrison_(sniper)" title="Craig Harrison (sniper)">Craig Harrison</a> to establish a new record for the <a href="/wiki/Longest_recorded_sniper_kills" title="Longest recorded sniper kills">longest confirmed sniper kill in combat</a>, at a range of 2,475 m (2,707 yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to its military role, it is increasingly used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big-game_hunter" title="Big-game hunter">hunters</a> and civilian long-range shooting enthusiasts. The .338 Lapua Magnum is capable of taking down any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_animal" title="Game animal">game animal</a>, though its suitability for some dangerous game (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_buffalo" title="Cape buffalo">Cape buffalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamus</a>, white <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceros</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a>) is arguable, unless accompanied by a larger "backup" caliber: "There is a huge difference between calibers that will kill an elephant and those that can be relied upon to stop one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In Namibia the .338 Lapua Magnum is legal for hunting Africa's <a href="/wiki/Big_five_game" title="Big five game">Big five game</a> if the loads have ≥ 5,400 J (3,983 ft·lbf) muzzle energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_development">Initial development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initial development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1983, Research Armament Industries (RAI) in the United States began development of a new, long-range sniper cartridge capable of firing a 16.2-gram (250 gr), 0.338-inch (8.6 mm) diameter bullet at 914 metres per second (3,000 ft/s) that could lethally penetrate five layers of military body armour at 1,000 m (1,094 yd). After preliminary experiments, a <a href="/wiki/.416_Rigby" title=".416 Rigby">.416 Rigby</a> case necked down to take a 0.338-inch (8.6 mm) <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullet</a> was selected, since this diameter presents an optimum of <a href="/wiki/Sectional_density" title="Sectional density">sectional density</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penetration_(weapons)" title="Penetration (weapons)">penetrating capability</a> for practical spin stabilized rifle bullets (bullets up to about 5 to 5.5 calibers in length).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The .416 Rigby is an English big game cartridge that was designed to accommodate 325 MPa (47,137 psi) pressures. One of the disadvantages of these old cartridge cases, which were intended for firing <a href="/wiki/Cordite" title="Cordite">cordite</a> charges instead of modern <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a>, is the thickness of the sidewall just forward of the web. During ignition, the cartridge's base, forward to the bolt face, is not supported. The case is driven back against the bolt face, which results in the stretching of the case, particularly the sidewall immediately forward of the web. When the sidewall resists the outward expansion against the chamber, the pressure stretches the case, thereby increasing its length, resulting in the sidewall becoming thinner at that stretch point.</p>
<p>During the process RAI employed Brass Extrusion Labs Ltd. (BELL) of Bensenville, Illinois, to make the <b>.338/416</b> or <b>8.58×71mm</b> cartridge cases, <a href="/wiki/Hornady" title="Hornady">Hornady</a> produced bullets, and RAI built a sniper rifle under contract for the U.S. Navy. RAI found that the BELL cases did not fulfill the requirements. Pressed by military deadlines RAI looked for another case producer and contacted Lapua of Finland in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> RAI was forced to drop out of the program due to financial difficulties. Subsequently, Lapua of Finland put this cartridge into limited production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotw_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cotw-10">[10]</a></sup> The .338/416 rifle program was later canceled when the contractors were unable to make the cartridge meet the project's velocity target of 914 m/s (3,000 ft/s) for a 16.2 g (250 gr) bullet, due to weak brass cases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_development">Final development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Final development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current .338 Lapua Magnum cartridge was developed as a joint venture between the Finnish rifle manufacturer <a href="/wiki/SAKO" title="SAKO">SAKO</a> and the British rifle manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_International" title="Accuracy International">Accuracy International</a> along with the Finnish ammunition manufacturer Lapua, or more officially Nammo Lapua Oy, which since 1998 is part of the <a href="/wiki/Nammo" title="Nammo">Nordic Ammunition Group (Nammo)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lapua opted to redesign the .338/416 cartridge. In the new case design, particular attention was directed toward thickening and metallurgically strengthening the case's web and sidewall immediately forward of the web. In modern solid head cases, the hardness of the brass is the major factor that determines a case's pressure limit before undergoing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" title="Plastic deformation">plastic deformation</a>. Lapua tackled this problem by creating a hardness distribution ranging from the head and web (hard) to the mouth (soft) as well as a strengthened (thicker) case web and sidewall immediately forward of the web. This resulted in a very pressure resistant case, allowing it to operate at high pressure and come within 15 m/s (50 ft/s) of the original velocity goal. Lapua also designed a 16.2-gram (250 gr) .338 calibre Lock Base B408 <a href="/wiki/Full_metal_jacket_bullet" title="Full metal jacket bullet">full metal jacket bullet</a>, modeled after its .30 calibre Lock Base bullet configuration. The result was the .338 Lapua Magnum cartridge which was registered with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a> (<a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_Permanente_pour_l%27Epreuve_des_Armes_%C3%A0_Feu_Portatives" title="Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives">Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives</a>) in 1989. With the procurement by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Army" title="Dutch Army">Dutch Army</a>, the cartridge became NATO codified.</p>
<p>The .338 Lapua Magnum fills the gap between weapons chambered for standard military rounds such as the <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> and large, weighty rifles firing the <a href="/wiki/.50_BMG" title=".50 BMG">.50 BMG</a> cartridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It also offers a tolerable amount of barrel wear, which is important to military snipers who tend to fire thousands of rounds a year in practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[notes 1]</a></sup> Like every other comparable big magnum rifle cartridge the .338 Lapua Magnum presents a stout <a href="/wiki/Free_recoil" title="Free recoil">recoil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> An appropriate fitting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_(firearm)" title="Stock (firearm)">stock</a> and an effective <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_brake" title="Muzzle brake">muzzle brake</a> will help to reduce recoil induced problems, enabling the operator to fire more rounds before getting too uncomfortable to shoot accurately. Good factory loads, multiple projectile weights and factory special application ammunition are all available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[notes 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its growing civilian popularity, several high quality tactical and match (semi) custom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolt_actions" title="Bolt actions">bolt actions</a> designed for the .338 Lapua Magnum are becoming available. These (semi) custom bolt actions are used with other high grade rifle and sighting components to build custom sporting and target rifles.</p>

<p>The .338 Lapua Magnum has been designated a "cartridge of interest" by the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Industrial_Association" title="National Defense Industrial Association">National Defense Industrial Association</a> (NDIA). It is being groomed to replace the <a href="/wiki/.300_Winchester_Magnum" title=".300 Winchester Magnum">.300 Winchester Magnum</a> and the .50 BMG for anti-personnel long-range service in the U.S. military. On June 17, 2008, the U.S. government issued a market survey to support a requirement for a Precision Sniper Rifle (PRS) to possibly replace the currently fielded Bolt Action SOF Sniper Systems MK 13 (.300 Winchester Magnum) and the <a href="/wiki/M40_rifle" title="M40 rifle">M40</a> and <a href="/wiki/M24_Sniper_Weapon_System" title="M24 Sniper Weapon System">M24</a> (7.62×51mm NATO) chambered to safely fire factory produced "non-wildcat" .338 caliber ammunition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[58]</a></sup> On 7 March 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Remington_MSR" title="Remington MSR">Remington MSR</a> was declared the winner of the <a href="/wiki/Precision_Sniper_Rifle" title="Precision Sniper Rifle">Precision Sniper Rifle</a> competition. Remington announced that the MSR had won on March 8, and it was publicly confirmed on March 9. This was followed by a $79.7 million contract for 5,150 rifles with suppressors, along with 4,696,800 rounds of ammunition to be supplied over the next ten years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winner_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-winner-61">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[60]</a></sup> The contract was awarded on 12 September 2013. Remington Defense produces the sniper rifles and utilizes two other companies for other system components, with <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Group#Companies" title="Freedom Group">Barnes Bullets</a> for ammunition and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Armament_Corporation" title="Advanced Armament Corporation">Advanced Armament Corporation</a> for muzzle brakes and suppressors; all three companies are subsidiaries of <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Group" title="Freedom Group">Freedom Group Incorporated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridge_dimensions">Cartridge dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cartridge dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Extremely thick-walled brass results in a 7.40 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a> (114 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(measure)" title="Grain (measure)">grains</a> H<sub>2</sub>O) cartridge case capacity for the .338 Lapua Magnum. The exterior shape of the case was designed to promote reliable feeding and extraction in <a href="/wiki/Bolt_action" title="Bolt action">bolt action</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_firearm" title="Semi-automatic firearm">semi-automatic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Automatic_firearm" title="Automatic firearm">automatic firearms</a> alike, under extreme conditions.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg"><img alt=".338 Lapaua Magnum.svg" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="845" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg/400px-.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg/600px-.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg/800px-.338_Lapaua_Magnum.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a></p>
<p><b>.338 Lapua Magnum maximum C.I.P. cartridge dimensions.</b> All sizes in millimeters (mm).</p>
<p>Americans would define the shoulder angle at alpha/2 ≈ 20 degrees. The common <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">twist rate</a> for this cartridge is 254 mm (1 in 10 in), 6 grooves, Ø lands = 8.38 mm, Ø grooves = 8.58 mm, land width = 2.79 mm and the <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">primer type</a> is large rifle magnum.</p>
<p>According to the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a> (Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives) decisions and tables edition 2007 the .338 Lapua Magnum case can handle up to 420.00 MPa (60,916 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. This now prevails over the C.I.P. decisions and tables edition 2003, that rated the .338 Lapua Magnum at 470.00 MPa (68,168 psi) P<sub>max</sub> maximum piezo pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[62]</a></sup> The 470.00 MPa (68,168 psi) P<sub>max</sub> maximum piezo pressure C.I.P. ruling for the <a href="/wiki/.300_Lapua_Magnum" title=".300 Lapua Magnum">.300 Lapua Magnum</a> cartridge, which is based on the same case, was not accordingly changed. In C.I.P. regulated countries every rifle cartridge combo has to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(firearms)" title="Proof (firearms)">proofed</a> at 125% of the prevailing maximum C.I.P. pressure to certify for sale to consumers. This means that .338 Lapua Magnum chambered arms in C.I.P. regulated countries are currently (2013) proof tested at 525.00 MPa (76,145 psi) PE piezo pressure.</p>
<p>Lapua is ambivalent on the maximum piezo pressure of this cartridge. In the article 'From an American dream to a Finnish success story'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[63]</a></sup> by Janne Pohjoispää Lapua propagates the C.I.P. 2007 ruling of 420.00 MPa (60,916 psi) maximum piezo pressure. To further complicate matters the mentioned 56,000 <a href="/wiki/Copper_units_of_pressure" title="Copper units of pressure">CUP</a> C.I.P. copper crusher pressure in this article would translate in ≈ 447.50 MPa (64,904 psi) C.I.P. piezo pressure according to a study on the conversion from CUP to PSI for rifle cartridges by Denton Bramwell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[64]</a></sup> The C.I.P. 2003 ruling of 470.00 MPa (68,168 psi) piezo pressure is corroborated by Lapua Australia in the 'History and development of the .338 Lapua Magnum' article by Alan C. Paulson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup> A reverse engineering simulation with <a href="/wiki/QuickLOAD" title="QuickLOAD">QuickLOAD</a> internal ballistic software predicted that Lapua load their factory .338 Lapua Magnum ammunition at ≈ 420.00 MPa (60,916 psi) piezo pressure as Alan C. Paulson asserts in his article.</p>
<p>The large boltface combined with the high maximum pressure means that the .338 Lapua Magnum should only be chambered in rifles that are capable of handling such large and fierce cartridges and thus high <a href="/wiki/Bolt_thrust" title="Bolt thrust">bolt thrust</a> safely. Chambering such powerful super magnum cartridges in rifles intended for normal magnum rifle cartridges and using high pressure loads can cause serious or fatal injury to the shooter and bystanders.</p>
<p>The American <a href="/wiki/.338-378_Weatherby_Magnum" title=".338-378 Weatherby Magnum">.338-378 Weatherby Magnum</a> cartridge introduced in 1998 and the American <a href="/wiki/.338_Remington_Ultra_Magnum" title=".338 Remington Ultra Magnum">.338 Remington Ultra Magnum</a> (.338 RUM) cartridge introduced in 2000 are probably the closest currently (2007) commercially available ballistic twins of the .338 Lapua Magnum. The .338-378 Weatherby Magnum is however a <a href="/wiki/Rim_(firearms)" title="Rim (firearms)">belted</a> cartridge and the .338 Remington Ultra Magnum is a <a href="/wiki/Rim_(firearms)" title="Rim (firearms)">rebated rim</a> cartridge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supersonic_range_performance_of_the_.338_Lapua_Magnum">Supersonic range performance of the .338 Lapua Magnum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Supersonic range performance of the .338 Lapua Magnum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_with_C.I.P._conform_cartridges">Performance with C.I.P. conform cartridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Performance with C.I.P. conform cartridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For a typical .338 Lapua Magnum high end factory military sniper rifle like the Sako <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRG-42" title="TRG-42">TRG-42</a> with a 690 mm (27.2 in) long 305 mm (1 in 12 inch) rifling twist rate barrel at sea level, 1,500 m (1,640 yd) is considered to be the maximum shooting distance for man sized targets. When using standard Lapua military 16.2 g (250 gr) loads it has a supersonic range of 1,500 m (1,640 yd) under warm summer conditions at a muzzle velocity of 915 m/s (3,000 ft/s). However, to be able to maintain 80 to 90% hit probability on non-moving 45 cm × 90 cm (17.7 in × 35.4 in) reactive army targets, this maximum shooting distance has to be reduced to 1,300 metres (1,422 yd) at freezing point conditions or 1,100 m (1,203 yd) in Arctic winter conditions, when the muzzle velocity may drop to 880 m/s (2,887 ft/s)—i.e. only during optimal warm summer conditions the 1,500 m (1,640 yd) maximum shooting distance is realistically achievable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Loaded with more aerodynamic <a href="/wiki/Very-low-drag_bullet" title="Very-low-drag bullet">very-low-drag bullets</a> such as the traditionally lead cored 19.44 g (300.0 gr) Lapua Scenar GB528 VLD bullet (<a href="/wiki/Ballistic_coefficient" title="Ballistic coefficient">G1 BC</a> = 0.736) or the Lost River Ballistics J40 .338 17.5 gram (270 gr) CNC manufactured mono-metal bullet (G1 BC = 0.871) the long-range performance and supersonic range of .338 Lapua Magnum rifles can be improved. These longer very-low-drag bullets require a 254 mm (1 in 10 inch) twist rate to stabilize them. Due to the lower practically possible muzzle velocities for a relative heavy bullet like the 19.44 g (300 gr) Lapua Scenar GB528 VLD bullet it gains about 104 m (114 yd) extra supersonic range under <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> sea level conditions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> ρ = 1.225 kg/m<sup>3</sup>) at a muzzle velocity of 837 m/s (2750 ft/s) when compared to the standard 16.2 g (250 gr) Lapua Scenar GB488 VLD at a muzzle velocity of 915 m/s (3002 ft/s). For significant supersonic range improvement the aerodynamic efficiency of the employed bullets has to be significantly improved without sacrificing a lot of practically achievable muzzle velocity - meaning that besides the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_drag" title="Coefficient of drag">coefficient of drag</a> of the projectile weight is also an important parameter for its actual downrange flight behavior. The .338 17.5 gram (270 gr) Lost River Ballistic Technologies J40 match bullet made out of a copper-nickel alloy is one of the most aerodynamic .338 calibre bullets available. It has an 1,800 m (1,970 yd) supersonic range under optimal warm summer conditions at a muzzle velocity of 869 m/s (2,850 ft/s). This makes engaging static targets up to 1,800 m (1,970 yd) feasible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_improvement_experiments_with_non-C.I.P.-conforming_cartridges">Performance improvement experiments with non-C.I.P.-conforming cartridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Performance improvement experiments with non-C.I.P.-conforming cartridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improvement beyond this standard while still using standard .338 Lapua Magnum brass is possible, but the bullets have to be very long (over 5.5 calibers in length) and the normal cartridge overall length of 93.5 mm has to be exceeded making such cartridges <a href="/wiki/Wildcat_cartridge" title="Wildcat cartridge">wildcats</a>. The common 254 mm (1:10 inch) rifling twist rate also has to be tightened to stabilize very long projectiles. Such commercially non-existent cartridges are termed <a href="/wiki/Wildcat_cartridge" title="Wildcat cartridge">"wildcats"</a>. The use of a wildcat .338 Lapua Magnum based cartridge demands the use of a custom or customized rifle with an appropriately cut chamber and fast-twist bore. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearm_action" title="Firearm action">firearm action</a> and if a repeating arm is required the <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(firearms)" title="Magazine (firearms)">magazine(s)</a> must also be able to cope with dimensional increases.</p>
<p>An example of such a special .338 calibre extreme range bullet is the German CNC manufactured mono-metal 18.92 gram (292 gr) <a href="/wiki/Very-low-drag_bullet#Modern_design" title="Very-low-drag bullet">LM-105</a> (C<sub>d</sub> = 0.2487 at <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 2.216 – this <a href="/wiki/Drag_coefficient" title="Drag coefficient">drag coefficient</a> and the corresponding G1, G7 and G8 ballistic coefficients are established by <a href="/wiki/Doppler_radar" title="Doppler radar">Doppler radar</a> <a href="/wiki/External_ballistics#Doppler_radar-measurements" title="External ballistics">measurements</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutzmoeller_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-lutzmoeller-70">[68]</a></sup> The LM-105 has a supersonic range of ≈ 1,860 metres (2,030 yd) at a muzzle velocity of 915 metres per second (3,002 ft/s) under <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> sea level conditions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> ρ = 1.225 kg/m<sup>3</sup>). The 2010 version of the LM-105 bullet has an overall length of 54.3 millimetres (2.14 in) or 6.33 calibers and derives its exceptional low drag from a radical <a href="/wiki/Nose_cone_design#Haack_series" title="Nose cone design">LD Haack</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sears-Haack_body" title="Sears-Haack body">Sears-Haack</a> profile in the bullet's nose area. Rifles chambered for this wildcat cartridge, with a cartridge overall length of 105 millimetres (4.1 in), and equipped with custom made 178 mm (1:7 inch) progressive twist rate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[69]</a></sup> 900 millimetres (35.4 in) long barrels with a 2° cone-angle (the standard C.I.P. cone-angle for the .338 Lapua Magnum is 6°) cone area finished first and second at several long range competitions. Its most recent win (2007) was in an international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Forces" title="Special Forces">Special Forces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> sniper competition in Switzerland against rifles chambered for <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> up to .50 BMG at ranges from 100 m – 1,500 m (109 yd – 1,640 yd). The LM-105 bullet exhibited its very low wind drift susceptibility notably at ranges beyond 800 metres (870 yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutzmoeller_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-lutzmoeller-70">[68]</a></sup> A real world average G1 BC of ≈ 0.83 or a G7 BC of ≈ 0.42 is commonly adopted by the users of this bullet, for making long range trajectory predictions using ballistics calculators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutzmoeller_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-lutzmoeller-70">[68]</a></sup> In contrast the LM-105 designer Lutz Möller originally calculated an optimistic G1 BC of ≈ 0.93 and a supersonic range of ≈ 2,000 metres (2,190 yd) at a muzzle velocity of 915 metres per second (3,002 ft/s) under <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> sea level conditions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> ρ = 1.225 kg/m<sup>3</sup>).</p>
<p>The .343 Lapua Magnum LM-107 was a wildcat cartridge under development based on the standard .338 Lapua Magnum cartridge case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multiref1_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-multiref1-72">[70]</a></sup> The LM-107 was hoped to boost the ballistic performance of the LM-105 by achieving an increase in supersonic range. The 19.3 g (298 gr) LM-107 projectile design is 59 millimetres (2.3 in) long and has a Haack profiled nose and an Adams profiled tail. The <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">twist rate</a> for the .343 Lapua Magnum LM-107 wildcat cartridge was chosen at 180 mm (1:7 inch), Ø lands = 8.72 mm, Ø grooves = 8.45 mm and loaded with the LM-107 projectile has a cartridge overall length of 107 millimetres (4.2 in). The length of the neck is increased from 8,31 to 8,50 mm to support the bigger LM-107 bullet. Several other dimensions of the .338 Lapua Magnum parental cartridge are also changed. The shoulder angle gets steepened from 40° to 60° and the body taper is set at 1°. The throat area is set at a 2° cone-angle. All this modifications make the .343 Lapua Magnum a fairly comprehensively revised wildcat cartridge. Out of a 900 millimetres (35.4 in) long progressive twist barrel Mr. Möller expected to achieve 909 metres per second (2,982 ft/s) muzzle velocity. If Möller's design assumptions are correct the LM-107 projectile with a calculated G1 BC of 1.02 will offer a supersonic range of ≈ 2,170 metres (2,370 yd) at a muzzle velocity of 909 metres per second (2,982 ft/s) under <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> sea level conditions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> ρ = 1.225 kg/m<sup>3</sup>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".338_Lapua_Magnum_as_a_parent_case">.338 Lapua Magnum as a parent case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: .338 Lapua Magnum as a parent case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.300_Lapua_Magnum">The .300 Lapua Magnum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The .300 Lapua Magnum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The commercially successful .338 Lapua Magnum cartridge has functioned as the parent case for the <a href="/wiki/.300_Lapua_Magnum" title=".300 Lapua Magnum">.300 Lapua Magnum</a>, which is essentially a necked-down version of the .338 Lapua Magnum. The .338 cartridge case was used for this since it has the capability to operate with high chamber pressures which, combined with smaller and hence lighter <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullets</a> result in very high muzzle velocities.</p>
<p>The Finnish ammunition manufacturer Lapua got the .300 Lapua Magnum C.I.P. certified, so it became an officially registered and sanctioned member of the Finnish "family" of super magnum rifle cartridges. The .300 Lapua Magnum is not commercially available and currently exists only as a C.I.P. datasheet. It is however still used by a few shooters who produce the cases from .338 Lapua Magnum brass by reshaping the shoulder and neck, and handloading it with .30 calibre bullets.</p>
<p>The .300 Lapua Magnum has a 7.33 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a> (113 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(measure)" title="Grain (measure)">grains</a> H<sub>2</sub>O) cartridge case capacity.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg"><img alt=".300 Lapua Magnum.svg" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="850" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg/400px-.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg/600px-.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg/800px-.300_Lapua_Magnum.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a></p>
<p><b>.300 Lapua Magnum maximum C.I.P. cartridge dimensions.</b> All sizes in millimeters (mm).</p>
<p>Americans would define the shoulder angle at alpha/2 ≈ 25 degrees. The common <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">twist rate</a> for this cartridge is 240 mm (1 in 9.45 in), 4 grooves, Ø lands = 7.62 mm, Ø grooves = 7.82 mm, land width = 4.47 mm and the <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">primer type</a> is large rifle magnum.</p>
<p>According to the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a> (Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives) rulings the .300 Lapua Magnum can handle up to 440.00 MPa (63,817 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. This now prevails over the C.I.P. decisions and tables edition 2007, that rated the .300 Lapua Magnum at 470.00 MPa (68,168 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. In C.I.P. regulated countries every rifle cartridge combo has to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(firearms)" title="Proof (firearms)">proofed</a> at 125% of this maximum C.I.P. pressure to certify for sale to consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup> This means that .300 Lapua Magnum chambered arms in C.I.P. regulated countries are currently (2013) proof tested at 550.00 MPa (79,771 psi) PE piezo pressure.</p>
<p>This for rifles very high maximum allowed chamber pressure level indicates that the cases of the .300 and .338 Lapua Magnum are built extremely sturdy to cope with this for rifles very high operating pressure. The large boltface combined with the high 470 MPa (68,000 PSI) maximum pressure makes that the .300 Lapua Magnum should only be chambered in rifles that are capable of handling such large and fierce cartridges and thus high bolt thrust safely. Chambering such powerful super magnum cartridges in rifles intended for normal magnum rifle cartridges and using 470 MPa (68,000 PSI) loads can cause serious or fatal injury to the shooter and bystanders.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_7.62_UKM">The 7.62 UKM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The 7.62 UKM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The .338 Lapua Magnum cartridge is also used as the parent case for the German designed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7.62_UKM" title="7.62 UKM">7.62 UKM</a>, which is essentially a necked-down shortened version of the .338 Lapua Magnum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup> The use of the .338 cartridge case with its capability to operate at high chamber pressures resulted in magnum case capable of producing high muzzle velocities.</p>
<p>The 7.62 UKM was developed by Michael Uekötter and was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a>-certified in 2002, making it an officially registered and sanctioned member of the Finnish "family" of super magnum rifle cartridges. The 7.62 UKM is not commercially available and currently exists only as a C.I.P. datasheet. It is however still used by a few shooters who produce the cases from .338 Lapua Magnum brass by reshaping the shoulder and neck, and handloading it with .30 calibre bullets.</p>
<p>The 7.62 UKM has a 5.84 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a> (90 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(measure)" title="Grain (measure)">grains</a> H<sub>2</sub>O) cartridge case capacity.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:7.62_UKM.jpg"><img alt="7.62 UKM.jpg" data-file-height="801" data-file-width="1126" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/7.62_UKM.jpg/400px-7.62_UKM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/7.62_UKM.jpg/600px-7.62_UKM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/7.62_UKM.jpg/800px-7.62_UKM.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a></p>

<p>Americans would define the shoulder angle at alpha/2 ≈ 20 degrees. The common <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">twist rate</a> for this cartridge is 254 mm (1 in 10 in), 6 grooves, Ø lands = 7.62 mm, Ø grooves = 7.82 mm, land width = 2.79 mm and the <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">primer type</a> is large rifle magnum.</p>
<p>According to the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a> (Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives) rulings the 7.62 UKM Magnum can handle up to 440.00 MPa (63,817 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. This now prevails over the C.I.P. decisions and tables edition 2007, that rated the 7.62 UKM at 470.00 MPa (68,168 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. In C.I.P. regulated countries every rifle cartridge combo has to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(firearms)" title="Proof (firearms)">proofed</a> at 125% of this maximum C.I.P. pressure to certify for sale to consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> This means that 7.62 UKM chambered arms in C.I.P. regulated countries are currently (2013) proof tested at 550.00 MPa (79,771 psi) PE piezo pressure.</p>

<p>The .338 Lapua Magnum case is also used as the parent case for a host of modified variants that are not officially registered with or sanctioned by C.I.P. or its American equivalent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAAMI" title="SAAMI">SAAMI</a>. Such cartridges which use commercial factory cases are generally known as <a href="/wiki/Wildcat_cartridge" title="Wildcat cartridge">wildcats</a>. By changing the shape of standard factory cases (decreasing case taper and/or changing the shoulder geometry) the wildcatter generally increases the case capacity of the factory parent cartridge case, allowing more propellant to be used to generate higher velocities. Besides changing the shape and internal volume of the parent cartridge case, wildcatters also can change the original calibre. A reason to change the original calibre can be to comply with a minimal permitted calibre or bullet weight for the legal hunting of certain species of game. Because the .338 Lapua offers a large and exceptionally sturdy, pressure resistant cartridge case that can be relatively easily reloaded and hence be reused several times it has become quite popular amongst wildcatters. With the .338 Lapua Magnum as the parent case wildcatters have created 7 mm (7 mm Allen Magnum, 7 mm Katzmeier, 7 mm Fatso<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup>), .30 (.30-338 Lapua (Triebel), .30 Wolf, 300 Allen Express), 8 mm (8 mm-338 Lapua (Triebel), LM-101), .338 (.338 Yogi, LM-105), .343 (.343 Lapua Magnum LM-107<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multiref1_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-multiref1-72">[70]</a></sup>), 9.3 mm (9,3-338 Lapua Magnum (Triebel)), .375 (9.5×70 ELR) and .50 calibre (<a href="/wiki/.510_Whisper" title=".510 Whisper">.510 Whisper</a>) variants. The current (2008) <a href="/wiki/Benchrest_shooting" title="Benchrest shooting">benchrest</a> 1,000-yard (914.4 m) 5-shot group world record holder Tom Sarver used a .300 Hulk wildcat cartridge, which is basically a necked-down, blown out, shortened .338 Lapua Magnum variant, to achieve a 1.403-inch (35.64 mm) diameter 5-shot group on 7 July 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.338_Lapua_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Finnish Army found out during a test/trails program and 7 years of service that the barrels of their Sako <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRG-42" title="TRG-42">TRG-42</a> sniper rifles lasted 7,000 to 8,000 rounds with Lapua Lock Base B408 factory ammunition before showing impermissible accuracy decay. The Finnish Army consistent accuracy requirement for these rifles is ≤ 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">MOA</a> at 1,000 m. If this requirement is not met the TRG-42 gets a new barrel. This is normal practice for active high performance precision rifle operators who regard barrels as expendable items. The continuous use of very powerful <a href="/wiki/Handloading" title="Handloading">handloads</a> (which results in higher muzzle velocities) resulted in much quicker throat erosion reducing the TRG-42 barrel's accuracy life to 1500 to 2000 rounds.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-16"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-16">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In 1990, the US military Adjutant General's Office issued a legal opinion holding that the Sierra MatchKing bullet (and similar bullets of other manufacturers), despite being a hollow point design, is not designed specifically to cause greater damage or suffering in a human target, and in fact normally does not create a wound readily distinguishable from wounds caused by conventional full metal jacket bullets, and is therefore in their opinion legal under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_(1899_and_1907)" title="Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907)">Hague Convention</a> for use in war.</span></li>
</ol>
