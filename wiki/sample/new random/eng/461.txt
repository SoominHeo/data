<p>The <b>.30-06 Springfield</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartridge_(weaponry)" title="Cartridge (weaponry)">cartridge</a> (pronounced "thirty-<a href="/wiki/Names_for_the_number_0_in_English" title="Names for the number 0 in English">aught</a>-six" or "thirty-oh-six"), <b>7.62×63mm</b> in metric notation and called ".30 Gov't '06" by Winchester,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> in 1906 and later <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standardized</a>; it remained in use until the early 1980s. The ".30" refers to the <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">caliber</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullet</a>, and the "06" refers to the year the cartridge was adopted—1906. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/.30-03" title=".30-03">.30-03</a>, <a href="/wiki/6mm_Lee_Navy" title="6mm Lee Navy">6mm Lee Navy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/.30-40_Krag" title=".30-40 Krag">.30-40 Krag</a> cartridges. The .30-06 remained the U.S. Army's primary rifle and machine gun cartridge for nearly 50 years before being replaced by the <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> (commercial <a href="/wiki/.308_Winchester" title=".308 Winchester">.308 Winchester</a>) and <a href="/wiki/5.56%C3%9745mm_NATO" title="5.56×45mm NATO">5.56×45mm NATO</a>, both of which remain in current U.S. and NATO service. It remains a very popular sporting round, with ammunition produced by all major manufacturers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1890s, the U.S. military adopted the <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a> <a href="/wiki/.30-40_Krag" title=".30-40 Krag">.30-40 Krag</a> rimmed cartridge. The 1894 version of that cartridge used a 220-grain (14 g) round-nose bullet. Around 1901, the U.S. started developing an experimental rimless cartridge for a Mauser action with box magazine. That led to the 1903 <a href="/wiki/.30-03" title=".30-03">.30-03</a> rimless service round that used the same 220-grain (14 g) round-nose bullet as the Krag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe591_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe591-4">[4]</a></sup> The .30-03 achieved a muzzle velocity of 2,300 ft/s (700 m/s).</p>
<p>Many European militaries at the turn of the 20th century were adopting lighter-weight (roughly 150-to-200-grain (9.7 to 13.0 g)), higher velocity, service rounds with <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_(bullet)" title="Spitzer (bullet)">pointed (spitzer) bullets</a>: France in 1898 (<a href="/wiki/8mm_Lebel" title="8mm Lebel">8mm Lebel</a> <i>Balle D</i> spitzer 198 grains (12.8 g) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boat-tail_bullet" title="Boat-tail bullet">boat-tail</a>), Germany in 1905 (<a href="/wiki/7.92%C3%9757mm_Mauser" title="7.92×57mm Mauser">7.92×57mm Mauser</a> <i>S Patrone</i> 153 grains (9.9 g) [S ball])), Russia in 1908 (<a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9754mmR" title="7.62×54mmR">7.62×54mmR</a> <i>Lyokhkaya pulya</i> [Light Bullet]), and Britain in 1910 (<a href="/wiki/.303_British#Mark_VII" title=".303 British">.303 British Mark VII</a> 174 grains (11.3 g)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Consequently, the round-nosed U.S. <a href="/wiki/.30-03" title=".30-03">.30-03</a> service cartridge was falling behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>For these reasons, the U.S. military developed a new, lighter, cartridge in 1906, the .30-06 Springfield, "Cartridge, Ball, Caliber .30, Model of 1906", or just <b>M1906</b>. The .30-03 case was modified to have a slightly shorter neck to fire a spitzer flat-based 150-grain (9.7 g) bullet that had a <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_coefficient" title="Ballistic coefficient">ballistic coefficient</a> (G1 BC) of approximately 0.405, a muzzle velocity of 2,700 ft/s (820 m/s), and a muzzle energy of 2,429 ft·lbf (3,293 J). The cartridge was loaded with Military Rifle (MR) 21 propellant, and its maximum range was claimed (falsely) to be 4,700 yd (4,300 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1903_Springfield_rifle" title="M1903 Springfield rifle">M1903 Springfield rifle</a>, which had been introduced alongside the .30-03 cartridge, was modified to accept the new .30-06 Springfield cartridge. Modifications to the rifle included shortening the barrel at its breech and resizing the chamber, so that the more tapered bullet would not have to jump too far to reach the rifling. Other changes to the rifle included elimination of the troublesome "rod bayonet" of the earlier Springfield rifles.</p>
<p>The M1906 maximum range was originally overstated. When the M1906 cartridge was developed, the range tests had been done to only 1,800 yards (1,650 m); distances beyond that were estimated, but the estimate for extreme range was wrong by almost 40 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The range discrepancy became evident during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Before the widespread employment of light mortars and artillery, long-range machine gun "barrage" or <a href="/wiki/Indirect_fire" title="Indirect fire">indirect fires</a> were considered important in U.S. infantry tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When the US entered World War I, it did not have many machine guns, so it acquired British and French machine guns. When those weapons were later replaced with US machine guns firing the M1906 round, the effective range of the barrage was 50 percent less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Firing tests performed around 1918 at Borden Brook Reservoir (Massachusetts), Miami, and Daytona Beach showed the actual maximum range of the M1906 cartridge to be 3,300 to 3,400 yards (3,020 to 3,110 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Germany, which was using the <i>S Patrone</i> (S ball cartridge) loaded with a similar 153-grain (9.9 g) flat-based bullet in its rifles, had apparently confronted and solved the same problem by developing an aerodynamically more refined bullet for long range machine gun use. The <i>s.S. Patrone</i> was inroduced in 1914 and used a 197.5-grain (12.80 g) <i>s.S. - schweres Spitzgeschoß</i> ("heavy spitzer") boat-tail bullet which had a maximum range of approximately 5,140 yd (4,700 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_and_s.S._Patrone_ballistics_2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-S_and_s.S._Patrone_ballistics_2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>For these reasons, in 1926, the Ordnance Corps developed the <b>.30 M1 Ball</b> cartridge loaded with a new <a href="/wiki/Improved_Military_Rifle" title="Improved Military Rifle">Improved Military Rifle (IMR) 1185</a> propellant and 174-grain (11.3 g) bullet with a 9° boat tail and an <a href="/wiki/Ogive" title="Ogive">ogive</a> of 7 calibers that had a higher <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_coefficient" title="Ballistic coefficient">ballistic coefficient</a> of roughly 0.494 (G1 BC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> that achieved a muzzle velocity of 2,647 ft/s (807 m/s) and muzzle energy of 2,675 ft·lbf (3,627 J).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatcher_1962_29_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatcher_1962_29-15">[15]</a></sup> This bullet further reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_resistance" title="Air resistance">air resistance</a> in flight, resulting in less rapid downrange deceleration, less lateral drift caused by crosswinds, and significantly greater supersonic and maximum effective range from machine guns and rifles alike. Its maximum range was approximately 5,500 yd (5,030 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgsc.edu_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgsc.edu-16">[16]</a></sup> Additionally, a <a href="/wiki/Gilding_metal" title="Gilding metal">gilding metal</a> jacket was developed that all but eliminated the metal fouling that plagued the earlier M1906 cartridge.</p>
<p>Wartime surplus totaled over 2 billion rounds of ammunition. Army regulations called for training use of the oldest ammunition first. As a result, the older .30-06 ammunition was expended for training; stocks of .30 M1 Ball ammunition were allowed to slowly grow until all of the older M1906 ammunition had been fired. By 1936, it was discovered that the maximum range of the .30 M1 Ball ammunition with its boat-tailed spitzer bullets was beyond the safety limitations of many ranges. An emergency order was made to manufacture quantities of ammunition that matched the external ballistics of the earlier M1906 cartridge as soon as possible. A new cartridge was developed in 1938 that was essentially a duplicate of the old M1906 round, but loaded with <a href="/wiki/Improved_Military_Rifle#IMR_.23_4895" title="Improved Military Rifle">IMR 4895</a> propellant and a new flat-based bullet that had a gilding metal jacket and a different lead alloy, and weighed 152 grains (9.8 g) instead of 150 grains (9.7 g). This 1938 pattern cartridge, the <b>Cartridge, Caliber .30, Ball, M2</b> achieved a muzzle velocity of 2,805 ft/s (855 m/s) and muzzle energy of 2,656 ft·lbf (3,601 J).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatcher_1962_29_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hatcher_1962_29-15">[15]</a></sup> Its maximum range was approximately 3,450 yd (3,150 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgsc.edu_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-cgsc.edu-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>In military service, the 30-06 was used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolt-action" title="Bolt-action">bolt-action</a> <a href="/wiki/M1903_Springfield" title="M1903 Springfield">M1903 Springfield</a> rifle, the bolt-action <a href="/wiki/M1917_Enfield" title="M1917 Enfield">M1917 Enfield</a> rifle, the <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_firearm" title="Semi-automatic firearm">semi-automatic</a> <a href="/wiki/M1_Garand" title="M1 Garand">M1 Garand</a> rifle, the semi-automatic <a href="/wiki/M1941_Johnson_rifle" title="M1941 Johnson rifle">M1941 Johnson rifle</a>, the Famage Mauser, the <a href="/wiki/M1918_Browning_Automatic_Rifle" title="M1918 Browning Automatic Rifle">Browning Automatic Rifle</a> (BAR), and numerous machine guns, including the <a href="/wiki/M1917_Browning_machine_gun" title="M1917 Browning machine gun">M1917</a> and <a href="/wiki/M1919_Browning_machine_gun" title="M1919 Browning machine gun">M1919</a> series. It served the United States in both World Wars and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_war" title="Korean war">Korean War</a>, its last major use being in Vietnam.</p>
<p>The Belgian army (ABL) bought the <a href="/wiki/FN_Model_1949" title="FN Model 1949">FN Model 1949</a> rifle in 30-06 calibre. (both as a sniper version with telescopic sights and as a general service weapon). The <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> armed forces used the round widely in the Korean war, where the 30-06 calibre FN-49 proved to be a superior weapon in terms of both accuracy and reliability to the American <a href="/wiki/M1_Garand" title="M1 Garand">M1 Garand</a>. The 30-06 FN-49 saw widespread use in the various wars in and around the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a>. The 30-06 FN-49 was also sold to the armies of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. Another customer was <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> where it served the Navy.</p>
<p>Large volumes of surplus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearm_brass" title="Firearm brass">brass</a> made it the basis for dozens of commercial and <a href="/wiki/.30-06_Springfield_Wildcat_Cartridges" title=".30-06 Springfield Wildcat Cartridges">wildcat</a> cartridges, as well as being extensively used for <a href="/wiki/Handloading" title="Handloading">reloading</a>. In 1908 the <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Model_1895" title="Winchester Model 1895">Model 1895 Winchester</a> lever-action rifle became the first commercially produced sporting rifle chambered in .30-06 Springfield. It is still a very common round for hunting and is suitable for large game such as <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">bison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sambar_deer" title="Sambar deer">Sambar deer</a>, and bear, when used at close to medium ranges.</p>
<p>In 1903, the Army converted its M1900 <a href="/wiki/Gatling_gun" title="Gatling gun">Gatling guns</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/.30_Army" title=".30 Army">.30 Army</a> to fit the new <a href="/wiki/.30-03" title=".30-03">.30-03</a> cartridge as the M1903. The later M1903-'06 was an M1903 converted to .30-06. This conversion was principally carried out at the Army's Springfield Armory arsenal repair shops. All models of Gatling guns were declared obsolete by the U.S. Army in 1911, after 45 years of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ballistically, the .30-06 is one of the most versatile cartridges ever designed. With "hot" <a href="/wiki/Handloading" title="Handloading">handloads</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifle</a> capable of handling them, the .30-06 is capable of performance rivaling many "magnum" cartridges. On the other hand, when loaded more closely to the original government spec, .30-06 remains within the upper limit of felt recoil most shooters consider 'tolerable' over multiple rounds, unlike the magnums, and isn't unnecessarily destructive of meat on game such as deer. With appropriate loads, it is suitable for any small or large heavy game found in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The .30-06's power and versatility (combined with the availability of surplus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearms" title="Firearms">firearms</a> chambered for it and demand for commercial <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a>) have kept the round as one of the most popular for <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .30-06 cartridge was designed when shots of 1,000 yards (900 m) were expected. In 1906, the original M1906 .30-06 cartridge consisted of a 150 grains (9.7 g), flat-base <a href="/wiki/Cupronickel" title="Cupronickel">cupronickel</a>-jacketed-bullet. After World War I, the U.S. military needed better long-range performance machine guns. Based on weapons performance reports from Europe, a streamlined, 173 grains (11.2 g) boattail, <a href="/wiki/Gilding_metal" title="Gilding metal">gilding-metal</a> bullet was used. The .30-06 cartridge, with the 173 grains (11.2 g) bullet was called <i>Cartridge, .30, M1 Ball</i>. The .30-06 cartridge was far more powerful than the smaller Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6.5%C3%9750mm_Arisaka" title="6.5×50mm Arisaka">6.5×50mm Arisaka</a> cartridge and comparable to the Japanese <a href="/wiki/7.7%C3%9758mm_Arisaka" title="7.7×58mm Arisaka">7.7×58mm Arisaka</a>. The new M1 ammunition proved to be significantly more accurate than the M1906 round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multiref1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-multiref1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1938, the unstained, 9.8 grams (151 gr), flat-base bullet combined with the .30-06 case became the M2 ball cartridge. The M2 Ball specifications required 2,740 feet per second (840 m/s) minimum velocity, measured 78 feet (24 m) from the muzzle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> M2 Ball was the standard-issue ammunition for military rifles and machine guns until it was replaced by the 7.62×51mm NATO round in 1954. For rifle use, M2 Ball ammunition proved to be less accurate than the earlier M1 cartridge; even with match rifles, a target group of 5 inches (130 mm) diameter at 200 yards (180 m) using the 150-grain (9.7 g) M2 bullet was considered optimal, and many rifles performed less well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multiref1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-multiref1-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> retained stocks of M1 ammunition for use by snipers and trained marksmen throughout the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands_campaign" title="Solomon Islands campaign">Solomon Islands campaign</a> in the early years of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an effort to increase accuracy some snipers resorted to use of the heavier .30-06 M2 armor-piercing round, a practice that re-emerged during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Others sought out lots of M2 ammunition produced by <a href="/wiki/Denver_Federal_Center" title="Denver Federal Center">Denver Ordnance</a>, which had proved to be more accurate than those produced by other wartime ammunition plants when used for sniping at long range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> With regards to penetration, the M2 ball can penetrate 0.4 in (10.16 mm) of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel#Mild_steel" title="Carbon steel">mild steel</a> at 100 yards (91 m), and 0.3 in (7.62 mm) at 200 yards (180 m). M2 AP can penetrate 0.42 in (10.67 mm) of armor steel at 100 yards (91 m). These figures come from army documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, a test done by Brass Fetchers shows that M2 AP can actually penetrate up to 0.5 in (12.70 mm) of MIL-A-12560 armor steel from a distance of 100 yards (91 m). The round struck the plate at a velocity of 2601 fps, and made a complete penetration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>Commercially manufactured rifles chambered in .30-06 are popular for hunting. Current .30-06 factory ammunition varies in bullet weight from 7.1 to 14.3 grams (109.6 to 220.7 gr) in solid bullets, and as low as 3.6 grams (55.6 gr) with the use of a sub-caliber bullet in a <a href="/wiki/Sabot" title="Sabot">sabot</a>. Loads are available with reduced velocity and pressure as well as increased velocity and pressure for stronger firearms. The .30-06 remains one of the most popular sporting cartridges in the world. Many hunting loads have over 3,000 foot-pounds (4,100 J) of energy at the muzzle and use expanding bullets that can deliver rapid energy transfer to targets.</p>

<p>The table above shows typical muzzle velocities available in commercial 30-06 loads along with maximum 30-06 muzzle velocities reported by several reloading manuals for common bullet weights. Hodgdon, Nosler, and Barnes report velocities for 24 inches (610 mm) barrels. Hornady and Speer report velocities for 22 inches (560 mm) barrels. <p>The newer <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO/.308 Winchester</a> cartridge offers similar performance to standard military .30-06 loadings in a smaller cartridge. However, the greater cartridge capacity of the .30-06 allows much more powerful loadings if the shooter desires.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recoil">Recoil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recoil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One reason that the .30-06 has remained a popular round for so long is that the cartridge is at the upper limit of power that is tolerable to most shooters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Recoil energy (<a href="/wiki/Free_recoil" title="Free recoil">Free recoil</a>) greater than 20 foot-pounds force (27 J) will cause most shooters to develop a serious flinch, and the recoil energy of an 8-pound (3.6 kg) rifle firing a 165-grain (10.7 g) 30-06 bullet at 2,900 feet per second (880 m/s) is 20.1 foot-pounds force (27.3 J). Recoil-shy shooters can opt for lighter bullets, such as a 150-grain (9.7 g) bullet. In the same 8-pound (3.6 kg) rifle, a 150-grain (9.7 g) bullet at 2,910 feet per second (890 m/s) will only generate 17.6 foot-pounds force (23.9 J) of recoil energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Young shooters can start out with even lighter bullets weighing 110, 125 or 130 grains (7.1, 8.1 or 8.4 g).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridge_dimensions">Cartridge dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cartridge dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cartridge_30-06.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="640" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Cartridge_30-06.png/300px-Cartridge_30-06.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Cartridge_30-06.png/450px-Cartridge_30-06.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Cartridge_30-06.png/600px-Cartridge_30-06.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cartridge_30-06.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
.30-06 Springfield cartridge dimensions. All sizes in inches</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The .30-06 Springfield cartridge case can hold 68.2 grains (4.42 g) of water and has a volume of 4.42 millilitres (0.270 in<sup>3</sup>). The exterior shape of the case was designed to promote reliable case feeding and extraction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolt-action" title="Bolt-action">bolt-action</a> rifles and <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine guns</a> alike, under extreme conditions.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:.30-06_Springfield.svg"><img alt=".30-06 Springfield.svg" data-file-height="657" data-file-width="1000" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/.30-06_Springfield.svg/400px-.30-06_Springfield.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/.30-06_Springfield.svg/600px-.30-06_Springfield.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/.30-06_Springfield.svg/800px-.30-06_Springfield.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a></p>
<p>.30-06 Springfield maximum C.I.P. cartridge dimensions. All sizes in millimeters.</p>
<p>Americans defined the shoulder angle at alpha/2 = 17.5 degrees. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_Permanente_pour_l%27Epreuve_des_Armes_%C3%A0_Feu_Portatives" title="Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives">Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives</a></i> (C.I.P.) the common <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">twist rate</a> for this cartridge is 254 mm (1 in 10 in), 4 grooves, Ø lands = 7.62 mm (.30 in), Ø grooves = 7.82 mm (.308 in), land width = 4.49 mm (.1768 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">primer type</a> is large rifle. According to the official C.I.P. guidelines, the .30-06 Springfield case can handle up to 405 MPa (58,740 psi) piezo pressure. In C.I.P.-regulated countries, every rifle cartridge combination has to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(firearms)" title="Proof (firearms)">proofed</a> at 125% of this maximum C.I.P. pressure to certify for sale to consumers. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7.92%C3%9757mm" title="7.92×57mm">7.92×57mm</a> and <a href="/wiki/8%C3%9764mm_S" title="8×64mm S">8×64mm S</a> are the closest European ballistic twins of the .30-06 Springfield.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_cartridge_types">Military cartridge types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Military cartridge types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Note:</i> .30-06 cartridges are produced commercially with many different bullets and to a number of different specifications.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The .30-06 round was replaced by the 7.62×51mm NATO round in 1954. However, it did soldier on in the Army Reserves and National Guard for some time; Frankfort Arsenal only stopped production in 1961 and <a href="/wiki/Lake_City_Army_Ammunition_Plant" title="Lake City Army Ammunition Plant">Lake City Army Ammunition Plant</a> was making .30-06 until the late 1970s, with new production batches in 1993 and 2002.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Armor-Piercing, M1917</b>: The M1917 was the first service-issue Armor-Piercing rifle ammunition used by the US Army. It had a steel core in a lead envelope with a partial cupro-nickel jacket which had an exposed soft tip. The exposed tip was designed to aid in the envelope peeling away on impact to allow the core to strike the target. It had the unintended effect of making the thin lead envelope an expanding bullet. Since expanding bullets were seen as violating the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_of_1899_and_1907#Hague_Convention_of_1899" title="Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907">Hague Convention</a> it did not see service in World War I.</li>
<li><b>Armor-Piercing, M1918</b>: The M1918 was similar to the Armor-Piercing M1917 round except it had a smooth cannilure near the case-mouth and had a full-metal-jacketed bullet. It replaced the AP M1917 round in service.</li>
<li><b>Armor-Piercing, M1922</b> <b>(<i>1922–1934</i>)</b>: This was a redesigned armor-piercing round with a heavier steel core. It was the first Armor-Piercing round to have a black-painted bullet tip.</li>
<li><b>Armor-Piercing, M1</b> <b>(<i>1934–1939</i>)</b>: This was a higher-velocity (3,180 fps) AP round that was under development throughout its service. It was replaced in 1939 by the AP M2, a redesign of the AP M1922.</li>
<li><b>Armor Piercing, M2</b> <b>(<i>1939–1954</i>)</b>: This cartridge is used against lightly armored vehicles, protective shelters, and personnel, and can be identified by its black bullet tip. Bullet is flat base, weight 163–168 grains. Defense against the M2 projectile by name is one of the performance standards for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_armor" title="Personal armor">Type IV body armor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIJ_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIJ-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Armor Piercing Incendiary, T15/M14 (<i>1943–?</i>) and M14A1</b> <b>(<i>?–?</i>)</b>: This cartridge may be substituted for the M2 armor-piercing round and is normally employed against flammable targets. The tip of the M14 bullet is colored with a blue tip over a black ring. The M14A1 featured an improved core design and incendiary charge. The M14A1 bullet is colored with aluminum paint.</li>
<li><b>Ball, M1906</b> <b>(<i>1906–1925</i>)</b>: This cartridge is used against personnel and unarmored targets, and can be identified by its silver-colored bullet. The M1906 has a 9.7 g (150-grain) projectile and flat base. Its jacket is a cupro-nickel alloy which was found to quickly foul the bore.</li>
<li><b>Ball, M1</b> <b>(<i>1925–1937</i>)</b>: The M1 has an 11.2 g (173-grain), nine-degree boat-tailed projectile designed for aerodynamic efficiency. Though it had a lower initial velocity, velocity and energy were greater at longer ranges due to its efficient shape. The harder bullet was made of 7 parts lead to 1 part antimony. The jacket material was changed to gilding metal (an alloy of 95% copper and 5% zinc) to reduce fouling.</li>
<li><b>Ball, M2</b> <b>(<i>1937–1954</i>)</b>: With a 9.7 g (150-grain) bullet based on the profile of the M1906, this cartridge incorporated the gilding-metal jacket of the M1 projectile combined with a slightly heavier, pure-lead core. It had a higher muzzle velocity than either of the earlier cartridges.</li>
<li><b>Ball, M2 Alternate</b> <b>(<i>1943–1945</i>)</b>: A wartime economy variant of the Ball M2 that used a steel full metal jacket coated with gilding metal to conserve copper and tin.</li>
<li><b>Blank, M1906</b>: This cartridge is used to simulate rifle fire. It is derived from the M1903 Blank, but with a resized neck. It has a paper bullet that contains a tiny black powder charge to aid in breaking it up.</li>
<li><b>Blank, M1909</b>: This cartridge is used to simulate rifle fire. The cartridge is identified by having no bullet, having a cannelure in the neck of the case, and the crimp is sealed by red lacquer. This is still a current cartridge for ceremonial M1 Garands. Modern M1909 blanks are rose crimped, but they have the same designation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Dummy, M40</b>: This cartridge is used for training. The cartridge has six longitudinal corrugations and there is no primer.</li>
<li><b>Explosive, T99</b>: Development of a cartridge that contained a small explosive charge which more effectively marked its impact. Often referred to as an "observation explosive" cartridge, it was meant to designate line-of-sight targets for cannon. The T99 was never adopted.</li>
<li><b>Frangible, T44/M22</b> (April 1945): The bullet disintegrates upon striking a hard or armored target, leaving a pencil-like mark to indicate a hit during bomber aerial gunnery practice. The cartridge is identified by a green bullet tip with a white ring to the rear of the green color.</li>
<li><b>Gallery Practice M1919</b>: A reduced-charge Ball cartridge with a 140-gr. wax-coated lead round-nosed bullet used for target shooting at indoor facilities or near built-up areas. It was later renamed the <b>Guard M1</b> in 1933 and was used for guard and sentry duty at defense plants and military installations during World War 2.</li>
<li><b>Guard M1906</b> (1907–1918): A reduced-charge Ball cartridge with a 150-gr. FMJ pointed-nose bullet used for guard and sentry duty in built-up areas. The cartridge was originally marked with six cannelures in the middle, but this weakened the case and caused it to burst in a dirty rifle. It was later marked by six dents or flutes on the shoulder of the cartridge. Although production stopped in 1918, there were plenty of stocks of the oddball round left until the 1920s.</li>
<li><b>High Pressure Test, M1</b>: The cartridge is used to proof test 30-06 rifles and machine guns after manufacture, test, or repair. The cartridge is identified by stannic-stained (silvered) cartridge case.</li>
<li><b>Incendiary, M1917</b>: Early incendiary cartridge, bullet had a large cavity in the nose to allow the material to more easily shoot forward on impact. As a result, the M1917 had a tendency to expand on impact. The M1917 had a blackened tip.</li>
<li><b>Incendiary, M1918</b>: Variant of the M1917 with a normal bullet profile to comply with international laws regarding open-tipped expanding bullets.</li>
<li><b>Incendiary, M1</b>: This cartridge is used against unarmored, flammable targets. The tip of the bullet is painted blue.</li>
<li><b>Match, M72</b>: This cartridge is used in marksmanship competition firing, and can be identified by the word "MATCH" on the head stamp.</li>
<li><b>Tracer, M1</b>: Tracer for observing fire, signaling, target designation, and incendiary purposes. The M1 has a red tip.</li>
<li><b>Tracer, M2</b>: Tracer for observing fire, signaling, target designation, and incendiary purposes. Has a short burn time. The M2 originally had a white tip, but then switched to a red tip like the M1.</li>
<li><b>Tracer, M2 Alternate (<i>1943–1945</i>)</b>: A wartime economy variant of the Tracer M2 that used a steel full metal jacket coated with gilding metal. It was ballistically matched to the <i>Ball M2 Alternate</i>.</li>
<li><b>Tracer, T10/M25</b>: Improved tracer over M1/M2. Designed to be less intense in terms of brightness than either the M1 or M2 tracers. The M25 had an orange tip.</li>
<li><b>Rifle Grenade Cartridges, M1 (<i>1933–1945</i>), M2 (<i>1941</i>), M3 (<i>1941–1953</i>), and M3E1 (<i>1953–present</i>)</b>: These cartridges are used in conjunction with rifle grenade launchers to propel rifle grenades. The cartridge has no bullet and the mouth is crimped. The differences between the three cartridges have to do with the powder charge and the subsequent range of the launchers. The M1 was a universal Rifle Grenade cartridge that replaced the specialized <i>Rifle Grenade</i> (RG), <i>Chemical Rifle Grenade</i> (CRG), and <i>Chemical Warfare Grenade</i> (CWG) cartridges and was used with cup-discharger rifle grenade launchers. The M2 was an experimental cartridge used as a testbed; it used a propellant mixture of black powder and smokeless powder because it was feared that the smokeless powder would not reliably ignite by itself. The M3 was designed to be used with the M1 series (for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1903_Springfield_rifle" title="M1903 Springfield rifle">M1903 Springfield rifle</a>), M2 series (for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1917_Enfield_rifle" title="M1917 Enfield rifle">M1917 Enfield rifle</a>), M7 series (for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1_Garand_rifle" title="M1 Garand rifle">M1 Garand rifle</a>), and M8-series (for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1_Carbine" title="M1 Carbine">M1 Carbine</a>) <a href="/wiki/M7_grenade_launcher" title="M7 grenade launcher">spigot grenade launchers</a> and used a propellant that was a mixture of a 5-grain amount of FFFG black powder and a 40-grain amount of IMR-4898 smokeless powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The M3E1 featured an extended case neck, a rosepetal crimp, and a full smokeless powder loading and was designed for use with the <a href="/wiki/M7_grenade_launcher" title="M7 grenade launcher">M7A3 spigot rifle grenade launcher</a> and larger <a href="/wiki/ENERGA_anti-tank_rifle_grenade" title="ENERGA anti-tank rifle grenade">ENERGA rifle grenade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The Grenade Blanks were issued in double-rowed 10-round cartons, usually as part of a set with the M13 metal <i>Grenade Launcher Assortment</i> ammo can.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commonwealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The .30-06 cartridge was adopted in 1940 during the beginnings of the Lend-Lease program in anticipation of using American weapons in front-line service. The British used American-made ammunition during the war, which was designated as <i>Cartridge S.A, .30</i> to avoid confusing it with their own <a href="/wiki/.303_British" title=".303 British">.303 British</a> service round. It was used after the war as belted machinegun ammunition by the Royal Armored Corps and was not declared obsolete until October, 1993. The "z" after the numeral indicates that it used a nitrocellulose propellant rather than cordite. Marks of ammunition were originally designated with Roman numerals (i.e., .303 Ball Mark VII), but were replaced with Arabic numerals by 1945 (i.e., .303 Ball MK 7).</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, .30 Ball MK Iz</b>: This was the War Ministry's designation for the .30-06 Ball M1 round. It was not accepted for use in service, as the American military had already marked it as limited standard.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, .30 Ball MK IIz</b>: This was the War Ministry's designation for the .30-06 Ball M2 round. Lend-Lease ammo made for the British government had no mark designation and was headstamped <i>.300z</i>.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, .30 Ball MK 3z</b>: This was the designation for an experimental round designed in 1945 for Royal Navy use that was based on the .30-06 Ball M2. It was not adopted for service.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, .30 Ball MK 4z</b>: This is a boat-tailed cartridge with a 150-grain Full Metal Jacketed bullet. It is marked with a purple annulus. It was normally packed in 20-round cartons. However, Commonwealth countries that used the M1 Garand (like Pakistan) bundled it in 16-round cartons that contained two 8-round Mannlicher-style en-bloc clips.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, .30 Ball MK 5z</b>: This was made under contract by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrique_Nationale" title="Fabrique Nationale">Fabrique Nationale</a> in the 1970s.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, Tracer .30 inch G Mark 1z</b>: This is a boat-tailed cartridge with a 150-grain Full Metal Jacketed bullet and a Berdan primer. It is marked with a red annulus and has a headstamp of <i>.30 G1z</i>.</li>
<li><b>Cartridge S.A, Tracer .30 inch G Mark 2z</b>: This was made under contract by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrique_Nationale" title="Fabrique Nationale">Fabrique Nationale</a> in the 1970s and is paired with .30 Ball MK 5z. It is the same as the <i>G Mark 1z</i>, except it has a non-corrosive Berdan primer. It is marked with a red bullet tip and has a headstamp of <i>.30 G2z</i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Union">French Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.30-06_Springfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: French Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The .30-06 round was adopted in 1949 for use in American war surplus military aid weapons like the M1 Garand and M1919 medium machinegun.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>7.62mm Modele 1949 Ball Ordinaire</b>: This cartridge was based on the USGI .30-06 Ball M2 round.</li>
</ul>
