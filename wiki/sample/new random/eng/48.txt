<p><b>Keith Allen Haring</b> (May 4, 1958 – February 16, 1990) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> artist and <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">social activist</a> whose work responded to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> street culture of the 1980s by expressing concepts of birth, death, sexuality, and war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring's work was often heavily political<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes2002-3">[3]</a></sup> and his imagery has become a widely recognized visual language of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haggerty2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haggerty2013-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mural_de_Keith_Haring_(1).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG/220px-Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG/330px-Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG/440px-Mural_de_Keith_Haring_%281%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mural_de_Keith_Haring_(1).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Keith Haring was born in <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Pennsylvania" title="Reading, Pennsylvania">Reading, Pennsylvania</a>, on May 4, 1958. He was raised in <a href="/wiki/Kutztown,_Pennsylvania" title="Kutztown, Pennsylvania">Kutztown, Pennsylvania</a>, by his mother Joan Haring, and father Allen Haring, an engineer and amateur cartoonist. He had three younger sisters, Kay, Karen and Kristen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Haring became interested in art at a very early age spending time with his father producing creative drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup> His early influences included Walt Disney cartoons, Dr. Seuss, Charles Schulz, and the Looney Tunes characters in <i>The Bugs Bunny Show</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup> In Haring's teenage years, he left his religious background behind and hitchhiked across the country, selling vintage t-shirts and experimenting with drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He studied <a href="/wiki/Commercial_art" title="Commercial art">commercial art</a> from 1976 to 1978 at <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>'s Ivy School of Professional Art but lost interest in it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring_Bio_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haring_Bio-8">[8]</a></sup> He made this decision having read Robert Henri's <i>The Art Spirit</i> (1923) which inspired him to concentrate on his own art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring had a maintenance job at the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Center_for_the_Arts" title="Pittsburgh Center for the Arts">Pittsburgh Center for the Arts</a> and was able to explore the art of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dubuffet" title="Jean Dubuffet">Jean Dubuffet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">Jackson Pollock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Tobey" title="Mark Tobey">Mark Tobey</a>. His most critical influences at this time were a 1977 retrospective of the work of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Alechinsky" title="Pierre Alechinsky">Pierre Alechinsky</a> and a lecture by the sculptor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christo" title="Christo">Christo</a> in 1978. Alechinsky's work, connected to the international Expressionist group <a href="/wiki/COBRA_(avant-garde_movement)" title="COBRA (avant-garde movement)">CoBrA</a>, gave Haring confidence to create larger paintings of calligraphic images. Christo introduced him to the possibilities of involving the public with his art. Haring's first important one-man exhibition was in Pittsburgh at the Center for the Arts in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He moved to New York to study <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a> at the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Visual_Arts" title="School of Visual Arts">School of Visual Arts</a>. He studied <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a> with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Beckley" title="Bill Beckley">Bill Beckley</a> as well as exploring the possibilities of video and performance art. Profoundly influenced at this time by the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Burroughs" title="William Burroughs">William Burroughs</a>, he was inspired to experiment with the cross-referencing and interconnection of images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup> In his junior/senior year, he was behind on credits, because his professors could not give him credit for the very loose artwork he was doing with themes of social activism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He first received public attention with his public art in subways. Starting in 1980, he organized exhibitions at <a href="/wiki/Club_57" title="Club 57">Club 57</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> which were filmed by the photographer <a href="/wiki/Tseng_Kwong_Chi" title="Tseng Kwong Chi">Tseng Kwong Chi</a>. Around this time, "The Radiant Baby" became his symbol. His bold lines, vivid colors, and active figures carry strong messages of life and unity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> He participated in the Times Square Exhibition and drew animals and human faces for the first time. That same year, he photocopied and pasted provocative collages made from cut-up and recombined <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> headlines around the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1981, he sketched his first chalk drawings on black paper and painted plastic, metal, and <a href="/wiki/Found_object" title="Found object">found objects</a>.</p>
<p>By 1982, Haring had established friendships with fellow emerging artists <a href="/wiki/Futura_2000" title="Futura 2000">Futura 2000</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Scharf" title="Kenny Scharf">Kenny Scharf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Michel_Basquiat" title="Jean-Michel Basquiat">Jean-Michel Basquiat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> He created more than 50 public works between 1982 and 1989 in dozens of cities around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring_Bio_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haring_Bio-8">[8]</a></sup> His "Crack is Wack" mural, created in 1986, is visible from New York's FDR Drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring_Bio_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haring_Bio-8">[8]</a></sup> He got to know <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>, who was the theme of several of Haring's pieces, including "Andy Mouse". His friendship with Warhol would prove to be a decisive element in his eventual success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2007, an area of the American Textile Building in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TriBeCa" title="TriBeCa">TriBeCa</a> neighborhood of New York City was discovered to contain a painting of Haring's from 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hope_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hope-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_breakthrough">International breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1984, Haring visited Australia and painted wall murals in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> (such as the 1984 'Detail-Mural at <a href="/wiki/Collingwood_College,_Victoria" title="Collingwood College, Victoria">Collingwood College, Victoria</a>') and <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> and received a commission from the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Victoria" title="National Gallery of Victoria">National Gallery of Victoria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Centre_for_Contemporary_Art" title="Australian Centre for Contemporary Art">Australian Centre for Contemporary Art</a> to create a mural which temporarily replaced the water curtain at the National Gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He also visited and painted in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Art_Moderne_de_la_Ville_de_Paris" title="Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris">Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> He became politically active, designing a Free South Africa poster in 1985, and in 1986, painting a section of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>. He was interested in working with children and this inspired the project Citykids Speak on Liberty, which involved 1,000 children collaborating on a project for the centennial of the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaringEncy_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-HaringEncy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked about the commercialism of his work, Haring said: "I could earn more money if I just painted a few things and jacked up the price. My shop is an extension of what I was doing in the subway stations, breaking down the barriers between high and low art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haring-nytimes_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-haring-nytimes-13">[13]</a></sup> By the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Pop_Shop" title="Pop Shop">Pop Shop</a>, his work began reflecting more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socio-political" title="Socio-political">socio-political</a> themes, such as anti-<a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">Apartheid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> awareness, and the <a href="/wiki/Crack_epidemic" title="Crack epidemic">crack cocaine epidemic</a>. He even created several <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">pop art</a> pieces influenced by other products: <a href="/wiki/Absolut_Vodka" title="Absolut Vodka">Absolut Vodka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Strike" title="Lucky Strike">Lucky Strike</a> cigarettes, and <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1987 he had his own exhibitions in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, and elsewhere. He also designed the cover for the benefit album <i><a href="/wiki/A_Very_Special_Christmas_(album)" title="A Very Special Christmas (album)">A Very Special Christmas</a></i>, on which Madonna was included. In 1988 he joined a select group of artists whose work has appeared on the label of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chateau_Mouton_Rothschild" title="Chateau Mouton Rothschild">Chateau Mouton Rothschild</a> wine.</p>
<p>Haring also created public murals in the lobby and ambulatory care department of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodhull_Medical_and_Mental_Health_Center" title="Woodhull Medical and Mental Health Center">Woodhull Medical and Mental Health Center</a> on <a href="/wiki/Flushing_Avenue" title="Flushing Avenue">Flushing Avenue</a>, Brooklyn.</p>
<p>A rare video of Haring at work<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs-haring-video_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbs-haring-video-14">[14]</a></sup> shows his energetic style. Haring wrote: "I am becoming much more aware of movement. The importance of movement is intensified when a painting becomes a performance. The performance (the act of painting) becomes as important as the resulting painting."</p>
<p>When his friend <a href="/wiki/Jean-Michel_Basquiat" title="Jean-Michel Basquiat">Jean-Michel Basquiat</a> died of an overdose in New York in 1988, he paid homage to him with his work <i>A Pile of Crowns, for Jean-Michel Basquiat</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring was openly <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a> and was a strong advocate of <a href="/wiki/Safe_sex" title="Safe sex">safe sex</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sheff_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sheff-1">[1]</a></sup> however, in 1988, he was diagnosed with AIDS. In 1989, he established the Keith Haring Foundation to provide funding and imagery to AIDS organizations and children's programs, and to expand the audience for his work through exhibitions, publications and the licensing of his images. Haring used his imagery during the last years of his life to speak about his illness and to generate activism and awareness about AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring_Bio_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haring_Bio-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1989, he was invited by the Lesbian and Gay Community Services Center to join a show of site-specific artwork for the building at 208 West 13th Street. Haring chose the second-floor men's room for his mural <i>Once Upon a Time</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In June, on the rear wall of the convent of the Church of Sant'Antonio (in Italian: <i>Chiesa di Sant'Antonio abate</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>), he painted the last public work of his life, the mural "Tuttomondo" (translation: "All world").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion">Fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Haring collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Grace_Jones" title="Grace Jones">Grace Jones</a>, whom he had met through Andy Warhol. In 1985, Haring and Jones worked together on the two live performances Jones at the Paradise Garage, which <a href="/wiki/Robert_Farris_Thompson" title="Robert Farris Thompson">Robert Farris Thompson</a> has called a "epicenter for black dance". Each time, Haring covered Jones' body with graffiti. He also collaborated with fashion designers Vivienne Westwood and Malcolm McLaren on their A/W 1983/84 Witches collection, with his artwork covering the clothing which was most famously worn by a pink-wigged Madonna for a performance of her song "<a href="/wiki/Like_a_Virgin_(song)" title="Like a Virgin (song)">Like a Virgin</a>" on the British pop-music programme <i>Top of the Pops</i> and the American TV dance program <i><a href="/wiki/Solid_Gold_(TV_series)" title="Solid Gold (TV series)">Solid Gold</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHBio_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-KHBio-9">[9]</a></sup> Haring also collaborated with David Spada, a jewelry designer, to design the sculptural adornments for Jones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haring's work very clearly demonstrates many important political and personal influences. Ideas about his sexual orientation are apparent throughout his work and his journals clearly confirm its impact on his work. Heavy symbolism speaking about the AIDS epidemic is vivid in his later pieces, such as <i>Untitled (cat. no. 27)</i>, <i>Silence=Death</i> and his sketch <i>Weeping Woman</i>. In some of his works—including cat. no. 27—the symbolism is subtle, but Haring also produced some blatantly activist works. <i>Silence=Death</i> is almost universally agreed upon as a work of <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">HIV/AIDS activism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexuality">Sexuality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sexuality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of many consistent ideas, sexuality was a predominant theme throughout Haring's work. Through much of his art there are scenes of penetration, in a bodily and sexual sense. These scenes are often filled with monsters, skeletons and beasts, which almost always add a nightmarish feeling to the work. Rather than being something positive or affirming, sexuality is almost always presented as threatening or silence. "Rape, sexual compulsion and castration are the fundamental forms in which individual self-determination is forcibly prevented."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup> For Haring, sex was part of his work because it was part of his life. "It's inevitable that the subway drawings have it too because it's part of my life and part of the rest of the body of work," he said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1991_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1991-19">[19]</a></sup> Haring's perception of sex was later affected by constant fear as the threat of AIDS became apparent.</p>
<p>It is possible that the drama in Haring's work demonstrates the stigma associated with homosexual relationships, in particular male-male relationships. Because all of the sexual acts portrayed were shown as being experienced illicitly, Haring felt they would always "carry with them the feeling of guilt with which they were imagined, portrayed and executed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup> Haring was one of the first to present homosexuality in a politically progressive way. Because of that, almost – not in spite of it – the then-inevitable link between homosexual sexual activity and AIDS is apparent in Haring's later works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The possible messages that can be identified in this painting have more power when the viewer takes into account Haring's aim of being sexually progressive in his work, and progressive on behalf of the homosexual community. Cultural views toward homosexuality, especially as they existed then, add a layer of guilt and loss to Haring's paintings. Haring's aim, in many ways, was to move sexuality (and specifically homosexuality) away from the subculture and stereotypes. With the rise of AIDS, Haring aimed to turn the message about the disease outward by presenting it through the joint lenses of political power, religious institutionalism, and anti-sexual morals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="AIDS">AIDS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: AIDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theme of AIDS permeates Haring's late work, most likely because it had a heavy influence on his personal life. Midway through Haring's journals there is mention of the disease claiming his friends' lives, and later passages show Haring worrying increasingly about his own HIV status. For Haring, the fear was based in watching his friends die, not in his own fragility. On March 20, 1987, Haring wrote, "I'm scared of having to watch more people die in front of me...I refuse to die like that. If the time comes, I think suicide is much more dignified and much easier on friends and loved ones. Nobody deserves to watch this kind of slow death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup> Haring avoided diagnosis for many years, but he felt it coming even then, and the impending diagnosis drove his work. "The odds are very great and, in fact, the symptoms already exist," he wrote in 1987. "I know in my heart that is only divine intervention that has kept me alive this long. I don't know if I have five months or five years, but I know my days are numbered. This is why my activities and projects are so important now. To do as much as possible as quickly as possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>When looking at Haring's work post-1988, after his diagnosis, the focus seems to be primarily on male sexuality. The odd, foreboding symbol of the horned sperm begins to arise in much of his work, and this figure has been regarded as his personification of the AIDS virus. Perhaps the most representative example of Haring's post-AIDS work is the large scale work <i>cat no. 27</i>. The piece features a large, horned sperm, drawn in white on a black background. The symbol was meant to embody everything that had become real in Haring's life: death as a result of physical love, or a deathly threat to sexuality. The horned sperm is shown hatching from an egg strapped to an individual's back. At the time of Haring's diagnosis, AIDS was nowhere near acceptance, and it was very much viewed through the prism of homophobia. "The discussion of AIDS as a 'gay cancer' and a 'divine punishment' for indecent living in the eighties was the subject of heated debate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_2001_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_2001-18">[18]</a></sup> Haring highlighted homophobia, and the corresponding AIDS-phobia, in his journal entries on many occasions, but there came a point when it no longer surprised him. "Read yet another AIDS article in the <i>Herald Tribune</i>," he wrote. "Article about homophobia increase on American college campuses. Violence, etc. Very frightening, but very predictable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symbolism in Haring's work bears the weight of this oppression. The burden of the egg in <i>cat. no. 27</i> could be seen to represent the oppressive effects AIDS had brought on the individuals dealing with the disease. The idea of anonymous sex was becoming more and more taboo in the homosexual community and the community was facing more and more discrimination as people reacted in fear to the AIDS epidemic. Haring states in his diary that he wanted danger declared, that he had to call it off, as does everyone else: "There really can't be any more anonymous sex." He probes into the horror that could result from extreme promiscuity at the height of the AIDS epidemic; talking about individuals who have literally "fucked themselves to death". Haring was conflicted about his dark perceptions of sex, but they persisted nonetheless. "Maybe I don't have any right to complain," he wrote. "I've had an incredible life and had enough sex in ten years for an entire lifetime, but it doesn't work like that. It's not a rational thing that can be explained away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his work <i>Silence=Death</i> Haring portrays multiple figures covering their eyes, mouths, and ears. The piece is intended to illustrate the oppression and invisibility that AIDS victims felt in the 1980s. Works like Haring's helped to give those living with AIDS more visibility at a time when many were suffering in absolute silence, with no voice, no visibility and no support from those around them. In <i>Silence=Death</i>, the figures are all laid over a <a href="/wiki/Pink_triangle" title="Pink triangle">pink triangle</a>, a symbol associated with homosexual men. Originally used during the Holocaust, the pink triangle was used to signify those who were being targeted for their homosexuality. Since then, the symbol has often been reclaimed and re-appropriated by the gay rights movement. The AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power (ACT UP) also used the symbol starting in 1987.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AIDS_diagnosis">AIDS diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: AIDS diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 1987, Haring visited an AIDS specialist. On May 1, 1987, he mentioned in his journal rumors that were circulating throughout the art community about his AIDS status. He wrote that his friend Tony was "still worried because the Art World gossip hotline still says I have AIDS... If anything causes me tension and stress it's him, not staying up late or working hard. I live for work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring's diagnosis was never a secret; it was public knowledge and an accepted part of his persona in the media. Those publicly shared thoughts were reflected, often with more depth, in his work. Despite all the fear that led up to diagnosis, in some ways Haring found his impending death liberating. It pushed him to produce more work as quickly as possible. In a 1989 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine, Haring stated, "That's the point that I am at now, not knowing where it stops but knowing how important it is to do it now. The whole thing is getting more articulate. In a way it's really liberating."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1991_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1991-19">[19]</a></sup> Critics have recognized this about Haring's works – particularly his later works – as well. "Haring's way of living life – liberated and with death in mind at a young age – allowed him to pull himself away from his diagnosis," Blinderman writes. "A year after his original diagnosis he was producing radiant paintings of birth and life." The introduction to the compilation of Haring's journals sings the same song: "Haring accepts his death. For in his art he found the key to transform desire, the force that killed him, into a flowering elegance that will live beyond his time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haring.2C_Keith_1996_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-Haring.2C_Keith_1996-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haring died on February 16, 1990 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>-related complications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a celebration of his life, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> declared the first New York date of her <a href="/wiki/Blond_Ambition_World_Tour" title="Blond Ambition World Tour">Blond Ambition World Tour</a> a benefit concert for Haring's memory and donated all proceeds from her ticket sales to AIDS charities including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS_Project_Los_Angeles" title="AIDS Project Los Angeles">AIDS Project Los Angeles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AmfAR" title="AmfAR">amfAR</a>; the act was documented in her film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Truth_or_Dare_(documentary)" title="Truth or Dare (documentary)">Truth or Dare</a>.</i> Additionally, Haring's work was featured in several of <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Organization" title="Red Hot Organization">Red Hot Organization</a>'s efforts to raise money for AIDS and AIDS awareness, specifically its first two albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_%2B_Blue" title="Red Hot + Blue">Red Hot + Blue</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_%2B_Dance" title="Red Hot + Dance">Red Hot + Dance</a></i>, the latter of which used Haring's work on its cover.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haring contributed to the New York New Wave display in 1981 and in 1982, and had his first exclusive exhibition in the <a href="/wiki/Tony_Shafrazi" title="Tony Shafrazi">Tony Shafrazi</a> Gallery. That same year, he took part in <a href="/wiki/Documenta" title="Documenta">Documenta</a> 7 in <a href="/wiki/Kassel" title="Kassel">Kassel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Public_Art_Fund" title="Public Art Fund">Public Art Fund</a>'s "Messages to the Public" in which he created work for a Spectacolor Board in Times Square.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHFamous_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-KHFamous-22">[22]</a></sup> He contributed work to the <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Biennial" title="Whitney Biennial">Whitney Biennial</a> in 1983, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_Art_Biennial" title="São Paulo Art Biennial">Biennial</a>. In 1985, the <a href="/wiki/CAPC_mus%C3%A9e_d%27art_contemporain_de_Bordeaux" title="CAPC musée d'art contemporain de Bordeaux">CAPC</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> opened an exhibition of his works, and took part in the Paris Biennial.</p>
<p>Since his death Haring has been the subject of several international retrospectives. His art was the subject of a 1997 retrospective at the Whitney Museum in New York, curated by Elisabeth Sussman. In 1996, a retrospective at the Museum of Contemporary Art Australia was the first major exhibition of his work in Australia. In 2008 there was a retrospective exhibition at the MAC in Lyon, France. In February 2010, on occasion of the 20th anniversary of the artist's death, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Shafrazi" title="Tony Shafrazi">Tony Shafrazi</a> Gallery showed an exhibition containing dozens of works from every stage of Haring's mature work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In March 2012, a retrospective exhibit of Haring's work, <i>Keith Haring: 1978-1982</i>, opened at the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a> in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In April 2013, <i>Keith Haring: The Political Line</i> opened at the Musee d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris and Le <a href="/wiki/Cent_Quatre" title="Cent Quatre">Cent Quatre</a> In November 2014, then at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Young_Museum" title="De Young Museum">De Young Museum</a> in San Francisco, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haring's work is in major private and public collections, including the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Museum_of_American_Art" title="Whitney Museum of American Art">Whitney Museum of American Art</a>, New York; <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Museum_of_Art" title="Los Angeles County Museum of Art">Los Angeles County Museum of Art</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Art_Institute_of_Chicago" title="Art Institute of Chicago">Art Institute of Chicago</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Museum" title="Bass Museum">Bass Museum</a>, Miami; <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Art_Moderne_de_la_Ville_de_Paris" title="Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris">Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig_Museum" title="Ludwig Museum">Ludwig Museum</a>, Cologne; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stedelijk_Museum" title="Stedelijk Museum">Stedelijk Museum</a>, Amsterdam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Haring did a wide variety of public works, including the infirmary at Children's Village in <a href="/wiki/Dobbs_Ferry,_New_York" title="Dobbs Ferry, New York">Dobbs Ferry, New York</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-khfI_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-khfI-27">[27]</a></sup> and the second floor men's room in the <a href="/wiki/Lesbian,_Gay,_Bisexual_%26_Transgender_Community_Center" title="Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual &amp; Transgender Community Center">Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual &amp; Transgender Community Center</a> in Manhattan, which was later transformed into an office and is known as the Keith Haring room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-khfII_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-khfII-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GC_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-GC-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_market">Art market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Art market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haring was represented by dealer <a href="/wiki/Tony_Shafrazi" title="Tony Shafrazi">Tony Shafrazi</a> until he died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artinfo.com_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-artinfo.com-30">[30]</a></sup> Since the artist's death in 1990, his estate has been administered by the Keith Haring Foundation. The foundation has a twofold mission of supporting educational opportunities for underprivileged children and financing AIDS research and patient care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The foundation is represented by <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Gladstone" title="Barbara Gladstone">Gladstone Gallery</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Authentication_issues">Authentication issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Authentication issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is no <a href="/wiki/Catalogue_raisonn%C3%A9" title="Catalogue raisonné">catalogue raisonné</a> for Haring; however, there is copious information about Haring available on the estate's website and elsewhere, enabling prospective buyers or sellers to research exhibition history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2012, the Keith Haring Foundation disbanded its authentication board; that same year, it donated $1 million to support exhibitions at the <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Museum_of_American_Art" title="Whitney Museum of American Art">Whitney Museum of American Art</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artinfo.com_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-artinfo.com-30">[30]</a></sup> and $1 million to <a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood" title="Planned Parenthood">Planned Parenthood</a> of New York City's Project Street Beat. A 2014 lawsuit, filed by a group of nine art collectors at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">United States District Court for the Southern District of New York</a>, argued that the Keith Haring Foundation's actions have "limited the number of Haring works in the public domain, thereby increasing the value of the Haring works that the foundation and its members own or sell."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keith_Haring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boxer_von_Keith_Haring,_1987,_Berlin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1632" data-file-width="1232" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg/220px-Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg/330px-Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg/440px-Boxer_von_Keith_Haring%2C_1987%2C_Berlin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<i><a href="/wiki/The_Boxers_(sculpture)" title="The Boxers (sculpture)">The Boxers</a></i> (1987), located near <a href="/wiki/Potsdamer_Platz" title="Potsdamer Platz">Potsdamer Platz</a>, Berlin</div>

<p>Haring is the subject of a composition, <i><a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ferrero#Chamber.2FInstrumental" title="Lorenzo Ferrero">Haring at the Exhibition</a></i>, written and performed by Italian composer <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ferrero" title="Lorenzo Ferrero">Lorenzo Ferrero</a> in collaboration with DJ Nicola Guiducci. The work combines excerpts from popular chart music of the 1980s with samples of classical music compositions by Lorenzo Ferrero and synthesized sounds. It was featured at "The Keith Haring Show",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> an exhibition which took place in 2005 at the <a href="/wiki/Triennale" title="Triennale">Triennale di Milano</a>.</p>
<p>In 2006, Keith Haring was named by Equality Forum as one of their 31 Icons of the <a href="/wiki/LGBT_History_Month" title="LGBT History Month">LGBT History Month</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, filmmaker Christina Clausen released the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/The_Universe_of_Keith_Haring" title="The Universe of Keith Haring">The Universe of Keith Haring</a></i>. In the film, the legacy of Haring is resurrected through colorful archival footage and remembered by friends and admirers such as artists <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Scharf" title="Kenny Scharf">Kenny Scharf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">Yoko Ono</a>, gallery owners <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Deitch" title="Jeffrey Deitch">Jeffrey Deitch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Shafrazi" title="Tony Shafrazi">Tony Shafrazi</a>, and the choreographer <a href="/wiki/Bill_T._Jones" title="Bill T. Jones">Bill T. Jones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>, who was friends with Haring during the 1980s, used his art as animated backdrops for her 2008/2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sticky_and_Sweet_Tour" title="Sticky and Sweet Tour">Sticky and Sweet Tour</a>. The animation is standard Haring featuring his trademark blocky figures dancing in beat to an updated remix of "<a href="/wiki/Into_the_Groove" title="Into the Groove">Into the Groove</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Keith Haring: Double Retrospect</i> is a monster sized <a href="/wiki/Jigsaw_puzzle" title="Jigsaw puzzle">jigsaw puzzle</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ravensburger" title="Ravensburger">Ravensburger</a> measuring in at 17' x 6' with 32,256 pieces, breaking <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records"><i>Guinness Book of World Records</i></a> for the largest puzzle ever made. The puzzle uses 32 pieces of work from Haring and weighs 42 pounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puzzle_record_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-puzzle_record-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 4, 2012, on what would have been Haring's 54th birthday, Google honored him in a <a href="/wiki/Google_Doodle" title="Google Doodle">Google Doodle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring designed the album cover for the <i><a href="/wiki/A_Very_Special_Christmas_(album)" title="A Very Special Christmas (album)">A Very Special Christmas</a></i> music compilation album which consists of a typical Haring figure holding a baby. Its "Jesus iconography " is considered unusual in modern rock holiday albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Santino1996_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Santino1996-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haring had a balloon in tribute to him at the <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade">Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade</a>. The balloon slammed into the NBC broadcast booth during the 2008 parade, and the program went off the air for a moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
