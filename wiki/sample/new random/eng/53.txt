<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Measure word</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about measure words in general. For measure words in Chinese, see <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classifier" title="Chinese classifier">Chinese classifier</a>. For measure words in Japanese, see <a href="/wiki/Japanese_counter_word" title="Japanese counter word">Japanese counter word</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <b>measure words</b> are words (or <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a>) that are used in combination with a numeral to indicate an amount of something represented by some <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measure_word&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Measure words denote a unit or measurement and are used with <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">mass nouns</a> (uncountable nouns), and in some cases also with <a href="/wiki/Count_noun" title="Count noun">count nouns</a>. For instance, in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mud" title="wikt:mud">mud</a></i> is a mass noun and thus one cannot say "three muds", but one can say "three <u>drops</u> of mud", "three <u>pails</u> of mud", etc. In these examples, <i>drops</i> and <i>pails</i> function as measure words. One can also say "three <u>pails</u> of shells"; in this case the measure word <i>pails</i> accompanies a count noun (<i>shells</i>).</p>
<p>The term <i>measure word</i> is also sometimes used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Classifier_(linguistics)" title="Classifier (linguistics)">numeral classifiers</a>, which are used with count nouns in some languages. For instance, in English no extra word is needed when saying "three people", but in many <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">East Asian languages</a> a numeral classifier is added, just as a measure word is added for uncountable nouns in English. While many linguists maintain a distinction between measure words and numeral classifiers, the terms are sometimes used interchangeably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> For instance, materials for teaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_as_a_second_language" title="Chinese as a second language">Chinese as a second language</a> generally refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_classifiers" title="Chinese classifiers">Chinese classifiers</a> as "measure words". The corresponding Chinese term is <i>liàngcí</i> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified</a> 量词, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional</a> 量詞), which can be directly translated as "measure word".</p>
<p>Most measure words in English correspond to <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a> or containers, and are themselves count nouns rather than <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">grammatical particles</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>one <i>quart</i> of water</li>
<li>three <i>cups</i> of coffee</li>
<li>four <i>kernels</i> of corn, three <i>ears</i> of corn, two <i>bushels</i> of corn</li>
</ul>
<p>In many languages, including the East Asian languages referred to above, the analogous constructions do not include any equivalent of the English <i>of</i>. In German, for example, <i>ein Glas Bier</i> means "a glass of beer".</p>
