<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical year</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>tropical year</b> (also known as a <b>solar year</b>), for general purposes, is the <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> takes to return to the same position in the cycle of <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, as seen from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>; for example, the time from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">vernal equinox</a> to vernal equinox, or from <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">summer solstice</a> to summer solstice. Because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">precession of the equinoxes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonal</a> cycle does not remain exactly synchronized with the position of the Earth in its orbit around the Sun. As a consequence, the tropical year is about 20 minutes <i>shorter</i> than the time it takes Earth to complete one full orbit around the Sun as measured with respect to the fixed stars (the <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal year</a>).</p>
<p>Since antiquity, astronomers have progressively refined the definition of the tropical year. The <i>Astronomical Almanac Online Glossary</i> 2015 states:</p>
<blockquote>
<dl>
<dd>year, tropical:
<dl>
<dd>the period of time for the ecliptic longitude of the Sun to increase 360 <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>. Since the Sun's ecliptic longitude is measured with respect to the equinox, the tropical year comprises a complete cycle of seasons, and its length is approximated in the long term by the civil (Gregorian) calendar. The mean tropical year is approximately 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45 seconds.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>An equivalent, more descriptive, definition is "The natural basis for computing passing tropical years is the mean longitude of the Sun reckoned from the precessionally moving equinox (the dynamical equinox or equinox of date). Whenever the longitude reaches a multiple of 360 degrees the mean Sun crosses the vernal equinox and a new tropical year begins". (Borkowski 1991, p.Â 122)</p>
<p>The mean tropical year in 2000 was 365.24219 <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris_day" title="Ephemeris day">ephemeris days</a>; each ephemeris day lasting 86,400 SI seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIsecond_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIsecond-1">[1]</a></sup> This is 365.24217 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_solar_day" title="Mean solar day">mean solar days</a>. (Richards, 2013, p. 587)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span></h3>
<p>The word "tropical" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>tropikos</i> meaning "turn". (tropic, 1992) Thus, the tropics of <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Capricorn</a> mark the extreme north and south <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> where the Sun can appear directly overhead, and where it appears to "turn" in its annual seasonal motion. Because of this connection between the tropics and the seasonal cycle of the apparent position of the Sun, the word "tropical" also lent its name to the "tropical year". The early Chinese, Hindus, Greeks, and others made approximate measures of the tropical year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_value.2C_precession_discovery">Early value, precession discovery</span></h3>
<p>In the 2nd century BC <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a> measured the time required for the Sun to travel from an <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a> to the same equinox again. He reckoned the length of the year to be 1/300 of a day less than 365.25 days (365 days, 5 hours, 55 minutes, 12 seconds, or 365.24667 days). Hipparchus used this method because he was better able to detect the time of the equinoxes, compared to that of the solstices (Meeus &amp; Savoie, 1992, p.Â 40).</p>
<p>Hipparchus also discovered that the equinoctial points moved along the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> (plane of the Earth's orbit, or what Hipparchus would have thought of as the plane of the Sun's orbit about the Earth) in a direction opposite that of the movement of the Sun, a phenomenon that came to be named "precession of the equinoxes". He reckoned the value as 1Â° per century, a value that was not improved upon until about 1000 years later, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomy_in_medieval_Islam" title="Astronomy in medieval Islam">Islamic astronomers</a>. Since this discovery a distinction has been made between the tropical year and the sidereal year. (Meeus &amp; Savoie, 1992, p.Â 40)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_the_Renaissance">Middle Ages and the Renaissance</span></h3>
<p>During the Middle Ages and Renaissance a number of progressively better tables were published that allowed computation of the positions of the Sun, <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a> relative to the fixed stars. An important application of these tables was the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_reform" title="Calendar reform">reform of the calendar</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonsine_Tables" title="Alfonsine Tables">Alfonsine Tables</a>, published in 1252, were based on the theories of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> and were revised and updated after the original publication; the most recent update in 1978 was by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_National_Centre_for_Scientific_Research" title="French National Centre for Scientific Research">French National Centre for Scientific Research</a>. The length of the tropical year was given as 365 solar days 5 hours 49 minutes 16 seconds (âÂ 365.24255 days). This length was used in devising the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> of 1582. (Meeus &amp; Savoie, 1992, p.Â 41)</p>
<p>In the 16th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernicus" title="Copernicus">Copernicus</a> put forward a <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">heliocentric cosmology</a>. Erasmus Reinhold used Copernicus' theory to compute the <a href="/wiki/Prutenic_Tables" title="Prutenic Tables">Prutenic Tables</a> in 1551, and gave a tropical year length of 365 solar days, 5 hours, 55 minutes, 58 seconds (365.24720 days), based on the length of a <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal year</a> and the presumed rate of precession. This was actually less accurate than the earlier value of the Alfonsine Tables.</p>
<p>Major advances in the 17th century were made by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>. In 1609 and 1619 Kepler published his three laws of planetary motion. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, p.Â 26) In 1627, Kepler used the observations of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> and Waltherus to produce the most accurate tables up to that time, the <a href="/wiki/Rudolphine_Tables" title="Rudolphine Tables">Rudolphine Tables</a>. He evaluated the mean tropical year as 365 solar days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45 seconds (365.24219 days). (Meeus &amp; Savoie, 1992, p.Â 41)</p>
<p>Newton's three laws of dynamics and theory of gravity were published in his <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="PhilosophiÃ¦ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">PhilosophiÃ¦ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i> in 1687. Newton's theoretical and mathematical advances influenced tables by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmund_Halley" title="Edmund Halley">Edmund Halley</a> published in 1693 and 1749 (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, pp.Â 26â28) and provided the underpinnings of all solar system models until <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">General relativity</a> in the 20th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_and_19th_century">18th and 19th century</span></h3>
<p>From the time of Hipparchus and Ptolemy, the year was based on two equinoxes (or two solstices) a number of years apart, to average out both observational errors and the effects of <a href="/wiki/Nutation" title="Nutation">nutation</a> (periodic motions of the axis of rotation of the earth, the main cycle being 18.6 years) and the movement of the Sun caused by the gravitational pull of the planets. These effects did not begin to be understood until Newton's time. To model short-term variations of the time between equinoxes (and prevent them from confounding efforts to measure long-term variations) requires either precise observations or an elaborate theory of the apparent motion of the Sun. The necessary theories and mathematical tools came together in the 18th century due to the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_de_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon de Laplace">Pierre-Simon de Laplace</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph Louis Lagrange">Joseph Louis Lagrange</a>, and other specialists in <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a>. They were able to express the mean longitude of the Sun as</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>L</i><sub>0</sub> = <i>A</i><sub>0</sub> + <i>A</i><sub>1</sub><i>T</i> + <i>A</i><sub>2</sub><i>T</i><sup>2</sup> days</dd>
</dl>
<p>where <i>T</i> is the time in Julian centuries. The inverse of the derivative of <i>L</i><sub>0</sub>, <i>dT/dL</i><sub>0</sub> gives the length of the tropical year as a linear function of T. When this is computed, an expression giving the length of the tropical year as a function of T results.</p>

<p>Newcomb's tables were successful enough that they were used by the joint American-British <i><a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac" title="Astronomical Almanac">Astronomical Almanac</a></i> for the Sun, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> through 1983. (Seidelmann, 1992, p.Â 317)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_and_21st_centuries">20th and 21st centuries</span></h3>
<p>The length of the mean tropical year is derived from a model of the solar system, so any advance that improves the solar system model potentially improves the accuracy of the mean tropical year. Many new observing instruments became available, including</p>
<ul>
<li>artificial satellites</li>
<li>tracking of deep space probes such as <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_4" title="Pioneer 4">Pioneer 4</a> beginning in 1959 (Jet Propulsion Laboratory 2005)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">radars</a> able to measure other planets beginning in 1961 (Butrica, 1996)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_laser_ranging" title="Lunar laser ranging">lunar laser ranging</a> since the 1969 <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> left the first of a series of <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">retroreflectors</a> which allow greater accuracy than reflectorless measurements</li>
<li>artificial satellites such as <a href="/wiki/LAGEOS" title="LAGEOS">LAGEOS</a> (1976) and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> (initial operation in 1993)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Long_Baseline_Interferometry" title="Very Long Baseline Interferometry">Very Long Baseline Interferometry</a> which finds precise directions to <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> in distant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a>, and allows determination of the Earth's orientation with respect to these objects whose distance is so great they can be considered to show minimal space motion (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, p.Â 265)</li>
</ul>
<p>The complexity of the model used for the solar system must be limited to the available computation facilities. In the 1920s punched card equipment came into use by L. J. Comrie in Britain. At the <i>American Ephemeris</i> an electromagnetic computer, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Selective_Sequence_Electronic_Calculator" title="IBM Selective Sequence Electronic Calculator">IBM Selective Sequence Electronic Calculator</a> was used since 1948. When modern computers became available, it was possible to compute ephemerides using <a href="/wiki/Numerical_integration" title="Numerical integration">numerical integration</a> rather than general theories; numerical integration came into use in 1984 for the joint US-UK almanacs. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, p.Â 32)</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein" title="Einstein">Einstein</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Theory_of_Relativity" title="General Theory of Relativity">General Theory of Relativity</a> provided a more accurate theory, but the accuracy of theories and observations did not require the refinement provided by this theory (except for the advance of the perihelion of Mercury) until 1984. Time scales incorporated general relativity beginning in the 1970s. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, p.Â 37)</p>
<p>A key development in understanding the tropical year over long periods of time is the discovery that the rate of rotation of the earth, or equivalently, the length of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_day" title="Solar day">mean solar day</a>, is not constant. William Ferrel in 1864 and <a href="/wiki/Charles-Eug%C3%A8ne_Delaunay" title="Charles-EugÃ¨ne Delaunay">Charles-EugÃ¨ne Delaunay</a> in 1865 indicated the rotation of the Earth was being retarded by tides. In 1921 William H Shortt invented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortt-Synchronome_clock" title="Shortt-Synchronome clock">Shortt-Synchronome clock</a>, the most accurate commercially produced pendulum clock; it was the first clock capable of measuring variations in the Earth's rotation. The next major time-keeping advance was the <a href="/wiki/Quartz_clock" title="Quartz clock">quartz clock</a>, first built by Warren Marrison and J. W. Horton in 1927; in the late 1930s quartz clocks began to replace pendulum clocks as time standards. (McCarthy and Seidelmann, 2009, ch. 9)</p>
<p>A series of experiments beginning in the late 1930s led to the development of the first <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">atomic clock</a> by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Essen" title="Louis Essen">Louis Essen</a> and J. V. L. Parry in 1955. Their clock was based on a transition in the cesium atom. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, pp.Â 157â9) Due to the accuracy the <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> in 1960 redefined the second in terms of the cesium transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIsecond_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SIsecond-1">[1]</a></sup> The atomic second, often called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> second, was intended to agree with the ephemeris second based on Newcomb's work, which in turn makes it agree with the mean solar second of the mid-19th century. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelman, 2009, pp.Â 81â2, 191â7)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_scales">Time scales</span></h2>
<p>As mentioned in <a href="#History">History</a>, advances in time-keeping have resulted in various time scales. One useful time scale is <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">Universal Time</a> (especially the UT1 variant), which is the mean solar time at 0 degrees <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwich_meridian" title="Greenwich meridian">Greenwich meridian</a>). One second of UT is 1/86,400 of a mean solar day. This time scale is known to be somewhat variable. Since all civil calendars count actual solar days, they must ultimately be based on UT (but the actual timing of official midnight is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>).</p>
<p>The other time scale has two parts. Ephemeris time (ET) is the independent variable in the equations of motion of the solar system, in particular, the equations in use from 1960 to 1984. (McCarthy &amp; Seidelmann, 2009, p.Â 378) That is, the length of the second used in the solar system calculations could be adjusted until the length that gives the best agreement with observations is found. With the introduction of atomic clocks in the 1950s, it was found that ET could be better realized as atomic time. This also means that ET is a uniform time scale, as is atomic time. ET was given a new name, <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">Terrestrial Time</a> (TT), and for most purposes ET = TT = <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Time" title="International Atomic Time">International Atomic Time</a> + 32.184 SI seconds. As of January 2010, TT is ahead of UT1 by about 66 seconds. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_Service" title="International Earth Rotation Service">International Earth Rotation Service</a>, 2010; McCarthy &amp; Seidelman, 2009, pp.Â 86â7).</p>
<p>As explained below, long-term estimates of the length of the tropical year were used in connection with the reform of the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, which resulted in the Gregorian calendar. Participants in that reform were only partially aware of the non-uniform rotation of the Earth, but now this can be taken into account to some degree. The amount that TT is ahead of UT1 is known as <a href="/wiki/%CE%94T" title="ÎT">Î<i>T</i></a>, or Delta <i>T</i>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Length_of_tropical_year">Length of tropical year</span></h2>
<p>An oversimplified definition of the tropical year would be the time required for the Sun, beginning at a chosen ecliptic longitude, to make one complete cycle of the seasons and return to the same ecliptic longitude. Before considering an example, the equinox must be examined. There are two important planes in solar system calculations, the plane of the ecliptic (the Earth's orbit around the Sun), and the plane of the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a> (the Earth's equator projected into space). These two planes intersect in a line. This <b>direction</b> is given the symbol â (the symbol looks like the horns of a <a href="/wiki/Bighorn_sheep" title="Bighorn sheep">ram</a> because it used to be toward the constellation <a href="/wiki/Aries_(astrology)" title="Aries (astrology)">Aries</a>). The opposite <b>direction</b> is given the symbol â (because it used to be toward <a href="/wiki/Libra_(astrology)" title="Libra (astrology)">Libra</a>). Because of precession and nutation these directions change, compared to the direction of distant stars and galaxies, whose directions have no measurable motion due to their great distance (see <a href="/wiki/International_Celestial_Reference_Frame" title="International Celestial Reference Frame">International Celestial Reference Frame</a>).</p>
<p>The ecliptic longitude of the Sun is the angle between â and the Sun, measured eastward along the ecliptic. This creates a complicated measurement, because as the Sun is moving, the direction the angle is measured from is also moving. It is convenient to have a fixed (with respect to distant stars) direction to measure from; the direction of â at noon January 1, 2000 fills this role and is given the symbol â<sub>0</sub>.</p>
<p>Using the oversimplified definition, there was an equinox on March 20, 2009, 11:44:43.6 TT. The 2010 March equinox was March 20, 17:33:18.1 TT, which gives a duration of 365 days 5 hours 48 minutes 34.5 seconds.(Astronomical Applications Dept., 2009) While the Sun moves, â moves in the opposite direction . When the Sun and â met at the 2010 March equinox, the Sun had moved east 359Â°59'09" while â had moved west 51" for a total of 360Â° (all with respect to â<sub>0</sub>). (Seidelmann, 1992, p.Â 104, expression for p<sub>A</sub>)</p>
<p>If a different starting longitude for the Sun is chosen, the duration for the Sun to return to the same longitude will be different. This is because although â changes at a nearly steady rate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> there is considerable variation in the angular speed of the Sun. Thus, the 50 or so arcseconds that the Sun does not have to move to complete the tropical year "saves" varying amounts of time depending on the position in the orbit.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calendar_year">Calendar year</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, as used for civil and scientific purposes, is an international standard. It is a solar calendar that is designed to maintain synchrony with the mean tropical year. (Dobrzycki 1983, p.Â 123) It has a cycle of 400 years (146,097 days). Each cycle repeats the months, dates, and weekdays. <p>The Gregorian calendar is a reformed version of the Julian calendar. By the time of the reform in 1582, the date of the vernal equinox had shifted about 10 days, from about March 21 at the time of the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> in 325, to about March 11. According to North, the real motivation for reform was not primarily a matter of getting agricultural cycles back to where they had once been in the seasonal cycle; the primary concern of Christians was the correct observance of Easter. The rules used to <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">compute the date of Easter</a> used a conventional date for the vernal equinox (March 21), and it was considered important to keep March 21 close to the actual equinox. (North, 1983, pp.Â 75â76)</p>
<p>If society in the future still attaches importance to the synchronization between the civil calendar and the seasons, another reform of the calendar will eventually be necessary. According to Blackburn and Holford-Strevens (who used Newcomb's value for the tropical year) if the tropical year remained at its 1900 value of <span style="white-space:nowrap">365.242<span style="margin-left:0.25em">198</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em">781</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em">25</span></span> days the Gregorian calendar would be 3 days, 17 min, 33 s behind the Sun after 10,000 years. Aggravating this error, the length of the tropical year (measured in Terrestrial Time) is decreasing at a rate of approximately 0.53 s per century. Also, the mean solar day is getting longer at a rate of about 1.5 ms per century. These effects will cause the calendar to be nearly a day behind in 3200. The number of solar days in a "tropical millennium" is decreasing by about 0.06 per millennium (neglecting the oscillatory changes in the real length of the tropical year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This means there should be fewer and fewer leap days as time goes on. A possible reform would be to omit the leap day in 3200, keep 3600 and 4000 as leap years, and thereafter make all centennial years common except 4500, 5000, 5500, 6000, etc. But the quantity <a href="/wiki/%CE%94T" title="ÎT">ÎT</a> is not sufficiently predictable to form more precise proposals. (Blackburn &amp; Holford-Strevens, 2003, p.Â 692)</p>
