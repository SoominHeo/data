<p><b>Nii-jima</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新島</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islands_of_Japan" title="Islands of Japan">Japanese island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Sea" title="Philippine Sea">Philippine Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The island is administered by <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> Metropolitan Government. It is one of the Izu Seven Islands group of the seven northern islands of the <a href="/wiki/Izu_Islands" title="Izu Islands">Izu archipelago</a>, and is located approximately 163 kilometres (101 mi) south of Tōkyō and 36 kilometres (22 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Shimoda,_Shizuoka" title="Shimoda, Shizuoka">Shimoda</a> <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka_Prefecture" title="Shizuoka Prefecture">Shizuoka Prefecture</a>. The island is the larger inhabited component of the village of <a href="/wiki/Niijima,_Tokyo" title="Niijima, Tokyo">Niijima Village</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cshima_Subprefecture_(Tokyo)" title="Ōshima Subprefecture (Tokyo)">Ōshima Subprefecture</a> of Tokyo Metropolis, which also contains the larger, neighboring island of <a href="/wiki/Shikine-jima" title="Shikine-jima">Shikine-jima</a> and the smaller, uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Jinai-t%C5%8D" title="Jinai-tō">Jinai-tō</a>. Nii-jima is also within the boundaries of the <a href="/wiki/Fuji-Hakone-Izu_National_Park" title="Fuji-Hakone-Izu National Park">Fuji-Hakone-Izu National Park</a>.</p>
<p>On the southern tip of Nii-jima, there was formerly a launch site for experimental and <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rockets</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nii-jima is unusual amongst the Izu Islands in that it has an elongated shape. Measuring approximately 11 kilometres (6.8 mi) long by 2.5 kilometres (1.6 mi) wide, it has a land area of 23.87 km<sup>2</sup>. The island is made of eight <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolitic lava</a> domes in two groups at the northern and southern ends of the island, separated by a low, flat isthmus. The Mukai-yama <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">向山</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> complex in the southern portion of the island and the Achiyama lava dome at the northern end were formed during Nii-jima's only historical eruptions in the 9th century AD. The northern end also contains Miyatsuka-yama <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">宮塚山</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, the island’s highest point, at 432 metres (1,417 ft). Shikine-jima and Jinnai-to are part of the same complex, and form separate islands to the southwest and west of Nii-jima. Rhyolite lava gives the island its famed white cliffs and white sandy beaches</p>
<p>Nii-jima is prone to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_swarms" title="Earthquake swarms">earthquake swarms</a>. According to the US Geological Survey map, the area around Nii-jima averages 10–20 earthquakes, with a magnitude of 5 or greater, each year. However, the often repeated legend that Nii-jima and Shikine-jima were once a single island that was separated by huge <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/1703_Genroku_earthquake" title="1703 Genroku earthquake">1703 Genroku earthquake</a> has no basis in geology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature">Nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even though Nii-jima is located relatively close to <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a>, the small population and lower exploitation has allowed the island's natural wildlife to be well preserved, and many varieties can be seen. Riding on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuroshio" title="Kuroshio">Kuroshio</a> current, many oceanic species and birds migrate thorough the island, <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific_bottlenose_dolphin" title="Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphin">Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins</a> that have seemingly moved to here, and other areas, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikurajima" title="Mikurajima">Mikurajima</a> inhabit the near shores; along with nesting <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, and the occasional visiting <a href="/wiki/Manta_ray" title="Manta ray">manta ray</a>.</p>
<p>Almost completely wiped out, due to past hunting, biodiversity of other marine mammals, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetacean" title="Cetacean">cetaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">pinnipeds</a>, have become very small and today are only a remnant of their historical numbers.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sperm_whale" title="Sperm whale">Sperm whales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">humpback whales</a>, are the most likely of the larger whales to be observed around Nii-jima, using the island as either a resting ground or a migratory collider from, and to, the wintering grounds around the <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a>.</p>
<p>There are also sightings of extremely rare and critically endangered species such as the <a href="/wiki/Northern_elephant_seal" title="Northern elephant seal">northern elephant seal</a> (a vagrant was captured in 1989, making it the first record of the species in Japan)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_right_whale" title="North Pacific right whale">North Pacific right whales</a> (one whale stayed just several meters off the port in 2011, and kept <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whale_surfacing_behaviour" title="Whale surfacing behaviour">tail-slapping</a> continuously for several hours).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally the, possibly now-extinct, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_sea_lion" title="Japanese sea lion">Japanese sea lion</a> used to breed in the Izu Islands and in the vicinity of Nii-jima, there was a colony on <a href="/wiki/Udone-shima" title="Udone-shima">Udone-shima</a>. Although on a different island, in the past a <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_crocodile" title="Saltwater crocodile">saltwater crocodile</a> has appeared on <a href="/wiki/Hachij%C5%8D-jima" title="Hachijō-jima">Hachijō-jima</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access">Access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Nii-jima is 2 hours and 20 minutes away by jet boat from Takeshiba Sanbashi Pier, in Tokyo, operated by Tōkai Kisen. Tōkai Kisen also operates a 9-hour overnight ferry. The ferry leaves Takeshiba Sanbashi at 22h00 (23h00 in the summer months) and arrives early morning in <a href="/wiki/Izu_%C5%8Cshima" title="Izu Ōshima">Izu Ōshima</a> (approximately 6h00), before continuing on to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/To-shima" title="To-shima">To-shima</a> (7h00), Nii-jima (8h00), Shikine-jima (8h30), and <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dzu-shima" title="Kōzu-shima">Kōzu-shima</a> (9h30). The ferry then returns following the same route, leaving Nii-jima at 12h00 and docking in Tokyo at 17h00. It is possible that in rough weather, the ferry is unable to dock in Nii-jima.</p>
<p>There are daily flights, weather permitting, from <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dfu_Airport" title="Chōfu Airport">Chōfu Airport</a> located in western Tokyo. The flight takes approximately 45 minutes.</p>
<p>Other ferries leave from <a href="/wiki/Shimoda,_Shizuoka" title="Shimoda, Shizuoka">Shimoda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka_Prefecture" title="Shizuoka Prefecture">Shizuoka Prefecture</a>.</p>
<p>Niijima-mura also operates a high-speed ferry between Nii-jima and Shikine-jima with 3 boats per day, and 4 per day in the summer months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nii-jima has been inhabited since prehistoric times, and archaeologists have found numerous remains from the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period" title="Jōmon period">Jōmon period</a>, including stone and ceramic utensils. During the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, as with <a href="/wiki/Hachij%C5%8D-jima" title="Hachijō-jima">Hachijō-jima</a>, Nii-jima was used as a place of <a href="/wiki/Criminal_punishment_in_Edo-period_Japan" title="Criminal punishment in Edo-period Japan">exile for convicts</a>. The practice was discontinued after the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="717" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg/150px-Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg/225px-Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg/300px-Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doraemon_visits_Niijima_High_School.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Doraemon" title="Doraemon">Doraemon</a> visits Niijima High School. A <i>moyai</i> sits in the upper right of the photo.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Main industries in Nii-jima include commercial and sports fishing, construction, <i>koga</i> rock mining, and tourism. There is also some small scale farming.</p>
<p>Koga stone, a pumice-derived rock of <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a> was utilized as house building material. Now its silica component is used to make transparent green <a href="/wiki/Glass_art" title="Glass art">glass art</a>. It can also be used to make <i>moyai</i> art. The stone is indigenous only to Nii-jima and <a href="/wiki/Lipari" title="Lipari">Lipari</a> Island, Italy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sites">Sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="717" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg/150px-Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg/225px-Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg/300px-Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niijima_Harbor_in_Winter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Western, or <i>Maehama</i> side of Nii-jima with December waves crashing through the port. To-shima is the island top left.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Maehama Beach on the western side of Nii-jima sees many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_surfing" title="Wind surfing">wind surfers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a> and ocean water swims take place here. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Fuji" title="Mt. Fuji">Mt. Fuji</a> can often be seen from Maehama.</p>
<p>Habushi Beach, on the eastern side of the island, is a nationally protected reserve with its waves and white sand, and is a good location for <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a>. The beach is approximately 6.5 km long and is overlooked by <i>koga</i> volcanic cliffs, the highest of which is 250 meters.</p>
<p>Moyai Hill, overlooking Yunohama and Maehama beaches, contains more than 100 large stone carvings. In the local dialect, <i>moyai</i> means 'to work together in effort', and these statues make evident this effort. On the western side of JR <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shibuya,_Tokyo" title="Shibuya, Tokyo">Shibuya</a> in Tokyo proper is a giant <i>moyai</i> statue, a gift from the people of Nii-jima.</p>
<p>Yunohama Onsen <a href="/wiki/Onsen" title="Onsen">hot spring</a>, on Yunohama Beach, is a large outdoor bath built in the style of pseudo-Greek ruins that provides stunning panoramic views of the setting sun and the Pacific Ocean. The bath itself accommodates up to 100 bathers. Water used in the bath is drawn from the ocean below.</p>
<p>Jūsansha Jinja, is a <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shinto shrine</a> at the base of the cliffs of Mt. Miyatsuka in the north-western corner of the main village on the island. This shrine, built in the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, is recognized as caretakers of intangible cultural assets by the Tokyo government for the <a href="/wiki/Kagura" title="Kagura">kagura</a> music and sacred dancing, known as <i>shishi-kiyari</i> that are held every December 8.</p>
<p>Nearby Jūsansha is Chōei-ji, Chōei Temple, a temple dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Nichiren_Buddhism" title="Nichiren Buddhism">Nichiren Buddhism</a>. Beside the temple lies the Exiles' Cemetery. The cemetery, covered with the local white sand, is dominated by the gravestones of the 118 exiles, banished to Niijima by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Shogunate" title="Tokugawa Shogunate">Tokugawa Shogunate</a> during the Edo era for non-political crimes.</p>
<p>A short walk from Chōei Temple is the Exile Execution Ground. Eleven exiles who committed crimes on the island were executed here. Komori Yasu, from the <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> story 'Yowa Nasake Ukinano Yokoguchi' is buried here.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Niijima_Glass_Art_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Niijima Glass Art Center (page does not exist)">Niijima Glass Art Center</a> is a world-renowned site which hosts the Niijima International Glass Art Festival every autumn. At the center, visitors are able to create their own glass work to take home. Next to the museum is the Niijima Glass Art Museum which houses works from guest artists at the festival.</p>
<p>Niijima-mura Museum, houses artifacts from the island’s pre-history up to its modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfing_culture" title="Surfing culture">surfing culture</a>. Included is a replica fishing vessel and house from the Edo period. Details of the criminal exiles are given. A collaborative effort between the education board and the English department at Niijima High School ensured that the museum is completely bilingual: Japanese-English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="717" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg/150px-Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg/225px-Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg/300px-Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niijima_Triathlon_begins.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Swimmers begin the Tokyo Islands Triathlon in Nii-jima at Maehama Beach</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Niijima hosts the Tokyo Islands Triathlon in Niijima (1.5 km swim, 35 km bicycle ride, and a 10 km run) every May and the Niijima Open Water Swimming (1.5, 3, and 4.5 km open water swims). In the summer months, a number of surfing competitions take place at Habushi Beach. Sport fishing and scuba diving are also popular with both residents and tourists.</p>
<p>Every autumn, the Niijima International Glass Art Festival takes place. The festival has taken place since 1988. The festival draws leading glass artists who lead workshops and demonstrations in glass art. Artists who have participated in the festival in the past include: <a href="/wiki/Dale_Chihuly" title="Dale Chihuly">Dale Chihuly</a>, William Morris, and Lucio Bubaco. Work by the above artists, and many more, are on display in the museum.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_attractions">Other attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nii-jima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other attractions on Nii-jima include: <i>Ebine</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Calanthe_discolor" title="Calanthe discolor">Calanthe discolor</a></i>), a pinkish-purplish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid">orchid</a>, can be found throughout the island, especially in Ebine Park, which opens from early spring.</p>
<p>Souvenirs from Niijima include the island specialty <i><a href="/wiki/Kusaya" title="Kusaya">kusaya</a></i> - a dried fish, usually <a href="/wiki/Mackerel_scad" title="Mackerel scad">mackerel scad</a> (<i>Decapterus macarellus</i>), that has been soaked in a special brine. <i>Kusaya</i>, which means <i>smells bad</i> in Japanese, has an odor that is indeed 'quite' distinct. The village museum has a video on the making of <i>kusaya</i>. If you can get past the smell, you will be rewarded with a rich, delicious meal. Other souvenirs are milk <i><a href="/wiki/Senbei" title="Senbei">senbei</a>,</i> the <i><a href="/wiki/Ashitaba" title="Ashitaba">ashitaba</a></i> plant, Niijima glass art, and a local <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dch%C5%AB" title="Shōchū">Shōchū</a> called <i>Shima Jiman</i>, island pride.</p>
<p>Popular YouTuber from Japan, <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/user/streetenglishtv" rel="nofollow">Chris Gen</a>, featured a trip to Nii-jima and some of his favorite spots of interest in his video.</p>
