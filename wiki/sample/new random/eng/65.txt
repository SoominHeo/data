<p><i><b>Mean Girls</b></i> is a 2004 American <a href="/wiki/Teen_film" title="Teen film">teen</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Waters_(director)" title="Mark Waters (director)">Mark Waters</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a>. The film is based in part on <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Wiseman" title="Rosalind Wiseman">Rosalind Wiseman</a>'s non-fiction self-help book <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Bees_and_Wannabes" title="Queen Bees and Wannabes">Queen Bees and Wannabes</a></i>, which describes female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> social <a href="/wiki/Clique" title="Clique">cliques</a> and the damaging effects they can have on girls.</p>
<p>The film stars <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Lohan" title="Lindsay Lohan">Lindsay Lohan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_McAdams" title="Rachel McAdams">Rachel McAdams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lacey_Chabert" title="Lacey Chabert">Lacey Chabert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizzy_Caplan" title="Lizzy Caplan">Lizzy Caplan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Franzese" title="Daniel Franzese">Daniel Franzese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Bennett_(actor)" title="Jonathan Bennett (actor)">Jonathan Bennett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Seyfried" title="Amanda Seyfried">Amanda Seyfried</a> (in her film debut). <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> (<i>SNL</i>) creator <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Michaels" title="Lorne Michaels">Lorne Michaels</a> produced the film. Screenwriter and co-star of the film, Tina Fey, was a long-term cast member and writer for <i>SNL</i>. Also featuring appearances from <i>SNL</i> cast members <a href="/wiki/Tim_Meadows" title="Tim Meadows">Tim Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ana_Gasteyer" title="Ana Gasteyer">Ana Gasteyer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amy_Poehler" title="Amy Poehler">Amy Poehler</a>. The film marks Lohan's second collaboration with director Waters, the first being <i><a href="/wiki/Freaky_Friday_(2003_film)" title="Freaky Friday (2003 film)">Freaky Friday</a></i> (2003), released a year earlier. Although set in <a href="/wiki/Evanston,_Illinois" title="Evanston, Illinois">Evanston, Illinois</a> (wealthy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburbs" title="Suburbs">suburbs</a>), the film was mostly shot in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, Ontario, Canada.</p>
<p>The film grossed $129 million worldwide and has developed a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls_2" title="Mean Girls 2">Mean Girls 2</a></i>, was released in 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sixteen-year-old <a href="/wiki/Homeschooling" title="Homeschooling">homeschooled</a> Cady Heron and her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoologist" title="Zoologist">zoologist</a> parents return to the United States after a 12-year research trip in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, settling down in <a href="/wiki/Evanston,_Illinois" title="Evanston, Illinois">Evanston, Illinois</a>. Attending a public school for the first time, Cady meets new classmates Janis Ian and Damian Leigh. The two educate Cady on the school's various cliques and warn her to avoid the most popular and infamous one, the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics_(subculture)" title="Plastics (subculture)">Plastics</a>", who are led by <a href="/wiki/Queen_bee_(sociology)" title="Queen bee (sociology)">queen bee</a> Regina George and include rich but insecure Gretchen Wieners and sweet airhead Karen Smith. The Plastics take an interest in Cady, and invite her to sit with them at lunch. Seeing that Cady is getting along with the Plastics, Janis hatches a plan of revenge against Regina for some past slight, using Cady as the infiltrator.</p>
<p>Cady soon learns about Regina's "Burn Book", a journal filled with rumors, secrets, and gossip about the other girls and some teachers at school. Despite this discovery, Cady decides to forgo Janis's scheme for ethical reasons. Meanwhile, Cady begins to have a crush on Regina's ex-boyfriend, Aaron Samuels, whom a jealous Regina proceeds to steal back at a Halloween party in front of Cady. This spurs Cady to fully commit to Janis's plan to cut off Regina's "resources", which involve breaking Regina and Aaron up, tricking her into eating nutrition bars that actually make her gain weight, and turning Regina's fellow Plastics against her. In the process, Cady unwittingly remakes herself in Regina's image, becoming spiteful and superficial, and abandons Janis and Damian.</p>
<p>When Regina is finally made aware of Cady's treachery, she retaliates by spreading the contents of her Burn Book all over the school, quickly inciting a riot. To avoid suspicion, Regina inserts a fake libel of herself in the book in order to blame Cady, Gretchen, and Karen, the only female students not mentioned in the book. Karen convinces Principal Duvall, that they did not spread the Book. Duvall soon quells the riot, and gathers all the girls in the school in the gymnasium. Math teacher Ms. Norbury, whom the Burn Book defamed as a drug dealer, makes the girls face the terrible way they all treat each other, confess their transgressions, and apologize to each other and the teachers. When Janis's turn comes, she confesses her plan to destroy Regina with Cady's help, and openly mocks Regina with the support of the entire school. Pursued by an apologetic Cady, Regina storms out and gets hit by a school bus, breaking her spine.</p>
<p>Without any friends, shunned by Aaron, and despised by the school, Cady takes full blame for the Burn Book and becomes an outcast. After making amends with Regina, Cady's guilt soon dissolves and she returns to her old personality. As part of her punishment for lying and failing Ms. Norbury's class, she joins the Mathletes in the state championship finals, and ends up winning the competition for her team after her opponent answers incorrectly. At the Spring Fling dance, Regina's new boyfriend, Shane Oman, is elected King, while Cady is elected Queen. Onstage, Cady declares that all her classmates are wonderful in their own way, breaks her plastic tiara, and distributes the pieces to some other girls. She then makes amends with Janis and Damian, reconciles with Aaron, and reaches a truce with the Plastics.</p>
<p>By the start of the new school year, the Plastics have disbanded. Regina has joined the lacrosse team to deal with her anger, Karen has become the school weather reporter (claiming earlier that her breasts can tell when it is raining), and Gretchen joins the "Cool Asians". Aaron graduates from high school and attends Northwestern University, Janis begins dating Kevin Gnapoor, whom she initially despised, and Cady declares that she is now normal. A group of new "Junior Plastics" has arisen, and Cady imagines them being hit by a bus.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a> read <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Wiseman" title="Rosalind Wiseman">Rosalind Wiseman</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Bees_and_Wannabes" title="Queen Bees and Wannabes">Queen Bees and Wannabes</a></i> and called <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> producer <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Michaels" title="Lorne Michaels">Lorne Michaels</a> to suggest it could be turned into a film. Michaels contacted <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>, who purchased the rights to the book. As the book is nonfiction, Fey wrote the plot from scratch, borrowing elements from her own high school experience. The real <a href="/wiki/Janis_Ian" title="Janis Ian">Janis Ian</a> was one of the first musical guests on <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live_(season_1)#Episodes" title="Saturday Night Live (season 1)">the first Saturday Night Live episode</a>, in which she sang the song "<a href="/wiki/At_Seventeen" title="At Seventeen">At Seventeen</a>", which can be heard playing in the background when the girls are fighting at Regina's house. Other characters bullying Caplan's character persistently call her a lesbian throughout the film; the real Janis Ian is an out lesbian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strong_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-strong-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Lohan" title="Lindsay Lohan">Lindsay Lohan</a> first read for Regina George, but the casting team felt she was closer to what they were looking for in the actress who played Cady, and since Lohan feared the "mean girl" role would harm her reputation, she agreed to play the lead. <a href="/wiki/Rachel_McAdams" title="Rachel McAdams">Rachel McAdams</a> was cast as Regina because Fey felt McAdams being "kind and polite" made her perfect for such an evil-spirited character. <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Seyfried" title="Amanda Seyfried">Amanda Seyfried</a> also read for Regina, and the producers instead suggested her for Karen due to Seyfried's "spacey and daffy sense of humor". Both <a href="/wiki/Lacey_Chabert" title="Lacey Chabert">Lacey Chabert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Franzese" title="Daniel Franzese">Daniel Franzese</a> were the last actors tested for their roles. <a href="/wiki/Lizzy_Caplan" title="Lizzy Caplan">Lizzy Caplan</a> was at first considered too pretty for the part of Janis, for which director <a href="/wiki/Mark_Waters_(director)" title="Mark Waters (director)">Mark Waters</a> felt a "<a href="/wiki/Kelly_Osbourne" title="Kelly Osbourne">Kelly Osbourne</a>-like actress" was necessary, but Caplan was picked for being able to portray raw emotion. Fey wrote two roles based on fellow <i>SNL</i> alumni, <a href="/wiki/Amy_Poehler" title="Amy Poehler">Amy Poehler</a> (whom Fey thought the producers would not accept for being too young) and <a href="/wiki/Tim_Meadows" title="Tim Meadows">Tim Meadows</a>, and the cast ended up with a fourth veteran of the show, <a href="/wiki/Ana_Gasteyer" title="Ana Gasteyer">Ana Gasteyer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strong_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-strong-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although set in <a href="/wiki/Evanston,_Illinois" title="Evanston, Illinois">Evanston, Illinois</a>, the film was mostly shot in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> at <a href="/wiki/Etobicoke_Collegiate_Institute" title="Etobicoke Collegiate Institute">Etobicoke Collegiate Institute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Collegiate_Institute" title="Malvern Collegiate Institute">Malvern Collegiate Institute</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Montclair,_New_Jersey" title="Montclair, New Jersey">Montclair, New Jersey</a> at <a href="/wiki/Montclair_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Montclair High School (New Jersey)">Montclair High School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Notable landmarks include the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Convocation_Hall_(University_of_Toronto)" title="Convocation Hall (University of Toronto)">Convocation Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sherway_Gardens" title="Sherway Gardens">Sherway Gardens</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its opening weekend, the film grossed $24.4 million in 2,839 theaters in the United States, ranking #1 at the box office and averaging $8,606 per venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boxofficemojo_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boxofficemojo-2">[2]</a></sup> By the end of its run, <i>Mean Girls</i> grossed approximately $86.1 million domestically and $43 million internationally, totaling $129 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boxofficemojo_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boxofficemojo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Critical_reception"></span><span id="Critics"></span> <i>Mean Girls</i> received generally positive reviews, critics lauded McAdams' performance and labeled it as Lohan's, Seyfried's and Caplan's breakthrough performances. Review aggregation website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives it a rating of 84%, based on 176 reviews, with the site's critical consensus stating that the film is "funnier and more smartly written than the average teen comedy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has a score of 66 out of 100, based on 39 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ann Hornaday of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> stated that it "Boasts a one-two-three punch in star Lindsay Lohan, screenwriter Tina Fey and director Mark Waters, and, indeed, it delivers a knockout". The screenplay was highly praised by critics with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> calling it "comic gold".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In November 2012, Rotten Tomatoes named it in its 'Top 50 Greatest Teen Comedies'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="DVD"></span><span id="Home_video_release"></span> <i>Mean Girls</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> in North America on September 21, 2004, five months after it opened in theaters. It was released in a widescreen special collector's edition and a fullscreen collector's edition, both including several <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted scenes</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Blooper" title="Blooper">blooper</a> reel, three interstitials, the theatrical trailer, previews, and three featurettes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeanGirlsDVD_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeanGirlsDVD-16">[16]</a></sup> A Blu-ray version of the film was released on April 14, 2009.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stand-alone_sequel">Stand-alone sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Stand-alone sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls_2" title="Mean Girls 2">Mean Girls 2</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-DVD</a> <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls_2" title="Mean Girls 2">Mean Girls 2</a></i>, was aired on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_Family" title="ABC Family">ABC Family</a> on January 23, 2011, and released on DVD on February 1. The film is a stand-alone sequel, and the plot does not continue the story of the first film nor does it have the same cast, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Tim_Meadows" title="Tim Meadows">Tim Meadows</a>, who reprises his role as Principal Ron Duvall. The film is directed by <a href="/wiki/Melanie_Mayron" title="Melanie Mayron">Melanie Mayron</a> and stars <a href="/wiki/Meaghan_Martin" title="Meaghan Martin">Meaghan Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Stone" title="Jennifer Stone">Jennifer Stone</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_cultural_impact">Legacy and cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legacy and cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film has become a pop-culture phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Fans have made <a href="/wiki/GIF" title="GIF">GIFs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meme" title="Meme">memes</a> of the film and posted them on social media, including <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tumblr" title="Tumblr">Tumblr</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a> expressed several times that she is a fan of the film, using some quotes from the film in several interviews. Carey released a single, "<a href="/wiki/Obsessed_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Obsessed (Mariah Carey song)">Obsessed</a>", which begins with an interlude quote where she says, "And I was like, 'Why are you so obsessed with me?'", a line said by Regina in the film. Carey's ex-husband, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cannon" title="Nick Cannon">Nick Cannon</a> revealed the song was inspired by the film itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> She then referenced the film again in 2013 during an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a></i>.</p>
<p>In an interview about the film, Fey noted, "Adults find it funny. They are the ones who are laughing. Young people watch it like a reality show. It's much too close to their real experiences so they are not exactly guffawing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> put it on its end-of-the-decade, "best-of" list, saying, "'Fetch' may never happen, but 2004's eminently quotable movie is still one of the sharpest high school satires ever. Which is pretty grool, if you ask me!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2006, <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> also named it the twelfth best high school film of all time, explaining: "There was a time when Lindsay Lohan was best known for her acting rather than her party-hopping. Showcasing Lindsay Lohan in arguably her best role to date, this Tina Fey-scripted film also boasts a breakout turn by Rachel McAdams as evil queen bee Regina George (<i>Gretchen, stop trying to make 'fetch' happen! It's not going to happen!</i>). While <i>Mean Girls</i> is technically a comedy, its depiction of girl-on-girl cattiness stings incredibly true."</p>
<p>At the 2013 People's Choice Awards, <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lawrence" title="Jennifer Lawrence">Jennifer Lawrence</a> mentioned the film in her speech when she won "Favorite Movie Actress" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>October 3 has been dubbed on social media as "Mean Girls Day" in reference to a quote from the movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 6th episode of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/How_to_Get_Away_With_Murder" title="How to Get Away With Murder">How to Get Away With Murder</a> season 3 included several references to the film, including Aja Naomi King's character Michaela Pratt using the line "you can't sit with us"; <a href="/wiki/Viola_Davis" title="Viola Davis">Viola Davis</a> character <a href="/wiki/Annalise_Keating" title="Annalise Keating">Annalise Keating</a> eating her lunch in a toilet cubicle after feeling like an outcast; Karla Souza's character Laurel Castillo using sweat pants on a monday and Behzad Dabu's character Simon Drake calling several other students "mean girls".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_game">Video game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Video game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls_DS" title="Mean Girls DS">Mean Girls DS</a></div>
<p>A game for PC and <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> was released in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The video game features characters specifically created for the game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_Moms"><i>Mean Moms</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mean Moms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 2014, <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line</a> announced a planned release date of May 8, 2015 for a proposed spin-off of <i>Mean Girls</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Aniston" title="Jennifer Aniston">Jennifer Aniston</a> in talks to lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adapted from another book penned by <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Wiseman" title="Rosalind Wiseman">Rosalind Wiseman</a>, <i>Mean Moms</i> would be written by <a href="/wiki/Sean_Anders" title="Sean Anders">Sean Anders</a> and John Morris and would star Jennifer Aniston as a mom facing the cut-throat life of modern suburbia. However, in May 2014, <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> pulled the film from its proposed release date of May 2015; even though the film is still slated for development, there is not currently a release date for the spin-off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On October 7, it was announced that the film was added to the California Film Tax Credit program for the 2014–15 fiscal year, in which the production must start in California within 180 days of notification from the state to receive the $6.7 million production tax credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In May 2015, it was confirmed the project was still happening and <a href="/wiki/Sean_Anders" title="Sean Anders">Sean Anders</a> would direct the film;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> in late 2015 Anders told <i>Cinema Blend</i> the project was stalled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_musical">Stage musical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Stage musical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls_(musical)" title="Mean Girls (musical)">Mean Girls (musical)</a></div>
<p>On January 28, 2013, Fey confirmed that a musical adaption of <i>Mean Girls</i> is in the works. <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a> will be the writer and possibly the director of the musical while <i><a href="/wiki/30_Rock" title="30 Rock">30 Rock</a></i> composer and Fey's husband <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Richmond" title="Jeff Richmond">Jeff Richmond</a>, will work on the music. <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount</a> will also be involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It will premiere at Washington DC in late 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_direct_sequel">Potential direct sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Potential direct sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late September 2014, discussions arose that Lohan had pitched an idea to Fey for a sequel. In October 2014, Lohan, along with other cast members of the original film, asked Fey to write a screenplay for a sequel. The idea was brought up during a 10th anniversary for the film in <i>People</i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In December 2016, Lohan stated she was still trying hard to make the sequel, revealing she had written a treatment for the film, with the hopes of <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Lee_Curtis" title="Jamie Lee Curtis">Jamie Lee Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a> appearing in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> She also stated she knew <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Michaels" title="Lorne Michaels">Lorne Michaels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> were busy, stating : "I will keep forcing it and pushing it on them until we do it.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
