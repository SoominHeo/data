<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Isotopes of indium</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b><a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">Indium</a></b> (<b>In</b>) consists of two <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a>, with the most common (~ 95.7%) nuclide (<sup>115</sup>In) being measurably though weakly radioactive. Its spin-forbidden decay has a half life of 441 trillion years.</p>
<p>The stable isotope <sup>113</sup>In is only 4.3% of naturally occurring indium. Among elements with a known stable isotope, only <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a> similarly occur with a stable isotope in lower abundance than the long-lived radioactive isotope. Other than <sup>115</sup>In, the longest-lived radioisotope is <sup>111</sup>In, with a half-life of 2.8047 days. All other radioisotopes have half-lives less than a day. This element also has 47 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">isomers</a>, the longest-lived being <sup>114m1</sup>In, with a half-life of 49.51 days. All other meta-states have half-lives less than a day, most less than an hour, and many measured in milliseconds or less.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indium-111" title="Indium-111">Indium-111</a> is used medically in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_imaging" title="Nuclear imaging">nuclear imaging</a>, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotracer" title="Radiotracer">radiotracer</a> nuclide tag for <a href="/wiki/Gamma_camera" title="Gamma camera">gamma camera</a> localization of protein <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceuticals</a>, such as In-111-labeled <a href="/wiki/Octreotide" title="Octreotide">octreotide</a>, which binds to receptors on certain endocrine tumors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octreoscan" title="Octreoscan">Octreoscan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Indium-111 is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indium_white_blood_cell_scan" title="Indium white blood cell scan">indium white blood cell scans</a>, which use nuclear medical techniques to search for hidden infections.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Relative_atomic_mass" title="Relative atomic mass">Relative atomic mass</a>: 114.818(3).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Geologically exceptional samples are known in which the isotopic composition lies outside the reported range. The uncertainty in the atomic mass may exceed the stated value for such specimens.</li>
<li>Values marked # are not purely derived from experimental data, but at least partly from systematic trends. Spins with weak assignment arguments are enclosed in parentheses.</li>
<li>Uncertainties are given in concise form in parentheses after the corresponding last digits. Uncertainty values denote one standard deviation, except isotopic composition and standard atomic mass from IUPAC, which use expanded uncertainties.</li>
</ul>
