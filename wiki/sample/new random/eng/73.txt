<p><b>Crimean Gothic</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> dialect spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Goths" title="Crimean Goths">Crimean Goths</a> in some isolated locations in <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> until the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestation">Attestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimean_Gothic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Attestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The existence of a Germanic dialect in the Crimea is attested in a number of sources from the 9th century to the 18th century. However, only a single source provides any details of the language itself: a letter by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemings" title="Flemings">Flemish</a> ambassador <a href="/wiki/Ogier_Ghiselin_de_Busbecq" title="Ogier Ghiselin de Busbecq">Ogier Ghiselin de Busbecq</a>, dated 1562 and first published in 1589, gives a list of some eighty words and a song supposedly in the language.</p>
<p>Busbecq's information is problematic in a number of ways: his <a href="/wiki/Informant_(linguistics)" title="Informant (linguistics)">informants</a> were not unimpeachable (one was a Greek speaker who knew Crimean Gothic as a second language, the other a Goth who had abandoned his native language in favour of Greek); there is the possibility that Busbecq's transcription was influenced by his own language (a <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> dialect of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>); there are undoubted misprints in the printed text, which is the only source.</p>

<p>Busbecq mentions a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">definite article</a>, which he records as either <i>tho</i> or <i>the</i> (which may be either a gender difference, or an allophonic pronunciation much as with English "the", which is pronounced either /ðə/ or /ðiː/), and possibly attesting to Crimean Gothic's having retained /θ/ or /ð/ like <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, at least in some positions.</p>
<p>A possible Gothic speaker was freed by Mondorf, a Jesuit in 1750.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley1890_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley1890-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_and_classification">Identification and classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimean_Gothic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identification and classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the initial identification of this language as "Gothic" probably rests on ethnological rather than linguistic grounds — that is, the speakers were identified as Goths therefore the language must be Gothic — it shares a number of distinctive phonological developments with the Gothic of <a href="/wiki/Ulfilas" title="Ulfilas">Ulfilas</a>'s Bible. For example, the word <i>ada</i> "egg" shows the typical Gothic "sharpening" of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>-jj-</i> into <i>-ddj-</i> (as in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Ulfilian Gothic</a> <i>iddja</i> "went" from PGmc. *<i>ijjē</i>), being from Proto-Germanic *<i>ajja-</i>.</p>
<p>There are also examples of features preserved in Crimean Gothic and Biblical Gothic but which have undergone changes in West and North Germanic. For example, both Crimean Gothic and Biblical Gothic preserve Germanic /z/ as a <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">sibilant</a>, while it became /r/ in all other Germanic dialects. Crimean Gothic and Biblical Gothic both preserve the medial -d- in Proto-Germanic *<i>fedwōr</i> (stem *<i>fedur-</i>) "four", attested as <i>fyder</i> in the former and <i>fidwōr</i> in the latter. This -d- is lost in all North and West Germanic languages, which have forms descending from *<i>fewōr</i> or *<i>feur</i>; Old English <i>fēower</i>, Old Saxon <i>fiuwar</i>, Old High German <i>fior</i>, Old Norse <i>fjórir</i>.</p>
<p>However, there are problems in assuming that Crimean Gothic represents simply a later stage in the development of the Gothic attested in Ulfilas' Bible. Some innovations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical" title="Biblical">Biblical</a> Gothic are not found in Crimean Gothic, for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Crimean Gothic preserves Germanic /e/, whereas in Biblical Gothic it has become /i/, e.g. Crimean Gothic <i>reghen</i>, <i>suuester</i>, Biblical Gothic <i>rign</i>, <i>swistar</i></li>
<li>Crimean Gothic preserves Germanic /u/ before /r/ whereas Biblical Gothic has /au/, e.g. Crimean Gothic <i>vvurt</i>, Biblical Gothic <i>waurþi</i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>However, there are also similarities with developments in West Germanic, such as the change of /þ/ to a <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop</a> seen in Crimean Gothic <i>tria</i> (cf. Biblical Gothic <i>þriu</i>). Several historical accounts mention the similarity to <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> and the intelligibility of Crimean Gothic to German speakers, with the Dutch-speaking Busbecq's account being by far the most important.</p>
<p>There are two alternative solutions: that Crimean Gothic presents a separate branch of <a href="/wiki/East_Germanic_languages" title="East Germanic languages">East Germanic</a>, distinct from Ulfilas' Gothic; or that Crimean Gothic is descended from the dialect of <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">West Germanic</a> settlers who migrated to the Crimea in the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and whose language was subsequently influenced by Gothic.</p>
<p>Both of these were first suggested in the 19th century and are most recently argued by Stearns and <a href="/wiki/Ottar_Gr%C3%B8nvik" title="Ottar Grønvik">Grønvik</a>, respectively. While there is no consensus on a definitive solution to this problem, it is accepted that Crimean Gothic is not a descendant of Biblical Gothic.</p>
<p>The song quoted by Busbecq is less obviously Germanic and has proved impossible to interpret definitively. There is no consensus as to whether it is in fact Crimean Gothic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sources_of_Crimean_Gothic">Other sources of Crimean Gothic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimean_Gothic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other sources of Crimean Gothic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until 2015, the only non-Busbecqian additions to this very small corpus are two potentially Crimean Gothic terms from other sources: the first is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proper_name" title="Proper name">proper name</a>, <i>Harfidel</i>, found in a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> inscription on a grave stone dating from the 5th century AD; the second word, <i>razn</i> ("house"), may have lived on as a loan word meaning "roof lath" in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Tatar_language" title="Crimean Tatar language">Crimean Tatar language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, five Crimean Gothic inscriptions were found by Andrey Vinogradov, a Russian historian, on stone plates excavated in <a href="/wiki/Mangup" title="Mangup">Mangup</a> in 1938, and deciphered by him and Maksim Korobov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinogradov_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-vinogradov-6">[6]</a></sup> The inscriptions were written in the second half of the 9th century or in the first half of the 10th century.</p>
<p>One of them is a Biblical Gothic version of <a href="/wiki/Psalm_77" title="Psalm 77">Psalm 77</a>:13. It is not included in the known manuscripts of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Bible" title="Gothic Bible">Gothic Bible</a>, but the wording is just as one would expect. In the inscription, it is followed by a sentence which does not come from the Bible:</p>
<ul>
<li>ƕas g(u)þ mikils</li>
<li>swe g(u)þ unsar? þu</li>
<li>is g(u)þ waurkjands</li>
<li>sildaleika. ainn[s]</li>
<li>usstoþ</li>
<li>und aiwins</li>
<li>us dauþaum</li>
<li>jah in midjun[gard-]</li>
</ul>
<p>"Who is so great a God as our God? Thou art the God that doest wonders. One rose in eternity from the dead and in the world ..."</p>
<p>Another inscription may be reconstructed as a translation of a Byzantine formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinogradov_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-vinogradov-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>f(rauj)a hilp skalkis þei[nis]</li>
<li>damja[na]us us w(e)inag[ardjam ?]</li>
<li>jah frawaur(h)tis</li>
</ul>
<p>"Lord, help your servant Damian from Winag- (the vinyard?) and the sinner"</p>
