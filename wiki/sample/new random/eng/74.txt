<p>A <b>puppy mill</b>, sometimes known as a <b>puppy farm</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a commercial <a href="/wiki/Dog_breeding" title="Dog breeding">dog breeding</a> facility.</p>
<p>According to the Humane Society of the United States, there are an estimated 10,000 licensed and unlicensed puppy mills in the United States, in total selling more than 2,000,000 puppies annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsus_pm_faq_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsus_pm_faq-2">[2]</a></sup> Although no standardized legal definition for "puppy mill" exists, a definition was established in <i>Avenson v. Zegart</i> in 1984 as "a dog breeding operation in which the health of the dogs is disregarded in order to maintain a low overhead and maximize profits."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avenson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Avenson-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a> uses a similar definition: "a large-scale commercial dog breeding operation where profit is given priority over the well-being of the dogs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_laws_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_laws-4">[4]</a></sup> Commercial <a href="/wiki/Kennel" title="Kennel">kennels</a> may be licensed by the United States Department of Agriculture and state and local jurisdictions which may inspect the kennels routinely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "mill" is also applied to operations involving other animals commercially bred for profit, including cats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_glossary_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_glossary-6">[6]</a></sup> For-profit breeding on a smaller scale may be referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backyard_breeding" title="Backyard breeding">backyard breeding</a>, although this term has negative connotations and may also refer to unplanned or non-commercial breeding.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_in_breeding_conditions">Differences in breeding conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Differences in breeding conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kennel Club and the American Kennel Club state that responsible breeders should raise their animals with the intent to produce healthy dogs, and to ensure that all animals are provided responsible homes and <a href="/wiki/Socialization" title="Socialization">socialization</a>, though there is no way to enforce these wishes or records kept to prove this is the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-akc_breed_to_improve_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-akc_breed_to_improve-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wkc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wkc-8">[8]</a></sup> The socialization period in a puppy occurs between the ages of 4 weeks up to 14 weeks of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> This period is crucial for adult development as almost all adult abilities are learnt in this stage. With puppy mills essentially skipping this stage, it can result in problems when the puppy matures to adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In puppy mills, females are sometimes bred every time they are <a href="/wiki/Estrous_cycle" title="Estrous cycle">in heat</a>, resulting in gradually decreasing sizes of litters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_puppy_mills_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_puppy_mills-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boston_animal_control_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-boston_animal_control-11">[11]</a></sup> As puppies, mill dogs are also often <a href="/wiki/Weaning" title="Weaning">weaned</a> from their mothers well before the eight to ten weeks recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_responsible_breeding_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_responsible_breeding-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ASPCA states that some puppy mills can have up to 1,000 bitches under one roof. With high volumes of breeding bitches, the mill runner will often resort to housing the bitches in wired cages resulting in the animals to have poor locomotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-13">[13]</a></sup> The conditions in these mills are so unsanitary that the animals are often coated in their own urine and feces causing for mats in their fur. Due to unsanitary conditions, puppies from puppy mills will often have internal parasites affecting their health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The puppy mill often is not heated and thus resulting in increasing deaths in the breeding bitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition to all this, the mills can also be too hot in the warmer weather leading to hyperthermia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_207.E2.80.93232_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merck_207.E2.80.93232-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other common conditions in mills include malnutrition and untreated injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_207.E2.80.93232_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merck_207.E2.80.93232-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_problems">Common problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Common problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the frequently poor breeding conditions in puppy mills, puppies bred there often suffer from health and/or social problems. Puppies raised in a cramped environment shared by many other dogs become poorly socialized to other dogs and to humans. Dogs are then transported over long distances in poor conditions, sometimes resulting in animal stress and death. As the surviving mill dogs grow older, they are more prone to developing respiratory ailments and <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, as well as hereditary defects such as <a href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia_(canine)" title="Hip dysplasia (canine)">hip dysplasia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_puppy_mills_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_puppy_mills-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, mill dogs are more prone to have problems with their temperament due to lack of socialization, enrichment, and positive human contact. Puppies from mills are usually sold as purebred dogs in an attempt to attract the higher prices associated with purebreds. However, due to the indiscriminate breeding practices of puppy mills, the dog may not actually be a purebred puppy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennel_club_uk_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennel_club_uk-16">[16]</a></sup> A high population of puppies from mills are inbred due to uncontrolled breeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The vast majority of puppy mill animals are sold to pet stores by "dealers" or "brokers". Some puppies are sold by dealers masquerading as authentic breeders.</p>
<p>Puppy mills in the US often start with hundreds of female dogs which serve their entire lives in the establishment. The females are bred until they can no longer conceive puppies, and are often euthanized after that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The conditions in puppy mills are considered inhumane because all of the dogs are in a small, dirty area which is confined with disease and bacteria. Because of the poor living conditions, dogs are often sick and malnourished. Food is often found crawling with bugs and feces is almost everywhere. Health issues that are prevalent in puppy mills consist of giardia, mange, heartworm, respiratory infections, and much more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Chanis Major V. publications, puppy mills originated in the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> era. Midwestern farmers looking for an alternative crop reacted to a growing demand for puppies, resulting in the development of the first commercial puppy business. As the business grew, both small and large retail outlets began to sell puppies through pet departments. At around the same time, the first pet store chains were born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canis_major_dilemma_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-canis_major_dilemma-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through indifference or ignorance, many puppies were left unsocialized or exposed in existing chicken coops or rabbit hutches. In addition, veterinary care for these puppies was often overlooked because of an inability to pay. As a result, organizations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humane_Society_of_the_United_States" title="Humane Society of the United States">Humane Society of the United States</a> began to investigate breeding kennels, leading to the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Animal_Welfare_Act_of_1966" title="Animal Welfare Act of 1966">Animal Welfare Act</a> of August 24, 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canis_major_dilemma_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-canis_major_dilemma-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">Prevalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prevalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the Humane Society of the United States, there are an estimated 10,000 licensed and unlicensed puppy mills in the United States, in total selling more than 2,000,000 puppies annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsus_pm_faq_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsus_pm_faq-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A high concentration of both puppy mills and breeders has been reported in the states of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pennsylvania, and in particular, <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Lancaster County, Pennsylvania">Lancaster County</a>, has been labeled the "Puppy Mill Capital of the East" due to its high volume of puppy mills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs_pm_pa_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_pm_pa-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_ap_pm_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-fox_ap_pm-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readingeagle_pm1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-readingeagle_pm1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Missouri has been labeled the "Puppy Mill Capital of the U.S." by animal welfare and consumer protection groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspca_laws_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-aspca_laws-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsus_ny_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsus_ny-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbb_study_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbb_study-27">[27]</a></sup> A study by the <a href="/wiki/Better_Business_Bureau" title="Better Business Bureau">Better Business Bureau</a> concluded that the southwest corner of Missouri is the hub of the nation's puppy mill industry, and termed it the "national hot spot of the puppy industry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbb_study_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbb_study-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hobby_breeders">Hobby breeders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hobby breeders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The term "puppy mill" has been widely used by <a href="/wiki/List_of_animal_rights_groups" title="List of animal rights groups">animal rights groups</a> in protests against breeders who have substandard breeding conditions. Critics in the breeder community claim that emotional rhetoric, sensationalism, and pictures of dirty kennels are used to justify additional legislation or additional restrictive licensing that travels well beyond the initial goal of removing dogs from truly deplorable conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canis_major_dilemma_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-canis_major_dilemma-20">[20]</a></sup> or that attempts to legislate puppy mills would put them out of business. They argue the laws requiring additional costs in updating and maintaining their facility and licensing would be detrimental to the dogs in their care. They cite existing <a href="/wiki/Lemon_law" title="Lemon law">lemon laws</a> for puppies as sufficient protection for both dogs and prospective buyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-time-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_coverage">Media coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Media coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 29, 2008, <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a> aired a report about puppy mills, in which talk show host <a href="/wiki/Oprah_Winfrey" title="Oprah Winfrey">Oprah Winfrey</a> revealed an industry fraught with problems and apparent cruelty. The broadcast showed puppy mills with small cages, with <a href="/wiki/Chicken_wire" title="Chicken wire">chicken wire</a> floors, stacked in rows from floor to ceiling, and stated that many dogs spend their entire lives within these tiny cages. Many of these dogs are sold on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Pet_store" title="Pet store">pet retailers</a> to buyers who are unaware of the dogs' background. The report claimed that customers who object to this treatment of puppies unknowingly allow the industry to thrive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It also pointed out that many of the puppy mill bred dogs suffer long-term health problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislative_response">Legislative response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legislative response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, some elements of the dog breeding industry are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Animal_Welfare_Act_of_1966" title="Animal Welfare Act of 1966">Animal Welfare Act</a> of August 24, 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awa_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-awa-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years, state legislatures have passed new laws aimed at eliminating the worst abuses at puppy mills. New laws include limits on the number of breeding females, requirements that facilities be licensed and inspected, and requirements that dogs be given proper veterinary care. Louisiana, Pennsylvania and Virginia passed puppy mill laws in 2008, and 10 states passed laws in 2009 to crack down on abusive puppy mills. In 2010, Missouri voters passed Proposition B, the "Puppy Mill Cruelty Prevention Act", which establishes minimum standards of humane care and limits breeders to 50 intact dogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_laws_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-new_laws-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, voters experienced push-back from the industry in the state of Missouri. Dog breeders and other farmers, worried about costly animal welfare measures spreading to their farms, pressured the state to repeal the law. In April 2011 Proposition B was repealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puppy_mill_politics_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-puppy_mill_politics-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A compromise, dubbed the <i>Missouri Solution</i>, was signed by Missouri Governor Jay Nixon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missouri_solution_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-missouri_solution-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>It retained some of the provisions of Proposition B, and made available some state funding for inspections. Humane Society of Missouri President Kathy Warnick reacted favorably, seeing a step in the right direction for animal welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missouri_solution_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-missouri_solution-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Detractors, which include the President and CEO of the Humane Society of the United States, point to the "shameful" overturn of the voter's will to weaken Proposition B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shameful_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-shameful-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>“Lawmakers should never have substituted their judgment for the people of Missouri and gutted core provisions of Prop B,” said Wayne Pacelle, president and CEO of the Humane Society of the United States. “The so-called compromise was not about protecting animals, it was about placating agri-business.”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missouri_solution_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-missouri_solution-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Below is a set of lists describing some of the key differences between Proposition B and the so-called <i>Missouri Solution</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puppy_mill_politics_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-puppy_mill_politics-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Proposition B</b> (approved by Missouri voters in November, 2010) <i>Wire flooring for cages eliminated by November 2011;</i> <i>Maximum allowable breeding females per business = 50;</i> <i>Cage height = taller than any dog standing erect;</i> <i>Maximum number of times a female may be bred within 18 months time = 2;</i> <i>Larger enclosures by November 2011.</i></p>
<p><b>the “Missouri Solution”</b> (compromise law signed by Missouri Gov. Jay Nixon, April 2011, after repealing Proposition B) <i>No wire flooring for new enclosures in 2016, existing wire flooring permitted to remain indefinitely;</i> <i>No limit on number of breeding females;</i> <i>No restriction on cage height;</i> <i>No restrictions on how often females are impregnated;</i> <i>Larger enclosures in 2016.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sydney Lord Mayor Clover Moore has responded to the problem of puppy mills in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> by proposing the Animals Regulation of Sale Bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It would ban the sale of dogs through pet shops, the internet or newspapers. The aim is to crack down on <a href="/wiki/Impulse_purchase" title="Impulse purchase">impulse purchases</a> and shut down unregistered backyard breeders. These breeders should no longer easily profit from the sale of the dogs and the number of unwanted and abandoned animals could drop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also recently there were a few bold initiatives to fight against puppy mills. Namely RSPCA (Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals) strategy, Oscar's law (The organization’s name originates from the story of a dog called Oscar, who was rescued from a puppy factory in central Victoria), and Victorian Labor Party Efforts that restrict the number of dogs per breeding facility and require that pet shop owners to keep records of every dog sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, Britain passed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Acts_of_the_Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom,_1980%E2%80%931999" title="List of Acts of the Parliament of the United Kingdom, 1980–1999">the Breeding and Sale of Dogs Act</a> which requires annual veterinary inspections for anyone breeding five or more litters in one year. Breeding females are restricted to one litter per year and four per lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Breeders who choose to be members of the UK <a href="/wiki/Kennel_club" title="Kennel club">Kennel Club</a> are required to register purebred puppies for sale with that organization and must certify the conditions under which the puppies were raised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Breeders who sell puppies by misrepresenting these standards may be liable to prosecution under the Sale of Goods Act 1979.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_cases">Legal cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legal cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Animal_Legal_Defense_Fund" title="Animal Legal Defense Fund">Animal Legal Defense Fund</a> and three Pennsylvania residents filed a lawsuit against the Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture, alleging illegal expenditure of state tax dollars on unlawful regulatory activity. According to ALDF, the Pennsylvania General Assembly amended the Dog Law in 2008 to strengthen standards of care for dogs housed in commercial breeding facilities known as “puppy mills.” Just two years later, the Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture weakened those minimum standards by creating regulatory exemptions for nursing mothers and their puppies. The lawsuit asks the court to order the Department of Agriculture to follow the law and end the suffering of mother dogs and their puppies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Puppy_mill_raids">Puppy mill raids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Puppy mill raids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clewiston.2C_Florida">Clewiston, Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Clewiston, Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the American Society for the Prevention of Cruelty towards Animals (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a>), on November 17, 2015 over 100 dogs were rescued from a puppy mill raid in Clewiston, Florida. Found in overcrowded and untidy pens with limited access to food or water, 116 dogs, including Huskies, Chihuahuas, and Poodles, were found having several ailments and untreated medical conditions that called for immediate veterinary care. According to Tim Rickey, the Vice President of ASPCA's Field Investigations and Response team, "[ASPCA's] goal is to remove these dogs from a life of neglect, help them become healthy and eventually find them safe and loving homes." Reports state that the two puppy mill owners, Beatriz Perez, 46, and Alexei Fernandez, 47, have been arrested on charges of animal cruelty while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a> transported the 116 dogs to a safe and secure location. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a> is currently asking for donations to fund the medical treatments that many of the dogs need as well as to help these dogs find a forever home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gibson_County.2C_Tennessee">Gibson County, Tennessee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gibson County, Tennessee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 5, 2014, the Animal Rescue Corps rescued about 100 dogs from what was said to be severely neglectful conditions. Both adult and newborn dogs were found in extremely crowded cages exposed to ammonia with limited access to any water or food. According to the Animal Rescue Corps's spokesman, Michael Cunningham, "the dogs were suffering from untreated, painful eye infections, respiratory conditions, dental issues, severe matting that limited their mobility and vision, and urine-soaked, feces-caked fur." Since the owners could not be reached, no criminal charges have been filed. However, the dogs were taken for thorough veterinary screenings and care followed by their transportation to in-state and out-of-state dog adoption centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White_Hall.2C_Arkansas">White Hall, Arkansas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppy_mill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: White Hall, Arkansas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 28, 2014, 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humane_Society_of_the_United_States" title="Humane Society of the United States">Humane Society of the United States</a> workers rescued 183 animals from a puppy mill, including 121 dogs, 20 horses, 19 chickens, 11 exotic birds, and several cats, rabbits, and turtles. 30 miles south of <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a>, White Hall puppy mill was found with severe stenches of ammonia from the uncleaned urine and feces from the numerous neglected animals. Many animals were found without any access to food or water. Humane Society workers stated that many of the animals required emergency veterinary care while still others suffered from severe eye conditions, dental problems, and severe dietary deficiencies. One humane worker said, "I held dogs that were trembling and shaking and with heavy mats." The puppy mill owners, James and Tara Best, were both charged with animal cruelty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
