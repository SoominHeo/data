<p>The international name for the body of water which is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> is disputed. In 1992, objections to the name <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a> were first raised by North Korea and South Korea at the Sixth United Nations Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFAJ_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFAJ-1">[1]</a></sup> The Japanese government supports the use of the name "Sea of Japan", while South Korea supports the name "East Sea", and North Korea supports the name "East Sea of Korea". Currently, most international maps and documents use either the name Sea of Japan (or equivalent translation) by itself, or include both the name Sea of Japan and East Sea, often with East Sea listed in parentheses or otherwise marked as a secondary name. The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a>, the international governing body for the naming of bodies of water around the world, in 2012 decided not to change the current single name "Sea of Japan" rejecting South Korea's request to use "East Sea" together with "Sea of Japan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sea_of_Japan_name_dispute_rolls_on_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sea_of_Japan_name_dispute_rolls_on-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The involved countries (especially Japan and South Korea) have advanced a variety of arguments to support their preferred name(s). Many of the arguments revolve around determining when the name Sea of Japan became the common name. South Korea argues that historically the more common name was East Sea, Sea of Korea, or another similar variant. South Korea further argues that the name Sea of Japan did not become common until Korea was under <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese rule</a>, at which time it had no ability to influence international affairs. Japan argues that the name Sea of Japan has been the most common international name since at least the beginning of the 19th century, long before its annexation of Korea. Both sides have conducted studies of antiquarian maps, but the two countries have produced divergent research results. Additional arguments have been raised regarding the underlying geography of the sea as well as potential problems regarding the ambiguity of one name or the other.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments">Arguments</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2463" data-file-width="1925" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg/220px-Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg/330px-Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg/440px-Matteo_Ricci_Far_East_1602_Larger.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Both sides in the dispute have put forward a number of arguments to support their claims.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_based_on_historical_maps">Arguments based on historical maps</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_from_South_Korea">Arguments from South Korea</span></h4>
<p>According to the Ministry of Foreign Affairs for South Korea, the name Donghae (동해, literally East Sea) has been used in Korea for over 2,000 years, including in <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">History of the Three Kingdoms</a></i> (三國史記, 1145), the monument of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Gwanggaeto" title="King Gwanggaeto">King Gwanggaeto</a>, and "Map of Eight Provinces of Korea" (八道總圖, 1530).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-km1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-km1-6">[6]</a></sup> The first documented map to name the area the Sea of Japan was the world map drawn by the Italian missionary <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Ricci" title="Matteo Ricci">Matteo Ricci</a> in China (1602) named <i><a href="/wiki/Kunyu_Wanguo_Quantu" title="Kunyu Wanguo Quantu">Kunyu Wanguo Quantu</a></i> (坤輿萬國全圖). No Japanese record published up to the late-18th century indicated any name for the body of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEHF_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEHF-7">[7]</a></sup> Furthermore, South Korea has pointed out that a few 19th-century Japanese maps referred to the sea as Chōsenkai (朝鮮海, literally Sea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon</a>), including the "Simplified Map of Japan's Periphery" (日本邊界略圖, 1809) and "New World Map" (新製輿地全圖, 1844).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-km1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-km1-6">[6]</a></sup> South Korea argues there was no standard name prior to Japan's military expansion in the region in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Additionally, it specifically states that the name Sea of Japan was not widely used, even in Japan, as late as the mid 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legitimacy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-legitimacy-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2011)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Sea_of_Japan_naming_dispute#Dubious" title="Talk:Sea of Japan naming dispute">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> Thus, South Korea argues that the current name reflects active promotion by Japan during a time when Korea could not represent its interests internationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorkingPaper48_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorkingPaper48-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_from_Japan">Arguments from Japan</span></h4>
<p>The Japanese government claims that the name Sea of Japan was internationally used since the 17th century and established by the early 19th century, during a period in which Japan was under an isolationist policy (<a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">Sakoku</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>, which restricted cultural exchange and commerce with foreign countries except China and the Netherlands until 1854.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFARussianFederationstudy_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFARussianFederationstudy-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> Accordingly, they state, Japan could not have, at that time, had an influence on the international community regarding the naming of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">marine chronometer</a> in the late 18th century enabled Western explorers, such as <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Galaup,_comte_de_Lap%C3%A9rouse" title="Jean-François de Galaup, comte de Lapérouse">Jean-François de Galaup</a> from France, <a href="/wiki/William_Robert_Broughton" title="William Robert Broughton">William Robert Broughton</a> from Britain, and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Johann_von_Krusenstern" title="Adam Johann von Krusenstern">Adam Johann von Krusenstern</a> (Ivan Fyodorovich Kruzenshtern) from Russia, to measure time and longitudes on the sea precisely and map the detailed shape of the Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> Krusenstern was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Admiral" title="Russian Admiral">admiral</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Russian_explorers" title="List of Russian explorers">explorer</a>, who led the <a href="/wiki/First_Russian_circumnavigation" title="First Russian circumnavigation">first Russian circumnavigation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">globe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> According to Japanese records, it was Krusenstern who popularized the name "Mer du Japon" (Sea of Japan) in the West. In his work "Reise um die Welt in den Jahren" (1812), he wrote, "People also call this sea area the Sea of Korea, but because only a small part of this sea touches the Korean coast, it is better to name it the Sea of Japan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> The original book was published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a> in German and Russian, translated into Dutch, French, Swedish, Italian and English, and distributed widely among Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, the international name of the sea changed from no name to the Sea of Japan, on the maps drawn by countries other than Japan or Korea during the 17th to 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> Thus, the Japanese side argues that the South Koreans misunderstand the history of the name.</p>

<p>To provide evidence for the date when Sea of Japan came to be used internationally, both South Korea and Japan have undertaken surveys of various historical maps.</p>
<p>In 2004, South Korea surveyed ancient maps archived in the British Library, the Cambridge University Library, the University of Southern California (USC) East Asian Map Collection, the U.S. Library of Congress, the National Library of Russia, and the French National Library examined 762 maps. They found that 440 maps had used Sea of Korea (Corea), Oriental Sea/East Sea, 122 had used Sea of Japan, and 200 had used other terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAHF_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAHF-14">[14]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a>, the word <i>orientale</i> includes both the meaning of "eastern" related to compass direction and the meaning of "oriental", the Asiatic region. The same ambiguity is present in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian language</a>, with both "eastern" and "oriental" indicated by one word.</p>
<p>From 2003 to 2008, Japan conducted a number of surveys of different collections. In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Japan)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a> (MOFA) published their conclusions; they found that among 1,332 maps from the Berlin Library, 279 used Sea of Korea, Oriental Sea, or East Sea (or some combination thereof), 579 used Sea of Japan exclusively, 47 used China Sea (with or without other names), 33 used another term, and 384 used no term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFAstudy7f_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFAstudy7f-15">[15]</a></sup> MOFA said the Struck collection (a collection of antiquarian maps owned by a European map collector) showed that out of 79 maps, 35 used Sea of Japan, 9 used the Sea of Korea, 2 used Oriental Sea, and 33 were unmarked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFAstudy7f_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-MOFAstudy7f-15">[15]</a></sup> MOFA reported that among four Russian libraries and document archives holding 51 maps, 29 used Sea of Japan, 8 used Sea of Korea, 1 used Korea Strait, 1 used East Sea, 1 used Sea of China, and 11 used no name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFAstudy6_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFAstudy6-16">[16]</a></sup> MOFA said that 1,213 maps from the U.S. Library of Congress, ones that gave a name for this body of water, showed that 87 percent used Sea of Japan, 8 percent used Sea of Korea, 5 percent used other terms, and none used Oriental Sea or East Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFApamphlet2003_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFApamphlet2003-17">[17]</a></sup> Similarly, MOFA said that 58 maps from the British Library and the University of Cambridge showed 86 percent used Sea of Japan, 14 percent used Sea of Korea, and none used Oriental Sea, East Sea, or other terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFApamphlet2003_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-MOFApamphlet2003-17">[17]</a></sup> MOFA said that they looked at 1,485 maps in the French National Library. They reported that 95 percent of 215 French maps used Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFApamphlet2003_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-MOFApamphlet2003-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2007, the National Geographic Information Institute of South Korea published a report on a survey of 400 ancient maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to the report, nine maps used East Sea for the water currently called Sea of Japan, while 31 maps used East Sea for the water currently called East China Sea. The number of maps used Sea of Japan is not disclosed. Furthermore, the report says "<i>In the late 18th century (1790–1830) the name Sea of Japan emerged. From the 19th century (1830 onward), there was a rapid increase in the use of the name Sea of Japan.</i>" Japan stated, "This clearly shows the fallacy of the ROK's assertion that the name Sea of Japan was the result of the Japanese policy of expansionism and colonial rule, and can be interpreted as affirming that the name Sea of Japan was in widespread use well before Japan's colonial rule over the Korean Peninsula".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFA.236_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOFA.236-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Japan argues that, the name Sea of Japan has been and should be used because the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> is separated from the Pacific Ocean by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Archipelago" title="Japanese Archipelago">Japanese Archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mofapamph0208_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-mofapamph0208-20">[20]</a></sup> Korea argues that the adjective "East" describes its geographical position east of the Asian continent, although it is west of Japan and south of Russia. It states that this is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, which lies north of the European continent, but west of Scandinavian countries and east of Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorkingPaper48_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-WorkingPaper48-9">[9]</a></sup> However Koreans call the sea on their east side the East Sea (동해, Donghae),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> on their south side the <a href="/wiki/South_Sea_(Korea)" title="South Sea (Korea)">South Sea</a> (남해, Namhae),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and on their west side the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">West Sea</a> (서해, Seohae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_relating_to_ambiguity">Arguments relating to ambiguity</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="728" data-file-width="1104" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png/220px-East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png/330px-East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png/440px-East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:East_Seas_in_East_Asia.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The name "East Sea" is used to refer to several different seas in Eastern Eurasia.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Japanese Hydrographic and Oceanographic Department of the Japanese Coast Guard has claimed that the name East Sea is confusing and unsuitable as an international geographic name, because the local name for a variety of seas can be translated into English as East Sea. Examples include Dōng Hǎi (东海), the Chinese name for the <a href="/wiki/East_China_Sea" title="East China Sea">East China Sea</a>; Biển Đông, the Vietnamese name for the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, which means East Sea in several European languages such as German (<i>Ostsee</i>), Swedish (<i>Östersjön</i>) and Finnish (<i>Itämeri</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> East Sea is officially used as an English name for the water by the Government of Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup> Thus the Government of Vietnam uses East Sea for South China Sea in its English publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Also the Ministry of Foreign Affairs of China uses East Sea for East China Sea in its English publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In addition, East Sea <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja"><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/%E6%9D%B1%E6%B5%B7_(disambiguation)" title="東海 (disambiguation)">東海</a></span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkai_(disambiguation)" title="Tōkai (disambiguation)">Tōkai</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is not the Sea of Japan but the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in the Japanese context. The regions on the east coast of Japan were named accordingly as the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkaid%C5%8D_(region)" title="Tōkaidō (region)">Tōkaidō region</a> and the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkai_region" title="Tōkai region">Tōkai region</a>. The Japanese government is concerned that the name change could set a bad precedent and cause more naming disputes worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basic_Position_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Position-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Position_of_international_bodies">Position of international bodies</span></h2>
<p>The main two international organizations which have been involved in the naming dispute are the <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Hydrographic_Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</span></h3>
<p>The International Hydrographic Organization is an organization that coordinates with member countries over <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrographic</a> issues. One of the organization's functions is to help set international standards on the naming and delineation of nautical regions. In 1929, the organization (then called the International Hydrographic Bureau) published "IHO Special Publication 23" (IHO SP 23), which established the name of the sea as Sea of Japan; however, at that time, Korea could not participate in the IHO because it was under Japanese rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KHOA_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-KHOA-31">[31]</a></sup> South Korea officially joined the IHO in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCGchronology_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-JCGchronology-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, IHO released Technical Resolution A.4.2.6. This resolution stated that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is recommended that where two or more countries share a given geographical feature (such as a bay, a strait, channel or archipelago) under different names, they should endeavor to reach agreement on a single name for the feature concerned. If they have different official languages and cannot agree on a common name form, it is recommended that the name forms of each of the languages in question should be accepted for charts and publications unless technical reasons prevent this practice on small scale charts.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>South Korea has argued that this resolution is relevant to the debate about the Sea of Japan and implies that both names should be used; Japan, however, argues that the resolution does not apply to the Sea of Japan, because it does not specify this body of water and only applies to geographical features for which sovereignty is shared between two or more countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFApamphlet2003_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-MOFApamphlet2003-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IHO agreed to conduct a survey of available evidence in 2011. Previously, South Korea had been pushing the IHO to recommend only using the term East Sea, but announced on 2 May 2011, that it now preferred the gradual approach of using both names now, and eventually dropping the Sea of Japan name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 26 April 2012, the IHO announced that it had decided to use only "Sea of Japan" in its governing publication <i>Limits of Oceans and Seas</i> and rejected the alternative use of "East Sea" as proposed by South Korea. According to the South Korean government, the organization will again discuss the issue of the name when it reconvenes in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sea_of_Japan_name_dispute_rolls_on_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sea_of_Japan_name_dispute_rolls_on-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span></h3>
<p>While the United Nations has never directly addressed the issue of establishing an official, standardized name for the sea, several resolutions and statements by the UN have had relevance to the topic. Japan joined the United Nations in 1956, while South and North Korea both joined in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_members_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_members-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977, the third U.N. Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names (UNCSGN) adopted Resolution III/20, entitled "Names of Features beyond a Single Sovereignty". The resolution recommended that "when countries sharing a given geographical feature do not agree on a common name, it should be a general rule of cartography that the name used by each of the countries concerned will be accepted. A policy of accepting only one or some of such names while excluding the rest would be inconsistent as well as inexpedient in practice." As with IHO Technical Resolution A.4.2.6, South Korea and Japan disagree about whether or not this policy applies to the Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFApamphlet2003_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-MOFApamphlet2003-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, during the 1992 Sixth UNCSGN, the South Korean government, in their first time participating in UNCSGN, requested that the name of the sea be determined through consultation, which the North Korean representative concurred with. The Japanese representative stated that the name of the Sea of Japan had already been accepted worldwide and that any change would introduce confusion. The conference recommended that the parties work together on the issue outside of the conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, South Korea raised the issue again at the Seventh UNCSGN. Japan, however, opposed the method by which the South Korean government proposed the issue, arguing that they had not followed the proper procedure for doing so. Following some debate, South Korea withdrew the issue, and instead recommended that the United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names work so that a resolution could be submitted to the Eighth UNSCGN conference. The president of the conference urged that Japan, South Korea, and North Korea work towards a mutually acceptable agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNSCGN7_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNSCGN7-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the Eighth UNCSGN in 2002, South Korea and Japan presented a number of papers to the conference regarding their positions on the naming issue. South Korea asked for a resolution to adjudicate the name, while Japan asked that the name be decided through resolution outside of the conference. No resolution was passed, and the Committee again urged the countries to develop a mutually agreeable solution. The chairman further noted that standardization could only occur after consensus had been reached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNCSGN8_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNCSGN8-37">[37]</a></sup> The same situation occurred at the Ninth conference in 2007. South Korea and North Korea both proposed a resolution by the UNCSGN, while Japan expressed a desire to settle the matter outside of the conference, and the Committee urged the members to seek a mutual agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 23 April 2004, the United Nations affirmed in a written document to the Japanese government that it will continue using the name Sea of Japan in its official documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> However, it agreed to leave the topic open for further discussion. In a letter to South Korea, it was explained that the UN was not determining the validity of either name, but wished to use the term most widely used until the parties resolved the disagreement. The letter further stated, "The use of an appellation by the Secretariat based on the practice is without prejudice to any negotiations or agreements between the interested parties and should not be interpreted as advocating or endorsing any party's position, and can in no way be invoked by any party in support of a particular position in the matter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 August 2012, representatives from North and South Korea addressed an assembly at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Standardization_of_Geographical_Names" title="United Nations Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names">United Nations Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names</a>, asking that the names "East Sea" and "Sea of Japan" be used concurrently for the sea. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ferjan_Ormeling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ferjan Ormeling (page does not exist)">Ferjan Ormeling</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferjan_Ormeling" title="nl:Ferjan Ormeling">nl</a>)</span>, chairman of the conference, responded that the organization had no authority to decide the issue and requested that the involved countries resolve the differences over the name amongst themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span></h2>
<p>Russia calls this sea "Япо́нское мо́ре" (Yapónskoye móre, Japanese Sea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FEB_RAS_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FEB_RAS-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Univ.Press_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Univ.Press-43">[43]</a></sup> Japan believes that Russia played a major role in establishing this name internationally, as mentioned above. Chinese government websites exclusively use the name <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%97%A5%E6%9C%AC%E6%B5%B7" title="zh:日本海">日本海</a> (rìběnhǎi, Japan Sea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2003, the French Defense Ministry issued nautical maps that included both terms Sea of Japan and East Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mofqafeb2003_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-mofqafeb2003-45">[45]</a></sup> It reverted to Sea of Japan as a single name in the map issued in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The United Kingdom and Germany officially use the Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOFAJ_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MOFAJ-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Board_on_Geographic_Names" title="United States Board on Geographic Names">United States Board on Geographic Names</a> (BGN) continues to advocate the use of Sea of Japan without qualification in <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S. government</a> publications. <a href="/wiki/The_World_Factbook" title="The World Factbook">The World Factbook</a> published by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> follows the BGN's guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On 8 August 2011, a spokesman for the United States Department of State stated that the United States Board on Geographic Names considered the official name of the sea to be "Sea of Japan." According to <a href="/wiki/Yonhap" title="Yonhap">Yonhap</a>, the U.S. has officially recommended to the IHO that "Sea of Japan" remain as the official name for the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In response to this failure of the South Korean campaign, South Korean foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Kim_Sung-hwan_(politician)" title="Kim Sung-hwan (politician)">Kim Sung-hwan</a> suggested advocating other historical names, such as "Sea of Korea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> state lawmaker <a href="/wiki/David_W._Marsden" title="David W. Marsden">David W. Marsden</a>, acting on behalf of Korean-American voters, introduced a bill to the education panel of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Virginia" title="Senate of Virginia">Senate of Virginia</a> that would have required public school textbooks to include both "Sea of Japan" and "East Sea" as names. The panel rejected the bill by an 8–7 vote on 26 January 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The issue was revisited two years later on 3 February 2014, with the Education Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia House of Delegates</a> passing legislation to use both "Sea of Japan" and "East Sea" in school textbooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 June 2012, Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs <a href="/wiki/Kurt_M._Campbell" title="Kurt M. Campbell">Kurt M. Campbell</a> affirmed the BGN's position in his response, published on the White House website, to a <a href="/wiki/We_the_People_(petitioning_system)" title="We the People (petitioning system)">We the People petition</a> concerning the usage of "Sea of Japan", in which he stated, "It is longstanding United States policy to refer to each sea or ocean by a single name. This policy applies to all seas, including those bordered by multiple countries that may each have their own names for such bodies of water. Concerning the body of water between the Japanese archipelago and the Korean peninsula, longstanding U.S. policy is to refer to it as the "Sea of Japan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> He also stated, "We are aware the Republic of Korea refers to the body of water as the 'East Sea,' and the United States is not asking the Republic of Korea to change its nomenclature. U.S. usage of the 'Sea of Japan' in no way implies an opinion regarding any issue related to sovereignty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compromise_names">Compromise names</span></h2>
<p>On 18 November 2006, during the APEC summit in Hanoi, South Korean President <a href="/wiki/Roh_Moo-hyun" title="Roh Moo-hyun">Roh Moo-hyun</a> informally proposed to the Japanese Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> that the sea be called instead the "Sea of Peace" or "Sea of Friendship", which Abe rejected. In January 2007 Japanese Chief Cabinet Secretary <a href="/wiki/Yasuhisa_Shiozaki" title="Yasuhisa Shiozaki">Yasuhisa Shiozaki</a> opposed the idea, saying that there was no need to change the name of the Sea of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>A number of maps, encyclopedias, and other publications have switched to using both names. For example, the Manual of Style of the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> states that disputed place-names in international waters or jointly controlled by two or more countries should use the conventional name first with other names following in parentheses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGSPlaceNamesentry_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGSPlaceNamesentry-56">[56]</a></sup> As such, their policy on this sea states that "The internationally accepted name is Sea of Japan, although Korea prefers East Sea. When scale permits, Geographic maps show the alternative name East Sea in parentheses after Sea of Japan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGSSeaofJapanentry_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGSSeaofJapanentry-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Google put both names on <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a>, using East Sea near the Korean coast and Sea of Japan near the Japanese coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoreaTimes2006_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-KoreaTimes2006-58">[58]</a></sup> In the 2007 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>, the primary article is called "Sea of Japan". A secondary article called "East Sea" notes "see Japan, Sea of". On the encyclopedia's map of Japan and other Asia maps, Sea of Japan appears as the primary label and East Sea appears as a secondary label in parentheses. However, on the map of Korea the name East Sea appears as the primary label and Sea of Japan appears as a secondary label in parentheses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Other examples of publishers who use similar systems include Microsoft Encarta,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNEncarta_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNEncarta-60">[60]</a></sup> the Columbia Electronic Encyclopedia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colum6thed_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Colum6thed-61">[61]</a></sup> and About.com.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, French encyclopedia publisher <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ditions_Larousse" title="Éditions Larousse">Larousse</a> replaced "Mer du Japon" ("Sea of Japan") with "Mer de L'est (Mer du Japon)" ("East Sea (Sea of Japan)") in maps of South Korea and North Korea in two of its books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larousse_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larousse-64">[64]</a></sup>　However other maps such as those of Asia, China, Japan, and Russia continue to use "Mer du Japon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larousse_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Larousse-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Furthermore, the article "Mer du Japon" does not mention "East Sea" at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larousse_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-Larousse-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
