<p><b>Tuskegee</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Macon_County,_Alabama" title="Macon County, Alabama">Macon County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It was founded and laid out in 1833 by General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Simpson_Woodward&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Simpson Woodward (page does not exist)">Thomas Simpson Woodward</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Creek_War" title="Creek War">Creek War</a> veteran under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, and made the county seat that year. It was incorporated in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also the largest city in Macon County. At the 2010 census the population was 9,865, down from 11,846 in 2000.</p>
<p>Tuskegee has been an important site in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> history and highly influential in United States history since the 19th century. Before the American Civil War, the area was largely used as a cotton plantation, dependent on African-American slave labor. After the war, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedmen" title="Freedmen">freedmen</a> continued to work on plantations in the rural area, which was devoted to agriculture. In 1881 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Institute" title="Tuskegee Institute">Tuskegee Normal School</a> (now Tuskegee University, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historically_black_college" title="Historically black college">historically black college</a>) was founded and its director, <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington" title="Booker T. Washington">Booker T. Washington</a> developed a national reputation and philanthropic network to support education of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedmen" title="Freedmen">freedmen</a> and their children.</p>
<p>In 1923, the <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Veterans_Administration_Medical_Center" title="Tuskegee Veterans Administration Medical Center">Tuskegee Veterans Administration Medical Center</a> was established here, initially for the estimated 300,000 African-American veterans of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in the South, when public facilities were racially segregated. Twenty-seven buildings were constructed on the 464-acre campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vamc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vamc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was the subject of a notable civil rights case, <i><a href="/wiki/Gomillion_v._Lightfoot" title="Gomillion v. Lightfoot">Gomillion v. Lightfoot</a></i> (1960), in which the United States Supreme Court ruled that the state legislature had violated the <a href="/wiki/Fifteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fifteenth Amendment</a> in 1957 by <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">gerrymandering</a> city boundaries as a 28-sided figure that excluded nearly all black voters and residents, and none of the white voters or residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-alenc-4">[4]</a></sup> The city's boundaries were restored in 1961 after the ruling.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area was settled by European Americans in the 1830s after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creek_people" title="Creek people">Creek</a> Native American tribes had been removed to Indian Territory west of the Mississippi River. Pioneer planters brought or purchased African-American slaves to develop the rich soil for cotton plantations, as short-staple cotton was the chief commodity crop through the 19th century. Invention of the <a href="/wiki/Cotton_gin" title="Cotton gin">cotton gin</a> at the end of the 18th century meant that this type of cotton could be processed profitably and it was adaptable to the upland areas. Designated as the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of rural Macon County, Tuskegee developed as its only city.</p>
<p>In 1881, the young <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington" title="Booker T. Washington">Booker T. Washington</a> was hired to develop the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Institute" title="Tuskegee Institute">Tuskegee Normal School for Colored Teachers</a> on the grounds of a former plantation. It was founded to train teachers for the segregated school system and <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedmen</a> for self-sufficiency. Washington established a work-study program by which students practiced skills and trades. Over the decades, the programs were expanded. This was later named the Tuskegee Institute. Graduate courses were added and it became <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_University" title="Tuskegee University">Tuskegee University</a>. Washington was known for his emphasis on education and self-improvement. The institute became known for stressing a practical education with work experience by students, to prepare them for the work available in the small towns and rural areas to which most would return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Teaching was a highly respected calling, as education was a major goal among the freedmen and their children. Washington believed that African Americans would achieve acceptance by southern whites when they had raised themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taper_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-taper-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Washington led the school for decades, building a wide national network of white industrialist donors among some of the major philanthropists of the era, including <a href="/wiki/George_Eastman" title="George Eastman">George Eastman</a>. At the same time, Washington secretly provided funding to the NAACP for its legal defense of some highly visible civil rights cases, including supporting challenges to southern states' discriminatory constitutions and practices that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disfranchisement_after_Reconstruction_era" title="Disfranchisement after Reconstruction era">disenfranchised African Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pildes_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pildes-7">[7]</a></sup> Through the 1920s and 1930s, Washington worked with <a href="/wiki/Julius_Rosenwald" title="Julius Rosenwald">Julius Rosenwald</a> and architects at the college to develop models for rural schools, to be used with Rosenwald's matching funds to gain construction of more rural schools for black children in the South.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1932, the school was the site of the now-infamous <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_syphilis_experiment" title="Tuskegee syphilis experiment">Tuskegee syphilis experiment</a> (1932–1972), started to test treatments of the disease. With funding cut by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the Institute staff cut back on medications to treat the disease and studied the effects of untreated syphilis on patients and their partners. In addition, participants were not informed that treatment was available for their disease after antibiotics had been developed.</p>
<p>One of the most famous teachers at Tuskegee was <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Carver" title="George Washington Carver">George Washington Carver</a>, whose name is synonymous with innovative research into <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern</a> farming methods and the development of hundreds of commercial products derived from regional crops, including peanuts and sweet potatoes.</p>
<p>During World War II, Tuskegee and Tuskegee Institute were also home to the famed <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Airmen" title="Tuskegee Airmen">Tuskegee Airmen</a>. This was the first squadron of African-American <a href="/wiki/Pilot_(aeronautics)" title="Pilot (aeronautics)">pilots</a> trained in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Military" title="U.S. Military">U.S. Military</a> for service in that war.</p>
<p>The university in the 21st century is a center of excellence for African-American education. The heart of the university has been designated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark_District" title="National Historic Landmark District">National Historic District</a> and was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1974.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Veterans_Administration_Medical_Center" title="Tuskegee Veterans Administration Medical Center">Tuskegee Veterans Administration Medical Center</a> was opened in 1923, authorized by Congress. A total of 27 buildings were constructed on the 464-acre campus, which provided housing and a hospital to serve the needs of more than 300,000 African-American veterans in the South from World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vamc_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-vamc-3">[3]</a></sup> It attracted doctors from top schools, such as Dr. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Toussaint_Tourgee_Tildon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Toussaint Tourgee Tildon (page does not exist)">Toussaint Tourgee Tildon</a>, a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a>. He was one of the first six African-American doctors to work at the hospital; as director of the complex for 12 years (1946–1958), he achieved accreditation for a medical residency program at the hospital. He also worked to ensure accessibility for graduates to good medical positions in the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tildon_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tildon-8">[8]</a></sup> By 1975, the hospital complex had</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voting_rights_challenge">Voting rights challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Voting rights challenge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gomillion_v._Lightfoot" title="Gomillion v. Lightfoot">Gomillion v. Lightfoot</a></div>
<p>Following passage of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Act_of_1957" title="Civil Rights Act of 1957">Civil Rights Act of 1957</a>, activists had made progress in registering black voters in the city. African Americans in Tuskegee and other Alabama cities had been largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disfranchisement_after_Reconstruction_era" title="Disfranchisement after Reconstruction era">disenfranchised</a> after passage of a new state constitution in 1901, which had requirements that were discriminatory in practice, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poll_tax_(United_States)" title="Poll tax (United States)">poll tax</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literacy_tests" title="Literacy tests">literacy tests</a>.</p>
<p>In 1957 the 400 registered black voters nearly equaled white voters; a total of 1000 voters were registered. But African Americans in the city outnumbered whites on a four-to-one basis; among them were many highly educated, professional African Americans working at Tuskegee and the VA hospital. That year, without debate and against the protests of many African Americans, the state legislature redefined the boundaries of the city; it enacted Local Law 140, creating an irregular, 28-sided city boundary that excluded 420 black voters from the city, leaving only ten black voters within the redefined boundaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> Those excluded included the entire professional staffs of the Institute and the hospital. No white voters were excluded by the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-alenc-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The law was intended to guarantee that minority whites could retain control of the city even if more blacks succeeded in the arduous process of registering to vote. Some 3,000 African-American residents protested passage of the law at a church in Tuskegee; they also began an economic boycott of white businesses in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomillion_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gomillion-10">[10]</a></sup> The boycott was referred to as a "selective buying campaign" due to the fact that boycotting was illegal under state law. It lasted about four years, during which twenty-six businesses operated by white proprietors closed down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>African Americans also organized to challenge the law in court, in a case supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a>. The law was initially upheld by the US District Court and affirmed by the Appeals Court based in New Orleans. Known as <i><a href="/wiki/Gomillion_v._Lightfoot" title="Gomillion v. Lightfoot">Gomillion v. Lightfoot</a>,</i> it was decided by the US Supreme Court in 1960, with the ruling implemented in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomillion_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-gomillion-10">[10]</a></sup> The court ruled that the <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">gerrymandering</a> of city boundaries was racially motivated and violated the <a href="/wiki/Fifteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a> which states that "states were not insulated from federal judicial review when they jeopardized federally protected rights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenc_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-alenc-4">[4]</a></sup> The exclusionary gerrymandering was overturned and the city restored to its previous boundaries.</p>
<p>This case was cited in the later <i><a href="/wiki/Baker_v._Carr" title="Baker v. Carr">Baker v. Carr</a></i> (1964), in which the Supreme Court ruled that Tennessee's malapportionment of election districts violated civil rights. It ruled that representation in both houses of all state legislatures had to be based on population, under the "<a href="/wiki/One_man,_one_vote" title="One man, one vote">one man, one vote</a>" doctrine, and that such districts had to be regularly updated to reflect population changes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 21st century, Tuskegee has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council-manager_government" title="Council-manager government">council-manager government</a> led by a four-member city council, a mayor, and an appointed city manager.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> acts as a legislative body of the city, passing laws and regulations and appointing citizens to the city's various boards. Each member of the city council is elected for a four-year term from one of three geographic single-member districts. Tuskegee has one city council member who is elected at-large to a four-year term and serves as mayor-pro tem. The duties of the mayor are to promote the city, communicate with residents, and preside over City Council meetings. As such, the position of mayor in Tuskegee is primarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceremonial" title="Ceremonial">ceremonial</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The table at right shows the effects of the state passing a law in 1957 to redefine the city of Tuskegee in a way that excluded nearly all black residents, dramatically reducing the population by 1960. The city and other officials were sued under <i><a href="/wiki/Gomillion_v._Lightfoot" title="Gomillion v. Lightfoot">Gomillion v. Lightfoot</a></i> (1960); the US Supreme Court ruled against the state's action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenc_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-alenc-4">[4]</a></sup> The city boundaries were reinstituted, as reflected by the dramatic "increase" of population in the city recorded in 1970. The population in 1960, with the restored borders, was 7,240, according to the 1970 U.S. Census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because of lack of economic opportunities in the largely rural area, both the city and rural county have lost population since the late 20th century.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-18">[18]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 11,846 people, 4,169 households, and 2,326 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 765.7 people per square mile (295.7/km²). There were 5,101 housing units at an average density of 329.7 per square mile (127.3/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 95.48% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 2.59% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 0.19% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.69% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.01% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.17% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.88% from two or more races. 0.68% of the population were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.</p>
<p>There were 4,169 households out of which 27.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 22.6% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 29.0% had a female householder with no husband present, and 44.2% were non-families. 37.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.3% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.30 and the average family size was 3.06.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 22.6% under the age of 18, 25.4% from 18 to 24, 19.9% from 25 to 44, 17.2% from 45 to 64, and 14.9% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 26 years. For every 100 females there were 80.7 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 75.7 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $18,889, and the median income for a family was $26,862. Males had a median income of $23,333 versus $22,951 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $12,340. About 30.0% of families and 35.7% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 44.1% of those under age 18 and 26.3% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_census_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010_census-19">[19]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 9,865 people, 3,749 households, and 1,956 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 636.5 people per square mile (246.0/km²). There were 4,624 housing units at an average density of 298.3 per square mile (115.3/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 95.8% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 1.9% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 0.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.5% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.0% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.3% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1.3% from two or more races. 1.3% of the population were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.</p>
<p>There were 3,749 households out of which 21.3% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 19.0% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 28.6% had a female householder with no husband present, and 47.8% were non-families. 40.1% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.0% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.17 and the average family size was 2.96.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 18.5% under the age of 18, 27.8% from 18 to 24, 18.9% from 25 to 44, 21.7% from 45 to 64, and 13.1% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 27.6 years. For every 100 females there were 78.8 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 75.6 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $24,251, and the median income for a family was $43,472. Males had a median income of $40,653 versus $26,631 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $15,471. About 22.2% of families and 31.6% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 40.0% of those under age 18 and 10.2% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuskegee has one weekly newspaper, <i>The Tuskegee News</i>, which has operated since 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuskegee,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sadie_Peterson_Delaney" title="Sadie Peterson Delaney">Sadie Peterson Delaney</a> was the chief librarian of the Veterans Administration Hospital.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosa_Louise_Parks" title="Rosa Louise Parks">Rosa Louise Parks</a> was born here; she became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activist" title="Activist">activist</a> in the 1950s in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery, Alabama</a>, contributing to the more than year-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montgomery_Bus_Boycott" title="Montgomery Bus Boycott">Montgomery Bus Boycott</a> that achieved desegregation of the city system.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lionel_Richie" title="Lionel Richie">Lionel Richie</a> was born here; rhythm &amp; blues singer, songwriter, musician, record producer, and occasional actor.</li>
<li>Toussaint Tildon, one of the first six African-American doctors to work at Tuskegee VA Medical Center, built in 1923, he served as a psychiatrist for more than 30 years and directed the hospital for six years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vamc_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-vamc-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sammy_Younge_Jr." title="Sammy Younge Jr.">Sammy Younge Jr.</a> was born in Tuskegee; he was a civil rights activist.</li>
</ul>
