<p>In <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>, a <b>regional power</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> that has <a href="/wiki/Power_(international_relations)" title="Power (international relations)">power</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographic</a> <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-es_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-es-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-po_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-po-2">[2]</a></sup> States which wield unrivalled power and influence within a region of the world possess <a href="/wiki/Regional_hegemony" title="Regional hegemony">regional hegemony</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regional powers shape the <a href="/wiki/Polarity_(international_relations)" title="Polarity (international relations)">polarity</a> of a regional area. Typically, regional powers have capabilities which are important in the <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> but do not have capabilities at a global scale. Slightly contrasting definitions differ as to what makes a regional power. The <a href="/wiki/European_Consortium_for_Political_Research" title="European Consortium for Political Research">European Consortium for Political Research</a> defines a regional power as: "A state belonging to a geographically defined region, dominating this region in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> terms, able to exercise <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_(international_relations)" title="Power (international relations)">influence</a> in the region and considerable influence on the world scale, willing to make use of power <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a> and recognized or even accepted as the regional leader by its neighbours".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-es_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-es-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Institute_of_Global_and_Area_Studies" title="German Institute of Global and Area Studies">German Institute of Global and Area Studies</a> states that a regional power must:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-po_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-po-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>form part of a definable region with its own identity</li>
<li>claim to be a regional power (self-image of a regional power)</li>
<li>exert decisive influence on the geographic extension of the region as well as on its ideological construction</li>
<li>dispose over comparatively high <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demographic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideological</a> capabilities</li>
<li>be well integrated into the region</li>
<li>define the regional security agenda to a high degree</li>
<li>be appreciated as a regional power by other powers in the region and beyond, especially by other regional powers</li>
<li>be well connected with regional and global fora</li>
</ul>

<p>Below are states that have been described as regional powers by international relations and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia#Academic_personnel" title="Academia">academics</a>, analysts, or other experts. These states to some extent meet the criteria to have regional power status, as described above. Different experts have differing views on exactly which states are regional powers. States are arranged by their region, and in alphabetic order. Primary, or major, regional powers (also known as <i>pivotal powers</i>) are placed in the major regions as identified by analysts. Secondary, or minor, regional powers are listed within their sub regions. Major regional powers in <b>bold</b>, and minor regional powers in normal font.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> was the dominant power in eastern Asia. However, many decades later, the Empire of Japan first became an important player in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> as one of the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allied powers</a>, then turmoil economic, its expulsion from the League of Nations, and interest in expansion of mainland caused Japan to become a major player in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as one of the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a> and China became a key player in World War II as one of the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied powers</a>. In recent years, a re-balancing of military and economic might towards countries such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> has made significant changes in the geopolitics of Asia. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> have also earned greater influence over regions outside Asia.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_balance_of_power" title="European balance of power">European balance of power</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> are regarded as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Four_(European_Union)" title="Big Four (European Union)">Big Four</a> of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Historically, dominant powers in this region created large colonial empires worldwide (such as the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French Empire</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Empire" title="Italian Empire">Italian Empire</a>). Most of the continent is now integrated as a consequence of the enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</p>
