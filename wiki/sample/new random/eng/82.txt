<p>A <b>public service announcement</b> (<b>PSA</b>) or <b>public service ad,</b> is a message in the public interest disseminated by the media without charge, with the objective of raising awareness, changing public attitudes and behavior towards a social issue. In the UK, they are generally called '<a href="/wiki/Public_information_film" title="Public information film">public information films</a>' (PIFs); in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, they are known as 'announcements in the public interest' ('APIs').</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_service_announcement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest public service announcements were made before and during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> years in both the UK and the US.</p>
<p>In the UK, amateur actor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Massingham" title="Richard Massingham">Richard Massingham</a> set up Public Relationship Films Ltd in 1938 as a specialist agency for producing short educational films for the public. In the films, he typically played a bumbling character who was slightly stupider than average, and often explained the message of the film through demonstrating the risks if it was ignored. Films' topics included how to cross the road, how to prevent the spread of diseases, how to swim and how to drive without causing the road to be unsafe for other users. During the war, he was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Information_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Information (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Information</a> to produce films for the war effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Massingham began to produce longer films, for both private companies and the Government, after the War.</p>
<p>In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Ad_Council" title="Ad Council">Ad Council</a> (initially called the War Advertising Council) was set up in 1941, when America entered World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">[2]</a></sup> It began implementing on a massive scale the idea of using advertising to influence American society on a range of fronts. Their first campaigns focused on the country's needs during World War II, such as encouraging the American public to invest their savings in <a href="/wiki/Government_bond" title="Government bond">government bonds</a>.</p>
<p>After the War, PSAs were used to educate the public on a broader range of important issues. In the UK, they were produced for the <a href="/wiki/Central_Office_of_Information" title="Central Office of Information">Central Office of Information</a> (COI), and again by private contractors, which were usually small film companies, such as Richard Taylor Cartoons. They were supplied to broadcasters free of charge for them to use whenever they wished. Their usefulness as a cost-free means to fill the gaps in fixed-duration commercial breaks left by unsold advertising airtime led to their being used regularly and extensively in the 60s, 70s and much of the 80s, and consequently, within both the COI and broadcasting companies they were typically known as "fillers". They are still being produced, although the vastly reduced need for broadcasters to turn to third-party filler material to deal with unused airtime during breaks or junctions means they are now only seen rarely.</p>
<p>In the US, the Ad Council expanded its focus to address issues such as forest fires, blood donations and highway safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adcouncil_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-adcouncil-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_service_announcement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USA">USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_service_announcement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common topics of PSAs are health and safety, such as the multimedia <i><a href="/wiki/WVOX#Notable_Past_Programming" title="WVOX">Emergency Preparedness &amp; Safety Tips On Air and Online</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Talk_radio" title="Talk radio">talk radio</a>/blog) campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A typical PSA is part of a public awareness <a href="/wiki/Advertising_campaign" title="Advertising campaign">campaign</a> to inform or educate the public about an issue such as <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_addiction" title="Behavioral addiction">compulsive gambling</a>. The range of possible topics has expanded over time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From time to time a <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charitable organization</a> enlists the support of a celebrity for a PSA; examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actress" title="Actress">actress</a> <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Erbe" title="Kathryn Erbe">Kathryn Erbe</a> telling people to <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">be green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crips" title="Crips">Crips</a> <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">gang</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Williams" title="Stanley Williams">Stanley Williams</a> speaking from prison to urge youth not to join gangs. Some PSAs tell people to adopt animals instead of buying them. Protecting our Earth, also known as being green, is another example of a current PSA topic.</p>
<p>Some television shows featuring <a href="/wiki/Very_special_episode" title="Very special episode">very special episodes</a> made PSAs after the episodes. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit">Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit</a></i> talked about <a href="/wiki/Child_abduction" title="Child abduction">child abduction</a> in one episode, so it had a PSA about child abduction. Another example is when the original <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i> did an episode about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drunk_driving" title="Drunk driving">drunk driving</a>, which had a PSA about drunk driving.</p>
<p>One of the earliest television public service announcements came in the form of <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bear" title="Smokey Bear">Smokey Bear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1980s, a large number of American cartoon shows contained PSA's at the end of their shows. These may or may not have been relevant to the episode itself. Three of the most widely known are the closing moral segments at the end of <i><a href="/wiki/He-Man_and_the_Masters_of_the_Universe" title="He-Man and the Masters of the Universe">He-Man and the Masters of the Universe</a></i>, the "Knowing is Half the Battle" epilogues in <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero">G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</a></i> and the "Sonic Sez" segments from <i><a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Sonic_the_Hedgehog" title="Adventures of Sonic the Hedgehog">Adventures of Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i>.</p>
<p>Some television PSAs have topics such as on not watching so much television, or not taking fictional shows literally; or about television, movie, or video game ratings. Public service advertising has become a significant force in changing public attitudes on topics such as drinking and driving, crime abatement and various health/safety issues. While stations have never been mandated by the FCC to use a prescribed number of PSAs, they are required to prove they broadcast in the public interest and PSAs are one of the ways they meet that requirement as part of serving as a "public trustee."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-regulation_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-regulation-10">[10]</a></sup> Many television affiliates and cable networks employ PSA directors who review all the PSAs provided by nonprofit organizations and choose which ones to schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selection_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-selection-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK">UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_service_announcement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Public_information_film" title="Public information film">Public information film</a></div>
<p>Public Information Films produced by the Britain's COI covered a wide range of subjects, most related to <a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">safety</a>, but also on other subjects, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> prevention and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vote" title="Vote">voting</a>.</p>
<p>Advertisements produced by companies and private groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Society_for_the_Prevention_of_Cruelty_to_Children" title="National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children">NSPCC</a>'s <i>Cartoon Boy</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Centrica" title="Centrica">British Gas</a>'s commercials about gas leaks are considered by fans as a form of non-Governmental PIFs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_countries">In other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_service_announcement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China's first PSAs in 1978 were about saving water and was broadcast on Guiyang television.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, terrestrial television networks have been required since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Day_(PRC)" title="National Day (PRC)">National Day</a>, 2004, to preface their main evening news broadcasts with a minute-long announcement in the public interest which plays the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_National_Anthem" title="Chinese National Anthem">Chinese National Anthem</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin" title="Standard Mandarin">Mandarin</a> over various patriotic montages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
