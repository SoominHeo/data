<p>The <b>Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup</b>, commonly known as the <b>U.S. Open Cup</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">knock-out cup competition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Soccer_in_the_United_States" title="Soccer in the United States">American soccer</a>. It is the oldest ongoing national soccer competition in the U.S. and the world's third-longest-running open soccer tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2016_Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="2016 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">103rd edition</a>, held in 2016, was contested by 91 clubs from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_soccer_league_system" title="United States soccer league system">three professional leagues</a> sanctioned by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">United States Soccer Federation</a>: <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> (MLS), the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League" title="North American Soccer League">North American Soccer League</a> (NASL) and the <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">United Soccer League</a> (USL), and also amateur clubs in the earlier rounds of the tournament after qualifying through their leagues. The overall champion earns a total of $250,000 in <a href="/wiki/Prize_money" title="Prize money">prize money</a>, while the runner-up receives $60,000, and the furthest-advancing team from each lower division league receives $15,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100thEdition_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-100thEdition-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition, the tournament winner qualifies for the group stage of the <a href="/wiki/CONCACAF_Champions_League" title="CONCACAF Champions League">CONCACAF Champions League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCL2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCL2008-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The competition was first held during the <a href="/wiki/1913%E2%80%9314_National_Challenge_Cup" title="1913–14 National Challenge Cup">1913–14 season</a> as the <b>National Challenge Cup</b>, with <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Field_Club" title="Brooklyn Field Club">Brooklyn Field Club</a> winning a trophy donated by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dewar,_1st_Baron_Dewar" title="Thomas Dewar, 1st Baron Dewar">Thomas Dewar</a> for the promotion of American soccer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was renamed and then dedicated to MLS owner <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Hunt" title="Lamar Hunt">Lamar Hunt</a> by the United States Soccer Federation in <a href="/wiki/1999_Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="1999 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">1999</a>.</p>
<p>Major League Soccer teams have dominated the competition since MLS began play in 1996. No lower division team has won the U.S. Open Cup since the <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Rhinos" title="Rochester Rhinos">Rochester Rhinos</a> in 1999, reached the U.S. Open Cup final since the <a href="/wiki/Charleston_Battery" title="Charleston Battery">Charleston Battery</a> in 2008, or reached the semifinals since the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Kickers" title="Richmond Kickers">Richmond Kickers</a> in 2011. The most recent champions of the competition, <a href="/wiki/FC_Dallas" title="FC Dallas">FC Dallas</a>, won their second title (the first title came when they were known as the Dallas Burn) after defeating the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Revolution" title="New England Revolution">New England Revolution</a> 4–2 in the <a href="/wiki/2015_Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup_Final" title="2015 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup Final">2016 final</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Format">Format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The competition is a <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">single-elimination tournament</a> that has been contested by 80 teams since the <a href="/wiki/2014_Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="2014 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">2014 edition</a>. This pool consists of the 38 American clubs in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_soccer_league_system" title="United States soccer league system">three professional leagues</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League" title="North American Soccer League">North American Soccer League</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">United Soccer League</a> (formerly USL PRO), as well as 42 amateur teams from the <a href="/wiki/Premier_Development_League" title="Premier Development League">Premier Development League</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Premier_Soccer_League" title="National Premier Soccer League">National Premier Soccer League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Adult_Soccer_Association" title="United States Adult Soccer Association">United States Adult Soccer Association</a>, and <a href="/wiki/US_Club_Soccer" title="US Club Soccer">US Club Soccer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2014_Format_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-2014_Format-6">[6]</a></sup> The first three rounds, consisting of amateur and lower-league teams, are played during consecutive weekdays in May with amateur teams advancing to face USL and NASL clubs in geographical pairings in the third round. The winner of each match progresses to the next round and the loser is eliminated from the tournament. MLS clubs enter play in the fourth round, matched geographically with the winners of the third round to play on a date in June that is determined by the home side specifically selected for non-interference with league games. After the fourth round, no new teams are introduced, leading to a quarterfinal round in July, a semifinal round in August, and a final match to determine the champion in September. Every match, including the final, is a one-legged tie that lasts 90 minutes plus any additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoppage_time" title="Stoppage time">stoppage time</a>. If no clear winner has been determined after 90 minutes of normal time, 30 minutes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_time" title="Extra time">extra time</a> is played. If the score is still level after extra time then the winner is decided by a <a href="/wiki/Penalty_shoot-out_(association_football)" title="Penalty shoot-out (association football)">penalty shoot-out</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2014_Format_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-2014_Format-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualification">Qualification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Qualification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through the 2011 edition, eight teams from each level of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_soccer_league_system" title="United States soccer league system">American Soccer Pyramid</a> took part in the competition proper, with each league narrowing its delegation separately in the spring before the competition officially began in the summer. In some cases, additional teams played in qualifying rounds to gain entry. One example was found with MLS clubs, as only the top six from the previous regular season received automatic bids, while the bottom U.S.-based MLS teams faced each other to qualify for the remaining two MLS slots.</p>
<p>Beginning in 2012, the competition was expanded from its previous 40 teams to 64, with the qualifying process radically changed. The <a href="/wiki/National_Premier_Soccer_League" title="National Premier Soccer League">National Premier Soccer League</a> received six places, plus the possibility of a seventh in a playoff against a team from the amateur <a href="/wiki/US_Club_Soccer" title="US Club Soccer">US Club Soccer</a> setup. Nine clubs from the USASA earned places, as did 16 <a href="/wiki/Premier_Development_League" title="Premier Development League">USL Premier Development League</a> teams. Each of these organizations has its own qualifying process to determine its entrants. These 32 teams competed in the first round of the Cup, with the winners meeting all 16 USL Pro and NASL teams in the second round. The 16 U.S.-based MLS teams entered in the third round.</p>
<p>In 2013 the competition was expanded to 68 teams. All U.S. based Division I, II and III teams participated in the tournament proper: 16 from Major League Soccer, six from the North American Soccer League and 12 from USL PRO. The remaining 34 spots in the tournament field were filled by amateur teams from the Adult Council category–16 from the Premier Development League, eight from U.S. Adult Soccer Association regional qualifying, eight from the National Premier Soccer League, one from US Club Soccer and one from the United States Specialty Sports Association.</p>
<p>The process for determining the site for the Open Cup tournament semifinals and final was changed in 2013. In past years, the sites for the final three matches of the tournament had been determined through a sealed-bid process, but in 2013 the hosts of those games were determined by a coin flip. Home teams throughout the entire tournament were determined by random selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100thEdition_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-100thEdition-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2008, the champion of the U.S. Open Cup has earned the right to play in the <a href="/wiki/CONCACAF_Champions_League" title="CONCACAF Champions League">CONCACAF Champions League</a>. The first team to represent the U.S. as Open Cup champion was 2007's winner, <a href="/wiki/New_England_Revolution" title="New England Revolution">New England Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCL2008_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CCL2008-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in 2016, lower-division professional clubs owned by higher-division professional clubs are no longer eligible to participate in the US Open Cup. This serves to remove the MLS reserve clubs in USL from the 2016 competition, after issues of clubs holding back players from their USL sides in 2015 in order to keep them eligible to play for the parent MLS club. Players are <a href="/wiki/Cup-tied" title="Cup-tied">only allowed to play for one club</a> in any US Open Cup season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Amateur clubs remain eligible to enter even if they are owned by professional clubs. Initially, "hybrid affiliate" clubs—i.e., lower-division professional clubs that are staffed but not owned by higher-division clubs—also remained eligible, but those clubs were also banned effective with the 2016 competition. This last change was proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Dynamo" title="Houston Dynamo">Houston Dynamo</a>, which were the senior club to <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Valley_FC_Toros" title="Rio Grande Valley FC Toros">Rio Grande Valley FC Toros</a> in the first such arrangement in the U.S. game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The competition dates back to 1913-14, when it was known as the <b>National Challenge Cup</b>. In 1999, U.S. Soccer honored one of American soccer's most important patrons, <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Hunt" title="Lamar Hunt">Lamar Hunt</a>, by changing the official title of the tournament to the <b>Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup</b>. The winners of the tournament were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Dewar_Cup" title="Dewar Cup">Dewar Cup</a>, donated by Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Dewar" title="Thomas Dewar">Thomas Dewar</a> for the promotion of soccer in the United States in 1912, until it was retired due to poor condition in 1979. It was brought back into use by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Adult_Soccer_Association" title="United States Adult Soccer Association">United States Adult Soccer Association</a> in 1997, but is now back on permanent display at the <a href="/wiki/National_Soccer_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Soccer Hall of Fame">National Soccer Hall of Fame</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oneonta,_New_York" title="Oneonta, New York">Oneonta, New York</a>, and the recent winners of the tournament have been awarded a new, different trophy. Despite this, the name of each winning club is still added to the base of the original Dewar Cup.</p>

<p>The National Challenge Cup was the first truly national cup competition in the United States, as previous cups had been effectively relegated to regional status by the difficulties in coordination and travel caused by the size of the United States in the early 1900s. While U.S. Soccer had initially administered the competition, in 1985 they handed over management to the USASA. In 1995, U.S. Soccer resumed its administration of the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maccabi_Los_Angeles" title="Maccabi Los Angeles">Maccabi Los Angeles</a> of California and <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel_F.C._(1907%E2%80%9330)" title="Bethlehem Steel F.C. (1907–30)">Bethlehem Steel</a> of Pennsylvania have both won the cup a record five times, while <a href="/wiki/Greek_American_AA" title="Greek American AA">Greek American AA</a> of New York and <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Sounders_FC" title="Seattle Sounders FC">Seattle Sounders FC</a> are tied for the record for most consecutive cup victories at three. Most of these records are likely to fall over time, now that <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> offers a fully professional league, and its teams typically dominate the competition. The old <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League_(1968%E2%80%9384)" title="North American Soccer League (1968–84)">NASL</a> did not participate in the Open Cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since MLS' debut in 1996, MLS clubs have won the cup in all but one of those years. The <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Rhinos" title="Rochester Rhinos">Rochester Rhinos</a> of the 2nd division <a href="/wiki/A-League_(1995%E2%80%932004)" title="A-League (1995–2004)">A-League</a> were surprise winners in 1999, defeating four MLS clubs, including the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rapids" title="Colorado Rapids">Colorado Rapids</a> 2–0 in the championship match. The first professional team to win in the modern era were the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Kickers" title="Richmond Kickers">Richmond Kickers</a> of the USISL (the predecessor to the A-League, later known as the <a href="/wiki/USL_First_Division" title="USL First Division">USL First Division</a>, USL Pro, and now as the <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">United Soccer League</a>) in 1995, one year before the start of MLS. <a href="/wiki/D.C._United" title="D.C. United">D.C. United</a> were the first MLS team to win in 1996.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hosting">Hosting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hosting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>U.S. Soccer uses a simple coin toss to decide which team hosts each match all the way through the final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Up until the 2011 U.S. Open Cup, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">U.S. Soccer</a> had used sealed bids to award home matches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> which resulted in certain clubs outbidding other teams. From 2007 to 2011, MLS side <a href="/wiki/D.C._United" title="D.C. United">D.C. United</a> hosted 17 straight matches including two finals, and from 2008–2010 MLS side <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Sounders_FC" title="Seattle Sounders FC">Seattle Sounders FC</a> hosted 11 of 14 matches in its three championship seasons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
