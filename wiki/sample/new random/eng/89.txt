<p><b>Maryam Mirzakhani</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">مریم میرزاخانی</span>‎‎; born May 3, 1977) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian-American" title="Iranian-American">Iranian-American</a> mathematician and a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weil-Petersson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weil-Petersson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 August 2014, Mirzakhani became both the first woman and the first Iranian honored with the <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">Fields Medal</a>, the most prestigious award in mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farsnews_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farsnews-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The award committee cited her work in "the dynamics and geometry of Riemann surfaces and their moduli spaces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her research topics include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teichm%C3%BCller_theory" title="Teichmüller theory">Teichmüller theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">hyperbolic geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Symplectic_geometry" title="Symplectic geometry">symplectic geometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmcv2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mmcv2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maryam_Mirzakhani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mirzakhani was born in 1977 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tehran,_Iran" title="Tehran, Iran">Tehran, Iran</a>. She went to high school in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farzanegan_School" title="Farzanegan School">Farzanegan</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Development_of_Exceptional_Talents" title="National Organization for Development of Exceptional Talents">National Organization for Development of Exceptional Talents</a> (NODET).</p>
<p>In 1994, Mirzakhani won a gold medal in the <a href="/wiki/International_Mathematical_Olympiad" title="International Mathematical Olympiad">International Mathematical Olympiad</a>, the first female Iranian student to do so. In the 1995 <a href="/wiki/International_Mathematical_Olympiad" title="International Mathematical Olympiad">International Mathematical Olympiad</a>, she became the first Iranian student to achieve a perfect score and to win two gold medals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olympiad_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-olympiad-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>She obtained her BSc in mathematics (1999) from <a href="/wiki/Sharif_University_of_Technology" title="Sharif University of Technology">Sharif University of Technology</a> in Tehran. She went to the United States for graduate work, earning a PhD from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> (2004), where she worked under the supervision of the Fields Medalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curtis_McMullen" title="Curtis McMullen">Curtis McMullen</a>. She was also a 2004 research fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Clay_Mathematics_Institute" title="Clay Mathematics Institute">Clay Mathematics Institute</a> and a professor at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_work">Research work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maryam_Mirzakhani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mirzakhani has made several contributions to the theory of moduli spaces of <a href="/wiki/Riemann_surface" title="Riemann surface">Riemann surfaces</a>. In her early work, Mirzakhani discovered a formula expressing the volume of a <a href="/wiki/Moduli_space" title="Moduli space">moduli space</a> with a given genus as a polynomial in the number of boundary components. This led her to obtain a new proof for the formula discovered by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Witten" title="Edward Witten">Edward Witten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maxim_Kontsevich" title="Maxim Kontsevich">Maxim Kontsevich</a> on the intersection numbers of tautological classes on moduli space,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weil-Petersson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weil-Petersson-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as an asymptotic formula for the growth of the number of simple <a href="/wiki/Closed_geodesic" title="Closed geodesic">closed geodesics</a> on a compact hyperbolic surface, generalizing the <a href="/wiki/Theorem_of_the_three_geodesics" title="Theorem of the three geodesics">theorem of the three geodesics</a> for spherical surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Her subsequent work has focused on Teichmüller dynamics of moduli space. In particular, she was able to prove the long-standing conjecture that <a href="/wiki/William_Thurston" title="William Thurston">William Thurston</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_map" title="Earthquake map">earthquake</a> flow on <a href="/wiki/Teichm%C3%BCller_space" title="Teichmüller space">Teichmüller space</a> is ergodic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most recently as of 2014, with <a href="/wiki/Alex_Eskin" title="Alex Eskin">Alex Eskin</a> and with input from Amir Mohammadi, Mirzakhani proved that complex geodesics and their closures in moduli space are surprisingly regular, rather than irregular or fractal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMU-pressrelease_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMU-pressrelease-18">[18]</a></sup> The closures of complex geodesics are algebraic objects defined in terms of polynomials and therefore they have certain rigidity properties, which is analogous to a celebrated result that <a href="/wiki/Marina_Ratner" title="Marina Ratner">Marina Ratner</a> arrived at during the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMU-pressrelease_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMU-pressrelease-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Mathematical_Union" title="International Mathematical Union">International Mathematical Union</a> said in its press release that, "It is astounding to find that the rigidity in homogeneous spaces has an echo in the inhomogeneous world of moduli space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMU-pressrelease_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMU-pressrelease-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mirzakhani was awarded the Fields Medal in 2014 for "her outstanding contributions to the <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a> and geometry of <a href="/wiki/Riemann_surface" title="Riemann surface">Riemann surfaces</a> and their moduli spaces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The award was made in Seoul at the International Congress of Mathematicians on 13 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-date_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-date-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of the award, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">Wisconsin</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Ellenberg" title="Jordan Ellenberg">Jordan Ellenberg</a> explained her research to a popular audience:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>... [Her] work expertly blends dynamics with geometry. Among other things, she studies billiards. But now, in a move very characteristic of modern mathematics, it gets kind of meta: She considers not just one billiard table, but the universe of all <i>possible</i> billiard tables. And the kind of dynamics she studies doesn't directly concern the motion of the billiards on the table, but instead a transformation of the billiard table itself, which is changing its shape in a rule-governed way; if you like, the table itself moves like a strange planet around the universe of all possible tables ... This isn't the kind of thing you do to win at pool, but it's the kind of thing you do to win a Fields Medal. And it's what you need to do in order to expose the dynamics at the heart of geometry; for there's no question that they're there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_Fields_article_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate_Fields_article-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Hassan_Rouhani" title="Hassan Rouhani">Hassan Rouhani</a> of Iran congratulated her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maryam_Mirzakhani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She is married to Jan Vondrák, a <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech</a> <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science" title="Theoretical computer science">theoretical computer scientist</a> who works at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Almaden_Research_Center" title="IBM Almaden Research Center">IBM Almaden Research Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> They have a daughter named Anahita (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">آناهيتا</span>‎‎) .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
