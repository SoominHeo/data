<p>A <b>deck</b> is a permanent covering over a <a href="/wiki/Compartment_(ship)" title="Compartment (ship)">compartment</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> of a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a>. On a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> or ship, the primary or upper deck is the horizontal structure that forms the "roof" of the hull, strengthening it and serving as the primary working surface. Vessels often have more than one level both within the hull and in the superstructure above the primary deck, similar to the floors of a multi-story building, that are also referred to as decks, as are certain compartments and decks built over specific areas of the superstructure. Decks for some purposes have specific names.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main purpose of the upper or primary deck is structural, and only secondarily to provide weather-tightness and support people and equipment. The deck serves as the lid to the complex box girder which is the hull. It resists <a href="/wiki/Tension_(physics)" title="Tension (physics)">tension</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compression_(physics)" title="Compression (physics)">compression</a>, and racking forces. The deck's <a href="/wiki/Scantling" title="Scantling">scantling</a> is usually the same as the <a href="/wiki/Topsides" title="Topsides">topsides</a>, or might be heavier if the deck is expected to carry heavier loads (for example a <a href="/wiki/Container_ship" title="Container ship">container ship</a>). The deck will be reinforced around deck fittings such as the <a href="/wiki/Capstan_(nautical)" title="Capstan (nautical)">capstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleat_(nautical)" title="Cleat (nautical)">cleats</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bollard" title="Bollard">bollards</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="896" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/22/QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg/220px-QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/22/QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg/330px-QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/22/QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg/440px-QM2-wraparound-deck.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On ships with more than one level, deck refers to the level itself. The actual floor surface is called the sole, the term deck refers to a structural member tying the ships frames or ribs together over the keel. In modern ships, the interior decks are usually numbered from the primary deck, which is #1, downward and upward. So the first deck below the primary deck will be #2, and the first above the primary deck will be #A2 or #S2 (for "Above" or "Superstructure"). Some merchant ships may alternatively designate decks below the primary deck, usually machinery spaces, by numbers, and those above it, in the accommodation block, by letters. Ships may also call decks by common names, or (especially on <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ship" title="Cruise ship">cruise ships</a>) may invent fanciful and romantic names for a specific deck or area of that specific ship, such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lido_deck" title="Lido deck">Lido deck</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Princess_Cruises" title="Princess Cruises">Princess Cruises</a>' <i>Love Boat</i>.</p>
<p>Equipment mounted on deck, such as the ship's wheel, <a href="/wiki/Binnacle" title="Binnacle">binnacle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fife_rail" title="Fife rail">fife rails</a>, and so forth, may be collectively referred to as deck furniture. Weather decks in western designs evolved from having structures fore (forward or front) and <a href="/wiki/Aft" title="Aft">aft</a> (rear) of the ship mostly clear; in the 19th century, pilothouses/wheelhouses and <a href="/wiki/Cabin_(ship)" title="Cabin (ship)">deckhouses</a> began to appear, eventually developing into the superstructure of modern ships. Eastern designs developed earlier, with efficient middle decks and minimalist fore and aft cabin structures across a range of designs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_names_for_decks">Common names for decks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Common names for decks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In vessels having more than one deck there are various naming conventions, numerically, alphabetically, etc. However, there are also various common historical names and types of decks:</p>
<p><span id="01_level"></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>01 level</b> is the term used in naval services to refer to the deck above the main deck. The next higher decks are referred to as the 02 level, the 03 level, and so on. Although these are formally called decks, they are usually referred to as levels, because they are usually incomplete decks that do not extend all the way from the <a href="/wiki/Stem_(ship)" title="Stem (ship)">stem</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a> or across the ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><span id="Berth_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Berth deck</b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) A deck next below the gun deck, where the hammocks of the <a href="/wiki/Crew" title="Crew">crew</a> are slung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><span id="Boat_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Boat deck</b>: Especially on ships with <a href="/wiki/Sponson" title="Sponson">sponsons</a>, the deck area where lifeboats or the ship's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain%27s_Gig" title="Captain's Gig">gig</a> are stored.<span id="Boiler_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Boiler deck</b>: (river steamers) The passenger deck above the vessel's <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>.<span id="Bridge_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Bridge deck</b>: (a) The deck area including the <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_wheel" title="Ship's wheel">helm</a> and navigation station, and where the <a href="/wiki/Watchstanding" title="Watchstanding">Officer of the Deck/Watch</a> will be found, also known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Conn_(nautical)" title="Conn (nautical)">conn</a></i> (b) An <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_nautical_terms#A" title="Glossary of nautical terms">athwartships</a> structure at the forward end of the <a href="/wiki/Cockpit_(sailing)" title="Cockpit (sailing)">cockpit</a> with a deck, often somewhat lower than the primary deck, to prevent a <a href="#Glossary">pooping wave</a> from entering through the <a href="/wiki/Companionway" title="Companionway">companionway</a>. May also refer to the <a href="/wiki/Deck_(bridge)" title="Deck (bridge)">deck</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(nautical)" title="Bridge (nautical)">bridge</a>.<span id="Flight_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Flight_deck" title="Flight deck">Flight deck</a></b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) A deck from which aircraft take off or land.<span id="Flush_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Flush_deck" title="Flush deck">Flush deck</a></b>: Any continuous unbroken deck from stem to stern.<span id="Forecastle_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Forecastle deck</b>: A partial deck above the main deck under which the sailors have their berths, extending from the foremast to the bow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><span id="Gun_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Freeboard deck</b>: assigned by a <a href="/wiki/Classification_society" title="Classification society">classification society</a> to determine the ship's <a href="/wiki/Freeboard_(nautical)" title="Freeboard (nautical)">freeboard</a>; usually the highest continuous deck, i.e. equivalent to the main deck.<span id="Freeboard_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Gun_deck" title="Gun deck">Gun deck</a></b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) a deck below the <a href="/wiki/Spar_(sailing)" title="Spar (sailing)">spar</a> deck where the ships' cannon were carried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> The term originally referred to a deck aboard a ship that was primarily used for the mounting of cannon to be fired in broadsides. However, on many smaller vessels such as frigates and unrated vessels the upper deck, forecastle and quarterdeck bore all of the cannons but were not referred to as the gun deck. The completely covered level under the upper deck was, however, still called the gun deck although it had no guns at all.<span id="Hangar_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hangar#Hangars_aboard_ships" title="Hangar">Hangar deck</a></b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) A deck aboard an aircraft carrier used to store and maintain aircraft.</li>
</ul>
<p><span id="half-deck"></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Half-deck</b>: That portion of the deck next below the spar deck which is between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainmast" title="Mainmast">mainmast</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cabin_(ship)" title="Cabin (ship)">cabin</a>.<span id="Helo_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Helicopter_deck" title="Helicopter deck">Helicopter deck</a></b> (<b>Heli or Helo deck</b>): Usually located near the stern and always kept clear of obstacles hazardous to a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> landing.<span id="Hurricane_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Hurricane deck</b>: (River Steamers, etc.), the upper deck, usually a light deck, erected above the frame of the hull (deriving its name from the wind that always seemed to blow on the deck).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><span id="Lido_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lido_deck" title="Lido deck">Lido deck</a></b>: Open area, typically at or near the stern of a passenger ship, housing the main outdoor swimming pool and sunbathing area.<span id="Lower_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Lower deck</b>: (a) the deck immediately over the hold, orig. only of a ship with two decks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (b) synonym for berth deck. (c) alternative name for a secondary gun deck<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><span id="Main_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Main_deck" title="Main deck">Main deck</a></b>: The principal deck of a vessel; in some ships the highest deck of the hull, usually but not always the weather deck; in sailing warships often a deck under the upper deck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><span id="Middle_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Middle or Waist deck</b> The upper deck amidships, the working area of the deck.<span id="Orlop_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Orlop_deck" title="Orlop deck">Orlop deck</a></b>: The deck or part of a deck where the <a href="/wiki/Cable" title="Cable">cables</a> are stowed, usually below the <a href="/wiki/Waterline" title="Waterline">waterline</a>. It is the lowest deck in a ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><span id="Poop_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Poop_deck" title="Poop deck">Poop deck</a></b>: The deck forming the roof of a poop or poop cabin, built on the upper deck and extending from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mizzenmast" title="Mizzenmast">mizzenmast</a> aft.<span id="Promenade_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Promenade_deck" title="Promenade deck">Promenade deck</a></b>: A "wrap-around porch" found on passenger ships and <a href="/wiki/Riverboat" title="Riverboat">riverboats</a> encircling the superstructure. This can have open railings or be enclosed in glass, or a combination. Often the entire level where this is located is referred to as the promenade deck. <span id="Quarterdeck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Quarterdeck" title="Quarterdeck">Quarterdeck</a></b>: (a) The part of the upper deck <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abaft" title="Abaft">abaft</a> the mainmast, including the poop deck when there is one. Usually reserved for ship's officers, guests, and passengers. (b) (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) The area to which a gangway for officers and diplomatic guests to board the vessel leads. Also any entry point for personnel.<span id="Side-deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Side-deck" title="Side-deck">Side-deck</a></b>: The upper deck outboard of any structures such as a coachroof or doghouse, also called a <a href="/wiki/Breezeway" title="Breezeway">breezeway</a><span id="Spar_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Spar deck</b>: (a) Same as the upper deck. (b) Sometimes a light deck fitted over the upper deck. On Great Lakes bulk freighters this is the term used for the deck over the cargo holds<span id="Sweep_deck"></span>.</li>
<li><b>Sundeck</b>: Deck onboard passenger watercraft used for Suntanning.</li>
<li><b>Sweep deck</b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) The aftmost deck on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minesweeper_(ship)" title="Minesweeper (ship)">minesweeper</a>, set close to the waterline for ease in launch and recovery of equipment.</li>
<li><b>Topgallant forecastle deck</b>: Any raised deck occurring above the forecastle deck (see above).<span id="Tween_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Tween deck</b>: the storage space between the hold and the main deck, often retractable.<span id="Upper_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Upper deck</b>: The highest deck of the hull, extending from <a href="/wiki/Stem_(ship)" title="Stem (ship)">stem</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>.<span id="Vehicle_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Vehicle deck</b>: (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval" title="Naval">Naval</a></i>) aboard amphibious assault ships the deck or decks used to carry vehicles, aboard civilian ferries and other commercial vessels a deck used for a similar purpose.<span id="Weather_deck"></span></li>
<li><b>Weather deck</b>: (a) Any deck exposed to the outside. (b) The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windward" title="Windward">windward</a> side decks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <span id="Well_deck"></span></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Well_deck" title="Well deck">Well deck</a></b>: (a) An exposed deck (weather deck) lower than decks fore and aft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In particular it is one enclosed by bulwarks limiting flow of water and thus drainage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (b) (<i>Modern naval</i>) A hangar-like deck located at the <a href="/wiki/Waterline" title="Waterline">waterline</a> in the stern of some amphibious assault ships, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Well_dock" title="Well dock">well dock</a>. By taking on water the ship can lower the stern flooding the well deck and allowing boats and <a href="/wiki/Landing_craft" title="Landing craft">landing craft</a> to dock within the ship.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_in_wood">Methods in wood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methods in wood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A traditional <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> deck would consist of planks laid <a href="#Glossary">fore and aft</a> over <a href="#Glossary">beams</a> and along <a href="#Glossary">carlins</a>, the seams of which are <a href="#Glossary">caulked</a> and <a href="#Glossary">paid</a> with <a href="#Glossary">tar</a>. A yacht or other fancy boat might then have the deck canvased, with the <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">fabric</a> laid down in a thick layer of <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> or sealant, and additional coats painted over. The wash or apron boards form the joint between the deck planking and that of the topsides, and are caulked similarly.</p>
<p>Modern "constructed decks" are used primarily on <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a>, composite, and cold-molded hulls. The under structure of beams and carlins is the same as above. The decking itself is usually multiple layers of marine-grade <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a>, covered over with layers of fibreglass in a plastic resin such as <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> overlapped onto the <a href="#Glossary">topsides</a> of the hull.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_in_metal">Methods in metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methods in metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally speaking, the method outlined for "constructed decks" is most similar to <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> decks. The deck <a href="#Glossary">plating</a> is laid over metal <a href="/wiki/Beam_(structure)" title="Beam (structure)">beams</a> and carlins and tacked temporarily in place. The difficulty in metal construction is avoiding distortion of the plate while <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> due to the high <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> involved in the process. Welds are usually double pass, meaning each seam is welded twice, a time consuming process which may take longer than building the wood deck. But welds result in a waterproof deck which is strong and easily repairable. The deck structure is welded to the hull, making it structurally a single unit.</p>
<p>Because a metal deck, painted to reduce <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, can pick up heat from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, and be quite slippery and noisy to work on, a layer of wood decking or thick non-skid paint is often applied to its surface.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_in_fiberglass">Methods in fiberglass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Methods in fiberglass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The process for building a deck in fiberglass is the same as for building a hull: a female mould is built, a layer of <a href="#Glossary">gel coat</a> is sprayed in, then layers of fiberglass in resin are built up to the required deck thickness (if the deck has a <a href="#Glossary">core</a>, the outer skin layers of fiberglass and resin are laid, then the core material, and finally the inner skin layers.) The deck is removed from the mould and usually mechanically fastened to the hull.</p>
<p>Fiberglass decks are quite slick with their mirror-smooth surfaces, so a non-skid texture is often moulded into their surface, or non-skid pads glued down in working areas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules_of_thumb_to_determine_the_deck_scantlings">Rules of thumb to determine the deck scantlings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deck_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rules of thumb to determine the deck scantlings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The thickness of the decking affects how strong the hull is, and is directly related to how thick the skin of the hull itself is, which is of course related to how large the vessel is, the kind of work it is expected to do, and the kind of weather it may reasonably be expected to endure. While a naval engineer or architect may have precise methods of determining what the scantlings should be, traditional builders used previous experiences and simpler rules-of-thumb to determine how thick the deck should be built.</p>
<p>The numbers derived by these formulae gives a rough number for determining the average thickness of materials based on some crude hull measurements.
