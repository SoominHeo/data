<p><i><b>Ashigaru</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">足軽</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"light [of] foot"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> were <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">foot-soldiers</a> employed by the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> class of feudal Japan. The first known reference to <i>ashigaru</i> was in the 14th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but it was during the <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_shogunate" title="Ashikaga shogunate">Ashikaga shogunate</a>–<a href="/wiki/Muromachi_period" title="Muromachi period">Muromachi period</a> that the use of <i>ashigaru</i> became prevalent by various warring factions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashigaru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Attempts were made in Japan by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tenmu" title="Emperor Tenmu">Emperor Tenmu</a> (673–686) to have a conscripted national army, but this did not come about, and by the 10th century Japan instead relied on individual landowners to provide men for conflicts and wars. These horse-owning landowners were the beginnings of the samurai class and the men who worked the land for the landowners became the common foot soldiers during times of war. These foot soldiers could have long ties and loyalty to the landowners which went back many generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Land-owning samurai, together with peasant foot soldiers, fought in many wars and conflicts including the Mongol invasions of Japan in 1274 and 1281. Constant warfare between the 14th and 16th centuries made the hiring of foot soldiers with no particular loyalty necessary at times. Paid only in loot, these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercenaries" title="Mercenaries">mercenaries</a> were not well-trained and thus could not always be depended upon in battle. These wandering foot soldiers eventually became the <i>ashigaru</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons_and_armour">Weapons and armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashigaru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Weapons and armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

A plain undecorated <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)" title="Tanegashima (Japanese matchlock)"><i>Tanegashima</i></a>, this type was used by the <i>ashigaru</i>.</div>
</div>

<p><i>Ashigaru</i> were commonly armed with <i><a href="/wiki/Naginata" title="Naginata">naginata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Yari" title="Yari">yari</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Yumi" title="Yumi">yumi</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_sword" title="Japanese sword">swords</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Ashigaru</i> armour varied depending on the period, from no armour to heavily armored and could consist of conical hats called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jingasa" title="Jingasa">jingasa</a></i> made of lacquered hardened leather or iron, cuirasses (<i>dō</i>), helmets (<i>kabuto</i>), armoured hoods (<i>tatami zukin</i>), armored sleeves (<i>kote</i>), greaves (<i>suneate</i>), and cuisses (<i>haidate</i>).</p>
<p>The warfare of the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a> (15th and 16th centuries) required large quantities of armour to be produced for the ever-growing armies of <i>ashigaru</i>. Simple munition quality<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> cuirasses and helmets were mass-produced including <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Tatami_gusoku" title="commons:Category:Tatami gusoku"><i>tatami</i></a> armour which could be folded or were collapsible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><i>Tatami</i> armour were made from small rectangular or hexagonal iron plates that were usually connected to each other by chainmail and sewn to a cloth backing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 16th century the <i>ashigaru</i> were also armed with <a href="/wiki/Matchlock" title="Matchlock">matchlocks</a> of the type known as <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)" title="Tanegashima (Japanese matchlock)"><i>tanegashima</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Small banners called <i><a href="/wiki/Sashimono" title="Sashimono">sashimono</a></i> could be worn on their backs during battle for identification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cnin_War" title="Ōnin War">Ōnin War</a>, ashigaru gained a reputation as unruly troops when they looted and burned Miyako (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>). In the following <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a> the aspect of the battle changed from single combat to massed formations. Therefore, ashigaru became the backbone of many feudal armies and some of them rose to greater prominence.</p>
<p>Those who were given control of ashigaru were called <i>ashigarugashira</i> (足軽頭) The most famous of them was <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a>, who also raised many of his warrior followers to samurai status. <a href="/wiki/Yamauchi_Kazutoyo" title="Yamauchi Kazutoyo">Yamauchi Kazutoyo</a> was one of such samurai and later daimyo who rose from ashigaru.</p>

<p>Ashigaru formed the backbone of samurai armies in the later periods. The real change for the ashigaru began in 1543 with the introduction of matchlock firearms by the Portuguese. Almost immediately local <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyōs</a></i> started to equip their ashigaru with the new weapon that required little training to use proficiently, as compared to the longbow which took many years to learn. As battles became more complex and forces larger, ashigaru were rigorously trained so that they would hold their ranks in the face of enemy fire.</p>
<p>The advantage of the matchlock guns proved decisive to samurai warfare. This was demonstrated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nagashino" title="Battle of Nagashino">Battle of Nagashino</a> in 1575, where carefully positioned ashigaru gunners of the Oda and Tokugawa clans thwarted the <a href="/wiki/Takeda_clan" title="Takeda clan">Takeda clan</a>'s repeated heavy cavalry charges against the <a href="/wiki/Oda_clan" title="Oda clan">Oda clan</a>'s defensive lines and broke the back of the Takeda war machine.</p>
<p>After the battle, the ashigarus' role in the armies were cemented as a very powerful complement to the samurai. The advantage was used in the two invasions of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> in 1592 and 1597 against the Koreans and later the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming-dynasty Chinese</a>. Though the ratio of the guns (matchlocks) and the bows was 2:1 at the first invasion the ratio became 4:1 at the second invasion since the guns were very effective .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuation_of_conscription">Discontinuation of conscription</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashigaru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discontinuation of conscription">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a> the conscription of ashigaru fell into disuse. Since ashigaru's change to the professional soldier was advanced after <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>, the ashigaru separated from the farmer gradually. When entering the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, the ashigaru's position was fixed and the use of conscripts was abandoned for over two hundred years in Japan. Ashigaru were considered to be of the samurai class in some han (domains), but not in others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
