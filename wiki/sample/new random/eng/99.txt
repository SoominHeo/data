<div class="hatnote" role="note">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is <a href="/wiki/Liu" title="Liu">Liu</a>.</div>
<p><b>Liu Shan</b> (207–271),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> <b>Gongsi</b>, was the second and last emperor of the state of <a href="/wiki/Shu_Han" title="Shu Han">Shu Han</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period. As he ascended the throne at the age of 16, Liu Shan was entrusted to the care of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_(China)" title="Chancellor (China)">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang" title="Zhuge Liang">Zhuge Liang</a> and Imperial Secretariat <a href="/wiki/Li_Yan_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Li Yan (Three Kingdoms)">Li Yan</a>. His reign of 40 years was the longest of all in the Three Kingdoms era. During Liu Shan's reign, many campaigns were led against the rival state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>, primarily by Zhuge Liang and his successor <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Wei" title="Jiang Wei">Jiang Wei</a>, but to little avail. Liu Shan eventually surrendered to Wei in 263 after <a href="/wiki/Deng_Ai" title="Deng Ai">Deng Ai</a> led a surprise attack on the Shu capital <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a>. He was quickly relocated to <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>, capital of Wei, and enfeoffed as "Duke Anle". There he enjoyed his last years peacefully before dying, most probably of natural causes, in 271.</p>
<p>Widely known by his infant name "<b>Adou / Edou</b>" (阿斗), Liu Shan was commonly perceived as an incapable, even mentally handicapped ruler. He was also accused of indulging in pleasures while neglecting state affairs. Some critics, however, believe that Liu Shan, like his father <a href="/wiki/Liu_Bei" title="Liu Bei">Liu Bei</a>, had excellent people management skills, being able to balance the interests of the two major factions in his court, headed respectively by Zhuge Liang and Li Yan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These critics also attribute political stability of his court compared to the courts of the other two states of the Three Kingdoms to Liu Shan's people management skills. They also praise Liu Shan for feigning incompetence in the most ingenious and natural way after the fall of Shu so as to avoid personal harm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Nevertheless, the name "Adou" is today still commonly used in Chinese to describe incapable people who would not achieve anything even with significant assistance.</p>
<p>Liu Shan's given name, when combined with that of <a href="/wiki/Liu_Feng" title="Liu Feng">Liu Feng</a>, whom Liu Bei adopted before Liu Shan's birth, would become "<i>fengshan</i>" (封禪), meaning "to ascend the throne in a ceremony". Scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Yi_Zhongtian" title="Yi Zhongtian">Yi Zhongtian</a> believe that this is a subtle indication of Liu Bei's (hidden) ambition to become an emperor, even long before the abdication of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xian_of_Han" title="Emperor Xian of Han">Emperor Xian</a>, the last ruler of the Han Dynasty. This undermines Liu Bei's claim that he was forced to declare himself emperor so as to carry on the lineage of the Han Dynasty.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Chen_Shou" title="Chen Shou">Chen Shou</a>, the author of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Records_of_Three_Kingdoms" title="Records of Three Kingdoms">Records of Three Kingdoms</a></i>, noted, contrary to tradition, Zhuge Liang banned official historians at Liu Shan's court, and after Zhuge's death, it appeared Liu did not revive that post. Therefore, many of the events during his reign were unrecorded, causing limited details about Liu Shan's reign in historical records.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Liu Shan was the eldest son of the warlord <a href="/wiki/Liu_Bei" title="Liu Bei">Liu Bei</a> and was born to Liu Bei's concubine <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gan" title="Lady Gan">Lady Gan</a>. In 208, Liu Bei's rival <a href="/wiki/Cao_Cao" title="Cao Cao">Cao Cao</a>, who had by then occupied most of northern China, launched a campaign on <a href="/wiki/Jingzhou_(ancient_China)" title="Jingzhou (ancient China)">Jing Province</a> (covering present-day <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>). During his retreat south, Liu Bei was caught up by an elite cavalry force led by Cao Cao at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Changban" title="Battle of Changban">Battle of Changban</a>, and forced to leave behind Lady Gan and Liu Shan to resume his escape. Liu Bei's general <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Yun" title="Zhao Yun">Zhao Yun</a> stayed behind to protect the family members of Liu Bei. Holding the infant Liu Shan in his arms, Zhao Yun led the mother and child to safety. (It appears likely that Lady Gan had died sometime before 209, because when Liu Bei's wife <a href="/wiki/Lady_Sun" title="Lady Sun">Lady Sun</a> effectively divorced Liu Bei in 211, he was in her custody.)</p>
<p>An alternative story of Liu Shan's early life was given in <i><a href="/wiki/Weil%C3%BCe" title="Weilüe">A Brief History of Wei</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Yu_Huan" title="Yu Huan">Yu Huan</a>. It was said that Liu Shan, then already several years old, was separated from Liu Bei when the latter was attacked by Cao Cao in Xiaopei (小沛; present-day <a href="/wiki/Pei_County" title="Pei County">Pei County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a>) in 200. He somehow landed in <a href="/wiki/Hanzhong" title="Hanzhong">Hanzhong</a> and was sold by human traffickers. Only when Liu Bei declared himself emperor in 221 was Liu Shan reunited with his father. However, this story was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Pei_Songzhi" title="Pei Songzhi">Pei Songzhi</a>, who made annotations to <i>Records of the Three Kingdoms</i>, taking into account various other sources.</p>
<p>After Liu Bei declared himself emperor of <a href="/wiki/Shu_Han" title="Shu Han">Shu Han</a> in 221, Liu Shan was formally made the crown prince. In the following year, Liu Bei left the capital <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a> on a campaign against <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a>, who had sent his general <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BC_Meng" title="Lü Meng">Lü Meng</a> to <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BC_Meng%27s_invasion_of_Jing_Province" title="Lü Meng's invasion of Jing Province">invade and seize Jing Province</a> from Liu Bei in 219. Liu Bei was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Xiaoting" title="Battle of Xiaoting">Battle of Xiaoting</a> and, having retreated to the city of <a href="/wiki/Baidicheng" title="Baidicheng">Baidicheng</a>, eventually died in 223. Before his death, Liu Bei entrusted the young Liu Shan to the care of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_(China)" title="Chancellor (China)">chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang" title="Zhuge Liang">Zhuge Liang</a>. Liu Bei even told Zhuge Liang to take the throne if Liu Shan proved to be incapable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zhuge_Liang.27s_regency">Zhuge Liang's regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Zhuge Liang's regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the early years of his reign, Liu Shan was not an unwise ruler. While Zhuge Liang was alive, Liu Shan treated him as a father figure, allowing Zhuge to handle all state affairs. Zhuge Liang recommended many trusted officials, including <a href="/wiki/Fei_Yi" title="Fei Yi">Fei Yi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dong_Yun" title="Dong Yun">Dong Yun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guo_Youzhi" title="Guo Youzhi">Guo Youzhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xiang_Chong_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Xiang Chong (Three Kingdoms)">Xiang Chong</a> into key positions. Under Zhuge Liang's advice, Liu Shan entered into an alliance with the state of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wu" title="Eastern Wu">Eastern Wu</a>, helping both states to survive against the much larger state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>. During Zhuge Liang's regency, the government was largely efficient and not corrupt, allowing the relatively small state of Shu to prepare itself for military campaigns.</p>
<p>In 223, Liu Shan married <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Fei" title="Zhang Fei">Zhang Fei</a>'s daughter, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Zhang_(Liu_Shan%27s_first_wife)" title="Empress Zhang (Liu Shan's first wife)">Empress Zhang</a>.</p>
<p>In the aftermath of Liu Bei's death, the southern <a href="/wiki/Nanman" title="Nanman">Nanman</a> tribes had peeled away from Shu dominion. In 225, Zhuge Liang <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang%27s_Southern_Campaign" title="Zhuge Liang's Southern Campaign">headed south</a> and was able to, by both military victories and persuasion, reintegrate the southern region into the empire. For the rest of Zhuge Liang's regency, the southern Nanman people would be key contributors to Shu's campaigns against Wei.</p>
<p>Starting in 227, Zhuge Liang launched his five <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang%27s_Northern_Expeditions" title="Zhuge Liang's Northern Expeditions">Northern Expeditions</a> against Wei, but all except one were military failures (albeit not military disasters) in that his forces ran out of food before they were able to inflict significant damage on Wei and therefore were forced to withdraw. It was during one of Zhuge Liang's campaigns that the only real political crisis during Zhuge's regency would occur. In 231, unable to supply the troops sufficiently, Zhuge Liang's co-regent <a href="/wiki/Li_Yan_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Li Yan (Three Kingdoms)">Li Yan</a> forged an edict by Liu Shan, ordering Zhuge Liang to retreat. When Zhuge Liang discovered this, he recommended that Li Yan be removed from his office and put under house arrest, and Liu Shan accepted the recommendation.</p>
<p>In 234, while Zhuge Liang was on his final campaign against Wei, he grew seriously ill. Hearing about Zhuge's illness, Liu Shan sent his secretary Li Fu (李福) to the front line to visit Zhuge Liang and to request Zhuge to leave instructions on important state matters. Among other things, Zhuge Liang recommended that <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Wan" title="Jiang Wan">Jiang Wan</a> succeed him, and that Fei Yi succeed Jiang Wan. Zhuge Liang refused to answer Li Fu's next question — who should succeed Fei Yi. Zhuge Liang died soon thereafter. Liu Shan followed Zhuge's instructions and installed Jiang Wan as the new regent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jiang_Wan.27s_regency">Jiang Wan's regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jiang Wan's regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jiang Wan was a capable administrator, and he continued Zhuge Liang's domestic policies, leaving the government largely efficient. He was also known for his tolerance of dissension and his humility. Not having much military aptitude, however, he soon abandoned Zhuge Liang's aggressive foreign policy towards Wei, and indeed in 241 withdrew most of the troops from the important border city of <a href="/wiki/Hanzhong" title="Hanzhong">Hanzhong</a> to Fu County (涪縣; in present-day <a href="/wiki/Mianyang" title="Mianyang">Mianyang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>). From that point on, Shu was generally in a defensive posture and no longer posed a threat to Wei. (This was in fact misinterpreted by many Wu officials as a sign that Shu was abandoning the alliance and entering into a treaty with Wei, but was correctly read by Wu's emperor Sun Quan as merely a sign of weakness, not an abandonment of the alliance.)</p>
<p>In 237, Empress Zhang died. That year, Liu Shan took her younger sister as a consort, and in 238 created her empress. Her title remained the same as her sister, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Zhang_(Liu_Shan%27s_second_wife)" title="Empress Zhang (Liu Shan's second wife)">Empress Zhang</a>.</p>
<p>In 243, Jiang Wan grew ill and transferred most of his authority to Fei Yi and Fei's assistant <a href="/wiki/Dong_Yun" title="Dong Yun">Dong Yun</a>. In 244, when Wei's regent <a href="/wiki/Cao_Shuang" title="Cao Shuang">Cao Shuang</a> attacked Hanzhong, it was Fei Yi who led the troops against Cao Shuang and dealt Wei a major defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Xingshi" title="Battle of Xingshi">Battle of Xingshi</a>. Jiang Wan, however, remained influential until his death in 245. Soon after Jiang Wan's death, Dong Yun also died — allowing the eunuch <a href="/wiki/Huang_Hao" title="Huang Hao">Huang Hao</a>, a favourite of Liu Shan's, whose power Dong Yun had curbed, to start aggrandising his power. Huang Hao was viewed as corrupt and highly manipulative in domestic matters, and the governmental efficiency that was achieved during Zhuge Liang's and Jiang Wan's regencies began to deteriorate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fei_Yi.27s_regency">Fei Yi's regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fei Yi's regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Jiang Wan and Dong Yun's deaths, Liu Shan named <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Wei" title="Jiang Wei">Jiang Wei</a> as Fei Yi's assistant, but both were largely involved only in military matters, as Liu Shan gradually became more self-assertive in non-military matters. It was also around this time that he became more interested in touring the countryside and increasing the use of luxury items, both of which added stress on the treasury, albeit not cripplingly so. Jiang Wei was interested in resuming Zhuge Liang's policies of attacking Wei aggressively, a strategy that Fei Yi partially agreed with — as he allowed Jiang Wei to make raids on Wei's borders, but never gave him a large amount of troops, reasoning that Shu was in no position for a major military confrontation with Wei.</p>
<p>In 253, Fei Yi was assassinated by the general Guo Xun (郭循) -- a former Wei general who had been forced to surrender but who secretly maintained his loyalty to Wei. Fei Yi's death left Jiang Wei as the <i>de facto</i> regent, but with a power vacuum in domestic affairs, as Jiang Wei continued to be on the borders, conducting campaigns against Wei. Huang Hao's influence increased greatly as a result.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jiang_Wei.27s_semi-regency">Jiang Wei's semi-regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jiang Wei's semi-regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Fei Yi's death, Jiang Wei assumed command of Shu's troops and began a number of <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Wei%27s_Northern_Expeditions" title="Jiang Wei's Northern Expeditions">campaigns against Wei</a>—but while they were troubling to the Wei regents <a href="/wiki/Sima_Shi" title="Sima Shi">Sima Shi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a>, the attacks largely inflicted no real damage against Wei, as Jiang Wei's campaigns were plagued by one problem that had plagued Zhuge Liang's -- the lack of adequate food supply—and largely had to be terminated after a short duration. These campaigns instead had a detrimental effect on Shu, whose government no longer had the efficiency that it had during Zhuge Liang's and Jiang Wan's regencies, and therefore was unable to cope with the drain of resources that Jiang Wei's campaigns were having.</p>
<p>In 253, Jiang Wei made a coordinated attack on Wei, along with Wu's regent <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Ke" title="Zhuge Ke">Zhuge Ke</a>, but was eventually forced to withdraw after his troops ran out of food supplies — allowing Sima Shi to concentrate against Zhuge Ke, dealing Wu forces a devastating defeat that eventually caused so much resentment that Zhuge Ke was assassinated. This was the last of the coordinated attacks by Shu and Wu on Wei in the duration of the Shu-Wu alliance.</p>
<p>In 255, on one of Jiang Wei's campaigns, he dealt Wei forces a major defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Didao" title="Battle of Didao">Battle of Didao</a>, nearly capturing the important Wei border city Didao (狄道; in present-day <a href="/wiki/Dingxi" title="Dingxi">Dingxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>), but in 256, as he tried to again confront the Wei forces, he was instead dealt a defeat by <a href="/wiki/Deng_Ai" title="Deng Ai">Deng Ai</a>, and this was a fairly devastating loss that left Jiang Wei with a weakened standing with the people. Many officials now openly questioned Jiang Wei's strategy, but Liu Shan took no actions to stop Jiang. Further, in 259, under Jiang Wei's suggestion, Liu Shan approved a plan where the main troops were withdrawn from major border cities to try to induce a Wei attack, with troops positioned in such a way as to intend a trapping of the Wei troops — a strategy that would be used several years later, in 263, when Wei did attack, but which would prove to be a failure.</p>
<p>By 261, Huang Hao's power appeared paramount. Among the key domestic officials, only <a href="/wiki/Dong_Jue" title="Dong Jue">Dong Jue</a> and Zhuge Liang's son <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Zhan" title="Zhuge Zhan">Zhuge Zhan</a> were able to maintain their posts without flattering Huang Hao. In 262, Huang Hao would in fact try to remove Jiang Wei and replace him with his friend <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yan_Yu_(Shu_Han)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yan Yu (Shu Han) (page does not exist)">Yan Yu</a> (閻宇). Upon hearing this, Jiang Wei advised Liu Shan to execute Huang Hao, but the emperor denied the request, saying that the eunuch was but a servant who ran errands. Fearing retaliation, Jiang Wei left Chengdu to garrison troops at Tazhong (沓中; northwest of present-day <a href="/wiki/Zhugqu_County" title="Zhugqu County">Zhugqu County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>).</p>
<p>According to the Wu ambassador <a href="/wiki/Xue_Xu" title="Xue Xu">Xue Xu</a>, who visited Shu in 261 at the order of the Wu emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_Xiu_(emperor)" title="Sun Xiu (emperor)">Sun Xiu</a>, the status that Shu was in at this point was:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>The emperor is incompetent and does not know his errors; his subordinates just try to get by without causing trouble for themselves. When I was visiting them, I heard no honest words, and when I visited their countryside, the people looked hungry. I have heard of a story of swallows and sparrows making nests on top of mansions and being content, believing that it was the safest place, not realising that the haystack and the support beams were on fire and that disaster was about to come. This might be what they are like.</i></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_of_Shu">Fall of Shu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fall of Shu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Shu_by_Wei" title="Conquest of Shu by Wei">Conquest of Shu by Wei</a></div>
<p>In 262, aggravated by <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Wei" title="Jiang Wei">Jiang Wei</a>'s constant attacks, Wei's regent Sima Zhao planned to carry out a major campaign to terminate the Shu threat once and for all. Upon hearing rumours of this plan, Jiang Wei submitted a request to Liu Shan, warning him about the mustering of Wei troops under the generals <a href="/wiki/Deng_Ai" title="Deng Ai">Deng Ai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Xu" title="Zhuge Xu">Zhuge Xu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zhong_Hui" title="Zhong Hui">Zhong Hui</a> near the border. However, <a href="/wiki/Huang_Hao" title="Huang Hao">Huang Hao</a> persuaded Liu Shan with fortunetelling to take no action on Jiang Wei's requests for war preparations.</p>
<p>In 263, Sima Zhao launched his attacks, led by Deng Ai, Zhuge Xu, and Zhong Hui. Liu Shan followed Jiang Wei's previous plans and ordered the border troops to withdraw and prepare to trap Wei forces, rather than to confront them directly. The plan, however, had a fatal flaw — it assumed that Wei forces would siege the border cities, which, instead, Deng Ai and Zhong Hui ignored, and they advanced instead on Yang'an Pass (陽安關; in present-day <a href="/wiki/Hanzhong" title="Hanzhong">Hanzhong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>), capturing it. Jiang Wei was able to meet their troops and initially repel them, but Deng Ai led his army through a treacherous mountain pass and deep into Shu territory. There he launched a surprise attack on Jiangyou (江油; in present-day <a href="/wiki/Mianyang" title="Mianyang">Mianyang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>). After defeating <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Zhan" title="Zhuge Zhan">Zhuge Zhan</a> there, Deng Ai had virtually no Shu troops left between his army and the Shu capital <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a>. Faced with the prospect of defending Chengdu against Deng Ai's troops with no defences, Liu Shan took the advice of Secretary <a href="/wiki/Qiao_Zhou" title="Qiao Zhou">Qiao Zhou</a> and promptly surrendered. While the surrender was criticised by many, the historian Wang Yin (王隱), in his <i>Records of Shu</i> (蜀記), described the move as a policy that placed the welfare of the people on top.</p>
<p>In 264, Zhong Hui would carry out an <a href="/wiki/Zhong_Hui%27s_Rebellion" title="Zhong Hui's Rebellion">attempt to seize power</a> — which Jiang Wei, who had surrendered to Zhong Hui, tried to take advantage of to revive Shu. He advised Zhong Hui to falsely accuse Deng Ai of treason and arrest him, and, with their combined troops, rebel against Sima Zhao. Zhong Hui did so, and Jiang Wei planned to next kill Zhong Hui and his followers, and then redeclare Shu's independence under emperor Liu Shan, and had in fact written Liu Shan to inform him of those plans. However, Zhong Hui's troops rebelled against him, and both Jiang Wei and Zhong Hui were killed in battle. Liu Shan himself was not harmed in the disturbance, although his crown prince <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xuan_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Liu Xuan (Three Kingdoms)">Liu Xuan</a> was killed in the confusion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_the_fall_of_Shu">Life after the fall of Shu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Shan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life after the fall of Shu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 264, Liu Shan and his entire family was relocated to the Wei capital <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>. He was enfeoffed as the "Duke of Anle" (安樂公; literally meaning "duke of peace and comfort") while his sons and grandsons became marquises. This practice was referred to as <i>er wang san ke</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%BA%8C%E7%8E%8B%E4%B8%89%E6%81%AA" title="zh:二王三恪">二王三恪</a>). According to the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_and_Autumn_Annals_of_Han_and_Jin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spring and Autumn Annals of Han and Jin (page does not exist)">Spring and Autumn Annals of Han and Jin</a></i> (漢晉春秋) by <a href="/wiki/Xi_Zuochi" title="Xi Zuochi">Xi Zuochi</a>, one day, the Wei regent <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a> invited Liu Shan and his followers to a feast, during which Sima Zhao arranged to have entertainers perform traditional Shu music and dance. The former Shu officials present were all saddened, but Liu Shan was visibly unmoved. When asked by Sima Zhao if he missed his former state, Liu Shan replied:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I enjoy life here and do not think of Shu at all. (此間樂，不思蜀)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This phrase became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_idiom" title="Chinese idiom">Chinese idiom</a> - <i>le bu si shu</i> (樂不思蜀; literally meaning "too joyful to think about home", but often with a negative implication). This was noted by Sima Zhao as a sign that Liu Shan was an incompetent fool; however, some later historians believed that it showed Liu Shan's wisdom in intentionally displaying a lack of ambition so that Sima Zhao would not view him as a threat.</p>
<p>Liu Shan died in 271 in Luoyang, and was given the posthumous name "Duke Si of Anle" (安樂思公; literally "the deep-thinking duke of Anle"). His dukedom lasted several generations during Wei's successor state, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265-420)">Jin Dynasty</a>, before being extinguished in the turmoils caused by the <a href="/wiki/Five_Barbarians" title="Five Barbarians">Wu Hu</a>. <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yuan_(Han_Zhao)" title="Liu Yuan (Han Zhao)">Liu Yuan</a>, the founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Zhao" title="Han Zhao">Han Zhao</a>, one of the states in the <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Kingdoms" title="Sixteen Kingdoms">Sixteen Kingdoms</a>, claimed to be a legitimate successor of the Han Dynasty. He gave Liu Shan the posthumous name "Emperor Xiaohuai" (孝懷皇帝; literally: "the filial and kind emperor").</p>

<p>Liu Shan appears as a character in the historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Romance of the Three Kingdoms">Romance of the Three Kingdoms</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Luo_Guanzhong" title="Luo Guanzhong">Luo Guanzhong</a>, which romanticises the historical events before and during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period. In the novel, Liu Shan is generally portrayed as an incapable ruler who was easily swayed by words, especially those from the eunuch <a href="/wiki/Huang_Hao" title="Huang Hao">Huang Hao</a>, whom he favoured.</p>
