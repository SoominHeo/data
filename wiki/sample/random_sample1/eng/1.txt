<p><b>The Million Dollar Homepage</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">website</a> conceived in 2005 by Alex Tew, a student from <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, England, to raise money for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_education" title="University education">university education</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Home_page" title="Home page">home page</a> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Million" title="Million">million</a> <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a> arranged in a <span class="nowrap">1000 × 1000</span> pixel grid; the image-based links on it were sold for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>1 per pixel in <span class="nowrap">10 × 10</span> blocks. The purchasers of these pixel blocks provided tiny images to be displayed on them, a <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URL</a> to which the images were linked, and a slogan to be displayed when hovering a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cursor_(computers)" title="Cursor (computers)">cursor</a> over the link. The aim of the website was to sell all of the pixels in the image, thus generating a million dollars of income for the creator. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> has commented that the site inspired other websites that sell pixels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Launched on 26 August 2005, the website became an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_phenomenon" title="Internet phenomenon">Internet phenomenon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa</a> ranking of <a href="/wiki/Web_traffic" title="Web traffic">web traffic</a> peaked at around 127; as of 9 May 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Million_Dollar_Homepage&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it is 40,044.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On 1 January 2006, the final 1,000 pixels were put up for auction on <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>. The auction closed on 11 January with a winning bid of $38,100 that brought the final tally to $1,037,100 in gross income.</p>
<p>During the January 2006 auction, the website was subject to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distributed_denial-of-service_attack" title="Distributed denial-of-service attack">distributed denial-of-service attack</a> (DDOS) and ransom demand, which left it inaccessible to visitors for a week while its security system was upgraded. The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Constabulary" title="Wiltshire Constabulary">Wiltshire Constabulary</a> investigated the attack and extortion attempt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_DDoS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_DDoS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Million_Dollar_Homepage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:33%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>From the outset I knew the idea had potential, but it was one of those things that could have gone either way. My thinking was I had nothing to lose (apart from the 50 Euros or so it cost to register the domain and setup the hosting). I knew that the idea was quirky enough to create interest ... The Internet is a very powerful medium.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Alex Tew, 22 February 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Alex Tew, a student from <a href="/wiki/Cricklade" title="Cricklade">Cricklade</a> in Wiltshire, England, conceived The Million Dollar Homepage in August 2005 when he was 21 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> He was about to begin a three-year Business Management course at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University of Nottingham</a>, and was concerned that he would be left with a <a href="/wiki/Student_loans_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Student loans in the United Kingdom">student loan</a> that could take years to repay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> As a money-raising idea, Tew decided to sell a million pixels on a website for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>1 each; purchasers would add their own image, <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> or advertisement, and have the option of including a <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlink</a> to their website. Pixels were sold for US dollars rather than <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">UK pounds</a>; the US has a larger online population than the UK, and Tew believed more people would relate to the concept if the pixels were sold in US currency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2005, the pound was <a href="/wiki/Hard_currency" title="Hard currency">strong</a> against the dollar: £1 was worth approximately $1.80,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and that cost per pixel may have been too expensive for many potential buyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-9">[9]</a></sup> Tew's setup costs were <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>50, which paid for the registration of the domain name and a basic web-hosting package. The website went live on 26 August 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_mag_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_mag-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homepage" title="Homepage">homepage</a> featured a <a href="/wiki/Web_banner" title="Web banner">Web banner</a> with the site's name and a pixel counter displaying the number of pixels sold, a <a href="/wiki/Navigation_bar" title="Navigation bar">navigation bar</a> containing nine small links to the site's internal <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web pages</a>, and an empty square grid of 1,000,000 pixels divided into 10,000 100-pixel blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Tew promised customers that the site would remain online for at least five years – that is, until at least 26 August 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pixel_sales">Pixel sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Million_Dollar_Homepage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pixel sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because individual pixels are too small to be seen easily, Pixels were sold in 100-pixel "blocks" measuring <span class="nowrap">10 × 10</span> pixels; the minimum price was thus $100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-16">[16]</a></sup> The first sale, three days after the site began operating, was to an online music website operated by a friend of Tew's. He bought 400 pixels in a <span class="nowrap">20 × 20</span> block. After two weeks, Tew's friends and family members had purchased a total of 4,700 pixels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> The site was initially marketed only through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_of_mouth_marketing" title="Word of mouth marketing">word of mouth</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2-3">[3]</a></sup> however, after the site had made $1,000, a <a href="/wiki/Press_release" title="Press release">press release</a> was sent out that was picked up by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> The technology news website <a href="/wiki/The_Register" title="The Register">The Register</a> featured two articles on The Million Dollar Homepage in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Register-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> By the end of the month, The Million Dollar Homepage had received $250,000 and was ranked Number 3 on <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa Internet</a>'s list of "Movers and Shakers" behind the websites for <a href="/wiki/Britney_Spears" title="Britney Spears">Britney Spears</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Photo_District_News" title="Photo District News">Photo District News</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On 6 October, Tew reported the site received 65,000 unique visitors; it received 1465 <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">Diggs</a>, becoming one of the most Dugg links that week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Eleven days later, the number had increased to 100,000 unique visitors. On 26 October, two months after the Million Dollar Homepage was launched, more than 500,900 pixels had been sold to 1,400 customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blog1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blog1-22">[22]</a></sup> By New Year's Eve, Tew reported that the site was receiving hits from 25,000 unique visitors every hour and had an <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa</a> Rank of 127,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blog1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blog1-22">[22]</a></sup> and that 999,000 of the 1,000,000 pixels had been sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 January 2006, Tew announced that because the demand was so great for the last 1,000 pixels, "the most fair and logical thing" to do was auction them on <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> rather than lose "the integrity and degree of exclusivity intrinsic to the million-pixel concept" by launching a second Million Dollar Homepage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blog1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blog1-22">[22]</a></sup> The auction lasted ten days and received 99 legitimate bids. Although bids were received for amounts as high as $160,109.99, many were either retracted by the bidders or cancelled as hoaxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Startup_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Startup-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blog_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blog-24">[24]</a></sup> "I actually contacted the people by phone and turns out they weren't serious, which is fairly frustrating, so I removed those bidders at the last minute", said Tew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Startup_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Startup-23">[23]</a></sup> The winning bid was $38,100,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> placed by MillionDollarWeightLoss.com, an online store selling diet-related products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Tew remarked that he had expected the final bid amount to be higher due to the media attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Startup_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Startup-23">[23]</a></sup> The Million Dollar Homepage made a <a href="/wiki/Gross_(economics)" title="Gross (economics)">gross</a> total of $1,037,100 in five months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blog_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blog-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> After costs, taxes and a donation to <a href="/wiki/The_Prince%27s_Trust" title="The Prince's Trust">The Prince's Trust</a>, a charity for young people, Tew expected his <a href="/wiki/Net_(economics)" title="Net (economics)">net</a> income to be $650,000–$700,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ2_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_attention">Media attention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Million_Dollar_Homepage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Media attention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the September press release that first brought attention to the site, The Million Dollar Homepage was featured in articles on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Online" title="BBC Online">BBC Online</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Register" title="The Register">The Register</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Register-18">[18]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-29">[29]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/PC_Pro" title="PC Pro">PC Pro</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Tew also appeared on the national <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_television" title="Breakfast television">breakfast television</a> programmes <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_News_Sunrise" title="Sky News Sunrise">Sky News Sunrise</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/BBC_Breakfast" title="BBC Breakfast">BBC Breakfast</a></i> to discuss the website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:33%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>The crucial thing in creating the media interest was the idea itself: it was unique and quirky enough to stand out. I only had to push the idea a bit in the first few days by sending out a press release which essentially acted as a catalyst. This interest coupled with traditional word-of-mouth created a real buzz about the homepage, which in turn created more interest.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Alex Tew, 22 February 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FT-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>By November the website was becoming popular around the world, receiving attention from <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times_Deutschland" title="Financial Times Deutschland">Financial Times Deutschland</a></i> in Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Television_New_Zealand" title="Television New Zealand">TVNZ</a> in New Zealand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Terra_Networks" title="Terra Networks">Terra Networks</a> in Latin America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> the <i><a href="/wiki/China_Daily" title="China Daily">China Daily</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and especially in the United States where it was covered in <i><a href="/wiki/Adweek" title="Adweek">Adweek</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Florida_Today" title="Florida Today">Florida Today</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Florida-38">[38]</a></sup> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-2">[2]</a></sup> Tew hired a US-based publicist to help with the attention from the American media and made a week-long trip to the US, where he was interviewed on <a href="/wiki/ABC_News_Radio" title="ABC News Radio">ABC News Radio</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News Channel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Attack_of_the_Show!" title="Attack of the Show!">Attack of the Show!</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and local news programmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept was described as "simple and brilliant",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Florida-38">[38]</a></sup> "clever",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> "ingenious",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> and "a unique platform [for advertising] which is also a bit of fun".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-29">[29]</a></sup> Professor Martin Binks, director of the Nottingham University Institute for Entrepreneurial Innovation, said, "It is brilliant in its simplicity ... advertisers have been attracted to it by its novelty ... the site has become a phenomenon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">Popular Mechanics</a></i> said, "There's no content. No cool graphics, giveaways or steamy Paris Hilton videos for viewers to salivate over. Imagine a TV channel that shows nothing but commercials, a magazine with nothing but ads. That's The Million Dollar Homepage. An astonishing example of the power of <a href="/wiki/Viral_marketing" title="Viral marketing">viral marketing</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Don Oldenburg of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> was one of the few without praise for the site, calling it a "cheap, mind-bogglingly lucrative marketing monstrosity, an advertising badlands of spam, banner ads and pop-ups."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-16">[16]</a></sup> Oldenburg continues, "it looks like a bulletin board on designer steroids, an advertising train wreck you can't <i>not</i> look at. It's like getting every pop-up ad you ever got in your life, at once. It's the Internet equivalent of suddenly feeling like you want to take a shower."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the final pixels were being auctioned, Tew was interviewed on <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_%26_Judy" title="Richard &amp; Judy">Richard &amp; Judy</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and profiled in the online <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_News_Magazine" title="BBC News Magazine">BBC News Magazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_mag_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_mag-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>The Wall Street Journal</i> wrote about The Million Dollar Homepage and its impact on the Internet community. "Mr. Tew himself has taken on celebrity status in the Internet community ... the creative juice ... paints an interesting picture of online entrepreneurship".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ2_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tew dropped out of the business degree the site was set up to fund after one term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In 2008, Tew founded Popjam, an Internet aggregation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_networking" title="Social networking">social networking</a> business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DDoS_attack">DDoS attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Million_Dollar_Homepage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: DDoS attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 7 January 2006, three days before the auction of the final 1,000 pixels was due to end, Tew received an e-mail from an organisation called "The Dark Group", and was told The Million Dollar Homepage would become the victim of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distributed_denial-of-service_attack" title="Distributed denial-of-service attack">distributed denial-of-service attack</a> (DDoS) if a ransom of $5,000 was not paid by 10 January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Financial_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Financial-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tgraph_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tgraph-51">[51]</a></sup> Believing the threat to be a hoax, he ignored it, but a week later received a second e-mail threat: "Hello u website is under us atack to stop the DDoS send us 50000$."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Financial_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Financial-50">[50]</a></sup> Again, he ignored the threat, and the website was flooded with extra traffic and e-mails, causing it to <a href="/wiki/Crash_(computing)" title="Crash (computing)">crash</a>. "I haven't replied to any of them as I don't want to give them the satisfaction and I certainly don't intend to pay them any money. What is happening to my website is like terrorism. If you pay them, new attacks will start," Tew said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_DDoS_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_DDoS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The website was inaccessible to visitors for a week until the host server upgraded the security system, and filtered traffic through anti-DDoS software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_DDoS_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_DDoS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tgraph_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tgraph-51">[51]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Constabulary" title="Wiltshire Constabulary">Wiltshire Constabulary</a>'s Hi-Tech Crime Unit and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> were called to investigate the extortion and attack; they believed it originated in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_DDoS_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_DDoS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
