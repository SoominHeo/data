<p><b>FX</b> (originally an initialism of "<b>F</b>ox E<b>x</b>tended", pronounced and suggesting "<a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">effects</a>") is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">basic cable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_channel" title="Television channel">channel</a> launched on June 1, 1994, based in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles, California</a> and owned by <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Fox" title="21st Century Fox">21st Century Fox</a> through FX Networks, LLC. FX's programming primarily includes original drama and comedy series (which aspire to the standards of <a href="/wiki/Pay_television" title="Pay television">premium cable</a> channels like <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a>, in regard to adult themes and higher-quality writing/directing/acting), and <a href="/wiki/Rerun" title="Rerun">reruns</a> of theatrical films and "broadcast network" <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcoms</a>.</p>
<p>As of July 2015, FX is available to approximately 94,006,000 pay television households (80.8% of households with at least one television set) in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> In addition to the flagship U.S. network, the "FX" name is licensed to a number of related <a href="/wiki/Multichannel_video_programming_distributor" title="Multichannel video programming distributor">pay television</a> channels in various countries around the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1994.E2.80.9397:_Early_years">1994–97: Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1994–97: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FX, originally stylized as "fX", launched on June 1, 1994. Broadcasting from a large "<a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatiron_District,_Manhattan" title="Flatiron District, Manhattan">Flatiron District</a>, fX was one of the first forays into large-scale <a href="/wiki/Interactive_television" title="Interactive television">interactive television</a>. The channel centered on original programming, which was broadcast live every day from the "fX Apartment," and rebroadcasts of classic television shows from the 1960s, 1970s and 1980s, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_(TV_series)" title="Batman (TV series)">Batman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman_(TV_series)" title="Wonder Woman (TV series)">Wonder Woman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eight_Is_Enough" title="Eight Is Enough">Eight Is Enough</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nanny_and_the_Professor" title="Nanny and the Professor">Nanny and the Professor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Hornet_(TV_series)" title="The Green Hornet (TV series)">The Green Hornet</a></i>. fX had two taglines during this period: "TV Made Fresh Daily" and "The World's First Living Television Network".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The "f" in the channel's name and logo was rendered in lower-case to portray a type of relaxed friendliness; the stylized "X" represented the channel's roots: the crossing searchlights of the <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> logo.</p>
<p>The live shows were each mostly focused on one broad topic. Shows included <i>Personal fX</i> (collectibles and antiques), <i>The Pet Department</i> (pets), <i>Under Scrutiny with <a href="/wiki/Jane_Wallace_(journalist)" title="Jane Wallace (journalist)">Jane Wallace</a></i> (news) and <i>Sound fX</i> (music). The channel's flagship show, <i>Breakfast Time</i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Hibberd" title="Laurie Hibberd">Laurie Hibberd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bergeron" title="Tom Bergeron">Tom Bergeron</a>, was formatted like an informal magazine program and was an Americanized version of Great Britain's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Breakfast" title="The Big Breakfast">The Big Breakfast</a></i>. <i>Breakfast Time</i> and <i>Personal fX</i> would regularly feature the channel's "roving reporters" – which included <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Whang" title="Suzanne Whang">Suzanne Whang</a>, John Burke and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Keoghan" title="Phil Keoghan">Phil Keoghan</a> – visiting unique places around the United States live via satellite. Other notable fX personalities included <a href="/wiki/Karyn_Bryant" title="Karyn Bryant">Karyn Bryant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Jones" title="Orlando Jones">Orlando Jones</a>, who were panelists on <i>Sound fX</i>.</p>
<p>The channel prided itself on its interactivity with viewers. fX, in 1994, was an early adopter of the internet, embracing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> as methods of feedback. Most of the shows would feature instant responses to e-mailed questions, and one show, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backchat" title="Backchat">Backchat</a></i> (hosted by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Probst" title="Jeff Probst">Jeff Probst</a>), was exclusively devoted to responding to viewer mail, whether sent through e-mail or traditional postal mail. Select viewers were allowed to spend a day at the "apartment" and take part in all of the channel's shows. Inside the channel's syndicated programming blocks, channel hosts would frequently appear during commercial breaks to read news headlines, respond to e-mails from viewers about the episode that was airing, or to promote upcoming programming.</p>

<p>The first incarnation of fX was not available on <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Cable" title="Time Warner Cable">Time Warner Cable</a>, one of the major cable systems in New York City, where its programming originated. TWC would not carry the channel until September 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The live shows gradually disappeared one by one until only <i>Personal fX</i> remained. <i>Breakfast Time</i> was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> network and renamed <i>Fox After Breakfast</i> in mid-1996. It underwent several format changes, but never found a substantial audience and was canceled less than a year later. By the time that all live programming (with the exception of <i>Personal fX</i>) was dropped, the channel focused entirely on its classic television shows until its relaunch in mid-1997. <i>Personal fX</i> remained on the refocused FX until May 1, 1998. FX vacated the "apartment" in the summer of 1998 and the channel's operations were streamlined with the other Fox-owned cable channels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1997.E2.80.932001:_Fox_Gone_Cable">1997–2001: Fox Gone Cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1997–2001: Fox Gone Cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1997, fX was relaunched as "FX: Fox Gone Cable",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_faces_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-two_faces-6">[5]</a></sup> refocusing the channel's target audience towards men aged 18 to 49. During the first few years after its relaunch, FX was known for little else than airing reruns of such Fox shows as <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Married..._with_Children" title="Married... with Children">Married... with Children</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>-produced shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/M*A*S*H_(TV_series)" title="M*A*S*H (TV series)">M*A*S*H</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_(TV_series)" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer (TV series)">Buffy the Vampire Slayer</a></i>. The channel also added <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> games to its lineup at that time (at one point sharing rights with then-sister network <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_networks_preceding_ABC_Family" title="Television networks preceding ABC Family">Fox Family</a>), and eventually expanded its sports programming to include <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> races in 2001.</p>
<p>In the summer of 1998, FX debuted three original series: <i><a href="/wiki/Bobcat%27s_Big_Ass_Show" title="Bobcat's Big Ass Show">Bobcat's Big Ass Show</a></i>, <i>Instant Comedy with <a href="/wiki/The_Groundlings" title="The Groundlings">the Groundlings</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Penn_%26_Teller%27s_Sin_City_Spectacular" title="Penn &amp; Teller's Sin City Spectacular">Penn &amp; Teller's Sin City Spectacular</a></i>. All three series were cancelled the following year. Soon after its relaunch, the "Fox Gone Cable" tagline was dropped. By 1999, new original programs were added with the debut of shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Son_of_the_Beach" title="Son of the Beach">Son of the Beach</a></i> (a <i><a href="/wiki/Baywatch" title="Baywatch">Baywatch</a></i> parody that starred <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Stack" title="Timothy Stack">Timothy Stack</a> and was executive produced by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Stern" title="Howard Stern">Howard Stern</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_X_Show" title="The X Show">The X Show</a></i> (a male-oriented late night panel talk show).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> The channel also acquired the cable syndication rights to reruns of series such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal" title="Ally McBeal">Ally McBeal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/NYPD_Blue" title="NYPD Blue">NYPD Blue</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Practice" title="The Practice">The Practice</a></i> for then-record high prices then unseen in the cable industry despite all three <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox_Television" title="20th Century Fox Television">20th Century Fox Television</a> series being under common ownership; when these shows expensively fumbled in primetime, FX predominantly ran movies in its more high-profile time periods, though with the move of premiere film rights from broadcast television to basic cable, FX unexpectedly would end up a benefactor of this change.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002.E2.80.9307:_Emergence_in_original_programming">2002–07: Emergence in original programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2002–07: Emergence in original programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 2002, the channel emerged as a major force in original cable programming, gaining both acclaim and notoriety for edgy dramas. That year, FX debuted the police drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">The Shield</a></i>, which became a breakout hit. This trend continued the following year with <i><a href="/wiki/Nip/Tuck" title="Nip/Tuck">Nip/Tuck</a></i>, a drama about two plastic surgeons, and the <a href="/wiki/Denis_Leary" title="Denis Leary">Denis Leary</a>-helmed <i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_Me_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Rescue Me (U.S. TV series)">Rescue Me</a></i>, about the lives of a crew of firemen from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Fire_Department" title="New York City Fire Department">New York City Fire Department</a> post-<a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a>. Both shows were lauded by critics, and achieved equal success with viewers. <i>Rescue Me</i> was one of the few television series to be given an order for an additional season prior to the broadcast of its most recent season: in June 2009 FX renewed the show for an 18-episode sixth season, although the fifth season had not premiered at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike many broadcast networks, FX has chosen to take risks with its programming and push the envelope of what can be shown on television; as a result, most (though not all) of the channel's original programming are assigned <a href="/wiki/TV_Parental_Guidelines" title="TV Parental Guidelines">TV-MA</a> ratings, often for strong profanity, sexual and/or violent content. Opinions on these shows are mixed; some organizations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Parents_Television_Council" title="Parents Television Council">Parents Television Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Family_Association" title="American Family Association">American Family Association</a>, have asked advertisers to <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a> these shows due to their graphic content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The shows have also been critically acclaimed for their strong storylines and characters.</p>
<p>Capitalizing on the success of the hit documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Size_Me" title="Super Size Me">Super Size Me</a></i>, filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Spurlock" title="Morgan Spurlock">Morgan Spurlock</a> launched a new series, <i><a href="/wiki/30_Days_(TV_series)" title="30 Days (TV series)">30 Days</a></i>, which debuted on FX in June 2005. The series place its subjects in situations uncomfortable to them for 30 days, such as making millionaires work for <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a>, and having <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christians</a> live in a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> community.</p>
<p>In the summer of 2005, FX debuted two new comedy series, <i><a href="/wiki/Starved" title="Starved">Starved</a></i>, about the daily lives of four friends with <a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">eating disorders</a> who live in New York City; and <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_Always_Sunny_in_Philadelphia" title="It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia">It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia</a></i>, about the usually very <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">politically incorrect</a> comic misadventures of four people who own a bar in the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">titular city</a>. Both of these shows feature frank sexual dialogue and strong language, and were pitched as "The Dark Side of Comedy." <i>Starved</i> was derided by groups that sought to publicize eating disorders and was cancelled after its first season due to low ratings. Conversely, <i>Sunny</i> quickly became a critical darling, consistently achieved high viewership, and was picked up for a second season within days of its first season finale. Fox aired an edited version of <i>Sunny</i> for a three-episode run in the summer of 2006, in an effort to further promote the series.</p>
<p>In 2006, FX debuted two new series, the <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Black._White." title="Black. White.">Black. White.</a></i> and the drama <i><a href="/wiki/Thief_(miniseries)" title="Thief (miniseries)">Thief</a></i>; neither series was picked up for a second season. During 2007, FX introduced three new dramas: <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_(TV_series)" title="Dirt (TV series)">Dirt</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Courteney_Cox" title="Courteney Cox">Courteney Cox</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Riches" title="The Riches">The Riches</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Izzard" title="Eddie Izzard">Eddie Izzard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnie_Driver" title="Minnie Driver">Minnie Driver</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Damages_(TV_series)" title="Damages (TV series)">Damages</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Close" title="Glenn Close">Glenn Close</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Danson" title="Ted Danson">Ted Danson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose_Byrne" title="Rose Byrne">Rose Byrne</a>. All three performed well in the ratings and were renewed for second seasons. By 2008, FX was available in 90.6 million homes in the U.S.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.9313:_.22There_Is_No_Box.22">2008–13: "There Is No Box"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: 2008–13: "There Is No Box"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2008, the channel launched a new branding campaign built around the theme "There Is No Box." It alluded to the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Thinking_outside_the_box" title="Thinking outside the box">thinking outside the box</a>" and referred to how the channel's programming goes beyond "the box" concept. In addition, this was a pun related to the channel's creating original programs to compete against premium channels such as <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a>. The channel's logo was updated on December 18, 2007, retaining only the FX <a href="/wiki/Wordmark" title="Wordmark">wordmark</a> while removing the <a href="/wiki/Klieg_light" title="Klieg light">klieg light</a> logo box that had been placed to its left since the 1997 rebrand. The new branding included an advertising campaign featuring a post-game ad for the channel during Fox's coverage of <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLII" title="Super Bowl XLII">Super Bowl XLII</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The promo used the <a href="/wiki/James_Morrison_(singer)" title="James Morrison (singer)">James Morrison</a> song "<a href="/wiki/You_Give_Me_Something" title="You Give Me Something">You Give Me Something</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 2008, competition with other cable channels increased, which was evident in the second season ratings for series <i>Dirt</i> and <i>The Riches,</i> whose ratings decreased significantly from their freshman seasons. During some weeks, viewership for both shows barely exceeded 1 million. Both shows were cancelled in 2008; acquired programs <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dharma_and_Greg" title="Dharma and Greg">Dharma and Greg</a></i>, <i>Buffy the Vampire Slayer,</i> <i>Married... with Children,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fear_Factor" title="Fear Factor">Fear Factor</a></i> were also removed from the schedule.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On September 3, 2008, FX debuted <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Anarchy" title="Sons of Anarchy">Sons of Anarchy</a>,</i> a drama series created by <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Sutter" title="Kurt Sutter">Kurt Sutter</a> (who previously served as executive producer of <i>The Shield</i>) about a fictional <a href="/wiki/Outlaw_motorcycle_club" title="Outlaw motorcycle club">outlaw motorcycle club</a> devoted to protecting their sheltered <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> town from corporate developers and drug dealers; its September premiere coincided with that of <i>The Shield</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s final season. <i>Sons of Anarchy</i> became a critical and commercial success, having aired for seven seasons as of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>. In 2010, the series attracted an average of 4.9 million viewers per week, making it <i>FX</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s highest rated series to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Other new shows that premiered in 2010 included the <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Hotz" title="Kenny Hotz">Kenny Hotz</a> comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Testees" title="Testees">Testees</a>,</i> which debuted in October 2008 and was cancelled after its first season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In August 2008, FX relaunched its website, adding streaming of full episodes of its original programs. In 2009, reruns of the former <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_City" title="Spin City">Spin City</a></i> were removed from the schedule (though it was restored early the following year).</p>
<p>In March 2010, the channel debuted <i><a href="/wiki/Justified_(TV_series)" title="Justified (TV series)">Justified</a>,</i> a drama series created by <a href="/wiki/Graham_Yost" title="Graham Yost">Graham Yost</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Elmore_Leonard" title="Elmore Leonard">Elmore Leonard</a>'s short story "Fire in the Hole" (which was the series' original <a href="/wiki/Working_title" title="Working title">working title</a>). It starred <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Olyphant" title="Timothy Olyphant">Timothy Olyphant</a> as U.S. Marshal Raylan Givens – a tough, soft-spoken lawman with a rough side – and chronicles his cases and personal life, including unfinished business with an ex-wife and his aging father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> FX also picked up <i><a href="/wiki/Terriers_(TV_series)" title="Terriers (TV series)">Terriers</a></i> for its fall 2010 lineup; in 2011, the channel debuted the boxing drama <i><a href="/wiki/Lights_Out_(2011_TV_series)" title="Lights Out (2011 TV series)">Lights Out</a>.</i> Both series were cancelled after their first seasons; <i>Terriers</i> had gained critical acclaim but apparently insufficient viewers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In July 2009, FX ordered three new comedy pilots: <i><a href="/wiki/Archer_(TV_series)" title="Archer (TV series)">Archer</a>,</i> an animated series featuring a spy agency, which premiered on January 14, 2010;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_League" title="The League">The League</a>,</i> with a group of friends who are part of a <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_football_(American)" title="Fantasy football (American)">fantasy football</a> league;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Louie_(TV_series)" title="Louie (TV series)">Louie</a>,</i> a sitcom starring stand-up comedian and writer <a href="/wiki/Louis_C.K." title="Louis C.K.">Louis C.K.</a>, which "blend[s] <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_comedy" title="Stand-up comedy">stand-up</a> material with[...] 'extended vignettes' depicting moments from [the comedian's] offstage experiences."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> The following year, FX debuted <i><a href="/wiki/Wilfred_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Wilfred (U.S. TV series)">Wilfred</a></i>, a comedy series starring <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Wood" title="Elijah Wood">Elijah Wood</a>. It is based on the <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_(Australian_TV_series)" title="Wilfred (Australian TV series)">Australian comedy series of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 1, 2010, parent company <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a> (which spun off FX and the company's other U.S.-based entertainment properties to <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Fox" title="21st Century Fox">21st Century Fox</a> in July 2013) pulled its channels from <a href="/wiki/Dish_Network" title="Dish Network">Dish Network</a> due to a <a href="/wiki/Carriage_dispute" title="Carriage dispute">carriage dispute</a> over <a href="/wiki/Retransmission_consent" title="Retransmission consent">retransmission consent</a> revenue. FX returned to the satellite provider's channel lineup on October 29, 2010, after Dish Network and News Corporation signed a long-term carriage agreement. On November 1, 2010, following a similar dispute, FX and its sister channels were restored by New York City-based cable provider <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cablevision" title="Cablevision">Cablevision</a> through a separate carriage agreement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On October 14, 2011, FX announced that it picked up the rights to develop a series based on <i><a href="/wiki/Scar_Tissue_(book)" title="Scar Tissue (book)">Scar Tissue</a></i> and <i>Lords of the Sunset Strip,</i> the autobiographies of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Kiedis" title="Anthony Kiedis">Anthony Kiedis</a> and his father, Blackie Dammett. HBO had picked up the series, which was to be titled <i>Spider &amp; Son,</i> a few years before but never completed the project. <i><a href="/wiki/Entourage_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Entourage (U.S. TV series)">Entourage</a></i> writer/producers Marc Abrams and Mike Benson were tapped as its <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunners</a> and Kiedis was to be involved as a co-producer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Dammett said in 2013 that the show has been "mothballed," and he hopes interest will resume on the project once the Red Hot Chili Peppers wrapped up their world tour that year. As of 2014 there has been no mention from FX, Kiedis or Dammett on the status of the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> On January 30, 2013, FX premiered the 1980s-set <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Americans_(2013_TV_series)" title="The Americans (2013 TV series)">The Americans</a></i>. On June 1, 2014, FX celebrated its 20th anniversary of programming.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming">Programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_programs_broadcast_by_FX" title="List of programs broadcast by FX">List of programs broadcast by FX</a></div>
<p>FX's most popular original series past and present include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">The Shield</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nip/Tuck" title="Nip/Tuck">Nip/Tuck</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_Me_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Rescue Me (U.S. TV series)">Rescue Me</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Anarchy" title="Sons of Anarchy">Sons of Anarchy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Archer_(TV_series)" title="Archer (TV series)">Archer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Strain_(TV_series)" title="The Strain (TV series)">The Strain</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/American_Horror_Story" title="American Horror Story">American Horror Story</a></i>, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Anger_Management_(TV_series)" title="Anger Management (TV series)">Anger Management</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_Always_Sunny_in_Philadelphia" title="It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia">It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_League" title="The League">The League</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>.</p>
<p>The channel also broadcasts theatrically released feature films from network sister company <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">film studios</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment">Sony Pictures Entertainment</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>), which take up much of FX's primetime and the majority of its weekend schedules. It airs repeats of network television sitcoms (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Two_and_a_Half_Men" title="Two and a Half Men">Two and a Half Men</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/How_I_Met_Your_Mother" title="How I Met Your Mother">How I Met Your Mother</a></i>). From the late 1990s to the mid-2000s, the acquired programs which FX broadcast consisted largely of series originally broadcast on Fox between the late 1980s and the 2000s (such as <i><a href="/wiki/That_%2770s_Show" title="That '70s Show">That '70s Show</a></i>, <i>Married... with Children,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/In_Living_Color" title="In Living Color">In Living Color</a></i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_programming">Sports programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sports programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After obtaining the spring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASCAR_on_Fox" title="NASCAR on Fox">broadcast rights</a> to <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_(United_States)" title="Fox Sports (United States)">Fox Sports</a> announced that FX would serve as its cable partner for the 2001 inaugural season of race telecasts. As a result, FX covered several races in the series then known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationwide_Series" title="Nationwide Series">Busch Series</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sprint_Cup_Series" title="Sprint Cup Series">Winston Cup</a> (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASCAR_Sprint_All-Star_Race" title="NASCAR Sprint All-Star Race">All-Star Race</a>), as well as select qualifying and final practice sessions. Having FX carry the race telecasts was intended to promote the channel and encourage NASCAR fans to contact their cable providers to add FX to their lineup. In 2002, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Liguori" title="Peter Liguori">Peter Liguori</a>, who was then president of FX, praised NASCAR for its growth; the channel increased penetration from 58.5 million to 76.6 million households nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> FX lost the broadcast rights to NASCAR after the <a href="/wiki/2006_in_NASCAR" title="2006 in NASCAR">2006 season</a>, as sister channel <a href="/wiki/Speed_(TV_channel)" title="Speed (TV channel)">Speed Channel</a> became the new cable partner for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASCAR_on_Fox" title="NASCAR on Fox">NASCAR on Fox</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1997_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1997 Major League Baseball season">1997</a>, FX obtained partial cable television rights to <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> games; the channel initially aired game telecasts on Monday nights, before moving them to Saturday nights in 1998. In 2000, FX began sharing the Major League Baseball cable rights with then-sister network <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_networks_preceding_ABC_Family" title="Television networks preceding ABC Family">Fox Family Channel</a> (taking rights to the league's Thursday evening games from <a href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_Networks" title="Fox Sports Networks">Fox Sports Net</a>), with games being scheduled on an alternating basis with FX. Starting with the 2001 season, FX also obtained rights to games from the <a href="/wiki/Division_Series" title="Division Series">MLB Division Series</a>, the only playoff round to which Fox did not hold television rights. Among the games televised on FX was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cal_Ripken,_Jr." title="Cal Ripken, Jr.">Cal Ripken, Jr.</a>'s final home game with the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> in September 2001.</p>
<p>On April 27, 2011, FX began airing <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> games from the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> as part of the league's overall television deal with Fox Sports. In the fall of 2011, FX began broadcasting <a href="/wiki/Big_12_Conference" title="Big 12 Conference">Big 12</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conference_USA" title="Conference USA">Conference USA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pac-12_Conference" title="Pac-12 Conference">Pac-12</a> <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">college football</a> games on Saturdays (mainly primetime games, with some daytime games mixed in), as part of Fox Sports' broadcasting contracts with the three conferences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> In January 2012, FX began broadcasting content from the <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_Fighting_Championship" title="Ultimate Fighting Championship">Ultimate Fighting Championship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the August 2013 launches of national sports cable networks <a href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_1" title="Fox Sports 1">Fox Sports 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_2" title="Fox Sports 2">Fox Sports 2</a>, FX no longer serves as a regular cable outlet for Fox Sports. However, <a href="/wiki/UFC_185" title="UFC 185">UFC 185</a> preliminary fights aired on FX due to FS1 showing college basketball. Also on March 5, 2016 FX aired a <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> match between that league's top two teams <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayern_Munich" title="Bayern Munich">Bayern Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a> to provide wider distribution of the fixture, as Fox, FS1, and FS2 all had sporting commitments of their own at the time of the match and the match was to air on the little-distributed <a href="/wiki/Fox_Soccer_Plus" title="Fox Soccer Plus">Fox Soccer Plus</a> (which does not have carriage on numerous TV providers). Because this was Fox's first year of coverage of Germany's top soccer league and Bayern and Borussia are the two most successful Bundesliga teams (and have an intense rivalry known as Der Klassiker), Fox wanted to give the game wider distribution, and so the game was moved to FX in a last-minute decision.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sister_channels">Sister channels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sister channels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FX_Movie_Channel">FX Movie Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: FX Movie Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FX_Movie_Channel" title="FX Movie Channel">FX Movie Channel</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FX_Movie_Channel.jpg"><img alt="FX Movie Channel.jpg" data-file-height="138" data-file-width="237" height="76" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/24/FX_Movie_Channel.jpg/130px-FX_Movie_Channel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/24/FX_Movie_Channel.jpg/195px-FX_Movie_Channel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/24/FX_Movie_Channel.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a></div>
<p><b>FX Movie Channel</b> (or <b>FXM</b>) launched on October 31, 1994 as fXM: Movies from Fox (prior to its launch, the channel was originally named "Fox Movie Studio")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Originally launched as a spinoff of FX, the channel focused on feature films from the <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> film library from the 1930s to the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> fXM became a separately branded channel on March 1, 2000, when it was renamed <b>Fox Movie Channel</b>.</p>
<p>On January 1, 2012, Fox Movie Channel's programming was divided into two 12-hour blocks: its main programming schedule, from 3:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. Eastern Time, was a commercial-free block retaining the older movies from the 20th Century Fox library. Another block, from 3:00 p.m. to 3:00 a.m. Eastern Time and a very largely advertiser-supported version, carried an expanded slate of more recent feature films from Fox and other film studios, which were targeted at audiences between the ages of 12 and 49.</p>
<p>On March 27, 2013, Fox Entertainment Group announced that Fox Movie Channel would be fully rebranded under the FXM name and format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> FX Movie Channel became the primary brand for the channel in September 2013; the classic film block retained the Fox Movie Channel name until June 9, 2014, when the block (which retains a commercial-free format) was renamed <i>FXM Retro</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FXX">FXX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: FXX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FXX" title="FXX">FXX</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FXX_Logo.svg"><img alt="FXX Logo.svg" data-file-height="310" data-file-width="342" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/FXX_Logo.svg/130px-FXX_Logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/FXX_Logo.svg/195px-FXX_Logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/FXX_Logo.svg/260px-FXX_Logo.svg.png 2x" width="130"/></a></div>
<p>Aimed at young men in the 18-34 age range, <b>FXX</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Digital_cable" title="Digital cable">digital cable</a> and satellite channel that launched on September 2, 2013, replacing the sports-oriented <a href="/wiki/Fox_Soccer" title="Fox Soccer">Fox Soccer</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FXX_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FXX-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> FXX is a general entertainment channel that primarily focuses on comedies (whereas FX focuses primarily on drama series and films, although FX and FXX do not maintain the same genre-exclusive format as <a href="/wiki/TBS_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="TBS (U.S. TV channel)">TBS</a> and <a href="/wiki/TNT_(TV_channel)" title="TNT (TV channel)">TNT</a> as FX continues to carry sitcoms and comedic films, while FXX carries a limited selection of dramatic series and films); its programming includes original and acquired comedy series, some feature films and drama series.</p>
<p>With the launch of the channel, first-run episodes of some of FX's original comedy series (such as <i>It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Totally_Biased_with_W._Kamau_Bell" title="Totally Biased with W. Kamau Bell">Totally Biased with W. Kamau Bell</a></i>) were shifted over to FXX. At its launch, most providers that have agreements to carry FXX have placed the channel in extra-cost sports packages (despite being a general entertainment service) as an artifact of carriage deals with the previous holder of FXX's channel space, Fox Soccer, though this is expected to be rectified over time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FX_NOW">FX NOW</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: FX NOW">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FX NOW is a website for <a href="/wiki/Desktop_computer" title="Desktop computer">desktop computers</a>, as well as an <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">application</a> for <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Windows_10" title="Windows 10">Windows 10</a>. It allows subscribers of participating cable and satellite providers (such as <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Cable" title="Time Warner Cable">Time Warner Cable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast Xfinity</a>) numerous viewing options:</p>
<ul>
<li>individual episodes of FX and FXX's original series (which are made available the morning after their original airdate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup>),</li>
<li>acquired series (most notably, the 552-episode catalog of the first 25 seasons of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a>,</i> which was added on August 21, 2014 as part of FXX's acquisition of cable syndication rights to the series), and feature films (with an initial library of 165 film titles, which will increase to more than 200 titles beginning in 2015),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Additional content includes behind-the-scenes features on computers and mobile devices via their <a href="/wiki/TV_Everywhere" title="TV Everywhere">TV Everywhere</a> login provided by their cable provider.</li>
</ul>
<p>Launched in January 2014, the service is also available through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IOS_(Apple)" title="IOS (Apple)">iOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windows_8" title="Windows 8">Windows 8</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Windows_10" title="Windows 10">Windows 10</a>) devices, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Roku" title="Roku">Roku</a> streaming player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> Although the service is available for free to subscribers of participating cable, telco and satellite providers, programs available for streaming on FXNOW feature commercial interruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FX_(India)" title="FX (India)">FX (India)</a></div>
<p>In 2004, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_International_Channels" title="Fox International Channels">Fox International Channels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STAR_India" title="STAR India">STAR India</a> (through a distribution agreement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> partnered to launch FX Asia. The channel is divided into four services: a national channel distributed throughout much of the continent, and three regional channels serving <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (the Indian channel was launched in September 2012, replacing the main Asian feed on most cable and satellite providers in that country).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/SoHo_(Australian_TV_channel)" title="SoHo (Australian TV channel)">SoHo (Australian TV channel)</a> and <a href="/wiki/FX_Australia" title="FX Australia">FX Australia</a></div>
<p>fX launched in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> in 1995, with a format reliant on classic television series (often branded on-air as "Golden Years of Television"). fXM was a nightly block on the channel that featured classic films from the 20th Century Fox library, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Collins_(television_presenter)" title="Bill Collins (television presenter)">Bill Collins</a> (the block was often branded as "Bill Collins' Golden Years of Hollywood", which later aired on Fox Classics). In late 1998, fX became FX, a channel aimed at women (a format change affirmed in late 2000, as part of a rebrand that identified it as "Australia's first TV channel for women"), featuring shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_View_(U.S._TV_series)" title="The View (U.S. TV series)">The View</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Donny_%26_Marie_(1998_TV_series)" title="Donny &amp; Marie (1998 TV series)">Donny and Marie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> The channel was relaunched as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._Channel" title="W. Channel">W.</a></i> on November 1, 2003, to emphasize its appeal to women. In 2010 it rebranded as <i><a href="/wiki/SoHo_(Australian_TV_channel)" title="SoHo (Australian TV channel)">SoHo</a>.</i></p>
<p>On October 9, 2011, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_International_Channels" title="Fox International Channels">Fox International Channels</a> announced that a new Australian version of FX would be launched in early 2012, featuring series such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Walking_Dead_(TV_series)" title="The Walking Dead (TV series)">The Walking Dead</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Transporter:_The_Series" title="Transporter: The Series">Transporter: The Series</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Wheels_(TV_series)" title="Hell on Wheels (TV series)">Hell on Wheels</a>,</i> designed to increase the breadth of its audience<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Launch_of_FX_Australia_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Launch_of_FX_Australia-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FX_(Canada)" title="FX (Canada)">FX (Canada)</a></div>
<p>On August 6, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Media" title="Rogers Media">Rogers Media</a> entered into a licensing agreement with FX Networks to launch FX Canada as a <a href="/wiki/Category_B_services" title="Category B services">Category B</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_cable" title="Digital cable">digital cable</a> and satellite <a href="/wiki/Specialty_channel" title="Specialty channel">specialty channel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> The channel is also carried by other providers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EastLink_(company)" title="EastLink (company)">EastLink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Communications" title="Shaw Communications">Shaw Cable</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTS_TV" title="MTS TV">MTS TV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> The network, which launched on November 1, 2011, features a mix of FX original series; acquired American movies and series; and original Canadian programming and sporting events (as required by <a href="/wiki/Canadian_content" title="Canadian content">content</a> rules imposed by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Radio-television_and_Telecommunications_Commission" title="Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission">Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> FX Canada's broadcast license requires that 15% of its programming consist of Canadian content in its first year, 20% in its second year and 25% by its third year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FX_Latin_America" title="FX Latin America">FX Latin America</a></div>
<p>The FX channel in Latin America, is intended almost entirely aimed at a male audience, as a counterpart of <a href="/wiki/Fox_Life" title="Fox Life">Fox Life</a>, created for the female viewers. In Brazil, it is broadcast mainly by NET TV, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Televis%C3%A3o_Abril" title="Televisão Abril">TVA</a>, Oi TV, <a href="/wiki/SKY_Brasil" title="SKY Brasil">SKY</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Via_Embratel" title="Via Embratel">Embratel TV</a> and GVT TV.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FX was launched in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StarSat" title="StarSat">StarSat</a> offering in May 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_definition">High definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FX_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: High definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
