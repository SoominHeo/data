<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fuck</h1>

<p><i><b>Fuck</b></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscene</a> <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> word,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which refers to the act of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> and is also commonly used as an <a href="/wiki/Intensifier" title="Intensifier">intensifier</a> or to denote disdain. Its origin is obscure but is usually considered to be first attested to around 1475, although it may be considerably older. In modern usage, the term <i>fuck</i> and its <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">derivatives</a> (such as <i>fucker</i> and <i>fucking</i>) can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjection</a>, or an <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverb</a>. There are many common phrases that employ the word, as well as <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compounds</a> that incorporate it, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Motherfucker" title="Motherfucker">motherfucker</a>,</i> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fuckwit" title="wikt:fuckwit">fuckwit</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fucknut" title="wikt:fucknut">fucknut</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Offensiveness">Offensiveness</span></h2>
<p>The word is considered obscene, but is common in many informal and familiar situations.</p>
<p>It is unclear whether the word has always been considered vulgar, or, if not, when it first came to be used to describe (often in an extremely angry, hostile or belligerent manner) unpleasant circumstances or people in an intentionally offensive way, such as in the term <i><a href="/wiki/Motherfucker" title="Motherfucker">motherfucker</a></i>, one of its more common usages in some parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_English_is_an_official_language" title="List of countries where English is an official language">English-speaking world</a>. Some English-speaking countries censor it on television and radio. Andrea Millwood Hargrave's 2000 study of the attitudes of the British public found that <i>fuck</i> was considered the third most severe profanity and its derivative <i>motherfucker</i> second. <i><a href="/wiki/Cunt" title="Cunt">Cunt</a></i> was considered the most severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hargrave_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hargrave-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, the word has become increasingly less vulgar and more publicly acceptable, an example of the "<a href="/wiki/Dysphemism" title="Dysphemism">dysphemism treadmill</a>", wherein former vulgarities become inoffensive and commonplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christian_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christian-4">[4]</a></sup> According to linguist Pamela Hobbs, "notwithstanding its increasing public use, enduring cultural models that inform our beliefs about the nature of sexuality and sexual acts preserve its status as a vile utterance that continues to inspire moral outrage." Hobbs considers <i>users</i> rather than <i>usage</i> of the word and sub-divides <i>users</i> into 'non-users', for whom the word "evokes the core sexual meanings and associated sexual imagery that motivate the taboo", and 'users' for whom "metaphorical uses of the word <i>fuck</i> no more evoke images of sexual intercourse than a ten-year-old’s ‘My mom’ll kill me if she finds out’ evokes images of murder," so that the "criteria of taboo are missing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of its increasing usage in the public forum, in 2005 the word was included for the first time as one of three vulgarities in <a href="/wiki/The_Canadian_Press" title="The Canadian Press">The Canadian Press</a>'s <i>Canadian Press Caps and Spelling</i> guide. Journalists were advised to refrain from censoring the word but use it sparingly and only when its inclusion was essential to the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPmanual_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPmanual-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> states that the ultimate etymology is uncertain, but that the word is "probably cognate" with a number of native <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> words with meanings involving striking, rubbing, and having sex or is derivative of the Old French word that meant "to fuck".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_use_in_sexual_sense">First use in sexual sense</span></h3>
<p>In 2015, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Booth_(historian)" title="Paul Booth (historian)">Paul Booth</a> claimed to have found "(possibly) the earliest known use of the word 'fuck' that clearly has a sexual connotation": in English court records of 1310–11, a man local to <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Roger_Fuckebythenavele" title="Roger Fuckebythenavele">Roger Fuckebythenavele</a>", probably a nickname. "Either this refers to an inexperienced copulator, referring to someone trying to have sex with the navel, or it's a rather extravagant explanation for a dimwit, someone so stupid they think this that is the way to have sex," says Booth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> An earlier name, that of <a href="/wiki/John_le_Fucker" title="John le Fucker">John le Fucker</a> recorded in 1278, has been the subject of debate, but is thought by many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">philologists</a> to have had some separate and non-sexual origin.</p>
<p>Otherwise, the usually accepted first known occurrence of the word is found in <a href="/wiki/Code_(cryptography)" title="Code (cryptography)">code</a> in a poem in a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and English composed in the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheidlower1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheidlower1998-9">[9]</a></sup> The poem, which satirizes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmelite" title="Carmelite">Carmelite</a> friars of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge,_England" title="Cambridge, England">Cambridge</a>, England, takes its title, "<a href="/wiki/Flen_flyys" title="Flen flyys">Flen flyys</a>", from the first words of its opening line, <i>Flen, flyys, and freris</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">Fleas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flies" title="Flies">flies</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friars" title="Friars">friars</a>"). The line that contains <i>fuck</i> reads <i>Non sunt in coeli, quia gxddbov xxkxzt pg ifmk</i>. <a href="/wiki/Substitution_cipher" title="Substitution cipher">Deciphering</a> the phrase "<i>gxddbov xxkxzt pg ifmk</i>", here by replacing each letter by the previous letter in <a href="/wiki/Alphabetical_order" title="Alphabetical order">alphabetical order</a>, as the English alphabet was then, yields the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macaronic" title="Macaronic">macaronic</a> <i>non sunt in coeli, quia fvccant vvivys of heli</i>, which translated means, "They are not in heaven, because they fuck wives of <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The phrase was probably encoded because it accused monks of breaking their vows of celibacy;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheidlower1998_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sheidlower1998-9">[9]</a></sup> it is uncertain to what extent the word <i>fuck</i> was considered acceptable at the time. The stem of <i>fvccant</i> is an English word used as Latin: English <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">medieval Latin</a> has many examples of writers using English words when they did not know the Latin word: "<i>workmannus</i>" is an example. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> of this poem, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Wife" title="Wife">wife</a></i> was still used generically for "<a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">woman</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Older_etymology">Older etymology</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Via_Germanic">Via Germanic</span></h4>
<p>The word has probable <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a> in other Germanic languages, such as <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>ficken</i> (to fuck); <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>fokken</i> (to breed, to beget); dialectal <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> <i>fukka</i> (to copulate), and dialectal <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> <i>focka</i> (to strike, to copulate) and <i>fock</i> (<a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-7">[7]</a></sup> This points to a possible etymology where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" title="Common Germanic">Common Germanic</a> <i>fuk–</i> comes from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language" title="Indo-European language">Indo-European</a> root meaning "to strike", cognate with non-Germanic words such as Latin <i>pugno</i> "I fight" or <i>pugnus</i> "fist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-oed-7">[7]</a></sup> By application of <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_law" title="Grimm's law">Grimm's law</a>, this hypothetical root has the form *<i>pug–</i>.</p>
<p>Yet another possible etymology is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> word <i>pfluog</i>, meaning "to plow, as in a field." This is supported in part by a book by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung_publications" title="Carl Jung publications">Psychology of the Unconscious: A Study of the Transformations and Symbolisms of the Libido</a></i>, in which he discusses the "primitive play of words" and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phallic" title="Phallic">phallic</a> representation of the plough, including its appearance on a vase found in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> dig near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florence,_Italy" title="Florence, Italy">Florence</a>, Italy, which depicts six men with erect penises carrying a plow.</p>
<p>There is a theory that <i>fuck</i> is most likely derived from Flemish, German, or Dutch roots, and is probably not from Old English roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheidlower1998_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sheidlower1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Via_Latin_or_Greek">Via Latin or Greek</span></h4>
<p>There may be a kinship with the Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/futuo#Latin" title="wikt:futuo">futuere</a></i> (<i>futuo</i>), a verb with almost exactly the same meaning as the English verb "to fuck". From <i>fūtuere</i> came <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>foutre</i>, <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> <i>fotre</i>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <i>fottere</i>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> <i>futere</i>, vulgar peninsular <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <i>joder</i>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> <i>foder</i>, and the obscure English equivalent <i>to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/futter" title="wikt:futter">futter</a></i>, coined by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Francis_Burton" title="Richard Francis Burton">Richard Francis Burton</a>. However, there is no clear past lineage or derivation for the Latin word. These roots, even if cognates, are not the original Indo-European word for <i>to copulate</i>, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wayland_Young" title="Wayland Young">Wayland Young</a> argues that they derive from the Indo-European *<i>b<sup>h</sup>u–</i> or *<i>b<sup>h</sup>ug–</i> ("be", "become"), or as causative "create" [see Young, 1964]. A possible intermediate might be a Latin 4th-<a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declension</a> <a href="/wiki/Verbal_noun" title="Verbal noun">verbal noun</a> *<i>fūtus</i>, with possible meanings including "act of (pro)creating".</p>
<p>However, the connection to <i>futuere</i> has been disputed‍—‌<a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Liberman" title="Anatoly Liberman">Anatoly Liberman</a> calls it a "coincidence" and writes that it is not likely to have been borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> precursors to <i>fuck</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liberman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liberman-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greek <i>phyō</i> (φύω) has various meanings, including (of a man) "to beget", or (of a woman), "to give birth to".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a> <i>pephyka</i> (πέφυκα) can be likened<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to "fuck" and its equivalents in other Germanic languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="False_etymologies">False etymologies</span></h3>
<p>One reason that the word <i>fuck</i> is so hard to trace etymologically is that it was used far more extensively in common speech than in easily traceable written forms. There are several <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban-legend</a> <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">false etymologies</a> postulating an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronymic</a> origin for the word. None of these acronyms was ever recorded before the 1960s, according to the authoritative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicographer" title="Lexicographer">lexicographical</a> work <i><a href="/wiki/The_F-Word_(book)" title="The F-Word (book)">The F-Word</a></i>, and thus are <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronyms</a>. In any event, the word <i>fuck</i> has been in use far too long for some of these supposed origins to be possible. Some of these urban legends are that the word <i>fuck</i> came from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> law. If a couple were caught committing <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a>, they would be punished "For Unlawful <a href="/wiki/Carnal_knowledge" title="Carnal knowledge">Carnal Knowledge</a> In the Nude," with "FUCKIN" written on the <a href="/wiki/Stocks" title="Stocks">stocks</a> above them to denote the crime. A similar variant on this theory involves the recording by church clerks of the crime of "Forbidden Use of Carnal Knowledge." Another theory is that of a royal permission. During the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, towns were trying to control populations and their interactions. Since uncontaminated resources were scarce, supposedly many towns required permission to have children. Hence, the legend goes, that couples that were having children were required to first obtain royal permission (usually from a local magistrate or lord) and then place a sign somewhere visible from the road in their home that said "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fornicate" title="Fornicate">Fornicating</a> Under Consent of King," which was later shortened to "FUCK." This story is hard to document, but has persisted in oral and literary traditions for many years; however, it has been demonstrated to be an urban legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A different false etymology, first made popular on the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/Car_Talk" title="Car Talk">Car Talk</a></i>, states that the phrase "fuck you" comes from the phrase "pluck yew" and relates the origins of <i>fuck</i> to the myth surrounding the <a href="/wiki/V_sign" title="V sign">V sign</a>. This myth states that French archers at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Battle of Agincourt</a> insulted the English troops' ability to shoot their weapons by waving their fingers in a V shape; after the English secured a landslide victory, they returned the gesture. The addition of the phrase "fuck you" to the myth came when it was claimed that the English yelled that they could still "pluck yew" (<a href="/wiki/Taxus_baccata" title="Taxus baccata">yew</a> wood being the preferred material for longbows at the time), a phrase that evolved into the modern "fuck you".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheidlower1998_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sheidlower1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span></h2>
<p><i>Fuck</i> has a very flexible role in English grammar, including use as both a <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">transitive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">intransitive</a> <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>, and as an <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It can also be used as an <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjection</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Ejaculation_(grammar)" title="Ejaculation (grammar)">grammatical ejaculation</a>. Linguist Geoffrey Hughes found eight distinct usages for English curse words, and <i>fuck</i> can apply to each. For example, it fits in the "curse" sense ("fuck you!") as well as the "personal" sense ("You fucker"). Its vulgarity also contributes to its mostly figurative sense, though the word itself is used in its literal sense to refer to sexual intercourse, its most common usage is figurative- to indicate the speaker's strong sentiment and to offend or shock the listener.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_usage">Early usage</span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Dunbar_(poet)" title="William Dunbar (poet)">William Dunbar</a>'s 1503 poem "Brash of Wowing" includes the lines: "Yit be his feiris he wald haue fukkit: / Ye brek my hairt, my bony ane" (ll. 13–14).</p>
<p>The oldest occurrence of the word in adjectival form (which implies use of the verb) in English comes from the margins of a 1528 manuscript copy of <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/De_Officiis" title="De Officiis">De Officiis</a></i>. A monk had scrawled in the margin notes, "fuckin Abbot". Whether the monk meant the word literally, to accuse this abbott of "questionable monastic morals," or whether he used it "as an intensifier, to convey his extreme dismay" is unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Florio" title="John Florio">John Florio's</a> 1598 Italian-English dictionary, <i>A Worlde of Wordes</i>, included the term, along with several now-archaic, but then-vulgar synonyms, in this definition:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fottere: <i>To jape, to sard, to fucke, to swive, to occupy.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Of these, "occupy" and "jape" still survive as verbs, though with less profane meanings, while "sard" was a descendant of the Anglo-Saxon verb <i>seordan</i> (or <i>seorðan</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">ON</a> <i>serða</i>), to copulate; and "swive" had derived from earlier <i>swīfan</i>, to revolve i.e. to swivel (compare modern-day "screw").</p>
<p>A 1790 poem by <a href="/wiki/George_Tucker_(politician)" title="George Tucker (politician)">George Tucker</a> has a father upset with his bookish son say "I'd not give [a fuck] for all you've read". Originally printed as "I'd not give ------ for all you've read", scholars agree that the words "a fuck" were removed, making the poem the first recorded instance of the now-common phrase "I don't give a fuck".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farmer and Henley's 1893 dictionary of slang notes both the adverbial and adjectival forms of <i>fuck</i> as similar to but "more violent" than <i>bloody</i> and indicating extreme insult, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_modern_usage">Rise of modern usage</span></h2>
<p>Though it appeared in English lexicographer <a href="/wiki/John_Ash_(divine)" title="John Ash (divine)">John Ash</a>'s 1775 <i>A New and Complete Dictionary</i>, listed as "low" and "vulgar," and appearing with several definitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>fuck</i> did not appear in any widely consulted dictionary of the English language from 1795 to 1965. Its first appearance in the <i>Oxford English Dictionary</i> (along with the word <i>cunt</i>) was in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The variant <i>feck</i> appeared in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Dialect_Dictionary" title="English Dialect Dictionary">English Dialect Dictionary</a></i>, compiled by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wright_(linguist)" title="Joseph Wright (linguist)">Joseph Wright</a> in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span></h2>

<p>The modern usage and flexibility of <i>fuck</i> was established by the mid to late 19th century, and has been fairly stable since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15">[15]</a></sup> Most literally, to <i>fuck</i> is to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">copulate</a>, but it is also used as a more general <a href="/wiki/Expletive_attributive" title="Expletive attributive">expletive</a> or intensifier. Some instances of the word can be taken at face value, such as "Let's fuck," "I would fuck her/him," or "He/she fucks." Other uses are dysphemistic: The sexual connotation, usually connected to <a href="/wiki/Masturbation" title="Masturbation">masturbation</a> (in the case of "go fuck yourself" or "go fuck yourself in the ass"), is invoked to incite additional disgust, or express anger or outrage. For example, "Fuck that!", "Fuck no!", "Fuck off!", or "Fuck you!" By itself, <i>fuck</i> is usually used as an exclamation, indicating surprise, pain, fear, disgust, disappointment, anger, or a sense of extreme elation. In this usage, there is no connection to the sexual meaning of the word implied, and is used purely for its "strength" as a vulgarity. Additionally, other uses are similarly vacuous; <i>fuck</i> (or variations such as <i>the fuck</i> or <i>fucking</i>) could be removed and leave a sentence of identical <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactical</a> meaning. For example, rap music often uses the word <i>fucking</i> as an emphatic adjective ("I'm the fucking man") for the word's rhythmic properties. The word <i>fuck</i> can also be used to express surprise and/or disbelief towards a given statement or a question, most often in the form of "does/is it fuck", however this usage is more common in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>.</p>
<p>Insertion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trochaic" title="Trochaic">trochaic</a> word <i>fucking</i> can also be used as an exercise for diagnosing the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cadence" title="wikt:cadence">cadence</a> of an English-language word. This is the use of <i>fuck</i> or more specifically <i>fucking</i> as an <a href="/wiki/Infix" title="Infix">infix</a>, or more properly, a <a href="/wiki/Tmesis" title="Tmesis">tmesis</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Expletive_infixation" title="Expletive infixation">expletive infixation</a>). For example, the word <i>in-fucking-credible</i> sounds acceptable to the English ear, and is in fairly common use, while <i>incred-fucking-ible</i> would sound very clumsy (though, depending on the context, this might be perceived as a humorous <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">improvisation</a> of the word). <i>Abso-fucking-lutely</i> and <i>motherfucking</i> are also common uses of <i>fuck</i> as an <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affix</a>. While neither dysphemistic nor connected to the sexual connotations of the word, even the vacuous usages are considered offensive and gratuitous, and censored in some media; for example, "None of your fucking business!" or "Shut the fuck up!" A common insult is "Get fucked", which in a non-offensive context would translate as "get stuffed." The word is one of the few that has legitimate colloquial usage as a <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>, command, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">exclamatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronoun</a>.</p>
<p>In another usage, the word <i>fucker</i> is used as a term of endearment rather than antipathy. This usage is not uncommon; to say "you're one smart fucker" is often a term of affection. However, because of its ambiguity and vulgarity, the word <i>fucker</i> in reference to another person can easily be misinterpreted. Though <i>fuck</i> can serve as a noun, the <i>fucker</i> form is used in a context that refers to an individual. Normally in these cases, if <i>fuck</i> is used instead of <i>fucker,</i> the sentence refers to the sexual ability of the subject (for example, "He's a great fuck!"), although confusingly in a minority of occasions the word "fuck" can hold exactly the same meaning as <i>fucker</i> (e.g., when preceded by an adjective: "You're a pretty clever fuck.").</p>
<p>Related to <i>fucker</i> is the word <i><a href="/wiki/Motherfucker" title="Motherfucker">motherfucker</a>.</i> Sometimes used as an extreme insult—an accusation of <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>—this term is also occasionally used to connote respectful awe. For example, "He's a mean motherfucker" does not mean "He's abusive, filthy and copulates with his mother," but "He's someone to be afraid of." In this context, some people (particularly young men) might even describe themselves as "motherfuckers." The word <i>fuck</i> is used in many forms of music, with <i>motherfucker</i> being used as a <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic</a> filler in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_music" title="Urban music">urban music</a> and some rock genres; examples include <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Method" title="The Crystal Method">The Crystal Method</a>'s song "Name of the Game" and <a href="/wiki/Turbonegro" title="Turbonegro">Turbonegro</a>'s "Don't Say Motherfucker Motherfucker". Perhaps <i>motherfucker's</i> rhythmic compatibility is due to its <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">quadrisyllabic</a> <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">pronunciation</a>, making it a natural fit for popular music that is written in 4/4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" title="Meter (music)">metre</a>. Also contributing to its use in aggressive, high-energy music is the fact that it includes a hard "k" sound in its third syllable, making it easy to exclaim, particularly when pronounced as "mutha fucka". Despite these rhythmic qualities, <i>motherfucker</i> has not become as accepted in English usage as its root <i>fuck</i>.</p>
<p>The word was used to induce stress during military <a href="/wiki/Recruit_training" title="Recruit training">recruit training</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and became a dominant element of an English military dialect identified by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Army_creole" title="Army creole">Army creole</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A succinct example of the flexibility of the word is its use as almost every word in a sentence. In his book, <i>Wartime: Understanding and Behavior in the Second World War</i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Fussell" title="Paul Fussell">Paul Fussell</a>, literary <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> and professor emeritus of <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, recounted</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Once, on a misty Scottish airfield, an <a href="/wiki/Airman" title="Airman">airman</a> was changing the <a href="/wiki/Ignition_magneto" title="Ignition magneto">magneto</a> on the engine of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellington_bomber" title="Wellington bomber">Wellington bomber</a>. Suddenly his wrench slipped and he flung it on the grass and snarled, "Fuck! The fucking fucker's fucked." The bystanders were all quite well aware that he had stripped a bolt and skinned his knuckles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the United States military air services, the nicknames applied to some aircraft included the word fucker as the letter "F" in their slang-acronyms. For instance, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52_stratofortress" title="B-52 stratofortress">B-52 stratofortress</a> strategic bomber was often referred to as the BUFF, or Big Ugly Fat Fucker. Similarly, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair" title="A-7 Corsair">A-7 Corsair</a> was often referred to as the SLUF, or Short Little Ugly Fucker. In polite conversation, these acronyms usually substituted the word "Fellow" for fucker.</p>
<p>The phrase "Fuck you, you fucking fuck!" is a memorable quote from the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Velvet_(film)" title="Blue Velvet (film)">Blue Velvet</a></i> from 1986. Because of its vulgar status, the word <i>fuck</i> is usually restricted in mass media and barred from titles in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In 2002, when the controversial <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_France" title="Cinema of France">French film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Baise-moi" title="Baise-moi">Baise-moi</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2000_in_film" title="2000 in film">2000</a>) was released in the US, its title was changed to <i>Rape Me</i>, rather than the literal <i>Fuck Me</i>. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fucking_%C3%85m%C3%A5l" title="Fucking Åmål">Fucking Åmål</a></i> was retitled <i>Show Me Love</i>.</p>
<p>Online forums and public <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> may censor the word by use of <a href="/wiki/Wordfilter" title="Wordfilter">automatic filters</a>. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fark.com" title="Fark.com">Fark.com</a> replaces the word <i>fuck</i> with <i>fark</i>. Others replace the word with <a href="/wiki/Asterisk" title="Asterisk">asterisks</a> (<i>****</i>) to censor it (and other profanities) entirely. To avert these filters, many online posters will use the word <i>fvck</i>. This particular alteration is in common usage at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, where students use it in reference to the inscriptions on MIT's <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> buildings, in which the letter <i><a href="/wiki/U" title="U">U</a></i> is replaced by <i><a href="/wiki/V" title="V">V</a>.</i> A typical coinage in this idiom would be "I'm fvcked by the Institvte." (Other less common spellings to cheat a censor are "fück" and "phuck".) Another way to bypass a word filter is to use <a href="/wiki/Leet" title="Leet">leet</a>: fuck becomes F(_) c|&lt; or |=(_)Ck, for example.</p>

<p><span id="WTF"></span> The word <i>fuck</i> is a component of many acronyms, some of which—like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_acronym" title="Military acronym">SNAFU</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FUBAR" title="FUBAR">FUBAR</a></i>—date as far back as <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Many more recent coinages, such as the shorthand "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/WTF" title="wikt:WTF">WTF</a>?" for "what the fuck?," "<a href="/wiki/Shut_up" title="Shut up">STFU</a>" for "shut the fuck up," or "FML" for "fuck my life," have been widely extant on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, and may count as examples of <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">internet memes</a>. Many acronyms will also have an "F" or "MF" added to increase emphasis; for example, "OMG" ("<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oh_my_God" title="wikt:oh my God">oh my God</a>") becomes "OMFG" ("oh my fucking God"). Abbreviated versions of the word do not tend to be considered offensive. Although the word is proclaimed vulgar, several comedians rely on <i>fuck</i> for comedic routines. <a href="/wiki/George_Carlin" title="George Carlin">George Carlin</a> created several literary works based upon the word. Other comedians who use or have used the word consistently in their routines include <a href="/wiki/Billy_Connolly" title="Billy Connolly">Billy Connolly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denis_Leary" title="Denis Leary">Denis Leary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Black" title="Lewis Black">Lewis Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dice_Clay" title="Andrew Dice Clay">Andrew Dice Clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pryor" title="Richard Pryor">Richard Pryor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy" title="Eddie Murphy">Eddie Murphy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Kinison" title="Sam Kinison">Sam Kinison</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_more_recent_usage">Examples of more recent usage</span></h3>
<p>In 1928, English writer <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence's</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley's Lover">Lady Chatterley's Lover</a></i> gained notoriety for its frequent use of the words <i>fuck</i>, <i>fucked</i>, and <i>fucking</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/J._D._Salinger" title="J. D. Salinger">J. D. Salinger</a> featured the use of <i>fuck you</i> in print. First published in the United States in 1951, the novel remains controversial to this day due in part to its use of the word, standing at number 13 for the most banned books from 1990 to 2000 according to the American Library Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first documented use of the word <i>fuck</i> on live British television (and probably on any television system) has been attributed to theatre critic <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Tynan" title="Kenneth Tynan">Kenneth Tynan</a> in 1965, though it has been claimed Irish playwright <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Behan" title="Brendan Behan">Brendan Behan</a> used the word on <a href="/wiki/Panorama_(TV_series)" title="Panorama (TV series)"><i>Panorama</i></a> in 1956 (although no one could understand him because he was drunk) or the man who painted the railings on Stranmillis Embankment alongside the river Lagan in Belfast, who in 1959 told Ulster TV's teatime magazine programme, Roundabout, that his job was "fucking boring".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Controversy also ensued in 1976 when <i>Today</i> host <a href="/wiki/Bill_Grundy" title="Bill Grundy">Bill Grundy</a> interviewed the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a>, after guitarist <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jones_(musician)" title="Steve Jones (musician)">Steve Jones</a> called Grundy a "dirty fucker" and a "fucking rotter"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols#EMI_and_the_Grundy_incident" title="Sex Pistols">EMI and the Grundy incident</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Cleese" title="John Cleese">John Cleese</a> during his eulogy in the memorial service for <a href="/wiki/Graham_Chapman" title="Graham Chapman">Graham Chapman</a> was proud to declare himself to be the first person to use the word "fuck" on such occasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word began to break into cinema when it was used several times in the 1969 British film <i><a href="/wiki/Bronco_Bullfrog" title="Bronco Bullfrog">Bronco Bullfrog</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The first time the word "fuck" was used in American cinema was in the 1970 movie <a href="/wiki/M*A*S*H" title="M*A*S*H">M*A*S*H</a> spoken by Painless during the football match at the end of the film.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_marketing">Use in marketing</span></h3>
<p>In April 1997, clothing retailer <a href="/wiki/French_Connection_(clothing)" title="French Connection (clothing)">French Connection</a> began branding their clothes "fcuk" (usually written in lowercase). Stating it was an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for "French Connection United Kingdom", its similarity to the word "fuck" caused controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> French Connection produced a range of T-shirts with messages such as "fcuk this", "hot as fcuk", "mile high fcuk", "fcuk me", etc.</p>
<p>In 2009, the European Union's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OHIM" title="OHIM">OHIM</a> trade marks agency disallowed a German brewery to market a beer called "<a href="/wiki/Fucking_Hell" title="Fucking Hell">Fucking Hell</a>". They sued, and on 26 March 2010 got permission to market the beer. They claim that it is actually named after the Austrian village of <a href="/wiki/Fucking,_Austria" title="Fucking, Austria">Fucking</a> and the German term for light beer, <i>hell</i> (which is simply the word for "light in colour").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_fuck">Holy fuck</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">This section is about the English-language term. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Holy_fuck_(disambiguation)" title="Holy fuck (disambiguation)">Holy fuck (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>"Holy fuck" is an example of 'liturgical profanity' used interjectionally to express anger, contempt, disgust, or amazement. Usually vulgar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-0xford_slang_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-0xford_slang-58">[58]</a></sup> Noted by academics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Impertinent_Gallop_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Impertinent_Gallop-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fat_Rice_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fat_Rice-60">[60]</a></sup> and used in literature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crobsie_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crobsie-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tested_Faith_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tested_Faith-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shadow_worlds_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shadow_worlds-63">[63]</a></sup> deriving its power from a combination of the sacred, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy" title="Holy">holy</a>, and the profane, fuck. An exclamation, similar to "<a href="/wiki/Shit" title="Shit">holy shit</a>", but more offensive, also used informally for sex within a religious context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Male_lust_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Male_lust-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machine_mistranslation">Machine mistranslation</span></h3>
<p>The word <i>fuck</i> occurs sometimes in Chinese/English bilingual public notices in China as a machine translation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese" title="Simplified Chinese">Simplified Chinese</a> character <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E5%B9%B2" title="干">干</a> (干), which replaced the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese" title="Traditional Chinese">Traditional Chinese</a> characters 干 meaning "a pole", 幹 meaning "to do" or "to work" (and having a secondary slang meaning of "to fuck"), and 乾 meaning "dried". (Traditional Chinese 乾 can also be pronounced <i>qian</i>, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> stem word meaning "sky".) Some examples are "spread to fuck the fruit" for "loose dried fruit", "fuck to adjust the area" for "dry seasonings section", and "fuck the certain price of goods" for "dry foods price counter". The fault occurred in some versions of commonly used Chinese to English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machine_translators" title="Machine translators">machine translators</a>, for example Jinshan (金山 = "Gold Mountain") by <a href="/wiki/Kingsoft" title="Kingsoft">Kingsoft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="F-bomb">F-bomb</span></h3>
<p>The term "F-bomb" usually refers to the unanticipated use of the word "fuck" in a particularly inappropriate setting, such as public media. The term was first reported in a newspaper (<a href="/wiki/Newsday" title="Newsday">Newsday</a>) in 1988 when Hall of Fame baseball catcher <a href="/wiki/Gary_Carter" title="Gary Carter">Gary Carter</a> used it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In 2012 it was listed, for the first time, in the mainstream <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster%27s_Collegiate_Dictionary" title="Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary">Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship">Censorship</span></h2>
<p>On August 19, 1969 the <a href="/wiki/Acid_rock" title="Acid rock">acid rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Airplane" title="Jefferson Airplane">Jefferson Airplane</a> played their song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/We_Can_Be_Together" title="We Can Be Together">We Can Be Together</a>" uncensored on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dick_Cavett_Show" title="The Dick Cavett Show">The Dick Cavett Show</a></i>, including the 1960s countercultural slogan "Up against the wall, motherfucker!" (which was also <a href="/wiki/Up_Against_the_Wall_Motherfuckers" title="Up Against the Wall Motherfuckers">the name of an anarchist group</a> at that time). This was the first appearance of the word on U.S. television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The word "motherfucker" was also censored on the song's printed lyrics that came with the <a href="/wiki/Volunteers_(Jefferson_Airplane_album)" title="Volunteers (Jefferson Airplane album)">Volunteers album</a> as "fred."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Films edited for broadcast use matching <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemisms</a> so that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lip_synch" title="Lip synch">lip synching</a> will not be thrown off. In the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Lebowski" title="The Big Lebowski">The Big Lebowski</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Goodman" title="John Goodman">John Goodman</a>'s character repeatedly yells, "This is what happens when you fuck a stranger in the ass" while trashing a car. It was censored on television as "This is what happens when you find a stranger in the Alps."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom_of_expression">Freedom of expression</span></h3>
<p>In 1971, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> decided that the public display of <i>fuck</i> is protected under the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth amendments</a> and cannot be made a criminal offense. In 1968, Paul Robert Cohen had been convicted of "disturbing the peace" for wearing a jacket with "fuck the draft" on it (in reference to conscription in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>). The conviction was upheld by the court of appeals and overturned by the supreme court. <i><a href="/wiki/Cohen_v._California" title="Cohen v. California">Cohen v. California</a></i>, <i>403 U.S. 15</i> (1971).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_alternatives">Common alternatives</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Minced_oath" title="Minced oath">Minced oath</a></div>
<p>In conversation or writing, reference to or use of the word <i>fuck</i> may be replaced by any of many alternative words or phrases, including "the F-word" or "the F-bomb" (a play on "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">A-bomb</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">H-bomb</a>"), or simply, <i>eff</i> or <i>f</i> (as in "What the eff" or "You effing fool", "What the F" or "You f'ing fool"). Also, there are many commonly used substitutes, such as <i>flipping</i>, <i>frigging</i>, <i>fricking</i>, <i>freaking</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Feck" title="Feck">feck</a></i>, <i>fudge</i>, <i>flaming</i> or any of a number of similar-sounding nonsense words. In print, there are alternatives such as, "F***", "F––k", etc.; or a string of non-<a href="/wiki/Alphanumeric" title="Alphanumeric">alphanumeric</a> characters, for example, "@$#*%!" and similar (especially favored in <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A replacement word used mainly on the Internet is "<a href="/wiki/Fsck" title="Fsck">fsck</a>", derived from the name of the Unix <i>f</i>ile <i>s</i>ystem <i>c</i>hec<i>k</i>ing utility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica" title="Battlestar Galactica">Battlestar Galactica</a></i> the bowdlerized form '<a href="/wiki/Frak_(expletive)" title="Frak (expletive)">frack</a>' (spelt '<a href="/wiki/Frak_(expletive)" title="Frak (expletive)">f-r-a-k</a>' in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(reimagining)" title="Battlestar Galactica (reimagining)">reimagined 2003 version</a>) was used as a substitute for fuck. The word was sometimes jokingly used as a curse by fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Similarly, the word "frell" is used as a substitute on the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Farscape" title="Farscape">Farscape</a></i>, and Dr. <a href="/wiki/Elliot_Reid" title="Elliot Reid">Elliot Reid</a> (played by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Chalke" title="Sarah Chalke">Sarah Chalke</a>) has frequently used the substitute "frick" on the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Scrubs_(TV_series)" title="Scrubs (TV series)">Scrubs</a></i>.</p>
<p>The phrase <i><a href="/wiki/Feck" title="Feck">feck</a></i> is a common substitute for <i>fuck</i> in Ireland, where it is considered to be less rude, though still not acceptable in many contexts. It has come into occasional use across the UK in the last 15 years as a result of its frequent use in the <i><a href="/wiki/Father_Ted" title="Father Ted">Father Ted</a></i> comedy series. Although the word is considered to be equally as rude as <i>fuck</i>, its appearance in <i>Father Ted</i> and in a <a href="/wiki/C%26C_Group#21st_Century" title="C&amp;C Group">Magner's Cider</a> advert suggest the opposite.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
