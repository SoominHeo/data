<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">G.722</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>G.722</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">ITU-T</a> standard 7 kHz <a href="/wiki/Wideband_audio" title="Wideband audio">Wideband audio</a> <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a> operating at 48, 56 and 64 kbit/s. It was approved by ITU-T in November 1988. Technology of the codec is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-band_ADPCM" title="Sub-band ADPCM">sub-band ADPCM</a> (SB-ADPCM).</p>
<p>G722 provides improved speech quality due to a wider speech bandwidth of 50–7000 Hz compared to narrowband speech coders like <a href="/wiki/G.711" title="G.711">G.711</a> which in general are optimized for <a href="/wiki/Plain_old_telephone_service" title="Plain old telephone service">POTS</a> wireline quality of 300–3400 Hz. G.722 sample audio data at a rate of 16 kHz (using 14 bits), double that of traditional telephony interfaces, which results in superior audio quality and clarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G_series1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-G_series1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other ITU-T 7 kHz <a href="/wiki/Wideband_audio" title="Wideband audio">wideband</a> codecs include <a href="/wiki/G.722.1" title="G.722.1">G.722.1</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.722.2" title="G.722.2">G.722.2</a>. These codecs are not variants of G.722 and they use different patented compression technologies. G.722.1 is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siren_Codec" title="Siren Codec">Siren</a> codecs and offers lower bit-rate compressions (24 kbit/s or 32 kbit/s). A more recent G.722.2, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMR-WB" title="AMR-WB">AMR-WB</a> ("Adaptive Multirate Wideband") is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACELP" title="ACELP">ACELP</a> and offers even lower bit-rate compressions (6.6 kbit/s to 23.85 kbit/s),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G_series1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-G_series1-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as the ability to quickly adapt to varying compressions as the network topography mutates. In the latter case, bandwidth is automatically conserved when <a href="/wiki/Network_congestion" title="Network congestion">network congestion</a> is high. When congestion returns to a normal level, a lower-compression, higher-quality bitrate is restored.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.722&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>G.722 is an ITU standard codec that provides 7 kHz <a href="/wiki/Wideband_audio" title="Wideband audio">wideband</a> audio at data rates from 48, 56 and 64 kbit/s. This is useful for <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">voice over IP</a> applications, such as on a <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a> where network bandwidth is readily available, and offers a significant improvement in speech quality over older narrowband codecs such as <a href="/wiki/G.711" title="G.711">G.711</a>, without an excessive increase in implementation complexity. Environments where bandwidth is more constrained may prefer one of the more bitrate-efficient codecs, such as G.722.1 (Siren7) or G.722.2 (AMR-WB).</p>
<p>G.722 has also been widely used by radio broadcasters for sending commentary-grade audio over a single 56 or 64 kbit/s ISDN B-channel (the <a href="/wiki/Least_significant_bit" title="Least significant bit">least significant bit</a> is dropped on 56kb circuits).</p>
<p>G.722 works by having the inbound voice signal pass through a digital filter that splits the audio signal into 0 Hz-to-4 kHz and 4 kHz-to-8 kHz audio bands. These sub-bands are then encoded using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SB-ADPCM" title="SB-ADPCM">sub-band ADPCM</a>. Most of the human voice energy is concentrated in the lower half of the audio band (0–4 kHz), so 48 kbit/s of the bandwidth is dedicated to the lower sub-band and the other 16 kbit/s is allocated to the higher sub-band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G_series1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-G_series1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cisco_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cisco-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since August 2014">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RTP_encapsulation">RTP encapsulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.722&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: RTP encapsulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>G.722 VoIP is typically carried in <a href="/wiki/Real-time_Transport_Protocol" title="Real-time Transport Protocol">RTP</a> payload type 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Note that <a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">IANA</a> records the clock rate for type 9 G.722 as 8 kHz (instead of 16 kHz), RFC3551<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> clarifies that this is due to a historical error and is retained in order to maintain backward compatibility. Consequently correct implementations represent the value 8,000 where required but encode and decode audio at 16 kHz.</p>
<p>Whilst G.722 allows for bitrates of 64, 56 and 48 kbit/s, in practice, data is encoded at 64 kbit/s, with bits from the lower sub-band being used to encode auxiliary data. The greater the number of bits allocated to aux data, the lower the bit rate.</p>
