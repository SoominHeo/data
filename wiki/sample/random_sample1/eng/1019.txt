<p><b>Gwendolyn Willow Wilson</b> (born August 31, 1982), known professionally as <b>G. Willow Wilson</b>, is an American comics writer, prose author, essayist, and journalist. She lived in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> during her early twenties; her first <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a>, <i>Cairo</i> (Vertigo, 2007), was based there and was listed as a top graphic novel for teens by both the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i>. Her comic series <i>Air</i> was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Award</a>, and her first novel, <i>Alif the Unseen</i>, won the 2013 <a href="/wiki/World_Fantasy_Award" title="World Fantasy Award">World Fantasy Award</a>.</p>
<p>Wilson is herself Muslim, and writes <a href="/wiki/Ms._Marvel_(Kamala_Khan)" title="Ms. Marvel (Kamala Khan)"><i>Ms. Marvel</i></a><i>,</i> a comic series starring a 16-year-old Muslim <a href="/wiki/Shapeshifting" title="Shapeshifting">shapeshifter</a> <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamala_Khan" title="Kamala Khan">Kamala Khan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G._Willow_Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After converting to Islam while attending <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, Wilson moved to <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, where she contributed articles to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Monthly" title="Atlantic Monthly">Atlantic Monthly</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Post" title="National Post">National Post</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cairo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cairo-1">[1]</a></sup> She was also a regular contributor to the now-defunct Egyptian opposition weekly <i>Cairo Magazine</i>. Wilson was the first Western journalist to be granted a private interview with <a href="/wiki/Ali_Gomaa" title="Ali Gomaa">Ali Gomaa</a> after his promotion to the position of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Mufti" title="Grand Mufti">Grand Mufti</a> of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Additionally, Wilson released a memoir titled <i>The Butterfly Mosque</i> about life in Egypt during the Mubarak regime, which was named a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle_Times" title="Seattle Times">Seattle Times</a></i> Best Book of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ala.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ala.org-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilson's writing career began from her work as a freelance music critic for <a href="/wiki/DigBoston" title="DigBoston">DigBoston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ala.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ala.org-3">[3]</a></sup> Her first graphic novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Cairo_(comics)" title="Cairo (comics)">Cairo</a></i>, with art by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.K._Perker" title="M.K. Perker">M.K. Perker</a>, was published by <a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(DC_Comics)" title="Vertigo (DC Comics)">Vertigo</a> in 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cairo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cairo-1">[1]</a></sup> and named one of the best graphic novels of 2007 by <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Edmonton_Journal" title="The Edmonton Journal">The Edmonton Journal</a>/<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CanWest_News&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CanWest News (page does not exist)">CanWest News</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Comics_Worth_Reading&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Comics Worth Reading (page does not exist)">Comics Worth Reading</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The paperback edition of <i>Cairo</i> was named one of Best Graphic Novels for High School Students in 2008 by <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i>, and one of 2009's Top Ten Graphic Novels for Teens by the American Library Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009_Great_Graphic_Novels_for_Teens_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009_Great_Graphic_Novels_for_Teens-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her first ongoing comic series, <i><a href="/wiki/Air_(comics)" title="Air (comics)">Air</a></i>, launched by Vertigo in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> reunited her with Perker, and was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Award</a> for 'Best New Series' of 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> NPR named <i>Air</i> one of the top comics of 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and it also received acclaim from the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fairfield_Weekly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fairfield Weekly (page does not exist)">Fairfield Weekly</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Comic_Book_Resources" title="Comic Book Resources">Comic Book Resources</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Marie_Claire" title="Marie Claire">Marie Claire</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Library_Journal" title="Library Journal">Library Journal</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Other works for DC include fill-in issues #704 and 706 of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superman_(comics)" title="Superman (comics)">Superman</a></i>, the five-issue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_series" title="Limited series">mini-series</a> <i>Vixen: Return of the Lion</i>, starring the <a href="/wiki/Justice_League" title="Justice League">Justice League</a> member <a href="/wiki/Vixen_(comics)" title="Vixen (comics)">Vixen</a> with art by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CAFU_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CAFU (comics) (page does not exist)">CAFU</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Outsiders_(comics)" title="Outsiders (comics)">The Outsiders</a></i>.</p>
<p>Wilson then wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Mystic_(comics)" title="Mystic (comics)">Mystic</a></i>, a four-issue miniseries for <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> with art by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Lopez_(artist)" title="David Lopez (artist)">David Lopez</a>. Although a <a href="/wiki/CrossGen" title="CrossGen">CrossGen</a> revival, Willow's <i>Mystic</i> bears little resemblance to its previous incarnation.</p>
<p>Her debut novel <i>Alif the Unseen</i> (Grove/Atlantic) won the 2013 World Fantasy Award for best novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Marvel debuted a new <i><a href="/wiki/Ms._Marvel_(Kamala_Khan)" title="Ms. Marvel (Kamala Khan)">Ms. Marvel</a></i> series written by Wilson. The book stars <a href="/wiki/Ms._Marvel_(Kamala_Khan)" title="Ms. Marvel (Kamala Khan)">Kamala Khan</a>, a Muslim teenager living in <a href="/wiki/Jersey_City,_New_Jersey" title="Jersey City, New Jersey">Jersey City, New Jersey</a>, who takes up the mantle after the previous Ms. Marvel, <a href="/wiki/Carol_Danvers" title="Carol Danvers">Carol Danvers</a>, took up the name <a href="/wiki/Captain_Marvel_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Captain Marvel (Marvel Comics)">Captain Marvel</a>.</p>
