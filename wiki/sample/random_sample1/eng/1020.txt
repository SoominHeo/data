<p><b>G4S plc</b> (formerly <b>Group 4 Securicor</b>) is a British <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Private_security_company" title="Private security company">security services</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Crawley" title="Crawley">Crawley</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company was set up in 2004 when London-based company Securicor amalgamated with Danish business Group 4 Falck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linkedin.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-linkedin.com-4">[4]</a></sup> The company offers a range of services, including the supply of security personnel, monitoring equipment, response units and secure prisoner transportation. G4S also works with governments overseas to deliver security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linkedin.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-linkedin.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the world's largest security company measured by revenues and has operations in around 125 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-overview-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> With 618,000 employees, it is world's third-largest private employer, the largest European and African private employer, and among the largest on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-overview-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> G4S was founded in 2004 by the merger of the UK-based <a href="/wiki/Securicor" title="Securicor">Securicor plc</a> with the Denmark-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_4_Falck" title="Group 4 Falck">Group 4 Falck</a>.</p>
<p>G4S has a primary listing on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_250_Index" title="FTSE 250 Index">FTSE 250 Index</a>, having been relegated from the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a> in December 2015.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>G4S has its origins in a guarding business founded in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> in 1901 by Marius Hogrefe, originally known as <i>København Frederiksberg Nattevagt</i> (English: Copenhagen and <a href="/wiki/Frederiksberg" title="Frederiksberg">Frederiksberg</a> Night Watch) and subsequently renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Falck_(Denmark)" title="Falck (Denmark)">Falck</a></i> (English: <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">Falcon</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Group_4_(company)" title="Group 4 (company)">Group 4</a>, a security firm formed in the 1960s, merged with <i>Falck</i> to form <i>Group 4 Falck</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> and by 2000 the company was described as "the world's largest private security systems company".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2002 <i>Group 4 Falck</i> went on to buy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wackenhut" title="Wackenhut">The Wackenhut Corporation</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_to_2010">2004 to 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2004 to 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>G4S was formed in July 2004, when Group 4 Falck's security business merged with <a href="/wiki/Securicor" title="Securicor">Securicor</a> to create Group 4 Securicor and began trading on both the London and <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Stock_Exchange" title="Copenhagen Stock Exchange">Copenhagen Stock Exchanges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2005, Lars Nørby Johansen was succeeded as chief executive by Nick Buckles and, in 2006, the new G4S brand identity was rolled out across its business worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the same year, Alf Duch-Pedersen succeeded Jorgen Philip-Sorensen to become the non-executive chairman of the business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, 2007 and 2008, G4S was the subject of a global campaign by union workers alleging that its subsidiaries undermine labour and human rights standards. Some of these groups were organised under the banner of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEIU" title="SEIU">SEIU</a>-funded Focus on Group 4 Securicor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This group supported protests at Group 4 Securicor's annual general meeting in London in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2006, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=US_State_Department_Report_on_Human_Rights_in_Indonesia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="US State Department Report on Human Rights in Indonesia (page does not exist)">US State Department Report on Human Rights in Indonesia</a></i>, released in March 2006, featured the ongoing dispute in Jakarta with Group4/Securicor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In July 2006, the Indonesian Securicor workers had a substantial win – but the campaigners continue to support other Group 4 Securicor workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The company disputed these claims and pointed to its strong relationships with unions around the world, including the GMB in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2008, it was announced that G4S were taking over Scottish <a href="/wiki/Rock_Steady_Group" title="Rock Steady Group">Rock Steady Group</a> – who steward at major sporting and music events mostly in the UK. Rock Steady events have included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live8" title="Live8">Live8</a> concerts in London, Scottish FA Cup Final &amp; the <a href="/wiki/Download_Festival" title="Download Festival">Download Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In April 2008, G4S acquired RONCO Consulting Corporation, one of the world's premier humanitarian and commercial mine action, ordnance disposal and security companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2008, G4S acquired <a href="/wiki/ArmorGroup" title="ArmorGroup">ArmorGroup International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> GSL, a provider of outsourced justice services, was also acquired by G4S in May 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, in the same month, G4S acquired Serbian company <a href="/wiki/G4S_Secure_Solutions_d.o.o." title="G4S Secure Solutions d.o.o.">Progard Securitas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, G4S also acquired Touchcom, Inc. for US$23 million. Touchcom, Inc. is located in the Burlington/Bedford, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, area. The rebranding of Touchcom, Inc. to G4S was completed by 1 January 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2008, G4S and <a href="/wiki/UNI_Global_Union" title="UNI Global Union">UNI Global Union</a>, announced the launch of an <i>Ethical Employment Partnership</i>, which will drive improvements in standards across the global security industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Simultaneous to this, G4S and the SEIU reached an agreement to end their long dispute and establish a framework to work together in the interest of employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, G4S continued to acquire companies: Secura Monde International Limited and Shiremoor International Engineering Limited, together, the UK's leading specialist banknote and high security technical and commercial advisory companies; All Star International for $60 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> one of the premier facilities management and base operations support companies providing services to the US government; Adesta, US-based provider of integrated security systems and communication systems; and Hill &amp; Associates Consultants Limited, Asia's leading provider of specialist risk-mitigation consulting services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4stberga_helicopter_robbery" title="Västberga helicopter robbery">Västberga helicopter robbery</a> occurred on 23 September 2009 at 05:15 CET when a G4S cash service depot was robbed in Västberga in southern <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the autumn of 2009, G4S personnel in Australia went on strike, arguing that the company had subjected them to low pay and poor working conditions. The strike imperiled the operations of the court system in the state of Victoria. The guards provided entry-point screening for weapons and bombs in both the County Court and Magistrates Court, as well as additional security in the court rooms themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_2010">Since 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Since 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>G4S acquired the South African security systems company Skycom in September 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Then in April 2011, it bought the Cotswold Group, a surveillance and investigations company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions_of_Guidance_Monitoring_and_Chubb">Acquisitions of Guidance Monitoring and Chubb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acquisitions of Guidance Monitoring and Chubb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In April 2011, G4S acquired Guidance Monitoring, an international designer and manufacturer of electronic monitoring technologies, including hardware and software used for offender monitoring and tracking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In December 2011, G4S acquired the assets of Chubb Emergency Response, a large key holding company in the UK. The deal was finalised on 17 December 2011. The business has now been integrated into their existing key holding and response service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aborted_acquisition_of_ISS">Aborted acquisition of ISS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aborted acquisition of ISS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 17 October 2011, G4S announced it would purchase the Denmark-based facilities management group <a href="/wiki/ISS_A/S" title="ISS A/S">ISS A/S</a> for £5.2 billion. The acquisition would have created the world's largest facilities management company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Within two weeks, the deal was dropped due to lack of shareholder support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> G4S's chief, Nick Buckles, recounted the events of the failed acquisition, which cost the company tens of millions of dollars, as "...one of the most bruising experiences of my life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> A combination of institutional investors who led the response and the minority shareholders who followed, objected to a variety of factors, not the least of which was the additional leverage and debt the deal would introduce to G4S's balance sheet. General consensus is that lack of planning for shareholder response on the G4S side doomed the deal. Furthermore, many investors and analysts questioned why the company would want to purchase a firm so far removed from its area of expertise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Alf Duch-Pedersen resigned as G4S chairman after the failure of this acquisition and was replaced by John Connolly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_and_other_changes">Management and other changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Management and other changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In March 2012, G4S announced that it would sell the "struggling" G4S Government Solutions business (the former Wackenhut (WSI) and All Star business) to exit the US government services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In May 2013, Buckles resigned as chief executive of G4S with £1.2 million payoff and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Almanza" title="Ashley Almanza">Ashley Almanza</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_to_meet_London_2012_Security_Contract">Failure to meet London 2012 Security Contract</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Failure to meet London 2012 Security Contract">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 12 July 2012, it was announced that 3,500 British troops would be deployed at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">2012 Summer Olympics</a> due to a shortage of adequately trained G4S security staff, with Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Keith_Vaz" title="Keith Vaz">Keith Vaz</a> claiming that, "G4S has let the country down and we have literally had to send in the troops".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Shares in G4S later dropped 9 percent after the firm claimed it faced a possible £50 million loss as a result of failing to provide sufficient trained staff for the 2012 Olympic Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On 17 July, the company's chief executive, Nick Buckles, appeared before the <a href="/wiki/Home_Affairs_Select_Committee" title="Home Affairs Select Committee">Home Affairs Select Committee</a>, where he apologised for the organisational failings, expressed regret at having taken on the Olympic security contract, and agreed in principle to pay bonuses to soldiers drafted at the last moment as replacement security staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Pressed by Labour MP <a href="/wiki/David_Winnick" title="David Winnick">David Winnick</a>, he was forced to admit that organisational situation had become a "humiliating shambles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teen_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-teen-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Newcastle, G4S were replaced by 500 staff from local security firms for Olympic events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In Scotland, G4S were stripped of the security role with it passing to Strathclyde Police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> At Dorney Lake, the Olympic rowing and canoeing venue, a G4S manager reported G4S radios were not working so staff were relying on personal mobile phones to communicate; G4S confirmed the venue was being manned by military personnel after 66 percent of rostered G4S staff were failing to show. The venue manager reported parts of the CCTV system had already been replaced by Army patrols and a complete army takeover was "on the cards".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 July 2012, a contractor reported that a third of his expected staff had not turned up; instead he was sent a group of mainly female, teenage students with minimal training, whom he turned away as he did not feel comfortable leaving them for night duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 8 August 2012, G4S announced that it had finally contracted enough employees to fully fulfil its contract for the Olympic games. Although missing its initial target of 10,000, G4S announced the sending of 7,000 personnel each day to Olympic venues, in a way they felt would allow them to fully secure each venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequent to the Olympics contract failures, the chief constables of Bedfordshire, Cambridgeshire and Hertfordshire were to recommend abandoning outsourcing work to G4S. They were backed by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Paice" title="Jim Paice">Jim Paice</a>, the agriculture minister and Conservative MP for South East Cambridgeshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the Olympics games, G4S provided a donation of £2.5 million to military charities as a goodwill gesture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_of_Wackenhut_Pakistan">Sale of Wackenhut Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sale of Wackenhut Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>G4S announced in late August 2012 that it would be selling its Pakistan division, Wackenhut Pakistan Limited, to its chairman <a href="/wiki/Ikram_Sehgal" title="Ikram Sehgal">Ikram Sehgal</a> for a figure of around $10 million, according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPL_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPL-54">[54]</a></sup></i> <a href="/wiki/Ikram_Sehgal" title="Ikram Sehgal">Sehgal</a> disputed this figure, calling it "speculative" in a statement to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Express_Tribune" title="Express Tribune">Express Tribune</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPL_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPL-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Eye_Award">Public Eye Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public Eye Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>G4S was one of the nominees for the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Public_Eye_Award" title="Public Eye Award">Public Eye Award</a>, a counter-event created by NGOs, which awards the worst company of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Employee_Omar_Mateen">Employee Omar Mateen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Employee Omar Mateen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>G4S was in the news in June 2016 because of employee <a href="/wiki/Omar_Mateen" title="Omar Mateen">Omar Mateen</a>, the gunman behind the worst mass-shooting incident in United States history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> who was employed as a security guard by the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> XXX In October 2006, Mateen had begun working as a recruit for the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Department_of_Corrections" title="Florida Department of Corrections">Florida Department of Corrections</a>, being assigned to the Martin Correctional Institution. In a letter explaining his juvenile record as part of his successful application, Mateen explained the incident of when he was arrested at school when he was fourteen. Following the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_shooting" title="Virginia Tech shooting">Virginia Tech shooting</a> in April 2007, Mateen suggested in a corrections officer training class that he would bring a gun to class. P.H. Skipper, who was the warden at institution, wrote that "in light of the tragic events at Virginia Tech officer Mateen's inquiry about bringing a weapon to class is at best extremely disturbing". Days later on April 27, 2007, Mateen "was involuntarily dismissed" from the program and never became a certified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrections_officer" title="Corrections officer">corrections officer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mateen then worked for British-based security firm <a href="/wiki/G4S_Secure_Solutions" title="G4S Secure Solutions">G4S Secure Solutions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter, Florida</a>, from September 2007 until his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The company said two screenings of Mateen—one conducted upon hiring and the other in 2013—had raised no red flags.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Nonetheless, G4S removed Mateen from his job post at a courthouse because of threats he made towards coworkers, including one threat where he claimed he would have <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">al-Qaeda</a> kill a deputy's family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPTV.Present_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPTV.Present-65">[65]</a></sup> Mateen had claimed that his coworkers and courthouse deputies were making racist comments towards him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPTV.Present_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPTV.Present-65">[65]</a></sup> Despite this, G4S "kept Mateen as an employee" but moved him "to a kiosk at a gated community in Palm Beach County."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clerical_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-clerical-66">[66]</a></sup> They never informed the community or its property management company about why he was transferred there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPTV.Present_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-WPTV.Present-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mateen held an active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concealed_carry_permit" title="Concealed carry permit">concealed carry permit</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Security_guard" title="Security guard">armed security guard</a> license. He was said to have passed a psychological test and medical exam performed by Dr. Syed Shafeeq Rahman, who had close ties with Mateen's family. It was also noted that Mateen had no criminal record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> According to licensing records, he was a proficient shooter who scored in the 98th <a href="/wiki/Percentile" title="Percentile">percentile</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9mm" title="9mm">9mm</a> semiautomatic pistol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the shooting, a psychologist, who was said to have evaluated and cleared Mateen for his firearms license in 2007, according to the records of the security company G4S, denied ever meeting him or having lived in Florida at the time, and said she had stopped her practice in Florida in January 2006. G4S admitted Mateen's form had a "clerical error", and clarified that he had instead been cleared by Rahman, who was from the same firm that bought the wrongly-named doctor's practice. Rahman had not interviewed Mateen, but evaluated the results of a standard test used in the screening he undertook before being hired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clerical_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-clerical-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_HM_Prison_Birmingham_riot">2016 HM Prison Birmingham riot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2016 HM Prison Birmingham riot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 16 December 2016, some 600 prisoners took part in a riot at <a href="/wiki/HM_Prison_Birmingham" title="HM Prison Birmingham">HM Prison Birmingham</a>, a prison in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, operated by G4S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halliday_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halliday-69">[69]</a></sup> The disturbance was described as the worst in Britain since the 1990 Strangeways riot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halliday_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Halliday-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haddad_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haddad-70">[70]</a></sup> The national chairman of the <a href="/wiki/POA_(trade_union)" title="POA (trade union)">Prison Officers Association</a> said more than 30 guards at the prison had quit in the weeks before the riot and that further prison convulsions were inevitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halliday_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-Halliday-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haddad_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haddad-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a statement to <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Justice" title="Secretary of State for Justice">Secretary of State for Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Truss" title="Elizabeth Truss">Elizabeth Truss</a> said that insufficient staffing lay at the root of the twelve-hour Birmingham riot and that G4S would have to pay the costs of deploying ten highly-trained public sector Tornado elite teams, who were sent to quell the riot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-71">[71]</a></sup> John Thornhill, the president of the independent monitoring board, agreed that insufficient staff had caused rising levels of violence in prisons in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, leading to "unrestrained violence" and the transfer of a 'large number of prisoners ... to other prisons that are already stretched with their own problems and staffing issues."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>G4S's core services include 'manned security services' – where it provides trained and screened security officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The company also provides 'security systems' such as access control, CCTV, intruder alarms, ﬁre detection, video analytics and security and building systems integration technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> 'Monitoring and response services' is another core service, where G4S provides key holding, mobile security patrol and response services and alarm receiving and monitoring facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> G4S also provides 'secure facilities services' which includes integrated facilities services for entire sites or estates for commercial customers and governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The business provides 'risk management and consultancy services' which also includes mine detection and clearance services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> G4S also provides electronic tagging and monitoring of offenders at home or in the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The company provides back-office support functions for police forces, support for front-line policing, including the provision of custody suite services and forensic medical services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> It also manages juvenile and adult custody centres. This includes the management of all aspects of a facility and those held within the facility – similar centres are also used for the detention of asylum applicants. Prisoner escorting is another core service. G4S transports prisoners and asylum applicants between courts, police stations and custody centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar2012_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar2012-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 2000s, the company (then still known as Group 4 Falck) bid unsuccessfully for the right to operate several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_franchising_in_Great_Britain" title="Rail franchising in Great Britain">railway franchises in the United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, G4S Forensic and Medical Services were awarded a three-year contract to run services providing medical examinations and counselling for victims of rape and sexual assault in the West Midlands. It has been working in this area since 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> The firm provides patient transport services for NHS Trusts including <a href="/wiki/Barking,_Havering_and_Redbridge_University_Hospitals_NHS_Trust" title="Barking, Havering and Redbridge University Hospitals NHS Trust">Barking, Havering and Redbridge University Hospitals NHS Trust</a>; <a href="/wiki/Surrey_and_Sussex_Healthcare_NHS_Trust" title="Surrey and Sussex Healthcare NHS Trust">Surrey and Sussex Healthcare NHS Trust</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Healthcare_NHS_Trust" title="St George's Healthcare NHS Trust">St George's Healthcare NHS Trust</a> (£2.7 million a year), and <a href="/wiki/Epsom_and_St_Helier_University_Hospitals_NHS_Trust" title="Epsom and St Helier University Hospitals NHS Trust">Epsom and St Helier University Hospitals NHS Trust</a> (£3.5 million a year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_structure">Operating structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Operating structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>G4S segments its business into two areas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar2012_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar2012-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/G4S_Secure_Solutions" title="G4S Secure Solutions">Secure Solutions</a></i> which includes services for commercial and government organisations in areas such as risk consulting, manned security, and security systems.</li>
<li><i>Cash Solutions</i> which is the outsourcing of cash cycle management for banks, financial institutions and retailers – making up 18 percent of G4S's turnover.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_social_responsibility">Corporate social responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Corporate social responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, G4S became a signatory to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Global_Compact" title="UN Global Compact">UN Global Compact</a>, the international standard to promote socially responsible business behaviour including human rights, labour, environment and anti-corruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> In 2013, G4S launched a Human Rights Policy, co-authored by Dr Hugo Slim, an internationally recognised human rights expert, aiming to align the company's practices with 'UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights' and to introduce additional global guidelines for areas not currently covered by existing standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>G4S is a founder signatory of the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Conduct_for_Private_Security_Service_Providers" title="International Code of Conduct for Private Security Service Providers">International Code of Conduct for Private Security Service Providers</a> (ICoC), a multi-stakeholder initiative convened by the Swiss government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorships">Sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G4S&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sponsorships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, G4S began the G4S 4teen, an award-winning<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> program to support 14 young athletes around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-89">[89]</a></sup> G4S provided financial, social and logistical support to a number of athletes taking part in London 2012, including Colombian cyclist and 2012 and 2016 Olympic gold medallist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mariana_Pajon" title="Mariana Pajon">Mariana Pajon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Kenyan long-distance runner <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Korikwiang" title="Pauline Korikwiang">Pauline Korikwiang</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and Estonia's former discus thrower and shot-putter, now-<a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Bengals" title="Cincinnati Bengals">Cincinnati Bengals</a> defensive end <a href="/wiki/Margus_Hunt" title="Margus Hunt">Margus Hunt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
