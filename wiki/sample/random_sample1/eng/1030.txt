<p><b>GOG.com</b> (formerly <b>Good Old Games</b>) is a digital distribution platform service for <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> and films, operated by GOG Limited, a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/CD_Projekt" title="CD Projekt">CD Projekt</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Nicosia" title="Nicosia">Nicosia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, with an office in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> GOG delivers video games through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Rights_Management" title="Digital Rights Management">DRM</a>-free digital platform for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. In March 2012, it began selling more recent titles such as <i><a href="/wiki/Alan_Wake" title="Alan Wake">Alan Wake</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed_(video_game)" title="Assassin's Creed (video game)">Assassin's Creed</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universe_of_Metro_2033" title="Universe of Metro 2033">Metro Redux series</a></i>, among many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_of_Good_Old_Games">Launch of Good Old Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch of Good Old Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, Marcin Iwiński and Michał Kiciński founded <a href="/wiki/CD_Projekt" title="CD Projekt">CD Projekt</a>. They originally imported games from wholesalers in America to sell in Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurog_cdprojekt_history_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurog_cdprojekt_history-6">[6]</a></sup> In late 2008, CD Projekt launched GOG.com under the moniker Good Old Games. The initial goal was to release classic games to a modern audience without <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a> restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspot_gog_six_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gspot_gog_six-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_stunt_and_relaunch">Marketing stunt and relaunch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marketing stunt and relaunch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During a period of days from September 19 to 22, 2010, the GOG.com website was disabled leaving behind messages on the web site and their <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> accounts that the site had been closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A spokesman for Good Old Games reiterated that the site is not being shut down, and confirmed news would be forthcoming about changes to the service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A clarification posted on the site on September 20, 2010, said they had to shut down the site temporarily "due to business and technical reasons", with industry journalists believing the shutdown may be related to the nature of <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">DRM</a>-free strategy, based on Twitter messages from the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On September 22, 2010, GOG.com revealed that this shutdown was a marketing hoax as part of the site coming out of beta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamer_gog_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamer_gog-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The site's management, aware of the reactions to the fake closure, stated: "First of all we'd like to apologize to everyone who felt deceived or harmed in any way by the closedown of GOG.com. As a small company we don't have a huge marketing budget and this is why we could not miss a chance to generate some buzz around an event as big as launching a brand new version of our website and even more important, bringing back <i>Baldur's Gate</i> to life!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamer_gog_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamer_gog-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site returned on September 23, 2010, with an improved storefront and additional benefits, as outlined during a webcast presentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> During the presentation, GOG.com's co-founder Marcin Iwinski and managing director Guillaume Rambourg had dressed as <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> to atone for their sins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The relaunch of the site was considered by Rambourg to have been successful, having brought new customers that were previously unaware of GOG.com.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As promised after its relaunch, GOG.com was able to offer several <a href="/wiki/Black_Isle_Studios" title="Black Isle Studios">Black Isle Studios</a> games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Baldur%27s_Gate" title="Baldur's Gate">Baldur's Gate</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Planescape:_Torment" title="Planescape: Torment">Planescape: Torment</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Icewind_Dale" title="Icewind Dale">Icewind Dale</a></i> which have previously been unreleased through any download service due to legal issues between the ownership of <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>-related games between <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a>, and other companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 27, 2012, Good Old Games announced that it was branching out to feature "AAA" and independent titles in addition to older games. The site was rebranded to GOG.com.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OS_X_and_Linux_support">OS X and Linux support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: OS X and Linux support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2012, GOG.com was announced to be bringing DRM-free games to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a>. This included the previously Steam exclusive (OS X version) <i>The Witcher</i> and <i>The Witcher 2</i>, both made by CD Projekt RED. GOG.com gathered user feedback in a community wishlist, and one of the most demanded feature requests was support for native Linux games, which gathered close to 15,000 votes before it was marked as "in progress".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Originally GOG.com representatives said, that there are technical and operational issues which make it harder than it seems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> however it's something they would love to do, and they have been looking at.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On March 18, 2014, GOG.com officially announced that they would be adding support for Linux, initially targeting <a href="/wiki/Ubuntu_(operating_system)" title="Ubuntu (operating system)">Ubuntu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux_Mint" title="Linux Mint">Linux Mint</a> in the fall of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On July 25, 2014, Linux support was released early, and 50 games were released compatible with the operating system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Several of the launch titles included games that were newly compatible with Linux, while most of the games already supported downloads made for the operating system on other distribution platforms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_to_DRM-free_video">Expansion to DRM-free video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion to DRM-free video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 27, 2014 GOG.com announced the launch of the new addition to their service – distribution of DRM-free films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> GOG.com offers DRM-free downloading in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mp4" title="Mp4">mp4</a> format and streaming of video in standard and DRM-free HTML fashion which doesn't bind users to any specific platforms or devices. The movies are made available in Full HD 1080p, 720p and 576p for limited bandwidth or download quotas; however, a few titles do not have the Full HD 1080p format available. GOG.com started with adding 21 documentaries about Internet culture and gaming. They also have plans for adding fiction films and series; according to GOG.com's managing director Guillaume Rambourg, they were in talks with many major studios. While studios' representatives liked the idea, they also were reluctant to let go of their current DRM approach until some other major studio would make the first step. Still GOG.com plan to work on overcoming the initial reluctance and moving DRM-free video forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 9, 2013, GOG.com introduced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Money-back_guarantee" title="Money-back guarantee">money-back guarantee</a> for the first 30 days if a customer faces unresolvable technical problems with a bought game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30days_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-30days-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Beginning April 2, 2015, GOG.com began to offer DRM-free downloads to holders of game keys from boxed copies of select games whose DRM validation systems no longer operate;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> examples are the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.T.A.L.K.E.R._(series)" title="S.T.A.L.K.E.R. (series)">S.T.A.L.K.E.R.</a></i> series and the <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion" title="Master of Orion">Master of Orion</a></i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Over $800,000 of retail game purchases have been redeemed through this system until 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GOG_Galaxy">GOG Galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: GOG Galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD_Projekt_RED" title="CD Projekt RED">CD Projekt RED</a> company update in June 2014, GOG.com announced that it would be bringing a <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a>-like client, GOG Galaxy, to Windows, Mac, and Linux platforms. The client is designed as a storefront, software delivery, and social network client, allowing players to buy and play games from GOG.com and share them with friends. Galaxy is also going to include an original multiplayer API, allowing developers to include the same kind of multiplayer functionality in GOG.com versions of games as on Steam. The client is optional and will retain the DRM-free objective of the GOG.com website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On October 15, 2014 the open multiplayer beta of the GOG Galaxy client was started, accompanied by the giveaway of <i><a href="/wiki/Aliens_versus_Predator_(1999_video_game)" title="Aliens versus Predator (1999 video game)">Alien vs Predator</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In July 2015 the GOG Galaxy beta client was reviewed favorably by the <a href="/wiki/PC_Gamer" title="PC Gamer">PC Gamer</a> magazine, especially noting the focus on user respect in comparison to a competing client.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GOG_Connect">GOG Connect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: GOG Connect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Revealed in June 2016, GOG Connect enables users with both GOG.com and Steam accounts to claim certain games they already own on Steam as part of their GOG.com library, allowing them to download the DRM-free version and other bonus items for that game offered by GOG.com. Not all such games are part of this offer, as it requires GOG.com to work with the game publishers to enable this. Further, the time to claim such games will be limited, though once a user has claimed their game on GOG.com, it otherwise remains in their library indefinitely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The offered <a href="/wiki/Digital_goods" title="Digital goods">digital goods</a> (<a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie" title="Movie">movies</a>) can be purchased and <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">downloaded online</a> and they are distributed without <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arsinterview_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-arsinterview-35">[35]</a></sup> The prices of products typically range from about $5 to $10 for older games, along with special offers in sales held several times a week. Some newer titles have a higher price. GOG.com's digital products can also be given to other persons via redeemable gift certificates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The user does not have to <a href="/wiki/Installation_(computer_programs)" title="Installation (computer programs)">install</a> special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Client_software" title="Client software">client software</a> to download or run the games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arspreview_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-arspreview-37">[37]</a></sup> although a <a href="/wiki/Download_manager" title="Download manager">download manager</a>, which is due to be phased out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goggalaxyfaq_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-goggalaxyfaq-38">[38]</a></sup> and the GOG Galaxy client, which is currently in beta, are available. After downloading, the customer is free to use the software for any personal use like installing on multiple devices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> archiving on any personal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storage_media" title="Storage media">storage media</a> for unlimited time, <a href="/wiki/Mod_(video_gaming)" title="Mod (video gaming)">modding</a> and patching; with the restriction that reselling and sharing is not permitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-academia.edu_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-academia.edu-40">[40]</a></sup> The software <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Installer" title="Installer">installers</a> are technically independent of the customer's GOG.com account, although still subject to GOG.com's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EULA" title="EULA">EULA</a>, where a "licensed, not sold" formulation is used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-academia.edu_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-academia.edu-40">[40]</a></sup> The "licensed, not sold" model frequently raises questions of ownership of digital goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doweownsteamgames_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-doweownsteamgames-41">[41]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> held that a copyright holder cannot oppose the resale of a digitally sold software, in accordance with the rule of copyright exhaustion on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_sale_doctrine" title="First sale doctrine">first sale</a> as ownership is transferred, and questions therefore the "licensed, not sold" EULA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecjforbes_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecjforbes-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to ensure <a href="/wiki/Software_incompatibility" title="Software incompatibility">compatibility</a> with newer versions of <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> and current PC hardware, games are <a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">pre-patched</a> and restored by GOG.com. This is achieved either by bundling such games with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open-source</a> <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulation</a> or compatibility software, such as <a href="/wiki/ScummVM" title="ScummVM">ScummVM</a> and <a href="/wiki/DOSBox" title="DOSBox">DOSBox</a>, or by <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source-code</a> adaption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gogrestoration_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-gogrestoration-48">[48]</a></sup> Additional <a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">compatibility fixes</a> are included if required, sometimes coming from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_community" title="Fan community">game community</a> itself (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community-made_patch" title="Community-made patch">community-made patch</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon2014_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-polygon2014-49">[49]</a></sup> Examples of games which have such additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bug_fix" title="Bug fix">fixes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Outcast_(video_game)" title="Outcast (video game)">Outcast</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Keeper_2" title="Dungeon Keeper 2">Dungeon Keeper 2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with the games, customers are also able to download numerous extra materials relating to the game they purchased. Often these extras include the game's <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a> (partly as <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">FLAC</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_wallpaper" title="Computer wallpaper">wallpapers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(computing)" title="Avatar (computing)">avatars</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instruction_manual_(gaming)" title="Instruction manual (gaming)">manuals</a>. GOG.com also offers full <a href="/wiki/Customer_support" title="Customer support">customer support</a> for all purchases and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Money-back_guarantee" title="Money-back guarantee">money-back guarantee</a> for the first 30 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30days_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-30days-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Promotions are organized regularly. The style of these promotions varies from a discount for products that are bought in <a href="/wiki/Product_bundling" title="Product bundling">bundles</a>, to thematic competitions like riddles, "guess a game from a picture" contests or "best time on a specific level". Also, GOG.com gives away promotion codes for a game with review contests.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_share">Market share</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Market share">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As GOG.com does not typically release absolute game selling numbers, market share considerations of GOG.com among the digital distributors are a challenge. But, sometimes an individual game developer releases their internal statistics about the selling performance on different game distribution channels for their specific game.</p>
<p>In an article dated November 11, 2011, <a href="/wiki/PC_Gamer" title="PC Gamer">PC Gamer</a> reported statistics for online sales of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Witcher_2" title="The Witcher 2">The Witcher 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures-55">[55]</a></sup> According to PC Gamer, Direct2Drive, Impulse and Gamersgate's combined sales were a total of 10,000 (4%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures-55">[55]</a></sup> GOG.com sold 40,000 copies (16%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures-55">[55]</a></sup> while Steam sold in the same time period 200,000 copies (80%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-PCGamer.C2.A0.E2.80.94_Witcher_2_Online_Sales_Figures-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 20, 2013, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Defender%27s_Quest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Defender's Quest (page does not exist)">Defender's Quest</a></i> developer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lars_Doucet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lars Doucet (page does not exist)">Lars Doucet</a> revealed the first three months of <a href="/wiki/Revenue" title="Revenue">revenue</a> following his game's release across 6 different digital distribution platforms, including 4 major digital game distributors and 2 methods of purchasing and downloading the game directly from the developer. The results showed that GOG.com generated 8.5% of the revenue – second only to <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a>'s 58.6% among the digital distribution platforms used. Doucet noted that "for the major [digital game distributors], GOG's star is clearly rising. Even under direct competition, GOG generated 14.5 percent as much revenue as Steam. [...] Steam enjoys a captive market of ardent loyalists, but GOG is swiftly becoming an attractive alternative and gaining loyalists of its own, especially in the anti-DRM crowd."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of June 9, 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, GOG.com had seen 690,000 units<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> of CD Projekt Red's game <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witcher_3:_Wild_Hunt" title="The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt">The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt</a></i> redeemed through the service, more than the second largest digital seller Steam (approx. 580,000 units<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steamspyw3_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-steamspyw3-58">[58]</a></sup>) and all other PC digital distribution services combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gs2_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-gs2-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publishing_agreements">Publishing agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GOG.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Publishing agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 26, 2009, GOG.com announced it had signed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> to publish games from their back catalogue; this was the first deal with a major publisher to offer DRM-free downloads. The deal to publish through GOG.com also included games that were not available through any other online distribution channel. On September 5, 2014, GOG.com started to sell <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> games from <a href="/wiki/Cinemaware" title="Cinemaware">Cinemaware</a>'s catalogue, starting with <a href="/wiki/Defender_of_the_Crown" title="Defender of the Crown">Defender of the Crown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOGAmigagames_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOGAmigagames-60">[60]</a></sup> This was technically made possible through Cinemaware's own written <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulator</a> called "Rocklobster".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> On October 28, 2014, GOG.com was able to secure another major publisher as a DRM-free partner, <a href="/wiki/Disney_Interactive" title="Disney Interactive">Disney Interactive</a> / <a href="/wiki/LucasArts" title="LucasArts">LucasArts</a>. With this new partnership, GOG.com began to re-release several often-requested game titles from LucasArts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOGDisney_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOGDisney-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> starting with six titles (<i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_X-Wing" title="Star Wars: X-Wing">Star Wars: X-Wing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_TIE_Fighter" title="Star Wars: TIE Fighter">Star Wars: TIE Fighter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sam_%26_Max_Hit_the_Road" title="Sam &amp; Max Hit the Road">Sam &amp; Max Hit the Road</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_Monkey_Island:_Special_Edition" title="The Secret of Monkey Island: Special Edition">The Secret of Monkey Island: Special Edition</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Fate_of_Atlantis" title="Indiana Jones and the Fate of Atlantis">Indiana Jones and the Fate of Atlantis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Knights_of_the_Old_Republic" title="Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic">Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOGDisney2_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOGDisney2-64">[64]</a></sup> On May 5, 2015, GOG.com released <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_General" title="Pacific General">Pacific General</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasy_General" title="Fantasy General">Fantasy General</a></i> and named itself, GOG Ltd, as the publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The company revealed that it had acquired the copyright to these titles and that it intends to acquire more in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> On August 26, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Bethesda_Softworks" title="Bethesda Softworks">Bethesda Softworks</a> joined GOG.com with classical titles as <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fallout_(video_game)" title="Fallout (video game)">Fallout</a> (which had been sold on GOG by Interplay before the rights changed hands), and also some classic <a more than 1,800 DRM-free games in its catalogue and new ones are added several times a week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Games_Catalogue_68-0"><a
