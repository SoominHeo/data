<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">GT200</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For Nvidia's GT200 graphic processing unit (GPU), see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GeForce_200_Series" title="GeForce 200 Series">GeForce 200 Series</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>GT200</b> is a fraudulent "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_range_locator" title="Long range locator">remote substance detector</a>" that was claimed by its manufacturer, UK-based Global Technical Ltd, to be able to detect, from a distance, various substances including explosives and drugs. The GT200 was sold to a number of countries for a cost of up to £22,000 per unit, but the device has been described as little more than "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divining_rod" title="Divining rod">divining rods</a>" which lack any scientific explanation for why they should work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-docstoc.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-docstoc.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-UK_bans_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-UK_bans-2">[2]</a></sup> After the similar <a href="/wiki/ADE_651" title="ADE 651">ADE 651</a> was exposed as a fraud, the UK Government banned the export of such devices to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> in January 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-statement_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-statement-3">[3]</a></sup> and warned foreign governments that the GT200 and ADE 651 are "wholly ineffective" at detecting bombs and explosives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-UK_warns_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-UK_warns-4">[4]</a></sup> The owner of Global Technical, Gary Bolton, was convicted on 26 July 2013 on two charges of fraud relating to the sale and manufacture of the GT200<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and sentenced to seven years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_background">Description and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GT200 consists of three main components—a swivelling antenna mounted via a hinge to a plastic handgrip, into which "sensor cards" can be inserted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGJP_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGJP-7">[7]</a></sup> It requires no battery or other power source and is said to be powered solely by the user's static electricity. The device becomes active when the operator starts moving and detects various substances via "DIA/PARA magnetism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EK9_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EK9-8">[8]</a></sup> It is made by Global Technical Ltd of <a href="/wiki/Ashford,_Kent" title="Ashford, Kent">Ashford, Kent</a>. The company (registered number 03300333) was established as a <a href="/wiki/Private_limited_company" title="Private limited company">private limited company</a> on 9 January 1997 with Gary Bolton as director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A number of overseas partners including Segtec, Napco, Nikunj Eximp Enterprises, Electronic K9 Singapore, Aviasatcom and Concord Consulting have distributed its products in Central America, the Middle East, India, Southeast Asia and Thailand respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HT-UK_defence_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HT-UK_defence-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST-Plenty_of_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ST-Plenty_of-11">[11]</a></sup> Global Technical also had a sister company, Global Technical Training Services Ltd (registered company 03793910), which was established on 23 June 1999 but is now dissolved.</p>
<p>Promotional material issued about the GT200 claims that it can detect a wide variety of items including ammunition, explosives, drugs, gold, ivory, currency, tobacco and "human bodies" at ranges of up to 700 metres (2,300 ft) on the surface, depths of up to 60 m (200 ft) underground or under 800 m (2,600 ft) of water, or even from aircraft at an altitude of up to 4 kilometres (2.5 mi). A "Substance Sensor Card" inserted into the device is said to create an "attracting field" utilising "dia/para magnetism" between the device and the substance that is to be detected. The field is claimed to make the antenna of the GT200 lock onto a signal, indicating the direction in which the substance can be located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EK9_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-EK9-8">[8]</a></sup> According to the promotional material, if the device is used correctly, it "can detect substance(s) through walls, (even lead-lined and metal ones), water, (fresh and salted), fresh and frozen food, (fish, fruit, tea, coffee, ice), vacuum flask, containers, petrol and diesel fuel and even buried in the earth" and can detect narcotics for up to two weeks after they have been ingested by a target individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EK9_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-EK9-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Thai newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation_(Thailand)" title="The Nation (Thailand)">The Nation</a></i>, the GT200 is "just a new name" for a previous Global Technical product, the MOLE programmable substance detection system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nation-GT200_little_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nation-GT200_little-12">[12]</a></sup> It operated in the same way as the GT200, using a swinging antenna to point to a target material indicated via "programmable cards" inserted into a reader. The MOLE was tested in the United States in 2002 by <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> but was found to perform no better than random chance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to the Sandia report, the MOLE appears "physically nearly identical" to a product Sandia examined in October 1995 called the <a href="/wiki/Quadro_Tracker" title="Quadro Tracker">Quadro Tracker</a>, which was marketed by a <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> company but which was banned in 1996 and the makers prosecuted for fraud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Newsnight" title="BBC Newsnight">BBC Newsnight</a></i> television investigation of the GT200 in January 2010 found that the "sensor card" consisted only of two sheets of card between which was sandwiched a sheet of paper, white on one side and black on the other, that had been cut off from a larger sheet with a knife or scissors. It contained no electronic components whatsoever. When the device's case was dismantled, it too was found to contain no electronic components. Explosives expert Sidney Alford told Newsnight: "Speaking as a professional, I would say that is an empty plastic case." Gary Bolton of Global Technical said that the lack of any electronic parts "does not mean it does not operate to the specification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A GT200 unit was examined on Thailand's <a href="/wiki/Nation_Multimedia_Group" title="Nation Multimedia Group">Nation Channel</a> in an interview with Lt Col Somchai Chalermsuksan of the Thai Central Institute of Forensic Science. The host commented that "there is no battery here or way of powering it" and that the bottom half of the device was completely empty. Asked if there was anything in the sealed top half of the device, Lt Col Somchai said: "There is nothing. Once there was an accident and the device came apart. There was nothing inside." The host concluded: "So it is just two pieces of plastic put together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Export_ban.2C_police_investigation_and_criminal_charges">Export ban, police investigation and criminal charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Export ban, police investigation and criminal charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following controversy over a similar device, the <a href="/wiki/ADE_651" title="ADE 651">ADE 651</a>, the UK Government issued an order under the Export Control Act 2002 that came into force on 27 January 2010, banning the export to Iraq and Afghanistan of "‘electro-statically powered’ equipment for detecting 'explosives'",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> on the grounds that such equipment "could cause harm to UK and other friendly forces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-statement_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-statement-3">[3]</a></sup> The export ban covers all such devices, including the GT200.</p>
<p>Officers from the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Police" title="City of London Police">City of London Police</a> Overseas Anti-Corruption Unit subsequently raided the offices of Global Technical and two other makers of similar "bomb detectors". A large amount of cash and several hundred of the devices and their component parts were seized. The police said that they were investigating on suspicion of fraud by false representation and were also investigating whether bribes had been paid to secure contracts to supply the devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 February 2011 the British government told BBC Newsnight that it had helped Global Technical sell the GT200 around the world between 2001 and 2004. Royal Engineers sales teams demonstrated the devices at arms fairs and the UK <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Trade_and_Industry_(United_Kingdom)" title="Department of Trade and Industry (United Kingdom)">Department of Trade and Industry</a> helped two companies sell the GT200 and similar products in Mexico and the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc27jan11_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc27jan11-21">[21]</a></sup> On 12 July 2012, Andrew Penhale, Deputy Head of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prosecution_Service" title="Crown Prosecution Service">Crown Prosecution Service</a>'s Central Fraud Division, authorised charges against six individuals, including Gary Bolton, for the manufacture, promotion and sale of a range of fraudulent substance detector devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cps20120712_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cps20120712-22">[22]</a></sup> Bolton was formally charged at the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> Magistrates' Court on 19 July with one count of fraud by false representation and one count of making or supplying an item for use in fraud between January and July 2012, and pleaded not guilty to both charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Bolton was convicted on 26 July 2013 at The Old Bailey and released on bail pending sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Bolton was subsequently sentenced to 7 years Imprisonment on 20 August 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Bolton was also ordered to pay over £1.25 million to prevent seven additional years of imprisonment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Bolton reportedly made £45 million selling the fraudulent devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Users">Users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GT200 was used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Concerns_over_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Concerns_over-28">[28]</a></sup> Reportedly, some 818 GT200 units were procured by Thai public bodies since 2004. These include 535 bought by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_Army" title="Royal Thai Army">Royal Thai Army</a> for use combating the <a href="/wiki/South_Thailand_insurgency" title="South Thailand insurgency">South Thailand insurgency</a> and another 222 for use in other areas, 50 purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_Police" title="Royal Thai Police">Royal Thai Police</a> for use in Police Region 4 (<a href="/wiki/Khon_Kaen" title="Khon Kaen">Khon Kaen</a>), six bought by the Central Institute of Forensic Science, six by the Customs Department, four by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_Air_Force" title="Royal Thai Air Force">Royal Thai Air Force</a>, and one by <a href="/wiki/Chai_Nat" title="Chai Nat">Chai Nat</a> police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Other agencies such as the Border Patrol Police Bureau and the Office of the Narcotics Control Board use a similar device, the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_6_(device)" title="Alpha 6 (device)">Alpha 6</a>, procured from another company, Comstrac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Concerns_over_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Concerns_over-28">[28]</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Bangkok_Post" title="Bangkok Post">Bangkok Post</a></i>, the Royal Thai Air Force first procured the GT200 to detect explosives and drugs at airports, followed by the army in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-GT200_doubts_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-GT200_doubts-30">[30]</a></sup> According to Lt Gen Daopong Rattansuwan, the Deputy Chief of Staff of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_Army" title="Royal Thai Army">Royal Thai Army</a>, each GT200 bought by the army cost 900,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baht" title="Baht">baht</a> (£17,000/US$27,000), rising to 1.2 million baht (£22,000/US$36,000) if 21 "sensor cards" were included with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Concerns_over_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Concerns_over-28">[28]</a></sup> In total, Thailand's government and security forces have spent between 800–900 million baht (US$21 million) on the devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nation-Bt800_million_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nation-Bt800_million-31">[31]</a></sup> Figures updated in 2016 claim that the Thai government spent 1.4 billion baht on the purchase of 1,358 devices between 2006 and 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>The <i>Bangkok Post</i> commented that, "The GT200 case was a unique scandal because the devices...seemed to fool only the people closely connected to their sale and purchase."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The device was<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> also widely used in Mexico, where was jokingly called "The devil's Ouja" (Spanish: La Ouija del diablo),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> as security forces ineffectively used it in an attempt combat drug traffickers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and to search for explosives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mexican government has spent over 340 million <a href="/wiki/Mexican_peso" title="Mexican peso">pesos</a> ($27 million USD) buying GT200s at a cost of 286,000 pesos ($22,000) each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> According to the government of <a href="/wiki/Guanajuato" title="Guanajuato">Guanajuato</a> state, the federal government has bought more than 700 GT200s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> State governments have also bought their own GT200s; the device is reported to be in use by police in the Mexican states of <a href="/wiki/Tabasco" title="Tabasco">Tabasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa" title="Sinaloa">Sinaloa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michoac%C3%A1n" title="Michoacán">Michoacán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Mexican military also utilises the GT200. In 2008 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretariat_of_National_Defense" title="Secretariat of National Defense">Secretariat of National Defense</a> had purchased 300 GT200s for use throughout the country, including at 133 strategic locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> By late 2009 the figure had increased to 521 GT200s, which had been deployed to 11 strategic checkpoints and 284 regional control stations around the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In the violence-wracked city of <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Juárez">Ciudad Juárez</a>, the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Exc%C3%A9lsior" title="Excélsior">Excélsior</a></i> reported that "military squads roam the streets and go from house to house, using a molecular detector known as GT200" to find weapons, drugs and money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Prison personnel in Juárez and its parent state, <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua_(state)" title="Chihuahua (state)">Chihuahua</a>, have been provided with GT200s to detect escape tunnels being dug by prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GT200 has been demonstrated in India for organisations including the Central Reserve Police Force, Indo-Tibetan Border Police, National Security Guards, Narcotics Control Bureau and the Bureau of Civil Aviation Security<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HT-UK_defence_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-HT-UK_defence-10">[10]</a></sup> and was procured by the <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> Customs in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Hotels in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> use it to detect bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST-Plenty_of_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ST-Plenty_of-11">[11]</a></sup> It is also in use in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, Jordan and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-UK_warns_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-UK_warns-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="African_states">African states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: African states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The device is used to detect smuggled ivory in the central African states of <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, Uganda, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, Tanzania and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-UK_warns_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-UK_warns-4">[4]</a></sup> The Lusaka Agreement Task Force (LATF) and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> bought 15 GT200s in 2005 at a cost of $5,000 each and distributed them to the five LATF member states. In Uganda, the GT200s were installed at <a href="/wiki/Entebbe_International_Airport" title="Entebbe International Airport">Entebbe International Airport</a>, border crossings and internal checkpoints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> A report submitted by Kenya to the <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> Standing Committee in 2006 stated that "three staffs from Tanzania were trained on the use of GT 200 Ivory Detector for the purposes of law enforcement against illegal dealing in ivory products, in the country. Four (4) Ivory Detectors are currently in place and are used for law enforcement activities especially at entry and exit points."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_over_use">Controversy over use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversy over use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand_2">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The effectiveness of the GT200 has been the subject of controversy in Thailand, where the device has been reported to have been implicated in several deaths when it failed to detect improvised explosive devices which detonated, killing civilians and personnel from the security forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-UK_bans_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BP-UK_bans-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three members of a border patrol police unit were killed on 7 November 2008 in <a href="/wiki/Panare_District" title="Panare District">Panare District</a> when the GT200 they were using failed to detect a bomb planted on a road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGJP_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WGJP-7">[7]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Mueang_Yala_District" title="Mueang Yala District">Mueang Yala District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yala_Province" title="Yala Province">Yala Province</a>, security forces used a GT200 to investigate the scene of the murder of two officials, but were unable to detect a follow-up boobytrap bomb, which exploded just after they had declared the area to be clear of bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-When_faulty_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-When_faulty-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous people were killed and injured in two bomb attacks in October 2009 in which the GT200 was used by security forces. On 6 October 2009, a car bomb exploded opposite the Merlin Hotel in <a href="/wiki/Su-ngai_Kolok" title="Su-ngai Kolok">Su-ngai Kolok</a>, killing one person and injuring 20, after it had been "scanned" using a GT200 and declared to be free of explosives. A motorcycle bomb exploded on 19 October in Yala, injuring another 26 people, again after a scan with a GT200 had returned negative results for explosives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Concerns_over_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Concerns_over-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>False positives have also been reported, with civilians being arrested after a GT200 "detected" traces of explosives on them. Similar erroneous readings were reported to have caused "pandemonium" at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pattani" title="Pattani">Pattani</a> Hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-When_faulty_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-BP-When_faulty-48">[48]</a></sup> On one occasion the device was reported to have "incriminated the top of a coconut tree", though it was said the alert turned out to have been triggered by a plastic bag with vegetable oil inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGJP_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-WGJP-7">[7]</a></sup> Thai journalist Charoon Thongnual reported personally experiencing the GT200 showing false positives:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A device used by a defence volunteer pointed repeatedly to a motorcycle parked in front of the Pattani Provincial Court. However, a search of the motorbike discovered nothing illegal. In another incident, a device pointed at a handbag carried by a female college student in Pattani who was strolling with friends near the CS Hotel. A search of her bag found cosmetics and other items that were completely lawful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <i>Bangkok Post</i> reported on a Thai army field training session using the GT200 near <a href="/wiki/Yala,_Thailand" title="Yala, Thailand">Yala city</a> in November 2009, in which a bomb squad using GT200 devices repeatedly failed to detect explosives:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Various types of explosives had been placed at different spots, unknown to the personnel using altogether four GT200 devices. After more than 30 minutes, all four devices failed to locate the explosives. Eventually, the head of the bomb disposal squad showed them where the bombs were hidden, much to the embarrassment of all concerned.</p>
<p>One of the authors of this story, Mr Surapan, did his own detective work as well. While the bomb squad was busy scouring the roadside bushes, he drove into town and borrowed a few sticks of dynamite from a security officer, who is a longtime friend. He wrapped the dynamite with tin foil and black carbon paper and drove back to the same location. Mr Surapan parked his car on the roadside about a metre behind a car belonging to one of the officers. Then he placed the wrapped dynamite sticks under the bonnet of his car. All four GT200 devices indicated nothing unusual as the handlers walked past his car. But they led the officers to the car in front, where several automatic rifles were kept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Danger_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Danger-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hundreds of people are said to have been detained by Thai security forces on the basis of GT200 readings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGJP_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-WGJP-7">[7]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a>, about 10 percent of those detained on suspicion of involvement in the insurgency have been arrested on this basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRW_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRW-51">[51]</a></sup> In one village in <a href="/wiki/Narathiwat_Province" title="Narathiwat Province">Narathiwat Province</a>, 32 people were arrested after GT200s were used to "detect" traces of explosive substances on their bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Most of them were detained without charge for an extended period. Brad Adams of Human Rights Watch commented: "It is common during security sweeps in the south to see Muslim men lined up on the roadside with their shirts off while being screened by a GT200. Many of those implicated by the GT200 have been arrested and then tortured."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRW_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-HRW-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Working Group on Justice for Peace, a Thai non-governmental human rights organisation, published an article in November 2009 that was strongly critical of the GT200: "The operation of the device is causing a lot of suspicion among scientists in Thailand for several reasons. According to them, the procedure of finding suspicious objects is not based on a reliable scientific method. Besides, GT200 is not being used by credible international organisations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Danger_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Danger-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_response">Government response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Government response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Concerns were raised by some within the Thai establishment. General Pathomphong Kasornsuk, the former chief advisor of the Supreme Command, urged Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Abhisit_Vejjajiva" title="Abhisit Vejjajiva">Abhisit Vejjajiva</a> to investigate the procurement scheme for the GT200 devices and other elements of the counter-insurgency campaign in southern Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Jetsada Denduangboripan, a scientist at Chulalongkorn University, told a committee of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Thailand" title="House of Representatives of Thailand">House of Representatives of Thailand</a> in January 2010 that "The GT200 cannot detect explosives. It is not scientific equipment. It works on the users' hunch. It is similar to a wood stick that people used to detect dead bodies buried in a cemetery." He used a set of pliers, a radio antenna and a piece of paper to produce a "bomb detector" that he said worked just as effectively as the GT200. The House committee on national security said that it would investigate the GT200 further and set up an inquiry into its effectiveness that would involve the military and scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Cloud_hangs_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Cloud_hangs-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deputy Interior Minister <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thaworn_Senneam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thaworn Senneam (page does not exist)">Thaworn Senneam</a> told journalists after a fatal bomb attack in southern Thailand on 6 October 2009 that the police had failed to detect the bomb "because the officer handling the GT200 detector was too nervous... His nervousness caused his temperature to rise which, in turn, caused the bomb detector to malfunction." He announced that in future two officers would be assigned to use the device, with the second ready to take over from the first if he was "not ready to use it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following media criticism, army chief General <a href="/wiki/Anupong_Paochinda" title="Anupong Paochinda">Anupong Paochinda</a> accused the press of working for Avia Satcom's competitors. He organised a demonstration to "prove" to the media that the devices worked. Fourth Army chief Lt. General Pichet Wisaijorn told the press, "It is not Gen Anupong saying the device is effective. Officers in the South and the North and the current and former 4th Army commanders also say the same thing. We have bought them and if the users insist they are good, that's end of the discussion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Cloud_hangs_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Cloud_hangs-54">[54]</a></sup> Joint Military Police Civilian Taskforce commander Lt-General Kasikorn Kirisri said any issues with the GT200 scanner were due to human error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> According to Col Banpot Poonpian of the Thai Army's Internal Security Operations Command, the GT200 has "proved effective although it may not work perfectly". It was used only as an auxiliary tool to detect something suspicious, with two other devices being used to identify the suspicious object. A recent investigation had found that failures were the result of users relying solely on the GT200 and not using the other two devices, following which bomb disposal personnel had undergone additional training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a commander in the army, which makes extensive use of the device, how well it works "depends on the static electricity stored in the body of its user. If the person using the detector is feeling weak physically, his static electricity will be down and weaken the effectiveness of the device."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> reported that the British government was to warn foreign governments that the GT200 and other similar devices were "wholly ineffective" at detecting bombs and explosives, General Anupong said that the procurement of more GT200s would be halted if the device was proven to be ineffective, although existing GT200s would continue to be used for bomb detection purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP-Cloud_hangs_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-BP-Cloud_hangs-54">[54]</a></sup> Other senior military figures continued to insist that the device worked as advertised, saying that it had detected explosives, weapons and narcotics in 173 out of 236 incidents, with a success rate higher than 80 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Army Spokesperson Sansern Kaewkamnerd insisted that the GT200 units worked with 100 percent confidence and that the Army was ready to prove the units' effectiveness any time, anywhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pornthip_Rojanasunand" title="Pornthip Rojanasunand">Pornthip Rojanasunand</a>, Director of the Central Institute of Forensic Science, also defended the use of the GT200 devices, claiming that they were effective when searching for bombs and even nails under water. She said: "I do not feel embarrassed if the bomb detector is proven ineffective. Personally, I have never handled the device myself. But my people have used it and it is accurate every time. Long long time ago, people believed that the Earth is flat and anyone who said otherwise faced execution. Things which are not visible does not necessarily mean they do not exist. The devices are there and no one has the right to ban their use. I will continue to use it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Review">Review</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the start of February 2010, Prime Minister Abhisit Vejjajiva said that he agreed that the device should be tested to determine whether it was effective. He told the media: "It's dangerous if you think something unworkable is working. So we must test to see whether the device works. We should also discuss what we should do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> If the devices were found to be ineffective, an investigation would be ordered to determine why they had been bought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following Abhisit's intervention, the Thai Cabinet ordered the Ministry of Science and Technology to carry out tests on the GT200. The Interior and Justice Ministries also commissioned the ministry to test the similar Alpha 6 device, which they used to search for drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The evaluation team included engineers, scientists, military, police and representatives from the Office of the Narcotics Control Board and the National Statistics Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The tests were carried out by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Electronics_and_Computer_Technology_Center" title="National Electronics and Computer Technology Center">National Electronics and Computer Technology Center</a> at the Thailand Science Park's Sirindhorn Science Home in <a href="/wiki/Pathum_Thani" title="Pathum Thani">Pathum Thani</a>, in a process involving 30 GT200 operators, 30 members of the investigating committee and 10 independent observers. Ten GT200 units were used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_blind" title="Double blind">double blind</a> tests to detect 20 grams of <a href="/wiki/C-4_(explosive)" title="C-4 (explosive)">C4 explosive</a> concealed in one of four identical plastic boxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> However, the testers were not allowed to examine the interior of the GT200 because of confidentiality agreements prohibiting the disclosure of "any information regarding the device."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>The test results were announced by Prime Minister Abhisit on 16 February, who disclosed that the investigators found that the GT200 had correctly detected explosives only four times in 20 tests. He said, "The result has no statistical significance. The performance is equivalent to random chance." Following the test results, he ordered security forces to stop buying the devices and review the use of those already in service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nation-Bt800_million_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nation-Bt800_million-31">[31]</a></sup> He said that the government would consider suing the GT200's manufacturer, Global Technical, and its Thai distributor Avia Satcom Co.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> An Army spokesman said that units on the ground would have discretion to continue using the GT200 unless they could find a substitute, though the Army would be sending proven alternatives such as sniffer dogs to assist the troops. Pornthip Rojanasunand of the Central Institute of Forensic Science said that although she knew it was "not scientific equipment", she believed that forensic scientists could still use it effectively: "We won't buy more, but we won't stop using them either."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nation-Bt800_million_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nation-Bt800_million-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Global Technical rejected the tests' conclusions. The company issued a statement saying that it was "surprised and disappointed" by the outcome of the tests. It claimed that the results were "completely at odds with other tests carried out by independent bodies" and with "the experience of the large number of users of this product all over the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the outcome of the tests, Thai Army chief General Anupong Paojinda said that he would not order his soldiers to stop using the GT200. He insisted that "the device operators on the ground can use them effectively. This may not be explained scientifically, but I'm telling the truth." While not rejecting the result of the tests, he stood by the device's effectiveness and argued that "as the men on the ground are impressed with it and demanded the equipment, it is the duty of the commander to procure them." He rejected the use of sniffer dogs on the grounds that they could not detect explosives at a distance and were disliked by Muslims for religious reasons. The former army chief General Sonthi Boonyaratglin also argued that the purchase of the GT200 had been justified as there was demand from operational units, despite the high price of the device: "Price is not an issue if the device is able to save people's lives." Prime Minister Abhisit ordered the scientists who had conducted the tests to explain their findings to the military and ask them to stop using the device, but said that he did not want to confront the army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Some soldiers, however, were reported to have abandoned the GT200 and turned instead to using <a href="/wiki/Chopsticks" title="Chopsticks">chopsticks</a> and their own hands to detect bombs hidden on motorcycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2012, the Thai Department of Special Investigation (DSI), which had been carrying out a review of 13 government agencies' procurement of the GT200, stated that the buyers had been deceived by the device's manufacturer. The DSI's chief, Tarit Pengdith, told a press conference that the manufacturer had "set out to lure potential buyers into buying its devices from the beginning". He said that the government agencies were now considering prosecuting the GT200's manufacturer and resellers for fraud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-DSI_alleges_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post-DSI_alleges-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpha_6_controversy">Alpha 6 controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alpha 6 controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alpha_6_(device)" title="Alpha 6 (device)">Alpha 6 (device)</a></div>
<p>In the wake of the GT200 controversy, the procurement by the Interior and Justice Ministries of the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_6_(device)" title="Alpha 6 (device)">Alpha 6</a> "molecular detector" device has come under scrutiny. The Thailand Science and Technology Ministry announced that it would broaden its tests to verify the claimed effectiveness of the Alpha 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenya">Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of the GT200 as a means of tracking smuggled ivory in Kenya has also been questioned. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a>, who saw the GT200 being used by Kenyan rangers in an attempt to catch poachers, described the misinformation which accompanied the devices as "cynical, cruel and monstrous." He told the BBC's <i>Newsnight</i> programme: "I was horrified. They had spent a vast sum of money on a modern equivalent of a hazel twig divining rod. There was no possibility that such a thing could work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-UK_warns_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-UK_warns-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico_2">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GT200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the Mexican media coverage on GT200 lacked of a critical point of view,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> but science populariser Martin Bonfil Olivera wrote on 17 February 2010, the first article warning about this scam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> However, the impact of this article (and the next on 10 March 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>) on the rest of the press was almost imperceptible, with only Fausto Ovalle from La-Ch.com writing about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Only after Marc Lacey from The New York Times wrote on 15 March 2010 about the British government warning on the GT200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> the scam was exposed in some Mexican newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Later, Juan José Morales from Por Esto! wrote a couple of columns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> and Bonfil Olivera was interviewed by Ana Paula Ordorica and Enrique Acevedo for La Otra Agenda TV show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> A local newspaper in Cuernavaca published about the detector<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> in their science popularisation section in the charge of the Academy of Sciences of the State of Morelos. A few critical articles published in 2011 include two interviews by Benito Jiménez<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and an interview by Guillermo Cárdenas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>All of this had been not enough to create public awareness on this subject. Main press and TV continued not just ignoring the subject but even justifying it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Mexican military continues doing searches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and explosives are ruled out<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> based on GT200 results. This situation might have changed the publication by a major newspaper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refLC1_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-refLC1-94">[94]</a></sup> <i>El Universal</i>, of a critical article in its first page. Subsequent articles were published daily for a week in the main and editorial pages of that newspaper, and interviews with scientists and human rights defenders were transmitted in television and radio channels.</p>
<p>According to data available in Federal Institute of Access to Public Information (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=IFAI&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="IFAI (page does not exist)">IFAI</a>) and press notes, the GT200 has been bought among others by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEDENA" title="SEDENA">SEDENA</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEMAR" title="SEMAR">SEMAR</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEMEX" title="PEMEX">PEMEX</a> filials and some state police agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> As of 2011, more than MXN$340 million Mexican pesos (US$26 million US dollars (at 2011-09-16 rate)) have been spent to purchase more than 940 detectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 September 2011, a group of scientists, including members of the Science Council of the Presidency and the president of the Mexican Academy of Science, met members of the Science Committee of the Senate to discuss the GT200. The scientists argued that the claimed scientific basis for the operation of the GT200 was false and that the device was unlikely to work, they pointed out the risks to the armed forces and to the civilian population from the continued use of the "detector", and that notwithstanding the apparent number of favourable testimonies, the devices had to be subjected to controlled double blind tests. The senators announced they would present a resolution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> to the main assembly of the Senate in order to audit the armed forces and demand a scientific test of their equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 October 2011, Karla Macías Lovera, a judge in the state of Veracruz, took a historic decision,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refLC1_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-refLC1-94">[94]</a></sup> ordering the release of Ernesto Cayetano Aguilar, a man that had been accused of carrying drugs eight months back, when he was singled out by the GT200 from among the passengers of a bus where a kilogramme of marijuana was found. The judge argued that the GT200 provided no credible evidence, as there is no scientific proof that it actually works.</p>
<p>On 21 October 2011, double blind tests were finally performed in México to assess the efficacy of the GT200 detector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The results of this test were to be presented as a proof in an ongoing judicial process. Although the judge has not yet resolved the case (October 2012), <i>El Universal</i>, a major Mexican newspaper, published a detailed account of the results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> The test was conducted by two physicists, members of the Mexican Academy of Science, and several military men and policemen participated, including two certified expert operators of the GT200. More than 1600 amphetamine capsules and four bullets were hidden in one of eight cardboard boxes distributed on a large abandoned ballroom. In a first stage the <i>substances</i> were hidden in plain view of all the participants and the GT200 was 100% successful (four successes in four tries). However, the second stage was double blind, and only three searches succeeded out of twenty tries, a result consistent with chance but not with any useful effectiveness. Thus, it was demonstrated that the GT200 yields reliable results only when the operator knows beforehand where the substance is hidden, but is otherwise useless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/El_Paso_Times" title="El Paso Times">El Paso Times</a> reported on 24 October 2011 that the Mexican army turned down an offer from the Mexican Association for the Sciences to help test the effectiveness of the GT200. In a written response, the Mexican army stated that their contract with the GT200 supplier prevents them from accepting the offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 June 2012 the Mexican Congress approved a <a class="external text" href="http://www.senado.gob.mx/sgsp/gaceta/61/3/2012-06-06-1/assets/documentos/1ra_Com_detector_GT200.pdf" rel="nofollow">declaration</a> exhorting the President to collect from the scientific community and to analyse all opinions and evidence on the effectiveness of the GT200 as a molecular detector, with the help of his Science Council. It may be interesting to note that to this date (Aug.9, 2012), after five and a half years of his presidency, president Calderón had never met with his Science Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 August 2012, the Supreme Court of Justice announced that it would review a case involving the GT200. It seems that two young men were accused of dealing with cocaine. The judge disregarded the case stating that there was no scientific evidence proving the usefulness of the GT200. The Attorney General appealed this decision and the Supreme Court stepped in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 December 2012, a newspaper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> announced that the new Attorney General (Procurador General de la República) had prohibited its use by the police forces under his command, as the GT200 had failed scientific tests, and that their purchase was being investigated.</p>
