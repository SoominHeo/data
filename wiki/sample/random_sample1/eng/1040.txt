<p><b>H-IIB</b> (<b>H2B</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch system</a> used to launch <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">H-II Transfer Vehicles</a> (HTV, or <i>Kounotori</i>) towards the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. H-IIB <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> are liquid-fuelled with solid-fuel <a href="/wiki/Modular_rocket" title="Modular rocket">strap-on</a> boosters and are launched from the <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_Space_Center" title="Tanegashima Space Center">Tanegashima Space Center</a> in Japan. <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi</a> and <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a> have been primarily responsible for design, manufacture, and operation of H-IIB. H-IIB made its first flight in 2009, and has made a total of five flights through 2015.</p>
<p>H-IIB is able to carry a payload of up to 8,000 kilograms (18,000 lb) to <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">GTO</a>, compared with the payload of 4,000-6,000 kg for the <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">H-IIA</a>, a predecessor design. Its performance to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">LEO</a> is sufficient for the 16,500 kg (36,400 lb) <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">HTV</a>. The first H-IIB was launched in September 2009.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H-IIB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The H-IIB launch vehicle is a launch vehicle developed jointly by <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</a> to launch the <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">H-II Transfer Vehicle</a>. The H-IIB was designed to adopt methods and components that have already been verified by flights on the <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">H-IIA</a>, so that manufacturing the new launch vehicle would be more cost-effective, with less risk, in a shorter period of time. JAXA was in charge of preliminary design, readiness of the ground facility, and the development of new technologies for the H-IIB, in which the private sector has limited competencies, while the Mitsubishi Heavy Industries is responsible for manufacturing. JAXA successfully conducted eight firing tests of the new cluster design with the simulated first-stage propulsion system, called Battleship Firing Tests, since March 2008, at MHI's Tashiro Test Facility in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odate" title="Odate">Odate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akita_Prefecture" title="Akita Prefecture">Akita Prefecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before launch, two Captive Firing Tests were conducted on the H-IIB. The first test, which consisted of firing the first stage for ten seconds, was originally scheduled to occur at 02:30 GMT on 27 March 2009, however it was cancelled after the launch pad's coolant system failed to activate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This was later discovered to have been due to a manual supply valve not being open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The test was rescheduled for 1 April, but then postponed again due to a leak in a pipe associated with the launch facility's fire suppression system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The test was rescheduled for 2 April,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> when it was successfully conducted at 05:00 GMT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Following this, the second test, which involved a 150-second burn of the first stage, was scheduled for 20 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This was successfully conducted at 04:00 GMT on 22 April,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> following a two-day delay due to unfavourable weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A ground test, using a <a href="/wiki/Battleship_(rocketry)" title="Battleship (rocketry)">battleship</a> mockup of the rocket was subsequently conducted on 11 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2009, the development program of the H-IIB had cost approximately 27 billion yen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_description">Vehicle description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H-IIB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vehicle description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The H-IIB launch vehicle is a two-stage rocket. The first stage uses <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> as propellants and has four strap-on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_rocket" title="Solid rocket">solid rocket</a> boosters (<a href="/wiki/SRB-A" title="SRB-A">SRB-A3</a>) powered by <a href="/wiki/Polybutadiene" title="Polybutadiene">polybutadiene</a>. The first stage is powered by two <a href="/wiki/LE-7" title="LE-7">LE-7</a>A engines, instead of one for the <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">H-IIA</a>. It has four SRB-As attached to the body, while the standard version of H-IIA has two SRB-As. In addition, the first-stage body of the H-IIB is 5.2m in diameter compared with 4m for the H-IIA. The total length of the first stage is extended by 1m from that of H-IIA. As a result, the H-IIB first stage holds 70% more propellant than that of the H-IIA. The second stage is powered by a single <a href="/wiki/LE-5" title="LE-5">LE-5</a>B engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
