<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">H-dagurinn</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>H-dagurinn</b> or <b>Hægri dagurinn</b> (<a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>: <b>Right day</b>) on 26 May 1968 was the day that <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> changed from <a href="/wiki/Right-_and_left-hand_traffic" title="Right- and left-hand traffic">left hand traffic to right hand traffic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbl260519681_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mbl260519681-1">[1]</a></sup> The change itself occurred formally at 6:00 AM.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H-dagurinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Iceland had been ruled by <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, which officially adopted driving on the right in 1793, it had continued to drive on the left; a plan to switch to right-hand traffic was interrupted by <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when the country was under British military occupation, as military traffic was greater than that of civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The country's <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">parliament</a> made the following demand of the government on 13 May 1964:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Umferd1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Umferd1-3">[3]</a></sup> "Alþingi urges the government to initiate as soon as possible research into how best to move the traffic to the right hand side of the road."</p>
<p>A law was passed in 1965, under which the country would change to driving on the right in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SH-T_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SH-T-4">[4]</a></sup> The decision by <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to change to right hand traffic in 1967, bringing it into line with other Nordic countries, also influenced Iceland's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, Iceland was experiencing an increased number of visitors from the United States and mainland Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SH-T_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-SH-T-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Traffic Commission (Umferðarnefnd) was assigned to handle the task. Cost due to the change amounted to over 33 million <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_kr%C3%B3na" title="Icelandic króna">kronur</a> due to modification to <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a> and 12 million kronur due to changes to infrastructure. During the night before the change 1662 signs all over the country were changed, making the total of signs changed 5727.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbl196805263_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mbl196805263-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The only injury from the changeover was a boy on a bicycle who broke his leg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In accordance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk_homeostasis" title="Risk homeostasis">risk homeostasis</a> theory, traffic accident rates briefly dropped as drivers overcompensated for the increased risk from driving on the unfamiliar side of the road, before returning to the level predicted by the trend prior to the changeover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
