<p><b>HD 10647</b> (q<sup>1</sup> Eridani) is a 6th-<a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow-white_dwarf" title="Yellow-white dwarf">yellow-white dwarf</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>, 57 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eridanus_(constellation)" title="Eridanus (constellation)">Eridanus</a>. The star is visible to the unaided eye under very dark skies. It is slightly hotter and more luminous than the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, and at 1,750 million years old, it is also younger. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> was discovered orbiting this star in 2003.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_system">Planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_10647&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Mayor" title="Michel Mayor">Michel Mayor</a>'s team announced the discovery of a new planet, <a href="/wiki/HD_10647_b" title="HD 10647 b">HD 10647 b</a>, in Paris at the XIX IAP Colloquium <i>Extrasolar Planets: Today &amp; Tormorrow</i>* <a class="external autonumber" href="http://obswww.unige.ch/~udry/planet/hd10647.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Australian_Planet_Search" title="Anglo-Australian Planet Search">Anglo-Australian Planet Search</a> team initially did not detect the planet in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAT04_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAT04-4">[4]</a></sup> though a solution was made by 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butler2006-5">[5]</a></sup> The CORALIE data was finally published in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marmier2013_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Marmier2013-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="external autonumber" href="http://server3.sky-map.org/starview?object_type=1&amp;object_id=3122" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IRAS" title="IRAS">IRAS</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> space <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> detected an <a href="/wiki/Infrared_excess" title="Infrared excess">excess of infrared radiation</a> from the star, indicating a possible circumstellar disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Using this data and later observations with the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared_Space_Observatory" title="Infrared Space Observatory">Infrared Space Observatory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Pathfinder_Experiment" title="Atacama Pathfinder Experiment">Atacama Pathfinder Experiment</a> implies that the disk is located at 25 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">Astronomical units</a> and has a thin (0.02 AU), ring-like structure, with the sharp cutoff in the disk suggesting a planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, a much longer wavelength suggests the existence of a much wider belt of material, analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a>. The hierarchy of this system is somewhat reminiscent of that of <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Eridani" title="Epsilon Eridani">Epsilon Eridani</a>, without an inner asteroid belt.</p>
