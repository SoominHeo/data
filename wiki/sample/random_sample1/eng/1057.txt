<p><b>HD 40307</b> is an orange (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-type_star" title="K-type star">K-type</a>) <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main-sequence</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> located approximately 42 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pictor" title="Pictor">Pictor</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Easel" title="Easel">Easel</a>), taking its primary name from its <a href="/wiki/Henry_Draper_Catalogue" title="Henry Draper Catalogue">Henry Draper Catalogue</a> designation. It is calculated to be slightly less massive than the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. HD 40307 was observed during or before 1900 as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Photographic_Durchmusterung" title="Cape Photographic Durchmusterung">Cape Photographic Durchmusterung</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> The star has six known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">planets</a>, three discovered in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sb_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-sb-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-9">[8]</a></sup> and three more in 2012. One of them, <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_g" title="HD 40307 g">HD 40307 g</a>, is a potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super-Earth" title="Super-Earth">super-Earth</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a>, with an orbital period of about 200 days. This object might be capable of supporting liquid water on its surface, although much more information must be acquired before its habitability can be assessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-nov12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-nov12-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20121107a_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space-20121107a-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20121107b_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space-20121107b-13">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_nomenclature">History and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The designation <i>HD 40307</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Draper_Catalogue" title="Henry Draper Catalogue">Henry Draper Catalogue</a>, which is based on spectral classifications made between 1911 and 1915 by <a href="/wiki/Annie_Jump_Cannon" title="Annie Jump Cannon">Annie Jump Cannon</a> and her co-workers, and was published between 1918 and 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a K-type star, HD 40307 emits orange-tinted light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sb_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-sb-1">[1]</a></sup> It has only about three-quarters of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s radius and mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geneva_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-geneva-3">[3]</a></sup> Its temperature is measured at slightly under 5000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvin_scale" title="Kelvin scale">K</a>. This is relatively high for a K-type star, approaching the temperatures normally found in G-type stars such as the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The astronomers who discovered the planets orbiting HD 40307 suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicities</a> of stars determine whether or not the planetary bodies that orbit them will be terrestrial, like Earth, or gaseous, like <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arXiv_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_and_visibility">Distance and visibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distance and visibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite its relative proximity to the Sun at 42 light-years, HD 40307 is not visible to the naked eye, given its <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 7.17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> It came within 6.4 light-years of the Sun about 413,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bobylev:arXiv1003.2160_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bobylev:arXiv1003.2160-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_system">Planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HD40307.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="742" data-file-width="636" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/HD40307.svg/250px-HD40307.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/HD40307.svg/375px-HD40307.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/HD40307.svg/500px-HD40307.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HD40307.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The orbits of the inner three planets of HD 40307.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After spending five years observing the star,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOL_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOL-20">[19]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Organisation_for_Astronomical_Research_in_the_Southern_Hemisphere" title="European Organisation for Astronomical Research in the Southern Hemisphere">European Organisation for Astronomical Research in the Southern Hemisphere</a> (<i>ESO</i>) announced that they had discovered three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super-Earth" title="Super-Earth">super-Earths</a> in orbit around HD 40307 in June 2008. All three planets were detected by the <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a> method, using the <a href="/wiki/High_Accuracy_Radial_Velocity_Planet_Searcher" title="High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher">HARPS</a> spectrograph system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, an independent analysis carried out by a team of astronomers led by <a href="/wiki/Mikko_Tuomi" title="Mikko Tuomi">Mikko Tuomi</a> of the University of Hertfordshire confirmed the existence of these planets and found an additional three planets in the systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-nov12_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-nov12-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five of the planets orbit very close to the star,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup> with the farthest of them located twice as close to HD 40307 than is the planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> is to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[note 2]</a></sup> The outermost planet orbits at a distance similar to the distance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venus_(planet)" title="Venus (planet)">Venus</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and is situated well in the system's <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_liquid_water" title="Extraterrestrial liquid water">liquid water</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Minimum_mass" title="Minimum mass">minimum masses</a> of the planets in the system ranges from three to ten times the <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">mass of the Earth</a>, placing them somewhere between <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and gas giants like <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup> Dynamical analysis of the innermost planets suggests that planet <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_b" title="HD 40307 b">b</a> is unstable at its age unless it is an ice giant, having migrated from further away. That implies similar for the other planets, even further out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> The most recent discovery also indicates via dynamical analysis that the true planetary masses can not be much higher than their minimum masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
