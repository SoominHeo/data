<p><b>HD 40307 d</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> <a href="/wiki/HD_40307" title="HD 40307">HD 40307</a>, located 42 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from Earth in the direction of the southern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Pictor" title="Pictor">Pictor</a>. The planet was discovered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_velocity_method" title="Radial velocity method">radial velocity method</a>, using the <a href="/wiki/High_Accuracy_Radial_Velocity_Planet_Searcher" title="High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher">HARPS</a> apparatus in June 2008. It is the most massive of the six proposed planets in the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-5">[5]</a></sup> The planet is of interest as this star has relatively low <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a>, supporting a hypothesis that different metallicities in <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a> determine what kind of planets they will form.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307_d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HD 40307 d was discovered through the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy" title="Doppler spectroscopy">Doppler spectroscopy</a> method, which functions by measuring the variations in <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a> in a star produced by the gravitational effect of orbiting exoplanets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6">[6]</a></sup> The radial velocities were measured by the <a href="/wiki/High_Accuracy_Radial_Velocity_Planet_Searcher" title="High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher">High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher</a> spectrography system (HARPS) at the <a href="/wiki/La_Silla_Observatory" title="La Silla Observatory">La Silla Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>. The other less massive planets orbiting HD 40307 were discovered in the same way: <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_b" title="HD 40307 b">HD 40307 b</a> and <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_c" title="HD 40307 c">HD 40307 c</a> first,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6">[6]</a></sup> and then <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_e" title="HD 40307 e">HD 40307 e</a>, <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_f" title="HD 40307 f">HD 40307 f</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HD_40307_g" title="HD 40307 g">HD 40307 g</a>. The discovery of HD 40307 d and the former two was announced at the astrophysics conference that took place on June 16–18, 2008 in <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_and_mass">Orbit and mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307_d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit and mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HD 40307 d has a mass of at least 9.2 times Earth's; assuming that all planets in the system have coplanar orbits, it is the most massive planet known in the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-5">[5]</a></sup> The planet orbits approximately 0.135 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_units" title="Astronomical units">astronomical units</a> from its primary star, as compared to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s orbit at approximately one astronomical unit away from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. As a result, one year on HD 40307 d constitutes approximately 20.45 Earth days. The eccentricity of the planet's orbit was found to not differ significantly from zero, meaning that there is insufficient data to distinguish the orbit from an entirely circular one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayor08_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-mayor08-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The star around which HD 40307 d orbits has an unusually low metallicity compared to that of other planet-bearing stars. This supports a hypothesis concerning the possibility that the metallicity of stars during their births may determine whether a <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostar</a>'s accretion disk forms gas giants or terrestrial planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayor08_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-mayor08-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_40307_d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The planet has not been found to transit and, further, it is not likely to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuomi12_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-tuomi12-5">[5]</a></sup> It has also not been imaged. More specific physical characteristics such as <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a>, composition, and average surface <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> cannot be observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dynamical study of planets b, c, and d showed tidal effects at least on b and c, to the extent that b had to be a sub-Neptune. All the planets from b to at least f must have migrated inward. That study implied that d was a sub-Neptune as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-barnes2009-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>As such strong <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal forces</a> often result in the destruction of larger <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural satellites</a> in planets orbiting close to a star, it is unlikely that HD 40307 d hosts any satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes2002_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes2002-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
