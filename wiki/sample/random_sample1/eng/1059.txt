<p><b>HD 45350 b</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> located approximately 160 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Auriga_(constellation)" title="Auriga (constellation)">Auriga</a>. It has a <a href="/wiki/Minimum_mass" title="Minimum mass">minimum mass</a> about 1.79 times that of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>. The mean distance of the planet from the star is more than the distance between <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">eccentricity</a> of the orbit is nothing short of remarkable; at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periastron" title="Periastron">periastron</a> the planet is as close to the star as <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> is from the Sun, but at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apastron" title="Apastron">apastron</a> it is 8 times further. No doubt <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> on the planet would be extreme.</p>
<p>Dynamical simulations covering a period of 10<sup>7</sup> years show that a second, low-mass, planet could only orbit stably if it were no more than 0.2 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> away from the star; in the simulations, these planets show oscillations in eccentricity up to an eccentricity of 0.25. <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">Radial velocity</a> observations rule out any such planet whose mass is greater than 4 Neptune masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
