<p>The <b>dollar sign</b> (<b>$</b> or <b><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg"><img alt="Cifrão symbol.svg" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/13px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/20px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/26px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a></b>) is a symbol primarily used to indicate the various units of <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> around the world. The symbol can interchangeably have one or two vertical strokes. Note that while the two-stroked version is visually identical to the <a href="/wiki/Cifr%C3%A3o" title="Cifrão">cifrão</a>, it is not the same symbol.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg"><img alt="Dollar symbol evolution" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="281" data-file-width="498" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg/150px-Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg/225px-Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg/300px-Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dollar_Symbol_Evolution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Evolution of the dollar sign according to the best documented hypothesis (top) and one alternative hypothesis (bottom)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The sign is first attested in <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Spanish American</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada_under_British_rule_(1763%E2%80%931867)" title="Canada under British rule (1763–1867)">Canadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">Mexican</a> and other <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> business correspondence in the 1770s, referring to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish American</a> <a href="/wiki/Peso" title="Peso">peso</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> also known as "<a href="/wiki/Spanish_dollar" title="Spanish dollar">Spanish dollar</a>" or "piece of eight" in North America, which provided the model for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">currency</a> that the United States adopted in 1792 and the larger coins of the new Spanish American republics such as the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_peso" title="Mexican peso">Mexican peso</a>, Peruvian eight-<a href="/wiki/Peruvian_real" title="Peruvian real">real</a> and Bolivian eight-<a href="/wiki/Bolivian_sol" title="Bolivian sol">sol</a> coins.</p>
<p>The best documented explanation holds that the sign evolved out of the Spanish and Spanish American <a href="/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation" title="Scribal abbreviation">scribal abbreviation</a> "pˢ" for pesos. A study of late eighteenth- and early nineteenth-century manuscripts shows that the <i>s</i> gradually came to be written over the <i>p</i>, developing into a close equivalent to the "$" mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A variation, though less plausible, of this hypothesis derives the sign from a combination of the Greek character <a href="/wiki/Psi_(letter)" title="Psi (letter)">"psi" (ψ)</a> and "S".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_origin_hypotheses">Alternative origin hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative origin hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are a number of other hypotheses about the origin of the symbol, some with a measure of academic acceptance, others the symbolic equivalent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_etymologies" title="False etymologies">false etymologies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawn_with_two_vertical_lines">Drawn with two vertical lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drawn with two vertical lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cifr%C3%A3o" title="Cifrão">Cifrão</a></div>
<p>Several alternative hypotheses relate specifically to the dollar sign drawn with two vertical lines.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_coat_of_arms">Spanish coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spanish coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1113" data-file-width="1053" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png/200px-Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png/300px-Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png/400px-Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Columnas_Plus_Ultra.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a> with a small "S" shaped ribbon around in the City of <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, Spain (16th Century).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A common hypothesis holds that the sign derives from the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Spain" title="Coat of arms of Spain">Spanish coat of arms</a>, which showed the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a> with a banner curling between them.</p>
<p>In 1492, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a> adopted the symbol of the Pillars of Hercules and added the Latin warning <i>Non plus ultra</i> meaning "nothing further beyond", indicating "this is the end of the (known) world". But when Christopher Columbus came to America, the legend was changed to <i><a href="/wiki/Plus_ultra_(motto)" title="Plus ultra (motto)">Plus ultra</a></i>, meaning "further beyond".</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_(mazonado).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="900" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg/200px-Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg/300px-Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg/400px-Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_%28mazonado%29.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Escudo_de_Espa%C3%B1a_(mazonado).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Spain" title="Coat of arms of Spain">Spain's coat of arms</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The symbol was adopted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> and was part of his coat of arms representing Spain's American possessions. The symbol was later stamped on coins minted in gold and silver. The coin, also known as <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dollar" title="Spanish dollar">Spanish dollar</a>, was the first global currency used in the entire world since the Spanish Empire was the first global Empire. These coins, depicting the pillars over two hemispheres and a small "S"-shaped ribbon around each, were spread throughout America, Europe and Asia. According to this, traders wrote signs that, instead of saying Spanish dollar (piece of eight, <i>real de a ocho</i> in Spanish or <i>peso duro</i>), had this symbol made by hand, and this in turn evolved into a simple S with two vertical bars. When the US got independence from the UK, they created the American dollar, but in the first decades they still used the Spanish dollar because they were more popular in all markets.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_.22U.S..22">From "U.S."</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: From "U.S."'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A dollar sign with two vertical lines could have started off as a <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">monogram</a> of 'USA', used on money bags issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">United States Mint</a>. The letters U and S superimposed resemble the historical double-stroke dollar sign <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg"><img alt="Cifrão symbol.svg" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="19" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/19px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/29px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/38px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="19"/></a>: the bottom of the 'U' disappears into the bottom curve of the 'S', leaving two vertical lines. It is postulated from the papers of Dr. James Alton James, a professor of history at Northwestern University from 1897 to 1935, that the symbol with two strokes was an adapted design of the patriot <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier)" title="Robert Morris (financier)">Robert Morris</a> in 1778.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Robert Morris was such a zealous patriot – known as the "Financier of the Revolution in the West" – that conjecture does not overstep its bounds in purporting this hypothesis as viable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A similar idea claims that the letters U and S would stand for unit of silver, referencing pieces of eight again, but that is unlikely since one would expect it to be in Spanish instead.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="German_thaler">German thaler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German thaler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another hypothesis is that it derives from the symbol used on a German <a href="/wiki/Thaler" title="Thaler">Thaler</a>. According to Ovason (2004), on one type of thaler one side showed a <a href="/wiki/Crucifix" title="Crucifix">crucifix</a> while the other showed a <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">serpent</a> hanging from a <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">cross</a>, the letters NU near the serpent's head, and on the other side of the cross the number 21. This refers to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a>, Chapter 21 (see <a href="/wiki/Nehushtan" title="Nehushtan">Nehushtan</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>A similar symbol, constructed by superposition of "S" and "I" or "J", was used to denote German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joachimsthaler" title="Joachimsthaler">Joachimsthaler</a> ("S" and "J" standing for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Joachim" title="St. Joachim">St. Joachim</a> who gave his name to <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1chymov" title="Jáchymov">the place where the first thalers were minted</a>). It was known in the English-speaking world by the 17th century, appearing in 1686 edition of <i>An Introduction to Merchants' Accounts</i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Collins_(mathematician)" title="John Collins (mathematician)">John Collins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier)" title="Robert Morris (financier)">Robert Morris</a> was the first to use that symbol in official documents and in official communications with <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Pollock" title="Oliver Pollock">Oliver Pollock</a>. The US Dollar was directly based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Dollar" title="Spanish Dollar">Spanish Milled Dollar</a> when, in the <a href="/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1792" title="Coinage Act of 1792">Coinage Act of 1792</a>, the first Mint Act, its value was fixed (per the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Constitution" title="U.S. Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a>, Article I, Section 8, clause 1 power of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> "To coin Money, regulate the Value thereof, and of foreign Coin, and fix the Standard of Weights and Measures") as being "of the value of a Spanish milled dollar as the same is now current, and to contain three hundred and seventy-one grains and four sixteenth parts of a grain of pure, or four hundred and sixteen grains of standard silver".</p>
<p>According to a plaque in <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews" title="St Andrews">St Andrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the dollar sign was first cast into type at a foundry in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in 1797 by the Scottish immigrants John Baine, Archibald Binney and James Ronaldson.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg/220px-Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg/330px-Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg/440px-Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bailie_bell_plaque.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The plaque in St. Andrews.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The dollar sign did not appear on <a href="/wiki/Coins_of_the_United_States_dollar" title="Coins of the United States dollar">U.S. coinage</a> until February 2007,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when it was used on the reverse of a $1 coin authorized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidential_$1_Coin_Act_of_2005" title="Presidential $1 Coin Act of 2005">Presidential $1 Coin Act of 2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dollar sign appears as early as 1847 on the $100 Mexican War notes and the reverse of the 1869 $1000 United States note.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The dollar sign also appears on the reverse of the 1934 $100,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> note.</p>
<p>In Japanese and Korean, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_character" title="Han character">Han character</a> 弗 has been repurposed to represent the dollar sign due to its visual similarity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_computer_software">Use in computer software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use in computer software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dollar sign is one of the few symbols that are almost universally present in computer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Character_set" title="Character set">character sets</a> but rarely needed in its literal meaning within computer <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>. As a result, the character has been used on computers for many purposes unrelated to money. Its uses in <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> have often influenced or provoked its uses in <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">applications</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Encoding">Encoding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Encoding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dollar sign "$" has Unicode code point U+0024 (inherited from Latin-1).</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">U+</a>0024</span></span> <span style="font-size:"> $</span>  <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">DOLLAR SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#36;</code>)</li>
</ul>
<p>There are no separate characters for one and two line variants. This is typeface-dependent.</p>
<p>There are also three other codepoints that originate from other East Asian standards: the Taiwanese <a href="/wiki/Small_Form_Variants" title="Small Form Variants">small form variant</a>, the CJK <a href="/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms" title="Halfwidth and fullwidth forms">fullwidth form</a>, and the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Emoji" title="Emoji">emoji</a>. The glyphs for these codepoints are typically larger or smaller than the primary codepoint, but the difference is mostly aesthetic or typographic, and the meanings of the symbols are the same.</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">U+</a>FE69</span></span> <span style="font-size:"> ﹩</span>  <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">SMALL DOLLAR SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#65129;</code>)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">U+</a>FF04</span></span> <span style="font-size:"> ＄</span>  <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">FULL-WIDTH DOLLAR SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#65284;</code>)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">U+</a>1F4B2</span></span> <span style="font-size:"> 💲</span>  <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">HEAVY DOLLAR SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#128178;</code>)</li>
</ul>
<p>However, for usage as the special character in various computing applications (see following sections), U+0024 is typically the only code that is recognized.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>$ was used for defining <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">string</a> <a href="/wiki/BASIC#Data_types_and_variables" title="BASIC">variables</a> in older versions of the <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a> language ("$" was often pronounced "string" instead of "dollar" in this use).</li>
<li>$ is used for defining hexadecimal constants in <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>-like languages such as <a href="/wiki/Object_Pascal" title="Object Pascal">Delphi</a>, and in some variants of <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly language</a>.</li>
<li>$ is prefixed to names to define <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> in the <a href="/wiki/PHP" title="PHP">PHP</a> language and the <a href="/wiki/AutoIt" title="AutoIt">AutoIt</a> automation script language, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scalar_(computing)" title="Scalar (computing)">scalar</a> variables in the <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a> language (see <a href="/wiki/Sigil_(computer_programming)" title="Sigil (computer programming)">sigil (computer programming)</a>), and global variables in the <a href="/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language)" title="Ruby (programming language)">Ruby</a> language. In Perl programming this includes scalar elements of <a href="/wiki/Array_data_structure" title="Array data structure">arrays</a> <i>$array[7]</i> and <a href="/wiki/Hash_table#Associative_arrays" title="Hash table">hashes</a> <i>$hash{foo}</i>.</li>
<li>In most <a href="/wiki/Shell_script" title="Shell script">shell scripting</a> languages, $ is used for interpolating <a href="/wiki/Environment_variable" title="Environment variable">environment variables</a>, special variables, arithmetic computations and special characters, and for performing translation of localised strings.</li>
<li>$ is used in the <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a> language to delimit <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68#formatted_transput" title="ALGOL 68">transput format</a> regions.</li>
<li>$ is used in the <a href="/wiki/TeX" title="TeX">TeX</a> <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a> language to delimit mathematical regions.</li>
<li>In many versions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FORTRAN_66" title="FORTRAN 66">FORTRAN 66</a>, $ could be used as an alternative to a quotation mark for delimiting strings.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/PL/M" title="PL/M">PL/M</a>, $ can be used to put a visible separation between words in compound identifiers. For example, 'Some$Name' refers to the same thing as 'SomeName'.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Haskell_(programming_language)" title="Haskell (programming language)">Haskell</a>, $ is used as a function application operator.</li>
<li>In several <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> frameworks starting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prototype.js" title="Prototype.js">Prototype.js</a> and also popular in <a href="/wiki/JQuery" title="JQuery">jQuery</a>, $ is a common <a href="/wiki/Utility_class" title="Utility class">utility class</a>, and is often referred to as <i>the buck</i>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/ASP.NET" title="ASP.NET">ASP.NET</a>, the dollar sign used in a tag in the web page indicates an expression will follow it. The expression that follows is .NET <a href="/wiki/Language-independent_specification" title="Language-independent specification">language-agnostic</a>, as it will work with c#, vb.net, or any CLR supported language.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Erlang_(programming_language)" title="Erlang (programming language)">Erlang</a>, the dollar sign precedes character literals. The dollar sign as a character can be written $$.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobol" title="Cobol">COBOL</a> the $ sign is used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picture_clause" title="Picture clause">Picture clause</a> to depict a floating currency symbol as the left most character. The default symbol is $ however if the CURRENCY= or CURRENCY SIGN clause is specified, any single symbol can be used.</li>
<li>In some <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly languages</a> like <a href="/wiki/MIPS_instruction_set" title="MIPS instruction set">MIPS</a>, the $ sign is used to represent registers.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_6000_series" title="Honeywell 6000 series">Honeywell 6000 series</a> assembler, the $ sign, when used as an address, meant the address of the instruction in which it appeared.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/CMS-2_(programming_language)#Program_structure" title="CMS-2 (programming language)">CMS-2</a>, the $ sign is used as a statement terminator.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_systems">Operating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Operating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a> and subsequently in all versions of <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a> (<a href="/wiki/86-DOS" title="86-DOS">86-DOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/MS-DOS" title="MS-DOS">MS-DOS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_DOS" title="PC DOS">PC DOS</a>, more) and derivatives, $ is used as a string terminator (Int 21h with AH=09h).
<ul>
<li>$ is used by the <code>prompt</code> command to insert special sequences into the DOS command prompt string.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, $ is used at the end of the share name to hide a shared folder. For example, \\server\share is accessible and visible through browsing, while \\server\share$ is accessible only by explicit reference. Most <a href="/wiki/Administrative_share" title="Administrative share">administrative shares</a> are hidden.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Unix-like" title="Unix-like">Unix-like</a> systems the $ is often part of the <a href="/wiki/Command-line_interface#Command_prompt" title="Command-line interface">command prompt</a>, depending on the user's <a href="/wiki/Shell_(computing)" title="Shell (computing)">shell</a> and environment settings. For example, the default environment settings for the <a href="/wiki/Bash_(Unix_shell)" title="Bash (Unix shell)">bash</a> shell specify $ as part of the command prompt.
<dl>
<dd>The using <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=History_expansion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="History expansion (page does not exist)">history expansion</a> <code>!$</code> (same as <code>!!1$</code> and <code>!-1$</code>) means the last argument of the previous command in <a href="/wiki/Bash_(Unix_shell)" title="Bash (Unix shell)">bash</a>: <code>!-2$</code> expands to the last argument of the penultimate command, <code>!5$</code> expands into the last argument of the fifth command and so on. For example:</dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ul>
<div class="mw-highlight mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr">
<pre>
&gt; touch my_first_file
&gt; <span class="nb">echo</span> <span class="s2">"This is my file."</span> &gt; !$
</pre></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>where <code>!$</code> expands into <code>my_first_file</code>.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDAP" title="LDAP">LDAP</a> directory access protocol, $ is used as a line separator in various standard entry attributes such as postalAddress.</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNIVAC_EXEC_8" title="UNIVAC EXEC 8">UNIVAC EXEC 8</a> operating system, "$" meant "system." It was appended to entities such as the names of system files, the "sender" name in messages sent by the operator, and the default names of system-created files (like compiler output) when no specific name was specified (e.g., TPF$, NAME$, etc.)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Excel" title="Microsoft Excel">Microsoft Excel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and other spreadsheet software use the dollar sign ($) to denote an absolute cell reference.</li>
<li>$ matches the end of a line or string in <a href="/wiki/Sed" title="Sed">sed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grep" title="Grep">grep</a>, and <a href="/wiki/POSIX" title="POSIX">POSIX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a> <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expressions</a>, and, as a result:</li>
<li>$ signifies the end of a line or the file in text editors <a href="/wiki/Ed_(text_editor)" title="Ed (text editor)">ed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ex_(text_editor)" title="Ex (text editor)">ex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vi" title="Vi">vi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pico_(text_editor)" title="Pico (text editor)">pico</a> and derivatives.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Currencies_that_use_the_dollar_or_peso_sign">Currencies that use the dollar or peso sign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Currencies that use the dollar or peso sign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Dollar" title="Dollar">Dollar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peso" title="Peso">Peso</a></div>
<p>In addition to those countries of the world that use <a href="/wiki/Dollar#Economies_which_use_the_dollar" title="Dollar">dollars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Peso#Countries_currently_using_the_peso" title="Peso">pesos</a>, a number of other countries use the $ symbol to denote their currencies, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_c%C3%B3rdoba" title="Nicaraguan córdoba">Nicaraguan córdoba</a> (usually written as C$)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samoan_t%C4%81l%C4%81" title="Samoan tālā">Samoan tālā</a> (a transliteration of the word dollar)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tongan_pa%CA%BBanga" title="Tongan paʻanga">Tongan paʻanga</a></li>
</ul>
<p>An exception is the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_peso" title="Philippine peso">Philippine peso</a>, whose sign is written as <a href="/wiki/Philippine_peso_sign" title="Philippine peso sign">₱</a>.</p>
<p>The dollar sign is also still sometimes used to represent the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_ringgit" title="Malaysian ringgit">Malaysian ringgit</a> (which replaced the local <a href="/wiki/Malaya_and_British_Borneo_dollar" title="Malaya and British Borneo dollar">dollar</a>), though its official use to represent the currency has been discontinued since 1993.</p>
<p>Some currencies use the <i><a href="/wiki/Cifr%C3%A3o" title="Cifrão">cifrão</a></i> (<a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg"><img alt="Cifrão symbol.svg" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/15px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/23px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/30px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="15"/></a>), similar to the dollar sign, but always with two strokes:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brazilian_real" title="Brazilian real">Brazilian real</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_escudo" title="Cape Verde escudo">Cape Verde escudo</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_escudo" title="Portuguese escudo">Portuguese escudo</a> (defunct)</li>
</ul>
<p>In Mexico and other peso-using countries, the cifrão is used as a dollar sign when a document uses pesos and dollars at the same time, to avoid confusions, but, when it used alone, usually is represented as US $ (United States dollars). Example: US $5 (five US dollars).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, the $ sign is always used for pesos, and if they want to indicate dollars, they always write U$S 5 or US$5 (5 US dollars).</p>
<p>In the United States, Mexico, Australia, Argentina, New Zealand, Hong Kong, Pacific Island nations, and English-speaking Canada, the dollar or peso symbol precedes the number, unlike most currency symbols. Five dollars or pesos is written and printed as $5, whereas five cents is written as 5<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%A2" title="¢">¢</a>. In French-speaking Canada, the dollar symbol usually appears after the number (5$), although it sometimes appears in front of it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_sign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dollar sign is also used in library cataloging to represent subsections.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Also, it is used derisively to indicate greed or excess money such as in "<a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Micro$oft</a>", "<a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Luca$</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Lars_Ulrich" title="Lars Ulrich">Lar$ Ulrich</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Di$ney</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelsea_FC" title="Chelsea FC">Chel$ea</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Greater_Western_Sydney_Giants" title="Greater Western Sydney Giants">GW$</a>"; or supposed overt Americanization as in "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Sky_Broadcasting" title="British Sky Broadcasting">$ky</a>". The dollar sign is also used intentionally to stylize names such as <a href="/wiki/ASAP_Rocky" title="ASAP Rocky">A$AP Rocky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kesha" title="Kesha">Ke$ha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ty_Dolla_Sign" title="Ty Dolla Sign">Ty Dolla $ign</a> or words such as <b>¥€$</b>. In 1872, <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Bierce" title="Ambrose Bierce">Ambrose Bierce</a> referred to the California Governor as <a href="/wiki/Leland_Stanford" title="Leland Stanford">$tealand Landford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a> notation, a dollar sign is placed after a word to indicate that it is valid according to the <a href="/wiki/Official_Tournament_and_Club_Word_List" title="Official Tournament and Club Word List">North American word lists</a>, but not according to the British word lists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
