<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">HD ready</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>HD ready</b> is a certification program introduced in 2005 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EICTA" title="EICTA">EICTA</a> (European Information, Communications and Consumer Electronics Technology Industry Associations), now <a href="/wiki/DigitalEurope" title="DigitalEurope">DIGITALEUROPE</a>.</p>
<p>There are currently four different labels: "HD ready", "HD TV", "HD ready 1080p", "HD TV 1080p". The logos are awarded to television equipment capable of certain features.</p>
<p>In the United States, a similar "HD Ready" term usually refers to any display that is capable of accepting and displaying a high-definition signal at either <a href="/wiki/720p" title="720p">720p</a>, <a href="/wiki/1080i" title="1080i">1080i</a> or <a href="/wiki/1080p" title="1080p">1080p</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Component_video" title="Component video">component video</a> or digital input, but does not have a built-in HD-capable tuner.</p>
<p>The similar HD ready term in the US to the certification in Europe might falsely imply that a TV from one region works in another (for e.g. HD that differs by <a href="/wiki/Frame_rate" title="Frame rate">frame rate</a>, if not the older non-HD formats); The consensus seems to be the American TVs do not work (fully) with European [HD] broadcast, while European TVs are multi-system (may or may not have or strictly need a US TV tuner, but will support those broadcasts [with an external tuner]).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_ready&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "HD ready" certification program was introduced on January 19, 2005. The labels and relevant specifications are based on agreements between over 60 broadcasters and manufacturers of the European HDTV Forum at its second session in June 2004, held at the <a href="/wiki/Betzdorf,_Luxembourg" title="Betzdorf, Luxembourg">Betzdorf, Luxembourg</a> headquarters of founding member <a href="/wiki/SES_Astra" title="SES Astra">SES Astra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "HD ready" logo is awarded to television equipment capable of displaying <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">High Definition</a> (HD) pictures from an external source. However, it does not have to feature a digital tuner to decode an HD signal; devices with tuners were certified under a separate "HD TV" logo, which does not require a "HD ready" display device.</p>
<p>Before the introduction of the "HD ready" certification, many TV sources and displays were being promoted as capable of displaying high definition pictures when they were in fact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SDTV" title="SDTV">SDTV</a> devices; according to Alexander Oudendijk, senior VP of marketing for <a href="/wiki/SES_Astra" title="SES Astra">Astra</a>, in early 2005 there were 74 different devices being sold as ready for HD that were not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Devices advertised as HD-compatible or HD ready could take <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">HDTV</a>-signal as an input (via analog -<a href="/wiki/YPbPr" title="YPbPr">YPbPr</a> or digital <a href="/wiki/Digital_Visual_Interface" title="Digital Visual Interface">DVI</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-Definition_Multimedia_Interface" title="High-Definition Multimedia Interface">HDMI</a>), but they did not have enough pixels for true representation of even the lower HD resolution (1280 × 720) (<a href="/wiki/Plasma_display" title="Plasma display">plasma</a>-based sets with 853 × 480 resolution, <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT</a> based sets only capable of <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">SDTV</a>-resolution or <a href="/wiki/Video_Graphics_Array" title="Video Graphics Array">VGA</a>-resolution, 640×480 pixels), much less the higher HD resolution (1920 × 1080), and so were unable to display the HD picture without downscaling to a lower resolution. Industry-sponsored labels such as "Full HD" were misleading as well, as they can refer to devices which do not fulfil some essential requirements such as having <a href="/wiki/1:1_pixel_mapping" title="1:1 pixel mapping">1:1 pixel mapping</a> with no overscan or accepting a 1080p signal.</p>
<p>A UK <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television programme found that separate labels for display devices and TV tuners/decoders confused purchasers, many of whom bought HD-ready equipment expecting to be able to receive HD with no additional equipment;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rob_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rob-5">[5]</a></sup> they were sometimes actively misled by salespeople—a 2007 <a href="/wiki/Ofcom" title="Ofcom">Ofcom</a> survey found that 12% were told explicitly that they could view analog SDTV transmissions in HD, 7% that no extra equipment was needed, and 14% that HD-ready sets would receive existing digital SDTV transmissions in HD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rob_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-rob-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 30, 2007, 1080p versions of the logos and licensing agreements were introduced; as an improvement to the earlier scheme, "HD TV 1080p" logo now requires "HD ready 1080p" certification.</p>

<p><b>HD ready</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HD_ready_logo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HD_ready_logo-6">[6]</a></sup> and <b>HD ready 1080p</b> logos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1080p_logo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-1080p_logo-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> are awarded to displays (including integrated television sets, computer monitors and projectors) which have certain capabilities to process and display <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition</a> source video signal, outlined in a table below.</p>
<p>The <b>HD TV</b> logo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HD_TV_logo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HD_TV_logo-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> is awarded to either integrated <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">digital</a> television sets (containing a display conforming to "HD ready" requirements) or standalone <a href="/wiki/Set-top_box" title="Set-top box">set-top boxes</a> which are capable of receiving, decoding and outputting or displaying high-definition broadcasts (that is, include a <a href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcasting" title="Digital Video Broadcasting">DVB</a> tuner for cable, terrestrial or satellite broadcasting, a video decoder which supports <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264/MPEG-4 AVC</a> compression in 720p and 1080i signal formats, and either video outputs or an integrated display capable of handling such signals).</p>
<p>The <b>HD TV 1080p</b> logo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1080p_logo_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-1080p_logo-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is awarded to integrated digital television sets which have a display conforming to "HD ready 1080p" requirements, a DVB tuner and a decoder capable of processing 1080p signal.</p>
