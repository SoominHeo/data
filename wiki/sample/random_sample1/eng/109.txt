<p>The <b>British general election of 1722</b> elected members to serve in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Great_Britain" title="House of Commons of Great Britain">House of Commons</a> of the 6th <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of Great Britain</a>. This was the fifth such election since the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> in 1707. Thanks to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septennial_Act_1715" title="Septennial Act 1715">Septennial Act</a> of 1715, which swept away the maximum three-year life of a parliament created by the <a href="/wiki/Meeting_of_Parliament_Act_1694" title="Meeting of Parliament Act 1694">Meeting of Parliament Act 1694</a>, it followed some seven years after the previous election, that of 1715.</p>
<p>The election was fiercely fought, with contests taking place in more than half of the constituencies, which was unusual for the time. Despite the level of public involvement, however, with the Whigs having consolidated their control over virtually every branch of government, Walpole's party commanded almost a monopoly of electoral patronage, and was therefore able to increase its majority in Parliament even as its popular support fell.</p>
<p>In the midst of the election, word came from France of a <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> plot aimed at an imminent <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a>. Led by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Atterbury" title="Francis Atterbury">Francis Atterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rochester" title="Bishop of Rochester">Bishop of Rochester</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_North,_6th_Baron_North" title="William North, 6th Baron North">Lord North and Grey</a>, and other Tory opponents of Walpole, this was later known as the "<a href="/wiki/Atterbury_Plot" title="Atterbury Plot">Atterbury Plot</a>".</p>
<p>Previously election results had broadly reflected the opinion of at least the minority of adult males who had the vote, although the system had always been subject to the influence of corruption and patronage. However, now that one-party government had been established, those influences could be used systematically to ensure the governing party's victory. This election set the pattern for much of the rest of the eighteenth century; as partisan feeling began to decline during the years of the Whig oligarchy, the rigging of elections became ever easier, so that British governments could almost always guarantee victory.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_the_Constituencies">Summary of the Constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_general_election,_1722&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary of the Constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/British_general_election,_1796" title="British general election, 1796">British general election, 1796</a> for details. The constituencies used were the same throughout the existence of the Parliament of Great Britain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dates_of_Election">Dates of Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_general_election,_1722&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dates of Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The general election was held between 19 March 1722 and 9 May 1722. At this period elections did not take place at the same time in every constituency. The returning officer in each county or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_borough" title="Parliamentary borough">parliamentary borough</a> fixed the precise date (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hustings" title="Hustings">hustings</a> for details of the conduct of the elections).</p>
