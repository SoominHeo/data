<p>The <b>IAI Heron</b> (<b>Machatz-1</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium-altitude_long-endurance" title="Medium-altitude long-endurance">medium-altitude long-endurance</a> <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicle</a> (UAV) developed by the Malat (UAV) division of <a href="/wiki/Israel_Aerospace_Industries" title="Israel Aerospace Industries">Israel Aerospace Industries</a>. It is capable of Medium Altitude Long Endurance (MALE) operations of up to 52 hours' duration at up to 10.5 km (35,000 ft). It has demonstrated 52 hours of continuous flight, but the effective operational maximal flight duration is less, according to payload and flight profile. There is a new version, Heron TP, also known as <a href="/wiki/IAI_Eitan" title="IAI Eitan">IAI Eitan</a>.</p>
<p>On 11 September 2005, it was announced that the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a> purchased US$50 million worth of Heron systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IAI_Heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heron navigates using an internal <a href="/wiki/GPS_navigation_device" title="GPS navigation device">GPS navigation device</a>, and either a pre-programmed flight profile (in which case the system is fully autonomous from takeoff to landing), manual override from a <a href="/wiki/Ground_control_station" title="Ground control station">ground control station</a>, or a combination of both. It can autonomously return to base and land in case of lost communication with the ground station. The system has fully automatic launch and recovery (ALR) and all-weather capabilities.</p>
<p>Heron can carry an array of sensors, including <a href="/wiki/Thermographic_camera" title="Thermographic camera">thermographic camera</a> (infrared) and visible-light <a href="/wiki/Airborne_ground_surveillance" title="Airborne ground surveillance">airborne ground surveillance</a>, intelligence systems (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COMINT" title="COMINT">COMINT</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ELINT" title="ELINT">ELINT</a>) and various <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> systems, totaling up to 250 kg (550 lb). Heron is also capable of <a href="/wiki/Target_acquisition" title="Target acquisition">target acquisition</a> and artillery adjustment.</p>
<p>The payload sensors communicate with the ground control station in real time, using either direct <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line of sight</a> <a href="/wiki/Data_link" title="Data link">data link</a>, or via an airborne/satellite relay. Like the navigation system, the payload can also be used in either a fully pre-programmed autonomous mode, or manual real-time remote operation, or a combination of both.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Super_Heron">Super Heron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IAI_Heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Super Heron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singapore_Air_Show" title="Singapore Air Show">Singapore Air Show</a> in February 2014, IAI unveiled the <b>Super Heron</b> refinement of the Heron UAS. The Super Heron has a heavy fuel 200 horsepower engine that increases its rate of climb and performance. Its range is 250 km (160 mi) line-of-sight and 1,000 km (620 mi) by satellite control. Endurance is 45 hours at a maximum altitude of 30,000 ft (9,100 m). Cruising speed is 60 to 80 kn (110 to 150 km/h; 69 to 92 mph) and top speed over 150 kn (280 km/h; 170 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IAI_Heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Heron saw significant use during Operation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cast_Lead" title="Cast Lead">Cast Lead</a> in Gaza of 2008–2009. During the deployment, each brigade combat team was assigned a UAV squadron for close support. This was the first Israeli operation in which UAVs, helicopters, and fighter jets were allocated to ground forces directly without IAF central command authorizing sorties. Air-support controller teams operated alongside brigade commanders at the front emphasizing the brigade commander's utilization of direct air assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DefenceNews_2009-03-08_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DefenceNews_2009-03-08-4">[4]</a></sup> A high degree of situational awareness was achieved by maintaining at least a dozen UAVs in flight over Gaza at all times. Aerial surveillance was provided by Heron and <a href="/wiki/Elbit_Hermes_450" title="Elbit Hermes 450">Hermes 450</a> UAVs and Apache attack helicopters. Along with coordination between the Air Force and ground troops, Israel ground forces were able to utilize cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/Shin_Bet" title="Shin Bet">Israel Security Agency</a> by having operatives attached to the forward units. This inter-service coordination allowed for a higher level of tactical awareness and the ability to strike time-critical targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aviation_Week_2009-05-11_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aviation_Week_2009-05-11-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other countries operating the Heron include <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> operates a derivative of Heron named <b>Eagle</b> or <b>Harfang</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2008, Canada announced a plan to lease a Heron for use in Afghanistan, starting in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As of mid-2009, Australia leased two Herons as part of a multimillion-dollar lease to operate the vehicles in Afghanistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In early July 2013, the Heron reached 15,000 flight hours over Afghanistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Australia concluded its use of the Heron in support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Slipper" title="Operation Slipper">Operation Slipper</a> in Afghanistan on 30 November 2014, after it had accumulated 27,000 flight hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heron_variants">Heron variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IAI_Heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heron variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Turkey operates a special variant of the Heron, which utilizes Turkish-designed and manufactured electro-optical subsystems. For example, the Turkish Herons use the ASELFLIR-300T airborne thermal Imaging and targeting system designed and manufactured by <a href="/wiki/ASELSAN" title="ASELSAN">ASELSAN</a> of Turkey. The Turkish Herons also have stronger engines in order to compensate for the added payload created by the heavier ASELFLIR-300T. This is the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FLIR" title="FLIR">FLIR</a> system currently used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TAI/AgustaWestland_T-129" title="TAI/AgustaWestland T-129">TAI/AgustaWestland T-129</a> attack helicopter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and also the <a href="/wiki/TAI_Anka" title="TAI Anka">TAI Anka</a> <a href="/wiki/Medium-altitude_long-endurance_unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Medium-altitude long-endurance unmanned aerial vehicle">MALE UAV</a>. IAI staff maintain that the Turkish Heron's "with its enhanced performance, is better than all existing Heron UAVs operating worldwide”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Aerospace_Industries" title="Turkish Aerospace Industries">Turkish Aerospace Industries</a> (TUSAŞ) provides maintenance and overhaul services for its Herons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/EADS_Harfang" title="EADS Harfang">EADS Harfang</a> - variant operated by France</li>
</ul>

<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> <cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://www.israeli-weapons.com/weapons/aircraft/uav/heron/heron.html" rel="nofollow">"Heron/Shoval/Eitan"</a>. <p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> two on ground</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 8.5 m (27 ft 10 in)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 16.60 m (54 ft 5 in)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 1,150 kg (2,530 lb)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 1 × <a href="/wiki/Rotax_914" title="Rotax 914">Rotax 914</a>, 86 kW (115 hp)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 207 km/h (113 knots, 130 mph)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 350 km (189 nm, 217 mi)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 10,000 m (32,800 ft)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 150 m/min (492 ft/min)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 89 kg/m² (18.2 lb/ft²)<br/></li>
<li><b>Endurance:</b> 52 hours<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Payload:</b> 250 kg (550 lb)</li>
</ul>
