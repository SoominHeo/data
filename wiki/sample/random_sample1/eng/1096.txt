<p><b>Model M</b> is a designation for a group of <a href="/wiki/Computer_keyboard" title="Computer keyboard">computer keyboards</a> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> starting in 1984, and later by <a href="/wiki/Lexmark" title="Lexmark">Lexmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unicomp" title="Unicomp">Unicomp</a> and MaxiSwitch. The keyboard's many variations have their own distinct characteristics, with the vast majority having a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buckling-spring" title="Buckling-spring">buckling-spring</a> key design and swappable <a href="/wiki/Keycap" title="Keycap">keycaps</a>. Model M keyboards have been praised by computer enthusiasts and frequent typists due to their durability and consistency, and the tactile and auditory feedback they provide.</p>
<p>The Model M is also regarded as a timeless and durable piece of hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LifeHacker_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LifeHacker-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCWorld_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCWorld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCAdvisor_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCAdvisor-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the computers and computer peripherals produced concurrently with the Model M are considered obsolete, many Model M keyboards are still in use due to their physical durability and the continued validity of their ANSI 101-key and ISO 102-key layouts. Since their original popularity, new generations of writers and computer technicians have rediscovered their unique functionality and aesthetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-5">[5]</a></sup> The Kentucky-based company <a href="/wiki/Unicomp" title="Unicomp">Unicomp</a> continues to manufacture and sell Model M-style keyboards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Model_M_keyboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Capuchon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1198" data-file-width="1360" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Capuchon.jpg/200px-Capuchon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Capuchon.jpg/300px-Capuchon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Capuchon.jpg/400px-Capuchon.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

Keycap in a French Model M</div>

<p>The Model M was designed to be more cost effective than the <a href="/wiki/Model_F_keyboard" title="Model F keyboard">Model F keyboards</a> it replaced. Production of Model M's began in 1985; they were often bundled with new IBM computers. They were produced at IBM plants in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a>; <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, Scotland and <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara" title="Guadalajara">Guadalajara</a>, Mexico. The most common Model M variant is the part number 1391401, the US English layout keyboard of the IBM PS/2. Until around 1993, most Model M's included a sturdy, coiled, detachable cable, with either an <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer/AT" title="IBM Personal Computer/AT">AT</a> (pre-1987) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PS/2_connector" title="PS/2 connector">PS/2</a> connector, in 5- and 10-foot lengths (1.5 and 3 metres). From about 1994 onwards, flat non-detachable cables were used to reduce manufacturing costs; however, IBM retained its 101-key layout, never implementing the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> keys common on other keyboards from that time. Unicomp later designed a 104-key Model M with Windows keys.</p>
<p>On March 27, 1991, IBM divested a number of its hardware manufacturing operations, including keyboard manufacturing, to the investment firm Clayton &amp; Dubilier, Inc. in a leveraged buyout to form Lexmark International Group, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lexmark continued manufacturing the Model M in the United States, United Kingdom and Mexico, with IBM being Lexmark's major customer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Many of these keyboards had IBM assembly part numbers such as 52G9658, 52G9700, 82G2383 and 42H1292. Due to pricing pressures, many Model M's in this period were manufactured with a new lower-cost design<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> including lighter plastic, an integrated cable, and a single color for key legends.</p>
<p>On March 27, 1996, Lexmark's contract to make keyboards for IBM expired, and Lexmark transitioned its keyboard business to IBM and other vendors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Some of its manufacturing assets were sold to a group of Lexmark employees, who formed <a href="/wiki/Unicomp" title="Unicomp">Unicomp</a>. Unicomp still manufactures its version of the Model M (similar to part number 42H1292 but first renamed 42H1292U and subsequently the "Customizer"), as well as other configurations including updated 104- and 105-key layouts; a <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> layout (where the Ctrl and Caps Lock keys and Esc and tilde keys are transposed); models with integrated <a href="/wiki/Pointing_stick" title="Pointing stick">pointing sticks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trackball" title="Trackball">trackballs</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale" title="Point of sale">POS</a>-specific models such as those with built-in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magstripe" title="Magstripe">magstripe</a> readers. When Lexmark discontinued keyboard production in March 1996, IBM continued production in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenock,_Scotland" title="Greenock, Scotland">Greenock, Scotland</a> and subcontracted production of the Model M13 keyboard (IBM Enhanced Keyboard with TrackPoint II) to Maxi Switch in Mexico. These companies stopped producing Model M's in 1999 and 1998 respectively. Although current Unicomp models may still be purchased, original production models retain their value well among collectors and computer enthusiasts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Model_M_keyboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Model M's numerous variations (referred to as "part numbers") incorporated alternative features and/or colors. One of the most popular variants is the Space Saving Keyboard, which integrates the number pad into the keyboard's main section, substantially reducing its width.</p>
<p>IBM released the standard and Space Saving Model M's in an alternative 'gray/pebble' colour for use with their Industrial computers; this darker color was designed to conceal discoloration from handling in production environments. Other variable features include a grounded spacebar and, on some later models, drainage holes to deter damage from spilled drinks.</p>

<p>The variant most commonly referred to as "Model M" is Part No. 1391401, on which many other variants were based. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Keyboard" title="Enhanced Keyboard">Enhanced Keyboard</a> model included IBM's patented buckling spring design<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and swappable keycaps.</p>
<p>The Model M's design has been widely praised as durable and reliable, and has remained basically the same since the 1980s, while virtually all other computing hardware, from <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PCs</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_display" title="Computer display">monitors</a> to <a href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">mice</a>, has changed dramatically.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (April 2012)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> The M's sturdy design, including its heavy <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> backplate and strong <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> frame, has allowed even the most abused examples to survive for years.</p>
<p>The Model M's buckling spring key design gives it a unique feel and sound. Unlike more common and cheaper <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_technology#Dome-switch_keyboard" title="Keyboard technology">dome switch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Membrane_keyboard" title="Membrane keyboard">membrane</a> designs, buckling springs give users unmistakable tactile and auditory feedback. Because of its more defined touch, some users report they can type faster and more accurately on the Model M than on other keyboards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Model M is also less susceptible to dirt and wear. Dirt can interfere with the proper operation of most other keyboard technologies; however, the buckling spring switch's design is such that any dirt that falls between the keys is unlikely to enter the switch mechanism, which is covered by the key. Even if dirt were to enter a mechanism, a large amount would be necessary to prevent it from operating.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Most Model M's, until the late 4th-generation variants, have a 1.25" slotted, circular speaker grille molded into their bottom surfaces. Relatively few contain an actual speaker, however, which was useful only for sounding beep codes on older terminal systems. (The most common Model M with a speaker is p/n 1394540, made for RS/6000 UNIX workstations.)</p>
<p>There are some drawbacks to the Model M design:</p>
<ul>
<li>Because they are so large and heavy (over 3 lbs, heavier than some modern laptops) they are not as portable as more modern keyboards.</li>
<li>Their buckling-spring keys are noisy enough to be inappropriate in quiet locations such as <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">libraries</a> and medical facilities.</li>
<li>Liquids spilled on most Model M's do not drain out, and remain in the keyboard with potential to cause a <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">short circuit</a>. The later 42H1292 and 59G780 designs, as well as post-1993 1370477s and 1391401s made mostly by Lexmark and Unicomp, have drainage channels designed to avoid this problem.</li>
<li>Unicomp currently produces Model M's with USB connectors that can be connected directly to modern PCs. However, earlier Model M's have either AT or PS/2 connectors, requiring adapters for PCs lacking those capabilities. (PS/2-to-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus">USB</a> adapters exist for computers without PS/2 ports; and additional AT-to-PS/2 adapters, or SDL-to-PS/2 cables, for computers without AT ports.) Some PS/2-to-USB adapters are unreliable with early Model M's that require more power than the adapters can provide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Model_M_keyboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg/200px-Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg/300px-Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg/400px-Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Model_M_Logo_Comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The square aluminium badge on a 1390131 series keyboard compared to other variants.</div>

<p>All Model M keyboards made by IBM and Lexmark have an ID label on the underside indicating the assembly part number, individual serial number, and manufacturing date. The general period in which a Model M was made can also often be distinguished by the type of logo "badge" above its keys. The first model M's (part numbers 1390120 or 1390131) have a square aluminum logo badge in the top right corner. Part number 1391401, and most variants based on it, has a gray IBM logo in a recessed oval at the board's upper left. Later IBM-made Model M's, and variants subsequently made by Lexmark in the early 1990s (part numbers 1370477, 52G9658, 52G9700, 59G7980, 92G7453, 82G2383, 42H1292, etc.) have a similar oval badge area with a blue logo.</p>
