<p>The <b>IC 342/Maffei Group</b> (also known as the <b>IC 342 Group</b> or the <b>Maffei 1 Group</b>) is the nearest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_of_galaxies" title="Group of galaxies">group of galaxies</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>. The group can be described as a binary group; the member galaxies are mostly concentrated around either <a href="/wiki/IC_342" title="IC 342">IC 342</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maffei_1" title="Maffei 1">Maffei 1</a>, both of which are the brightest galaxies within the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsev2005_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsev2005-1">[1]</a></sup> The group is part of the <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster" title="Virgo Supercluster">Virgo Supercluster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tully1982_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tully1982-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreground_dust_obscuration">Foreground dust obscuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IC_342/Maffei_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreground dust obscuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As seen from Earth, the group lies near the plane of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> (a region sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance" title="Zone of Avoidance">Zone of Avoidance</a>). Consequently, the light from many of the galaxies is severely affected by dust obscuration within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. This complicates observational studies of the group, as uncertainties in the dust obscuration also affect measurements of the galaxies' <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosities</a> and distances as well as other related quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsev2005_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsev2005-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butamccall1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-butamccall1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moreover, the galaxies within the group have historically been difficult to identify. Many galaxies have only been discovered using late 20th century astronomical instrumentation. For example, while many fainter, more distant galaxies, such as the galaxies in the <a href="/wiki/New_General_Catalogue" title="New General Catalogue">New General Catalogue</a>, were already identified visually by the end of the nineteenth century,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Maffei_1" title="Maffei 1">Maffei 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maffei_2" title="Maffei 2">Maffei 2</a> were only discovered in 1968 using <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> photographic images of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maffei1968_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-maffei1968-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, it is difficult to determine whether some objects near IC 342 or Maffei 1 are galaxies associated with the IC 342/Maffei Group or diffuse foreground objects within the Milky Way that merely look like galaxies. For example, the objects <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MB_2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MB 2 (page does not exist)">MB 2</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Camelopardalis_C&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Camelopardalis C (page does not exist)">Camelopardalis C</a> were once thought to be dwarf galaxies in the IC 342/Maffei Group but are now known to be objects within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsevetal2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsevetal2003-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Group_formation_and_possible_interactions_with_the_Local_Group">Group formation and possible interactions with the Local Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IC_342/Maffei_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Group formation and possible interactions with the Local Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the IC 342/Maffei Group and the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a> are located physically close to each other, the two groups may have influenced each other's evolution during the early stages of galaxy formation. An analysis of the velocities and distances to the IC 342/Maffei Group as measured by M. J. Valtonen and collaborators suggested that <a href="/wiki/IC_342" title="IC 342">IC 342</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maffei_1" title="Maffei 1">Maffei 1</a> were moving faster than what could be accounted for in the expansion of the universe. They therefore suggested that IC 342 and Maffei 1 were ejected from the Local Group after a violent gravitational interaction with the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a> during the early stages of the formation of the two groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valtonenetal1993_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-valtonenetal1993-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, this interpretation is dependent on the distances measured to the galaxies in the group, which in turn is dependent on accurately measuring the degree to which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">interstellar dust</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> obscures the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butamccall1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-butamccall1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fingerhutetal2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fingerhutetal2006-8">[8]</a></sup> More recent observations have demonstrated that the dust obscuration may have been previously overestimated, so the distances may have been underestimated. If these new distance measurements are correct, then the galaxies in the IC 342/Maffei Group appear to be moving at the rate expected from the expansion of the universe, and the scenario of a collision between the IC 342/Maffei Group and the Local Group would be implausible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fingerhutetal2006_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-fingerhutetal2006-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
