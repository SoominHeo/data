<p>The <b>IDS Center</b> is a skyscraper located at 80 South 8th Street in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. Completed in 1972, it is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_in_Minnesota" title="List of tallest buildings in Minnesota">tallest building in the state</a> at a height of 792 feet (241 m). It originally stood 775 feet 6 inches (236.37 m), though a 16-foot (4.9 m) garage for window washing equipment was added between 1978 and 1979. The structure rises to 910 feet (10,900 in) when including communications spires on the roof, indisputably the highest points in the city. The IDS was constructed as the headquarters of Investors Diversified Services, Inc.—now <a href="/wiki/Ameriprise_Financial" title="Ameriprise Financial">Ameriprise Financial</a>. It also housed the headquarters of Dayton Hudson Corporation (now <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target Corporation</a>) from 1972 until 2001.</p>
<p>The complex consists of five parts: the 57-story IDS Tower itself at 8th &amp; Nicollet Streets, an 8-story annex building along Marquette Avenue, the 19-story Marquette Hotel at 7th Street &amp; Marquette Avenue, and a 2-story retail building that was originally dominated by <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Company" title="F. W. Woolworth Company">Woolworth's</a>. These four buildings are joined by the 7-story Crystal Court.</p>
<p>The 57-story IDS became the tallest skyscraper in Minneapolis when it surpassed the height of the 32-story <a href="/wiki/Foshay_Tower" title="Foshay Tower">Foshay Tower</a> in 1972, ending that building's 43-year reign over the city skyline. Construction of the building was followed with great interest, and the topping-off ceremony was a major civic event in the city. In addition to being taller, IDS occupies a much larger footprint than the obelisk-like Foshay.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_environment">Design and environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Lobby_(room)" title="Lobby (room)">lobby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shopping" title="Shopping">shopping</a> area at the bottom of the tower is known as the <b>Crystal Court</b>, and provides <a href="/wiki/Skyway" title="Skyway">skyway</a> connections between the tower and four adjacent blocks. The Concourse level is occupied by Globe College and University; originally this floor was an extension of the Crystal Court retail space and included a single-screen movie theater and shops. The building had a 51st floor <a href="/wiki/Observation_deck" title="Observation deck">observation deck</a> until 1983. Thousands of people came for one last visit on December 31, 1983. This floor is now office space. The 50th Floor contained an east-facing "Orion Room" restaurant, a north-facing bar and cocktail lounge, a private south-facing dining club ("Tower Club"), all which were converted to office space. The west-facing "University of Minnesota Alumni Club" closed to the public in 1994. Today, the entire 50th floor consists of four large ballrooms with a single central kitchen. The rooms are collectively known as "Windows on Minnesota," and they serve as banquet space for the Marquette Hotel.</p>
<p>Because of the IDS Center's peculiar and unique stepback design, termed "zogs" by its architect, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Johnson" title="Philip Johnson">Philip Johnson</a>, each floor has up to 32 corner offices. The area of <a href="/wiki/Nicollet_Mall" title="Nicollet Mall">Nicollet Mall</a> in front of the IDS Center is familiar to television viewers: The character of Mary Richards on "The Mary Tyler Moore Show" was seen on Nicollet Mall looking at an IDS shop in the opening montage of the series. Across 7th Street from the IDS was Donaldson's Department Store in front of which she tossed her hat in the air at the end of the opening sequence. A statue commemorating that shot stands kitty-corner from the view, created and maintained by TVLand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> She is also seen in the show opening dining with then-husband Grant Tinker at what is now the terrace of Basil's Restaurant on the hotel's third floor overlooking the Crystal Court, where diners can sit at the "Mary Tyler Moore Table."</p>
<p>The IDS Tower has an 8-story annex extending along the Marquette Avenue side of the building. This building is a true annex; the 4th through 8th floors can only be reached through the IDS Tower elevators. The 3rd floor can only be reached through the Marquette Hotel elevators.</p>
<p>The building has not been without structural problems. Since soon after its construction, the Crystal Court had issues with water leaking through the roof after rain or snow due to effects of Minnesota's extreme freeze-thaw cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib060407_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib060407-3">[3]</a></sup> There are also frequent problems in the winter when ice falls from the tower and onto the court's glass roof panels, often breaking through. Occasionally the court will be roped off to prevent injury to the public. The Center and Crystal Court were devastated by a summer wind storm in June 1979 that led to much glass breakage. The entire Crystal Court was sealed off by plywood barriers while this was repaired.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_battle_for_tallest_status">The battle for tallest status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The battle for tallest status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The owners of the <a href="/wiki/Capella_Tower" title="Capella Tower">Capella Tower</a> (formerly First Bank Place) and the <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architects</a> behind the design stated that it rose 774 feet (236 m) tall upon its completion in 1992. However, the height had been increased due to an engineering need, according to Tom O'Mara, one of the construction managers at the time of Capella Tower construction. There were some <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duct_(HVAC)" title="Duct (HVAC)">ducts</a> near the roof that required about 14 more inches (36 cm) of height. The construction team added an extra 10 inches (25 cm) to that, bringing the building to a total of 776 feet 0 inches (236.52 m).</p>
<p>In the years following completion, the actual height eventually became known as it was published in <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">almanacs</a> and other listings of building height. The owners of the <a href="/wiki/Capella_Tower" title="Capella Tower">Capella Tower</a> were hesitant to claim that their building was taller than IDS, and usually deferred the honor to the more well-known structure. As area journalists reported on the sale of the IDS Center to the John Buck Company in 2004 and the death of designer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Johnson" title="Philip Johnson">Philip Johnson</a> in 2005, they came face-to-face with the fact that the roof of the tower was one foot lower than its neighbor.</p>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://emporis.com" rel="nofollow">Emporis.com</a> restored the IDS Center to first-place status in the city in February 2005 by including the height of the window-washing garage, although that has not completely ended the dispute. A spokesperson for the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Tall_Buildings_and_Urban_Habitat" title="Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat">Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat</a>, which sometimes handles height disputes, stated that it would be unlikely for the garage to be included in the official height because many would not consider it to be an integral part of the building's design.</p>
<p>It is unclear if the height of the Capella Tower reaches to the top of the "halo" surrounding the screen walls (walls designed to hide cooling towers on the roof), so the height of the flat roof might be somewhat shorter, or that building might similarly be able to add to its height by including the additional structure. Presently, the IDS is considered to be 15 feet (4.6 m) taller than the former First Bank Tower.</p>
<p>It is also important to note that height measurements are sometimes incorrectly reported due to conversion from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._customary_units" title="U.S. customary units">U.S. customary units</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> and back again. The IDS was often reported as 774 feet (236 m) in height because of this problem, occasionally appearing to be two feet shorter than its competitor. It is the highest building in Minnesota currently since 1972.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_floors">Mechanical floors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanical floors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building has two <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_floor" title="Mechanical floor">mechanical floors</a> between the 8th and 9th floor and two mechanical floors between 51 and the roof level. They are known as 8A, 8B and 51A, 51B. They are not accessible from any of the building's passenger elevators and contain <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> equipment. As a result, the 9th floor is really the 11th floor and the 51st floor is really 53rd. This can be elucidated from the outside of the building or by walking down the stairwell from the 9th floor or higher. There is also a floor 2A which is inaccessible except from the freight elevators. There are also three floors beneath the IDS called P1, P2, and P3. These are storage levels for tenants and also are connected to the parking ramp below the IDS. Neither 2A, or P's 1, 2, and 3 are counted as actual floors. Half of the space of the complex (floors 9 to 57 of the Tower) is located above the 8th floor and half (including the retail, hotel, and floors 1 to 8 of the Tower and annex building) is located below the 8th floor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building was purchased by the John Buck Company in December 2004 for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>225 million. Just over a year later in January 2006, the company began looking for new buyers. In August 2006 it was sold to The Inland Real Estate Group of Companies, Inc., for approximately $277 million. Beacon Investment Properties of Hallandale Beach, FL purchased the building from Inland in April, 2013 for approximately $253 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The tower is also leased to smaller businesses.</p>
<p>The IDS has 1.4 million square feet (120,000 m²) of office and retail space.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting">Broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Communication spires on top of the building tower to 910 feet (280 m), the highest point in Minneapolis. A number of major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FM_band" title="FM band">FM</a> radio stations which formerly broadcast from the site now use the IDS as a backup in case their primary location in <a href="/wiki/Shoreview,_Minnesota" title="Shoreview, Minnesota">Shoreview, Minnesota</a> were to fail. One of the most notable broadcasters was <a href="/wiki/WLOL_(defunct)" title="WLOL (defunct)">99.5 WLOL</a> until the pop format signed off in late February 1991. In fact, one well-known reference was <i>"From the top-top-top of the IDS Center, 99 and 1/2 WLOL Minneapolis-Saint Paul!"</i> In 2009 the equipment was removed and digital towers were added for the national digital switch.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> broadcasters using the towers include <a href="/wiki/Univision" title="Univision">Univision</a> affiliate <a href="/wiki/WUMN-LP" title="WUMN-LP">WUMN-LP</a> and <a href="/wiki/KMBD-LD" title="KMBD-LD">KMBD-LD</a>, which broadcasts <a href="/wiki/Home_Shopping_Network" title="Home Shopping Network">HSN</a> programming, while the area's major television stations use them for their <a href="/wiki/Studio/transmitter_link" title="Studio/transmitter link">STL</a> towers and microwave relays to Shoreview and their studios and live trucks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deadly_falls">Deadly falls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IDS_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deadly falls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been three deaths as a result of falls from the IDS Tower, one by accident and two by suicide. In 2007, Fidel Danilo Sanchez-Flores, a worker removing snow from the IDS Center's Crystal Court roof, slipped and fell three stories through the glass canopied atrium to his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, a 30-year-old man jumped to his death from the 51st floor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> crashed through the Crystal Court, and landed by the fountain near Basil's restaurant. In 1996, a 32-year-old man knocked out a window in the 30th floor of the IDS Center and jumped to his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
