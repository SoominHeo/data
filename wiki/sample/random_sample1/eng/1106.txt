<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">IEEE 802.11g-2003</h1>

<p><b>IEEE 802.11g-2003</b> or <b>802.11g</b> is an amendment to the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">IEEE 802.11</a> specification that extended throughput to up to 54 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbit/s" title="Mbit/s">Mbit/s</a> using the same 2.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHz" title="GHz">GHz</a> band as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11b" title="802.11b">802.11b</a>. This specification under the marketing name of <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> has been implemented all over the world. The 802.11g protocol is now Clause 19 of the published <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11-2007#802.11-2007" title="IEEE 802.11-2007">IEEE 802.11-2007</a> standard, and Clause 19 of the published <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11#802.11-2012" title="IEEE 802.11">IEEE 802.11-2012</a> standard.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11" title="802.11">802.11</a> is a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> standards that govern wireless networking transmission methods. They are commonly used today in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11a" title="802.11a">802.11a</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11b" title="802.11b">802.11b</a>, 802.11g, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11n" title="802.11n">802.11n</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11ac" title="802.11ac">802.11ac</a> versions to provide wireless connectivity in the home, office and some commercial establishments.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions">Descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IEEE_802.11g-2003&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>802.11g is the third modulation standard for <a href="/wiki/Wireless_LAN" title="Wireless LAN">wireless LANs</a>. It works in the 2.4 GHz band (like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11b" title="802.11b">802.11b</a>) but operates at a maximum raw data rate of 54 Mbit/s. Using the CSMA/CA transmission scheme, 31.4 Mbit/s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theoretical_Maximum_Throughput_of_IEEE_802.11_and_its_Applications_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theoretical_Maximum_Throughput_of_IEEE_802.11_and_its_Applications-1">[1]</a></sup> is the maximum net <a href="/wiki/Throughput" title="Throughput">throughput</a> possible for packets of 1500 bytes in size and a 54 Mbit/s wireless rate (identical to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11a" title="802.11a">802.11a</a> core, except for some additional legacy overhead for backward compatibility). In practice, access points may not have an ideal implementation and may therefore not be able to achieve even 31.4 Mbit/s throughput with 1500 byte packets. 1500 bytes is the usual limit for packets on the Internet and therefore a relevant size to benchmark against. Smaller packets give even lower theoretical throughput, down to 3 Mbit/s using 54 Mbit/s rate and 64 byte packets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theoretical_Maximum_Throughput_of_IEEE_802.11_and_its_Applications_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Theoretical_Maximum_Throughput_of_IEEE_802.11_and_its_Applications-1">[1]</a></sup> Also, the available throughput is shared between all stations transmitting, including the AP so both downstream and upstream traffic is limited to a shared total of 31.4 Mbit/s using 1500 byte packets and 54 Mbit/s rate.</p>
<p>802.11g hardware is fully backwards compatible with 802.11b hardware. Details of making b and g work well together occupied much of the lingering technical process. In an 802.11g network, however, the presence of a legacy 802.11b participant will significantly reduce the speed of the overall 802.11g network. Some 802.11g routers employ a back-compatible mode for 802.11b clients called 54g LRS (Limited Rate Support).</p>
<p>The modulation scheme used in 802.11g is <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_frequency-division_multiplexing" title="Orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing">orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing</a> (OFDM) copied from 802.11a with data rates of 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 48, and 54 Mbit/s, and reverts to <a href="/wiki/Complementary_code_keying" title="Complementary code keying">CCK</a> (like the 802.11b standard) for 5.5 and 11 Mbit/s and <a href="/wiki/Phase-shift_keying" title="Phase-shift keying">DBPSK</a>/<a href="/wiki/Phase-shift_keying" title="Phase-shift keying">DQPSK</a>+<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSSS" title="DSSS">DSSS</a> for 1 and 2 Mbit/s. Even though 802.11g operates in the same frequency band as 802.11b, it can achieve higher data rates because of its heritage to 802.11a.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_description">Technical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IEEE_802.11g-2003&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of the 52 OFDM subcarriers, 48 are for data and 4 are <a href="/wiki/Pilot_signal" title="Pilot signal">pilot subcarriers</a> with a carrier separation of 0.3125 MHz (20 MHz/64). Each of these subcarriers can be a <a href="/wiki/Phase-shift_keying" title="Phase-shift keying">BPSK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase-shift_keying" title="Phase-shift keying">QPSK</a>, 16-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QAM" title="QAM">QAM</a> or 64-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QAM" title="QAM">QAM</a>. The total bandwidth is 20 MHz with an occupied bandwidth of 16.6 MHz. Symbol duration is 4 <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">microseconds</a>, which <i>includes</i> a guard interval of 0.8 microseconds. The actual generation and decoding of orthogonal components is done in baseband using DSP which is then upconverted to 2.4 GHz at the transmitter. Each of the subcarriers could be represented as a complex number. The time domain signal is generated by taking an Inverse <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">Fast Fourier transform</a> (IFFT). Correspondingly the receiver downconverts, samples at 20 MHz and does an FFT to retrieve the original coefficients. The advantages of using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OFDM" title="OFDM">OFDM</a> include reduced multipath effects in reception and increased spectral efficiency.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption">Adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IEEE_802.11g-2003&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The then-proposed 802.11g standard was rapidly adopted by consumers starting in January 2003, well before ratification, due to the desire for higher speeds and reductions in manufacturing costs. By summer 2003, most dual-band 802.11a/b products became dual-band/tri-mode, supporting a and b/g in a single mobile adapter card or access point.</p>
<p>Despite its major acceptance, 802.11g suffers from the same interference as 802.11b in the already crowded 2.4 GHz range. Devices operating in this range include microwave ovens, <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> devices, baby monitors and digital cordless telephones, which can lead to interference issues. Additionally, the success of the standard has caused usage/density problems related to crowding in urban areas. To prevent interference, there are only three non-overlapping usable channels in the U.S. and other countries with similar regulations (channels 1, 6, 11, with 25 MHz separation), and four in Europe (channels 1, 5, 9, 13, with only 20 MHz separation). Even with such separation, some interference due to <a href="/wiki/Side_lobe" title="Side lobe">side lobes</a> exists, though it is considerably weaker.</p>
