<p>The <b>.380 ACP</b> (9x17mm) (<a href="/wiki/Automatic_Colt_Pistol" title="Automatic Colt Pistol">Automatic Colt Pistol</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Rim_(firearms)" title="Rim (firearms)">rimless</a>, straight-walled <a href="/wiki/Pistol" title="Pistol">pistol</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartridge_(weaponry)" title="Cartridge (weaponry)">cartridge</a> developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearms" title="Firearms">firearms</a> designer <a href="/wiki/John_Browning" title="John Browning">John Moses Browning</a>. The cartridge <a href="/wiki/Headspace_(firearms)" title="Headspace (firearms)">headspaces</a> on the mouth of the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-2">[2]</a></sup> It was introduced in 1908 by <a href="/wiki/Colt%27s_Manufacturing_Company" title="Colt's Manufacturing Company">Colt</a>, for use in its new Colt Model 1908 pocket hammerless <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_pistol" title="Semi-automatic pistol">semi-automatic</a>, and has been a popular <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a> cartridge ever since, seeing wide use in numerous handguns (typically smaller weapons). Other names for .380 ACP include <b>.380 Auto</b>, <b>9mm Browning</b>, <b>9mm Corto</b>, <b>9mm Kurz</b>, <b>9mm Short</b>, <b>9×17mm</b> and <b>9 mm Browning Court</b> (which is the <a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_Permanente_pour_l%27Epreuve_des_Armes_%C3%A0_Feu_Portatives" title="Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives">C.I.P.</a> designation). It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/.38_ACP" title=".38 ACP">.38 ACP</a>, <a href="/wiki/9%C3%9718mm_Ultra" title="9×18mm Ultra">9mm Ultra</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9mm_Makarov" title="9mm Makarov">9mm Makarov</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9mm_Parabellum" title="9mm Parabellum">9mm Parabellum</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.380_ACP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .380 ACP cartridge was derived from Browning's earlier <a href="/wiki/9mm_Browning_Long" title="9mm Browning Long">9×20mm semi-rimmed</a> design, which was only marginally more powerful. The .380 ACP was designed to be truly <a href="/wiki/Rim_(firearms)#Rimless" title="Rim (firearms)">rimless</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Headspace_(firearms)" title="Headspace (firearms)">headspace</a> on the case mouth instead of the rim for better accuracy. These relatively low-powered designs were intended for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blowback_(arms)" title="Blowback (arms)">blowback</a> pistols which lacked a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrel_(firearms)" title="Barrel (firearms)">barrel</a> <a href="/wiki/Recoil_operation" title="Recoil operation">locking mechanism</a>, which is often required for any handgun firing a round more powerful than a .380. Using blowback operation, the design can be simplified, and lowered in cost; a locking mechanism is unnecessary since the mass of the slide and strength of the recoil spring are enough to absorb the recoil energy of the round, due to the round's relatively low <a href="/wiki/Bolt_thrust" title="Bolt thrust">bolt thrust</a>. Blowback operation also permits the barrel to be permanently fixed to the frame, which promotes accuracy, unlike a traditional <a href="/wiki/Recoil_operation" title="Recoil operation">short recoil-operation</a> pistol, which requires a "tilting" barrel to unlock the slide and barrel assembly when cycling. A drawback of the blowback system is that it requires a certain amount of slide <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> (weight) to counter the recoil of the round used. The higher the power the round, the heavier the slide assembly has to be in order for its inertia to safely absorb the recoil, meaning that a typical blowback pistol in a given caliber will be heavier than an equivalent recoil-operated weapon (alternatively, a very stiff spring will work, but will make operating the slide very difficult). Blowback weapons can be made in calibers larger than .380 ACP, but the required weight of slide and strength of spring makes this an unattractive option. Although the low power of the .380 ACP does not require a locking mechanism, there have also been a number of locked-breech pistols chambered in .380 ACP, such as the <a href="/wiki/Remington_Model_51" title="Remington Model 51">Remington Model 51</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kel-Tec_P-3AT" title="Kel-Tec P-3AT">Kel-Tec P3AT</a> and also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glock_42" title="Glock 42">Glock 42</a> (all three were designed to be lighter than blowback operated .380 ACP weapons). There have also been some relatively diminutive (blowback-operated) <a href="/wiki/Submachine_gun" title="Submachine gun">submachine guns</a>, such as the Ingram <a href="/wiki/MAC-11" title="MAC-11">MAC-11</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iannamico_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iannamico-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%A0korpion_vz._61#Variants" title="Škorpion vz. 61">Czech vz. 83</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Users">Users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.380_ACP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .380 ACP has experienced very widespread use in the years since its introduction (1908 USA, 1912 Europe). In 1914, it was used by <a href="/wiki/Gavrilo_Princip" title="Gavrilo Princip">Gavrilo Princip</a> to assassinate <a href="/wiki/Archduke_Franz_Ferdinand_of_Austria" title="Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria">Archduke Franz Ferdinand</a> and his wife Sophia, the event which is credited with starting the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>. It was later adopted by the armies of at least five European nations as their standard pistol cartridge before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>; Czechoslovakia <a href="/wiki/%C4%8CZ_vz._38" title="ČZ vz. 38">(Vz.38)</a>, Hungary <a href="/wiki/F%C3%89G_37M_Pistol" title="FÉG 37M Pistol">(FEMARU 37M)</a>, and Italy, all of whom used domestic designs, as well as The Netherlands and Yugoslavia, both of whom adopted the <a href="/wiki/FN_Model_1910" title="FN Model 1910">FN Model 1922</a>. It was also used extensively by Germany, who captured or purchased hundreds of thousands of pistols in this caliber during World War II. Popular German built commercial models, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walther_PPK" title="Walther PPK">Walther PPK</a> were very popular with German officers. The Italian Army used the <a href="/wiki/Beretta_M1934" title="Beretta M1934">Beretta M1934</a>, but the Italian Air Force and Navy stuck with the 7.65mm/.32 ACP when they adopted the <a href="/wiki/Beretta_M1935" title="Beretta M1935">Beretta M1935</a>.</p>
<p>While .380 ACP was considered to be a moderately powerful service pistol round before World War II when compared to the .32 ACP pistols it replaced, no nation retained it as a military service cartridge for very long after the war (when it was largely replaced by its more powerful <a href="/wiki/9%C3%9719mm_Parabellum" title="9×19mm Parabellum">9×19mm Parabellum</a> cousin). It was widely used by police forces in Europe until at least the 1980s when more powerful 9×19mm handguns began to replace it in this market as well. It does find some use as a backup gun due to the generally small and easily concealable size of the weapons that chambered it (very few "mini pistols" are made in calibers larger than .380 ACP, and those few that are recent developments), and is popular on the civilian market as a personal defense round. The .380 ACP round is marginally suitable for self-defense situations as a choice for concealed carry pistols. It was the round used in <a href="/wiki/Defense_Distributed" title="Defense Distributed">Defense Distributed</a>'s "Wiki Weapon" project to <a href="/wiki/Liberator_(gun)" title="Liberator (gun)">successfully 3D print a firearm</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.380_ACP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The .380 ACP is compact and light, but has a relatively short range and less <a href="/wiki/Stopping_power" title="Stopping power">stopping power</a> than other modern pistol cartridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to gun author <a href="/wiki/Massad_Ayoob" title="Massad Ayoob">Massad Ayoob</a>, "Some experts will say it's barely adequate, and others will say it's barely inadequate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Even so, it remains a popular self-defense cartridge for shooters who want a lightweight pistol with manageable recoil and/or smaller pistol. It is slightly less powerful than a standard-pressure <a href="/wiki/.38_Special" title=".38 Special">.38 Special</a> and uses 9 mm (.355 in) diameter bullets. The heaviest bullet that can be safely loaded into the .380 ACP is 115 grains (7.5 g),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> though the standard has long been 85, 90 or 95 grains (5.5, 5.8 or 6.2 g).</p>
<p>The wounding potential of bullets is often characterized in terms of a bullet's expanded diameter, penetration depth, and energy. Bullet energy for .380 ACP loads varies from roughly 190 to 220 foot-pounds force (260 to 300 J). The table below shows common performance parameters for several .380 ACP loads. Bullet weights ranging from 85 to 95 grains (5.5 to 6.2 g) are common. Penetration depths from 6.5 to 17 inches (16.5 to 43.2 cm) are available for various applications and risk assessments.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_firearms_in_.380_ACP">Popular firearms in .380 ACP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.380_ACP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popular firearms in .380 ACP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .380 ACP increased in popularity in the United States because of the spread of legal <a href="/wiki/Concealed_carry" title="Concealed carry">concealed carry</a>, as have the compact and inexpensive pistols that make use of it. Popular pistols chambered in .380 ACP include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Astra-Unceta_y_Cia_SA" title="Astra-Unceta y Cia SA">Astra</a> 300, 3000, 4000 and Constable.</li>

<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Browning_1911-380&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Browning 1911-380 (page does not exist)">Browning 1911-380</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bersa_Thunder_380" title="Bersa Thunder 380">Bersa Thunder 380</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Colt_Mustang" title="Colt Mustang">Colt Mustang</a> pocketlite.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CZ_83" title="CZ 83">CZ 83</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glock#.380_ACP" title="Glock">Glock G42 and G25</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hi-Point" title="Hi-Point">Hi-Point</a> CF380COMP</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kahr_P380" title="Kahr P380">Kahr P380</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kel-Tec_P-3AT" title="Kel-Tec P-3AT">Kel-Tec P-3AT</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kevin_ZP98" title="Kevin ZP98">Kevin ZP98 (Micro Desert Eagle)</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Llama_Firearms" title="Llama Firearms">Llama Firearms</a> Micromax.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Remington_Model_51" title="Remington Model 51">Remington Model 51</a> and RM380<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ruger_LCP" title="Ruger LCP">Ruger LCP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruger_LC380" title="Ruger LC380">Ruger LC380</a></li>
