<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jesus, King of the Jews</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>

For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/INRI_(disambiguation)" title="INRI (disambiguation)">INRI (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Thorns" title="Crown of Thorns">crowned with thorns</a> in a purple robe as the <i>King of the Jews</i>, being <a href="/wiki/Mocking_of_Jesus" title="Mocking of Jesus">mocked</a> and beaten during his Passion, depicted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirk_van_Baburen" title="Dirk van Baburen">van Baburen</a>, 1623.</div>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, <b>Jesus</b> is referred to as the <b>King of the Jews</b>, both at the beginning of his life and at the end. In the <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> of the New Testament, e.g. in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:3" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:3</a>, this is written <i><a href="/wiki/Basileus" title="Basileus">Basileus</a> ton <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ioudaion" title="Ioudaion">Ioudaion</a></i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">βασιλεὺς τῶν Ἰουδαίων</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both uses of the title lead to dramatic results in the New Testament accounts. In the account of the <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus" title="Nativity of Jesus">Nativity of Jesus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a>, the wise men (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">Magi</a>) who come from the east call Jesus the "King of the Judeans", causing <a href="/wiki/Herod_the_Great" title="Herod the Great">King Herod</a> to order the <a href="/wiki/Massacre_of_the_Innocents" title="Massacre of the Innocents">Massacre of the Innocents</a>. Towards the end of the accounts of all four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_Gospel" title="Canonical Gospel">Canonical Gospels</a>, in the narrative of the <a href="/wiki/Passion_of_Jesus" title="Passion of Jesus">Passion of Jesus</a>, the use of the "King of the Judeans" title leads to charges against Jesus that result in <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">his Crucifixion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-France2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initialism <b>INRI</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Iēsūs/Iēsus Nazarēnus, Rēx Iūdaeōrum</i></span>) represents the Latin inscription which in English reads as "Jesus the Nazarene, King of the Jews" and <a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John%2019:20" rel="nofollow">John 19:20</a> states that this was written in three languages — Aramaic, Latin, and Greek — during the crucifixion of Jesus. The Greek version of the initialism read <b>ΙΝΒΙ</b>, representing <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἰησοῦς ὁ Ναζωραῖος ὁ Bασιλεὺς τῶν Ἰουδαίων</span> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andreopoulos_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andreopoulos-4">[4]</a></sup> which is best translated, "Jesus the Nazorean, King of the Judeans."</p>
<p>In the New Testament, the "King of the Jews” title is used only by the <a href="/wiki/Gentile" title="Gentile">gentiles</a>, namely by the Magi, <a href="/wiki/Pontius_Pilate" title="Pontius Pilate">Pontius Pilate</a>, and the Roman soldiers. In contrast, the Jewish leaders use the designation "King of Israel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-France2-2">[2]</a></sup> The phrase has also been translated <b>King of the Judeans</b> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ioudaioi" title="Ioudaioi">Ioudaioi</a>).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_the_Nativity">Use in the Nativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use in the Nativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the account of the Nativity of Jesus in the Gospel of Matthew the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">Biblical Magi</a> go to see King Herod the Great in Jerusalem and in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#2:2" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 2:2</a> ask him: "Where is He that is born King of the Judeans?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-France-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The question troubles Herod who considers the title his own, and in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#2:7" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 2:7-8</a> he attempts to trick the Magi to reveal the exact location of the newborn King of the Jews. However, once the Magi depart without informing Herod of the location of Jesus, Herod orders the execution of all young male children in the village of Bethlehem, in the episode known as the Massacre of the Innocents (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#2:16" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 2:16</a>) to avoid the loss of his throne to the newborn King of the Jews whose birth had been announced to him by the Magi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-France-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_the_Passion">Use in the Passion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in the Passion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the accounts of the <a href="/wiki/Passion_of_Jesus" title="Passion of Jesus">Passion of Jesus</a>, the title King of the Judeans is used on three separate occasions. In the first such episode, all four Gospels state that the title was used for Jesus when he was interviewed by Pilate and that his Crucifixion was based on that charge, as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#27:11" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 27:11</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Mark#15:2" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Mark">Mark 15:2</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Luke#23:3" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Luke">Luke 23:3</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#18:33" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 18:33</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg/220px-Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg/330px-Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg/440px-Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ellwangen_St_Vitus_Vorhalle_Kreuzaltar_detail2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The acronym INRI (Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews) written in three languages (as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:20" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:20</a>) on the cross, <a href="/wiki/Ellwangen_Abbey" title="Ellwangen Abbey">Ellwangen Abbey</a>, Germany.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The use of the terms King and Kingdom and the role of the Jews in using the term King to accuse Jesus are central in the discussion between Jesus and Pilate. In <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#18:34" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 18:34</a>, Jesus hints that the King accusation did not originate with Pilate but with "others" and, in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#18:36" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 18:36</a>, he states: "My kingdom is not of this world". However, Jesus does not directly deny being the King of the Jews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the New Testament, Pilate writes "Jesus the Nazarene, King of the Judeans" as a sign to be affixed to the cross of Jesus. <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:21" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:21</a> states that the Jews told Pilate: "Do not write King of the Jews" but instead write that Jesus had merely claimed that title, but Pilate wrote it anyway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Pilate's response to the protest is recorded by John: <a href="/wiki/Quod_scripsi,_scripsi" title="Quod scripsi, scripsi">"What I have written, I have written."</a></p>
<p>After the trial by Pilate and after the <a href="/wiki/Flagellation_of_Christ" title="Flagellation of Christ">Flagellation of Christ</a> episode, the soldiers <a href="/wiki/Mocking_of_Jesus" title="Mocking of Jesus">mock</a> Jesus as the King of Jews by putting a purple robe (that signifies royal status) on him, place a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Thorns" title="Crown of Thorns">Crown of Thorns</a> on his head, and beat and mistreat him in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#27:29" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 27:29-30</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Mark#15:17" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Mark">Mark 15:17-19</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:2" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:2-3</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Senior_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Senior-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The continued reliance on the use of the term King by the Judeans to press charges against Jesus is a key element of the final decision to crucify him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:12" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:12</a> Pilate seeks to release Jesus, but the Jews object, saying: "If thou release this man, thou art not Caesar's friend: every one that maketh himself a king speaketh against Caesar", bringing the power of Caesar to the forefront of the discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:12" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:12</a>, the Jews then cry out: "Crucify him! ... We have no king but Caesar."</p>
<p>The use of the term "King of the Judeans" by the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early Church</a> after the death of Jesus was thus not without risk, for this term could have opened them to prosecution as <i>followers of Jesus</i>, who was accused of possible rebellion against Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The final use of the title only appears in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Luke#23:36" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Luke">Luke 23:36-37</a>. Here, after Jesus has carried the cross to <a href="/wiki/Calvary" title="Calvary">Calvary</a> and has been nailed to the cross, the soldiers look up on him on the cross, mock him, offer him <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> and say: "If thou art the King of the Jews, save thyself." In the parallel account in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#27:42" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 27:42</a>, the Jewish priests mock Jesus as "King of Israel", saying: "He is the King of Israel; let him now come down from the cross, and we will believe on him."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="King_of_the_Jews_vs_King_of_Israel">King of the Jews vs King of Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: King of the Jews vs King of Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the New Testament, the "King of the Jews” title is used only by the gentiles, namely by the Magi, Pontius Pilate, Roman soldiers. In contrast the Jewish leaders prefer the designation "King of Israel", as in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Matthew#27:42" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Matthew">Matthew 27:42</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Mark#15:32" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Mark">Mark 15:32</a>. But from Pilate's perspective, it is the term "King" (regardless of Jews or Israel) which is sensitive, for it implies possible rebellion against the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-France2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Gospel of Mark</a> the distinction between King of the Jews and King of Israel is made consciously, setting apart the two uses of the term by the Jews and the gentiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_INRI_and_.CE.99.CE.9D.CE.92.CE.99_acronyms">The INRI and ΙΝΒΙ acronyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The INRI and ΙΝΒΙ acronyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2996" data-file-width="2157" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg/180px-Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg/270px-Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg/360px-Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agias_Triados_frescos_cross.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Crucifix" title="Crucifix">crucifix</a>, displays the lettering in Greek: ΙΝΒΙ (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trapeza" title="Trapeza">Trapeza</a> of Holy Trinity Monastery, <a href="/wiki/Meteora" title="Meteora">Meteora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The acronym INRI represents the Latin inscription IESVS·NAZARENVS·REX·IVDÆORVM (Iesus Nazarenus, Rex Iudaeorum), in English reads as "Jesus the Nazarene, King of the Jews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/John#19:20" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/John">John 19:20</a> states that this was written in three languages: Hebrew, Latin and Greek and was put on the cross of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andreopoulos_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Andreopoulos-4">[4]</a></sup> The Greek version reads ΙΝΒΙ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andreopoulos_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Andreopoulos-4">[4]</a></sup> In Hebrew it read: Y-shua HaNotzri Vemelch HaY'hudim an acronym of the Hebrew God YHVH, meaning Y'shua was YHVH (On earth only) as he claimed during his lifetime.</p>
<p>Devotional enthusiasm greeted the discovery by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Gonz%C3%A1lez_de_Mendoza" title="Pedro González de Mendoza">Cardinal Pedro González de Mendoza</a> in 1492 of what was acclaimed as the <a href="/wiki/Titulus_Crucis" title="Titulus Crucis">actual tablet</a>, said to have been brought to Rome by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_(Empress)" title="Helena (Empress)">Saint Helena</a>, mother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Constantine" title="Emperor Constantine">Emperor Constantine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Christianity">Western Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>, most crucifixes and many depictions of the crucifixion of Jesus include a plaque or parchment placed above his head, called a <a href="/wiki/Titulus_(inscription)" title="Titulus (inscription)">titulus</a>, or title, bearing only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_letters" title="Latin letters">Latin letters</a> <b>INRI</b>, occasionally carved directly into the cross and usually just above the head of Jesus. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Church" title="Eastern Church">Eastern Church</a> "King of Glory" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">τῆς Δόξης</span>, <i>tês Dóxēs</i>) may be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andreopoulos_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Andreopoulos-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Christianity">Eastern Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jesus,_King_of_the_Jews&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic" title="Eastern Catholic">Eastern Catholic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particular_churches" title="Particular churches">particular churches</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_iuris" title="Sui iuris">sui iuris</a></i> use the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek letters</a> <b>ΙΝΒΙ</b>, based on the Greek version of the inscription <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἰησοῦς ὁ Ναζωραῖος ὁ βασιλεὺς τῶν Ἰουδαίων</span> (<i>Iēsûs ho Nazōraêos ho basileùs tôn Iudaéōn</i>). Some representations change the title to "ΙΝΒΚ," <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὁ Bασιλεὺς τοῦ κόσμου</span> (<i>ho Basileùs tû kósmu</i>, "The King of the World"), or to <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὁ Bασιλεὺς τῆς Δόξης</span> (<i>ho Basileùs tês Dóxēs</i>, "The King of Glory"), not implying that this was really what was written but reflecting the tradition that <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icons</a> depict the spiritual reality rather than the physical reality. Some other Eastern Orthodox churches (such as the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox_Church" title="Romanian Orthodox Church">Romanian Orthodox Church</a>) use the Latin version, INRI, which is the same to the abbreviation of the Romanian equivalent. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> uses ІНЦІ (<i>INCI</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Slavonic" title="Church Slavonic">Church Slavonic</a> equivalent of ΙΝΒΙ) or the abbreviation Царь Славы (<i>Carĭ Slavy</i>, "King of Glory").</p>
