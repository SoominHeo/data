<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">IS–LM model</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>IS–LM model</b>, or <b>Hicks–Hansen model</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> tool that shows the relationship between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interest_rates" title="Interest rates">interest rates</a> (ordinate) and assets market (also known as real output in goods and services market plus money market, as <a href="/wiki/Abscissa" title="Abscissa">abscissa</a>). The intersection of the "<a href="/wiki/Investment_(macroeconomics)" title="Investment (macroeconomics)">investment</a>–<a href="/wiki/Saving" title="Saving">saving</a>" (IS) and "<a href="/wiki/Liquidity_preference" title="Liquidity preference">liquidity preference</a>–<a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a>" (LM) curves models "general equilibrium" where supposed simultaneous equilibrium occurs in both interest and assets markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Yet two equivalent interpretations are possible: first, the IS–LM model explains changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_income" title="National income">national income</a> when price level is fixed short-run; second, the IS–LM model shows why an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand_curve" title="Aggregate demand curve">aggregate demand curve</a> can shift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Hence, this tool is sometimes used not only to analyse economic fluctuations but also to suggest potential levels for appropriate stabilisation policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The model was developed by <a href="/wiki/John_Hicks" title="John Hicks">John Hicks</a> in 1937,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hicks1937_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hicks1937-4">[4]</a></sup> and later extended by <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Hansen" title="Alvin Hansen">Alvin Hansen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as a mathematical representation of <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian macroeconomic theory</a>. Between the 1940s and mid-1970s, it was the leading framework of macroeconomic analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While it has been largely absent from macroeconomic research ever since, it is still a backbone conceptual introductory tool in many macroeconomics textbooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IS–LM model was born at a conference of the <a href="/wiki/Econometric_Society" title="Econometric Society">Econometric Society</a> held in Oxford during September 1936. <a href="/wiki/Roy_Harrod" title="Roy Harrod">Roy Harrod</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hicks" title="John Hicks">John R. Hicks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_Meade" title="James Meade">James Meade</a> all presented papers describing <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical models</a> attempting to summarize <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest,_and_Money" title="General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money">General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hicks1937_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hicks1937-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Hicks, who had seen a draft of Harrod's paper, invented the IS–LM model (originally using the <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbreviation</a> "LL", not "LM"). He later presented it in "Mr. Keynes and the Classics: A Suggested Interpretation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hicks1937_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hicks1937-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hicks later agreed that the model missed important points of Keynesian theory, criticizing it as having very limited use beyond "a classroom gadget", and criticizing equilibrium methods generally: "When one turns to questions of policy, looking towards the future instead of the past, the use of equilibrium methods is still more suspect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h1980_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-h1980-9">[9]</a></sup> The first problem was that it presents the real and monetary sectors as separate, something Keynes attempted to transcend. In addition, an equilibrium model ignores uncertainty—and that <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_preference" title="Liquidity preference">liquidity preference</a> only makes sense in the presence of uncertainty "For there is no sense in liquidity, unless expectations are uncertain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h1980_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-h1980-9">[9]</a></sup> A shift in one of the IS or LM curves will cause a change in expectations, which shifts the other curve. Most modern macroeconomists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (September 2014)">weasel words</span></a></i>]</sup> see the IS–LM model as being—at best—a starting approximation for understanding the real world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although generally accepted as being imperfect, the model is seen as a useful pedagogical tool for imparting an understanding of the questions that macroeconomists today attempt to answer through more nuanced approaches. As such, it is included in most undergraduate macroeconomics textbooks, but omitted from most graduate texts due to the current dominance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_business_cycle" title="Real business cycle">real business cycle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Keynesian" title="New Keynesian">new Keynesian</a> theories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The model is presented as a graph of two intersecting lines in the first quadrant.</p>
<p>The horizontal axis represents <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_income" title="National income">national income</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_vs._nominal_in_economics" title="Real vs. nominal in economics">real</a> <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> and is labelled <i>Y.</i> The vertical axis represents the <a href="/wiki/Real_interest_rate" title="Real interest rate">real interest rate</a>, <i>r.</i> Since this is a non-dynamic model, there is a fixed relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Nominal_interest_rate" title="Nominal interest rate">nominal interest rate</a> and the real interest rate (the former equals the latter plus the expected inflation rate which is <a href="/wiki/Exogeny" title="Exogeny">exogenous</a> in the short run); therefore variables such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Money_demand" title="Money demand">money demand</a> which actually depend on the nominal interest rate can equivalently be expressed as depending on the real interest rate.</p>
<p>The point where these schedules intersect represents a short-run <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_equilibrium" title="General equilibrium">equilibrium</a> in the real and monetary sectors (though not necessarily in other sectors, such as labor markets): both the product market and the money market are in equilibrium. This equilibrium yields a unique combination of the interest rate and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_GDP" title="Real GDP">real GDP</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IS_curve">IS curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: IS curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the investment-saving curve, the independent variable is the interest rate and the dependent variable is the level of income. (Note that economics graphs like this one typically place the independent variable—interest rate,in this example—on the vertical axis rather than the horizontal axis.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The IS curve is drawn as downward-<a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">sloping</a> with the interest rate (i) on the vertical axis and GDP (gross domestic product: Y) on the horizontal axis. The initials IS stand for "Investment and Saving equilibrium" but since 1937 have been used to represent the <a href="/wiki/Locus_(mathematics)" title="Locus (mathematics)">locus</a> of all equilibria where total spending (consumer spending + planned private investment + government purchases + net exports) equals an economy's total output (equivalent to real income, Y, or GDP). To keep the link with the historical meaning, the IS curve can be said to represent the equilibria where total private investment equals total saving, where the latter equals consumer saving <i>plus</i> government saving (the budget surplus) <i>plus</i> foreign saving (the trade surplus). In equilibrium, all spending is desired or planned; there is no unplanned inventory accumulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The level of real GDP (Y) is determined along this line for each <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rate</a>.</p>
<p>Thus the IS curve is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locus_of_points" title="Locus of points">locus of points</a> of equilibrium in the "real" (non-financial) economy. Each point on the curve represents the equilibrium between the Savings and Investment (S=I).</p>
<p>Given expectations about returns on fixed investment, every level of the real interest rate (i) will generate a certain level of planned fixed <a href="/wiki/Investment_(macroeconomics)" title="Investment (macroeconomics)">investment</a> and other interest-sensitive spending: lower interest rates encourage higher fixed investment and the like. Income is at the equilibrium level for a given interest rate when the saving that <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> and other economic participants choose to do out of this income equals investment (or, equivalently, when "leakages" from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_flow" title="Circular flow">circular flow</a> equal "injections"). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplier_effect" title="Multiplier effect">multiplier effect</a> of an increase in fixed investment resulting from a lower interest rate raises real GDP. This explains the downward slope of the IS curve. In summary, this line represents the causation from falling interest rates to rising planned fixed investment (etc.) to rising national income and output.</p>

<p>In this model, the level of C (consumption), G (government spending), EX (exports), IM (imports), and R<sub>t</sub> (real interest rate) are considered to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exogenous" title="Exogenous">exogenous</a>, meaning that they are taken as a given, because they are determined by factors outside of this model. Their equations are as follows: C<sub>t</sub> = ā<sub>t</sub> Ȳ<sub>t</sub> , G<sub>t</sub> = ā<sub>g</sub> Ỹ<sub>t</sub> , EX<sub>t</sub> = ā<sub>ex</sub> Ȳ<sub>t</sub> , and IM<sub>t</sub> = ā<sub>im</sub> Ȳ<sub>t</sub>. I (investment) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogenous_(economics)" title="Endogenous (economics)">endogenous</a>, meaning that it is explained by our model, and has the equation I<sub>t</sub> = (ā<sub>i</sub> Ȳ<sub>t</sub>) − b (R<sub>t</sub> − ṝ) Ȳ<sub>t</sub> , where ṝ is the marginal product of capital, R<sub>t</sub> is the real interest rate, ā<sub>i</sub> is the share of potential output that goes toward investment, and b is a constant. A few equations are necessary in order to derive the IS curve: ā = ā<sub>c</sub> + ā<sub>i</sub> + ā<sub>g</sub> + ā<sub>ex</sub> - ā<sub>im</sub> - 1, and Ỹ<sub>t</sub> = (Y<sub>t</sub> - Ȳ<sub>t</sub>) / Ȳ<sub>t</sub> = (Y<sub>t</sub> / Ȳ<sub>t</sub>) - (Ȳ<sub>t</sub> / Ȳ<sub>t</sub>) = (Y<sub>t</sub> / Ȳ<sub>t</sub>) - 1. When you plug in all the equations into the original Y<sub>t</sub> = C<sub>t</sub> + I<sub>t</sub> + G<sub>t</sub> + EX<sub>t</sub> - IM<sub>t</sub> and solve, you will get the short run output, which is Ỹ<sub>t</sub> = ā - b (R<sub>t</sub> - ṝ).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (August 2014)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LM_curve">LM curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: LM curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Money_Market_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Money_Market_diagram.svg/270px-Money_Market_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Money_Market_diagram.svg/405px-Money_Market_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Money_Market_diagram.svg/540px-Money_Market_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="270"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Money_Market_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The money market equilibrium diagram.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For the liquidity preference and money supply curve, the independent variable is "income" and the dependent variable is "the interest rate." The LM curve shows the combinations of interest rates and levels of real income for which the money market is in equilibrium. It is an upward-sloping curve representing the role of finance and money.</p>
<p>The LM function is the set of equilibrium points between the <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_preference" title="Liquidity preference">liquidity preference</a> (or demand for money) function and the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> function (as determined by <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central banks</a>).</p>
<p>Each point on the LM curve reflects a particular equilibrium situation in the money market equilibrium diagram, based on a particular level of income. In the money market equilibrium diagram, the liquidity preference function is simply the willingness to hold cash balances instead of <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a>. For this function, the nominal interest rate (on the vertical axis) is plotted against the quantity of cash balances (or liquidity), on the horizontal. The liquidity preference function is downward sloping. Two basic elements determine the quantity of cash balances demanded (liquidity preference) and therefore the position and slope of the function:</p>
<ul>
<li>1) <a href="/wiki/Transactions_demand" title="Transactions demand">Transactions demand</a> for money: this includes both (a) the willingness to hold cash for everyday transactions and (b) a precautionary measure (money demand in case of emergencies). Transactions demand is positively related to real GDP (represented by Y, and also referred to as income). This is simply explained – as GDP increases, so does spending and therefore transactions. As GDP is considered exogenous to the liquidity preference function, changes in GDP shift the curve. For example, an increase in GDP will increase transactions which will increase the demand for money for given interest rates, and cause the Liquidity preference curve to shift to the right.</li>
<li>2) <a href="/wiki/Speculative_demand" title="Speculative demand">Speculative demand</a> for money: this is the willingness to hold cash instead of securities as an asset for investment purposes. Speculative demand is inversely related to the interest rate. As the interest rate rises, the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a> of holding cash increases – the incentive will be to move into securities.</li>
</ul>
<p>The money supply function for this situation is plotted on the same graph as the liquidity preference function. The money supply is determined by the central bank decisions and willingness of commercial banks to loan money. Though the money supply is related indirectly to interest rates in the very short run, the money supply in effect is perfectly <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(economics)" title="Elasticity (economics)">inelastic</a> with respect to nominal interest rates (assuming the central bank chooses to control the money supply rather than focusing directly on the interest rate). Thus the money supply function is represented as a vertical line – money supply is a constant, independent of the interest rate, GDP, and other factors. Mathematically, the LM curve is defined by the equation <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="M/P=L(i,Y)" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aea2fd98859f734e019d04089bb03edfde89369f" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:15.555ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, where the supply of money is represented as the <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">real</a> amount M/P (as opposed to the nominal amount M), with P representing the <a href="/wiki/Price_level" title="Price level">price level</a>, and L being the real demand for money, which is some function of the interest rate i and the level Y of real income. The LM curve shows the combinations of interest rates and levels of real income for which money supply equals money demand—that is, for which the money market is in equilibrium.</p>
<p>For a given level of income, the intersection point between the liquidity preference and money supply functions implies a single point on the LM curve: specifically, the point giving the level of the interest rate which equilibrate the money market at the given level of income. Recalling that for the LM curve, the interest rate is plotted against real GDP (whereas the liquidity preference and money supply functions plot interest rates against the quantity of cash balances), an increase in GDP shifts the liquidity preference function rightward and hence increases the interest rate. Thus the LM function is positively sloped.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shifts">Shifts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Shifts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One hypothesis is that a government's <a href="/wiki/Deficit_spending" title="Deficit spending">deficit spending</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Fiscal_policy" title="Fiscal policy">fiscal policy</a>") has an effect similar to that of a lower saving rate or increased private fixed investment, increasing the amount of demand for goods at each individual interest rate. An increased deficit by the national government shifts the IS curve to the right. This raises the equilibrium interest rate (from i<sub>1</sub> to i<sub>2</sub>) and national income (from Y<sub>1</sub> to Y<sub>2</sub>), as shown in the graph above. The equilibrium level of national income in the IS-LM diagram is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a>.</p>
<p>Keynesians argue<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> spending may actually "crowd in" (encourage) private fixed investment via the <a href="/wiki/Accelerator_effect" title="Accelerator effect">accelerator effect</a>, which helps long-term growth. Further, if government deficits are spent on productive public investment (e.g., infrastructure or public health) that spending directly and eventually raises potential output, although not necessarily more (or less) than the lost private investment might have. The extent of any crowding out depends on the shape of the LM curve. A shift in the IS curve along a relatively flat LM curve can increase output substantially with little change in the interest rate. On the other hand, an rightward shift in the IS curve along a vertical LM curve will lead to higher interest rates, but no change in output (this case represents the "<a href="/wiki/Treasury_view" title="Treasury view">treasury view</a>").</p>
<p>Rightward shifts of the IS curve also result from <a href="/wiki/Exogeny" title="Exogeny">exogenous</a> increases in investment spending<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (i.e., for reasons other than interest rates or income), in consumer spending, and in export spending by people outside the economy being modelled, as well as by exogenous decreases in spending on imports. Thus these too raise both equilibrium income and the equilibrium interest rate. Of course, changes in these variables in the opposite direction shift the IS curve in the opposite direction.</p>
<p>The IS–LM model also allows for the role of <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a>. If the money supply is increased, that shifts the LM curve downward or to the right, lowering interest rates and raising equilibrium national income. Further, exogenous decreases in liquidity preference, perhaps due to improved transactions technologies, lead to downward shifts of the LM curve and thus increases in income and decreases in interest rates. Changes in these variables in the opposite direction shift the LM curve in the opposite direction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incorporation_into_larger_models">Incorporation into larger models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IS%E2%80%93LM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Incorporation into larger models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By itself, the IS–LM model is used to study the short run when <a href="/wiki/Price_level" title="Price level">prices</a> are fixed or sticky and no <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> is taken into consideration. But in practice the main role of the model is as a sub-model of larger models (especially the Aggregate Demand-Aggregate Supply model – the <a href="/wiki/AD%E2%80%93AS_model" title="AD–AS model">AD–AS model</a>) which allow for a flexible <a href="/wiki/Price_level" title="Price level">price level</a>. In the aggregate demand-aggregate supply model, each point on the aggregate demand curve is an outcome of the IS–LM model for aggregate demand Y based on a particular price level. Starting from one point on the aggregate demand curve, at a particular price level and a quantity of aggregate demand implied by the IS–LM model for that price level, if one considers a higher potential price level, in the IS–LM model the real money supply M/P will be lower and hence the LM curve will be shifted higher, leading to lower aggregate demand; hence at the higher price level the level of aggregate demand is lower, so the aggregate demand curve is negatively sloped.</p>
