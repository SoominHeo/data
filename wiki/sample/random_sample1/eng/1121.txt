<p><b>Industry Standard Architecture</b> (<b>ISA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a> term for the <a href="/wiki/16-bit" title="16-bit">16-bit</a> internal <a href="/wiki/Bus_(computing)" title="Bus (computing)">bus</a> of <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer/AT" title="IBM Personal Computer/AT">IBM PC/AT</a> and similar computers based on the <a href="/wiki/Intel_80286" title="Intel 80286">Intel 80286</a> and its immediate successors during the 1980s. The bus was (largely) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backward_compatible" title="Backward compatible">backward compatible</a> with the 8-bit bus of the <a href="/wiki/Intel_8088" title="Intel 8088">8088</a>-based <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer" title="IBM Personal Computer">IBM PC</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC/XT" title="IBM PC/XT">IBM PC/XT</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">IBM PC compatibles</a>.</p>
<p>Originally referred to as the PC/AT-bus it was also termed <i>I/O Channel</i> by IBM. The <b>ISA</b> concept was coined by competing PC-clone manufacturers in the late 1980s or early 1990s as a reaction to IBM attempts to replace the AT-bus with its new and incompatible <a href="/wiki/Micro_Channel_architecture" title="Micro Channel architecture">Micro Channel architecture</a>.</p>
<p>The 16-bit ISA bus was also used with 32-bit processors for several years. An attempt to extend it to 32 bits, called <a href="/wiki/Extended_Industry_Standard_Architecture" title="Extended Industry Standard Architecture">Extended Industry Standard Architecture</a> (EISA), was not very successful, however. Later buses such as <a href="/wiki/VESA_Local_Bus" title="VESA Local Bus">VESA Local Bus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conventional_PCI" title="Conventional PCI">PCI</a> were used instead, often along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISA_slot" title="ISA slot">ISA slots</a> on the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainboard" title="Mainboard">mainboard</a>. Derivatives of the AT bus structure were and still are used in <a href="/wiki/Parallel_ATA" title="Parallel ATA">ATA/IDE</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCMCIA" title="PCMCIA">PCMCIA</a> standard, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Flash" title="Compact Flash">Compact Flash</a>, the <a href="/wiki/PC/104" title="PC/104">PC/104</a> bus, and internally within <a href="/wiki/Super_I/O" title="Super I/O">Super I/O</a> chips.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Compaq" title="Compaq">Compaq</a> created the term "Industry Standard Architecture" (ISA) to replace "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_compatible" title="PC compatible">PC compatible</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iw19890123_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-iw19890123-1">[1]</a></sup> The ISA bus was developed by a team led by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dean_(computer_scientist)" title="Mark Dean (computer scientist)">Mark Dean</a> at <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> as part of the IBM PC project in 1981. It originated as an 8-bit system. The newer 16-bit standard, the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer/AT" title="IBM Personal Computer/AT">IBM AT</a> bus, was introduced in 1984. In 1988, the "Gang of Nine" PC compatible manufacturers, including Compaq, proposed the 32-bit <a href="/wiki/Extended_Industry_Standard_Architecture" title="Extended Industry Standard Architecture">Extended Industry Standard Architecture</a> (EISA) standard and in the process <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retroactively renamed</a> the AT bus to "ISA" to avoid infringing IBM's trademark on its PC/AT computer. IBM designed the 8 bit version as a buffered interface to the external bus of the <a href="/wiki/Intel_8088" title="Intel 8088">Intel 8088</a> (16/8 bit) CPU used in the original IBM PC and PC/XT, and the 16-bit version as an upgrade for the external bus of the Intel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/80286" title="80286">80286</a> CPU used in the IBM AT. Therefore, the ISA bus was synchronous with the CPU clock, until sophisticated buffering methods were developed and implemented by chipsets to interface ISA to much faster CPUs.</p>
<p>Designed to connect peripheral cards to the <a href="/wiki/Motherboard" title="Motherboard">motherboard</a>, ISA allows for <a href="/wiki/Bus_mastering" title="Bus mastering">bus mastering</a> although only the first 16 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> of main memory are available for direct access. The 8-bit bus ran at 4.77 MHz (the clock speed of the IBM PC and IBM PC/XT's 8088 CPU), while the 16-bit bus operated at 6 or 8 MHz (because the 80286 CPUs in IBM PC/AT computers ran at 6 MHz in early models and 8 MHz in later models.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_RT/PC" title="IBM RT/PC">IBM RT/PC</a> also used the 16-bit bus. It was also available on some non-IBM compatible machines such as Motorola <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/68k" title="68k">68k</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Computer" title="Apollo Computer">Apollo</a> (68020) and <a href="/wiki/Amiga_3000" title="Amiga 3000">Amiga 3000</a> (68030) workstations, the short-lived <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Hobbit" title="AT&amp;T Hobbit">AT&amp;T Hobbit</a> and later <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> based <a href="/wiki/BeBox" title="BeBox">BeBox</a>.</p>

<p>Companies like <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a> improved the AT bus's performance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis19880424_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis19880424-2">[2]</a></sup> but in 1987, IBM moved to replace the AT bus with their proprietary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro_Channel_Architecture" title="Micro Channel Architecture">Micro Channel Architecture</a> (MCA) in an effort to regain control of the PC architecture and the PC market. (Note the relationship between the IBM term "I/O Channel" for the AT-bus and the name "Micro Channel" for IBM's intended replacement.) MCA had many features that would later appear in PCI, the successor of ISA, but MCA was a closed standard, unlike ISA (PC-bus and AT-bus) for which IBM had released full specifications and even circuit schematics. The system was far more advanced than the AT bus, and computer manufacturers responded with the <a href="/wiki/Extended_Industry_Standard_Architecture" title="Extended Industry Standard Architecture">Extended Industry Standard Architecture</a> (EISA) and later, the <a href="/wiki/VESA_Local_Bus" title="VESA Local Bus">VESA Local Bus</a> (VLB). In fact, VLB used some electronic parts originally intended for MCA because component manufacturers already were equipped to manufacture them. Both EISA and VLB were backwards-compatible expansions of the AT (ISA) bus.</p>
<p>Users of ISA-based machines had to know special information about the hardware they were adding to the system. While a handful of devices were essentially "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plug-n-play" title="Plug-n-play">plug-n-play</a>", this was rare. Users frequently had to configure several parameters when adding a new device, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interrupt_request" title="Interrupt request">IRQ</a> line, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I/O_address" title="I/O address">I/O address</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Direct_memory_access" title="Direct memory access">DMA</a> channel. MCA had done away with this complication, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripheral_Component_Interconnect" title="Peripheral Component Interconnect">PCI</a> actually incorporated many of the ideas first explored with MCA (though it was more directly descended from EISA).</p>
<p>This trouble with configuration eventually led to the creation of <b>ISA PnP</b>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plug-n-play" title="Plug-n-play">plug-n-play</a> system that used a combination of modifications to hardware, the system <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> software to automatically manage resource allocations. In reality, ISA PnP can be troublesome, and did not become well-supported until the architecture was in its final days.</p>
<p>PCI slots were the first physically incompatible expansion ports to directly squeeze ISA off the <a href="/wiki/Motherboard" title="Motherboard">motherboard</a>. At first, motherboards were largely ISA, including a few PCI slots. By the mid-1990s, the two slot types were roughly balanced, and ISA slots soon were in the minority of consumer systems. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_99" title="PC 99">PC 99</a> specification recommended that ISA slots be removed entirely, though the system architecture still required ISA to be present in some vestigial way internally to handle the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floppy_drive" title="Floppy drive">floppy drive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serial_port" title="Serial port">serial ports</a>, etc., which was why the software compatible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LPC_bus" title="LPC bus">LPC bus</a> was created. ISA slots remained for a few more years, and towards the turn of the century it was common to see systems with an <a href="/wiki/Accelerated_Graphics_Port" title="Accelerated Graphics Port">Accelerated Graphics Port</a> (AGP) sitting near the <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing unit</a>, an array of PCI slots, and one or two ISA slots near the end. In late 2008, even floppy disk drives and serial ports were disappearing, and the extinction of vestigial ISA (by then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LPC_bus" title="LPC bus">LPC bus</a>) from chipsets was on the horizon.</p>
<p>It is also notable that PCI slots are "rotated" compared to their ISA counterparts—PCI cards were essentially inserted "upside-down," allowing ISA and PCI connectors to squeeze together on the motherboard. Only one of the two connectors can be used in each slot at a time, but this allowed for greater flexibility.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT_Attachment" title="AT Attachment">AT Attachment</a> (ATA) hard disk interface is directly descended from ISA (the AT bus). ATA has its origins in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardcards" title="Hardcards">hardcards</a> that integrated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk_controller" title="Hard disk controller">hard disk controller</a> (HDC) — usually with an ST-506/ST-412 interface — and a hard disk drive on the same ISA adapter. This was at best awkward from a mechanical structural standpoint, as ISA slots were not designed to support such heavy devices as hard disks (and the 3.5" form-factor hard disks of the time were about twice as tall and heavy as modern drives), so the next generation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Drive_Electronics" title="Integrated Drive Electronics">Integrated Drive Electronics</a> drives moved both the drive and controller to a drive bay and used a ribbon cable and a very simple interface board to connect it to an ISA slot. ATA, at its essence, is basically a standardization of this arrangement, combined with a uniform command structure for software to interface with the controller on a drive. ATA has since been separated from the ISA bus, and connected directly to the local bus (usually by integration into the chipset), to be clocked much much faster than ISA could support and with much higher throughput. (Notably when ISA was introduced as the AT bus, there <i>was</i> no distinction between a <i>local</i> and <i>extension</i> bus, and there were no chipsets.) Still, ATA retains details which reveal its relationship to ISA. The 16-bit transfer size is the most obvious example; the signal timing, particularly in the PIO modes, is also highly correlated, and the interrupt and DMA mechanisms are clearly from ISA. (The article about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT_Attachment" title="AT Attachment">ATA</a> has more detail about this history.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ISA_bus_architecture">ISA bus architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: ISA bus architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:XT_Bus_pins.svg"><img alt="XT Bus pins.svg" data-file-height="938" data-file-width="455" height="495" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/XT_Bus_pins.svg/240px-XT_Bus_pins.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/XT_Bus_pins.svg/360px-XT_Bus_pins.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/XT_Bus_pins.svg/480px-XT_Bus_pins.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ISA_Bus_pins.svg"><img alt="ISA Bus pins.svg" data-file-height="1537" data-file-width="970" height="380" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/ISA_Bus_pins.svg/240px-ISA_Bus_pins.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/ISA_Bus_pins.svg/360px-ISA_Bus_pins.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/ISA_Bus_pins.svg/480px-ISA_Bus_pins.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a></div>
<p>The <b>PC/XT-bus</b> is an eight-<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> ISA bus used by <a href="/wiki/Intel_8086" title="Intel 8086">Intel 8086</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intel_8088" title="Intel 8088">Intel 8088</a> systems in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC_XT" title="IBM PC XT">IBM PC XT</a> in the 1980s. Among its 62 pins were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demultiplex" title="Demultiplex">demultiplexed</a> and electrically buffered versions of the 8 data and 20 address lines of the 8088 processor, along with power lines, clocks, read/write strobes, interrupt lines, etc. Power lines included −5 V and ±12 V in order to directly support <a href="/wiki/PMOS_logic" title="PMOS logic">pMOS</a> and enhancement mode <a href="/wiki/NMOS_logic" title="NMOS logic">nMOS</a> circuits such as dynamic RAMs among other things. The XT bus architecture uses a single <a href="/wiki/Intel_8259" title="Intel 8259">Intel 8259</a> <a href="/wiki/Programmable_Interrupt_Controller" title="Programmable Interrupt Controller">PIC</a>, giving eight vectorized and prioritized interrupt lines. It has four <a href="/wiki/Direct_memory_access" title="Direct memory access">DMA</a> channels originally provided by the <a href="/wiki/Intel_8237" title="Intel 8237">Intel 8237</a>, 3 of the DMA channels are brought out to the XT bus expansion slots; of these, 2 are normally already allocated to machine functions (diskette drive and hard disk controller):</p>

<p>The <b>PC/AT-bus</b>, a 16-<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> (or 80286-) version of the PC/XT bus, was introduced with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC/AT" title="IBM PC/AT">IBM PC/AT</a>. This bus was officially termed <i>I/O Channel</i> by IBM. It extends the XT-bus by adding a second shorter <a href="/wiki/Edge_connector" title="Edge connector">edge connector</a> in-line with the eight-bit XT-bus connector, which is unchanged, retaining compatibility with most 8-bit cards. The second connector adds four additional address lines for a total of 24, and 8 additional data lines for a total of 16. It also adds new interrupt lines connected to a second <a href="/wiki/Intel_8259" title="Intel 8259">8259 PIC</a> (connected to one of the lines of the first) and 4 × 16-bit DMA channels, as well as control lines to select 8- or 16-bit transfers.</p>
<p>The 16-bit AT bus slot originally used two standard edge connector sockets in early IBM PC/AT machines. However, with the popularity of the AT-architecture and the 16-bit ISA bus, manufacturers introduced specialized 98-pin connectors that integrated the two sockets into one unit. These can be found in almost every AT-class PC manufactured after the mid-1980s. The ISA slot connector is typically black (distinguishing it from the brown EISA connectors and white PCI connectors).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_devices">Number of devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Number of devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Motherboard devices have dedicated IRQs (not present in the slots). 16-bit devices can use either PC-bus or PC/AT-bus IRQs. It is therefore possible to connect up to 6 devices that use one 8-bit IRQ each, or up to 5 devices that use one 16-bit IRQ each. At the same time, up to 4 devices may use one 8-bit DMA channel each, while up to 3 devices can use one 16-bit DMA channel each.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Varying_bus_speeds">Varying bus speeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Varying bus speeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally, the bus clock was synchronous with the CPU clock, resulting in varying bus clock frequencies among the many different IBM "clones" on the market (sometimes as high as 16 or 20 MHz), leading to software or electrical timing problems for certain ISA cards at bus speeds they were not designed for. Later motherboards or integrated <a href="/wiki/Chipset" title="Chipset">chipsets</a> used a separate clock generator, or a clock divider which either fixed the ISA bus frequency at 4, 6, or 8 MHz or allowed the user to adjust the frequency via the <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a> setup. When used at a higher bus frequency, some ISA cards (certain <a href="/wiki/Hercules_Graphics_Card" title="Hercules Graphics Card">Hercules-compatible</a> video cards, for instance), could show significant performance improvements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="8.2F16-bit_incompatibilities">8/16-bit incompatibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 8/16-bit incompatibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Memory address decoding for the selection of 8 or 16-bit transfer mode was limited to 128 kB sections: A0000<sub>16</sub> – BFFFF<sub>16</sub>, C0000<sub>16</sub> – DFFFF<sub>16</sub>, E0000<sub>16</sub> – FFFFF<sub>16</sub> leading to problems when mixing 8- and 16-bit cards, as they could not co-exist in the same 128 kiB area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_and_current_use">Past and current use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Past and current use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>ISA is still used today for specialized industrial purposes. In 2008 IEI Technologies released a modern motherboard for Intel Core 2 Duo processors which, in addition to other special I/O features, is equipped with two ISA slots. It is marketed to industrial and military users who have invested in expensive specialized ISA bus adaptors, which are not available in <a href="/wiki/Conventional_PCI" title="Conventional PCI">PCI</a> bus versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, ADEK Industrial Computers is releasing a motherboard in early 2013 for Intel Core i3/i5/i7 processors, which contains one (non-DMA) ISA slot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/PC/104" title="PC/104">PC/104</a> bus, used in industrial and embedded applications, is a derivative of the ISA bus, utilizing the same signal lines with different connectors. The <a href="/wiki/Low_Pin_Count" title="Low Pin Count">LPC</a> bus has replaced the ISA bus as the connection to the legacy I/O devices on recent motherboards; while physically quite different, LPC looks just like ISA to software, so that the peculiarities of ISA such as the 16 MiB DMA limit (which corresponds to the full address space of the Intel 80286 CPU used in the original IBM AT) are likely to stick around for a while.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATA">ATA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: ATA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As explained in the <i>History</i> section, ISA was the basis for development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Attachment" title="Advanced Technology Attachment">ATA</a> interface, used for ATA (a.k.a. IDE) and more recently <a href="/wiki/Serial_ATA" title="Serial ATA">Serial ATA (SATA)</a> hard disks. Physically, ATA is essentially a simple subset of ISA, with 16 data bits, support for exactly one IRQ and one DMA channel, and 3 address bits plus two IDE address select ("chip select") lines, plus a few unique signal lines specific to ATA/IDE hard disks (such as the Cable Select/Spindle Sync. line.) ATA goes beyond and far outside the scope of ISA by also specifying a set of physical device registers to be implemented on every ATA (IDE) drive and accessed using the address bits and address select signals in the ATA physical interface channel; ATA also specifies a full set of protocols and device commands for controlling fixed disk drives using these registers, through which all operations of ATA hard disks are performed. A further deviation between ISA and ATA is that while the ISA bus remained locked into a single standard clock rate (for backward compatibility), the ATA interface offered many different speed modes, could select among them to match the maximum speed supported by the attached drives, and kept adding faster speeds with later versions of the ATA standard (up to 133 MB/s for ATA-6, the latest.) In most forms, ATA ran much faster than ISA.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XT-IDE">XT-IDE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: XT-IDE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the 16-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Attachment" title="Advanced Technology Attachment">ATA/IDE</a> interface, there was an 8-bit XT-IDE (also known as XTA) interface for hard disks. It was not nearly as popular as ATA has become, and XT-IDE hardware is now fairly hard to find. Some XT-IDE adapters were available as 8-bit ISA cards, and XTA sockets were also present on the motherboards of <a href="/wiki/Amstrad" title="Amstrad">Amstrad</a>'s later XT clones as well as a short-lived line of <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> units. The XTA pinout was very similar to ATA, but only eight data lines and two address lines were used, and the physical device registers had completely different meanings. A few hard drives (such as the <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate</a> ST351A/X) could support either type of interface, selected with a jumper.</p>
<p>Many later AT (and AT successor) motherboards had no integrated hard drive interface but relied on a separate hard drive interface plugged into an ISA/EISA/VLB slot. There were even a few 80486 based units shipped with MFM/RLL interfaces and drives instead of the increasingly common AT-IDE.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> built the XT-IDE based peripheral hard drive / memory expansion unit A590 for their <a href="/wiki/Amiga_500" title="Amiga 500">Amiga 500</a> and 500+ computers that also supported a <a href="/wiki/SCSI" title="SCSI">SCSI</a> drive. AT-IDE type interfaces only entered the keyboard-cased Amiga line upon introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Amiga_600" title="Amiga 600">A600</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amiga_1200" title="Amiga 1200">A1200</a> which have an <a href="/wiki/Amiga_custom_chips#Gayle" title="Amiga custom chips">integrated interface</a> and 44 pin connector. Many owners removed the 2,5 inch bracket and installed a 3,5 inch drive with an adapter cable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PCMCIA">PCMCIA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: PCMCIA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCMCIA" title="PCMCIA">PCMCIA</a> specification can be seen as a superset of ATA. The standard for PCMCIA hard disk interfaces, which included PCMCIA flash drives, allows for the mutual configuration of the port and the drive in an ATA mode. As a de facto extension, most PCMCIA flash drives additionally allow for a simple ATA mode that is enabled by pulling a single pin low, so that PCMCIA hardware and firmware are unnecessary to use them as an ATA drive connected to an ATA port. PCMCIA flash drive to ATA adapters are thus simple and inexpensive, but are not guaranteed to work with any and every standard PCMCIA flash drive. Further, such adapters cannot be used as generic PCMCIA ports, as the PCMCIA interface is much more complex than ATA.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emulation_by_embedded_chips">Emulation by embedded chips</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Emulation by embedded chips">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although most modern computers do not have physical ISA buses, all IBM compatible computers — <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a>, and <a href="/wiki/X86-64" title="X86-64">x86-64</a> (most non-mainframe, non-embedded) — have ISA buses allocated in <a href="/wiki/Virtual_address_space" title="Virtual address space">virtual address space</a>. Embedded controller chips (<a href="/wiki/Southbridge_(computing)" title="Southbridge (computing)">southbridge</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPUs</a> themselves provide services such as temperature monitoring and voltage readings through these buses as ISA devices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization">Standardization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry_Standard_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Standardization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>IEEE started a standardization of the ISA bus in 1985, called the P996 specification. However, despite there even having been books published on the P996 specification, it never officially progressed past draft status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
