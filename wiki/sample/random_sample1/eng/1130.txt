<p>The <b>IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</b> (also known as the <b>IUCN Red List</b> or <b>Red Data List</b>), founded in 1964, is the world's most comprehensive inventory of the global <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> status of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">biological species</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) is the world's main authority on the conservation status of species. A series of <a href="/wiki/Regional_Red_List" title="Regional Red List">Regional Red Lists</a> are produced by countries or organizations, which assess the risk of extinction to species within a political management unit.</p>
<p>The IUCN Red List is set upon precise criteria to evaluate the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> risk of thousands of species and subspecies. These criteria are relevant to all species and all regions of the world. The aim is to convey the urgency of conservation issues to the public and policy makers, as well as help the international community to try to reduce species extinction. According to IUCN (1996), the formally stated goals of the Red List are (1) to provide scientifically based information on the status of species and subspecies at a global level, (2) to draw attention to the magnitude and importance of threatened biodiversity, (3) to influence national and international policy and decision-making, and (4) to provide information to guide actions to conserve biological diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Major species assessors include <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Zoology" title="Institute of Zoology">Institute of Zoology</a> (the research division of the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a>), the <a href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Monitoring_Centre" title="World Conservation Monitoring Centre">World Conservation Monitoring Centre</a>, and many Specialist Groups within the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Species_Survival_Commission" title="IUCN Species Survival Commission">IUCN Species Survival Commission</a> (SSC). Collectively, assessments by these organizations and groups account for nearly half the species on the Red List.</p>
<p>The IUCN aims to have the category of every species re-evaluated every five years if possible, or at least every ten years. This is done in a <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer reviewed</a> manner through IUCN Species Survival Commission (SSC) Specialist Groups, which are Red List Authorities responsible for a species, group of species or specific geographic area, or in the case of <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>, an entire class (<a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">Aves</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1964_Red_List_of_Threatened_Plants">1964 Red List of Threatened Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1964 Red List of Threatened Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1964 IUCN Red List of Threatened Plants used the older pre-criteria Red List assessment system. Plants listed may not, therefore, appear in the current Red List. IUCN advise that is best to check both the online Red List and the 1997 plants Red List publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN-FAQ_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-FAQ-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_release">2006 release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2006 release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2006 Red List, released on 4 May 2006 evaluated 40,168 species as a whole, plus an additional 2,160 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subspecies_(biology)" title="Subspecies (biology)">subspecies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variety_(biology)" title="Variety (biology)">varieties</a>, aquatic <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stocks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_population" title="Statistical population">subpopulations</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007_release">2007 release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2007 release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 12 September 2007, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Union" title="World Conservation Union">World Conservation Union</a> (IUCN) released the <b>2007 IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</b>. In this release, they have raised their classification of both the <a href="/wiki/Western_lowland_gorilla" title="Western lowland gorilla">western lowland gorilla</a> (<i>Gorilla gorilla gorilla</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Cross_River_gorilla" title="Cross River gorilla">Cross River gorilla</a> (<i>Gorilla gorilla diehli</i>) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a> to <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a>, which is the last category before <a href="/wiki/Extinct_in_the_wild" title="Extinct in the wild">extinct in the wild</a>, due to <a href="/wiki/Ebola_virus" title="Ebola virus">Ebola virus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>, along with other factors. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russ_Mittermeier" title="Russ Mittermeier">Russ Mittermeier</a>, chief of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a>-based IUCN's Primate Specialist Group, stated that 16,306 species are endangered with extinction, 188 more than in 2006 (total of 41,415 species on the Red List). The Red List includes the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan" title="Sumatran orangutan">Sumatran orangutan</a> (<i>Pongo abelii</i>) in the Critically Endangered category and the <a href="/wiki/Bornean_orangutan" title="Bornean orangutan">Bornean orangutan</a> (<i>Pongo pygmaeus</i>) in the Endangered category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_release">2008 release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2008 release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2008 Red List was released on 6 October 2008, at the IUCN World Conservation Congress in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, and "has confirmed an extinction crisis, with almost one in four [mammals] at risk of disappearing forever". The study shows at least 1,141 of the 5,487 mammals on Earth are known to be threatened with extinction, and 836 are listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_Deficient" title="Data Deficient">Data Deficient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_release">2012 release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2012 release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red List of 2012 was released 19 July 2012 at <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Sustainable_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development">Rio+20 Earth Summit</a>; nearly 2,000 species were added, with 4 species to the extinct list, 2 to the rediscovered list. The IUCN assessed a total of 63,837 species which revealed 19,817 are threatened with extinction. With 3,947 described as "critically endangered" and 5,766 as "endangered", while more than 10,000 species are listed as "vulnerable". At threat are 41% of amphibian species, 33% of reef-building corals, 30% of conifers, 25% of mammals, and 13% of birds. The IUCN Red List has listed 132 species of plants and animals from India as "Critically Endangered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Species are classified by the IUCN Red List into nine groups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> set through criteria such as rate of decline, population size, area of geographic distribution, and degree of population and distribution fragmentation.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">Extinct</a> (EX) – No known individuals remaining.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extinct_in_the_wild" title="Extinct in the wild">Extinct in the wild</a> (EW) – Known only to survive in captivity, or as a naturalized population outside its historic range.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_endangered_species" title="Critically endangered species">Critically endangered</a> (CR) – Extremely high risk of extinction in the wild.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">Endangered</a> (EN) – High risk of extinction in the wild.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> (VU) – High risk of endangerment in the wild.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_threatened" title="Near threatened">Near threatened</a> (NT) – Likely to become endangered in the near future.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_concern" title="Least concern">Least concern</a> (LC) – Lowest risk. Does not qualify for a more at-risk category. Widespread and abundant taxa are included in this category.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">Data deficient</a> (DD) – Not enough data to make an assessment of its risk of extinction.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Not_evaluated" title="Not evaluated">Not evaluated</a> (NE) – Has not yet been evaluated against the criteria.</li>
</ul>
<p>When discussing the IUCN Red List, the official term "<a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a>" is a grouping of three categories: Critically Endangered, Endangered, and Vulnerable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1994_categories_and_criteria">1994 categories and criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1994 categories and criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The older 1994 has only a single "Lower Risk" category which contained three subcategories:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_Dependent" title="Conservation Dependent">Conservation Dependent</a> (LR/cd)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> (LR/nt)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> (LR/lc)</li>
</ul>
<p>In the 2001 system Near Threatened and Least Concern have now become their own categories, while Conservation Dependent is no longer used and has been merged into Near Threatened.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possibly_extinct">Possibly extinct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Possibly extinct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tag of "possibly extinct" (PE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birdlife_International" title="Birdlife International">Birdlife International</a>, the Red List Authority for birds for the IUCN Red List.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> BirdLife International has recommended PE become an official tag for Critically Endangered species, and this has now been adopted, along with a "Possibly Extinct in the Wild" tag for species with populations surviving in captivity but likely to be extinct in the wild (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Spix%27s_macaw" title="Spix's macaw">Spix's macaw</a>).</p>

<p>There have been a number of versions, dating from 1991, including:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IUCN_Red_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Status_iucn2.3.svg"><img alt="Icons for Red List categories" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="64" data-file-width="240" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Status_iucn2.3.svg/220px-Status_iucn2.3.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Status_iucn2.3.svg/330px-Status_iucn2.3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Status_iucn2.3.svg/440px-Status_iucn2.3.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Status_iucn2.3.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1994 IUCN Red List categories (version 2.3), used for species which have not been reassessed since 2001.</div>
</div>

<p>In 1997, the IUCN Red List came under criticism on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Secrecy" title="Secrecy">secrecy</a> (or at least poor documentation) surrounding the sources of its data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>These allegations have led to efforts by the IUCN to improve its documentation and data quality, and to include peer reviews of taxa on the Red List. The list is also open to petitions against its classifications, on the basis of documentation or criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> editorial defended the Red List's relevance in October 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Editors_of_Nature_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Editors_of_Nature-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been suggested that the IUCN Red List and similar works are prone to misuse by governments and other groups that draw possibly inappropriate conclusions on the state of the environment or to effect exploitation of natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
