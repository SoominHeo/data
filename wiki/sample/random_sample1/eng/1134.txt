<p><b>I Zwicky 18</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy" title="Dwarf galaxy">dwarf</a> <a href="/wiki/Irregular_galaxy" title="Irregular galaxy">irregular galaxy</a> located about 59 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a> away<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubble2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hubble2007-2">[2]</a></sup> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Major" title="Ursa Major">Ursa Major</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> was first identified by Swiss <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky" title="Fritz Zwicky">Fritz Zwicky</a> in a 1930s photographic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_surveys" title="Astronomical surveys">survey</a> of galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age">Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Zwicky_18&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studies at the Palomar Observatory some 40 years ago led astronomers to believe that the galaxy erupted with star formation billions of years after its galactic neighbors. Galaxies resembling I Zwicky 18's youthful appearance are typically found only in the early universe. Early observations with the Hubble Space Telescope suggested an age of 500 million years old for I Zwicky 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, however, later found faint, older stars contained within the galaxy, suggesting its star formation started at least one billion years ago and possibly as much as ten billion years ago. The galaxy, therefore, may have formed at the same time as most other galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubble2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubble2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">Contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Zwicky_18&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">Spectroscopic</a> observations with ground-based telescopes have shown that I Zwicky 18 is almost exclusively composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, the main ingredients created in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>. The galaxy's primordial makeup suggests that its rate of star formation has been much lower than that of other galaxies of similar age. The galaxy has been studied with most of NASA's telescopes, including the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra X-ray Observatory</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Far_Ultraviolet_Spectroscopic_Explorer" title="Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer">Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer</a> (FUSE). However, it remains a mystery why I Zwicky 18 formed so few stars in the past, and why it is forming so many new stars right now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubble2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-hubble2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 a study found a very large region of ionised helium in this small galaxy, which tends to be more frequent in very distant galaxies with low presence of metals. The ionization of helium implies the presence of objects emitting a radiation intense enough to knock electrons off the helium atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Scientists therefore conclude that I Zwicky 18 must contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_III" title="Population III">Population III</a> stars, which are almost absent in all other galaxies in the local universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Zwicky_18&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Astronomers at the <a href="/wiki/Space_Telescope_Science_Institute" title="Space Telescope Science Institute">Space Telescope Science Institute</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore, Maryland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> discerned the distance by observing <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variable</a> stars within I Zwicky 18. These massive flashing stars pulse in a regular rhythm. The timing of their pulsations is directly related to their brightness. The team determined the <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">observed brightness</a> of three Cepheids and compared it with the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">actual brightness</a> predicted by theoretical models. These models were calculated specifically for I Zwicky 18's deficiency in heavy elements, indicating the galaxy's stars formed before these elements were abundant in the universe. The Cepheid distance also was validated through the observed brightness of the brightest red stars older than one billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubble2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hubble2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Hubble data suggest that I Zwicky 18 is 59 million <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from Earth. The galaxy's larger-than-expected distance may explain why astronomers have had difficulty detecting older, fainter stars within the galaxy. The faint, old stars in I Zwicky 18 are almost at the limit of Hubble's resolution and sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Zwicky_18&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>I Zwicky 18 is classified as a dwarf irregular galaxy and also a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy#Blue_compact_dwarf_galaxies" title="Dwarf galaxy">blue compact galaxy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">starburst</a> galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> I Zwicky 18 is much smaller than our <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and a mere 3,000 light-years across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> I Zwicky 18 may still be creating <a href="/wiki/Metallicity#Population_III_stars" title="Metallicity">Population III stars</a>—<a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> shows that its stars are composed almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, with heavier <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> almost completely absent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The concentrated bluish-white knots embedded in the heart of the galaxy are two major starburst regions where stars are forming at a furious rate. The wispy blue filaments surrounding the central starburst regions are bubbles of gas that have been blown away by stellar winds and supernovae explosions from a previous generation of hot, young stars. This gas is now heated by intense ultraviolet radiation unleashed by a new generation of hot, young stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
