<p><i><b>Insomniac</b></i> is the eighth studio album, and fourth English-language album, released by Spanish singer <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Iglesias" title="Enrique Iglesias">Enrique Iglesias</a>. The album was produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Shanks" title="John Shanks">John Shanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Lundin" title="Kristian Lundin">Kristian Lundin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Garrett" title="Sean Garrett">Sean Garrett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anders_Bagge" title="Anders Bagge">Anders Bagge</a>, Jesper Andersen, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Taylor_(record_producer)" title="Mark Taylor (record producer)">Mark Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stargate_(production_team)" title="Stargate (production team)">Stargate</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maratone" title="Maratone">Maratone</a>. It also contains his first collaboration with a rapper, <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, as well as a cover of indie band <a href="/wiki/Ringside" title="Ringside">Ringside</a>'s "Tired of Being Sorry". This album is said to be different from his prior albums, incorporating a more contemporary pop style. As well as the aforementioned hip hop collaboration, the album also includes elements of <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_pop" title="Latin pop">Latin pop</a>. It was released worldwide on 11 June 2007 and in the USA on 12 June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_composition">Background and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insomniac_(Enrique_Iglesias_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Sometimes I take a pill to sleep, or two or six. But I guess that is the way it goes - when you put three years into making an album, you also become a bit of an insomniac. I have always been more of a night person. I never lay vocals onto any song before eight or nine at night. I always work at night. I go to the studio at six or seven in the evening and leave again at six in the morning. So in a way the album was done by night."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—<a href="/wiki/Enrique_Iglesias" title="Enrique Iglesias">Enrique Iglesias</a> told to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Insider_(newspaper)" title="The Insider (newspaper)">The Insider</a></i> about recording the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The album is his first for four years since <i><a href="/wiki/7_(Enrique_Iglesias_album)" title="7 (Enrique Iglesias album)">7</a></i>, which failed to match the staggering sales of 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_(Enrique_Iglesias_album)" title="Escape (Enrique Iglesias album)">Escape</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail-3">[3]</a></sup> "Insomniac" is not just the title of the album, it is also a fact of Iglesias's life, who says he often takes sleeping pills to get a good night's rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup> Iglesias recorded most of the album in the evening and early hours of the morning. He wrote more than 50 songs before settling on 12 that he felt were strong enough to put on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Insomniac</i> runs the gamut of emotional anxiety, moody pining, enduring love and a constant sexual pulse. He says he wrote hundreds of songs for the album. "The more songs I wrote, the best chance that I could pick 12, 15 great ones. It's not that I wanted to make a musical change, I just wanted to write good songs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail-3">[3]</a></sup> The album is said to be Iglesias' most diverse to date. It features a song titled "Miss You", which he has dedicated to his girlfriend, former tennis star <a href="/wiki/Anna_Kournikova" title="Anna Kournikova">Anna Kournikova</a>, as well as a track, "Push", which he performs with rapper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lil%27_Wayne" title="Lil' Wayne">Lil' Wayne</a> -- a big departure from his usual material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup> The first single, "Do You Know", has become known as the "ping pong song" because of its stand-out beat. There is also a cover of indie pop band <a href="/wiki/Ringside" title="Ringside">Ringside</a>'s "Tired of Being Sorry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"I wanted to write about love because it is the one thing that we all identify with. Everyone knows what it feels like to be with someone you love, and to fall out of love. You suffer because of love and then love makes you happy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> reviewer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> gave to the album 2.5 out of 5 stars, writing: "'Insomniac' is pushed to a younger audience. But Enrique is too much of a crossover guy to spend the entire album doing the nasty. Sometimes, he pushes too hard in either direction, but sometimes Iglesias strikes the right balance between crossover pop and stylish retro-new wave production. What works on this slick, snazzy makeover is what always works for him: the ballads and the middle-of-the-road pop tunes. They may not be hip, but they're part of the family tradition, and when he sticks to them, he's as good as ever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup> Lauren Murphy from <a href="/wiki/Entertainment.ie" title="Entertainment.ie">Entertainment.ie</a> criticized the songs, writing: "Every track here is either a sombre, mumbled, slow-moving number that sounds dated and cumbersome, cheesy radio-friendly dirges that have been done a million times before and better or are just so amateur-sounding that they're embarrassing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entertainmentie_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-entertainmentie-6">[6]</a></sup> Jonathan Bernstein from <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> wrote "Insomniac sees Iglesias possessed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vengeful_spirit" title="Vengeful spirit">vengeful spirit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Toad_the_Wet_Sprocket" title="Toad the Wet Sprocket">Toad the Wet Sprocket</a> ("Stay Here Tonight"), outshone by Lil Wayne on the plodding club banger <i>Push,</i> and, yes, upstaged by a sampled Ping-Pong ball on <i>Do You Know? (The Ping Pong Song)</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ew-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jerome Blakeney from <a href="/wiki/BBC_Music" title="BBC Music">BBC Music</a> gave to the album a positive review, stating that "He remains the king of latin-tinged heart-tugging and at least half of this album keeps him firmly on top."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-5">[5]</a></sup> Joey Guerra from <i><a href="/wiki/Houston_Chronicle" title="Houston Chronicle">Houston Chronicle</a></i> also gave to the album a positive review, writing: "Insomniac's secret weapons, however, are its ballads, which cast Iglesias as — of course — a sensitive, soulful Spanish lover."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houston_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-houston-8">[8]</a></sup> Scott Shelter wrote for <i><a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a></i> that "The ballads are disappointing because the rest of Insomniac isn't half bad, and if all of the ballads were like the acoustic 'Don't You Forget About Me", Insomniac would've been worthwhile effort; instead, it's a case study in ironic album titles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slant_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-slant-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insomniac_(Enrique_Iglesias_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album debuted at number 17 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, with 45,000 copies sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboardbiz_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboardbiz-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the previous album, 2003's <i>7</i>, began at number 31, but with 77,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboardbiz_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-billboardbiz-10">[10]</a></sup> It entered the UK charts at number 3 and went on to be certified Gold. The album went Platinum (or multiplatinum) in countries such as Ireland, Russia and Poland.</p>
