<p><b>John Burdon Sanderson Haldane</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ÉË/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ÉË</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/eÉª/ long 'a' in 'base'">eÉª</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 5 November 1892Â â 1 December 1964)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a>-born scientist known for his work in the study of <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, where he made innovative contributions to the fields of <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biostatistics" title="Biostatistics">biostatistics</a>. He was the son of the equally famous <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Haldane" title="John Scott Haldane">John Scott Haldane</a> and a professed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheist" title="Atheist">atheist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Secular_humanism" title="Secular humanism">humanist</a> whose political dissent led him to leave England in 1956 and live in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, becoming a naturalised Indian citizen in 1961.</p>
<p>His first paper in 1915 demonstrated <a href="/wiki/Genetic_linkage" title="Genetic linkage">genetic linkage</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> while subsequent works helped to create <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a>, thus establishing a unification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mendelian_genetics" title="Mendelian genetics">Mendelian genetics</a> and Darwinian evolution by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> whilst laying the groundwork for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_evolutionary_synthesis" title="Modern evolutionary synthesis">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>. His article on <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a> in 1929 introduced the "Primordial Soup Theory", and it became the foundation to build physical models for the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">chemical origin of life</a>. Haldane established human <a href="/wiki/Gene_map" title="Gene map">gene maps</a> for <a href="/wiki/Haemophilia" title="Haemophilia">haemophilia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_blindness" title="Colour blindness">colour blindness</a> on the <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a>, and codified <a href="/wiki/Haldane%27s_rule" title="Haldane's rule">Haldane's rule</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sterility_(physiology)" title="Sterility (physiology)">sterility</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Heterogametic_sex" title="Heterogametic sex">heterogametic sex</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldane1922_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haldane1922-3">[3]</a></sup> He correctly proposed that <a href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" title="Sickle-cell disease">sickle-cell disease</a> confers some immunity to <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>. He was the first to suggest the central idea of <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation"><i>in vitro</i> fertilisation</a>, as well as concepts such as: the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a>, cis and <i><a href="/wiki/Trans-acting" title="Trans-acting">trans-acting</a></i> regulation, <i><a href="/wiki/Coupling_reaction" title="Coupling reaction">coupling reaction</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_repulsion" title="Molecular repulsion">molecular repulsion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Darwin_(unit)" title="Darwin (unit)">darwin</a></i> (as a unit of evolution) and <i><a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">organismal cloning</a></i>. In 1957 he articulated <a href="/wiki/Haldane%27s_dilemma" title="Haldane's dilemma">Haldane's dilemma</a>, a limit on the speed of beneficial <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> which subsequently proved incorrect. He willed his body for medical studies, as he wanted to remain useful even in death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a> credited him as "perhaps the most brilliant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_popularization" title="Science popularization">science populariser</a> of his generation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dronamjaru1992_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dronamjaru1992-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Medawar" title="Peter Medawar">Peter Medawar</a> called Haldane "the cleverest man I ever knew".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Haldane was born in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> to <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Haldane" title="John Scott Haldane">John Scott Haldane</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiologist</a>, scientist, a <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a>, and Louisa Kathleen Trotter, a <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>. His younger sister, <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Mitchison" title="Naomi Mitchison">Naomi Mitchison</a>, became a writer, and his uncle was <a href="/wiki/Richard_Haldane,_1st_Viscount_Haldane" title="Richard Haldane, 1st Viscount Haldane">Viscount Haldane</a> and his aunt the author <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Haldane" title="Elizabeth Haldane">Elizabeth Haldane</a>. Descended from an aristocratic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secularity_(non-religiosity)" title="Secularity (non-religiosity)">secular</a> family<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-acott-8">[8]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Haldane" title="Clan Haldane">Clan Haldane</a>, he would later claim that his <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> could be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hedrick_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hedrick-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He grew up at 11 Crick Road, North Oxford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He learnt to read at the age of three, and at four, after injuring his forehead he asked the doctor, "Is this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhaemoglobin" title="Oxyhaemoglobin">oxyhaemoglobin</a> or carboxyhaemoglobin?". From age eight he worked with his father in their home laboratory where he experienced his first <a href="/wiki/Self-experimentation" title="Self-experimentation">self-experimentation</a>, the method he would later be famous for. He and his father became their own "human guinea pigs", such as in their investigation on the effects of poison gases. In 1899 his family moved to "Cherwell", a late Victorian house at the outskirts of Oxford having its private laboratory.</p>
<p>His formal education began in 1897 at Oxford Preparatory School (now <a href="/wiki/Dragon_School" title="Dragon School">Dragon School</a>), where he gained a First Scholarship in 1904 to <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton</a>. In 1905 he joined Eton, where he experienced severe abuse from senior students for allegedly being arrogant. The indifference of authority left him with a lasting hatred for the English education system. However, the ordeal did not stop him from becoming Captain of the school. He studied mathematics and <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classics</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_College,_Oxford" title="New College, Oxford">New College</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and obtained first-class honours in mathematical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moderations" title="Moderations">moderations</a> in 1912 and a first-class honours in 1914. With his father he published his first scientific paper, age 20. He became engrossed in genetics and presented a paper on gene linkage in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> in the summer of 1912. His first technical paper, a 30-page long article on haemoglobin function, was published, as a co-author alongside his father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldane1912_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-haldane1912-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>His education was interrupted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> during which he fought in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, being commissioned a temporary second lieutenant in the 3rd Battalion of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Watch" title="Black Watch">Black Watch (Royal Highland Regiment)</a> on 15 August 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was promoted to temporary lieutenant on 18 February 1915 and to temporary captain on 18 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He served in France and Iraq, where he was wounded. He relinquished his commission on 1 April 1920, retaining his rank of captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> For his ferocity and aggressiveness in battles, his commander called him the "bravest and dirtiest officer in my Army."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cochran_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cochran-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1919 and 1922 he was a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellow</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_College,_Oxford" title="New College, Oxford">New College, Oxford</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biol_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biol-17">[17]</a></sup> where he researched physiology and genetics. He then moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, where he accepted a <a href="/wiki/Reader_(academic_rank)" title="Reader (academic rank)">readership</a> in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</a> and taught until 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-acott-8">[8]</a></sup> From 1927 until 1937 he was also Head of Genetical Research at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Innes_Horticultural_Institution" title="John Innes Horticultural Institution">John Innes Horticultural Institution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jic.ac.uk_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jic.ac.uk-18">[18]</a></sup> During his nine years at Cambridge, Haldane worked on <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, particularly the mathematical side of genetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-acott-8">[8]</a></sup> He was the <a href="/wiki/Fullerian_Professor_of_Physiology" title="Fullerian Professor of Physiology">Fullerian Professor of Physiology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">Royal Institution</a> from 1930 to 1932 and in 1933 he became full Professor of Genetics at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, where he spent most of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic">academic</a> career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucl_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucl-19">[19]</a></sup> Four years later he became the first Weldon Professor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biometry" title="Biometry">Biometry</a> at University College London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-acott-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1924, Haldane met <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Haldane" title="Charlotte Haldane">Charlotte Franken</a>. So that they could marry, Charlotte divorced her husband, Jack Burghes, causing some controversy. Haldane was almost dismissed from Cambridge for the way he handled his meeting with her, which led to the divorce. They married in 1926. Following their separation in 1942, the Haldanes divorced in 1945. He later married <a href="/wiki/Helen_Spurway" title="Helen Spurway">Helen Spurway</a>.</p>
<p>Haldane, inspired by his father, would expose himself to danger to obtain data. To test the effects of acidification of the blood he drank dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, enclosed himself in an airtight room containing 7% carbon dioxide, and found that it 'gives one a rather violent headache'. One experiment to study elevated levels of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation" title="Oxygen saturation">oxygen saturation</a> triggered a fit which resulted in him suffering crushed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrae" title="Vertebrae">vertebrae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jobling_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-jobling-20">[20]</a></sup> In his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_chamber" title="Decompression chamber">decompression chamber</a> experiments, he and his volunteers suffered perforated <a href="/wiki/Eardrum" title="Eardrum">eardrums</a>. But, as Haldane stated in <a class="external text" href="https://searchworks.stanford.edu/view/8631282" rel="nofollow"><i>What is Life,</i></a> "the drum generally heals up; and if a hole remains in it, although one is somewhat deaf, one can blow tobacco smoke out of the ear in question, which is a social accomplishment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1956, Haldane left <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, and joined the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Statistical_Institute" title="Indian Statistical Institute">Indian Statistical Institute</a> (ISI) in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joyson_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joyson-22">[22]</a></sup> where he headed the <a href="/wiki/Biostatistics" title="Biostatistics">biometry</a> unit. Officially he stated that he left the UK because of the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a>, writing: "Finally, I am going to India because I consider that recent acts of the British Government have been violations of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>." He believed that the warm <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> would do him good, and that India shared his socialist dreams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dronam_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-dronam-23">[23]</a></sup> The university had sacked his wife Helen for excessive drinking and refusing to pay a fine, triggering Haldane's resignation. He declared he would no longer wear socks, "Sixty years in socks is enough."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and always dressed in Indian attire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dronamjaru1992_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-dronamjaru1992-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was keenly interested in inexpensive research. He wrote to <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">Julian Huxley</a> about his observations on <i>Vanellus malabaricus</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow-wattled_Lapwing" title="Yellow-wattled Lapwing">yellow-wattled Lapwing</a>. He advocated the use of <i>Vigna sinensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cowpea" title="Cowpea">cowpea</a>) as a model for studying <a href="/wiki/Plant_genetics" title="Plant genetics">plant genetics</a>. He took an interest in the <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Lantana_camara" title="Lantana camara">Lantana camara</a></i>. He lamented that Indian universities forced those who took up biology to drop mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Haldane took an interest in the study of <a href="/wiki/Floral_symmetry" title="Floral symmetry">floral symmetry</a>. In January 1961 he befriended <a href="/wiki/Gary_Botting" title="Gary Botting">Gary Botting</a>, 1960 U.S. Science Fair winner in zoology (who had first visited the Haldanes along with Susan Brown, 1960 U.S. National Science Fair winner in botany), inviting him to share the results of his experiments hybridising <a href="/wiki/Antheraea" title="Antheraea">Antheraea</a> silk moths. J.B.S., his wife Helen Spurway, and Krishna Dronamraju were present at the Oberoi Grand Hotel in Kolkata when Brown reminded the Haldanes that she and Botting had a previously scheduled event that would prevent them from accepting an invitation to a banquet proposed by J.B.S. and Helen in their honour and had regretfully declined the honor. After the two students had left the hotel, Haldane went on his much-publicized hunger strike to protest what he regarded as a "U.S. insult."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> When the director of the ISI, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._C._Mahalanobis" title="P. C. Mahalanobis">P. C. Mahalanobis</a>, confronted Haldane about both the hunger strike and the unbudgeted banquet; Haldane resigned from his post, in February 1961, and moved to a newly established biometry unit in <a href="/wiki/Odisha" title="Odisha">Odisha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dronam_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-dronam-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haldane became an Indian citizen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joyson_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joyson-22">[22]</a></sup> He was also interested in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetarian" title="Vegetarian">vegetarian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dronam_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-dronam-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1961, Haldane described India as "the closest approximation to the Free World." <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_Neyman" title="Jerzy Neyman">Jerzy Neyman</a> objected that "India has its fair share of scoundrels and a tremendous amount of poor unthinking and disgustingly subservient individuals who are not attractive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joyson_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Joyson-22">[22]</a></sup> Haldane retorted:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Perhaps one is freer to be a scoundrel in India than elsewhere. So one was in the U.S.A in the days of people like <a href="/wiki/Jay_Gould" title="Jay Gould">Jay Gould</a>, when (in my opinion) there was more internal freedom in the U.S.A than there is today. The "disgusting subservience" of the others has its limits. The people of Calcutta riot, upset trams, and refuse to obey police regulations, in a manner which would have delighted Jefferson. I don't think their activities are very efficient, but that is not the question at issue.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When on 25 June 1962 he was described in print as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Citizen" title="World Citizen">Citizen of the World</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Groff_Conklin" title="Groff Conklin">Groff Conklin</a>, Haldane's response was as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joyson_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Joyson-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>No doubt I am in some sense a citizen of the world. But I believe with Thomas Jefferson that one of the chief duties of a citizen is to be a nuisance to the government of his state. As there is no world state, I cannot do this. On the other hand, I can be, and am, a nuisance to the government of India, which has the merit of permitting a good deal of criticism, though it reacts to it rather slowly. I also happen to be proud of being a citizen of India, which is a lot more diverse than Europe, let alone the U.S.A, the U.S.S.R or China, and thus a better model for a possible world organisation. It may of course break up, but it is a wonderful experiment. So, I want to be labeled as a citizen of India.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

It was read by his friends, who appreciated the consistent irreverence with which Haldane had lived his life. <blockquote>
<p>"<i>Cancer's a Funny Thing</i>:<br/>
I wish I had the voice of Homer<br/>
To sing of rectal carcinoma,<br/>
This kills a lot more chaps, in fact,<br/>
Than were bumped off when Troy was sackedÂ ..."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The poem ends:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"...Â I know that cancer often kills,<br/>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_achievements">Academic achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Academic achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With his sister Naomi Mitchison he started investigating Mendelian genetics in 1908, used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinea_pigs" title="Guinea pigs">guinea pigs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, publishing <i>Reduplication in mice</i> in 1915<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldane1915_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-haldane1915-32">[32]</a></sup> the first demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_linkage" title="Genetic linkage">genetic linkage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> (As the paper was written during Haldane's service in the First World War, James F. Crow called it "the most important science article ever written in a front-line trench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>) He demonstrated the same phenomenon in <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a> in 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his father's footsteps, Haldane's first publication was on the mechanism of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaseous_exchange" title="Gaseous exchange">gaseous exchange</a> by haemoglobin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haldane1912_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-haldane1912-11">[11]</a></sup> and subsequently worked on the chemical properties of blood as a pH buffer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> He investigated several aspects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_function" title="Kidney function">kidney functions</a> and mechanism of excretion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1925, with <a href="/wiki/George_Edward_Briggs" title="George Edward Briggs">G. E. Briggs</a>, Haldane derived a new interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme kinetics</a> law described by Victor Henri in 1903, different from the 1913 <a href="/wiki/Michaelis%E2%80%93Menten_kinetics" title="MichaelisâMenten kinetics">MichaelisâMenten equation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leonor_Michaelis" title="Leonor Michaelis">Leonor Michaelis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maud_Menten" title="Maud Menten">Maud Menten</a> assumed that enzyme (catalyst) and substrate (reactant) are in fast equilibrium with their complex, which then dissociates to yield product and free enzyme. The BriggsâHaldane equation was of the same algebraic form; but their derivation is based on the quasi-<a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">steady state</a> approximation, which is the concentration of intermediate complex (or complexes) does not change. As a result, the microscopic meaning of the "Michaelis Constant" (<i>K<sub>m</sub></i>) is different. Although commonly referring to it as MichaelisâMenten kinetics, most of the current models typically use the BriggsâHaldane derivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was one of the three major figures to develop the mathematical theory of <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a>. Thusly he played an important role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_evolutionary_synthesis" title="Modern evolutionary synthesis">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>, which is popularly called "neo-Darwinism", as in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a>' 1976 work titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Selfish_Gene" title="The Selfish Gene">The Selfish Gene</a>.</i> He re-established <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> as the premier mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> by explaining it in terms of the mathematical consequences of <a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">Mendelian inheritance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He wrote a series of ten papers called <a href="/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Natural_and_Artificial_Selection" title="A Mathematical Theory of Natural and Artificial Selection">A Mathematical Theory of Natural and Artificial Selection</a>, on the numerical formalism underpinning natural selection. It showed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_frequencies" title="Gene frequencies">gene frequencies</a> have direction and rates of change; and he pioneered the interaction of natural selection with <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">animal migration</a>. Haldane's book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Causes_of_Evolution" title="The Causes of Evolution">The Causes of Evolution</a></i> (1932), summarised these results, especially in its extensive <a href="/wiki/Addendum" title="Addendum">appendix</a>.</p>
<p>His contributions to statistical human genetics included: the first methods using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_likelihood" title="Maximum likelihood">maximum likelihood</a> for the estimation of human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linkage_map" title="Linkage map">linkage maps</a>; pioneering methods for estimating human mutation rates; the first estimates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rate_of_mutation" title="Rate of mutation">mutation rate</a> in humans, at 2 Ã 10<sup>â5</sup> mutations per gene per generation for the X-linked <a href="/wiki/Haemophilia" title="Haemophilia">haemophilia</a> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a>; and the first notion that there is a "cost of natural selection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Innes_Horticultural_Institution" title="John Innes Horticultural Institution">John Innes Horticultural Institution</a>, he developed the complicated linkage theory for polyploids;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jic.ac.uk_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jic.ac.uk-18">[18]</a></sup> and extended the idea of gene/enzyme relationships with the biochemical and genetic study of plant pigments. In his essay <i><a href="/wiki/On_Being_the_Right_Size" title="On Being the Right Size">On Being the Right Size</a></i> he outlines Haldane's principle, which states that the size very often defines what bodily equipment an animal must have: "Insects, being so small, do not have oxygen-carrying bloodstreams. What little oxygen their cells require can be absorbed by simple diffusion of air through their bodies. But being larger means an animal must have complicated oxygen pumping and distributing systems to reach all the cells." The <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_metaphor" title="Conceptual metaphor">conceptual metaphor</a> to animal allometry has been of use in <a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">energy economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">secession</a> ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haldane introduced the modern concept of <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a> in an eight-page article titled <i>The origin of life</i> in the <i>Rationalist Annual</i> in 1929,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lazcano_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-lazcano-47">[47]</a></sup> describing the primitive ocean as a "vast chemical laboratory" containing a mixture of inorganic compounds â like a "hot dilute soup" in which organic compounds could have formed. Under the solar energy the <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_event" title="Anoxic event">anoxic atmosphere</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> gave rise to a variety of organic compounds, "living or half-living things". The first molecules reacted with one another to produce more complex compounds, and ultimately the cellular components. At some point a kind of "oily film" was produced that enclosed <a href="/wiki/Self-replication" title="Self-replication">self-replicating</a> nucleic acids, thereby becoming the first cell. <a href="/wiki/John_Desmond_Bernal" title="John Desmond Bernal">J.D. Bernal</a> named the hypothesis biopoiesis or biopoesis, the process of living matter spontaneously evolving from self-replicating but lifeless molecules. Haldane further hypothesised that viruses were the intermediate entities between the prebiotic soup and the first cells. He asserted that prebiotic life would have been "in the virus stage for many millions of years before a suitable assemblage of elementary units was brought together in the first cell."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lazcano_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-lazcano-47">[47]</a></sup> The idea was generally dismissed as "wild speculation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 1924 <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Oparin" title="Alexander Oparin">Alexander Oparin</a> had suggested similar idea in <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, and in 1936 he introduced it to the English-speaking people. The experimental basis of the theory began in 1953 with the classic <a href="/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment" title="MillerâUrey experiment">MillerâUrey experiment</a>. Since then, the primordial soup theory became a dominant theory in the chemistry of life, and often is attributed as the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>He proposed in 1949 that genetic disorders in humans living in <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>-endemic regions provide a <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> with immunity to blood-borne haemophiles. He noted that mutations expressed in red blood cells, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_anemia" title="Sickle-cell anemia">sickle-cell anemia</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Thalassemia" title="Thalassemia">thalassemias</a>, were prevalent only in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_regions" title="Tropical regions">tropical regions</a> where malaria has been <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(epidemiology)" title="Endemic (epidemiology)">endemic</a> and were favourable traits for natural selection that protected individuals from receiving malarial infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This concept of resistance to malaria was eventually confirmed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthony_C._Allison" title="Anthony C. Allison">A.C. Allison</a> in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_views">Political views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Political views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Haldane became a <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> during the First World War; supported the <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">Second Spanish Republic</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>; and then became an open supporter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> Party in 1937; being a pragmatic and dialectical materialist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a>, writing many articles for the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Worker" title="Daily Worker">Daily Worker</a></i>. In <i><a href="/wiki/On_Being_the_Right_Size" title="On Being the Right Size">On Being the Right Size</a></i>, he wrote: "while <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalization</a> of certain industries is an obvious possibility in the largest of states, I find it no easier to picture a completely socialized British Empire or United States than an elephant turning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somersaults" title="Somersaults">somersaults</a> or a hippopotamus jumping a hedge."</p>
<p>In 1938, he proclaimed enthusiastically that "I think that Marxism is true." He joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party of Great Britain</a> in 1942. The first edition of his children's book <i>My Friend Mr. Leakey</i> contained an avowal of his party membership which was removed from later editions. He was pressed to speak out about the rise of <a href="/wiki/Lysenkoism" title="Lysenkoism">Lysenkoism</a> and the persecution of geneticists in the Soviet Union as anti-Darwinist and the denouncement of genetics as incompatible with <a href="/wiki/Dialectical_materialism" title="Dialectical materialism">dialectical materialism</a>. He shifted the focus to the United Kingdom and a criticism of the dependence of scientific research on financial patronage. In 1941 he wrote about the Soviet trial of his friend and fellow geneticist <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Vavilov" title="Nikolai Vavilov">Nikolai Vavilov</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The controversy among Soviet geneticists has been largely one between the academic scientist, represented by Vavilov and interested primarily in the collection of facts, and the man who wants results, represented by Lysenko. It has been conducted not with venom, but in a friendly spirit. Lysenko said (in the October discussions of 1939): 'The important thing is not to dispute; let us work in a friendly manner on a plan elaborated scientifically. Let us take up definite problems, receive assignments from the People's Commissariat of Agriculture of the USSR and fulfil them scientifically. Soviet genetics, as a whole, is a successful attempt at synthesis of these two contrasted points of view.'</p>
</blockquote>
<p>By the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> Haldane had become an explicit critic of the regime. He left the party in 1950, shortly after considering standing for <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> as a Communist Party candidate. He continued to admire <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, describing him in 1962 as "a very great man who did a very good job".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cochran_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-cochran-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Author_and_visionary">Author and visionary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Author and visionary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haldane was parodied as "the biologist too absorbed in his experiments to notice his friends bedding his wife" by his friend <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a> in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Antic_Hay" title="Antic Hay">Antic Hay</a></i> (1923). His essay <i><a href="/wiki/Daedalus;_or,_Science_and_the_Future" title="Daedalus; or, Science and the Future">Daedalus; or, Science and the Future</a></i> (1924), about <a href="/wiki/Ectogenesis" title="Ectogenesis">ectogenesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation"><i>in vitro</i> fertilisation</a> was an influence on Huxley's <i><a href="/wiki/Brave_New_World" title="Brave New World">Brave New World</a></i> (1932). Haldane's work was also admired by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Heard" title="Gerald Heard">Gerald Heard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Various essays on science were collected and published in a volume titled <i>Possible Worlds</i> in 1927. His book, <i>A.R.P. (Air Raid Precautions)</i> (1938) combined his physiological research into the effects of stress upon the human body with his experience of air raids during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> to provide a scientific explanation of the air raids that Britain was to endure during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.</p>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Olaf_Stapledon" title="Olaf Stapledon">Olaf Stapledon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kay_Ogden" title="Charles Kay Ogden">Charles Kay Ogden</a>, <a href="/wiki/I._A._Richards" title="I. A. Richards">I. A. Richards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a>, Haldane was accused by <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a>. Haldane criticised the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ransom_Trilogy" title="Ransom Trilogy">Ransom Trilogy</a> and Lewis for the "complete mischaracterisation of science, and his disparagement of the human race".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> He wrote a book for children titled <i>My Friend Mr Leakey</i> (1937), containing the stories "A Meal With a Magician", "A Day in the Life of a Magician", "Mr Leakey's Party", "Rats", "The Snake with the Golden Teeth", and "My Magic Collar Stud"; later editions featured illustrations by <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Blake" title="Quentin Blake">Quentin Blake</a>. Haldane edited <a href="/wiki/Gary_Botting" title="Gary Botting">Gary Botting</a>'s manuscript on the genetics of giant silk moths with margin notes.</p>
<p>In 1923, in a talk given in Cambridge titled "Science and the Future", Haldane, foreseeing the exhaustion of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> for power generation in Britain, proposed a network of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>-generating <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmills</a>. This is the first proposal of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen-based renewable energy economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his <i>An Autobiography in Brief</i>, written shortly before his death in India, Haldane named four close associates as those showing promise to become illustrious scientists: T. A. Davis, <a href="/wiki/Dronamraju_Krishna_Rao" title="Dronamraju Krishna Rao">K. R. Dronamraju</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suresh_Jayakar" title="Suresh Jayakar">S. D. Jayakar</a> and S. K. Roy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haldane was the first to have thought of the genetic basis for the <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloning</a> of humans, and eventually super-talented individuals. For this he coined the term "clone",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> though the synonym "clon" (from the Greek word <i>klon</i> meaning "twig") was used in horticulture since the 19th century. He introduced the term in his speech on "Biological Possibilities for the Human Species of the Next Ten Thousand Years" at the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciba_Foundation" title="Ciba Foundation">Ciba Foundation</a> Symposium on Man and his Future</i> in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._B._S._Haldane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haldane was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucl_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ucl-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Government" title="French Government">French Government</a> conferred him its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Order_of_the_Legion_of_Honour" title="National Order of the Legion of Honour">National Order of the Legion of Honour</a> in 1937. In 1952, he received the <a href="/wiki/Darwin_Medal" title="Darwin Medal">Darwin Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>. In 1956, he was awarded the Huxley Memorial Medal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropological_Institute_of_Great_Britain" title="Anthropological Institute of Great Britain">Anthropological Institute of Great Britain</a>. He received the <a href="/wiki/Feltrinelli_Prize" title="Feltrinelli Prize">Feltrinelli Prize</a> from <a href="/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei" title="Accademia dei Lincei">Accademia Nazionale dei Lincei</a> in 1961. He also received an Honorary Doctorate of Science, an Honorary Fellowship at <a href="/wiki/New_College,_Oxford" title="New College, Oxford">New College</a>, and the Kimber Award of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="US National Academy of Sciences">US National Academy of Sciences</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Linnean_Society_of_London" title="Linnean Society of London">Linnean Society of London</a>'s prestigious <a href="/wiki/Darwin%E2%80%93Wallace_Medal" title="DarwinâWallace Medal">DarwinâWallace Medal</a> in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vigyan_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-vigyan-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>He is famous for the (possibly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocryphal" title="Apocryphal">apocryphal</a>) response that he gave when some theologians asked him what could be inferred about the mind of the Creator from the works of His Creation: "An inordinate fondness for beetles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> This is in reference to there being over 400,000 known species of <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> in the world, and that this represents 40% of all known insect species (at the time of the statement, it was over half of all known insect species).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>He was often quoted for saying, "My own suspicion is that the universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we <i>can</i> suppose."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li>"It seems to me immensely unlikely that mind is a mere by-product of matter. For if my mental processes are determined wholly by the motions of atoms in my brain I have no reason to suppose that my beliefs are true. They may be sound chemically, but that does not make them sound logically. And hence I have no reason for supposing my brain to be composed of atoms."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
