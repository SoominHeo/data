<p><b>John Marc DeMatteis</b> (born December 15, 1953),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> usually cited as <b>J. M. DeMatteis</b>, is an American writer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_books" title="Comic books">comic books</a>, television and novels.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>J. M. DeMatteis's earliest aspirations were to be a rock musician and comic-book artist. He began playing in bands starting in the sixth grade, generally in the role of lead singer/songwriter/rhythm guitarist, and also wrote music reviews for a number of publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup> He began drawing at a young age, and was accepted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_the_Visual_Arts" title="School of the Visual Arts">School of the Visual Arts</a>. DeMatteis recalled, "...for some reason, I think it was financial, I ended up not going. Somewhere after that what little drawing skills I had began to atrophy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>DeMatteis then turned from drawing to writing. He got his start in comic books at <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> in the late 1970s. After a number of rejected submissions, his first accepted story was "The Lady-Killer Craves Blood", but it would not be published until years later,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup> in <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Mystery" title="House of Mystery">House of Mystery</a></i> #282. His first published story for the company was "The Blood Boat!" in <i><a href="/wiki/Weird_War_Tales" title="Weird War Tales">Weird War Tales</a></i> #70 (Dec. 1978).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCD_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCD-4">[4]</a></sup> He contributed to the company's line of <a href="/wiki/Horror_comics" title="Horror comics">horror comics</a> notably with the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Creature_Commandos" title="Creature Commandos">Creature Commandos</a> in <i>Weird War Tales</i> #93 (Nov. 1980)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/I%E2%80%A6Vampire" title="Iâ¦Vampire">Iâ¦Vampire</a> in <i>House of Mystery</i> #290 (March 1981).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He briefly wrote the <a href="/wiki/Aquaman" title="Aquaman">Aquaman</a> feature in <i><a href="/wiki/Adventure_Comics" title="Adventure Comics">Adventure Comics</a></i> as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> DeMatteis and artist <a href="/wiki/Brian_Bolland" title="Brian Bolland">Brian Bolland</a> produced a backup story titled "Falling Down to Heaven" in <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Xanadu" title="Madame Xanadu">Madame Xanadu</a></i>, DC's first attempt at marketing comics specifically to the "<a href="/wiki/Direct_market" title="Direct market">direct market</a>" of fans and collectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> DeMatteis had long been eager to work for <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, and following roughly a year in which editor-in-chief Jim Shooter kept him busy with odd jobs and fill-ins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup> in 1980 he began writing for Marvel on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Defenders_(comic_book)" title="The Defenders (comic book)">The Defenders</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and had lengthy runs on <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a></i>, paired with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penciler" title="Penciler">penciler</a> <a href="/wiki/Mike_Zeck" title="Mike Zeck">Mike Zeck</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCD_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCD-4">[4]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Team-Up" title="Marvel Team-Up">Marvel Team-Up</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After writing a negative review of the <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a>'s 1980 album <i><a href="/wiki/Go_to_Heaven" title="Go to Heaven">Go to Heaven</a></i> which was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i>, DeMatteis ended his career as a music critic. He explained, "Grateful Dead fans are like hardcore comic book fans, you know... and I know that when I sit down to write a review that I'm just some shmuck sitting down at a typewriter with an opinion - but then it's in print in something like <i>Rolling Stone</i>. I got all these letters, which I saved, from all these hardcore Grateful Dead fans - wounded. ... I said if I'm gonna review at all I'm not gonna write negative reviews anymore..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup> Around this time he also surrendered his professional career as a rock musician, after years of playing in New York City-based bands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAK38_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-DAK38-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984, DeMatteis and artist <a href="/wiki/Bob_Budiansky" title="Bob Budiansky">Bob Budiansky</a> produced a <i><a href="/wiki/Namor" title="Namor">Prince Namor</a></i> limited series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> DeMatteis and illustrator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jon_J._Muth" title="Jon J. Muth">Jon J. Muth</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Moonshadow_(comics)" title="Moonshadow (comics)">Moonshadow</a></i>, for Marvel's <a href="/wiki/Epic_Comics" title="Epic Comics">Epic</a> line: the groundbreaking story was the first fully painted series in American comics. DeMatteis followed this with the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a> graphic novel <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Into_Shamballa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Into Shamballa (page does not exist)">Into Shamballa</a></i> drawn by <a href="/wiki/Dan_Green_(artist)" title="Dan Green (artist)">Dan Green</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Blood:_A_Tale" title="Blood: A Tale">Blood: A Tale</a></i>, a hallucinatory vampire story drawn by <a href="/wiki/Kent_Williams_(artist)" title="Kent Williams (artist)">Kent Williams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCD_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-GCD-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1987, DeMatteis and Zeck re-teamed for the "<a href="/wiki/Kraven%27s_Last_Hunt" title="Kraven's Last Hunt">Kraven's Last Hunt</a>" arc that ran throughout Marvel's then three <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a> titles. The arc has been collected in multiple editions and remains one of the most popular, and respected, stories in Spider-Man's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moving back to DC, DeMatteis succeeded <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Conway" title="Gerry Conway">Gerry Conway</a> as writer of the superhero-team title <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League" title="Justice League">Justice League of America</a></i>. When that series was cancelled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in the wake of the company-wide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_crossover" title="Fictional crossover">crossover</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Legends_(comics)" title="Legends (comics)">Legends</a></i>, DeMatteis stayed through its relaunch as <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_International" title="Justice League International">Justice League International</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> scripting over the plots of <a href="/wiki/Keith_Giffen" title="Keith Giffen">Keith Giffen</a>.</p>
<p><i>JLI</i> took such lesser-known DC characters as <a href="/wiki/Martian_Manhunter" title="Martian Manhunter">Martian Manhunter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_Beetle" title="Blue Beetle">Blue Beetle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Booster_Gold" title="Booster Gold">Booster Gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mister_Miracle" title="Mister Miracle">Mister Miracle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Captain_Atom" title="Captain Atom">Captain Atom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Power_Girl" title="Power Girl">Power Girl</a> and turned the then-current preoccupation with "grim 'n' gritty" superheroes on its head. The lighthearted series emphasized the absurd aspects of people with strange powers, wearing colorful costumes, volunteering to fight evildoers. While the League had its serious side and often faced world-threatening <a href="/wiki/Villain" title="Villain">villains</a>, it also featured such characters as the lovably inept <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G%27Nort" title="G'Nort">G'Nort</a>, the worst <a href="/wiki/Green_Lantern" title="Green Lantern">Green Lantern</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Green_Lantern_Corps" title="Green Lantern Corps">Corps</a>; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mr._Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mr. Nebula (page does not exist)">Mr. Nebula</a>, the interplanetary decorator; the <a href="/wiki/Injustice_League" title="Injustice League">Injustice League</a>, a bunch of bumbling losers; and a flock of homicidal <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a> who had been hybridized with <a href="/wiki/Piranha" title="Piranha">piranhas</a>. The success of <i>Justice League International</i> led to a spinoff in 1989 titled <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_Europe" title="Justice League Europe">Justice League Europe</a></i> also co-written with Giffen and featuring art by <a href="/wiki/Bart_Sears" title="Bart Sears">Bart Sears</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Giffen/DeMatteis team worked on <i>Justice League</i> for five years and closed out their run with the "Breakdowns" storyline in 1991 and 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> DeMatteis scripted <i>Justice League</i> spin-offs such as solo series for <a href="/wiki/Mister_Miracle" title="Mister Miracle">Mister Miracle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Fate" title="Doctor Fate">Doctor Fate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCD_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-GCD-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Back at Marvel, DeMatteis again succeeded Conway, this time as writer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectacular_Spider-Man" title="The Spectacular Spider-Man">The Spectacular Spider-Man</a></i> in 1991, taking the series in a grimmer, more psychologically oriented direction. In collaboration with regular artist <a href="/wiki/Sal_Buscema" title="Sal Buscema">Sal Buscema</a>, DeMatteis' story arc "The Child Within" (#178-184) featured the return of the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Osborn" title="Harry Osborn">Harry Osborn</a> <a href="/wiki/Green_Goblin" title="Green Goblin">Green Goblin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Spider-Man's battle with the Goblin continued in "The Osborn Legacy" in #189<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and came to an end when Harry died in "The Best Of Enemies!" (#200).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, DeMatteis took over from <a href="/wiki/David_Michelinie" title="David Michelinie">David Michelinie</a> as writer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Spider-Man" title="The Amazing Spider-Man">The Amazing Spider-Man</a></i> #390-406 for a run that included the apparent death of <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Peter Parker</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Aunt_May" title="Aunt May">Aunt May</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and the beginnings of the "<a href="/wiki/Clone_Saga" title="Clone Saga">Clone Saga</a>" arc. DeMatteis as well worked on such characters as <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daredevil_(Marvel_Comics_character)" title="Daredevil (Marvel Comics character)">Daredevil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Man-Thing" title="Man-Thing">Man-Thing</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Surfer" title="Silver Surfer">Silver Surfer</a>.</p>
<p>DeMatteis helped launch DC's mature-audience <a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(DC_Comics)" title="Vertigo (DC Comics)">Vertigo</a> imprint, writing the graphic novels <i><a href="/wiki/Mercy_(Vertigo)" title="Mercy (Vertigo)">Mercy</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Farewell,_Moonshadow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Farewell, Moonshadow (page does not exist)">Farewell, Moonshadow</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Epic_Comics" title="Epic Comics">Epic Comics</a> series), the <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Last_One_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Last One (comics) (page does not exist)">The Last One</a></i>, and the 15-issue series <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seekers_Into_The_Mystery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seekers Into The Mystery (page does not exist)">Seekers Into The Mystery</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCD_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-GCD-4">[4]</a></sup> the story of a <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a> on a journey of <a href="/wiki/Self-discovery" title="Self-discovery">self-discovery</a> and the search for universal truths.</p>
<p>DeMatteis wrote an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autobiographical" title="Autobiographical">autobiographical</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digest-sized" title="Digest-sized">digest-sized</a> miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dreams" title="Brooklyn Dreams">Brooklyn Dreams</a></i>, published by DC's <a href="/wiki/Paradox_Press" title="Paradox Press">Paradox Press</a> imprint. DeMatteis' most personal work, it was later collected in one volume under the Vertigo imprint.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 2000s, DeMatteis redefined the <a href="/wiki/Spectre_(comics)" title="Spectre (comics)">Spectre</a>, through the character of Hal Jordan, as a spirit of redemption rather than of vengeance. DeMatteis co-scripted the "Gods of Gotham" storyline in <i><a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman" title="Wonder Woman">Wonder Woman</a></i> #164-166 (Jan.-March 2001) with <a href="/wiki/Phil_Jimenez" title="Phil Jimenez">Phil Jimenez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2003, with Giffen, he revived the Justice League International for the mini-series <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Buddies" title="Super Buddies">Formerly Known as the Justice League</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The series won Giffen, DeMatteis and artist <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Maguire_(artist)" title="Kevin Maguire (artist)">Kevin Maguire</a> an <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eisner2004_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-eisner2004-24">[24]</a></sup> The team followed this with "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Believe_It%27s_Not_the_Justice_League" title="I Can't Believe It's Not the Justice League">I Can't Believe It's Not the Justice League</a>" arc in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JLA_Classified" title="JLA Classified">JLA Classified</a></i> and, at Marvel, a five-issue run of <i>The Defenders</i>. In 2006, DeMatteis and Giffen began work on two original superhero comedy series, <i><a href="/wiki/Hero_Squared" title="Hero Squared">Hero Squared</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Planetary_Brigade" title="Planetary Brigade">Planetary Brigade</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Boom!_Studios" title="Boom! Studios">Boom! Studios</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbr-25">[25]</a></sup> DeMatteis teamed with veteran artist <a href="/wiki/Mike_Ploog" title="Mike Ploog">Mike Ploog</a> to create the <a href="/wiki/CrossGen" title="CrossGen">CrossGen</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> comic <i><a href="/wiki/Abadazad" title="Abadazad">Abadazad</a></i> (May 2004). The following year, Ploog and DeMatteis announced they were collaborating on a five-issue miniseries, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stardust_Kid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stardust Kid (page does not exist)">Stardust Kid</a></i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a> imprint Desperado Publishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbr_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbr-25">[25]</a></sup> The series moved to Boom! Studios in 2006.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> acquired <i>Abadazad</i> for its <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_Books" title="Hyperion Books">Hyperion Books for Children</a> imprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbr_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-cbr-25">[25]</a></sup> The first two books in the series â <i>Abadazad: The Road to Inconceivable</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <i>Abadazad: The Dream Thief</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> â were released June 2006. The third book â <i>Abadazad: The Puppet, The Professor and The Prophet</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> â was released in the United Kingdom in 2007.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In June 2010, DeMatteis's children's fantasy novel, <i>Imaginalis</i>, was published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katherine_Tegen_Books" title="Katherine Tegen Books">Katherine Tegen Books</a>, an imprint of <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, DeMatteis became editor-in-chief of Ardden Entertainment, guiding the launch of a new <i><a href="/wiki/Flash_Gordon#Comic_books" title="Flash Gordon">Flash Gordon</a></i> comic book series. In 2009, he wrote a five-issue comic book limited series, illustrated by Mike Cavallaro, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Times_of_Savior_28" title="The Life and Times of Savior 28">The Life and Times of Savior 28</a></i>, which was released by <a href="/wiki/IDW_Publishing" title="IDW Publishing">IDW Publishing</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He also wrote the <a href="/wiki/Metal_Men" title="Metal Men">Metal Men</a> back-up story in the new <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_Patrol" title="Doom Patrol">Doom Patrol</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and returned to Marvel Comics for a number of new Spider-Man stories. In 2010, DeMatteis reunited once again with frequent collaborator Keith Giffen for a run on the comic book series <i><a href="/wiki/Booster_Gold" title="Booster Gold">Booster Gold</a></i>. The two teamed on the <i><a href="/wiki/DC_Retroactive" title="DC Retroactive">DC Retroactive: JLA - The '90s</a></i> <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shot</a> in October 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Also in 2011, DeMatteis created the all-ages fantasy <i>The Adventures of Augusta Wind</i> for IDW Publishing. In 2013, he took over DC Comics' <i>Phantom Stranger</i> and launched the 12-issue <i>Larfleeze</i> series<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> with Giffen. DeMatteis became the writer of <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_Dark" title="Justice League Dark">Justice League Dark</a></i> in October 2013 and, again with Giffen, launched <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_3000" title="Justice League 3000">Justice League 3000</a></i> in December.</p>
<p>In 2015, DeMatteis teamed with animation legend Bruce Timm for <i>Justice League: Gods and Monsters,</i> a comic book prequel to the successful animated film. In 2016, Giffen and DeMatteis launched <i>Scooby Apocalypse</i> for DCâa more adult reimagining of the classic cartoonâand IDW published DeMatteis's <i>Augusta Wind</i> sequel <i>The Adventures of Augusta Wind: The Last Story.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._DeMatteis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DeMatteis has also written for television, having scripted episodes of <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1985_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1985 TV series)">the 1980s incarnation of <i>The Twilight Zone</i></a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_syndication" title="TV syndication">syndicated</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Superboy_(TV_series)" title="Superboy (TV series)">The Adventures of Superboy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Earth:_Final_Conflict" title="Earth: Final Conflict">Earth: Final Conflict</a></i>, as well as for the <a href="/wiki/Animated_series" title="Animated series">animated series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Real_Ghostbusters" title="The Real Ghostbusters">The Real Ghostbusters</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_Unlimited" title="Justice League Unlimited">Justice League Unlimited</a></i>, <i>Legion of Super-Heroes</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Brave_and_the_Bold" title="Batman: The Brave and the Bold">Batman: The Brave and the Bold</a></i>, <i>Ben 10: Ultimate Alien,</i> <i>Sym-Bionic Titan,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/ThunderCats_(2011_TV_series)" title="ThunderCats (2011 TV series)">ThunderCats</a></i> and <i>Teen Titans Go!</i> DeMatteis also wrote the 2015 animated DTV movie <i>Batman vs. Robin</i> and its 2016 sequel, <i>Batman: Bad Blood.</i> The same year, DeMatteis wrote multiple episodes of Cartoon Network's <i>Be Cool, Scooby-Doo.</i></p>
<p>Also a musician, DeMatteis released one album in the late 1990s, <i>How Many Lifetimes?</i>.</p>
