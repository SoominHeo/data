<p><b>JMicron Technology Corporation</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">智微科技</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zhìwēi Kējì</span></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabless" title="Fabless">fabless</a> technology design house based in <a href="/wiki/Hsinchu" title="Hsinchu">Hsinchu</a>, Taiwan. As a manufacturer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a>, they produce controller chips for bridge devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JMicron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001-2007">2001-2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JMicron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2001-2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company was founded in September 2001 and its headquarters is in Hsinchu, Taiwan. The company also operates an advanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26D" title="R&amp;D">R&amp;D</a> center based out of <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States.</p>
<p>In 2002, the company begins development of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USB_2.0" title="USB 2.0">USB 2.0</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SATA" title="SATA">SATA</a> bridge controller technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jmicron_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jmicron-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>JMicron begins developing SSD technology in 2006, launching their first generation SSD controller, the JMF601A/602A, towards the end of 2007.</p>
<p>JMicron's <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_drive" title="Solid-state drive">SSD</a> <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory_controller" title="Flash memory controller">controller</a> is widely adopted by many SSD manufacturers such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASUS_Eee_PC" title="ASUS Eee PC">ASUS Eee PC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corsair_Memory" title="Corsair Memory">Corsair</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/OCZ" title="OCZ">OCZ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transcend_Information" title="Transcend Information">Transcend</a>. JMicron was one of the first<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> companies to provide an SSD controller chip to these companies, allowing them to produce reasonably priced <a href="/wiki/Multi-level_cell" title="Multi-level cell">MLC</a> SSDs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_to_Present">2008 to Present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JMicron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2008 to Present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 2008, a write latency issue is discovered with the JMF601A/602A that occurs when system data is nearly full and the host continues to write data. Soon after, the company released the JMF601B/602B to address the write latency problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Also in 2008, JMicron showcases a number of peripheral products. This includes the JMB353, a hi-speed USB &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1394a" title="1394a">1394a</a> to SATA II external HDD controller chip; this device is the first to feature an integrated 1394a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHY" title="PHY">PHY</a> chip. Also included in the showcase is the JMB38x series, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCIe" title="PCIe">PCIe</a> to <a href="/wiki/Card_reader" title="Card reader">card reader</a> &amp; 1394 controller chip, the JMB211, a <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">Gigabit Ethernet</a> PHY controller chip, the JMB352, a hi-speed USB &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESATA" title="ESATA">eSATA</a> to 2-port SATA II external HDD controller chip, and the JMB325, a 1- to 5-port SATA II <a href="/wiki/Port_multiplier" title="Port multiplier">port multiplier</a> with hardware <a href="/wiki/RAID" title="RAID">RAID</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, the JMF612 is released, and sees popularity throughout 2010 in its use in a number of industry SSDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In particular, 2009 was a landmark year for JMicron in that the company achieved the top ranking as a supplier for external HDD and SATA-PATA bridge controllers, shipping roughly 35 million and 15 million units, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jmicron_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jmicron-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2010, JMicron was scheduled to list on the Taiwanese <a href="/wiki/Gre_Tai_Securities_Market" title="Gre Tai Securities Market">Gre Tai Securities Market</a> (GTSM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jmicron_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jmicron-2">[2]</a></sup> Also in 2010, a <a href="/wiki/Gartner" title="Gartner">Gartner</a> tandem research report reveals JMicron to be first in interface controller chip market share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computershow_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Computershow-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2010 stolen private keys certificates were used for digitally sign rootkit drivers in <a href="/wiki/Stuxnet" title="Stuxnet">Stuxnet</a> virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eset_microscope_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-eset_microscope-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After developing its own physical layer and high speed technology over the preceding years because of flat growth in the long term, JMicron diversifies from the base notebook and motherboard controller business. 2011 is characterized by JMicron winning orders from <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital" title="Western Digital">Western Digital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Electronics" title="Samsung Electronics">Samsung Electronics</a> for JMicron's <a href="/wiki/USB_3.0" title="USB 3.0">USB 3.0</a> HDD controller, with net sales projected to grow 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2011, the JMF661 is released as a third generation JMicron SSD controller, and is shown to be an effective entry-level product. Shortly thereafter, in 2013, the JMF667 series is released as a 256 GB capacity, 512 MB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDR3" title="DDR3">DDR3</a> external cache SSD controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Analysis in 2013 reveals that SSD controller technology and high-speed transmission interface IC accounts for 29% and 67% of JMicron's sales, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jmicron_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jmicron-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2014, JMicron announced the JMF670 and JMF670H. Both are 4-channel SATA SSD controllers capable of supporting up to 512 GB of storage capacity. Production was to begin in July 2014. In the same announcement, they showcased the JMF810, a PCIe Gen II 2-lane controller, and the JMF811, a PCIe Gen II 4-lane SSD controller. These controllers are designed for speeds of up to 1.5 GB/s in sequential read and 1.2 GB/s in sequential write. Another announcement was the JMS577, which is a USB 3.0 to SATA VI Gb/s bridge controller. The company also showcased their <a href="/wiki/USB_3.1" title="USB 3.1">USB 3.1</a> technology, which is expected to achieve data transmission speeds of 10 Gbit/s—doubling the speed of the previous USB Gen III controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Among these announcements included a single-chip, dual-port SATA III RAID/CLONE program within the JMS561/562/561U product line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computershow_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Computershow-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout 2014, JMicron's JMF667H was reviewed by a number of online review sites like <a href="/wiki/Tom%27s_Hardware" title="Tom's Hardware">Tom's Hardware</a>, <a href="/wiki/AnandTech" title="AnandTech">AnandTech</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TweakTown&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TweakTown (page does not exist)">TweakTown</a>.</p>
<p>In June of 2016, JMicron spun off its SSD division to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maxiotek_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maxiotek Corporation (page does not exist)">Maxiotek Corporation</a>, a Taiwan-based company designs and markets SSD controller products. Meanwhile, JMicron released the JMS576, its first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USB_Type-C" title="USB Type-C">USB Type-C</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USB_3.1_Gen_1" title="USB 3.1 Gen 1">USB 3.1 Gen 1</a> to SATA 6Gbps bridge controller.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_Reception">Market Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JMicron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Market Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_controller" title="Flash controller">flash controllers</a> were first offered to SSD manufacturers in 2008, JMicron's early JMF601 and JMF602 models were reported to have issues with write latency, causing a stuttering problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The performance problem was attributed to the small buffer size used in the controller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> After several corrective releases, JMicron released the JMF667H, which to date has received generally positive reviews, being cited as both competitive and budget-friendly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> when used with the correct type of NAND.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> For instance, when paired with Toshiba's A19 NAND, the JMF667H has been shown to be capable of achieving the following metrics: 500 MB/s for 128 KB sequential read, 450 MB/s for 128 KB sequential write, and reaching 80,000 4 KB IOPS for both random read and write.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chrisryan_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chrisryan-23">[23]</a></sup> The JMF667H has also been found to have very low power consumption, with active idle power consumption in the 0.2-0.4 W range depending on the capacity of the controller, as well as an average power consumption rating of 0.36-0.78 W when examined in <a href="/wiki/PCMark" title="PCMark">PCMark</a> 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chrisryan_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chrisryan-23">[23]</a></sup> Certain drives driven by the JMF667H controller, such as the Kingfast C-Drive F8, have achieved high scores on independent review sites—for example, 90% on TweakTown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
