<p><b>Product Red</b>, styled as <b>(PRODUCT)<sup>RED</sup></b>, is a licensed <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> that seeks to engage the private sector in raising awareness and funds to help eliminate HIV/AIDS in Africa. It is licensed to partner companies including <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> (UK), <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Converse_(company)" title="Converse (company)">Converse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Head_(company)" title="Head (company)">Head</a>, Bugaboo, <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Classics" title="Penguin Classics">Penguin Classics</a> (UK &amp; International), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gap_(clothing_retailer)" title="Gap (clothing retailer)">Gap</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emporio_Armani" title="Emporio Armani">Armani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hallmark_Cards" title="Hallmark Cards">Hallmark</a> (US), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAP_AG" title="SAP AG">SAP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beats_Electronics" title="Beats Electronics">Beats Electronics (Beats by Dr. Dre)</a>, and Supercell and the Youtuber Pewdiepie. The concept was founded in 2006 by <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontman" title="Frontman">frontman</a> and activist, <a href="/wiki/Bono" title="Bono">Bono</a>, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Sargent_Shriver_III" title="Robert Sargent Shriver III">Bobby Shriver</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_Campaign" title="One Campaign">ONE</a>/<a href="/wiki/DATA" title="DATA">DATA</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Global_Fund_to_Fight_AIDS,_Tuberculosis_and_Malaria" title="The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria">The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria</a> is a recipient of (RED) money.</p>
<p>As part of a new business model, each partner company creates a product with the Product Red logo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In return for the opportunity to increase revenue through the Product Red license, a 50 percent of the profit gained by each partner is donated to the Global Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As Product Red is a <a href="/wiki/Privately_held_company" title="Privately held company">private company</a>, a portion of the contributions received from the partner brands is assigned as <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a>. Such an amalgamation of <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_aid" title="Humanitarian aid">humanitarian aid</a> and for-profit businesses is one example of "<a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumerism</a>".</p>
<p>In 2012, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_Campaign" title="One Campaign">ONE</a> acquired (RED) as a division of ONE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Both organizations were co-founded by Bono and Bobby Shriver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Global_Fund">The Global Fund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Global Fund">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Created in 2002, <a href="/wiki/The_Global_Fund_to_Fight_AIDS,_Tuberculosis_and_Malaria" title="The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria">the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria</a> supports large-scale prevention, treatment and care programs for these three infectious diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Today, a quarter of all international funding for HIV/AIDS-related programs, over half for tuberculosis, and almost three-quarters for malaria worldwide comes from The Global Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The concept of “performance-based funding” is central to the organization and only those grant recipients who can demonstrate measurable and effective results from the monies received will be able to receive additional financing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 100% of the funds generated by Red partners and events goes to Global Fund programs that provide medical care and support services for people affected by HIV/AIDS in Africa. No overhead is taken by either Red or the Global Fund. Red is the largest private sector donor to the Global Fund, and has generated over $150 million for HIV/AIDS programs in Africa. In November 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jony_Ive" title="Jony Ive">Jony Ive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Newson" title="Marc Newson">Marc Newson</a> hosted an auction at Sotheby's to raise millions for the fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The event was attended by major celebrities including Bono, The Edge, Hayden Panettiere, and Courtney Love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

It is reported that Apple has raised more than $65 million for (RED) since 2006. Apple has now designed PRODUCT red Apps. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Product Red has been criticized for not having an effect proportional to the advertising investment, for being much less efficient than direct charitable contribution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-africom_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-africom-21">[21]</a></sup> and for having a lack of transparency with regards to the amount of money going to charity as a percentage of every purchase. Some critics argue that a retail middleman between donor and charity is unnecessary; donors should just give.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Like with the Gap website and Campaign Red, some argued that it encouraged consumption of the products, thus, encouraging companies to use the product for publicity, rather than social responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-23">[23]</a></sup> While Product Red has helped give funds and attention to the problem, it does not form a relationship between the donors and recipients. Scholars argue that this sacrifices the purpose of movements such as Product Red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-24">[24]</a></sup> Jessica Wirgau, professor at the Virginia Politechnic institute stated that, "Red not only misses the opportunity to promote civic engagement with its audience but also.... gives corporations the power to decide which causes should be supported and to what degree".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-24">[24]</a></sup> Another critique is that Product Red's expansion into traditional fundraising techniques, such as art auctions, undermines its claim to be a different and more sustainable approach to raising money for AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Other critics have pointed out that its emphasis on funding treatment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> sufferers meant that large amounts of the money will ultimately end up with pharmaceutical companies "unwilling to distribute their drugs for free".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-26">[26]</a></sup> Many accuse the campaign of profiting by using diseases as a marketing vehicle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-26">[26]</a></sup> for being "cause branding" rather than corporate social responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Stanford_Social_Innovation_Review" title="Stanford Social Innovation Review">Stanford Social Innovation Review</a></i>, Mark Rosenman wrote that it was an "example of the corporate world aligning its operations with its central purpose of increasing shareholder profit, except this time it is being cloaked in the patina of philanthropy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Labor_Committee_for_Worker_and_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Labor Committee for Worker and Human Rights (page does not exist)">National Labor Committee for Worker and Human Rights</a> criticised Product Red for its links with Gap,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which was historically a target of anti-sweatshop activists, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-sweatshop" title="Anti-sweatshop">anti-sweatshop</a> organisation <a href="/wiki/Labour_Behind_the_Label" title="Labour Behind the Label">Labour Behind the Label</a> states that Gap has "come further than many"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbtl_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbtl-30">[30]</a></sup> clothing companies to counter exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Gap's Product Red clothes are made in <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a>, rather than simply for the best price in China (this goes beyond the requirements of Product Red). Labour Behind the Label criticises Product Red for not requiring more measures to protect the rights of the workers who make their products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbtl_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-lbtl-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Data released in 2007 by <i><a href="/wiki/Advertising_Age" title="Advertising Age">Advertising Age</a></i> claimed retail participants in Product Red including Gap, Motorola and Apple had invested $100 million in advertising and raised only $18 million for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Global_Fund" title="The Global Fund">The Global Fund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-africom_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-africom-21">[21]</a></sup> In July 2010, however, Red claimed to have raised over $150 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an attempt to combat the critics, particularly regarding Product Red's Transparency, around 2008, they implemented a calculator to show consumers how many doses of treatment would be received with the purchase of a Red Product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-33">[33]</a></sup> With this, some have stated that, "the campaign offers the illusion of activism without requiring behavioral changes or political engagement". While this critic shows that there are negative aspects of this program, he also states that "it finds a way to generate something positive out of the currently existent system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:22-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
