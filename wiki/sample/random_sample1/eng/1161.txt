<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">SCMaglev</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>SCMaglev</b> (superconducting maglev) (formerly called the <b>MLU</b>) is a magnetic levitation (<a href="/wiki/Maglev" title="Maglev">maglev</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> system based on the principle of magnetic repulsion between the track and the cars. The <a href="/wiki/Central_Japan_Railway_Company" title="Central Japan Railway Company">Central Japan Railway Company</a> (JR Central) and the company's <a href="/wiki/Railway_Technical_Research_Institute" title="Railway Technical Research Institute">Railway Technical Research Institute</a> developed the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrc-hsr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jrc-hsr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osti_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-osti-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 21 April 2015, a manned seven-car <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L0_series" title="L0 series">L0 series</a> SCMaglev train reached a speed of 603 km/h (375 mph), less than a week after the same train clocked 590 km/h (370 mph), breaking the previous <a href="/wiki/Land_speed_record_for_rail_vehicles" title="Land speed record for rail vehicles">land speed record for rail vehicles</a> of 581 km/h (361 mph) set by a JR Central MLX01 maglev train in December 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian20150421_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian20150421-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The SCMaglev system uses an <a href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_suspension" title="Electrodynamic suspension">electrodynamic suspension</a> (EDS) system. Installed in the trains' bogies are <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> magnets, and the guideways contain two sets of metal coils.</p>
<p>The current levitation system utilizes a series of coils wound into a "figure 8" along both walls of the guideway. These coils are also cross-connected underneath the track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osti_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-osti-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>As the train accelerates, the magnetic fields of its superconducting magnets induce a current into these coils due to the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">magnetic field induction effect</a>. If the train were centered with the coils, the electrical potential would be balanced and no currents would be induced. However, as the train runs on rubber wheels at relatively low speeds, the magnetic fields are positioned below the center of the coils, causing the electrical potential to no longer be balanced. This creates a reactive magnetic field opposing the superconducting magnet's pole (in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Lenz%27s_law" title="Lenz's law">Lenz's law</a>), and a pole above that attracts it. Once the train reaches 150 km/h (93 mph), there is sufficient current flowing to lift the train 100 mm (4 in) above the guideway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osti_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-osti-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>These coils also generate guiding and stabilizing forces. Because they are cross-connected underneath the guideway, if the train moves off-center, currents are induced into the connections that correct its positioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osti_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-osti-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>SCMaglev also utilizes a <a href="/wiki/Linear_motor" title="Linear motor">linear synchronous motor</a> (LSM) propulsion system, which powers a second set of coils in the guideway.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Japanese_National_Railways" title="Japanese National Railways">Japanese National Railways</a> (JNR) began research on a linear propulsion railway system in 1962 with the goal of developing a train that could travel between <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> in one hour. Shortly after <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a> patented superconducting magnetic levitation technology in the United States in 1969, JNR announced development of the its own superconducting maglev (SCMaglev) system. The railway made its first successful SCMaglev run on a short track at its Railway Technical Research Institute in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usjmaglev_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-usjmaglev-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miyazaki_test_track">Miyazaki test track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Miyazaki test track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1977, SCMaglev testing moved to a new 7 km test track in <a href="/wiki/Hy%C5%ABga,_Miyazaki" title="Hyūga, Miyazaki">Hyūga, Miyazaki</a>. By 1980, the track was modified from a "reverse-T" shape to the "U" shape used today. In April 1987, JNR was privatized, and <a href="/wiki/Central_Japan_Railway_Company" title="Central Japan Railway Company">Central Japan Railway Company</a> (JR Central) took over SCMaglev development.</p>
<p>In 1989, JR Central decided to build a better testing facility with tunnels, steeper gradients, and curves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usjmaglev_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-usjmaglev-5">[5]</a></sup> After the company moved maglev tests to the new facility, the company's Railway Technical Research Institute began to allow testing of <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_train" title="Ground effect train">ground effect trains</a>, an alternate technology based on <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic</a> interaction between the train and the ground, at the Miyazaki Test Track in 1999.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yamanashi_maglev_test_line">Yamanashi maglev test line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yamanashi maglev test line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%AB%C5%8D_Shinkansen#Miyazaki_and_Yamanashi_Test_Tracks" title="Chūō Shinkansen">Chūō Shinkansen § Miyazaki and Yamanashi Test Tracks</a></div>
<p>Construction of the Yamanashi maglev test line began in 1990. The 18.4 km (11.4 mi) "priority section" of the line in <a href="/wiki/Tsuru,_Yamanashi" title="Tsuru, Yamanashi">Tsuru, Yamanashi</a>, opened in 1997. MLX01 trains were tested there from 1997 to fall 2011, when the facility was closed to extend the line to 42.8 km (26.6 mi) and to upgrade it to commercial specifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrc-data2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jrc-data2012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, Japan's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Land,_Infrastructure,_Transport_and_Tourism" title="Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism">Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism</a> decided that the SCMaglev system was ready for commercial operation. In 2011, the ministry gave JR Central permission to operate the SCMaglev system on their planned <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%AB%C5%8D_Shinkansen" title="Chūō Shinkansen">Chūō Shinkansen</a> linking Tokyo and <a href="/wiki/Nagoya" title="Nagoya">Nagoya</a> by 2027, and to Osaka by 2045. Environmental impact assessments are currently underway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrc-data2012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-jrc-data2012-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USA">USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2010, JR Central has promoted the SCMaglev system in international markets, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a> of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrc-hsr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jrc-hsr-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> met with U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> and offered to provide the first portion of the SC Maglev track free, a distance of approximately 40 miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SCMaglev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late 2015, JR Central partnered with <a href="/wiki/Mitsui" title="Mitsui">Mitsui</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> in Australia to form a joint venture named CLARA ("Consolidated Land and Rail Australia") to provide a commercial funding model using private investors that could build the SC Maglev (linking Sydney, Canberra and Melbourne), create 8 new self-sustaining inland cities linked to the high speed connection, and contribute to the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
