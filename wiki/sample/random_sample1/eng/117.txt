<p>The <b>Irish Rebellion of 1798</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Éirí Amach 1798</i></span>), also known as the <b>United Irishmen Rebellion</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Éirí Amach na nÉireannach Aontaithe</i></span>), was an uprising against <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">rule in Ireland</a> lasting from May to September 1798. The <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">United Irishmen</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Republicanism" title="Irish Republicanism">republican</a> revolutionary group influenced by the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French</a> revolutions, were the main organising force behind the rebellion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1691 and the end of the <a href="/wiki/Williamite_War_in_Ireland" title="Williamite War in Ireland">Williamite War</a>, Ireland had chiefly been controlled by the minority <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a> constituting members of the established <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> loyal to the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British Crown</a>. It governed through a form of institutionalised <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarianism</a> codified in the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws_(Ireland)" title="Penal Laws (Ireland)">Penal Laws</a> which discriminated against both the majority <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholic</a> population and non-Anglican Protestants (for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterians" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a>). In the late 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> elements among the ruling class were inspired by the example of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> (1775–1783) and sought to form common cause with the Catholic populace to achieve reform and greater autonomy from Britain. As in England, the majority of Protestants, as well as all Catholics, were barred from voting because they did not pass a property threshold. Another grievance was that Ireland, although nominally a sovereign kingdom governed by the monarch and Parliament of the island, in reality had less independence than most of Britain's North American colonies, due to a series of laws enacted by the English, such as Poynings' law of 1494 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declaratory_Act_of_1719" title="Declaratory Act of 1719">Declaratory Act of 1719</a>, the former of which gave the English veto power over Irish legislation, and the latter of which gave the British the right to legislate for the kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>When France joined the Americans in support of their <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>, London called for volunteers to join <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militias</a> to defend Ireland against the threat of invasion from France (since regular British forces had been dispatched to America). Many thousands joined the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers_(18th_century)" title="Irish Volunteers (18th century)">Irish Volunteers</a>. In 1782 they used their newly powerful position to force the Crown to grant the landed Ascendancy self-rule and a more independent parliament ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grattan%27s_Parliament" title="Grattan's Parliament">Grattan's Parliament</a>"). The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Patriot_Party" title="Irish Patriot Party">Irish Patriot Party</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Grattan" title="Henry Grattan">Henry Grattan</a>, pushed for greater enfranchisement. In 1793 parliament passed laws allowing Catholics with some property to vote, but they could neither be elected nor appointed as state officials. Liberal elements of the Ascendancy seeking a greater franchise for the people, and an end to religious discrimination, were further inspired by the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, which had taken place in a Catholic country.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_of_United_Irishmen">Society of United Irishmen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Society of United Irishmen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prospect of reform inspired a small group of Protestant liberals in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> to found the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">Society of United Irishmen</a> in 1791. The organisation crossed the religious divide with a membership comprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterians" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodists" title="Methodists">Methodists</a>, other Protestant "<a href="/wiki/Dissenter" title="Dissenter">dissenters</a>" groups, and some from the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a>. The Society openly put forward policies of further democratic reforms and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_Emancipation" title="Catholic Emancipation">Catholic emancipation</a>, reforms which the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Irish Parliament</a> had little intention of granting. The outbreak of <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">war with France</a> earlier in 1793, following the execution of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a>, forced the Society underground and toward armed insurrection with French aid. The avowed intent of the United Irishmen was to "break the connection with England"; the organisation spread throughout Ireland and had at least 200,000 members by 1797.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It linked up with Catholic agrarian resistance groups, known as the <a href="/wiki/Defenders_(Ireland)" title="Defenders (Ireland)">Defenders</a>, who had started raiding houses for arms in early 1793.</p>

<p>Despite their growing strength, the United Irish leadership decided to seek military help from the <a href="/wiki/French_Directory" title="French Directory">French revolutionary government</a> and to postpone the rising until French troops landed in Ireland. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone" title="Theobald Wolfe Tone">Theobald Wolfe Tone</a>, leader of the United Irishmen, travelled in exile from the United States to France to press the case for intervention.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aborted_invasion_.281796.29">Aborted invasion (1796) <span id="Bantry"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aborted invasion (1796)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tone's efforts succeeded with the dispatch of the <a href="/wiki/Exp%C3%A9dition_d%27Irlande" title="Expédition d'Irlande">Expédition d'Irlande</a>, and he accompanied a force of 14,000 French veteran troops under <a href="/wiki/Lazare_Hoche" title="Lazare Hoche">General Hoche</a> which arrived off the coast of Ireland at <a href="/wiki/Bantry_Bay" title="Bantry Bay">Bantry Bay</a> in December 1796 after eluding the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>; however, unremitting storms, indecisiveness of leaders and poor seamanship all combined to prevent a landing. The despairing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone" title="Theobald Wolfe Tone">Wolfe Tone</a> remarked, "England has had its luckiest escape since the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Armada</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The French fleet was forced to return home and the veteran army intended to spearhead the invasion of Ireland split up and was sent to fight in other theatres of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-insurgency_and_repression">Counter-insurgency and repression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Counter-insurgency and repression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Establishment responded to widespread disorder by launching a counter-campaign of <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> from 2 March 1798. It used tactics including house burnings, torture of captives, <a href="/wiki/Pitchcapping" title="Pitchcapping">pitchcapping</a> and murder, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> as it was the one area of Ireland where large numbers of Catholics and Protestants (mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterians" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a>) had effected common cause. In May 1797 the military in Belfast also violently suppressed the newspaper of the United Irishmen, the <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Star_(newspaper_of_the_Society_of_United_Irishmen)" title="Northern Star (newspaper of the Society of United Irishmen)">Northern Star</a></i>.</p>

<p>The British establishment recognised <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarianism</a> as a divisive tool to employ against the Protestant United Irishmen in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Divide_and_rule" title="Divide and rule">divide and conquer</a></i> method of colonial dominion was officially encouraged by the Government. Brigadier-General C.E. Knox wrote to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Lake" title="General Lake">General Lake</a> (who was responsible for Ulster): "I have arranged ... to increase the animosity between the Orangemen and the United Irishmen, or liberty men as they call themselves. Upon that animosity depends the safety of the centre counties of the North."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/John_FitzGibbon,_1st_Earl_of_Clare" title="John FitzGibbon, 1st Earl of Clare">The Earl of Clare</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor_of_Ireland" title="Lord Chancellor of Ireland">Lord Chancellor of Ireland</a>, wrote to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" title="Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council">Privy Council</a> in June 1798, "In the North nothing will keep the rebels quiet but the conviction that where treason has broken out the rebellion is merely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popish" title="Popish">popish</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> expressing the hope that the Presbyterian republicans might not rise if they thought that rebellion was supported only by Catholics.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">Loyalists</a> across Ireland had organised in support of the Government; many supplied recruits and vital local intelligence through the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Order" title="Orange Order">Orange Order</a> in 1795. The Government's founding of <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_College,_Maynooth" title="St Patrick's College, Maynooth">Maynooth College</a> in the same year, and the French <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic_(18th_century)" title="Roman Republic (18th century)">conquest of Rome</a> earlier in 1798 both helped secure the opposition of the Roman Catholic Church to rebellion; with a few individual exceptions, the Church was firmly on the side of the Crown throughout the entire period of turmoil.</p>
<p>In March 1798 intelligence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informants" title="Informants">informants</a> amongst the United Irish caused the Government to sweep up most of their leadership in raids in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">Martial law</a> was imposed over most of the country and its unrelenting brutality put the United Irish organisation under severe pressure to act before it was too late. A rising in <a href="/wiki/Cahir" title="Cahir">Cahir</a>, County Tipperary broke out in response, but was quickly crushed by the High Sheriff, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judkin-Fitzgerald_Baronets" title="Judkin-Fitzgerald Baronets">Col. Thomas Judkin-Fitzgerald</a>. Militants led by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Neilson" title="Samuel Neilson">Samuel Neilson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Edward_FitzGerald" title="Lord Edward FitzGerald">Lord Edward FitzGerald</a> dominated the rump United Irish leadership and planned to rise without French aid, fixing the date for 23 May.</p>

<p>The initial plan was to take <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, with the counties bordering Dublin to rise in support and prevent the arrival of reinforcements followed by the rest of the country who were to tie down other garrisons. The signal to rise was to be spread by the interception of the mail coaches from Dublin. However, last-minute intelligence from informants provided the Government with details of rebel assembly points in Dublin and a huge force of military occupied them barely one hour before rebels were to assemble. The Army then arrested most of the rebel leaders in the city. Deterred by the military, the gathering groups of rebels quickly dispersed, abandoning the intended rallying points, and dumping their weapons in the surrounding lanes. In addition, the plan to intercept the mail coaches miscarried, with only the <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>-bound coach halted at <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_County_Kildare" title="Johnstown, County Kildare">Johnstown</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Naas" title="Naas">Naas</a>, on the first night of the rebellion.</p>
<p>Although the planned nucleus of the rebellion had imploded, the surrounding districts of Dublin rose as planned and were swiftly followed by most of the counties surrounding Dublin. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battles_of_Irish_rebellion_1798" title="Battles of Irish rebellion 1798">clashes</a> of the rebellion took place just after dawn on 24 May. Fighting quickly spread throughout Leinster, with the heaviest fighting taking place in <a href="/wiki/County_Kildare" title="County Kildare">County Kildare</a> where, despite the Army successfully beating off almost every rebel attack, the rebels gained control of much of the county as military forces in Kildare were ordered to withdraw to <a href="/wiki/Naas" title="Naas">Naas</a> for fear of their isolation and destruction as at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Prosperous" title="Battle of Prosperous">Prosperous</a>. However, rebel defeats at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Carlow" title="Battle of Carlow">Carlow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tara_Hill" title="Battle of Tara Hill">hill of Tara</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">County Meath</a>, effectively ended the rebellion in those counties. In <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>, news of the rising spread panic and fear among loyalists; they responded by massacring rebel suspects held in custody at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massacre_of_Dunlavin_Green" title="Massacre of Dunlavin Green">Dunlavin Green</a> and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnew_massacre" title="Carnew massacre">Carnew</a>. A baronet, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Edward_Crosbie" title="Sir Edward Crosbie">Sir Edward Crosbie</a>, was found guilty of leading the rebellion in Carlow and executed for <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>, large numbers rose but chiefly engaged in a bloody rural guerrilla war with the military and loyalist forces. General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Holt_(rebel)" title="Joseph Holt (rebel)">Joseph Holt</a> led up to 1,000 men in the <a href="/wiki/Wicklow_Mountains" title="Wicklow Mountains">Wicklow Mountains</a> and forced the British to commit substantial forces to the area until his capitulation in October.</p>
<p>In the north-east, mostly Presbyterian rebels led by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Joy_McCracken" title="Henry Joy McCracken">Henry Joy McCracken</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> rose in <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a> on 6 June. They briefly held most of the county, but the rising there collapsed following <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Antrim" title="Battle of Antrim">defeat</a> at <a href="/wiki/Antrim,_County_Antrim" title="Antrim, County Antrim">Antrim town</a>. In <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, after initial success at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Saintfield" title="Battle of Saintfield">Saintfield</a>, rebels led by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Munro_(United_Irishman)" title="Henry Munro (United Irishman)">Henry Munro</a> were defeated in the longest battle of the rebellion at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ballynahinch" title="Battle of Ballynahinch">Ballynahinch</a>.</p>
<p>The rebels had most success in the south-eastern county of <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">Wexford</a> where they seized control of the county, but a series of bloody defeats at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Ross_(1798)" title="Battle of New Ross (1798)">Battle of New Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Arklow" title="Battle of Arklow">Battle of Arklow</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunclody" title="Battle of Bunclody">Battle of Bunclody</a> prevented the effective spread of the rebellion beyond the county borders. 20,000 troops eventually poured into Wexford and defeated the rebels at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vinegar_Hill" title="Battle of Vinegar Hill">Battle of Vinegar Hill</a> on 21 June. The dispersed rebels spread in two columns through the midlands, <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny" title="Kilkenny">Kilkenny</a>, and finally towards Ulster. The last remnants of these forces fought on until their final defeat on 14 July at the battles of Knightstown Bog, <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">County Meath</a> and Ballyboughal, <a href="/wiki/County_Dublin" title="County Dublin">County Dublin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atrocities">Atrocities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Atrocities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:184px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hanging.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="267" data-file-width="182" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/Hanging.gif" width="182"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hanging.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Half-hanging" title="Half-hanging">Half-hanging</a> of suspected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Irishmen" title="United Irishmen">United Irishmen</a> by government troops.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The intimate nature of the conflict meant that the rebellion at times took on the worst characteristics of a civil war, especially in Leinster. Sectarian resentment was fuelled by the remaining Penal Laws still in force. Rumours of planned massacres by both sides were common in the days before the rising and led to a widespread climate of fear.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The aftermath of almost every British victory in the rising was marked by the massacre of captured and wounded rebels with some on a large scale such as at Carlow, New Ross, Ballinamuck and Killala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The British were responsible for particularly gruesome massacres at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibbet_Rath_massacre" title="Gibbet Rath massacre">Gibbet Rath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Ross_(1798)" title="Battle of New Ross (1798)">New Ross</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enniscorthy#The_1798_Visitor_Centre_.28.C3.81ras_1798.29" title="Enniscorthy">Enniscorthy</a>, burning rebels alive in the latter two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For those rebels who were taken alive in the aftermath of battle, being regarded as traitors to the Crown, they were not treated as prisoners of war but were executed, usually by hanging. Local forces publicly executed suspected members of the United Irishmen without trial in Dunlavin in what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Dunlavin_Green_executions" title="Dunlavin Green executions">Dunlavin Green Executions</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Carnew_executions" title="Carnew executions">Carnew</a> days after the outbreak of the rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, non-combatant civilians were murdered by the military, who also carried out many instances of rape, particularly in County Wexford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Many individual instances of murder were also unofficially carried out by local <a href="/wiki/Yeomanry" title="Yeomanry">Yeomanry</a> units before, during and after the rebellion as their local knowledge led them to attack suspected rebels. "Pardoned" rebels were a particular target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebel">Rebel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rebel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>County Wexford was the only area which saw widespread atrocities by the rebels during the <a href="/wiki/Wexford_Rebellion" title="Wexford Rebellion">Wexford Rebellion</a>. Massacres of loyalist prisoners took place at the <a href="/wiki/Enniscorthy#Vinegar_Hill" title="Enniscorthy">Vinegar Hill</a> camp and on <a href="/wiki/Wexford_bridge" title="Wexford bridge">Wexford bridge</a>. After the defeat of a rebel attack at <a href="/wiki/New_Ross" title="New Ross">New Ross</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scullabogue_Barn_Massacre" title="Scullabogue Barn Massacre">Scullabogue Barn Massacre</a> occurred where between 80<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and 200<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lydon274_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lydon274-18">[18]</a></sup> mostly Protestant men, women, and children were imprisoned in a barn which was then set alight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunne_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunne-19">[19]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford town</a>, on 20 June some 70 loyalist prisoners were marched to the bridge (according to an unsourced claim by historian James Lydon, first stripped naked)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lydon274_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lydon274-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pike_(weapon)" title="Pike (weapon)">piked</a> to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="French_intervention">French intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: French intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cornwallis_in_Ireland" title="Cornwallis in Ireland">Cornwallis in Ireland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="602" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg/220px-Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg/330px-Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg/440px-Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castlebar_Races_WynneC.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Castlebar" title="Battle of Castlebar">Battle of Castlebar</a>, 1798</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 22 August, nearly two months after the main uprisings had been defeated, about 1,000 French soldiers under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Humbert" title="General Humbert">General Humbert</a> landed in the north-west of the country, at <a href="/wiki/Kilcummin" title="Kilcummin">Kilcummin</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a>. Joined by up to 5,000 local rebels, they had some initial success, inflicting a humiliating defeat on the British in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Castlebar" title="Battle of Castlebar">Castlebar</a> (also known as the <i>Castlebar races</i> to commemorate the speed of the retreat) and setting up a short-lived "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Connacht" title="Republic of Connacht">Republic of Connacht</a>" under the presidency of <a href="/wiki/John_Moore_(Irish_politician)" title="John Moore (Irish politician)">John Moore</a>. This sparked some supportive risings in Longford and Westmeath which were quickly defeated, and the main force was defeated at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ballinamuck" title="Battle of Ballinamuck">the battle of Ballinamuck</a>, in <a href="/wiki/County_Longford" title="County Longford">County Longford</a>, on 8 September 1798. The Republic of Connacht had only lasted twelve days from its declaration of independence to its collapse. The French troops who surrendered were repatriated to <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">France</a> in exchange for British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>, but hundreds of the captured Irish rebels were executed. This episode of the 1798 Rebellion became a major event in the heritage and collective memory of the West of Ireland and was commonly known in Irish as <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Bliain na bhFrancach</i></span> and in English as "The Year of the French".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 October 1798, a larger French force consisting of 3,000 men, and including Wolfe Tone himself, attempted to land in <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a> near <a href="/wiki/Lough_Swilly" title="Lough Swilly">Lough Swilly</a>. They were intercepted by a larger Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">squadron</a>, and finally surrendered after a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tory_Island" title="Battle of Tory Island">three-hour battle</a> without ever landing in Ireland. Wolfe Tone was tried by court-martial in Dublin and found guilty. He asked for death by firing squad, but when this was refused, Tone cheated the hangman by slitting his own throat in prison on 12 November, and died a week later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holt.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="162" data-file-width="120" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Holt.png" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Holt.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Holt_(rebel)" title="Joseph Holt (rebel)">Joseph Holt</a> (1799).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Small fragments of the great rebel armies of the Summer of 1798 survived for a number of years and waged a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerrilla" title="Guerrilla">guerrilla</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Fugitive" title="Fugitive">fugitive</a>" warfare in several counties. In <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>, General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Holt_(rebel)" title="Joseph Holt (rebel)">Joseph Holt</a> fought on until his negotiated surrender in Autumn 1798. It was not until the failure of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmet" title="Robert Emmet">Robert Emmet's</a> rebellion in 1803 that the last organised rebel forces under Captain <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dwyer" title="Michael Dwyer">Michael Dwyer</a> capitulated. Small pockets of rebel resistance had also survived in Wexford and the last rebel group under <a href="/wiki/James_Corcoran" title="James Corcoran">James Corcoran</a> was not vanquished until February 1804.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="571" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/71/Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg/220px-Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/71/Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg/330px-Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/71/Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg/440px-Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guerilla_warfare_Wexford.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The last stand of <a href="/wiki/James_Corcoran" title="James Corcoran">James Corcoran</a> (February 11th 1804)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" title="Act of Union 1800">Act of Union</a>, having been passed in August 1800, came into effect on 1 January 1801 and took away the measure of autonomy granted to Ireland's <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It was passed largely in response to the rebellion and was underpinned by the perception that the rebellion was provoked by the brutish misrule of the Ascendancy as much as the efforts of the United Irishmen.</p>
<p>Religious, if not economic, discrimination against the Catholic majority was gradually abolished after the Act of Union but not before widespread mobilisation of the Catholic population under <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O'Connell">Daniel O'Connell</a>. Discontent at grievances and resentment persisted but resistance to British rule now largely manifested itself along anti-taxation lines, as in the <a href="/wiki/Tithe_War" title="Tithe War">Tithe War</a> of 1831–36.</p>
<p>Presbyterian radicalism was effectively tamed or reconciled to British rule by inclusion in a new Protestant Ascendancy, as opposed to a merely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> one. By mid-1798 a schism between the Presbyterians and Catholics had developed, with radical Presbyterians starting to waver in their support for revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> The government capitialised on this by acting against the Catholics in the radical movement instead of the northern Presbyterians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> Prior to the rebellion, anyone who admitted to being a member of the United Irishmen was expelled from the Yeomanry, however former Presbyterian radicals were now able to enlist in it, and those radicals that wavered in support saw it as their chance to reintegrate themselves into society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> The government also had news of the sectarian massacre of Protestants at Scullabogue spread to increase Protestant fears and enhance the growing division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> Anglican clergyman Edward Hudson claimed that "the brotherhood of affection is over", as he enlisted former radicals into his Portglenone Yeomanry corps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> On 1 July 1798 in Belfast, the birthplace of the United Irishmen movement, it is claimed that everyman had the red coat of the Yeomanry on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackstock_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-Blackstock-23">[23]</a></sup> However, the Protestant contribution to the United Irish cause was not yet entirely finished as several of the leaders of the 1803 rebellion were Anglican or Presbyterian.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, this fostering or resurgence of religious division meant that Irish politics was largely, until the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a> movement in the mid-19th century, steered away from the unifying vision of the egalitarian United Irishmen and based on sectarian fault lines with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unionism_(Ireland)" title="Unionism (Ireland)">Unionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Castle" title="Dublin Castle">Dublin Castle</a> individuals at the helm of power in Ireland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Rebellion_of_1798&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2460" data-file-width="3720" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg/200px-Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg/300px-Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg/400px-Grave_of_Wolfe_Tone.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Grave of Wolfe Tone<br/>

<p>Contemporary estimates put the death toll from 20,000 (<a href="/wiki/Dublin_Castle" title="Dublin Castle">Dublin Castle</a>) to as many as 50,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas1969_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-thomas1969-3">[3]</a></sup> of which 2,000 were military and 1,000 loyalist civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Some modern research argues that these figures may be too high. Firstly, a list of British soldiers killed, compiled for a fund to aid the families of dead soldiers, listed just 530 names. Secondly, professor Louis Cullen, through an examination of depletion of the population in County Wexford between 1798 and 1820, put the fatalities in that county due to the rebellion at 6,000. Historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bartlett_(historian)" title="Thomas Bartlett (historian)">Thomas Bartlett</a> therefore argues, "a death toll of 10,000 for the entire island would seem to be in order".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Other modern historians believe that the death toll may be even higher than contemporary estimates suggest as the widespread fear of repression among relatives of slain rebels led to mass concealment of casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the centenary of the Rebellion in 1898, conservative Irish nationalists and the Catholic Church would both claim that the United Irishmen had been fighting for "Faith and Fatherland", and this version of events is still, to some extent, the lasting popular memory of the rebellion. A series of popular "98 Clubs" were formed. At the bicentenary in 1998, the non-sectarian and democratic ideals of the Rebellion were emphasised in official commemorations, reflecting the desire for reconciliation at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> which was hoped would end "<a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.</p>
