<p><b>K-278 <i>Komsomolets</i></b> was the only Project 685 <i>Plavnik</i> (Плавник, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fin</a>", also known by its <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> of "Mike"-class) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_powered_submarine" title="Nuclear powered submarine">nuclear-powered</a> <a href="/wiki/Attack_submarine" title="Attack submarine">attack submarine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a>. On 4 August 1984 <i>K-278</i> reached a record submergence depth of 1,020 metres (3,350 feet) in the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The boat sank in 1989 and is currently resting on the floor of the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>, one mile deep, with its nuclear reactor and two nuclear warheads still on board. The single Project 685 was developed to test technologies for Soviet 4th generation nuclear submarines. Although primarily intended as a developmental model, it was fully combat capable, but sank after a fire broke out in the aft engineering compartment on its first operational patrol.</p>
<p>The <i>Komsomolets</i> was able to surface after the fire started and remained afloat for approximately 5 hours before sinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACT-2">[2]</a></sup> Of the 42 crewmembers who died, only 4 were killed by the fire and smoke, while 34 died of hypothermia and drowning in the frigid waters while awaiting rescue that did not arrive in time. Because of this shocking loss of life a very public enquiry was conducted and, as a result, many formerly classified details were revealed by the Soviet news media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (August 2016)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_submarine_K-278_Komsomolets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mike_class_SSN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="1110" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mike_class_SSN.svg/220px-Mike_class_SSN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mike_class_SSN.svg/330px-Mike_class_SSN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mike_class_SSN.svg/440px-Mike_class_SSN.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mike_class_SSN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Scheme of the Mike class</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Project 685 was designed by the Rubin Design Bureau in response to a challenge to develop an advanced submarine that could carry a mix of torpedoes and cruise missiles with conventional or nuclear warheads. The order to design the submarine was issued in 1966 and design was completed in 1974. The first (and only) keel was laid down on 22 April 1978 at <a href="/wiki/Severodvinsk" title="Severodvinsk">Severodvinsk</a>. <i>K-278</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">launched</a> on 3 June 1983 and <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> on 28 December 1983.</p>
<p><i>K-278</i> had a double hull, the inner one being composed of <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, which gave her an operating depth far greater than that of the best American submarines. The pressure hull was composed of seven compartments with the second and third protected by stronger forward and aft bulkheads creating a "safety zone" in case of an emergency. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escape_capsule" title="Escape capsule">escape capsule</a> was fitted in the <a href="/wiki/Sail_(submarine)" title="Sail (submarine)">sail</a> above these compartments to enable the crew to abandon ship in the event of an underwater emergency. Initial Western intelligence estimates of <i>K-278</i>’s speed were based on the assumption that it was powered by a pair of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal_cooled_reactor" title="Liquid metal cooled reactor">liquid-metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> reactors. When the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> revealed that the submarine used a single OK-650b-3 conventional <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized-water reactor</a>, these speed estimates were lowered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[Note 1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crew">Crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_submarine_K-278_Komsomolets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Norman Polmar and Kenneth J. Moore - two Western experts on Soviet submarine design and operations - the Project 685's advanced design included many automated systems which, in turn, allowed for fewer crewmembers than would be expected for a submarine of its size. The manning table approved by the Ministry of Defense in 1982 called for a crew of just 57 men. This was later increased to 64: 30 officers, 22 warrant officers, and 12 petty officers and seamen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Submarine_K-278_gets_a_name">Submarine K-278 gets a name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_submarine_K-278_Komsomolets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Submarine K-278 gets a name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1988, <i>K-278</i> was honored by becoming one of the few Soviet submarines to be given an actual name: <i>Komsomolets</i> (<i>Комсомолец</i>, meaning "a member of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Communist_League" title="Young Communist League">Young Communist League</a>"), and her commanding officer, Captain 1st Rank Yuriy Zelenskiy was honored for diving to a depth of 1,020 meters (3,350 feet).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinking">Sinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_submarine_K-278_Komsomolets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 7 April 1989, while under the command of Captain 1st Rank Evgeny Vanin and running submerged at a depth of 335 metres (1,099 ft) about 180 kilometres (100 nmi) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Bear_Island_(Norway)" title="Bear Island (Norway)">Bear Island (Norway)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> fire broke out in the engine room due to a short-circuit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-6">[5]</a></sup> and even though watertight doors were shut, the resulting fire spread through <a href="/wiki/Bulkhead_(partition)" title="Bulkhead (partition)">bulkhead</a> <a href="/wiki/Firestop" title="Firestop">cable penetrations</a>. The reactor <a href="/wiki/Scram" title="Scram">scrammed</a> and propulsion was lost. Electrical problems spread as cables burned through, and control of the boat was threatened. An emergency <a href="/wiki/Ballast_tank" title="Ballast tank">ballast tank</a> blow was performed and the submarine surfaced eleven minutes after the fire began. Distress calls were made, and most of the crew abandoned ship.</p>
<p>The fire continued to burn, fed by the <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a> system. At 15:15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-7">[6]</a></sup> several hours after the boat surfaced, it sank in 1,680 metres (5,510 ft) of water<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, about 250 kilometres (100 nmi) SSW off Bear Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-7">[6]</a></sup> The commanding officer and four others who were still on board entered the escape capsule and ejected it. Only one of the five to reach the surface was able to leave the capsule and survive before it sank again in the rough seas.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Air-sea_rescue" title="Air-sea rescue">Rescue aircraft</a> arrived quickly and dropped small rafts, but many men had already died from <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a> in the 2 °C (36 °F) water of the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>. The floating fish factory B-64/10 <i>Aleksey Khlobystov</i> (<i>Алексей Хлобыстов</i>) arrived 81 minutes after <i>K-278</i> sank, and took aboard 25 survivors and 5 fatalities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In total, 42 of the 69 crewmen died in the accident, including the commanding officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ACT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_submarine_K-278_Komsomolets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to her eight standard torpedoes <i>K-278</i> was carrying two torpedoes armed with nuclear warheads. Under pressure from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIR_(submersible)" title="MIR (submersible)">deep sea submersibles</a> operated from the oceanographic research ship <i><a href="/wiki/Akademik_Mstislav_Keldysh" title="Akademik Mstislav Keldysh">Akademik Mstislav Keldysh</a></i> to search for <i>K-278</i>. In June 1989, two months after the sinking, the wreck was located. Soviet officials stated that any possible leaks were insignificant and pose no threat to the environment.</p>
<p>Examination of the wreck in May 1992 revealed cracks along the entire length of the titanium hull, some of which were of 30–40 centimetres (12–16 inches) wide, as well as possible breaches in the reactor coolant pipes. An oceanographic survey of the area in August 1993 did suggest that waters at the site were not mixing vertically, and thus the sea life in the area was not being rapidly contaminated. That survey also revealed a hole over six metres (20 feet) wide in the forward torpedo compartment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An expedition in mid-1994 revealed some <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> leakage from one of the two nuclear torpedoes. On 24 June 1995, <i>Keldysh</i> set out again from St. Petersburg to the Mike datum to seal the hull fractures in Compartment 1 and cover the nuclear warheads, and declared success at the end of subsequent expedition in July 1996. The Russian government has declared the risk of radioactive contamination of the environment negligible until 2015 or 2025.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Norwegian authorities from the Marine Environmental Agency and Radiation Agency took several samples in August 2008 and no radiation was found. They checked for different radioactive substances including gamma emitters, plutonium, americium and strontium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, Vice Admiral (ret.) Chernov, commander of the submarine group of which the <i>Komsomolets</i> was part, founded the <a href="/wiki/Komsomolets_Nuclear_Submarine_Memorial_Society" title="Komsomolets Nuclear Submarine Memorial Society"><i>Komsomolets</i> Nuclear Submarine Memorial Society</a>, a charity to support the widows and orphans of his former command. Since then, the Society's charter has expanded to provide assistance to the families of all Soviet and Russian submariners lost at sea. Also, 7 April has become a day of commemoration for all submariners lost at sea.</p>
