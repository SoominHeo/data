<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Korean rock started in the late 1950s with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Jung-hyun" title="Shin Jung-hyun">Shin Jung-hyun</a> and his band. While this was happening, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Jung-hyun" title="Shin Jung-hyun">Shin Jung-hyun</a> was able to develop a distinctly Korean style of <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a>. Finally in 1962, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Jung-hyun" title="Shin Jung-hyun">Shin Jung-hyun</a> formed his first rock band in Korea, Add 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During the 1970s, music was heavily censored by the government. Shin Jung-hyun was imprisoned on drug charges. <a href="/wiki/Han_Dae-soo" title="Han Dae-soo">Han Dae-soo</a> moved to New York in self-exile after two of his albums were banned by the government. The imprisonment of Shin slowed the production of Korean rock, but other artists, most notably <a href="/wiki/Sanulrim" title="Sanulrim">Sanulrim</a> emerged during the late 1970s, before dance music came to dominate Korean popular music in the 1980s.</p>
<p>In the 1980s, popular musical tastes had moved away from rock music. The scene was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Boohwal" title="Boohwal">Boohwal</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baekdoosan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baekdoosan (page does not exist)">Baekdoosan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sinawe" title="Sinawe">Sinawe</a>, collectively known as the Big 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rock music was revived in the early 1990s with democratization following the presidency of Roh Tae-woo. As information flowed more freely into the country, Korean youths were exposed to decades of popular foreign music in a short span of time, and some began to form bands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Two of the earliest bands were <a href="/wiki/Crying_Nut" title="Crying Nut">Crying Nut</a> and <a href="/wiki/No_Brain" title="No Brain">No Brain</a>, which introduced the country to a variety of new genres in a localized blend called "Chosun Punk," spearheaded by indie label <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drug_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drug Records (page does not exist)">Drug Records</a> which also managed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Club_Drug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Club Drug (page does not exist)">Club Drug</a>. With increased globalization and access to the Internet, the music scene diversified and incorporated more styles of music. The late 1990s saw increasing diversity in musical influences, as younger bands like <a href="/wiki/Rux" title="Rux">Rux</a> emerged and <a href="/wiki/The_Geeks_(band)" title="The Geeks (band)">The Geeks</a> introduced Korea to <a href="/wiki/Straight_edge" title="Straight edge">straight edge</a> <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a>. Ska-punk was another strong early influence, producing bands including Lazybone and Beach Valley. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Skasucks" title="Skasucks">Skasucks</a> formed and led the ska-punk movement in Korea.</p>
