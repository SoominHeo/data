<p><b>KUKA</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_robot" title="Industrial robot">industrial robots</a> and solutions for <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factory</a> <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a>. The KUKA Robotics Corporation has 25 subsidiaries worldwide, mostly sales and service subsidiaries, including in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kukarussia_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-kukarussia-2">[2]</a></sup> and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_countries" title="European countries">European countries</a>. The company name, KUKA, is an acronym for <i><b>K</b>eller <b>u</b>nd <b>K</b>nappich <b>A</b>ugsburg</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:176px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1889_Keller_Knappich.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="174" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/92/1889_Keller_Knappich.jpg" width="174"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1889_Keller_Knappich.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The company was founded in 1898 by Johann Josef Keller and Jacob Knappich</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The company was founded in 1898 in Augsburg, Germany, by Johann Josef Keller and Jacob Knappich. At first, the company focused on house and street lights, but soon expanded to other products (welding equipment and solutions; big containers), to become the market leader in communal vehicles in Europe by 1966. Keller &amp; Knappich GmbH merged with part of Industrie-Werke Karlsruhe AG to become Industrie-Werke Karlsruhe Augsburg Aktiengesellschaft, eventually KUKA for short.</p>
<p>In 1973, KUKA created its own industrial robot FAMULUS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At that time, the company belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Quandt" title="Quandt">Quandt</a> group. However, in 1980, the <a href="/wiki/Quandt" title="Quandt">Quandt</a> family withdrew and a publicly owned firm was established. In 1995, the company was split into KUKA Robotics Corporation and KUKA Schweißanlagen (now KUKA Systems), now both subsidiaries of KUKA AG. The company is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robotics_Industries_Association" title="Robotics Industries Association">Robotics Industries Association</a> (RIA), of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Robotics" title="International Federation of Robotics">International Federation of Robotics</a> (IFR) and of the German engineering association <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VDMA" title="VDMA">VDMA</a>. Today, KUKA concentrates on solutions for the automation of industrial manufacturing processes.</p>
<p>In 2016, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midea_(company)" title="Midea (company)">Midea Group</a> offered to buy Kuka for about €4.5 billion ($5 billion). Midea is pursuing to become the largest shareholder by raising its stake beyond 70 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most robots are finished in "KUKA Orange" (the official corporate color) or black.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_data">Corporate data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. As of December, 2014, KUKA employed more than 12,000 workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnualReport2014_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-AnnualReport2014-1">[1]</a></sup> While previously emphasizing customers in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> industry, the company has since expanded to other industries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_milestones">Notable milestones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable milestones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>1971 – Europe’s first welding transfer line built for <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a>.</p>
<p>1973 – The world’s first industrial robot with six electromechanically driven axes, known as FAMULUS.</p>
<p>1976 – IR 6/60 – A completely new robot type with six electromechanically driven axes and an offset wrist.</p>
<p>1989 – A new generation of industrial robots is developed – brushless drive motors for a low maintenance and a higher technical availability.</p>
<p>2007 – KUKA Titan – at the time, the biggest and strongest industrial robot with six axes, entered into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>2010 – As the only robot family, the robot series KR QUANTEC completely covers the load range of 90 up to 300 kg with a reach of up to 3100 mm for the first time.</p>
<p>2012 – The new small robot series KR AGILUS is launched.</p>
<p>2014 – On February 9, the company uploaded a <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_mbdtupCbc4" rel="nofollow">video</a> on its official YouTube channel <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/user/KukaRobotGroup/" rel="nofollow">KukaRobotGroup</a>, teasing the audience with their new KUKA robot, specialized in Table Tennis. The teaser video shows a trailer of KUKA's robot initiating fight against a human in Table Tennis. The human is <a href="/wiki/Timo_Boll" title="Timo Boll">Timo Boll</a>, a legend in <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">table tennis</a>. This new robot is described as KUKA KR AGILUS, Fastest Robot on Earth. The <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tIIJME8-au8" rel="nofollow">full video</a> was available on March 10, 2014. The video is not a real match but a commercial with heavy CGI, and received strong criticism from the table tennis community. The video has been viewed over 6 million times on YouTube and has won numerous awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="System_information">System information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: System information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The KUKA system software is the operating software and the heart of the entire control. In it all basic functions are stored which are needed for the deployment of the robot system.</p>
<p>Robots come with a control panel that has a display resolution of 640 x 480 pixels and an integrated <a href="/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom" title="Six degrees of freedom">6D</a> mouse, with which the manipulator is moved, positions are saved (TouchUp), or where modules, functions, data lists, etc. are created and modified. To manually control the axes, the enabling switch on the back of the control panel (the KCP, or KUKAControlPanel) must be activated (today only with a panic function). The connection to the controller is a VGA interface and a CAN-bus.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Rugged_computer" title="Rugged computer">rugged computer</a> located in the control cabinet communicates with the robot system via an MFC card. Control signals between the manipulator and the controls are transferred using the so-called DSE-RDW connection. The DSE card is in the control cabinet, the RDW card in the robot socket.</p>
<p>Controls for the old KRC1 types used Windows 95 to run VxWorks-based software. Peripheral equipment includes a CD-ROM and a disk drive; <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Profibus" title="Profibus">Profibus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbus" title="Interbus">Interbus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devicenet" title="Devicenet">Devicenet</a> and ASI sockets are also available.</p>
<p>Controls for the newer KRC2 type use the <a href="/wiki/Windows_XP" title="Windows XP">Windows XP</a> operating system. Systems contain a CD-ROM drive and USB ports, Ethernet connection and feature optional connections for <a href="/wiki/Profibus" title="Profibus">Profibus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbus" title="Interbus">Interbus</a>, <a href="/wiki/DeviceNet" title="DeviceNet">DeviceNet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Profinet" title="Profinet">Profinet</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_areas">Application areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Application areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The industrial robots are used in a number of application areas, such as material handling, loading and unloading of machines, palletizing and depalletizing, spot and arc welding. They are used in a number of large companies, predominantly in the automotive industry, but also in other industries such as the aerospace industry. Specific applications include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Transport industry: for the transport of heavy loads, where their load capacity and free positioning are used.</li>
<li>Food and beverage industry: for tasks such as loading and unloading of packaging machines, cutting meat, stacking and palletizing, and quality control.</li>
<li>Construction industry: e.g. for ensuring an even flow of material.</li>
<li>Glass industry: used, for instance in the thermal treatment of glass and quartz glass in laboratory glass production, bending and forming operations.</li>
<li>Foundry and forging industry: the robots' heat and dirt resistance enables them to be used directly before, in and on the casting machines. They can also be used for operations such as deburring, grinding, or drilling, and for quality control.</li>
<li>Wood industry: for grinding, milling, drilling, sawing, palletising or sorting applications.</li>
<li>Metal processing: for operations such as drilling, milling, sawing or bending and punching. Industrial robots are used in welding, assembly, loading and unloading processes.</li>
<li>Stone processing: the ceramic and stone industries use the industrial robots for bridge sawing</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="KUKA_Entertainment">KUKA Entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KUKA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: KUKA Entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001, KUKA formed a partnership with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RoboCoaster_Ltd" title="RoboCoaster Ltd">RoboCoaster Ltd</a> to develop the world's first passenger-carrying industrial robot. The ride uses <a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster" title="Roller coaster">roller coaster</a>-style seats attached to robotic arms and provides a roller coaster-like motion sequence through a series of programmable maneuvers. Riders themselves can also program the motions of their ride. A second generation system, the RoboCoaster G2, was deployed at <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Islands_of_Adventure" title="Islands of Adventure">Islands of Adventure</a> theme park in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> in 2010, in conjunction with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_Attractions" title="Dynamic Attractions">Dynamic Structures</a>. <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Forbidden_Journey" title="Harry Potter and the Forbidden Journey">Harry Potter and the Forbidden Journey</a>'s seats are mounted on robotic arms, which are in turn mounted on a track allowing the arms to travel through the attraction while performing their movements in synchronization with the ride's show elements (animated props, projection surfaces, etc.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>KUKA's partnership with RoboCoaster has also seen KUKA robots appear in a number of Hollywood films. In the James Bond film <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Another_Day" title="Die Another Day">Die Another Day</a></i>, in a scene depicting an ice palace in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, NSA agent Jinx, played by <a href="/wiki/Halle_Berry" title="Halle Berry">Halle Berry</a>, is threatened by laser-wielding robots. In the <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i>, a KUKA robot hands <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a>’ character Robert Langdon a container containing a <a href="/wiki/Cryptex" title="Cryptex">cryptex</a>.</p>
<p>In 2007, KUKA introduced a simulator, based on the Robocoaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This product is not marketed by RoboCoaster Ltd. An installation of this version is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sum_Of_All_Thrills" title="The Sum Of All Thrills">The Sum Of All Thrills</a> ride at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPCOT" title="EPCOT">EPCOT</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Buena_Vista,_Florida" title="Lake Buena Vista, Florida">Lake Buena Vista, Florida</a>.</p>
