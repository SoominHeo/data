<p><b>Kudo3D</b>, based in <a href="/wiki/Pleasanton,_California" title="Pleasanton, California">Pleasanton, California</a>, manufactures professional desktop <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printers</a>. Its Titan 1 and Titan 2 <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printer</a> use a proprietary passive self-peeling technology, making it one of the leading professional high-resolution <a href="/wiki/Stereolithography" title="Stereolithography">stereolithography</a> printers. This technology allows both the Titan 1 and Titan 2 to be used in printing for various applications.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, founder Tedd Syao started creating the Titan 1 and developed the passive self-peeling technology from his garage. Shortly after, Tedd Syao and Jonathan Cheung, both UC Berkeley’s Haas School of Business alumni, co-founded Kudo3D.</p>
<p>Kudo3D launched its <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> in May 2014 that was funded within 2 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> After its successful campaign and delivery, Kudo3D continued to grow. The company has now expanded to two locations: <a href="/wiki/Pleasanton,_California" title="Pleasanton, California">Pleasanton, California</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hsinchu,_Taiwan" title="Hsinchu, Taiwan">Hsinchu, Taiwan</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titan_1">Titan 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Titan 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Titan 1 was constructed using modular design, utilizing industrial and consumer-grade components. The printer uses <a href="/wiki/Digital_Light_Processing" title="Digital Light Processing">Texas Instrument’s DLP technology</a> as its light source and printing time is further reduced as entire layers of resin can be cured at one time. Other components include an industrial grade linear stage module, an open-source controlling circuit, a stepping motor, a fast leveling build platform, and Kudo3D’s patented flexible PSP resin container lined with Teflon to extend container lifetime.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_Party_Resin">Third Party Resin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Third Party Resin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Titan 1 prints using third party resin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Titan 1 is most compatible with: MakerJuice G+, Spot A's Hard &amp; Tough for High Resolution, Spot A’s Flexible Resin, 3D Materials’ Castable and ABS resins.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Titan 1’s ability to print a wide range of sizes makes it versatile and usable for different applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Titan 1 grows extremely high resolutions prints, which is ideal for printing jewelry, miniatures, and research models. The Titan 1 has printed tissue scaffold for the biomedical research department at CCNY that is only 1mm by 2mm. However, it also possesses the ability to print larger items, making the Titan 1 perfect for art, architectural, engineering, and prototyping applications.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titan_2">Titan 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Titan 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>2 years after the successful <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> campaign, Kudo3D released their second generation 3D printer, the Titan 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Titan 2 promises a more reliable 3D printer with advanced controls, making it more user-friendly than its predecessor. With a built-in <a href="/wiki/Raspberry_Pi" title="Raspberry Pi">Raspberry Pi</a> and web-based controls, the Titan 2 is compatible with any computer, tablet, or smartphone device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Titan 2 incorporates all of the technology from the Titan 1, including the patented PSP resin container. In addition, the Titan 2 comes in 2 different cover colors, the Ruby Red and Green Emerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Titan_2_HR">Titan 2 HR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Titan 2 HR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2016, Kudo3D released a "spin-off" of the Titan 2 3D printer. Named Titan 2 HR, this printer is capable of printing objects at 23 microns XY resolution. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This capability makes it especially appealing to people who would like to print high-resolution, fine prints, such as jewelers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="PSP_Technology">PSP Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: PSP Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Passive self-peeling (PSP) technology is a patented, bottom-up, <a href="/wiki/Stereolithography" title="Stereolithography">SLA</a> technology, which was created by Kudo3D to minimize the separation force between the cured layers and the resin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> PSP employs a flexible resin container consisting of 5 different materials, which work together to aid in reducing the separation force. It ensures that features as fine as a strand of hair and larger objects, up to 10 inches tall, can be printed with the same machine.</p>
<p>This technology allows prints to require fewer supports, minimizing cleanup and scarring. It also enables features as fine as a strand of hair and larger objects, up to 10 inches tall, to be printed by the same machine while maintaining staggering resolution and impressive speeds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_Approval">Patent Approval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kudo3d&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Patent Approval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 27, 2016, Kudo3D received approval of the PSP (passive self-peeling) technology from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="US Patent and Trademark Office">US Patent and Trademark Office</a>.</p>
