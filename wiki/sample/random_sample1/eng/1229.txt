<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">LU decomposition</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>, <b>LU decomposition</b> (where 'LU' stands for 'lower upper', and also called <b>LU factorization</b>) factors a <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrix</a> as the product of a lower <a href="/wiki/Triangular_matrix" title="Triangular matrix">triangular matrix</a> and an upper triangular matrix. The product sometimes includes a <a href="/wiki/Permutation_matrix" title="Permutation matrix">permutation matrix</a> as well. The LU decomposition can be viewed as the matrix form of <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_elimination" title="Gaussian elimination">Gaussian elimination</a>. Computers usually solve square <a href="/wiki/System_of_linear_equations" title="System of linear equations">systems of linear equations</a> using the LU decomposition, and it is also a key step when inverting a matrix, or computing the <a href="/wiki/Determinant" title="Determinant">determinant</a> of a matrix. The LU decomposition was introduced by mathematician <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a> in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LU_factorization_with_Partial_Pivoting">LU factorization with Partial Pivoting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: LU factorization with Partial Pivoting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It turns out that a proper permutation in rows (or columns) is sufficient for the LU factorization. The <b>LU factorization with Partial Pivoting</b> (LUP) refers often to the LU factorization with row permutations only,</p>
<dl>
<dd><span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

<mi>P</mi>
<mi>A</mi>
<mo>=</mo>
<mi>L</mi>
<mi>U</mi>
<mo>,</mo>
<mspace width="thinmathspace"></mspace>
</mstyle>
</mrow>

</semantics>
</math></span><img alt=" PA = LU, \, " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83f70407ec9d1a107df55bf22c1bd43a72237f1c" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:11.049ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <i>L</i> and <i>U</i> are again lower and upper triangular matrices, and <i>P</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Permutation_matrix" title="Permutation matrix">permutation matrix</a> which, when left-multiplied to <i>A</i>, reorders the rows of <i>A</i>. It turns out that all square matrices can be factorized in this form,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okunev-cor3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-okunev-cor3-3">[3]</a></sup> and the factorization is numerically stable in practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This makes LUP decomposition a useful technique in practice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LU_factorization_with_full_pivoting">LU factorization with full pivoting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: LU factorization with full pivoting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <b>LU factorization with full pivoting</b> involves both row and column permutations,</p>

<dd><span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<mi>U</mi>
<mo>,</mo>

</math></span><img alt=" PAQ = LU, \, " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82e23d962656dc065feef5616a20494fdec6e60a" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:12.898ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span></dd>

<p>where <i>L</i>, <i>U</i> and <i>P</i> are defined as before, and <i>Q</i> is a permutation matrix that reorders the columns of <i>A</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LDU_decomposition">LDU decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: LDU decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <b>LDU decomposition</b> is a decomposition of the form</p>
<dl>
<dd><span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>A</mi>
<mo>=</mo>
<mi>L</mi>
<mi>D</mi>
<mi>U</mi>
<mo>,</mo>
<mspace width="thinmathspace"></mspace>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle A=LDU,\,}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt=" A = LDU, \, " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/063d178e783f3dc66eef5d2fc8bb8044c9f0a7cc" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:11.228ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <i>D</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Diagonal_matrix" title="Diagonal matrix">diagonal matrix</a> and <i>L</i> and <i>U</i> are <i>unit</i> triangular matrices, meaning that all the entries on the diagonals of <i>L</i> and <i>U</i> are one.</p>
<p>Above we required that <i>A</i> be a square matrix, but these decompositions can all be generalized to rectangular matrices as well. In that case, <i>L</i> and <i>D</i> are square matrices both of which have the same number of rows as <i>A</i>, and <i>U</i> has exactly the same dimensions as <i>A</i>. <i>Upper triangular</i> should be interpreted as having only zero entries below the main diagonal, which starts at the upper left corner.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_matrices">General matrices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: General matrices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For a (not necessarily invertible) matrix over any field, the exact necessary and sufficient conditions under which it has an LU factorization are known. The conditions are expressed in terms of the ranks of certain submatrices. The Gaussian elimination algorithm for obtaining LU decomposition has also been extended to this most general case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Algorithms">Algorithms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Algorithms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The LU decomposition is basically a modified form of <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_elimination" title="Gaussian elimination">Gaussian elimination</a>. We transform the matrix <i>A</i> into an upper triangular matrix <i>U</i> by eliminating the entries below the main diagonal. The Doolittle algorithm does the elimination column by column starting from the left, by multiplying <i>A</i> to the left with atomic lower triangular matrices. It results in a <i>unit lower triangular</i> matrix and an upper triangular matrix. The Crout algorithm is slightly different and constructs a lower triangular matrix and a <i>unit upper triangular</i> matrix.</p>
<p>Computing the LU decomposition using either of these algorithms requires 2<i>n</i><sup>3</sup> / 3 floating point operations, ignoring lower order terms. Partial <a href="/wiki/Pivot_element" title="Pivot element">pivoting</a> adds only a quadratic term; this is not the case for full pivoting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_complexity">Theoretical complexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Theoretical complexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If two matrices of order <i>n</i> can be multiplied in time <i>M</i>(<i>n</i>), where <i>M</i>(<i>n</i>) ≥ <i>n</i><sup><i>a</i></sup> for some <i>a</i>&gt;2, then the LU decomposition can be computed in time O(<i>M</i>(<i>n</i>)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This means, for example, that an O(<i>n</i><sup>2.376</sup>) algorithm exists based on the <a href="/wiki/Coppersmith%E2%80%93Winograd_algorithm" title="Coppersmith–Winograd algorithm">Coppersmith–Winograd algorithm</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sparse_matrix_decomposition">Sparse matrix decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LU_decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sparse matrix decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Special algorithms have been developed for factorizing large <a href="/wiki/Sparse_matrix" title="Sparse matrix">sparse matrices</a>. These algorithms attempt to find sparse factors <i>L</i> and <i>U</i>. Ideally, the cost of computation is determined by the number of nonzero entries, rather than by the size of the matrix.</p>
<p>These algorithms use the freedom to exchange rows and columns to minimize fill-in (entries which change from an initial zero to a non-zero value during the execution of an algorithm).</p>
<p>General treatment of orderings that minimize fill-in can be addressed using <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>.</p>

<li>Second, we solve the equation <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<p>Note that in both cases we are dealing with triangular matrices (<i>L</i> and <i>U</i>) which can be solved directly by <a href="/wiki/Triangular_matrix#Forward_and_back_substitution" title="Triangular matrix">forward and backward substitution</a> without using the <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_elimination" title="Gaussian elimination">Gaussian elimination</a> process (however we do need this process or equivalent to compute the <i>LU</i> decomposition itself).</p>
<p>The above procedure can be repeatedly applied to solve the equation multiple times for different <i>b</i>. In this case it is faster (and more convenient) to do an LU decomposition of the matrix <i>A</i> once and then solve the triangular matrices for the different <i>b</i>, rather than using Gaussian elimination each time. The matrices <i>L</i> and <i>U</i> could be thought to have "encoded" the Gaussian elimination process.</p>
