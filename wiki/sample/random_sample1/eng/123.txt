<p>The <b>election to the 5th <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> in 1812</b> was the fourth general election to be held after the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Union of Great Britain and Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>The fourth United Kingdom Parliament was dissolved on 29 September 1812. The new Parliament was summoned to meet on 24 November 1812, for a maximum seven-year term from that date. The maximum term could be and normally was curtailed, by the monarch dissolving the Parliament, before its term expired.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_situation">Political situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_general_election,_1812&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the 1807 election the Pittite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tory_(British_political_party)" title="Tory (British political party)">Tory</a> ministry, led as Prime Minister by the <a href="/wiki/William_Cavendish-Bentinck,_3rd_Duke_of_Portland" title="William Cavendish-Bentinck, 3rd Duke of Portland">Duke of Portland</a> (who still claimed to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whig_(British_political_party)" title="Whig (British political party)">Whig</a>), continued to prosecute the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.</p>
<p>At the core of the opposition were the <a href="/wiki/Foxite" title="Foxite">Foxite</a> Whigs, led since the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_Fox" title="Charles James Fox">Fox</a> in 1806 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">Earl Grey</a> (known by the <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_title" title="Courtesy title">courtesy title</a> of Viscount Howick and a member of the House of Commons 1806-07). However, as Foord observes "the affairs of the party during most of this period were in a state of uncertainty and confusion". Grey was not the commanding leader Fox had been. After Grey inherited his peerage and went to the House of Lords in 1807, the party leadership in the House of Commons was extremely weak.</p>
<p>The Grenvillites, associated with the Whig Prime Minister before Portland, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Wyndham_Grenville,_1st_Baron_Grenville" title="William Wyndham Grenville, 1st Baron Grenville">William Wyndham Grenville, 1st Baron Grenville</a> were also in opposition but were of less significance than the Foxites. Despite this Grenville was recognised as the first Leader of the Opposition in the House of Lords.</p>
<p>A relative of Grey's wife, <a href="/wiki/George_Ponsonby" title="George Ponsonby">George Ponsonby</a>, was proposed to Whig MPs by Grey and Grenville as the Whig leader in the House of Commons. Ponsonby was the first person recognised as the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(UK)" title="Leader of the Opposition (UK)">Leader of <b>the</b> Opposition</a> in the House of Commons, as opposed to the leader of <b>an</b> opposition faction. He proved to be incompetent but could not be persuaded to resign.</p>
<p>Until 1812 the Tory faction associated with another former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Henry_Addington,_1st_Viscount_Sidmouth" title="Henry Addington, 1st Viscount Sidmouth">Henry Addington, 1st Viscount Sidmouth</a> were also out of office.</p>
<p>The smallest component of the opposition were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_Party_(UK)" title="Radical Party (UK)">Radicals</a>, who were a largely middle class group of reformers. They had philosophical differences with the more aristocratic Whigs, but usually ended up voting with them in Parliament.</p>
<p>In 1809 Portland, whose health was failing, resigned. The new Tory Prime Minister was <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Perceval" title="Spencer Perceval">Spencer Perceval</a>. On 11 May 1812 Perceval was assassinated. The Premiership passed to the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Jenkinson,_2nd_Earl_of_Liverpool" title="Robert Jenkinson, 2nd Earl of Liverpool">Earl of Liverpool</a>, who brought Sidmouth into the cabinet but failed to attract Grenville. This was a further stage in the development of a two party system - just about all Tories supported the government and all Whigs opposed it.</p>
<p>The general election of 1812 returned the Tories to power for another term.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dates_of_election">Dates of election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_general_election,_1812&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dates of election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At this period there was not one election day. After receiving a writ (a royal command) for the election to be held, the local <a href="/wiki/Returning_officer" title="Returning officer">returning officer</a> fixed the election timetable for the particular constituency or constituencies he was concerned with. Polling in seats with contested elections could continue for many days.</p>
<p>An example of what happened in the 1812 election, was the Irish constituency of <a href="/wiki/Westmeath_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Westmeath (UK Parliament constituency)">Westmeath</a>. Walker confirms the date of election was 24 October 1812. Stooks Smith indicates that there were three days of polling, during which time 950 electors came to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hustings" title="Hustings">hustings</a> and voted. Longer contests were possible. The polling in the <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Berkshire (UK Parliament constituency)">Berkshire</a> election of 1812 went on for fifteen days (with 1,992 electors voting).</p>
<p>The time between the first and last contests in the general election was 5 October to 10 November 1812.</p>
