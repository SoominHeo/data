<p>"<b>Leave Me Alone</b>" is a single by American artist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>. The song was featured on Jackson's seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_(album)" title="Bad (album)">Bad</a></i> (1987), and was its eighth single in February 1989, but was released outside of the United States and Canada. "Leave Me Alone" was recorded during the original album sessions but the song only appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a> editions of <i>Bad</i> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonus_track" title="Bonus track">bonus track</a>, as well as on the 2001 cassette edition. The song was written and composed by Jackson and produced by <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a> (with Jackson serving as co-producer).</p>
<p>Internationally, the song was a success, peaking at number one and two in Ireland and the United Kingdom respectively and reaching the top 10 in Belgium, New Zealand and Spain. "Leave Me Alone" was generally well received by contemporary music critics. A short film was released for the song. In the film, Jackson pokes fun at the false rumors about him such as the stories about him purchasing the bones of "The Elephant Man" and his cosmetic surgeries. The short film was the recipient of a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in 1990 for Best Short Form Music Video. Despite the success of the single, the song never appeared on any of Jackson's world concert tours.</p>

<p>"Leave Me Alone" was a response to negative and false stories about Jackson that frequently appeared in the media and <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloids</a> post-1986 after the success of <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(Michael_Jackson_album)" title="Thriller (Michael Jackson album)">Thriller</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Beginning in 1986, the tabloids began to publish false stories about Jackson, one of the first being a story claiming that Jackson slept in a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_medicine" title="Hyperbaric medicine">hyperbaric oxygen chamber</a> to slow the aging process. A picture leaked out to the media of him lying down in a hyberbaric chamber at a hospital he visited. An unknown person took the picture of Jackson while he was testing out the chamber out of curiosity.</p>
<p>When Jackson bought a pet <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzee</a> named <a href="/wiki/Bubbles_(chimpanzee)" title="Bubbles (chimpanzee)">Bubbles</a>, the media viewed it as evidence of Jackson's increasing detachment from reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-2">[2]</a></sup> It was also reported that Jackson had offered to buy the bones of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Merrick" title="Joseph Merrick">Joseph Merrick</a>, the "Elephant Man"; Jackson stated that the story was "a complete lie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These stories inspired the nickname "Wacko Jacko", which Jackson acquired the following year, and would come to despise. Another frequent response from the media was about Jackson's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmetic_surgery" title="Cosmetic surgery">plastic surgery</a>. Jackson's manager said of the media's criticism towards the topic, "So many terrible things have been written. Okay, so he had his nose fixed, and the cleft — big deal. I got news for you, my nose has broke five times. It's been fixed twice. Who gives a shit? Who cares? <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis</a> had his nose done. <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a> had her nose done, had her breasts done? Everybody's had it done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song has been viewed as having a "paranoia theme", a theme that Jackson had frequently used on previous studio albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALM_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALM-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> felt that Jackson showed "obvious expressions of distrust" in the song and that the song was one of multiple songs where Jackson's "persistent loneliness in his music" was "prominent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2009, J. Edward Keyes, of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i>, described "Leave Me Alone" as sounding like "vintage Michael" and the song works because of its music, "a batch of thick chords for Jackson to vamp over".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R10_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-R10-7">[7]</a></sup> Keyes noted that the song was a "kind of darker inversion" of "<a href="/wiki/The_Way_You_Make_Me_Feel" title="The Way You Make Me Feel">The Way You Make Me Feel</a>", and that "Leave Me Alone" was "worked-up and angry, and Jackson's aggressive scraping of the high notes makes plain his frustration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R10_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-R10-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leave_Me_Alone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Leave Me Alone" is a funk song<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> played with a synthesizer and a guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bad_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bad-9">[9]</a></sup> According to MusicNotes.com, the song is set in the key of Gb Major with Jackson's voice range being sung from Bb3 to Ab5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sheet_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sheet-10">[10]</a></sup> The song's <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> is moderate and its metronome is 112 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beats_per_minute" title="Beats per minute">beats per minute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sheet_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-sheet-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Richard Harrington, a writer for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>"Leave Me Alone" was generally well received by contemporary music critics. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a>, a writer for <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>, stated he felt that "Leave Me Alone" was the best track on <i>Bad</i>, commenting "why are all of his best songs paranoid anthems?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALM_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ALM-5">[5]</a></sup> Steve Morse, a writer for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>, described "Leave Me Alone" as a "send-up" of Jackson's feuds with the "paparazzi-filled tabloids."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jon_Pareles" title="Jon Pareles">Jon Pareles</a>, of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, commented that "Leave Me Alone" had an "unmistakable message".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After Jackson's death in June 2009, <i>Rolling Stone</i> listed "Leave Me Alone" as being one of Jackson's most monumental work, and the song's composition was generally praised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R10_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-R10-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Leave Me Alone" performed well on various charts. It was released as a single outside of the United States and Canada. The song, similar to <i>Bad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s previous singles, proved to be a commercial success internationally. "Leave Me Alone"'s most successful territory was Ireland, where the song peaked at number one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRL_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-IRL-14">[14]</a></sup> The song saw similar chart success on the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, Norwegian and Switzerland charts, peaking within the top 10 at number two, five, six and ten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BEL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BEL-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK-16">[16]</a></sup> "Leave Me Alone" also peaked within the top 20 in Austria, France and Sweden, peaking at number 15, 17 and 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BEL_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-BEL-15">[15]</a></sup> The single was least successful in Australia, where the song peaked at number 37.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AUS_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AUS-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leave_Me_Alone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for "Leave Me Alone", was directed by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Blashfield" title="Jim Blashfield">Jim Blashfield</a>, produced by Jim Blashfield and Paul Diener and released on January 2, 1989. The video was also featured in the 1988 film <i><a href="/wiki/Moonwalker" title="Moonwalker">Moonwalker</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In essence, the video is an <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a> consisting of stylistically crude images based around Jackson's successful career since 1982's <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(Michael_Jackson_album)" title="Thriller (Michael Jackson album)">Thriller</a></i>. There is an emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a> view of Jackson's personal life and public image, referring to the nickname "Wacko Jacko" given to him by the press, and the various headlines associated with him in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-M1-19">[19]</a></sup> Lampooning rumours that he tried to purchase <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Merrick" title="Joseph Merrick">Joseph Merrick</a>'s bones, Jackson dances with <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop motion</a> "Elephant Man" bones in the video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-M1-19">[19]</a></sup> This particular segment (without the image of the bones) was used for the single's cover art, and the boneless video segment was featured in the <i>Moonwalker</i> trailer.</p>
<p>In the video, there are images of shrines to actress <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Elizabeth Taylor</a>, a real life close friend of Jackson. Throughout the video newspaper headlines, published by "National Intruder", with bizarre titles are shown, such as "Michael's Space-Age Diet" and "Michael Proposes to Liz". Another notable scene in the music video was a nose being chased by a surgical scalpel, which was reference to Jackson's plastic surgery being criticized by the media. At the end of the video, it is revealed that a gigantic Jackson himself is the amusement park. He breaks free, tearing the park to pieces. That scene is a somewhat reminiscent of <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver's Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a></i>, where Gulliver eventually breaks free from the Lilliputians' grasp from <a href="/wiki/Lilliput_and_Blefuscu" title="Lilliput and Blefuscu">Lilliput</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The video is featured on <i><a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson%27s_Vision" title="Michael Jackson's Vision">Michael Jackson's Vision</a></i> and the Target version DVD of <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_25" title="Bad 25">Bad 25</a></i>.</p>
<p>"Leave Me Alone" was the recipient of multiple nominations for its music video. The video won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in 1990 for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Music_Video" title="Grammy Award for Best Music Video">Best Short Form Music Video</a> at the <a href="/wiki/32nd_Annual_Grammy_Awards" title="32nd Annual Grammy Awards">32nd Grammy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The video also received six nominations at the <a href="/wiki/1989_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="1989 MTV Video Music Awards">1989 MTV Video Music Awards</a> for Video of the Year, Viewers' Choice, Breakthrough Video, Best Editing and Best Art Direction; the video won Best Special Effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-22">[22]</a></sup> The video lost its six nominations to Neil Young's "This Note Is for You" ("Video of the Year"), <a href="/wiki/Art_of_Noise" title="Art of Noise">Art of Noise</a> ("Breakthrough Video"), <a href="/wiki/Paula_Abdul" title="Paula Abdul">Paula Abdul</a> ("Best Editing"), and <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Express_Yourself_(Madonna_song)" title="Express Yourself (Madonna song)">Express Yourself</a>" ("Best Cinematography", "Best Art Direction") and "<a href="/wiki/Like_a_Prayer_(song)" title="Like a Prayer (song)">Like a Prayer</a>" ("Viewers' Choice").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-22">[22]</a></sup> Erlewine described the music video as being "weirdly claustrophobic" and felt that, "not coincidentally," it was the "best video from the album."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALM_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ALM-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
