<p><i><b>Led Zeppelin II</b></i> is the eponymous second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by the English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, released on 22 October 1969 in the United States and on 31 October 1969 in the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a>. Recording sessions for the album took place at several locations in both the United Kingdom and North America from January to August 1969. The album's production was credited to the band's lead guitarist and songwriter <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, and it also served as Led Zeppelin's first album to utilise the recording techniques of the engineer <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Kramer" title="Eddie Kramer">Eddie Kramer</a>. Incorporating several elements of <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> exhibited the band's evolving musical style of blues-derived material and their guitar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riff" title="Riff">riff</a>-based sound. It has been described as the band's heaviest album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBuckley2003502_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuckley2003502-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Led Zeppelin II</i> was a commercial success, and was the band's first album to reach number one on charts in the UK and the US. In 1970, the album's cover designer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Juniper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Juniper (page does not exist)">David Juniper</a> was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Recording_Package" title="Grammy Award for Best Recording Package">Grammy Award for Best Recording Package</a>. On 15 November 1999, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">12× Platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) for sales passing 12 million copies. Since its release, various writers and music critics have regularly cited <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> as one of the greatest and most influential rock albums of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acclaimed_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-acclaimed-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Led_Zeppelin_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Led Zeppelin II</i> was conceived during a hectic and much-travelled period of Led Zeppelin's career from January through August 1969, when they completed four European and three American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_concert_tour_chronology" title="Led Zeppelin concert tour chronology">concert tours</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosen-3">[3]</a></sup> Each song was separately recorded, mixed and produced at various studios in the UK and North America. The album was written on tour, during periods of a couple of hours in between concerts, a studio was booked and the recording process begun, resulting in a sound with spontaneity and urgency through necessity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosen_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rosen-3">[3]</a></sup> Bassist <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a> recalled that "We were touring a lot. Jimmy [Page]'s riffs were coming fast and furious. A lot of them came from onstage especially during the long improvised section of '<a href="/wiki/Dazed_and_Confused_(song)" title="Dazed and Confused (song)">Dazed and Confused</a>'. We'd remember the good stuff and dart into a studio along the way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the recording studios used by the band were not the most advanced. One studio in Vancouver, credited as "a hut",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mixing_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mixing-5">[5]</a></sup> had an 8-track set up that did not even have proper headphone facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulps_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulps-7">[7]</a></sup> The group's lead singer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a> later discussed the writing and recording process, stating "It was crazy really. We were writing the numbers in hotel rooms and then we'd do a rhythm track in London, add the vocal in New York, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overdub" title="Overdub">overdub</a> the harmonica in Vancouver and then come back to finish mixing at New York."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewispallett_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewispallett-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Thank_You_(Led_Zeppelin_song)" title="Thank You (Led Zeppelin song)">Thank You</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Lemon_Song" title="The Lemon Song">The Lemon Song</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Moby_Dick_(instrumental)" title="Moby Dick (instrumental)">Moby Dick</a>" were overdubbed during the tour, while the <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixing</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Whole_Lotta_Love" title="Whole Lotta Love">Whole Lotta Love</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Heartbreaker_(Led_Zeppelin_song)" title="Heartbreaker (Led Zeppelin song)">Heartbreaker</a>" was also done on tour. Page later stated "In other words, some of the material came out of rehearsing for the next tour and getting new material together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulps_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schulps-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Led_Zeppelin_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recording sessions for the album took place at <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Studios" title="Olympic Studios">Olympic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Studios" title="Morgan Studios">Morgan Studios</a> in London, England; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%26M_Studios" title="A&amp;M Studios">A&amp;M</a>, Quantum, <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Sound_Recorders" title="Sunset Sound Recorders">Sunset</a>, Mirror Sound and Mystic Studios in Los Angeles, California; <a href="/wiki/Ardent_Studios" title="Ardent Studios">Ardent Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>; <a href="/wiki/A_%26_R_Recording" title="A &amp; R Recording">A&amp;R</a>, Juggy Sound, Groove and Mayfair Studios in New York City; and the "hut" in Vancouver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lz2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-lz2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mixing_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mixing-5">[5]</a></sup> Production was entirely credited to Jimmy Page, while it also served as Led Zeppelin's first album to utilise the skills and recording techniques of engineer <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Kramer" title="Eddie Kramer">Eddie Kramer</a>. Led Zeppelin archivist Dave Lewis wrote of the album's production, stating "That the album turned out to be such a triumph, in particular for a production quality that still sounds fresh today, was in no small way due to the successful alliance with Page and Kramer in the control room."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> This partnership was particularly exhibited in the central section of the track "Whole Lotta Love". Kramer later said, "The famous <i>Whole Lotta Love</i> mix, where everything is going bananas, is a combination of Jimmy and myself just flying around on a small console twiddling every knob known to man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In another interview, Kramer later gave great credit to Page for the sound that was achieved, despite the inconsistent conditions in which it was recorded: "We did that album piece-meal. We cut some of the tracks in some of the most bizarre studios you can imagine, little holes in the wall. Cheap studios. But in the end it sounded bloody marvellous. There was a unification of sound on <i>[Led] Zeppelin II</i> because there was one guy in charge and that was Mr. Page."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theirtime_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-theirtime-10">[10]</a></sup> Page and Kramer spent two days mixing the album at A&amp;R Studios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theirtime_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-theirtime-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>One other song from the sessions, "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_La_(instrumental)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La La (instrumental) (page does not exist)">La La</a>" was included on the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_Deluxe_Edition#Led_Zeppelin_II" title="Led Zeppelin Deluxe Edition">deluxe edition of the album</a>.</p>

<p><i>Led Zeppelin II</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Blues_rock" title="Blues rock">blues rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> album. The finished tracks reflect the raw, evolving sound of the band and their ability as live performers. The album has been noted for featuring a further development of the lyrical themes established by Jimmy Page on Led Zeppelin's debut album, as Robert Plant had not until that point written any lyrics for the band. This all served to create a work which would then become more widely acclaimed and arguably more influential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicguide_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicguide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS2006_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS2006-15">[15]</a></sup> "Whole Lotta Love" and "The Lemon Song" both feature sexual themes, as the latter contains a metaphor, which, according to one music writer, implores "unnamed ladies to squeeze his lemon 'til the juice runs down my leg.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blender_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-blender-16">[16]</a></sup> As was later observed by Plant himself:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Led Zep II was very virile. That was the album that was going to dictate whether or not we had the staying power and the capacity to stimulate. It was still blues-based but it was a much more carnal approach to the music and quite flamboyant. It was created on the run between hotel rooms and <a href="/wiki/The_GTOs" title="The GTOs">the GTOs</a>, and that was quite something.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-williamson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-williamson-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Led Zeppelin II</i> also features experimentation with other musical styles and approaches, as on the alternately soft-and-loud "<a href="/wiki/What_Is_and_What_Should_Never_Be" title="What Is and What Should Never Be">What Is and What Should Never Be</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Ramble_On" title="Ramble On">Ramble On</a>" (which featured Page's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel-string_guitar" title="Steel-string guitar">acoustic guitar</a>), or the pop-influenced ballad "Thank You". With its mysterious atmospherics, "Ramble On" helped develop hard rock's association with <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> themes, which had been partly derived from the <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> genre of two to three years before, but also from Plant's personal interest in the writings of <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> This musical direction would later culminate on <i><a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_IV" title="Led Zeppelin IV">Led Zeppelin IV</a></i> (and countless subsequent groups would later carry the influence to further extremes). Conversely, the instrumental "Moby Dick" features an extended drum solo by <a href="/wiki/John_Bonham" title="John Bonham">John Bonham</a>, which would be extended further during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_concert_tour_chronology" title="Led Zeppelin concert tour chronology">Led Zeppelin concert</a> performances sometimes for as long as half an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Page's contribution to this album was significant, as his electric guitar solo on the song "Heartbreaker" was emulated by many younger rock guitarists, and exemplifies the group's intense musical attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> is the band's first album to feature Page playing a 1959 <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Gibson Les Paul</a>, the electric guitar he helped popularise. His innovative recording and drum <a href="/wiki/Microphone_practice" title="Microphone practice">miking</a> effects on tracks such as "Ramble On" and "Whole Lotta Love" also demonstrated his considerable skill, resourcefulness and originality as a producer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uncut09_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uncut09-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine later called Page's guitar riff for the latter song "one of the most exhilarating guitar riffs in rock &amp; roll."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs500_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rs500-19">[19]</a></sup> John Paul Jones later discussed Page's contributions:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Jimmy started coming into his own as a producer around "Whole Lotta Love". The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backwards_echo" title="Backwards echo">backwards echo</a> stuff. A lot of the microphone techniques were just inspired. Everybody thinks he goes into the studio with huge walls of amps, but he doesn't. He uses a really small amp and he just mic's it up really well, so it fits into a sonic picture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uncut09_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Uncut09-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The album's material also marked a certain honing of Plant's vocal approach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complete2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-complete2-20">[20]</a></sup> and signalled his emergence as a serious songwriter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulps_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schulps-7">[7]</a></sup> Plant's name had previously been absent from the songwriting credits of the band's first album due to the previous contractual commitments that resulted from his earlier association with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment" title="Sony Music Entertainment">CBS Records</a> as a solo artist. His influence on tracks such as "What Is and What Should Never Be" and "Ramble On" were pointers to the band's musical future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> Plant has commented that it was only during the sessions for <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> that he started to feel at home as a vocalist in the studio with Led Zeppelin. In a 2008 interview for <i><a href="/wiki/Uncut_(magazine)" title="Uncut (magazine)">Uncut</a></i>, he stated "[D]uring <i>Led Zep I</i> (1969) as far as I was concerned, I thought that I was going to [leave the band] anyway. I didn't feel that comfortable because there were a lot of demands on me vocally—which there were all the way through the Zeppelin thing. And I was quite nervous and didn't really get into enjoying it until <i>II</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plantuncut_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-plantuncut-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Led_Zeppelin_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501,_Jagdstaffel_11,_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="705" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501%2C_Jagdstaffel_11%2C_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-2004-0430-501,_Jagdstaffel_11,_Manfred_v._Richthofen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The World War I photograph on which the album sleeve was based</div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Record_sleeve" title="Record sleeve">album sleeve</a> design was from a poster by David Juniper, who was simply told by the band to come up with an interesting idea. Juniper was a fellow student of Page's at Sutton Art College in Surrey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> His design was based on a photograph of the <a href="/wiki/Jagdstaffel_11" title="Jagdstaffel 11">Jagdstaffel 11</a> Division of the <a href="/wiki/Luftstreitkr%C3%A4fte" title="Luftstreitkräfte">German Air Force</a> during World War I, the famed <a href="/wiki/Jagdgeschwader_1_(World_War_I)" title="Jagdgeschwader 1 (World War I)">Flying Circus</a> led by the <a href="/wiki/Manfred_von_Richthofen" title="Manfred von Richthofen">Red Baron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> After the picture was tinted, the faces of the four members of the band were airbrushed on from a 1969 publicity photograph. Other faces added, according to Juniper, were either <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Blind_Willie_Johnson" title="Blind Willie Johnson">Blind Willie Johnson</a>, a friend of <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> (possibly <a href="/wiki/Mary_Woronov" title="Mary Woronov">Mary Woronov</a>) and astronaut <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> although it is actually fellow astronaut <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The cover also pictured the outline of a Zeppelin on a brown background (similar to the cover of the band's first album), which gave the album its nickname "Brown Bomber".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The album was released on 22 October 1969 on <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a>, with advance orders of 400,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Welch_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Welch-36">[36]</a></sup> The advertising campaign was built around the slogans 'Led Zeppelin – The Only Way to Fly' and 'Led Zeppelin II Now Flying'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Commercially, <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> was the band's first album to hit No. 1 in the US, knocking <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road" title="Abbey Road">Abbey Road</a></i> (1969) twice from the top spot, where it remained for seven weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> By April 1970 it had registered three million American sales, whilst in Britain it enjoyed a 138-week residence on the LP chart, climbing to the top spot in February 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album also yielded Led Zeppelin's biggest hit with the track "<a href="/wiki/Whole_Lotta_Love" title="Whole Lotta Love">Whole Lotta Love</a>". This song reached No. 4 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in January 1970, after Atlantic went against the group's wishes by releasing a shorter version on 45. The single's <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-side</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Living_Loving_Maid_(She%27s_Just_a_Woman)" title="Living Loving Maid (She's Just a Woman)">Living Loving Maid (She's Just a Woman)</a>", also hit the <i>Billboard</i> chart, peaking at No. 65 in April 1970. The album helped establish Led Zeppelin as an international concert attraction, as for the next year, the group continued to tour relentlessly, initially performing in clubs and ballrooms, then in larger auditoriums and eventually stadiums as their popularity grew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1970 art director David Juniper was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in the category of <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Recording_Package" title="Grammy Award for Best Recording Package">best album package</a> for <i>Led Zeppelin II</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> On 10 November 1969, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> and in 1990 it was certified 5x platinum reflecting shipping of five million copies. By 14 November 1999, <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> had shipped twelve million copies and was certified 12x platinum by the RIAA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-United_StatesLed_ZeppelinLed_Zeppelin_IIalbumCertRef_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-United_StatesLed_ZeppelinLed_Zeppelin_IIalbumCertRef-39">[39]</a></sup> The 2014 reissue of the album helped itself get back into the <i>Billboard</i> Top 10 when it got to #9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Led_Zeppelin_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Led Zeppelin II</i> has been cited by music writers as a blueprint for <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> bands that followed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicguide_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicguide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Blues-derived songs like "Whole Lotta Love", "Heartbreaker", "The Lemon Song", "Moby Dick", and "Bring It On Home" have been seen as representing standards of the genre, where the guitar-based riff (rather than vocal <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verse-chorus_form" title="Verse-chorus form">verses</a>) defines the song and provides the key hook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-6">[6]</a></sup> Such arrangements and emphasis were at the time atypical in popular music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicguide_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicguide-14">[14]</a></sup> Page's guitar solo in "Heartbreaker" featuring <a href="/wiki/Tapping" title="Tapping">rapid-fire runs</a> of notes tapped only by the left hand, was a major inspiration to the later work of metal soloists and "<a href="/wiki/Shred_guitar" title="Shred guitar">shredders</a>" such as <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Van_Halen" title="Eddie Van Halen">Eddie Van Halen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Vai" title="Steve Vai">Steve Vai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> As such, the album is generally considered to be very influential on the development of rock music, being an early forerunner of heavy metal, and inspiring a host of other rock bands including <a href="/wiki/Aerosmith" title="Aerosmith">Aerosmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N' Roses">Guns N' Roses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicguide_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicguide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoard_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoard-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since its initial critical reception, <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> has been acknowledged by many critics and music writers as one of the most influential albums of rock music, and has earned several accolades from music publications, frequently placed at or near the top of "best album" lists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acclaimed_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-acclaimed-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1989, <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine ranked the album No. 5 on its list of <i>The 25 Greatest Albums of All Time</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acclaimed_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-acclaimed-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2000, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q_magazine" title="Q magazine">Q</a></i> magazine placed <i>Led Zeppelin II</i> at number 37 in its list of the <i>100 Greatest British Albums Ever</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2003, the album was ranked number 75 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs500_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs500-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_reissue">2014 reissue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Led_Zeppelin_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2014 reissue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with the group's <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_(album)#2014_reissue" title="Led Zeppelin (album)">self-titled debut album</a> and their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_III#2014_reissue" title="Led Zeppelin III">Led Zeppelin III</a></i>, the album was remastered and reissued on 2 June 2014. The reissue comes in six formats: a standard CD edition, a deluxe two-CD edition, a standard LP version, a deluxe two-LP version, a super deluxe two-CD plus two-LP version with a hardback book, and as high-resolution, 96k/24-bit digital downloads. The deluxe and super deluxe editions feature bonus material containing alternative takes, backing tracks and the previously unreleased instrumental, "La La".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The reissue was released with a <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">negative</a> version of the original album's artwork as its bonus disc's cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
