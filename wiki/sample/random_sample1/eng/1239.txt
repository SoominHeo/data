<p>"<b>Love, Day After Tomorrow</b>" is a song recorded by Japanese singer <a href="/wiki/Mai_Kuraki" title="Mai Kuraki">Mai Kuraki</a>, taken as the <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">lead single</a> of her debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Delicious_Way" title="Delicious Way">Delicious Way</a></i> (2000). It was released on December 8, 1999 via <a href="/wiki/Giza_Studio" title="Giza Studio">Giza Studio</a> and Tent House in two physical editions; a <a href="/wiki/CD_single" title="CD single">CD single</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">12" vinyl</a>. The track was written by Kuraki herself, while production was handled by Kanonji. The conception of the song started after the commercial failure of her <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> single "Baby I Like", which led to her American label Bip! Records to send her back to Japan, and she subsequently reverted back to the Japanese market.</p>
<p>Musically, "Love, Day After Tomorrow" is a <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> recording that incorporates elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teen_pop" title="Teen pop">teen music</a>, and lyrically discusses about themes of love. Upon its release, the single received positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>, who praised the singles commercial quality and production, but some noted similarities to another rising Japanese artist, <a href="/wiki/Utada_Hikaru" title="Utada Hikaru">Utada Hikaru</a>. Commercially, it experienced success in Japan, reaching number two on the <a href="/wiki/Oricon_Singles_Chart" title="Oricon Singles Chart">Oricon Singles Chart</a> and was certified million by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_Japan" title="Recording Industry Association of Japan">Recording Industry Association of Japan</a> (RIAJ) for shipments of one million copies. Despite not charting in North America, it sold over 5,000 copies.</p>
<p>An accompanying music video was directed and released in December 1999, where it featured the singer in a white/black room and shared intercut scenes with various locations in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and Japan. In order to promote the single, Kuraki performed it on several nationwide concert tours, and additionally added it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_hits" title="Greatest hits">greatest hits</a> albums including <i><a href="/wiki/All_My_Best_(Mai_Kuraki_album)" title="All My Best (Mai Kuraki album)">All My Best</a></i> (2009) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mai_Kuraki_Best_151A:_Love_%26_Hope" title="Mai Kuraki Best 151A: Love &amp; Hope">Mai Kuraki Best 151A: Love &amp; Hope</a></i> (2014). Since its release, it has become the singer's best-selling single and has sold over 1.385 million units in Japan, ranking it among many other best-selling entries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_composition">Background and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love,_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1999, Kuraki sent Japanese label <a href="/wiki/Giza_Studio" title="Giza Studio">Giza Studio</a> a demo tape of various compositions she had recorded, in order to secure a contract with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signed_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-signed-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite them being impressed by her performance, they signed her with American label Bip! Records, and sent her to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> in the United States to debut in that region. This attributed to her debut single "Baby I Like", which was recorded in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, and would coincide with her then-upcoming English studio album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-babyilike_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-babyilike-2">[2]</a></sup> The track impressed executives at <a href="/wiki/East_West_Records" title="East West Records">East West Records</a>, prompting the label to distribute it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, because it failed to chart on any <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> chart there, her label dropped her and sent her back to Japan, where she begun recording new material for a Japanese debut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the bunch, "Love, Day After Tomorrow" was written solely by Kuraki, while production was handled by Kanonji. Furthermore, it was composed by <a href="/wiki/Aika_Ohno" title="Aika Ohno">Aika Ohno</a> and arranged by American music team Cybersound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cd-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of Kuraki's experience with English, she wrote some lyrics and phrases in that language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-cd-5">[5]</a></sup> Musically, "Love, Day After Tomorrow" is a <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> recording that incorporates elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teen_pop" title="Teen pop">teen music</a>, and lyrically discusses about themes of love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdjournal_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdjournal-6">[6]</a></sup> In an article from Japanese magazine <i>CD Journal</i>, a contributing editor compared the music to Western culture in the <a href="/wiki/1990s_in_music" title="1990s in music">1990s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdjournal_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdjournal-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s Alexey Eremenko agreed, comparing the sound to the likes of <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love,_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Love, Day After Tomorrow" was taken as the lead single from Koda's debut record, <i><a href="/wiki/Delicious_Way" title="Delicious Way">Delicious Way</a></i> (2000), and serves as her Japanese debut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It premiered in Japan on December 8, 1999 via Giza Studio in two physical editions; a <a href="/wiki/CD_single" title="CD single">CD single</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">12" vinyl</a>. The compact disc included the original recording, an additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-side" title="B-side">B-side</a> titled "Everything's All Right"—along with a bonus remix—, plus the single's instrumental composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-cd-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, the vinyl release was issued on March 8, 2000, and included two numbers on each side; the original song, and a Day Tripper Drum N' Bass mix, whilst the reverse side had the same listing for "Everything's All Right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinyl_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-vinyl-8">[8]</a></sup> Upon its release, the single received positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. A member of <i>CD Journal</i> praised the singers vocals and the tracks production, but compared its sound and commercial appeal to the works of another rising Japanese artist at the time, <a href="/wiki/Utada_Hikaru" title="Utada Hikaru">Utada Hikaru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdjournal_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-cdjournal-6">[6]</a></sup> Furthermore, Alexey Eremenko of AllMusic selected it amongst her best singles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commercially, "Love, Day After Tomorrow" experienced success in Japan. It entered the weekly <a href="/wiki/Oricon_Singles_Chart" title="Oricon Singles Chart">Oricon Singles Chart</a> at number two, marking it her first charting experience and eventually starting her trend of non-consecutive number-two positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oricon_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-oricon-10">[10]</a></sup> By the end of 2000, Oricon ranked the single at number four on their year-end chart, with estimated sales of 1,385,190 units; this stands as Kuraki's best-selling single, and was the second highest entry by a female artist, only behind Utada's "<a href="/wiki/Wait_%26_See_(Risk)" title="Wait &amp; See (Risk)">Wait &amp; See (Risk)</a>" which placed at number three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yearend_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-yearend-11">[11]</a></sup> It was certified million by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_Japan" title="Recording Industry Association of Japan">Recording Industry Association of Japan</a> (RIAJ) for shipments of one million units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cert1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cert1-12">[12]</a></sup> The company also listed it as the 101st best-selling single in Japanese history, her only entry to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to <i>CD Journal</i>, more than 5,000 copies were pre-ordered and sold in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdjournal_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-cdjournal-6">[6]</a></sup> After the release of the singers compilation <i><a href="/wiki/All_My_Best_(Mai_Kuraki_album)" title="All My Best (Mai Kuraki album)">All My Best</a></i> in 2009, the song appeared at number 64 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Hot_100" title="Japan Hot 100">Japan Hot 100</a>, published by <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard-14">[14]</a></sup> It was certified gold by the RIAJ for digital sales of 100,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cert2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cert2-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love,_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An accompanying music video was directed and produced for the singers DVD <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Compilation" title="Compilation">compilation</a> <i>First Cut</i> (2000), as her management had no prior plans to create a visual for the single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-16">[16]</a></sup> It features the singer in a black and white room, singing the song in front of a chair. The clip has intercut scenes with various locations in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and Japan, showing the lifestyles of several people in the cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> To celebrate the artist's third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_hits" title="Greatest hits">greatest hits</a> album, Kuraki uploaded a new version of the video that featured numerous scenes that were taken by 15 different directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Japanese musician <a href="/wiki/Aika_Ohno" title="Aika Ohno">Aika Ohno</a>, who served as the composer to the track, <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">covered the song</a> in English language and appeared on her debut record <i>Shadows of Dreams</i> (2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to promote the single, Kuraki performed it on several nationwide concert tours. It's first appearance was on her Experience Live Tour, which commenced in early 2001, and was included on her Loving You... Tour the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Between 2003 and 2004, the singer added it to the set list of her Fairy Tale concert tour, and was used as one of the opening numbers to her 5th Anniversary show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It followed as an encore to her Diamond Wave 2006 tour, her 2008 Touch Me concert, her 10th anniversary tour, and her Happy Halloween Concert tour at. Saitama Super Arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On January 14, 2017, the singer, alongside vocalists Shizuka, <a href="/wiki/Ami_Nakashima" title="Ami Nakashima">Ami</a> and Reina Washio from Japanese band <a href="/wiki/E-girls" title="E-girls">E-girls</a>, performed a new rendition of the track on <i><a href="/wiki/Music_Fair" title="Music Fair">Music Fair</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
