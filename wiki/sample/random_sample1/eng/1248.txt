<p>The <b>M18 Hellcat</b> (officially designated the <b>76 mm Gun Motor Carriage M18</b> or M18 GMC) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Tank_destroyer" title="Tank destroyer">tank destroyer</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, used in the Italian, European, and Pacific theatres, and in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. It was the fastest American tracked armored vehicle until the <a href="/wiki/Turboshaft" title="Turboshaft">turboshaft</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a> main battle tank appeared decades later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Even then, most sources list the M1 Abrams with a top speed of only 45 mph (governed speed), leaving the Hellcat with a superior paved-road top operating speed. The speed was attained by keeping armor to a minimum, no more than one inch thick and roofless, open-top turrets (a standard design feature for all American fully tracked tank destroyers of World War II) and by powering the relatively small vehicle with a radial engine originally designed for aircraft usage. The Hellcat, along with the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a>-based <a href="/wiki/M10_tank_destroyer" title="M10 tank destroyer">M10 tank destroyer</a> and the highly effective, 90mm gun-armed <a href="/wiki/M36_tank_destroyer" title="M36 tank destroyer">M36 tank destroyer</a>, provided American and Allied forces with a respectable mobile anti-tank capability against the newer German armored types. Despite being armed with an only partially effective 76 mm cannon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> it performed well.</p>
<p>The Hellcat was the most effective American tank destroyer during World War II. It has a higher kill to loss ratio than any tank or tank destroyer fielded by American forces in World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the Tank Destroyer Force was organized in 1941, their commander Lieutenant Colonel (later General) <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Davis_Bruce" title="Andrew Davis Bruce">Andrew Davis Bruce</a> envisioned the units being equipped with something faster than a tank, with a better gun but less armor to allow for speed, a cruiser rather than a battleship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He objected to the 3 inch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M10_Tank_Destroyer" title="M10 Tank Destroyer">M10 Gun Motor Carriage</a> because it was too heavy and slow for his needs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and later on to the 90 mm <a href="/wiki/M36_tank_destroyer" title="M36 tank destroyer">M36 Gun Motor Carriage</a> because it was essentially an M10 with a bigger gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_2002.2C_p._19_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga_2002.2C_p._19-9">[9]</a></sup> The United States <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Ordnance Corps (United States Army)">Ordnance Corps</a> made several failed attempts to provide said vehicle using the weapons (the 37 mm, 57 mm, 3 inch, 75 mm and finally the lightweight 76 mm of 1942–1943) and technology available, including mounting the 3-inch gun on the fast <a href="/wiki/M3_Stuart" title="M3 Stuart">M3 Light Tank</a> chassis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The M18 was the end product of a long line of research vehicles aimed at providing the desired machine.</p>
<p>In December 1941, the Ordnance Corps issued a requirement for the design of a fast tank destroyer using a <a href="/wiki/Christie_suspension" title="Christie suspension">Christie suspension</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_R-975" title="Wright R-975">Wright-Continental R-975</a> <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/37_mm_Gun_M3" title="37 mm Gun M3">37 mm gun</a>. Two pilot vehicles were to be built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEp147_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEp147-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M18 originated in the design studio of <a href="/wiki/Harley_Earl" title="Harley Earl">Harley Earl</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a> motor company division of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, whose team also worked extensively on early camouflage paint.</p>
<p>Buick engineers used a <a href="/wiki/Torsion_bar_suspension" title="Torsion bar suspension">torsion bar suspension</a> that provided a steady ride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[a]</a></sup> Though it weighed about 20 tons, the Hellcat was capable of traveling upwards of 60 mph. Its power came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_R-975" title="Wright R-975">Wright R-975</a>, a nine-cylinder, 450-horsepower radial-type aircraft engine, paired with a three-speed 900T <a href="/wiki/Hydramatic" title="Hydramatic">Torqmatic</a> transmission.</p>
<p>Changes to the specification mean that the first pilot – the <b>57 mm Gun Motor Carriage T49</b> – was built with the British (57 mm) <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_QF_6-pounder" title="Ordnance QF 6-pounder">QF 6-pounder gun</a> instead of the 37 mm and a torsion bar suspension instead of the Christie suspension. It was tested in 1942 but the army wanted a heavier gun – the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75_mm_gun_(US)" title="75 mm gun (US)">75 mm gun M3</a> as used on the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> medium tank. The T49 project was cancelled and the second pilot was built with the 75 mm gun as the <b>75 mm Gun Motor Carriage T67</b>. This met approval, but in early 1943 the army requested a more powerful gun – the <a href="/wiki/76_mm_gun_M1" title="76 mm gun M1">76 mm gun M1</a> under development for the Sherman. Six pilot models – as the <b>76 mm Gun Motor Carriage T70</b> – were built with this gun. The trials of these models led to a new turret and changes to the hull front, but the design was otherwise accepted for production, which began in July 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEp147_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-CEp147-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once developed, the Hellcat was tested in the same manner as passenger cars before and after it, at the <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Proving_Grounds#Milford_Proving_Ground" title="General Motors Proving Grounds">General Motors Milford Proving Ground</a>. Top speed testing was done on a paved, banked oval and ride quality tests were done over specially developed stretches of bumps. The M18 also required tests of its ability to ford six feet of water, climb small walls, and ram through structures.</p>
<p>The first models of the tank destroyer were tested by the US Army's 704th Tank Destroyer Battalion. The unit had originally been trained on the <a href="/wiki/M3_Gun_Motor_Carriage" title="M3 Gun Motor Carriage">M3 Gun Motor Carriage</a> (a 75 mm gun installed in the bed of an M3 half-track). Despite its T70 prototypes requiring several improvements, the 704th had "superlative" testing record, and the unit was later issued production Hellcats after many of their suggestions were integrated into the vehicle. The testing phase of the Hellcat proved that teamwork was an essential element of the new light tank destroyer units<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and replaced the fixed, rigid structure of other units with a much more flexible command structure that allowed adapting to more complicated tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The M18's new design incorporated several labor saving and innovative maintenance features. The Wright R-975 engine was <a href="/wiki/Powerpack_(drivetrain)" title="Powerpack (drivetrain)">fully unitized</a>, as it was mounted on rails equipped with steel rollers that allowed maintenance crews to disconnect it easily from the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">transmission</a>, roll it out onto the lowered engine rear cover, service it, and then reconnect it to the transmission. The transmission could also be removed easily and rolled out onto a front deck plate to facilitate quick inspection and repairs.</p>
<p>In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/M10_tank_destroyer" title="M10 tank destroyer">M10 tank destroyer</a>, which used the heavy chassis of the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a>, the M18 Hellcat was designed from the start to be a fast tank destroyer. As a result, it was smaller, lighter, more comfortable, and significantly faster, but carried the same gun as the Sherman 76 mm models. The M18 carried a five-man crew as well as 45 rounds of main gun <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a>, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_machine_gun" title="M2 Browning machine gun">M2 Browning machine gun</a> on a flexible ring mount for use against aircraft and infantry.</p>
<p>The main disadvantages of the M18 were its very light open-topped armor protection, and the inconsistent performance of its 76 mm gun against the frontal <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">armor</a> of later German designs such as the <a href="/wiki/Tiger_I" title="Tiger I">Tiger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panther_tank" title="Panther tank">Panther</a>. The open-topped <a href="/wiki/Gun_turret" title="Gun turret">turret</a>—a characteristic which it shared with all American tank destroyers—left the crew exposed to <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">snipers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_grenade" title="Hand grenade">grenades</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">shell fragments</a>. The doctrinal priority of high speed at the cost of armor protection thus led to a relatively unbalanced design. The problem of the main gun performance was remedied with <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)#Armour-piercing.2C_composite_rigid_.28APCR.29" title="Shell (projectile)">High Velocity Armor Piercing</a> (HVAP) ammunition late in the war, which allowed the 76 mm gun to achieve greater armor penetration, but this was never available in quantity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZalAT_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZalAT-14">[13]</a></sup> The 76 mm gun with standard ammunition could only penetrate the frontal turret armor of Panther tanks at very close ranges,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jentz_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz-15">[14]</a></sup> whereas the HVAP ammunition gave it a possibility of effectively engaging some of the heavier German tanks and allowing to penetrate the Panther turret at ranges of about 1,000 m (1,100 yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Original plans called for a total of 8,986 M18s to be supplied – 1,600 for Lend Lease to other countries and 7,386 for the US Army. Production plans of the M18 were curtailed to 2,507, not including the six pilot models, of which 650 were converted to M39 armored utility vehicles, leaving 1,857 combat vehicles. The reasons behind the reduction (in no particular order) were:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Army Ground Forces preferred the 90 mm Gun Motor Carriage M36 for firepower and overrode Tank Destroyer Force commander <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Davis_Bruce" title="Andrew Davis Bruce">Andrew Bruce's</a> objections to adopting it</li>
<li>The number of self-propelled Tank Destroyer battalions had been approximately halved due to a policy change forced by the AGF, who wanted towed guns to be used and hence far fewer self-propelled units were needed for the Tank Destroyers</li>
<li>There was little potential Lend Lease activity: Britain and the Soviet Union did not care for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Two were transferred to the United Kingdom, and five to the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Production of M18 Hellcats ran from July 1943 until October 1944, with 2,507 built. Several changes were made during production. The 76 mm gun M1 fitted to most Hellcats kicked up large amounts of dust when fired. This was enough to impede the vision of the crew, who had to wait until the muzzle blast cleared to fire accurately again. To solve this problem, the 76 mm gun M1 was replaced with the muzzle brake-equipped 76 mm gun M1A2 as soon as it became available. Beginning in June 1944, the last 700 Hellcats received this new gun. Hellcats with serial numbers 1350 and below had the naturally-aspirated R-975 C1 engine. The ventilation grate for the transmission and oil coolers behind the driver's hatch on these early Hellcats had a bulged shape, protruding above the line of the upper hull. M18s with serial numbers 1351 and above had the supercharged R-975 C4 engine. At roughly the same time as the change in engine type, the shape of the ventilation grate was changed to be flush with the upper hull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were three production contracts for the Hellcat: RAD-563 covered the six pilot models. T-6641 was for the first 1,000 vehicles, and T-9167 was for the final 1,507 vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle_2012_p57_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle_2012_p57-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_2004_p44_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga_2004_p44-21">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_performance">Combat performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Combat performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The M18 served primarily in Western Europe, but was also present in the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_the_Pacific_Theatre">Use in the Pacific Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in the Pacific Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>M18s served in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tank_destroyer_battalion" title="Tank destroyer battalion">tank destroyer battalions</a> supporting US Army infantry divisions in the latter stages of the war, notably in the Philippines and Okinawa. M18s were not issued to Marine Corps units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Europe">Use in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The T70 prototype of the M18 first saw combat at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Shingle" title="Operation Shingle">Anzio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italy</a>, and production versions of the M18 were used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Theater_of_Operations" title="European Theater of Operations">North-West Europe</a> and Italy from the summer of 1944 onwards. Typically, every US Infantry or Armored Division had a Tank Destroyer Battalion attached on a long-term basis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_battles">Notable battles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notable battles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 19, 1944, in the <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a> bridgehead near <a href="/wiki/Arracourt" title="Arracourt">Arracourt</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the 704th Tank Destroyer Battalion was attached to the <a href="/wiki/4th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Armored Division (United States)">4th Armored Division</a>. Lt. Edwin Leiper led one M18 <a href="/wiki/Platoon#United_States_organization" title="Platoon">platoon</a> of C <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">Company</a> to Rechicourt-la-Petite, on the way to Moncourt. He saw a German tank gun muzzle appearing out of the fog 30 feet away, and deployed his platoon. In a five-minute period, five German tanks of the 113th Panzer Brigade were knocked out for the loss of one M18. The platoon continued to fire and destroyed ten more German tanks while losing another two M18s. One of the platoon's M18s commanded by Sgt Henry R. Hartman knocked out six of the German tanks, most of which were the much-feared <a href="/wiki/Panther_tank" title="Panther tank">Panthers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M18 Hellcat was a key element during World War II in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> On December 19–20, the 1st Battalion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/506th_PIR" title="506th PIR">506th Parachute Infantry Regiment</a> was ordered to support Team Desobry, a battalion-sized tank-infantry task force of the <a href="/wiki/10th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="10th Armored Division (United States)">10th Armored Division</a> assigned to defend Noville located north-northeast of both Foy and of Bastogne just 4.36 miles (7 km) away. With just four<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup> M18 tank destroyers of the 705th Tank Destroyer Battalion to assist, the paratroopers attacked units of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Panzer_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="2nd Panzer Division (Wehrmacht)">2nd Panzer Division</a>, whose mission was to proceed by secondary roads via Monaville (just northwest of Bastogne) to seize a key highway and capture, among other objectives, fuel dumps—for the lack of which the overall German counter-offensive faltered and failed. Worried about the threat to its left flank in Bastogne, it organized a major joint arms attack to seize Noville. Team Desobry's high speed highway journey to reach the blocking position is one of the few documented cases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup> in which the top speed of the M18 Hellcat – 50 miles per hour (80 km/h) – was actually used to get ahead of an enemy force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea_M39.JPEG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Korea_M39.JPEG/220px-Korea_M39.JPEG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Korea_M39.JPEG/330px-Korea_M39.JPEG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Korea_M39.JPEG/440px-Korea_M39.JPEG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korea_M39.JPEG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An M39 carrier used in the Korean War.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The attack of 1st Battalion and the M18 Hellcat tank destroyers of the 705th TD Battalion near Noville together destroyed at least 30 German tanks and inflicted 500 to 1,000 casualties on the attacking forces, in what amounted to a spoiling attack. A Military Channel historian credited the M18 destroyers with 24 kills, including several <a href="/wiki/Tiger_I" title="Tiger I">Tiger tanks</a>, and believes that in part, their ability to "shoot and scoot" at high speed and then reappear elsewhere on the battlefield, confused and slowed the German attack, which finally stalled, leaving the Americans in control of the town overnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup> However, actual German combat records show that no Tiger tanks were in operation in the area at the time and that the nearest Tigers were some 20 km to the south east, still in Luxembourg.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_tactics">Combat tactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Combat tactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The average combat range noted by the Americans for tank vs. tank action was around 800 to 900 m (2,600 to 3,000 ft). The Hellcat had a gun that could penetrate roughly 3.5 in (88 mm) of armor at 2,000 m (2,200 yd), which was enough to penetrate a <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a> medium tank frontally. However, against the Panther, a Hellcat would be facing a tank with roughly 5.5 in (140 mm) of line-of-sight armor frontally that could not be pierced at any range, with a 3.9 in (100 mm) mantlet that could be pierced only at extremely short ranges unless using the exceedingly rare <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)#Armour-piercing.2C_composite_rigid_.28APCR.29" title="Shell (projectile)">HVAP ammunition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZalAT_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZalAT-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jentz_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz-15">[14]</a></sup> The 76 mm gun could knock out the Panther with relative ease from the flanks and rear, as the turret and side armor was weak and the quality of German armor plate declined in the last two years of the war. While Panthers in defense were formidable, Panthers in the attack had great difficulty in not exposing their vulnerable large side profiles. A common tactic for any AFV is employing the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hull_down" title="Hull down">Hull down</a> firing positions, which allows it to remain mostly behind cover while engaging the enemy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tank_destroyer_doctrine">Tank destroyer doctrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tank destroyer doctrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tank_destroyer" title="Tank destroyer">Tank destroyer</a></div>
<p>American prewar armored doctrine was based on the assumption that massed enemy armored attacks would be delivered at a time and place of the enemy's choosing against an American defense. U.S. doctrine therefore originally called for the tank destroyer units to be held in reserve so that they could quickly deploy to destroy such massed armored thrusts. These independent tank destroyer groups were to quickly concentrate, to stop enemy tanks from penetrating deeply.</p>
<p>Similarly, US armored attacks would be delivered at a time and place of friendly forces choosing against an enemy defense. For this, the US envisioned using tanks in the attack in a support and exploitation role, usually in conjunction with infantry. Tank destroyers were to be used against tanks that had already penetrated the front lines. The Hellcat was not intended to engage in protracted combat, but to quickly respond to breakthroughs in the line by enemy armor. To accomplish this, it was designed for very high mobility at the expense of armor protection.</p>
<p>This doctrine failed on the European battlefield where attacking Sherman tanks ran into defending German tanks far more often than intended and the tank destroyers were called in to assist. Tank destroyers were often deployed to attack enemy armor at long range from an ambush position. During the battle for Italy, tank destroyers compensated for a shortage of 155mm artillery ammunition by using their 3-inch or 76 mm guns in indirect fire role. Near the end of the war, there were so few German tanks that tank destroyers were increasingly used as <a href="/wiki/Self-propelled_artillery" title="Self-propelled artillery">self-propelled artillery</a> in support of infantry for lack of any other targets. In effect, they were used exactly like tanks.</p>
<p>In practice, tank destroyer battalions were not held in reserve but were attached nearly permanently to divisions. Tank destroyer battalions assigned to divisions were often split up, with TD companies attached to infantry regiments, or platoons attached to infantry battalions. When so attached, defending tank destroyer units supplemented organic antitank weapons (bazookas and 57mm towed guns).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobility">Mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the M18 was capable of high road speeds, this attribute was difficult to use successfully in combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although sustained travel at road speeds was hardly ever used outside of the Allied response during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup> most Hellcat crews found the higher speeds especially useful in a sprint to <a href="/wiki/Flanking_maneuver" title="Flanking maneuver">flank</a> German tanks, which had relatively slow turret <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traverse_(gunnery)" title="Traverse (gunnery)">traverse</a> speeds, and such maneuvering allowed the tank destroyer crew to direct a shot into the enemy's thinner side or rear armor.</p>
<p>In general, Hellcat crews were complimentary of their vehicle's performance and capabilities, but did complain that the open top created a cold interior in the Northern European winter of 1944–45.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restorer_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-Restorer-26">[25]</a></sup> This problem was not helped by the fact that the air-cooled engine pulled a percentage of its cooling air through the crew compartment, creating, in effect, a large armor-plated refrigerator. It was not designed to do so, but it proved impossible to seal off the crew compartment entirely from engine induced drafts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_war">Post war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Post war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After World War II, many M18s were sold to other countries. These were rebuilt and refurbished by <a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)#Brown_.26_Root" title="KBR (company)">Brown &amp; Root</a> in northern Italy in the late 1940s and early 1950s, and bear data plates that indicate those rebuilds. One of the users was <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, which kept them in reserve until the early 1990s. A number of these vehicles were later used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Serbian_Krajina" title="Military of Serbian Krajina">Military of Serbian Krajina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Republika_Srpska" title="Army of Republika Srpska">Army of Republika Srpska</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_wars" title="Yugoslav wars">Yugoslav wars</a>. One example was used on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_train" title="Armored train">armored train</a> named the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krajina_express" title="Krajina express">Krajina express</a>" (<i>Krajina Ekspres</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Military of the Republic of China">Military of the Republic of China</a> also operated several M18s until their chassis and hulls were worn out, at which point the turrets were salvaged and installed onto surplus hulls of <a href="/wiki/M42_Duster" title="M42 Duster">M42 Duster</a> anti-aircraft vehicles to produce <a href="/wiki/Type_64_(tank)" title="Type 64 (tank)">Type 64</a> light tanks.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venezuelan" title="Venezuelan">Venezuelan</a> military still operates M18s, with 75 still in reserve service. An unknown number of these vehicles were modernized by a Yugoslavian firm in 1991.</p>
<p>An M18 is on display at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_Heritage_and_Education_Center" title="Army Heritage and Education Center">Army Heritage and Education Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlisle,_PA" title="Carlisle, PA">Carlisle, PA</a>. M18 Hellcat "Amaz N Grace" is on loan to the Museum of American Armor in Old Bethpage NY, from the US Military Museum.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The only M18 variant produced in significant numbers was the <a href="/wiki/M39_Armored_Utility_Vehicle" title="M39 Armored Utility Vehicle">M39 Armored Utility Vehicle</a>, a turretless variation used to transport personnel or cargo or as a <a href="/wiki/Artillery_tractor" title="Artillery tractor">gun tractor</a>. This version was armed with a single M2 machine gun on a flexible mount. 650 early production M18s were converted into M39s by removing the turret and fitting seats for up to eight men in the open fighting space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> M39s saw combat during the Korean War, primarily as armored personnel carriers and munitions carriers, and were finally declared obsolete on February 14, 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> About 100 M39s were transferred to the West German Bundeswehr in 1956, where they were used to train the reestablished Panzergrenadier armored infantry units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_2">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M18_Hellcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>105 mm Howitzer Motor Carriage T88</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>M18 with the 76 mm gun replaced with a 105 mm T12 <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzer</a>. A pilot was built in 1944 but project cancelled after the end of the war.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>90 mm Gun Motor Carriage M18</b>: M18 with the 76 mm gun replaced with a 90 mm gun; cancelled after the end of the war</li>
<li><b>76 mm Gun Motor Carriage T86 (Amphibious)</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>M18 with a much larger and lightweight flotation hull, using its tracks for water propulsion. The T86E1 variant test vehicle used propellers for propulsion.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>105 mm Howitzer Motor Carriage T87 (Amphibious)</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The T86 vehicles had shown that track propulsion was better and this was used for the T87 which had the same 105 mm T12 howitzer of the T88.</dd>
</dl>
<p>All work on the three amphibious models was canceled after the end of the war.</p>
