<p>The <b>Medium Tank M2</b> was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> that was first produced in 1939 by the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Island_Arsenal" title="Rock Island Arsenal">Rock Island Arsenal</a>, just prior to the commencement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Production was 18 M2 tanks, and 94 slightly improved M2A1 tanks, for a total of 112. <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Events in Western Europe</a> rapidly demonstrated that the M2 was obsolete, and it was never used overseas in combat; it was, however, used for training purposes throughout the war.</p>
<p>The M2's features included an unusually large number of <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine guns</a>, bullet deflector plates, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sloped_armor" title="Sloped armor">sloped armor</a> on the hull front (<a href="/wiki/Glacis#Armored_vehicles" title="Glacis">glacis plate</a>). The main armament was a 37 mm (1.5 in) gun, with 32 mm (1.3 in) armor; the M2A1 had a 51 mm (2.0 in) <a href="/wiki/Gun_mantlet" title="Gun mantlet">gun mantlet</a>. Some features of the M2 series, especially the suspension and powertrain, provided the basis for later, important U.S. tank designs including the <a href="/wiki/M3_Lee" title="M3 Lee">M3 Lee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M4_Medium_Tank" title="M4 Medium Tank">M4 Sherman</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_fighting_vehicle" title="Armored fighting vehicle">armored fighting vehicles</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M2_Medium_Tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rock_Island_Arsenal" title="Rock Island Arsenal">Rock Island Arsenal</a> started work on a new medium tank, based on the design of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Light_Tank" title="M2 Light Tank">M2 Light Tank</a>. Initially designated the T5, the redesigned model (with a 350 hp R-975 radial engine) was re-designated as the M2 Medium Tank in June, 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-3">[3]</a></sup> After the first 18 units had been produced at Rock Island Arsenal and evaluated by the Army, the upgraded M2A1 specification was approved with a redesigned turret and a more powerful engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rottman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rottman-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The medium tank M2 was a larger development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Light_Tank" title="M2 Light Tank">M2 Light Tank</a>. Many components were common or used a similar design, including the <a href="/wiki/Vertical_volute_spring_suspension" title="Vertical volute spring suspension">vertical volute spring suspension</a> which would be used in later tanks as well. Twin-wheeled <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogies</a> were mounted externally, which saved internal space compared to other suspension designs. The rubber-bushed and rubber-shod track proved durable on roads. The initial M2 model was powered by an air-cooled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_R-975" title="Wright R-975">Wright R-975</a> radial engine, originally designed for aircraft. For the M2A1, this engine was supercharged to provide an extra 50 hp (37 kW) for a total of 400 horsepower (300 kW), and designated as the R-975 C1 radial engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-6">[6]</a></sup> These track and suspension designs, with slight modifications, were later used on the M3 and M4 medium tanks.</p>
<p>The M2 had a high superstructure, with a <a href="/wiki/Sponson" title="Sponson">sponson</a>-mounted <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a> in each corner. In addition, two more machine guns were fixed in the <a href="/wiki/Glacis#Armored_vehicles" title="Glacis">glacis plate</a> and fired by the driver. Surmounting the superstructure was a small revolving <a href="/wiki/Turret" title="Turret">turret</a> armed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/37mm_Gun_M3" title="37mm Gun M3">37 mm Gun M3</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coaxial_weapon" title="Coaxial weapon">coaxial machine gun</a>. The 37 mm gun could penetrate 46 mm of face-hardened armor sloped 30° at a range of 500 yards (457 meters), and 40 mm at 1,000 yards (914 meters).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This armament configuration was a hybrid between the sponson-mounted weapons of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_VIII_(tank)" title="Mark VIII (tank)">Mark VIII Liberty tank</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> vintage, and the combination of turreted cannon, coaxial machine gun and glacis-mounted machine gun that was almost universal in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> medium tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga-5">[5]</a></sup> (Two additional .30-caliber machine guns could be mounted on pintles on either side of the turret for anti-aircraft use, bringing the total to nine—surely a record for any tank brought into service by any army.) The crew consisted of the tank commander, a driver and four gunners. The vehicle provided internal stowage for 200 rounds of 37 mm ammunition and up to 12,250 rounds of <a href="/wiki/.30-06_Springfield" title=".30-06 Springfield">.30-caliber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bullet <a href="/wiki/Deflection_(physics)" title="Deflection (physics)">deflector</a> plates were installed over the rear fenders. The idea behind these plates was that the tank could drive over a trench, and the rear sponson machine guns could then fire onto the plates; the bullets would deflect into the trench or the area directly behind the tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Like the sponson machine guns themselves, the deflector plates turned out to be useless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, the idea was used again on M5 light tanks in the Normandy campaign.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M2_Medium_Tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chrysler was appointed to manage a new tank plant, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit_Arsenal_Tank_Plant" title="Detroit Arsenal Tank Plant">Detroit Arsenal Tank Plant</a>, to manufacture the M2, and the US Government contracted on 15 August 1940 for 1,000 vehicles to be produced at a rate of 100 a month and to be delivered by August 1942 at a fixed price of $33,500 per tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stout_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stout-10">[10]</a></sup> Events in Europe made it obvious that the M2 was obsolete, and the government modified the contract two weeks later, before production began. Instead of M2 medium tanks, the plant would now build 1,000 M3 Grant tanks once they had been designed. In the interim production of the M2 was given to the Rock Island Arsenal, where 94 M2A1s were built up to August 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The M2A1 had slightly better armor and a slightly larger turret than the original M2, since it had the turret from the <a href="/wiki/M3_Stuart" title="M3 Stuart">M3 Light Tank</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Gun_mantlet" title="Gun mantlet">gun mantlet</a> armor 2 inches (51 mm) thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaloga_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zaloga-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service">Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M2_Medium_Tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The M2 was already obsolete when it entered service. It compared poorly with contemporary <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> tanks, such as the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somua_S35" title="Somua S35">S-35</a> and German <a href="/wiki/Panzer_III" title="Panzer III">Panzer III</a> which could withstand 37 mm (1.5 in) anti-tank guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zal-10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zal-10-12">[12]</a></sup> The 37 mm (1.5 in) main armament of the M2 was equivalent to the 37 mm (1.5 in) the Panzer III<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> , 47 mm (1.9 in) S-35 had more powerful guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zal-10_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zal-10-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1941, the Germans had begun upgrading their Panzer III with a 50 mm (2.0 in) L/42 gun, and the Soviets had fielded the vastly superior <a href="/wiki/T-34" title="T-34">T-34</a>, with a 76 mm (3.0 in) gun and a sloped 52 mm (2.0 in) glacis plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zal-10_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zal-10-12">[12]</a></sup> Given this, the M2 was essentially a stopgap measure until more capable tanks like the <a href="/wiki/M3_Lee" title="M3 Lee">M3 Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> came along in 1942-43. The Ordnance office recommended in January 1942 that the M2 should only be used for training purposes, and they were never sent overseas to combat areas. The U.S. Army fielded the M2 and M2A1 with the 67th Infantry Regiment (Medium Tanks) and, subsequently, the 1st Armored Division's 69th Armored Regiment during intensive training maneuvers in the United States in 1941, and the M2 design continued to prove useful in a basic training role for tank crewmen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The trained crewmen from the 69th Armored were reassigned to provide cadres to new armored units as U.S. forces were rapidly expanded in 1942-44.</p>
<p>The need for a new medium tank to match the German <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a>'s 75 mm (3.0 in) turret gun led to the development of the <a href="/wiki/M3_Lee" title="M3 Lee">M3 Lee</a>, designed first to mount a 75 mm (3.0 in) gun in the right sponson. This configuration had been tested on an M2; the experimental vehicle was designated T5E2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
