<p>The <b>Medium Tank M3</b> was an American <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In Britain, the tank was called by two names based on the turret configuration and crew size. Tanks employing US pattern turrets were called the "<b>Lee</b>," named after <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate</a> general <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">Robert E. Lee</a>. Variants using British pattern turrets were known as "<b>Grant</b>," named after U.S. general <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a>.</p>
<p>Design commenced in July 1940, and the first M3s were operational in late 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The U.S. Army needed a medium tank armed with a 75mm gun and, coupled with the United Kingdom's immediate demand for 3,650 medium tanks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the Lee began production by late 1940. The design was a compromise meant to produce a tank as soon as possible. The M3 had considerable firepower and good armor, but had serious drawbacks in its general design and shape, including a high silhouette, an archaic <a href="/wiki/Sponson" title="Sponson">sponson</a> mounting of the main gun preventing the tank from taking a <a href="/wiki/Hull-down" title="Hull-down">hull-down</a> position, riveted construction, and poor off-road performance.</p>
<p>Its overall performance was not satisfactory and the tank was withdrawn from combat in most theaters as soon as the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> tank became available in larger numbers. In spite of this, it was considered by <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_Luck" title="Hans von Luck">Hans von Luck</a> (an <i><a href="/wiki/Oberst" title="Oberst">Oberst</a></i> (Colonel) in the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)"><i>Wehrmacht Heer</i></a> and the author of <i>Panzer Commander</i>) to be superior to the best German tank at the time of its introduction, the <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a> (at least until the F1 variant).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite being replaced elsewhere, the British continued to use M3s in combat against the Japanese in <a href="/wiki/South-East_Asian_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South-East Asian theatre of World War II">southeast Asia</a> until 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1939, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> possessed approximately 400 tanks, mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Light_Tank" title="M2 Light Tank">M2 Light Tanks</a>, with 18 of the to-be-discontinued <a href="/wiki/M2_Medium_Tank" title="M2 Medium Tank">M2 Medium Tanks</a> as the only ones considered "modern".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The U.S. funded tank development poorly during the interwar years, and had little experience in design as well as poor doctrine to guide design efforts.</p>

M3 Lee being manufactured.</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/M2_Medium_Tank" title="M2 Medium Tank">M2 Medium Tank</a> was typical of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_fighting_vehicle" title="Armored fighting vehicle">armored fighting vehicles</a> (AFVs) many nations produced in 1939. When the U.S. entered the war, the M2 design was already obsolete with a <a href="/wiki/37_mm_Gun_M3" title="37 mm Gun M3">37 mm gun</a>, 32 mm frontal armor, an impractical number of secondary machine guns and a very high silhouette. The <a href="/wiki/Panzer_III" title="Panzer III">Panzer III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a>'s success in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">French campaign</a> led the U.S. Army to immediately order a new medium tank armed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75_mm_gun_(US)" title="75 mm gun (US)">75 mm gun</a> in a turret. This would be the M4 Sherman. Until the Sherman reached production, an interim design with a 75 mm gun was urgently needed.</p>
<p>The M3 was the solution. The design was unusual because the main weapon — a larger caliber, low-velocity 75 mm gun — was in an offset <a href="/wiki/Sponson" title="Sponson">sponson</a> mounted in the hull with limited traverse. The sponson mount was necessary because, at the time, American tank plants were incapable of casting a turret big enough to hold the 75mm main gun. A small turret with a lighter, high-velocity 37 mm gun sat on top of the tall hull. A small cupola on top of the turret held a <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a>. The use of two main guns was seen on the French <a href="/wiki/Char_B1" title="Char B1">Char B1</a> and the Mark I version of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Churchill_tank" title="Churchill tank">Churchill tank</a>. In each case, two weapons were mounted to give the tanks adequate capability in firing both anti-personnel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-explosive_shell" title="High-explosive shell">high explosive</a> ammunition and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Velocity_Armor_Piercing" title="High Velocity Armor Piercing">armor-piercing</a> ammunition for anti-tank combat. The M3 differed slightly from this pattern having a main gun that could fire an armor-piercing projectile at a velocity high enough for efficiently piercing armor, as well as deliver a high-explosive shell that was large enough to be effective. Using a hull mounted gun, the M3 design could be produced faster than a tank featuring a turreted gun. It was understood that the M3 design was flawed, but Britain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> urgently needed tanks. A draw-back of the sponson mount was that the M3 could not take a <a href="/wiki/Hull-down" title="Hull-down">hull-down</a> position and use its 75 mm gun at the same time.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mack_m3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="772" data-file-width="576" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Mack_m3.jpg/220px-Mack_m3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Mack_m3.jpg/330px-Mack_m3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Mack_m3.jpg/440px-Mack_m3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Mack_Trucks" title="Mack Trucks">Mack</a> Synchromesh 5-Speed transmission <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div>

<p>The M3 was tall and roomy: the power transmission ran through the crew compartment under the turret cage to the gearbox driving the front sprockets. Steering was by differential braking, with a turning circle of 37 ft (11 m). The vertical volute-sprung suspension (VVSS) units possessed a return roller mounted directly atop the main housing of each of the six suspension units (three per side), designed as self-contained and readily replaced modular units bolted to the hull sides. The turret was power-traversed by an electro-hydraulic system in the form of an electric motor providing the pressure for the hydraulic motor. This fully rotated the turret in 15 seconds. Control was from a spade grip on the gun. The same motor provided pressure for the gun stabilizing system.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2301" data-file-width="3000" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg/220px-Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg/330px-Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg/440px-Medium-tank-M3-front.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The 75-mm was operated by a gunner and a loader. Sighting the 75-mm gun used an M1 periscope — with an integral telescope — on the top of the sponson. The periscope rotated with the gun. The sight was marked from zero to 3,000 yd (2,700 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[i]</a></sup> with vertical markings to aid deflection shooting at a moving target. The gunner laid the gun on target through geared handwheels for traverse and elevation.</p>
<p>The 37-mm was aimed through the M2 periscope, though this was mounted in the mantlet to the side of the gun. It also sighted the coaxial machine gun. Two range scales were provided: 0-1,500 yd (1,400 m) for the 37-mm and 0-1,000 yd (910 m) for the machine gun.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="603" data-file-width="798" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg/220px-M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg/330px-M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg/440px-M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M-3Grants-E_014053.2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
British M3 Grant (left) and Lee (right) at <a href="/wiki/El_Alamein" title="El Alamein">El Alamein</a> (<a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>), in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, 1942, showing differences between the British turret and the original design.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Though not at war, the U.S. was willing to produce, sell and ship armored vehicles to Britain. The British had requested that their <a href="/wiki/Matilda_II" title="Matilda II">Matilda II</a> infantry tank and <a href="/wiki/Crusader_tank" title="Crusader tank">Crusader</a> cruiser tank designs be made by American factories, but this request was refused. With much of their equipment <a href="/wiki/Dunkirk_evacuation" title="Dunkirk evacuation">left on the beaches near Dunkirk</a>, the equipment needs of the British were acute. Though not entirely satisfied with the design, they ordered the M3 in large numbers. British experts had viewed the mock-up in 1940 and identified features that they considered flaws — the high profile, the hull mounted main gun, the lack of a radio in the turret (though the tank did have a radio down in the hull), the riveted armor plating (whose rivets tended <a href="/wiki/Spall" title="Spall">to pop off inside the interior in a deadly ricochet</a> when the tank was hit by a non-penetrating round), the smooth track design, insufficient armor plating and lack of splash-proofing of the joints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal90_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal90-10">[9]</a></sup> The British desired modifications for the tank they were purchasing. A bustle was to be made at the back of the turret to house the <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Set_No._19" title="Wireless Set No. 19">Wireless Set No. 19</a>. The tank was to be given thicker armor plate than the original U.S. design, and the machine gun cupola was to be replaced with a simple hatch. With these modifications accepted, the British ordered 1,250 M3s. The order was subsequently increased with the expectation that when the M4 Sherman was available, it could replace part of the order. Contracts were arranged with three U.S. companies. The total cost of the order was approximately 240 million US dollars, the sum of all British funds in the US; it took the <a href="/wiki/Lend-Lease" title="Lend-Lease">US Lend-Lease act</a> to solve the financial shortfall.</p>
<p>The prototype was completed in March 1941 and production models followed, with the first British-specification tanks produced in July. Both U.S. and British tanks had thicker armor than first planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal93_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal93-11">[10]</a></sup> The British design required one fewer crew member than the US version due to the radio in the turret. The U.S. eventually eliminated the full-time radio operator, assigning the task to the driver. After extensive losses in Africa and Greece, the British realized that to meet their needs for tanks, both the Lee and the Grant types would need to be accepted.</p>
<p>The U.S. military used the "M" (Model) letter to designate nearly all of their equipment. When the British Army received their new M3 medium tanks from the US, confusion immediately set in<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> between the different M3 medium tank and M3 light tank. The British Army began naming their American tanks after American military figures, although the U.S. Army never used those terms until after the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> M3 tanks with the cast turret and radio setup received the name "General Grant", while the original M3s were called "General Lee", or more usually just "Grant" and "Lee". The M3 brought much-needed firepower to British forces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_African_campaign" title="North African campaign">North African desert campaign</a>.</p>
<p>The chassis and running gear of the M3 design was adapted by the Canadians for their <a href="/wiki/Ram_tank" title="Ram tank">Ram tank</a>. The hull of the M3 was also used for self-propelled <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> as with the original design of the <a href="/wiki/M7_Priest" title="M7 Priest">M7 Priest</a>, of which nearly 3,500 were built, and recovery vehicles.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_African_theater">North African theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North African theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Of the 6,258 M3s produced by the U.S., 2,855 were supplied to the British Army, and about 1,386 to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The American M3 medium tank's first action during the war was in 1942, during the <a href="/wiki/North_African_Campaign" title="North African Campaign">North African Campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> British Lees and Grants were in action against <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Rommel" title="Erwin Rommel">Rommel's</a> forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gazala" title="Battle of Gazala">Battle of Gazala</a> on 27 May that year. Their appearance was a surprise to the Germans, who were unprepared for the M3s 75 mm gun. They soon discovered the M3 could engage them beyond the effective range of their <a href="/wiki/5_cm_Pak_38" title="5 cm Pak 38">5 cm Pak 38</a> anti-tank gun, and the <a href="/wiki/5_cm_KwK_39" title="5 cm KwK 39">5 cm KwK 39</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Panzer_III" title="Panzer III">Panzer III</a>, their main medium tank. The M3 was also vastly superior to the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_M13/40" title="Fiat M13/40">Fiat M13/40</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiat_M14/41" title="Fiat M14/41">M14/41</a> tanks employed by the Italian troops, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannone_da_47/32_M35" title="Cannone da 47/32 M35">47 mm gun</a> was effective only at point blank range, while only the few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semovente_75/18" title="Semovente 75/18">Semoventi da 75/18</a> self-propelled guns were able to destroy it using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HEAT" title="HEAT">HEAT</a> rounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Grants and Lees served with the British in North Africa until the end of the campaign. Following <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a> (the invasion of French North Africa), the U.S. also fought in North Africa using the M3 Lee. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division</a> had given up their new M4 Shermans to the British prior to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_El_Alamein" title="Second Battle of El Alamein">Second Battle of El Alamein</a>. Subsequently, a regiment of the division was still using the M3 Lee when they arrived to fight in North Africa. The M3 was generally appreciated during the North African campaign for its mechanical reliability, good armor protection and heavy firepower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[ii]</a></sup></p>
<p>In all three areas, the M3 was able to engage <a href="/wiki/Panzer" title="Panzer">German tanks</a> and towed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-tank" title="Anti-tank">anti-tank</a> guns. Yet the high silhouette and low, hull-mounted 75-mm were tactical drawbacks, since they prevented fighting from a <a href="/wiki/Hull-down" title="Hull-down">hull-down</a> firing position. The use of riveted hull superstructure armor on the early versions led to <a href="/wiki/Spall#Antitank_warfare" title="Spall">spalling</a>, where the impact of enemy shells caused the rivets to break off and become projectiles inside the tank. Later models were built with all-welded armor to eliminate this problem. These lessons were applied to the design and production of the M4. The M3 was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> as soon as the M4 was available. Several specialist vehicles based on the M3 were employed in Europe, such as the M31 <a href="/wiki/Armoured_recovery_vehicle" title="Armoured recovery vehicle">armored recovery vehicles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canal_Defence_Light" title="Canal Defence Light">Canal Defence Light</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_front">Eastern front</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern front">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

A pair of Soviet M3 Lees at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kursk" title="Battle of Kursk">Battle of Kursk</a>.</div>

<p>Starting in 1941 1386 M3 tanks were shipped to the Soviet Union but the Red Army only received 976 of these as the rest were lost in marine transit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> These were supplied through the <a href="/wiki/Lend-Lease" title="Lend-Lease">Lend-Lease</a> program in 1942-1943. All were <i>Lee</i> variants, although the Soviets sometimes referred to them generically as Grants. The M3 was unpopular in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>, which already used the more modern <a href="/wiki/T-34" title="T-34">T-34</a>. The faults of the M3 Lee revealed themselves in engagements against enemy armor and anti-tank weapons; the Soviets gave it the nickname <i>Братская могила на шестерых</i> ("a grave for six").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> The official Soviet designation was <i>М3с</i> (<i>М3 средний</i>, "M3 medium") to distinguish them from <i>М3л</i> (<i>М3 лёгкий</i>, "M3 light") <a href="/wiki/M3_Stuart" title="M3 Stuart">Stuart tanks</a>. With the Soviet Union producing close to 1,500 T-34s a month, their use of the M3 Lee declined after mid-1943. The Soviets still used them on secondary fronts, such as in the Arctic during the Red Army's <a href="/wiki/Petsamo%E2%80%93Kirkenes_Offensive" title="Petsamo–Kirkenes Offensive">Petsamo–Kirkenes Offensive</a> in October 1944.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_War">Pacific War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pacific War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="792" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg/220px-The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg/330px-The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg/440px-The_British_Army_in_Burma_1945_SE3270.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A British M3 Grant in <a href="/wiki/Mandalay" title="Mandalay">Mandalay</a> Burma, (<a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>), during the <a href="/wiki/Burma_Campaign_1944%E2%80%9345" title="Burma Campaign 1944–45">Burma Campaign 1944–45</a>, March 1945</div>
</div>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>, tank warfare played a secondary role as the primary battles were fought with naval<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> and aerial forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean_Theater" title="Pacific Ocean Theater">Pacific Ocean Theater</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwest_Pacific_Theater" title="Southwest Pacific Theater">Southwest Pacific Theater</a>, the U.S. Army deployed only a third of its 70 separate tank battalions, and none of its armored divisions, while the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> deployed all six of its tank battalions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the British Army received M4 Shermans, about 900 M3 tanks were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">Indian Army</a> and some of these saw action in the <a href="/wiki/South-East_Asian_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South-East Asian theatre of World War II">war in South East Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-24">[22]</a></sup> British Lees and Grants were used by the British <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Fourteenth Army (United Kingdom)">Fourteenth Army</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[23]</a></sup> until the fall of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rangoon" title="Rangoon">Rangoon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[23]</a></sup> performing "admirably" in Burma in 1944-45, in its original role: supporting infantry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>, the M3's main task was <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> support. It played a pivotal role during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Imphal" title="Battle of Imphal">Battle of Imphal</a>, during which the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a>'s 14th Tank Regiment (consisting of mostly captured British <a href="/wiki/M3_Stuart" title="M3 Stuart">M3 Stuart</a> light tanks and their own <a href="/wiki/Type_95_Ha-Go" title="Type 95 Ha-Go">Type 95 Ha-Go</a> light tanks) encountered M3 medium tanks for the first time and found itself outgunned and outmatched by the British armor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> Despite their worse-than-average off-road performance, the M3s performed well as they traversed the steep hillsides around <a href="/wiki/Imphal" title="Imphal">Imphal</a>. Officially declared obsolete in April 1944,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[23]</a></sup> the Lee nevertheless saw action until the end of the war.</p>
<p>The Australian Army also received approximately 1,700 M3 Grants that had originally been part of a UK order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-24">[22]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Armoured_Corps" title="Australian Armoured Corps">Australian Armoured Corps</a> had been formed in 1941 to take part in the <a href="/wiki/North_African_Campaign" title="North African Campaign">North African Campaign</a>, but was retained in Australia after the outbreak of hostilities with Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-28">[26]</a></sup> In 1941–42, the cadres of three armoured divisions were formed – all of them were equipped partly with the M3 Grant – in addition to <a href="/wiki/M3_Stuart" title="M3 Stuart">M3 Stuart</a> light tanks. In April and May 1942, the <a href="/wiki/1st_Armoured_Division_(Australia)" title="1st Armoured Division (Australia)">1st Armoured Division</a>'s regiments began re-equipping with M3 Grants and completed their training in a series of large exercises around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrabri,_New_South_Wales" title="Narrabri, New South Wales">Narrabri, New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-28">[26]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2nd_Armoured_Division_(Australia)" title="2nd Armoured Division (Australia)">2nd</a> and <a href="/wiki/3rd_Armoured_Division_(Australia)" title="3rd Armoured Division (Australia)">3rd Armoured Divisions</a> were officially formed in 1942 and were also partly equipped with M3 Grants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-29">[27]</a></sup> In January 1943, the main body of the 1st Armoured Division was deployed to home defence duties between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geraldton,_Western_Australia" title="Geraldton, Western Australia">Geraldton, Western Australia</a>, where it formed part of <a href="/wiki/III_Corps_(Australia)" title="III Corps (Australia)">III Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-28">[26]</a></sup> However, the Grants were deemed unsuitable for combat duties overseas and M3 units were re-equipped with the <a href="/wiki/Matilda_II" title="Matilda II">Matilda II</a> before being deployed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Guinea_Campaign" title="New Guinea Campaign">New Guinea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borneo_Campaign" title="Borneo Campaign">Borneo Campaigns</a>. Due to personnel shortages, all three divisions were officially disbanded during 1943 and downgraded to brigade- and battalion-level units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tarawa" title="Battle of Tarawa">battle for Tarawa island</a> in 1943, the US Army <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Makin" title="Battle of Makin">attacked nearby Makin Island</a>, which was considered a less costly operation. The army was supported by a platoon of M3A5 Lee medium tanks from the US Army's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/193rd_Tank_Battalion" title="193rd Tank Battalion">193rd Tank Battalion</a>, making this battle the only combat use of the M3 by America in the Pacific Theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> The US Marine Corps did not use M3 Lees; their light tank battalions were equipped with M3 Stuarts until they were replaced by M4 Shermans in mid 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_use_in_Australia">Post-war use in Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-war use in Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the war, the Australian Army had converted some M3 Grants for special purposes, including a small number of bulldozer variants, <a href="/wiki/Beach_armoured_recovery_vehicle" title="Beach armoured recovery vehicle">beach armoured recovery vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_vehicle#Tracked" title="Amphibious vehicle">wader</a> prototypes.</p>
<p>Following the end of the war 14 of the Australian Grants were converted to a local <a href="/wiki/Self-propelled_gun" title="Self-propelled gun">self-propelled gun</a> design, the <a href="/wiki/Yeramba" title="Yeramba">Yeramba</a>, becoming the only SPG ever deployed by the Australian Army. Fitted with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/25-pounder" title="25-pounder">25-pounder</a> field gun, the Yerambas remained in service with the 22nd Field Regiment, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Artillery" title="Royal Australian Artillery">Royal Australian Artillery</a>, until the late 1950s.</p>
<p>Many M3s deemed surplus to Australian Army requirements were acquired by civilian buyers during the 1950s and 1960s for conversion to earthmoving equipment and/or tractors.</p>

<p>Overall, the M3 was able to cope with the battlefield of 1942. Its armor and firepower were the equal or superior to most of the threats it faced, especially in the Pacific. Long-range, high velocity guns were not yet common on German tanks in the African theater. However, the rapid pace of tank development meant that the M3 was very quickly outclassed. By mid-1942, with the introduction of the German <a href="/wiki/Tiger_I" title="Tiger I">Tiger I</a>, the up-gunning of the <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a> to <a href="/wiki/7.5_cm_KwK_40" title="7.5 cm KwK 40">a long 75-mm gun</a>, and the first appearance in 1943 of the <a href="/wiki/Panther_tank" title="Panther tank">Panther</a>, along with the availability of large numbers of Shermans, the M3 was withdrawn from service in the European Theater.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_variants">US variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: US variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>M3 (Lee I/Grant I)</dt>
<dd>Riveted hull, high profile turret, gasoline engine. 4,724 built.</dd>
<dt>M3A1 (Lee II)</dt>
<dd>Cast (rounded) upper hull. 300 built.</dd>
<dt>M3A2 (Lee III)</dt>
<dd>Welded (sharp edged) hull. Only 12 vehicles produced.</dd>
<dt>M3A3 (Lee IV/Lee V)</dt>
<dd>Twin <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Diesel_Series_71" title="Detroit Diesel Series 71">GM 6-71</a> <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a> variant of welded hull. Side doors welded shut or eliminated. 322 built.</dd>
<dt>M3A4 (Lee VI)</dt>
<dd>Stretched riveted hull, 1 x <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_A57_multibank" title="Chrysler A57 multibank">A57 Multibank</a> engine, made up of five 4.12 litre displacement, 6-cyl L-head car engines (block upwards) mated to a common crankshaft, displacement 21 litres, 470 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a> (350 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>; 480 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_horsepower" title="Metric horsepower">PS</a>) at 2,700 rpm. Side doors eliminated. 109 built.</dd>
<dt>M3A5 (Grant II)</dt>
<dd>Twin GM 6-71 diesel variant of riveted hull M3. Although it had the original <i>Lee</i> turret, it was referred by the British as <i>Grant II</i>. 591 built.</dd>
<dt>M31 Tank Recovery Vehicle (Grant ARV I)</dt>
<dd>Based on M3 <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">chassis</a>, with dummy turret and dummy 75 gun. A 60,000 lb (27,000 kg) winch installed.</dd>
<dt>M31B1 Tank Recovery Vehicle</dt>
<dd>Based on M3A3.</dd>
<dt>M31B2 Tank Recovery Vehicle</dt>
<dd>Based on M3A5.</dd>
<dt>M33 Prime Mover</dt>
<dd>M31 TRV converted to the artillery tractor role, with turret and crane removed. 109 vehicles were converted in 1943-44.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/M7_Priest" title="M7 Priest">105 mm Howitzer Motor Carriage M7</a> (<i>Priest</i>)</dt>
<dd>105 mm M1/M2 <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzer</a> installed in open <a href="/wiki/Superstructure" title="Superstructure">superstructure</a>. A gunless version was used as an OP (observation post vehicle)</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/M12_Gun_Motor_Carriage" title="M12 Gun Motor Carriage">155 mm Gun Motor Carriage M12</a></dt>
<dd>Designed as the T6. A 155 mm howitzer on M3 chassis. 100 built in 1942-1943. M30 Cargo Carrier on same chassis to transport gun crew and ammunition.</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_appearances">Film appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M3_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Film appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
