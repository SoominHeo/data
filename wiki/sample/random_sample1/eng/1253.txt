<p>The <b>M40 recoilless rifle</b> is a lightweight,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordnance_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ordnance-3">[3]</a></sup> portable, crew-served 105 mm weapon intended primarily as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-tank" title="Anti-tank">anti-tank</a> <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a> made in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The weapon is commonly described as being 106 mm, but it is in fact 105 mm; the 106 mm designation was intended to prevent confusion with the incompatible 105 mm ammunition from the failed M27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It could also be employed in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antipersonnel" title="Antipersonnel">antipersonnel</a> role with the use of the antipersonnel-tracer flechette round. It can be fired primarily from a wheeled ground mount. The air-cooled, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breech-loaded" title="Breech-loaded">breech-loaded</a>, <a href="/wiki/Single-shot" title="Single-shot">single-shot</a> <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifle</a> fired fixed <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a>. It was designed for direct firing only, and <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)" title="Sight (device)">sighting</a> equipment for this purpose was furnished with each weapon.</p>
<p>The M27 recoilless rifle was a 105-mm weapon developed in the early 1950s and fielded in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. Although a recoilless rifle of this caliber had been a concept since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the weapon was hurriedly produced with the onset of the Korean War. The speed with which it was developed and fielded resulted in problems with reliability caused by trunnions that were mounted too far to the rear. The M27 was also considered too heavy by the U.S. Army and had a disappointing effective range due to the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Spotting_rifle" title="Spotting rifle">spotting rifle</a>. Taking the M27 as the basis for a new design, the Army developed an improved version of the M27 that was type-designated the M40 106-mm recoilless rifle in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Originally, along with its type-designation, it was also given the official name <b>BAT</b> for <i>Battalion Anti-Tank gun</i>, but that was soon dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although unsuitable for military purposes, M27 recoilless rifles were used to trigger controlled avalanches at ski resorts and mountain passes in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M40 primarily saw action during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. It was later replaced by the <a href="/wiki/BGM-71_TOW" title="BGM-71 TOW">BGM-71 TOW</a> anti-tank missile system. The weapon was also used by anti-communist forces in <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> mounted on <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rovers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M40_recoilless_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The M40 is shaped like a long tube with an <b>M8C .50 cal spotting rifle</b> above. The spotting rifle fires a round whose trajectory closely matches that of the 105 mm round and gives off a puff of smoke on impact with the target. On the left side, there is an elevating wheel, in the center of which is the trigger wheel used to fine adjust the elevation and at the same time firing the spotting rifle when pulled, and the gun when pushed. The mounting is a <a href="/wiki/Tripod" title="Tripod">tripod</a>, but the front leg has a castering wheel. On top of the mount is a traverse wheel. On the center of the traverse wheel is a locking wheel, when the wheel is down, the rifle is locked in traverse, and can only be moved right and left with the traverse wheel. When the wheel is raised, the rifle can be traversed by hand. <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> produced a two-wheeled mount for the M40.</p>
<p>The whole mounting can be placed on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M151" title="M151">M151</a> <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> for mobile use. It has also been mounted on M38A1 Willys Jeep, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_Rover_(Series/Defender)" title="Land Rover (Series/Defender)">Land Rover Defenders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M113_Armored_Personnel_Carrier" title="M113 Armored Personnel Carrier">M113s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_G-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz G-Class">Mercedes-Benz G-Wagen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMMWV" title="HMMWV">HMMWVs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hotchkiss_M201" title="Hotchkiss M201">Hotchkiss M201</a> jeeps, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Land_Cruiser" title="Toyota Land Cruiser">Toyota Land Cruisers</a>, <a href="/wiki/AIL_Storm" title="AIL Storm">AIL Storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Military_M274_Truck,_Platform,_Utility_1/2_Ton,_4X4" title="U.S. Military M274 Truck, Platform, Utility 1/2 Ton, 4X4">M274 Mechanical Mules</a>. They were also used on US Navy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minesweeper_(ship)" title="Minesweeper (ship)">minesweepers</a> (MSO) during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Market_Time" title="Operation Market Time">Operation Market Time</a> in Vietnam.</p>
<p>A special vehicle called the <a href="/wiki/M50_Ontos" title="M50 Ontos">Ontos</a> carried six M40s. A version specific to the T195E5 mount, the M40A1C, was used. It was used only by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> produced a self-propelled gun called the <a href="/wiki/Type_60_Self-propelled_106_mm_Recoilless_Gun" title="Type 60 Self-propelled 106 mm Recoilless Gun">Type 60</a>, which carried two side by side. Some <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistani</a> M113s have a dual mounting. Three Panagopoulos coastal patrol boats class of the Hellenic Coast Guard and the Hellenic Navy in service of 1976-2003 was armed with two sextuples M40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M40 was a very successful export item and continues to be used by <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> (ROK), <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Marine_Corps" title="Republic of China Marine Corps">Republic of China Marine Corps</a>), <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> and many others, as well as anti-government forces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_Civil_War" title="2011 Libyan Civil War">Libyan Civil War</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syrian_civil_war" title="Syrian civil war">Syrian civil war</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the period 1958–1990, the antitank companies of the Swiss Army Infantry Regiments were equipped with 12 M40 guns.</p>
<p>It is manufactured in Iran by <a href="/wiki/Defense_Industries_Organization" title="Defense Industries Organization">Defense Industries Organization</a> as the ANTI-TANK GUN 106.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ammunition">Ammunition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M40_recoilless_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ammunition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ammunition for the 105 mm rifle was issued as one-piece fixed cartridges. The term "fixed" means that the projectile and the cartridge case are crimped together. This ensures correct alignment of the <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectile</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartridge_case" title="Cartridge case">cartridge case</a>. It also permits faster loading because the projectile and the cartridge case are loaded as one unit. The rear end of the cartridge case is perforated, to allow the propellant gas to escape through the vented breech, thus neutralizing recoil. Most projectiles (except HEAT) used are pre-engraved, that is, the rotating bands are cut to engage the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rifled" title="Rifled">rifled</a> bore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> If the round was not rotated slightly when loading the M40 it could result in jamming in the breech.</p>
<p>Types of ammunition included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HEAT" title="HEAT">HEAT</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Explosive" title="High Explosive">High Explosive</a> Plastic-<a href="/wiki/Tracer_ammunition" title="Tracer ammunition">Tracer</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive_squash_head" title="High explosive squash head">HEP-T</a>), <a href="/wiki/Canister_shot" title="Canister shot">canister</a>, High Explosive Anti Personnel, and the M368 dummy round which could not be fired and was used for crew drill. The original U.S. HEAT round penetrated more than 400 mm of armor. Near the end of the M40's service life, both Austria and Sweden produced HEAT rounds for the weapon capable of penetrating more than 700 mm of armor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spotting_Rifle">Spotting Rifle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M40_recoilless_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spotting Rifle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ammunition for the 0.50 cal spotting rifle is not <a href="/wiki/.50_BMG" title=".50 BMG">.50 BMG</a>. The round used is a special round designed to simulate the flight path of the 106 mm ammunition.</p>
<p>Although the spotting rifle could conceivably be used in an antipersonnel role, historical U.S. military doctrine strongly discouraged this use. This limitation was entirely tactical in nature, having been intended to help conceal the vulnerable M40 and its crew from the enemy before the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-tank" title="Anti-tank">anti-tank</a> gun could be fired; however, this restriction is believed to be the source of a long-standing misconception that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_war" title="Laws of war">laws of war</a> restrict the use of .50-caliber projectiles against enemy personnel more generally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
