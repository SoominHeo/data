<p>The <b>M551 "Sheridan" AR/AAV</b> (Armored Reconnaissance/Airborne Assault Vehicle) was a <a href="/wiki/Light_tank" title="Light tank">light tank</a> developed by the United States and named after <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> General <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sheridan" title="Philip Sheridan">Philip Sheridan</a>. It was designed to be landed by parachute and to swim across rivers. It was armed with the technically advanced but troublesome M81/M81 Modified/M81E1 152mm gun/launcher, which fired conventional ammunition and the <a href="/wiki/MGM-51_Shillelagh" title="MGM-51 Shillelagh">MGM-51 Shillelagh</a> guided anti-tank missile.</p>
<p>The M551 Sheridan entered service with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> in 1967. At the urging of General <a href="/wiki/Creighton_Abrams" title="Creighton Abrams">Creighton Abrams</a>, the U.S. Commander of Military Forces in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a> at the time, the M551 was rushed into combat service in Vietnam in January 1969. In April and August 1969, M551s were deployed to units in Europe and Korea, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunnicutt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunnicutt-3">[3]</a></sup> Now retired from service, it saw extensive combat in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, and limited service in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Just_Cause" title="Operation Just Cause">Operation Just Cause</a> (<a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunnicutt_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hunnicutt-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Army" title="Australian Army">Australian Army</a> also trialled two Sheridans during 1967 and 1968, but judged that the type did not meet its requirements.</p>
<p>At the time of the M551's acceptance into service production in 1966,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunnicutt_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hunnicutt-3">[3]</a></sup> the United States Army no longer used the heavy, medium, and light tank classifications. In 1960, with the deactivation of its last (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M103_heavy_tank" title="M103 heavy tank">M103</a>) heavy tank battalion, and the fielding of the new <a href="/wiki/M60_Patton" title="M60 Patton">M60</a> series tank, the U.S. Army had adopted a <a href="/wiki/Main_battle_tank" title="Main battle tank">main battle tank</a> (MBT) doctrine; a single tank filling all combat roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunnicutt-Firepower_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunnicutt-Firepower-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunnicutt-Patton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunnicutt-Patton-5">[5]</a></sup> The U.S. Army still retained the <a href="/wiki/M41_Walker_Bulldog" title="M41 Walker Bulldog">M41 Walker Bulldog</a> light tank in the Army National Guard, but other than the units undergoing the transitional process, the regular army consisted of MBTs. Partly because of this policy, the new M551 could not be classified as a light tank, and was officially classified as an "Armored Reconnaissance/Airborne Assault Vehicle".</p>
<p>The Sheridan was retired without replacement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the immediate post-World War II era, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">US Army</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/M41_Walker_Bulldog" title="M41 Walker Bulldog">M41 Walker Bulldog</a> into service to fill the role of a <a href="/wiki/Light_tank" title="Light tank">light tank</a>. The lifespan of the M41 was fairly short; at 25 tons it was considered too heavy to be a true light tank, and had a rather short cruising range. Plans were started to build an even lighter replacement mounting the same gun, resulting in the <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/T71" title="T71">T71</a> and <a href="/wiki/T92_Light_Tank" title="T92 Light Tank">T92</a> test designs. Two prototypes of the 19 ton T92 were later ordered. However, as the prototypes were entering testing, information about the new Soviet <a href="/wiki/PT-76" title="PT-76">PT-76</a> tank became available. The PT-76 was an amphibious light tank, and soon there were demands that any U.S. light tank should be able to swim as well. The T92 was already in the prototype stage and could not be easily refitted for this role, so the design of an entirely new system started as the XM551.</p>
<p>The vehicle designed to mount the gun had a steel turret and aluminum hull. Although the hull could defeat heavy machinegun fire of up to 12.7mm AP,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> it was easily defeated by rocket propelled grenades, which could destroy the vehicle if the spalling contacted the <a href="/wiki/Caseless_ammunition" title="Caseless ammunition">caseless main gun rounds</a>. Like the M113 armored personnel carrier, it was also vulnerable to mines.</p>
<p>Swimming capability was provided by a flotation screen, similar to that used by the World War II, amphibious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DD_Tank" title="DD Tank">DD Tanks</a>. The front armor was overlain by a wooden "surfboard", actually three folded layers, hinged together. This could be opened up into a sloping vertical surface in front of the driver providing a bow of a boat hull, about level with the top of the turret. Fabric formed the rest of the water barrier, folding up from compartments lining the upper corner where the side met the top of the hull, and held up at the back with poles. The front of the "hull" was provided with a plastic window, but in practice it was found that water splashing onto it made it almost useless, and the driver instead usually had to take steering directions from the vehicle commander. The <a href="/wiki/M2_Bradley" title="M2 Bradley">M2 Bradley</a> adopted a similar solution, but dropped it with upgraded armor.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, firing the gun often adversely affected the delicate electronics, which were at the early stages of the transition to solid state devices, so the missile and its guidance system was omitted from vehicles deployed to Vietnam. The expensive missile ended up almost never being <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fire_in_anger" title="wikt:fire in anger">fired in anger</a>, despite a production run of 88,000 units.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Production started on 29 July 1966, and it entered service in June 1967 with 1st Battalion, 63rd Armor Regiment at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Riley" title="Fort Riley">Fort Riley</a>. In the end, 1,662 M551s were built between 1966 and 2 November 1970. Total cost of the M551 program was $1.3 billion. The M81 gun had problems with cracks developing near the breech after repeated firing, a problem that was later tracked to the "key" on the missiles that ran in a slot cut into the barrel. Most field units were modified to help address the problem, but later the modified M81E1 was introduced with a shallower slot, along with a matching modification to the missile, that cured the problem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The gun also has been criticized for having too much recoil for the vehicle weight, the second and even third road wheels coming clear off the ground when the main gun fired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some were experimentally fitted with conventional 76mm guns, but these never entered service.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_history">Service history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_War">Vietnam War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vietnam War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M551_Sheridan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="986" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/M551_Sheridan.jpg/220px-M551_Sheridan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/M551_Sheridan.jpg/330px-M551_Sheridan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/M551_Sheridan.jpg/440px-M551_Sheridan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M551_Sheridan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An M551 Sheridan and crew of the 3rd Squadron, 4th Cavalry in Vietnam (Note the add-on belly armor)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The US Army staff in Washington had been recommending since 1966 to the commander of US Forces in South Vietnam, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Westmoreland" title="General Westmoreland">General Westmoreland</a>, that the Sheridan should be used in Vietnam. However, since the main gun ammunition was not available, he argued that it was simply a $300,000 machine gun platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1968, the new, or soon to be, US commander in South Vietnam, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Creighton_Abrams" title="General Creighton Abrams">General Creighton Abrams</a>, had been notified that the 152mm shells were now available for the Sheridan. However, as General Abrams began to make preparations for the equipping of US Cavalry squadrons for the vehicle, the affected squadrons expressed their concerns that the new aluminum tanks were not only highly vulnerable to mines and anti-tank rocket fire, but they would not be as capable of "jungle busting" as the <a href="/wiki/M48_Patton" title="M48 Patton">M48A3</a> medium tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starry_p._143_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Starry_p._143-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 1968, General Abrams met with <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_S._Patton_IV" title="George S. Patton IV">George S. Patton IV</a> - the son of World War II <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Patton" title="General Patton">General Patton</a> - who was the regimental commander of the <a href="/wiki/11th_Armored_Cavalry_Regiment" title="11th Armored Cavalry Regiment">11th Armored Cavalry Regiment</a> (11th ACR Blackhorse), the only full regiment of cavalry in Vietnam. When General Abrams mentioned the cavalry's concerns over the new vehicle, Patton recommended that the Sheridans be combat tested by a <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">divisional</a> cavalry squadron as well as a squadron from his own <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiment</a>; as the squadrons had completely different missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starry_p._143_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Starry_p._143-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_deployment">First deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first Sheridans to arrive in Vietnam (January 1969) were accompanied by their factory representatives, instructors, and evaluators as the new vehicles were issued to the 3rd Squadron, 4th Cavalry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the 1st Squadron, 11th ACR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starry_p._143_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Starry_p._143-8">[8]</a></sup> By the end of 1970, there were more than 200 Sheridans in Vietnam,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and they stayed in the field until the last US armored cavalry unit, the 1st Squadron, 1st Cavalry prepared for re-deployment back to the United States on 10 April 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By the end of its combat debut in 1972, the Sheridan had seen extensive action in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, being assigned to nearly all armored cavalry squadrons involved in that conflict. In 1969, armored cavalry units (minus the 11th ACR, which retained its <a href="/wiki/M48_Patton" title="M48 Patton">M48 Patton</a> tank companies) began replacing their M48 Patton tanks, which in turn were normally transferred to the South Vietnamese military. Like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontos" title="Ontos">M50 Ontos</a> anti-tank vehicle, the battle reports from the troops were sometimes glowing, while the reports higher up the chain of command were often negative. This was largely due to the high casualty rate of both Sheridans and their crews as mines and <a href="/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenade" title="Rocket-propelled grenade">rocket-propelled grenades</a> (RPGs) that would only damage an M48 Patton tank, would destroy the Sheridan and kill or wound most, if not all, of its crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1969 evaluation of the vehicles found that the M551 was employed in reconnaissance, night patrol and road clearing, accumulating 39,455 road miles and 520 combat missions, with a ready rate of 81.3 percent. Despite vulnerability to rockets and mines, it was judged worthy of applying modifications and equipping all cavalry squadrons with the Sheridan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washer_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-washer-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_combat.2Ffirst_losses">First combat/first losses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First combat/first losses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In addition to the problems presented by aluminum construction, the Sheridan had a defect that no other common armored vehicle possessed: it fired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caseless" title="Caseless">caseless</a> 152mm main gun rounds. These rounds were "fixed", meaning that, unlike the artillery, the warhead was factory attached to the propellant, and if the warhead separated from the propellant during loading, which was not uncommon, the crewmen were instructed not to load the round. Sometimes, these unspent propellant charges remained on the turret floor due to the emergencies at the time, and in either case, all of the remaining serviceable 152mm shells still remained caseless, albeit attached to their warheads, and sleeved into a re-usable white nine-ply nylon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunstan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunstan-14">[14]</a></sup> bag, which was form-fitted to hold the propellant portion of the shell. The white/silver-colored bag had a strap attached to the bottom, which the loader would grab and pull off prior to gently inserting the shell into the breech. Once a mine or <a href="/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenade" title="Rocket-propelled grenade">RPG</a> type weapon created the spark, smoke and fire became imminent, and it became a matter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standing_operating_procedure" title="Standing operating procedure">Standing Operating Procedure</a> to abandon the tank immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starry_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-starry-15">[15]</a></sup> On 15 February 1969, just one month after the Sheridan's arrival in South Vietnam, an M551 from the 3rd Squadron, 4th Cavalry detonated a 25-pound pressure-triggered <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">land mine</a>, which ruptured its hull and ignited the 152mm shells, resulting in a secondary explosion that destroyed the tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starry_p._143_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Starry_p._143-8">[8]</a></sup> In late 1969, nine Sheridans from the 4th Squadron, 12th Cavalry were fording a river near the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Vietnamese Demilitarized Zone">DMZ</a>, when three of the M551s detonated mines, completely destroying them. On March 1971, five Sheridans from the 11th ACR were lost in one day to RPG fire; all five vehicles burst into flames and were totally destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunstan_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunstan-14">[14]</a></sup> It became a common scene to observe melted Sheridan hulls with their sunken steel turrets sitting at odd angles with their gun tubes pointing towards the sky in various parts of the country, either awaiting final disposition, or simply forgotten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nolan_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nolan-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>However, the Sheridan did not get stuck in the mud as often as the 52-ton <a href="/wiki/M48_Patton" title="M48 Patton">M48 Patton</a> tank did, nor did it throw its track off as often.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This alone was enough to win the tank crews' favor. The light weight and high mobility proved their worth, and the gun proved an effective anti-personnel weapon when used with either the M657 <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" title="Explosive material">HE</a> shell or the M625 <a href="/wiki/Canister_shot" title="Canister shot">canister round</a>, which used thousands of <a href="/wiki/Flechette" title="Flechette">flechettes</a> as projectiles.</p>
<p>Although an average M48 Patton crew could fire as many as seventeen 90mm shells during a "<a href="/wiki/Mad_minute" title="Mad minute">mad minute</a>" (60 seconds with all guns firing-on command), the Sheridan was known to put out only two 152mm shells during the same time frame. While the M48's 90mm cannon fired fixed metallic cased rounds, the 152mm was caseless. The caseless rounds needed air vents to clear the gun tube and breech prior to loading another round, while the M48 breech block opened as the used shell was ejected and closed as the new shell was shoved in. The faster the loader, the faster the Patton's gun could be fired. For the Sheridan, the loader had to wait for the mechanism. After firing, the loader would have to wait, as the breech slowly opened rearward then turned downward. After another instrument indicated that all turret systems were still operational, the loader would gently push the 152mm fixed round into the breech and watch the breech block slowly rotate upward, then forward into the breech, then again, wait for the lights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanton_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-stanton-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Sheridan was much appreciated by the infantry, who were desperate for direct-fire support, and generally served in armored cavalry units along with <a href="/wiki/M113_armored_personnel_carrier#M113_Armored_Cavalry_Assault_Vehicle_.28ACAV.29" title="M113 armored personnel carrier">ACAVs</a> (M113s). Armor units consisted solely of tanks (minus headquarters company) and mechanized infantry units consisted solely of M113s. In this role, the real problem with the Sheridan was its limited ammunition load; normally, only 20 rounds and 8 missiles; although, as the M551s in Vietnam service were not equipped with missiles or their guidance equipment, this increased the basic load of conventional rounds. Sheridan losses were heavy during normal operations, largely due to land mines and anti-armor weapons, but were especially heavy after President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> ordered US forces into <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> on 1 May 1970 in which, among other cavalry squadrons, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11th_Armored_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="11th Armored Cavalry Regiment (United States)">11th Armored Cavalry Regiment</a> (Blackhorse) was thrown into the fight. The second heaviest losses were during the U.S. Army's final offensive of the war, operation <a href="/wiki/Operation_Lam_Son_719#Dewey_Canyon_II" title="Operation Lam Son 719">Dewey Canyon II</a>, when the Cavalry's remaining Sheridan squadrons met near disaster on the Lao border during the early months of 1971, in particular the 1st Squadron, 1st Cavalry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nolan_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-nolan-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_field_modifications">Combat field modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Combat field modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A common field-modification was to mount a large <a href="/wiki/Gun_shield" title="Gun shield">steel shield</a>, known as an "ACAV set" (Armored Cavalry Assault Vehicle), around the commander's 50-cal. (12.7 mm) gun, allowing it to be fired with some level of protection. The driver has an unusual rotating hatch which has vision blocks when rotated forward. Included with the set was an extra layer of steel belly armor which was bolted onto the vehicle's bottom, although only covering from the front to half way to the end, possibly due to weight reasons.</p>
<p>A standard modification made during the mid-70s was the addition of the "Cereal Bowl" commander's cupola. This mod came about due to the "broken rib" effect that occurred when the Sheridan fired conventional rounds; the recoil would pitch the commander against the armor plating, resulting in cracked ribs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Vietnam_service">Post-Vietnam service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-Vietnam service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Army began to phase out the Sheridan in 1978, although at the time there was no real replacement. Nevertheless, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._82nd_Airborne_Division" title="U.S. 82nd Airborne Division">82d Airborne Division</a> was able to keep them until 1996. The Sheridan was the only "rapidly" air-deployable tank in the inventory. Their units were later upgraded to the <b>M551A1 TTS</b> model, including a thermal sighting system for the commander and gunner.</p>
<p>In the early 1980s, the M551A1 was fitted with a visual modification kit to resemble <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> vehicles at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Training_Center" title="National Training Center">National Training Center</a> at Fort Irwin, California. These modified vehicles no longer fulfill that role, having been retired at the end of 2003 and subsequently scrapped or made available as "hard targets" or, in a few cases, as museum pieces. Many were dumped to create <a href="/wiki/Artificial_reef" title="Artificial reef">artificial reefs</a>.</p>
<p>During Operations <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Shield" title="Operation Desert Shield">Desert Shield</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_Storm" title="Desert Storm">Desert Storm</a>, 51 Sheridans were deployed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._82nd_Airborne_Division" title="U.S. 82nd Airborne Division">82nd Airborne Division</a>, and were among the first tanks to be sent. Although photos that were published at the time showed rows of Sheridans ready to defend against Iraqi tanks, they would have a limited effect against the Soviet-designed <a href="/wiki/T-72" title="T-72">T-72s</a> which comprised the bulk of the Iraqi Republican Guard. Their role was limited to reconnaissance due to their age and light armor. It is likely that no more than six Shillelagh missiles were fired<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> at Iraqi anti-tank guns or T-55s; this appears to be the only occasion in which Shillelagh missiles were fired in a combat environment, from the inventory of 88,000 missiles produced.</p>
<p>The Sheridan's only air drop in combat occurred during the <a href="/wiki/United_States_invasion_of_Panama" title="United States invasion of Panama">United States invasion of Panama</a> (Operation Just Cause) in 1989, when fourteen M551s were deployed: four were transported by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-5_Galaxy" title="C-5 Galaxy">C-5 Galaxy</a> and ten were dropped by air, though two Sheridans were destroyed on landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The four M551s transported by the C-5 were secretly deployed to Panama<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in November 1989, where they were attached to TF Bayonet (193rd Infantry Brigade), and attached down further to TF Gator. These Sheridans took part in the attack on the Commandancia, initially supported by fire from Quarry Heights, and later displacing forward into the city. As part of Team Armor, these Sheridans later provided support to JSOC elements as they secured high-value targets throughout Panama City. The remaining eight Sheridans were delivered to <a href="/wiki/Tocumen_International_Airport" title="Tocumen International Airport">Torrijos-Tocumen Airport</a> some hours after H-hour by Low-Velocity Airdrop (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HALO/HAHO" title="HALO/HAHO">LVAD</a>) technique from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130</a> transports. The Sheridans' performance received mixed reviews. They were lauded by their operators and some commanders as providing firepower in needed situations to destroy hard targets. However, the Sheridans' use just of HEAT rounds limited their effectiveness against reinforced concrete construction.</p>

<p>Several attempts to improve or replace the Sheridan have been made over the years since it was introduced. Several experimental versions of the Sheridan mounting a new turret carrying the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>-standard 105mm gun were made, but the resulting recoil was so great as to make the vehicle almost unusable. Several possible replacements for the M551 were tested as a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M8-AGS" title="M8-AGS">XM8 Armored Gun System</a> effort of the 1980s, but none of these entered service. The <a href="/wiki/M1128_Mobile_Gun_System" title="M1128 Mobile Gun System">M1128 Mobile Gun System</a> variation of the <a href="/wiki/Stryker" title="Stryker">Stryker</a> <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicle</a> is the nearest analogue in the modern US Army force structure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_trials">Australian trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Australian trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 1967 and 1968 the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Army" title="Australian Army">Australian Army</a> trialled two Sheridans to determine if the type met a requirement for light armored fighting vehicles to serve with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Armoured_Corps" title="Royal Australian Armoured Corps">Royal Australian Armoured Corps</a> newly formed cavalry regiments. The main trials took place in the tropical <a href="/wiki/Innisfail,_Queensland" title="Innisfail, Queensland">Innisfail</a> area of north Queensland between January and June 1968. In January 1969 the Minister for the Army announced that Australia would not purchase any Sheridans as the tanks did not meet the Army's requirements. The main shortcoming revealed in the trials concerned the safety of the combustible case. The two Sheridans were returned to the US Army in early 1969, and the Australian Army met its requirement by fitting turrets from <a href="/wiki/Alvis_Saladin" title="Alvis Saladin">Alvis Saladin</a> armoured cars to M113 armored personnel carriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handel_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-handel-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armament">Armament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Armament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="720" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg/220px-MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg/330px-MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg/440px-MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MGM-51_Shillelagh2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/MGM-51_Shillelagh" title="MGM-51 Shillelagh">MGM-51 Shillelagh</a> fired from a Sheridan</div>

<p>Building a vehicle lighter than the T-92 required an innovative solution for the main armament. A gun firing <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy_penetrator" title="Kinetic energy penetrator">kinetic energy penetrators</a> to defeat modern tanks at reasonable range was too large for the XM551; gun weight was typically dependent on caliber and <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">muzzle velocity</a>. This was solved by arming the XM551 with a 152mm gun firing low velocity <b>M81</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive_anti-tank_warhead" title="High explosive anti-tank warhead">HEAT</a> rounds. The large caliber ensured a powerful <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">shaped charge</a> capable of destroying tanks; velocity has no impact on shaped charge penetration, and the short gun would reduce weight.</p>
<p>The gun was ideal for infantry support. The large, low velocity gun could fire a large explosive shell or canister shot. In comparison, high-velocity anti-tank guns over-penetrated soft targets, while smaller caliber weapons could not carry as great a payload.</p>
<p>The M81E1 was not ideal in medium- and long-range tank engagements as its low velocity produced extended flight times, and made it difficult to lead moving targets. Therefore, the gun was also designed to fire <a href="/wiki/MGM-51_Shillelagh" title="MGM-51 Shillelagh">MGM-51 Shillelagh</a> <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_missile" title="Anti-tank missile">anti-tank missiles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-23">[23]</a></sup> The low launch velocity against longer-ranged targets was not an issue for the missile. However, the MGM-51 was considered a risky project.</p>
<p>A number of existing vehicles already mounted only ATGMs, or alternately <a href="/wiki/Recoilless_rifle" title="Recoilless rifle">recoilless rifles</a> like the <a href="/wiki/M50_Ontos" title="M50 Ontos">M50 Ontos</a>, but these typically had limited utility in the infantry support role, or in the case of Ontos could not be reloaded from within the vehicle. The XM551 appeared to offer a superior balance between anti-tank and infantry support.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobility">Mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactical_mobility">Tactical mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tactical mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SheridanSwimboard.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/SheridanSwimboard.JPG/220px-SheridanSwimboard.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/SheridanSwimboard.JPG/330px-SheridanSwimboard.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/SheridanSwimboard.JPG/440px-SheridanSwimboard.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Sheridan was powered by a large 300-hp (224 kW) Detroit Diesel 6V53T diesel engine. The XM551 thus had an excellent <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">power-to-weight ratio</a> and mobility, able to run at speeds up to 45 mph. However, the vehicle proved to be very noisy and unreliable under combat conditions.</p>
<p>The Sheridan could swim across a river that was about 50 yards wide. Tanks in the Patton series (<a href="/wiki/M46_Patton" title="M46 Patton">M46</a>, <a href="/wiki/M47_Patton" title="M47 Patton">M47</a>, <a href="/wiki/M48_Patton" title="M48 Patton">M48</a>), as well as the <a href="/wiki/M60_Patton" title="M60 Patton">M60</a> <a href="/wiki/Main_battle_tank" title="Main battle tank">main battle tank</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> could not perform these operations; they would have to crawl along the river bottoms using <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_snorkel" title="Vehicle snorkel">snorkels</a>. Not by design, it was found that the swimming hardware acted to reduce the effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenade" title="Rocket-propelled grenade">RPG</a> hits, but it was rarely used in Vietnam.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_mobility">Strategic mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Strategic mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C-130_airdrop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="432" data-file-width="772" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/C-130_airdrop.jpg/220px-C-130_airdrop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/C-130_airdrop.jpg/330px-C-130_airdrop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/C-130_airdrop.jpg/440px-C-130_airdrop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C-130_airdrop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130</a> delivering an M551 Sheridan tank using LAPES (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Altitude_Parachute_Extraction_System" title="Low Altitude Parachute Extraction System">Low Altitude Parachute Extraction System</a>).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Sheridan can be rigged for low-velocity airdrop from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130</a> (42,000 lb max load), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-141" title="C-141">C-141</a> aircraft (38,500 lb max load),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garys_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-garys-25">[25]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-5_Galaxy" title="Lockheed C-5 Galaxy">C-5</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vid-drop_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-vid-drop-26">[26]</a></sup> Many films exist showing the Sheridan being pulled out of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130</a> Hercules transport by brake chutes and skidding to a stop. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Altitude_Parachute_Extraction_System" title="Low Altitude Parachute Extraction System">Low Altitude Parachute Extraction System</a> (LAPES) is a somewhat risky maneuver that allows accurate delivery onto a field when landing is not possible, and the practice was stopped in the late 1990s. The tank is strapped down to a special pallet which absorbs most of the landing impact. The crew does not ride in the tank during extraction, but parachutes from another plane. On landing, they go to their tank, release the lines, and drive it away.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M551_Sheridan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>XM551/M551</b> - The M551 was the basic production model, beginning production in 1967. The XM551 had been a limited run pre-production model produced in 1965.
<ul>
<li><b>"Two Box" M551</b> - With the obvious shortcomings of the Shillelagh missile, all but two of the guidance and fire control components of the missile system were removed (the power supply and rate sensor were retained. These were needed for stabilized turret operation.). The resulting additional space was filled with two separate boxes, one for <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> ammunition (coaxial machine gun), and one for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12.7%C3%9799mm_NATO" title="12.7×99mm NATO">12.7×99mm NATO</a> (.50 BMG) ammunition, and the missile stowage was redesigned to accept conventional rounds.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>M551A1</b> - Upgraded M551 with AN/VVG-1 laser rangefinder.
<ul>
<li><b>M551A1 TTS</b> - Tank Thermal Sight, fitted with the AN/VSG-2B thermal sight unit, similar to the unit used on the M60A3 MBT. This later became standard to all M551A1s.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>M551 NTC</b> - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Training_Center" title="National Training Center">National Training Center</a>. Using M551 hulls, the NTC created a number of mock vehicles for training exercises resembling common Soviet/Warsaw pact types. They were also known as '<a href="/wiki/Vismod" title="Vismod">vismods</a>', short for VISually MODified. They have since been retired in favor of similarly converted <a href="/wiki/M113_armored_personnel_carrier" title="M113 armored personnel carrier">M113s</a> and <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a>.</li>
</ul>
