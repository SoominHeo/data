<p><i><b>Mad</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Humor_magazine" title="Humor magazine">humor magazine</a> founded in 1952 by editor <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">Harvey Kurtzman</a> and publisher <a href="/wiki/William_Gaines" title="William Gaines">William Gaines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsa_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsa-2">[2]</a></sup> launched as a comic book before it became a magazine. It was widely imitated and influential, affecting <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> media as well as the cultural landscape of the 20th century, with editor <a href="/wiki/Al_Feldstein" title="Al Feldstein">Al Feldstein</a> increasing readership to more than two million during its 1974 circulation peak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As of January 12, 2017, <i>Mad</i> had published 543 issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The magazine is the last surviving title from the <a href="/wiki/EC_Comics" title="EC Comics">EC Comics</a> line, offering satire on all aspects of life and popular culture, politics, entertainment, and public figures. Its format is divided into a number of recurring segments such as TV and movie <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parodies</a>, as well as freeform articles. <i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s mascot, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Neuman" title="Alfred E. Neuman">Alfred E. Neuman</a>, is typically the focal point of the magazine's cover, with his face often replacing that of a celebrity or character who is lampooned within the issue.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Mad</i> began as a comic book published by <a href="/wiki/EC_Comics" title="EC Comics">EC</a>, debuting in August 1952 (<a href="/wiki/Cover_date" title="Cover date">cover date</a> October–November), and located in lower Manhattan at 225 Lafayette Street. In the early 1960s, the <i>Mad</i> office moved to 485 <a href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue" title="Madison Avenue">Madison Avenue</a>, a location given in the magazine as "485 MADison Avenue". The title is trademarked in capitals as <i>MAD</i>.</p>
<p>The first issue was written almost entirely by <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">Harvey Kurtzman</a>, and featured illustrations by Kurtzman, along with <a href="/wiki/Wally_Wood" title="Wally Wood">Wally Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Will_Elder" title="Will Elder">Will Elder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Davis_(cartoonist)" title="Jack Davis (cartoonist)">Jack Davis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Severin" title="John Severin">John Severin</a>. Wood, Elder, and Davis were the three main illustrators throughout the 23-issue run of the comic book.</p>
<p>To retain Kurtzman as its editor, the comic book converted to magazine format as of issue #24 (1955). The switchover only induced Kurtzman to remain for one more year, but crucially, the move had removed <i>Mad</i> from the strictures of the <a href="/wiki/Comics_Code_Authority" title="Comics Code Authority">Comics Code Authority</a>. After Kurtzman's departure in 1956, new editor Al Feldstein swiftly brought aboard contributors such as <a href="/wiki/Don_Martin_(cartoonist)" title="Don Martin (cartoonist)">Don Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Jacobs" title="Frank Jacobs">Frank Jacobs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mort_Drucker" title="Mort Drucker">Mort Drucker</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Proh%C3%ADas" title="Antonio Prohías">Antonio Prohías</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Berg_(cartoonist)" title="Dave Berg (cartoonist)">Dave Berg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Aragon%C3%A9s" title="Sergio Aragonés">Sergio Aragonés</a>. The magazine's circulation more than quadrupled during Feldstein's tenure, peaking at 2,132,655 in 1974; it later declined to a third of this figure by the end of his time as editor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slau_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-slau-5">[5]</a></sup> When Feldstein retired in 1984, he was replaced by the team of <a href="/wiki/Nick_Meglin" title="Nick Meglin">Nick Meglin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Ficarra" title="John Ficarra">John Ficarra</a>, who co-edited <i>Mad</i> for the next two decades. Since Meglin's retirement in 2004, Ficarra has continued to edit the magazine.</p>
<p>Gaines sold his company in the early 1960s to the <a href="/wiki/Kinney_Parking_Company" title="Kinney Parking Company">Kinney Parking Company</a>, which also acquired <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">National Periodicals</a> (a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> by the end of that decade. Gaines was named a Kinney board member, and was largely permitted to run <i>Mad</i> as he saw fit without corporate interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following Gaines' death, <i>Mad</i> became more ingrained within the <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a> corporate structure. Eventually, the magazine was obliged to abandon its long-time home at 485 Madison Avenue, and in the mid-1990s it moved into DC Comics' offices at the same time that DC relocated to 1700 <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a>. In 2001, the magazine broke its long-standing taboo and began running paid advertising. The outside revenue allowed the introduction of color printing and improved paper stock.</p>
<p>In its earliest incarnation, new issues of the magazine appeared erratically, between four and seven times a year. By the end of 1958, <i>Mad</i> had settled on an unusual eight-times-a-year schedule,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which lasted almost four decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Gaines felt the atypical timing was necessary to maintain the magazine's level of quality. <i>Mad</i> then began producing additional issues, until it reached a traditional monthly schedule with the January 1997 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> With its 500th issue (June 2009), amid company-wide cutbacks at Time Warner, the magazine temporarily regressed to a quarterly publication<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsa_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-newsa-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> before settling to six issues per year in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though there are antecedents to <i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s style of humor in print, radio and film, <i>Mad</i> became a pioneering example of it. Throughout the 1950s, <i>Mad</i> featured groundbreaking parodies combining a sentimental fondness for the familiar staples of American culture—such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archie_Andrews_(comics)" title="Archie Andrews (comics)">Archie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a>—with a keen joy in exposing the fakery behind the image. Its approach was described by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Kehr" title="Dave Kehr">Dave Kehr</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>: "<a href="/wiki/Bob_Elliott_(comedian)" title="Bob Elliott (comedian)">Bob Elliott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Goulding" title="Ray Goulding">Ray Goulding</a> on the radio, <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Kovacs" title="Ernie Kovacs">Ernie Kovacs</a> on television, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Freberg" title="Stan Freberg">Stan Freberg</a> on records, <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">Harvey Kurtzman</a> in the early issues of <i>Mad</i>: all of those pioneering humorists and many others realized that the real world mattered less to people than the sea of sounds and images that the ever more powerful mass media were pumping into American lives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bob_and_Ray" title="Bob and Ray">Bob and Ray</a>, Kovacs and Freberg all became contributors to <i>Mad</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977, Tony Hiss and Jeff Lewis wrote in <i>The New York Times</i> about the then 25-year-old publication's initial effect:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The skeptical generation of kids it shaped in the 1950s is the same generation that, in the 1960s, opposed a war and didn't feel bad when the United States lost for the first time and in the 1970s helped turn out an Administration and didn't feel bad about that either... It was magical, objective proof to kids that they weren't alone, that in New York City on Lafayette Street, if nowhere else, there were people who knew that there was something wrong, phony and funny about a world of bomb shelters, brinkmanship and toothpaste smiles. <i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s consciousness of itself, as trash, as comic book, as enemy of parents and teachers, even as money-making enterprise, thrilled kids. In 1955, such consciousness was possibly nowhere else to be found. In a <i>Mad</i> parody, comic-strip characters knew they were stuck in a strip. "Darnold Duck," for example, begins wondering why he has only three fingers and has to wear white gloves all the time. He ends up wanting to murder every other Disney character. G.I. Schmoe tries to win the sexy Asiatic Red Army broad by telling her, "O.K., baby! You're all mine! I gave you a chance to hit me witta gun butt... But naturally, you have immediately fallen in love with me, since I am a big hero of this story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Mad</i> is often credited with filling a vital gap in political satire from the 1950s to 1970s, when <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Cold War paranoia</a> and a general culture of censorship prevailed in the United States, especially in literature for teens. Activist <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hayden" title="Tom Hayden">Tom Hayden</a> said, "My own radical journey began with <i>Mad Magazine</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The rise of such factors as cable television and the Internet have diminished the influence and impact of <i>Mad</i>, although it remains a widely distributed magazine. In a way, <i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s power has been undone by its own success: what was subversive in the 1950s and 1960s is now commonplace. However, its impact on three generations of humorists is incalculable, as can be seen in the frequent references to <i>Mad</i> on the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Simpsons</i> producer <a href="/wiki/Bill_Oakley" title="Bill Oakley">Bill Oakley</a> said, "The Simpsons has transplanted <i>Mad</i> magazine. Basically everyone who was young between 1955 and 1975 read <i>Mad</i>, and that's where your sense of humor came from. And we knew all these people, you know, Dave Berg and Don Martin – all heroes, and unfortunately, now all dead. And I think <i>The Simpsons</i> has taken that spot in America's heart."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2009, <i>The New York Times</i> wrote, "<i>Mad</i> once defined American satire; now it heckles from the margins as all of culture competes for trickster status."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Longtime contributor <a href="/wiki/Al_Jaffee" title="Al Jaffee">Al Jaffee</a> described the dilemma to an interviewer in 2010: "When <i>Mad</i> first came out, in 1952, it was the only game in town. Now, you've got graduates from <i>Mad</i> who are doing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Today_Show" title="The Today Show">The Today Show</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Colbert" title="Stephen Colbert">Stephen Colbert</a> or <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a>. All of these people grew up on <i>Mad</i>. Now <i>Mad</i> has to top them. So <i>Mad</i> is almost in a competition with itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motherjones.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-motherjones.com-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s satiric net was cast wide. The magazine often featured parodies of ongoing American culture, including advertising campaigns, the nuclear family, the media, big business, education and publishing. In the 1960s and beyond, it satirized such burgeoning topics as the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Generation_gap" title="Generation gap">generation gap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_politics" title="Gun politics">gun politics</a>, pollution, the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">recreational drug use</a>. The magazine took a generally negative tone towards <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture</a> drugs such as <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid_diethylamide" title="Lysergic acid diethylamide">LSD</a>, but also savaged mainstream drugs such as tobacco and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcohol</a>. <i>Mad</i> always satirized <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> as mercilessly as it did <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republicans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2007, Al Feldstein recalled, "We even used to rake the hippies over the coals. They were protesting the Vietnam War, but we took aspects of their culture and had fun with it. <i>Mad</i> was wide open. <a href="/wiki/William_Gaines" title="William Gaines">Bill</a> loved it, and he was a capitalist Republican. I loved it, and I was a liberal Democrat. That went for the writers, too; they all had their own political leanings, and everybody had a voice. But the voices were mostly critical. It was social commentary, after all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Mad</i> also ran a good deal of less topical or contentious material on such varied subjects as <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greeting_card" title="Greeting card">greeting cards</a>, sports, <a href="/wiki/Conversation" title="Conversation">small talk</a>, poetry, marriage, <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strips</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_film_awards" title="List of film awards">awards shows</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">cars</a> and many other areas of general interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zine_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-zine-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maria_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-maria-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a>'</i> Robert Boyd wrote, "All I really need to know I learned from <i>Mad</i> magazine", going on to assert:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Plenty of it went right over my head, of course, but that's part of what made it attractive and valuable. Things that go over your head can make you raise your head a little higher.</p>
<p>The magazine instilled in me a habit of mind, a way of thinking about a world rife with false fronts, small print, deceptive ads, booby traps, treacherous language, double standards, half truths, subliminal pitches and product placements; it warned me that I was often merely the target of people who claimed to be my friend; it prompted me to mistrust authority, to read between the lines, to take nothing at face value, to see patterns in the often shoddy construction of movies and TV shows; and it got me to think critically in a way that few actual humans charged with my care ever bothered to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>In 1994, Brian Siano in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Humanist" title="The Humanist">The Humanist</a></i> discussed the effect of <i>Mad</i> on that segment of people already disaffected from society:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>For the smarter kids of two generations, <i>Mad</i> was a revelation: it was the first to tell us that the toys we were being sold were garbage, our teachers were phonies, our leaders were fools, our religious counselors were hypocrites, and even our parents were lying to us about damn near everything. An entire generation had William Gaines for a godfather: this same generation later went on to give us the sexual revolution, the environmental movement, the peace movement, greater freedom in artistic expression, and a host of other goodies. Coincidence? You be the judge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>–winning art comics maven <a href="/wiki/Art_Spiegelman" title="Art Spiegelman">Art Spiegelman</a> said, "The message <i>Mad</i> had in general is, 'The media is lying to you, and we are part of the media.' It was basically... 'Think for yourselves, kids.'" William Gaines offered his own view: when asked to cite <i>Mad'</i>s philosophy, his boisterous answer was, "We must never stop reminding the reader what little value they get for their money!"</p>
<p>Comics historian <a href="/wiki/Tom_Spurgeon" title="Tom Spurgeon">Tom Spurgeon</a> picked <i>Mad</i> as the medium's top series of all time, writing, "At the height of its influence, <i>Mad</i> was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Show" title="The Daily Show">The Daily Show</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Onion" title="The Onion">The Onion</a></i> combined."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Graydon_Carter" title="Graydon Carter">Graydon Carter</a> chose it as the sixth best magazine of any sort ever, describing <i>Mad</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s mission as being "ever ready to pounce on the illogical, hypocritical, self-serious and ludicrous" before concluding, "Nowadays, it's part of the oxygen we breathe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joyce_Carol_Oates" title="Joyce Carol Oates">Joyce Carol Oates</a> called it "wonderfully inventive, irresistibly irreverent and intermittently ingenious."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Monty Python</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a> wrote, "<i>Mad</i> became the Bible for me and my whole generation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weird_Al_Yankovic" title="Weird Al Yankovic">Weird Al Yankovic</a> was asked whether <i>Mad</i> had had any influence in putting him on a road to a career in parody, the musician replied, "[It was] more like going off a cliff."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yankovic_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-yankovic-33">[33]</a></sup> Critic <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I learned to be a movie critic by reading <i>Mad</i> magazine... <i>Mad</i>'s parodies made me aware of the machine inside the skin—of the way a movie might look original on the outside, while inside it was just recycling the same old dumb formulas. I did not read the magazine, I plundered it for clues to the universe. <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a> lost it at the movies; I lost it at <i>Mad</i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Rock singer <a href="/wiki/Patti_Smith" title="Patti Smith">Patti Smith</a> said more succinctly, "After <i>Mad</i>, drugs were nothing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_cases">Court cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Court cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The magazine has been involved in various legal actions over the decades, some of which have reached the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">United States Supreme Court</a>. The most far-reaching was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irving_Berlin_et_al._v._E.C._Publications,_Inc." title="Irving Berlin et al. v. E.C. Publications, Inc.">Irving Berlin et al. v. E.C. Publications, Inc.</a></i> In 1961, a group of music publishers representing songwriters such as <a href="/wiki/Irving_Berlin" title="Irving Berlin">Irving Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodgers" title="Richard Rodgers">Richard Rodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a> filed a $25 million lawsuit against <i>Mad</i> for copyright infringement following "Sing Along With <i>Mad</i>", a collection of parody lyrics which the magazine said could be "sung to the tune of" many popular songs. The publishing group hoped to establish a legal precedent that only a song's composers retained the right to parody that song. Judge Charles Metzner of U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York ruled largely in favor of <i>Mad</i> in 1963, affirming its right to print 23 of the 25 song parodies under dispute. However, in the case of two parodies, "Always" (sung to the tune of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Always_(1925_song)" title="Always (1925 song)">Always</a>") and "There's No Business Like No Business" (sung to the tune of "<a href="/wiki/There%27s_No_Business_Like_Show_Business" title="There's No Business Like Show Business">There's No Business Like Show Business</a>"), Judge Metzner decided that the issue of copyright infringement was closer, requiring a trial because in each case the parodies relied on the same verbal hooks ("always" and "business") as the originals. The music publishers appealed the ruling, but the U.S. Court of Appeals not only upheld the pro-<i>Mad</i> decision in regard to the 23 songs, it adopted an approach that was broad enough to strip the publishers of their limited victory regarding the remaining two songs. Writing a unanimous opinion for the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, Circuit Judge <a href="/wiki/Irving_Kaufman" title="Irving Kaufman">Irving Kaufman</a> pointedly observed, "We doubt that even so eminent a composer as plaintiff Irving Berlin should be permitted to claim a property interest in <a href="/wiki/Iambic_pentameter" title="Iambic pentameter">iambic pentameter</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcir_Musi_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcir_Musi-36">[36]</a></sup> The publishers again appealed, but the Supreme Court refused to hear it, thus allowing the decision to stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jac_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-jac-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>This precedent-setting case established the rights of parodists and satirists to mimic the meter of popular songs. However, the "Sing Along With <i>Mad</i>" songbook was not the magazine's first venture into musical parody. In 1960, <i>Mad</i> had published "My Fair Ad-Man", a full advertising-based spoof of the hit Broadway musical <i><a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady" title="My Fair Lady">My Fair Lady</a></i>. In 1959, "If <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert &amp; Sullivan</a> wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Dick_Tracy" title="Dick Tracy">Dick Tracy</a></i>" was one of the speculative pairings in "If Famous Authors Wrote the Comics". Three decades later, <i>Mad</i> was one of several parties that filed <i><a href="/wiki/Amicus_curiae" title="Amicus curiae">amicus curiae</a></i> briefs with the Supreme Court in support of <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> and its disputed song parody, during the 1993 <i><a href="/wiki/Campbell_v._Acuff-Rose_Music,_Inc." title="Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc.">Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc.</a></i> case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1966, a series of copyright infringement lawsuits against the magazine regarding ownership of the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Neuman" title="Alfred E. Neuman">Alfred E. Neuman</a> image eventually reached the appellate level. Although Harry Stuff had copyrighted the image in 1914, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit ruled that, by allowing many copies of the image to circulate without any copyright notice, the owner of the copyright had allowed the image to pass into the public domain, thus establishing the right of <i>Mad</i> – or anyone else for that matter – to use the image. In addition, <i>Mad</i> established that Stuff was not himself the creator of the image by producing numerous other examples dating back to the late 19th century. This decision was also allowed to stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maria_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-maria-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advertising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mad</i> was long noted for its absence of advertising, enabling it to satirize materialist culture without fear of reprisal. For decades, it was the most successful American magazine to publish ad-free,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> beginning with issue #33 (April 1957) and continuing through issue #402 (February 2001).</p>
<p>As a comic book, <i>Mad</i> had run the same advertisements as the rest of EC's line. The magazine later made a deal with <a href="/wiki/Moxie" title="Moxie">Moxie</a> soda that involved inserting the Moxie logo into various articles. <i>Mad</i> ran a limited number of ads in its first two years as a magazine, helpfully labeled "real advertisement" to differentiate the real from the parodies. The last authentic ad published under the original <i>Mad</i> regime was for <a href="/wiki/Famous_Artists_School" title="Famous Artists School">Famous Artists School</a>; two issues later, the inside front cover of issue #34 had a parody of the same ad. After this transitional period, the only promotions to appear in <i>Mad</i> for decades were house ads for <i>Mad'</i>s own books and specials, subscriptions, and promotional items such as ceramic busts, T-shirts, or a line of <i>Mad</i> jewelry. This rule was bent only a few times to promote outside products directly related to the magazine, such as <a href="/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a> <i>Mad Board Game</i>, the video game based on <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy" title="Spy vs. Spy">Spy vs. Spy</a></i>, and the notorious <i><a href="/wiki/Up_the_Academy" title="Up the Academy">Up the Academy</a></i> movie, (which the magazine later disowned). <i>Mad</i> explicitly promised that it would never make its mailing list available.</p>
<p>Both Kurtzman and Feldstein wanted the magazine to solicit advertising, feeling this could be accomplished without compromising <i>Mad'</i>s content or editorial independence. Kurtzman remembered <i><a href="/wiki/Ballyhoo_(magazine)" title="Ballyhoo (magazine)">Ballyhoo</a></i>, a boisterous 1930s humor publication that made an editorial point of mocking its own sponsors. Feldstein went so far as to propose an in-house <i>Mad</i> ad agency, and produced a "dummy" copy of what an issue with ads could look like. But Bill Gaines was intractable, telling the television news magazine <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i>, "We long ago decided we couldn't take money from Pepsi-Cola and make fun of Coca-Cola." Gaines' motivation in eschewing ad dollars was less philosophical than practical:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We'd have to improve our package. Most advertisers want to appear in a magazine that's loaded with color and has super-slick paper. So you find yourself being pushed into producing a more expensive package. You get bigger and fancier and attract more advertisers. Then you find you're losing some of your advertisers. Your readers still expect the fancy package, so you keep putting it out, but now you don't have your advertising income, which is why you got fancier in the first place—and now you're sunk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurring_features">Recurring features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recurring features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_recurring_features" title="Mad recurring features">Mad recurring features</a></div>
<p><i>Mad</i> is known for many regular and semi-regular recurring features in its pages, including "<a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy" title="Spy vs. Spy">Spy vs. Spy</a>", the "<a href="/wiki/Mad_Fold-in" title="Mad Fold-in">Mad Fold-in</a>", "The Lighter Side of..." and its television and movie parodies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mad_Lingo">Mad Lingo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mad Lingo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mad</i> used a number of words, most of which were real but obscure or little known, as nonsensical words on a regular basis. They were used as first names, surnames, objects, gadgets, or were, sometimes, just placed in ridiculous places in the magazine. The words included: <a href="/wiki/Axolotl" title="Axolotl">axolotl</a>, bitsko, furshlugginer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halvah" title="Halvah">halvah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moxie" title="Moxie">Moxie</a>, osszefogva, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poobah" title="Poobah">poobah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potrzebie" title="Potrzebie">potrzebie</a> and veeblefetzer. Moxie Cowsnofski became the name of Alfred E. Neuman's girlfriend.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alfred_E._Neuman">Alfred E. Neuman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alfred E. <p>The image most closely associated with the magazine is that of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Neuman" title="Alfred E. Neuman">Alfred E. Neuman</a>, the boy with misaligned eyes, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gap-toothed" title="Gap-toothed">gap-toothed</a> smile, and the perennial motto "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/What,_me_worry%3F" title="What, me worry?">What, me worry?</a>" The original image was a popular humorous graphic for many decades before <i>Mad</i> adopted it, but the face is now primarily associated with <i>Mad</i>.</p>
<p><i>Mad</i> first used the boy's face in November 1954. His first iconic full-cover appearance was as a write-in candidate for President on issue #30 (December 1956), in which he was identified by name and sported his "What, me worry?" motto. He has since appeared in a slew of guises and comic situations. According to <i>Mad</i> writer Frank Jacobs, a letter was once successfully delivered to the magazine through the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">U.S. mail</a> bearing only Neuman's face, without any address or other identifying information. This was compared to a similar recent event in which a letter only bearing the Playboy bunny insignia was delivered to <a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributors_and_criticism">Contributors and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Contributors and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mad</i> has provided an ongoing showcase for many long-running satirical writers and artists and has fostered an unusual group loyalty. Although several of the contributors earn far more than their <i>Mad</i> pay in fields such as television and advertising, they have steadily continued to provide material for the publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Among the notable artists were the aforementioned Davis, Elder and Wood, as well as <a href="/wiki/Mort_Drucker" title="Mort Drucker">Mort Drucker</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Woodbridge" title="George Woodbridge">George Woodbridge</a>, Harry North and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Coker" title="Paul Coker">Paul Coker</a>. Writers such as <a href="/wiki/Dick_DeBartolo" title="Dick DeBartolo">Dick DeBartolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Hart" title="Stan Hart">Stan Hart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Jacobs" title="Frank Jacobs">Frank Jacobs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Koch" title="Tom Koch">Tom Koch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arnie_Kogen" title="Arnie Kogen">Arnie Kogen</a> appeared regularly in the magazine's pages. In several cases, only infirmity or death has ended a contributor's run at <i>Mad.</i></p>
<p>Within the industry, <i>Mad</i> was known for the uncommonly prompt manner in which its contributors were paid. Publisher Gaines would typically write a personal check and give it to the artist upon receipt of the finished product. Wally Wood said, "I got spoiled... Other publishers don't do that. I started to get upset if I had to wait a whole week for my check." Another lure for contributors was the annual "Mad Trip", an all-expenses-paid tradition that began in 1960. The editorial staff was automatically invited, along with freelancers who had qualified for an invitation by selling a set amount of articles or pages during the previous year. Gaines was strict about enforcing this quota, and one year, longtime writer and frequent traveller Arnie Kogen was bumped off the list. Later that year, Gaines' mother died, and Kogen was asked if he would be attending the funeral. "I can't," said Kogen, "I don't have enough pages." Over the years, the <i>Mad</i> crew traveled to such locales as France, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, Russia, Hong Kong, England, <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, Italy, Greece, and Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jac_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-jac-37">[37]</a></sup> The tradition ended with Gaines' death, and a 1993 trip to <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo" title="Monte Carlo">Monte Carlo</a>.</p>
<p>Although <i>Mad</i> was an exclusively freelance publication, it achieved a remarkable stability, with numerous contributors remaining prominent for decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Critics of the magazine felt that this lack of turnover eventually led to a formulaic sameness, although there is little agreement on when the magazine peaked or plunged. Many have written that the key factor is when the reader first encountered <i>Mad</i>. According to Mad Senior Editor, Joe Raiola, "Mad is the only place in America where if you mature, you get fired."</p>
<p>Proclaiming the precise moment that began the magazine's irreversible decline is still debated, but its sales peak came with issue 161 which sold 2.1 million copies in 1973. From 1981 onwards the magazine sold under a million copies until its present circulation figures in the 100,000 range. <i>Mad</i> poked fun at the tendency of readers to accuse the magazine of declining in quality at various points in its history, depending on the age of the critic, in its "Untold History of <i>Mad</i> Magazine", a self-referential faux history in the 400th issue which joked: "The second issue of <i>Mad</i> goes on sale on December 9, 1952. On December 11, the first-ever letter complaining that <i>Mad</i> 'just isn't as funny and original like it used to be' arrives."</p>
<p>Among the most frequently cited "downward turning points" are: creator/editor Harvey Kurtzman's departure in 1957;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-That_Old_Feeling:_Hail.2C_Harvey.21_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-That_Old_Feeling:_Hail.2C_Harvey.21-44">[44]</a></sup> the magazine's mainstream success;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> adoption of recurring features starting in the early 1960s;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> the magazine's absorption into a more corporate structure in 1968 (or the mid-1990s);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsandtech.com_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsandtech.com-47">[47]</a></sup> founder Gaines' death in 1992;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsandtech.com_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-newsandtech.com-47">[47]</a></sup> the magazine's publicized "revamp" in 1997<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>; or the arrival of paid advertising in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <i>Mad</i> has been criticized<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for its over-reliance on a core group of aging regulars throughout the 1970s and 1980s and then criticized again<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for an alleged downturn as those same creators began to leave, die, retire, or contribute less frequently. It has been proposed that <i>Mad</i> is more susceptible to this criticism than many media because a sizable percentage of its readership turns over regularly as it ages, as <i>Mad</i> focuses greatly on current events and a changing popular culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jac_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-jac-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2010, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergio_Aragones" title="Sergio Aragones">Sergio Aragones</a> said, "Mad is written by people who never thought 'Okay, I'm going to write for kids,' or 'I'm going to write for adults.' ... And many people say 'I used to read <i>Mad</i>, but <i>Mad</i> has changed a lot.' Excuse me—you grew up! You have new interests. ... The change doesn't come from the magazine, it comes from the people who grow or don't grow."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The magazine's art director, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Viviano" title="Sam Viviano">Sam Viviano</a>, has suggested that historically, <i>Mad</i> was at its best "whenever you first started reading it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the loudest of those who insist the magazine is no longer funny are supporters of <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">Harvey Kurtzman</a>, who had the good critical fortune to leave <i>Mad</i> after just 28 issues, before his own formulaic tendencies might have become obtrusive. This also meant Kurtzman suffered the bad creative and financial timing of departing before the magazine became a runaway success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-That_Old_Feeling:_Hail.2C_Harvey.21_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-That_Old_Feeling:_Hail.2C_Harvey.21-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, just how much of that success was due to the original Kurtzman template that he left for his successor, and how much should be credited to the <a href="/wiki/Al_Feldstein" title="Al Feldstein">Al Feldstein</a> system and the depth of the post-Kurtzman talent pool, can be argued without resolution. In 2009, an interviewer proposed to <a href="/wiki/Al_Jaffee" title="Al Jaffee">Al Jaffee</a>, "There's a group of <i>Mad</i> aficionados who feel that if Harvey Kurtzman had stayed at <i>Mad</i>, the magazine would not only have been different, but better." Jaffee, a Kurtzman enthusiast, replied, "And then there's a large group who feel that if Harvey had stayed with <i>Mad</i>, he would have upgraded it to the point that only fifteen people would buy it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> During Kurtzman's final two-plus years at EC, <i>Mad</i> appeared erratically (ten issues appeared in 1954, followed by eight issues in 1955 and four issues in 1956). Feldstein was less well regarded creatively, but kept the magazine on a regular schedule, leading to decades of success. (Kurtzman and <a href="/wiki/Will_Elder" title="Will Elder">Will Elder</a> returned to <i>Mad</i> for a short time in the mid-1980s as an illustrating team.)</p>
<p>Many of the magazine's mainstays began retiring or dying by the 1980s. Newer contributors who appeared in the years that followed include <a href="/wiki/Joe_Raiola" title="Joe Raiola">Joe Raiola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Kadau" title="Charlie Kadau">Charlie Kadau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Barbieri" title="Tony Barbieri">Tony Barbieri</a>, Scott Bricher, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bunk" title="Tom Bunk">Tom Bunk</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Caldwell_(cartoonist)" title="John Caldwell (cartoonist)">John Caldwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Devlin" title="Desmond Devlin">Desmond Devlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drew_Friedman_(cartoonist)" title="Drew Friedman (cartoonist)">Drew Friedman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Liebmann" title="Barry Liebmann">Barry Liebmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Pope_(cartoonist)" title="Kevin Pope (cartoonist)">Kevin Pope</a>, Scott Maiko, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Mejia" title="Hermann Mejia">Hermann Mejia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Richmond_(illustrator)" title="Tom Richmond (illustrator)">Tom Richmond</a>, Andrew J. Schwartzberg, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Snider" title="Mike Snider">Mike Snider</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Theakston" title="Greg Theakston">Greg Theakston</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nadina_Simon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nadina Simon (page does not exist)">Nadina Simon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Tulka" title="Rick Tulka">Rick Tulka</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Wray" title="Bill Wray">Bill Wray</a>.</p>
<p>On April 1, 1997, the magazine publicized an alleged "revamp", ostensibly designed to reach an older, more sophisticated readership. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon</a>'s David Futrelle opined that such content was very much a part of <i>Mad'</i>s past:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The October 1971 issue, for example, with its war crimes fold-in and back cover "mini-poster" of "The Four Horsemen of the Metropolis" (Drugs, Graft, Pollution and Slums). With its Mad Pollution Primer. With its "Reality Street" TV satire, taking a poke at the idealized images of interracial harmony on <i><a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a></i>. ("It's a street of depression,/ Corruption, oppression!/ It's a sadist's dream come true!/ And masochists, too!") With its "This is America" photo feature, contrasting images of heroic astronauts with graphic photos of dead soldiers and junkies shooting up. I remember this issue pretty well; it was one of the ones I picked up at a garage sale and read to death. I seem to remember asking my parents what "graft" was. One of the joys of <i>Mad</i> for me at the time was that it was always slightly over my head. From "Mad's Up-Dated Modern Day <a href="/wiki/Mother_Goose" title="Mother Goose">Mother Goose</a>" I learned about <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Spiro Agnew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">Timothy Leary</a> ("Wee Timmy Leary/ Soars through the sky/ Upward and Upward/ Till he's, oh, so, high/ Since this rhyme's for kiddies/ How do we explain/ That Wee Timmy Leary/ Isn't in a plane?"). From "Greeting Cards for the Sexual Revolution" I learned about "Gay Liberationists" and leather-clad "Sex Fetishists." I read the <i>Mad</i> versions of a whole host of films I never in a million years would have been allowed to see: <i><a href="/wiki/Easy_Rider" title="Easy Rider">Easy Rider</a></i> ("Sleazy Riders"), <i><a href="/wiki/Midnight_Cowboy" title="Midnight Cowboy">Midnight Cowboy</a></i> ("Midnight Wowboy"), <i><a href="/wiki/Five_Easy_Pieces" title="Five Easy Pieces">Five Easy Pieces</a></i> ("Five Easy Pages [and two hard ones].") I learned about the <a href="/wiki/John_Birch_Society" title="John Birch Society">John Birch Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue" title="Madison Avenue">Madison Avenue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Futrelle_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Futrelle-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Mad</i> editor <a href="/wiki/John_Ficarra" title="John Ficarra">John Ficarra</a> acknowledges that changes in culture have made the task of creating fresh satire more difficult, telling an interviewer, "The editorial mission statement has always been the same: 'Everyone is lying to you, including magazines. Think for yourself. Question authority.' But it's gotten harder, as they've gotten better at lying and getting in on the joke."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Mad</i> contributor <a href="/wiki/Tom_Richmond_(illustrator)" title="Tom Richmond (illustrator)">Tom Richmond</a> has tweaked critics who say the magazine's decision to accept advertising would make late publisher <a href="/wiki/William_Gaines" title="William Gaines">William Gaines</a> "turn over in his grave", pointing out this is impossible because Gaines was cremated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributors">Contributors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contributors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mad</i> is known for the stability and longevity of its talent roster, billed as "The Usual Gang of Idiots", with several creators enjoying 30-, 40- and even 50-year careers in the magazine's pages.</p>
<p>According to the "Mad Magazine Contributor Appearances" website<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, more than 850 contributors have received bylines in at least one issue of <i>Mad</i>, but only three dozen of those have contributed to 100 issues or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-users.ipfw.edu_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-users.ipfw.edu-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Al_Jaffee" title="Al Jaffee">Al Jaffee</a> has appeared in the most issues (485 as of October 2015). The other three contributors to have appeared in more than 400 issues of <i>Mad</i> are <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Aragon%C3%A9s" title="Sergio Aragonés">Sergio Aragonés</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick_DeBartolo" title="Dick DeBartolo">Dick DeBartolo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mort_Drucker" title="Mort Drucker">Mort Drucker</a>; <a href="/wiki/Dave_Berg_(cartoonist)" title="Dave Berg (cartoonist)">Dave Berg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Coker" title="Paul Coker">Paul Coker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Jacobs" title="Frank Jacobs">Frank Jacobs</a> have each topped the 300 mark. (The list calculates appearances by issue only, not by individual articles or overall page count; e.g. although Jacobs wrote three separate articles that appeared in issue #172, his total is reckoned to have increased by one.)</p>

<p>Those who have contributed twice apiece <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-users.ipfw.edu_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-users.ipfw.edu-55">[55]</a></sup> include <a href="/wiki/Tom_Lehrer" title="Tom Lehrer">Tom Lehrer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wally_Cox" title="Wally Cox">Wally Cox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Dor%C3%A9" title="Gustave Doré">Gustave Doré</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Kaye" title="Danny Kaye">Danny Kaye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Freberg" title="Stan Freberg">Stan Freberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mort_Walker" title="Mort Walker">Mort Walker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>. (Leonardo's check is still waiting in the Mad offices for him to pick it up.) Appearing slightly more frequently were <a href="/wiki/Frank_Frazetta" title="Frank Frazetta">Frank Frazetta</a> (3 bylines), <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Kovacs" title="Ernie Kovacs">Ernie Kovacs</a> (11), <a href="/wiki/Bob_and_Ray" title="Bob and Ray">Bob and Ray</a> (12), <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morgan_(humorist)" title="Henry Morgan (humorist)">Henry Morgan</a> (3), and <a href="/wiki/Sid_Caesar" title="Sid Caesar">Sid Caesar</a> (4). In its earliest years, before amassing its own staff of regulars, the magazine frequently used outside "name" talent. Often, <i>Mad</i> would simply illustrate the celebrities' preexisting material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The Bob and Ray association was particularly fruitful. When the magazine learned that <a href="/wiki/Tom_Koch" title="Tom Koch">Tom Koch</a> was the writer behind the Bob and Ray radio sketches adapted by <i>Mad</i>, Koch was sought out by the editors and ultimately wrote more than 300 <i>Mad</i> articles over the next 37 years.</p>
<p>The magazine has occasionally run guest articles in which notables from show business or comic books have participated. In 1964, an article called "Comic Strips They'd Really Like To Do" featured one-shot proposals by cartoonists including <a href="/wiki/Mell_Lazarus" title="Mell Lazarus">Mell Lazarus</a> and Charles M. Schulz. More than once, the magazine has enlisted popular comic book artists such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">Frank Miller</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lee" title="Jim Lee">Jim Lee</a> to design and illustrate a series of "Rejected Superheroes." In 2008, the magazine got national coverage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> for its article "Why <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> is in Favor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Warming" title="Global Warming">Global Warming</a>". Each of the piece's 10 punchlines was illustrated by a different <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>–winning editorial cartoonist. In 2015, <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title='"Weird Al" Yankovic'>"Weird Al" Yankovic</a> served as the magazine's first and only guest editor, writing some material and guiding the content in issue #533.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yankovic_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-yankovic-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1955, Gaines began presenting reprints of material for <i>Mad</i> in black-and-white paperbacks, the first being <i>The Mad Reader</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Many of these featured new covers by <i>Mad</i> cover artist Norman Mingo. This practice continued into the 2000s, with more than 100 <i>Mad</i> paperbacks published. Gaines made a special effort to keep the entire line of paperbacks in print at all times, and the books were frequently reprinted in new editions with different covers.</p>
<p><i>Mad</i> also frequently repackaged its material in a long series of "Super Special" format magazines, beginning in 1958 with two concurrent annual series entitled <i>The Worst from Mad</i> and <i>More Trash from Mad</i>. Various other titles have been used through the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> These reprint issues were sometimes augmented by exclusive features such as posters, stickers and, on a few occasions, recordings on <a href="/wiki/Flexi_disc" title="Flexi disc">flexi-disc</a>, or comic book–formatted inserts reprinting material from the 1952–55 era.</p>
<p>One steady form of revenue has come from foreign editions of the magazine. <i>Mad</i> has been published in local versions in many countries, beginning with the United Kingdom in 1959, and Sweden in 1960. Each new market receives access to the publication's back catalog of articles and is also encouraged to produce its own localized material in the <i>Mad</i> vein. However, the sensibility of the American <i>Mad</i> has not always translated to other cultures, and many of the foreign editions have had short lives or interrupted publications. The Swedish, Danish, Italian and Mexican <i>Mad</i>s were each published on three separate occasions; Norway has had four runs cancelled. Australia (35 years and counting) United Kingdom (35 years), Sweden (34 years) and Brazil (33 years) have produced the longest uninterrupted <i>Mad</i> variants.</p>

<p>Conflicts over content have occasionally arisen between the parent magazine and its international franchisees. When a comic strip satirizing <a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">England's royal family</a> was reprinted in a <i>Mad</i> paperback, it was deemed necessary to rip out the page from 25,000 copies by hand before the book could be distributed in Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> But <i>Mad</i> was also protective of its own editorial standards. Bill Gaines sent "one of his typically dreadful, blistering letters" to his Dutch editors after they published a bawdy gag about a men's room urinal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <i>Mad</i> has since relaxed its requirements, and while the U.S. version still eschews overt profanity, the magazine generally poses no objections to more provocative content such as the Swedish edition's 1999 parody of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Show_Me_Love_(film)" title="Show Me Love (film)">Fucking Åmål</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off">Spin-off</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Spin-off">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mad_Kids"><i>Mad Kids</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mad Kids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mad_Kids" title="Mad Kids">Mad Kids</a></div>
<p>Between 2005 and February 17, 2009, the magazine published 14 issues of <i>Mad Kids,</i> a spinoff publication aimed at a younger demographic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsa_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-newsa-2">[2]</a></sup> Reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon" title="Nickelodeon">Nickelodeon</a>'s newsstand titles, it emphasized current kids' entertainment (i.e. <i><a href="/wiki/Yu-Gi-Oh!" title="Yu-Gi-Oh!">Yu-Gi-Oh!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Naruto" title="Naruto">Naruto</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/High_School_Musical" title="High School Musical">High School Musical</a></i>), albeit with an impudent voice. Much of the content of <i>Mad Kids</i> had originally appeared in the parent publication; reprinted material was chosen and edited to reflect grade schoolers' interests. But the quarterly magazine also included newly commissioned articles and cartoons, as well as puzzles, bonus inserts, a calendar, and the other activity-related content that is common to kids' magazines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_satiric-comics_magazines">Other satiric-comics magazines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other satiric-comics magazines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the success of <i>Mad</i>, other black-and-white magazines of topical, satiric comics began to be published. Most were short-lived. The three longest-lasting were <i><a href="/wiki/Cracked_(magazine)" title="Cracked (magazine)">Cracked</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sick_(magazine)" title="Sick (magazine)">Sick</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Crazy_Magazine" title="Crazy Magazine">Crazy Magazine</a></i>. Many featured a cover mascot along the lines of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Neuman" title="Alfred E. Neuman">Alfred E. Neuman</a>.</p>
<p>Color comic-book competitors, primarily in the mid-to-late 1950s, were <i>Nuts!</i>, <i>Get Lost</i>, <i>Whack</i>, <i>Riot</i>, <i>Flip</i>, <i>Eh!</i>, <i>From Here to Insanity</i>, and <i>Madhouse</i>; only the last of these lasted as many as eight issues, and some were canceled after an issue or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Later color satiric comic books included <i>Wild</i>, <i>Blast</i>, <i>Parody</i>, <i>Grin</i> and <i>Gag!</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> EC Comics itself offered the color comic <i><a href="/wiki/Panic_(comics)" title="Panic (comics)">Panic</a></i>, produced by future <i>Mad</i> editor Al Feldstein. Two years after EC's <i>Panic</i> had ceased publication in 1956, the title was used by another publisher for a similar comic.</p>
<p>In 1967, <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> produced the first of 13 issues of the comic book <i><a href="/wiki/Not_Brand_Echh" title="Not Brand Echh">Not Brand Echh</a></i>, which parodied the company's own superhero titles as well as other publishers. From 1973 to 1976, <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> published the comic <i><a href="/wiki/Plop!" title="Plop!">Plop!</a></i>, which featured <i>Mad</i> stalwart <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Aragon%C3%A9s" title="Sergio Aragonés">Sergio Aragonés</a> and frequent cover art by <a href="/wiki/Basil_Wolverton" title="Basil Wolverton">Basil Wolverton</a>. Another publisher's comic was <i>Trash</i> (1978)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> featured a blurb on the debut cover reading, "We mess with <i>Mad</i> (p. 21)" and depicted Alfred E. Neuman with a stubbly beard; the fourth and last issue showed two bodybuilders holding up copies of "Mud" and "Crocked" with the frowning faces of Neuman and <i><a href="/wiki/Cracked_(magazine)" title="Cracked (magazine)">Cracked</a></i> cover mascot Sylvester P. Smythe.</p>
<p>Among other U.S. humor magazines that included some degree of comics art as well as text articles were former <i>Mad</i> editor Harvey Kurtzman's <i><a href="/wiki/Humbug_(magazine)" title="Humbug (magazine)">Humbug</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Help!_(magazine)" title="Help! (magazine)">Help!</a></i>, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/National_Lampoon_(magazine)" title="National Lampoon (magazine)">National Lampoon</a></i>.</p>
<p>Spy vs Spy started Sunday strips originally in 2002 then 2014.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_media">In other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the years, <i>Mad</i> has branched out from print into other media. During the Gaines years, the publisher had an aversion to exploiting his fan base and expressed the fear that substandard <i>Mad</i> products would offend them. He was known to personally issue refunds to anyone who wrote to the magazine with a complaint. Among the few outside <i>Mad</i> items available in its first 40 years were cufflinks, a T-shirt designed like a straitjacket (complete with lock), and a small ceramic Alfred E. Neuman bust. For decades, the letters page advertised an inexpensive portrait of Neuman ("suitable for framing or for wrapping fish") with misleading slogans such as "Only 1 Left!" (The joke being that the picture was so undesirable that only one had left their office since the last ad.) After Gaines' death came an overt absorption into the Time-Warner publishing umbrella, with the result that <i>Mad</i> merchandise began to appear more frequently. Items were displayed in the Warner Bros. Studio Stores, and in 1994 <i>The Mad Style Guide</i> was created for licensing use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Mad</i> has sponsored or inspired a number of recordings. In 1959, Bernie Green "with the Stereo Mad-Men" recorded the album <i>Musically Mad</i> for <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Victor</a>, featuring music inspired by <i>Mad</i> and an image of Alfred E. Neuman on the cover;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> it has been reissued on CD. That same year, <i>The Worst from Mad</i> #2 included an original recording, "Meet the staff of Mad", on a cardboard 33 rpm record, while a single credited to Alfred E. Neuman &amp; The Furshlugginger Five: "What – Me Worry?" (b/w "Potrzebie"), was issued in late 1959 on the ABC Paramount label. Two additional albums of novelty songs were released by Big Top Records in 1962–63: "Mad 'Twists' Rock 'N' Roll" and "Fink Along with Mad". The latter album featured a song titled "It's a Gas", which punctuated an instrumental track with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belching" title="Belching">belches</a> (along with a saxophone break by an uncredited <a href="/wiki/King_Curtis" title="King Curtis">King Curtis</a>). <a href="/wiki/Dr._Demento" title="Dr. Demento">Dr. Demento</a> featured this gaseous performance on his radio show in Los Angeles in the early 1970s. <i>Mad</i> included some of these tracks as plastic-laminated cardboard inserts and (later) flexi-discs with their reprinted "Mad Specials". A number of original recordings also were released in this way in the 1970s and early 1980s, such as "Gall in the Family Fare" (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_play" title="Radio play">radio play</a> adaptation of their previously illustrated <i><a href="/wiki/All_in_the_Family" title="All in the Family">All in the Family</a></i> parody), a single entitled "Makin' Out", the octuple-grooved track "It's a Super Spectacular Day", which had eight possible endings, the spoken word <i>Meet the staff</i> insert, and a six-track, 30-minute <i>Mad Disco</i> EP (from the 1980 Special of the same title) that included a <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> version of "It's a Gas". The last turntable-playable recording <i>Mad</i> packaged with its magazines was "A Mad Look at Graduation", in a 1983 Special. A CD-ROM containing several audio tracks was included with issue #350 (October 1996). <a href="/wiki/Rhino_Entertainment" title="Rhino Entertainment">Rhino Records</a> compiled a number of <i>Mad</i>-recorded tracks as <i>Mad Grooves</i> (1996).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_show">Stage show</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Stage show">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off_Broadway" title="Off Broadway">off Broadway</a> production, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mad_Show" title="The Mad Show">The Mad Show</a></i>, was first staged in 1966. The show, which lasted for 871 performances during its initial run, featured sketches written by <i>Mad</i> regulars Stan Hart and Larry Siegel interspersed with comedic songs (one of which was written by an uncredited <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Sondheim" title="Stephen Sondheim">Stephen Sondheim</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maria_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-maria-26">[26]</a></sup> The cast album is available on CD.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaming">Gaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1979, Mad released a <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Mad_Magazine_Game" title="The Mad Magazine Game">The Mad Magazine Game</a> was an absurdist version of <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a> in which the first player to lose all his money and go bankrupt was the winner. Profusely illustrated with artwork by the magazine's contributors, the game included a $1,329,063-bill that could not be won unless one's name was "Alfred E. Neuman". It also featured a deck of cards (called "Card cards") with bizarre instructions, such as "If you can jump up and stay airborne for 37 seconds, you can lose $5,000. If not, jump up and lose $500." In 1980 a second game was released: <a href="/wiki/The_Mad_Magazine_Card_Game" title="The Mad Magazine Card Game">The Mad Magazine Card Game</a> by <a href="/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a>. In it, the player who first <a href="/wiki/Card_game#Shedding_games" title="Card game">loses all their cards</a> is declared the winner. The game is fairly similar to <a href="/wiki/Uno_(card_game)" title="Uno (card game)">Uno</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the success of the National Lampoon–backed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Lampoon%27s_Animal_House" title="National Lampoon's Animal House">Animal House</a></i>, <i>Mad</i> lent its name in 1980 to a similarly risque comedy film, <i><a href="/wiki/Up_the_Academy" title="Up the Academy">Up the Academy</a></i>. It was such a commercial debacle and critical failure that <i>Mad</i> successfully arranged for all references to the magazine (including a cameo by Alfred E. Neuman) to be removed from future TV and video releases of the film, although those references were eventually restored on the DVD version which was titled, "Mad Magazine Presents Up the Academy". <i>Mad</i> also devoted two pages of its magazine to an attack on the movie, titled <i>Throw Up the Academy</i>. The spoof's ending collapsed into a series of interoffice memos between the writer, artist, editor and publisher, all bewailing the fact that they had been forced to satirize such a terrible film.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A 1974 <i>Mad</i> animated television pilot using selected material from the magazine was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> but the network decided to not broadcast it. Dick DeBartolo noted, "Nobody wanted to sponsor a show that made fun of products that were advertised on TV, like car manufacturers." The program was instead created into a TV special, and is available for online viewing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, <a href="/wiki/Hanna-Barbera" title="Hanna-Barbera">Hanna-Barbera</a> developed another potential Mad animated television series which was never broadcast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox Broadcasting Company</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_TV" title="Mad TV">Mad TV</a></i> licensed the use of the magazine's logo and characters. However, aside from short bumpers which animated existing "Spy vs. Spy" (1994–1998) and Don Martin (1995–2000) cartoons during the show's first three seasons, there was no editorial or stylistic connection between the TV show and the magazine. Produced by <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a>, the sketch comedy series was in the vein of <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Global_Television_Network" title="Global Television Network">Global</a>/<a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">CBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Second_City_Television" title="Second City Television">SCTV</a></i>, and ran for 14 seasons and 321 episodes. On January 12, 2016, <a href="/wiki/The_CW" title="The CW">The CW</a> aired an hour-long special celebrating the series' 20th anniversary. A large portion of the original cast returned. An 8-episode revival featuring a brand new cast premiered on July 26, 2016.</p>
<p>Animated "Spy vs. Spy" sequences have also been seen in TV ads for <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Dew" title="Mountain Dew">Mountain Dew</a> soda in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zine_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-zine-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2010, <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> began airing the animated series, <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_(TV_series)" title="Mad (TV series)">Mad</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Animation" title="Warner Bros. Animation">Warner Bros. Animation</a> and executive producer Sam Register (<i><a href="/wiki/Teen_Titans_(TV_series)" title="Teen Titans (TV series)">Teen Titans</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ben_10_(TV_series)" title="Ben 10 (TV series)">Ben 10</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Brave_and_the_Bold" title="Batman: The Brave and the Bold">Batman: The Brave and the Bold</a></i>). The series aired short animated vignettes about current television shows, films, games and other aspects of popular culture. Much like <i>Mad TV's</i>, this series also features appearances by "Spy vs. Spy" and Don Martin cartoons. Produced by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Shinick" title="Kevin Shinick">Kevin Shinick</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Chicken" title="Robot Chicken">Robot Chicken</a></i>) and Mark Marek (<i><a href="/wiki/KaBlam!" title="KaBlam!">KaBlam!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andy_Milonakis_Show" title="The Andy Milonakis Show">The Andy Milonakis Show</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> the series ran from September 6, 2010 to December 2, 2013, lasting for 4 seasons and 103 episodes.</p>

<p>In the 1984, the <a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy" title="Spy vs. Spy">Spy vs. Spy</a> characters were given their own computer game <a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy_(1984_video_game)" title="Spy vs. Spy (1984 video game)">series</a>, in which players could set traps for each other, were made for various computer systems such as the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a> and <a href="/wiki/ZX_Spectrum" title="ZX Spectrum">ZX Spectrum</a>. Whereas the original game took place in a nondescript building, the sequels transposed the action to a desert island for <i>Spy vs. Spy: The Island Caper</i> and a polar setting for <i>Spy vs. Spy: Arctic Antics</i>.</p>
<p>Not to be confused with the later television show, <a href="/wiki/Mad_TV_(video_game)" title="Mad TV (video game)">Mad TV</a> is a television station management simulation computer game produced in 1991[1] by Rainbow Arts for the Mad franchise. It was released on the PC and the Amiga. It is faithful to the magazine's general style of cartoon humor, but does not include any of the original characters except for a brief closeup of Alfred E. Neuman's eyes during the opening screens.</p>
<p>In 1996, <i>Mad</i> #350 included a CD-ROM featuring <i>Mad</i>-related software as well as three audio files.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In 1999, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8derbund" title="Brøderbund">Brøderbund</a>/<a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a> released <i>Totally Mad,</i> a <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> 95/98 compatible CD-ROM set collecting the magazine's content from #1 through #376 (December 1998), plus over 100 <i>Mad Specials</i> including most of the recorded audio inserts. Despite the title, it omitted a handful of articles due to problems clearing the rights on some book excerpts and text taken from recordings, such as <a href="/wiki/Andy_Griffith" title="Andy Griffith">Andy Griffith</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/What_It_Was,_Was_Football" title="What It Was, Was Football">What It Was, Was Football</a>". In 2006, Graphic Imaging Technology's DVD-ROM <i>Absolutely Mad</i> updated the original <i>Totally Mad</i> content through 2005. A single seven-gigabyte disc, it is missing the same deleted material from the 1999 collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> It differs from the earlier release in that it is <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">Macintosh</a> compatible.</p>
<p>Another <a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy_(2005_video_game)" title="Spy vs. Spy (2005 video game)">Spy vs. Spy</a> video game was made in 2005 for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>. A <i>Mad</i> app was released for <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a> on April 1, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> It displays the contents of each new issue beginning with <i>Mad</i> #507, as well as video clips from <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_(TV_series)" title="Mad (TV series)">Mad-TV</a></i>, and material from the magazine's website, The Idiotical.</p>
