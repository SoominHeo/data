<p><b>Mars Atmosphere and Volatile EvolutioN Mission</b> (<b>MAVEN</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> designed to study the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">Martian atmosphere</a> while orbiting <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. Mission goals include determining how the planet's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">water</a>, presumed to have once been substantial, were lost over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140921_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140921-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140921_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140921-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20131115_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131115-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAM_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAM-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FB-MAVEN_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB-MAVEN-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>MAVEN was successfully launched aboard an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> at the beginning of the first <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch window</a> on November 18, 2013. Following the first engine burn of the <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a> second stage, the vehicle coasted in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> for 27 minutes before a second Centaur burn of five minutes to insert it into a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric</a> Mars <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">transit orbit</a>.</p>
<p>On September 22, 2014, MAVEN reached Mars and was inserted into an <a href="/wiki/Areocentric_orbit" title="Areocentric orbit">areocentric</a> <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a> 6,200 km (3,900 mi) by 150 km (93 mi) above the planet's surface. The principal investigator for the spacecraft is <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Jakosky" title="Bruce Jakosky">Bruce Jakosky</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Boulder" title="University of Colorado Boulder">University of Colorado Boulder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140921_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140921-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140921_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140921-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140917_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140917-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 November 2015, NASA announced that data from MAVEN shows that the deterioration of Mars’ atmosphere increases significantly during <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar storms</a>. That loss of atmosphere to space likely played a key role in Mars' gradual shift from its <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>-dominated atmosphere - which had kept Mars relatively warm and allowed the planet to support liquid surface water - to the cold, arid planet seen today. This shift took place between about 4.2 to 3.7 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-15217_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-15217-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MAVEN&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The mission was spawned by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mars_Scout_Program" title="Mars Scout Program">Mars Scout Program</a>, which, although discontinued in 2010, yielded <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft)" title="Phoenix (spacecraft)">Phoenix</a>, MAVEN, and numerous missions' studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Mars Scout missions target a cost of less than US$485 million, not including launch services, which cost approximately $187 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The total project costs up to $671 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 15, 2008, NASA announced that it had selected MAVEN to be the Mars Scout 2013 mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selection_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-selection-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There was one other finalist and eight other proposals that were competing against MAVEN. The name is a deliberate use of the word <i><a href="/wiki/Maven" title="Maven">maven</a></i>, "a person who has special knowledge or experience; an expert".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 2, 2013, the MAVEN spacecraft arrived at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> to begin launch preparations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> NASA scheduled the launch of MAVEN from the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a> on November 18, 2013, using an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> 401 rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The probe arrived in Mars orbit in September 2014, at approximately the same time as India's <a href="/wiki/Mars_Orbiter_Mission" title="Mars Orbiter Mission">Mars Orbiter Mission</a>.</p>
<p>On October 1, 2013, only seven weeks before launch, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_government_shutdown_of_2013" title="United States federal government shutdown of 2013">government shutdown</a> caused suspension of work for two days and initially threatened to force a 26-month postponement of the mission. With the spacecraft nominally scheduled to launch on November 18, a delay beyond December 7 would have caused MAVEN to miss the launch window as <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> moved too far out of alignment with the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, two days later, a public announcement was made that NASA had deemed the 2013 MAVEN launch so essential to ensuring future communication with current NASA assets on Mars—the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_(rover)" title="Opportunity (rover)">Opportunity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_rover" title="Curiosity rover">Curiosity rovers</a>—that emergency funding was authorized to restart spacecraft processing in preparation for an on-time launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 22, 2014, at approximately 2:24 UTC, MAVEN spacecraft entered orbit around Mars, completing an interplanetary journey of 10 months and 442 million miles (711 million kilometers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>Features on Mars that resemble dry <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">riverbeds</a> and the discovery of minerals that form in the presence of water indicate that Mars once had a <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">dense enough atmosphere</a> and was warm enough for <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">liquid water</a> to flow on the surface. However, that thick atmosphere was somehow lost to space. Scientists suspect that over millions of years, Mars lost 99% of its atmosphere as the planet’s core cooled and its magnetic field decayed, allowing the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> to sweep away most of the water and volatile compounds that the atmosphere once contained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun-steals_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-sun-steals-27">[27]</a></sup> The goal of MAVEN is to determine the history of the loss of atmospheric gases to space, providing answers about <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Mars" title="Climate of Mars">Martian climate</a> evolution. By measuring the rate with which the atmosphere is currently escaping to space and gathering enough information about the relevant processes, scientists will be able to infer how <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">the planet's atmosphere</a> evolved over time. The MAVEN mission has four primary scientific objectives:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg"><img alt="Artist conception of MAVEN spacecraft" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2251" data-file-width="4000" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg/200px-Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg/300px-Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg/400px-Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Artist_concept_of_MAVEN_spacecraft.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Artist conception of MAVEN spacecraft</div>
</div>
</div>
<ol>
<li>Determine the role that loss of <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">volatiles</a> to space from the Martian atmosphere has played through time.</li>
<li>Determine the current state of the upper atmosphere, ionosphere, and interactions with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</li>
<li>Determine the current rates of escape of <a href="/wiki/Critical_ionization_velocity" title="Critical ionization velocity">neutral gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> to space and the processes controlling them.</li>
<li>Determine the ratios of stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> in the Martian atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>MAVEN reached Mars and successfully maneuvered into orbit around the planet on September 21, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140921_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140921-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sample_Analysis_at_Mars" title="Sample Analysis at Mars">Sample Analysis at Mars</a> (SAM) instrument suite on board the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_rover" title="Curiosity rover">Curiosity rover</a> was scheduled to make similar surface measurements from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gale_crater" title="Gale crater">Gale crater</a> by that date. The data from Curiosity will help guide the interpretation of MAVEN's upper atmosphere measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAM_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SAM-7">[7]</a></sup> MAVEN's measurements will also provide additional scientific context with which to test models for current <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars#Methane" title="Atmosphere of Mars">methane formation in Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_overview">Spacecraft overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MAVEN&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spacecraft overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MAVEN was built and tested by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Space_Systems" title="Lockheed Martin Space Systems">Lockheed Martin Space Systems</a>. Its design is based on those of the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter">Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/2001_Mars_Odyssey" title="2001 Mars Odyssey">Mars Odyssey</a> spacecraft. The orbiter has a cubical shape of about <span class="nowrap">2.3 meters × 2.3 meters × 2 meters</span> high,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> with two <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar arrays</a> that hold the magnetometers on both ends. The total length is <span class="nowrap">11.4 meters</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relay_telecommunications">Relay telecommunications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MAVEN&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relay telecommunications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>NASA's <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> provided an <a href="/wiki/Electra_(radio)" title="Electra (radio)">Electra</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">ultra high frequency</a> (UHF) relay radio payload<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> which has a data return rate of up to <span class="nowrap">2048 kbit/s</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The highly elliptical orbit of the MAVEN spacecraft may limit its usefulness as a relay for operating landers on the surface, although the long view periods of MAVEN's orbit have afforded some of the largest relay data returns to date of any Mars orbiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telecom_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telecom-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> During the mission's first year of operations at Mars — the primary science phase — MAVEN served as a backup relay orbiter. Going forward into the extended mission for a period of up to ten years, MAVEN will provide UHF relay service for present and future Mars rovers and landers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>MAVEN will study Mars' upper atmosphere and its interactions with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. Its instruments will measure characteristics of Mars' atmospheric gases, upper atmosphere, <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, and the solar wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> MAVEN will perform measurements from a highly elliptical orbit over a period of one Earth year, with five "deep dips" at 150 km (93 mi) minimum altitude to sample the upper atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Boulder" title="University of Colorado Boulder">University of Colorado Boulder</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> each built a suite of instruments for the spacecraft, and they include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Particles and Field (P&amp;F) Package:</b></p>
<dl>
<dd>Built by the University of California, Berkeley <a href="/wiki/Space_Sciences_Laboratory" title="Space Sciences Laboratory">Space Sciences Laboratory</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Solar Wind Electron Analyzer (SWEA) – measures <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and ionosphere electrons</li>
<li>Solar Wind Ion Analyzer (SWIA) – measures solar wind and <a href="/wiki/Magnetosheath" title="Magnetosheath">magnetosheath</a> ion density and velocity</li>
<li>SupraThermal And Thermal Ion Composition (STATIC) – measures thermal ions to moderate-energy escaping ions</li>
<li>Solar Energetic Particle (SEP) – determines the impact of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">SEPs</a> on the upper atmosphere</li>
<li>Langmuir Probe and Waves (LPW) – determines ionosphere properties and wave heating of escaping ions and solar <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a> (EUV) input to atmosphere</li>
<li>Magnetometer (MAG) – measures interplanetary solar wind and ionosphere <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetic fields</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><b>Remote Sensing (RS) Package:</b></p>
<dl>
<dd>Built by the University of Colorado <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Imaging Ultraviolet Spectrometer (IUVS) – measures global characteristics of the upper atmosphere and ionosphere</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Neutral Gas and Ion Mass Spectrometer (NGIMS) Package:</b></p>
<dl>
<dd>Built by Goddard Space Flight Center</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Measures the composition and <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Critical_ionization_velocity" title="Critical ionization velocity">neutral gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MAVEN&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The orbiter is in Mars orbit and operational. The commissioning phase lasted until the first week of November 2014 (six weeks), and then the science phase of the mission commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In June 2015, the science phase was extended through September 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mission_Extension_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mission_Extension-2">[2]</a></sup> MAVEN carries enough fuel to extend its science mission for an additional two years, and then another six years to function as a telecomm orbiter at a higher, more circular orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mission_Extension_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mission_Extension-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MAVEN&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On November 5, 2015 NASA scientists reported, based on results from the MAVEN orbiter circling <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, that the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> is responsible for stripping away the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a> over the years, as the shielding effect of the global magnetic field was lost as the planet's internal dynamo cooled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151105_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151105-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YT-20151105_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-YT-20151105-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

Mars - <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">escaping atmosphere</a> - <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (MAVEN; <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet%E2%80%93visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy">UV</a>; October 14, 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20141014-NJ_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20141014-NJ-47">[47]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><br/>
Mars loses water into its thin atmosphere by evaporation. There, solar radiation can split the water molecules into their components, hydrogen and oxygen. The hydrogen, being the lightest element in the universe, then tends to rise far up to the highest levels of the Martian atmosphere, where several processes can strip it away into space, to be forever lost to the planet. This loss was thought to proceed at a fairly constant rate, but MAVEN's observations of Mars's atmospheric hydrogen through a full Martian year (almost two Earth years) show that the escape rate is highest when Mars's orbit brings it closest to the Sun, and only one-tenth as great when it is at its farthest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
