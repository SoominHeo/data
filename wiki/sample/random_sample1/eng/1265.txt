<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Transportation_Authority" title="Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority">Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority</a></b> operates <b>177 <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> routes (<a href="/wiki/List_of_MBTA_bus_routes" title="List of MBTA bus routes">list of routes</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Boston" title="Greater Boston">Greater Boston</a> area</b>, many of which were formerly part of a <a href="/wiki/Boston-area_streetcar_lines" title="Boston-area streetcar lines">large streetcar system</a>. Some routes are for local transport within the city; others bring passengers from surrounding areas to stops on the <a href="/wiki/MBTA_Commuter_Rail" title="MBTA Commuter Rail">MBTA Commuter Rail</a> or <a href="/wiki/MBTA_Subway" title="MBTA Subway">subway</a> lines. The MBTA has a policy objective to provide transit service within walking distance (defined as 0.25 miles (0.40 km)) for all residents living in areas with population densities greater than 5,000 inhabitants per square mile (1,900/km<sup>2</sup>) within the MBTA's service district. Much of this service is provided by bus.</p>
<p>The MBTA operates a four-route <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> service branded as the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Line_(MBTA)" title="Silver Line (MBTA)">Silver Line</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/MBTA_crosstown_bus_routes" title="MBTA crosstown bus routes">three crosstown routes</a> that were intended to become the first part of the now-suspended <a href="/wiki/Urban_Ring_Project_(MBTA)" title="Urban Ring Project (MBTA)">Urban Ring</a> project. Fifteen routes designed as <a href="/wiki/List_of_key_MBTA_bus_routes" title="List of key MBTA bus routes">key routes</a> run with higher frequency at all times, including extended service hours on Friday and Saturday nights over some of these routes.</p>
<p>Most MBTA Bus service is served by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_transmission" title="Diesel-electric transmission">diesel-electric hybrid</a> buses. Silver Line routes running in the Waterfront Tunnel use <a href="/wiki/Dual-mode_bus" title="Dual-mode bus">dual-mode</a> buses that operate as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trolleybuses" title="Trolleybuses">trolleybuses</a> in the tunnel and as diesel-electric hybrid buses on the surface. <a href="/wiki/Trolleybuses_in_Greater_Boston#Harvard-based_routes" title="Trolleybuses in Greater Boston">Four routes</a> based out of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_Bus_Tunnel" title="Harvard Bus Tunnel">Harvard Bus Tunnel</a> run with trolleybuses in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> and also serve several surrounding suburbs.</p>
<p>All buses and routes are wheelchair-accessible (see <a href="/wiki/MBTA_accessibility" title="MBTA accessibility">MBTA accessibility</a>); most of the MBTA's bus fleet consists of <a href="/wiki/Low-floor_bus" title="Low-floor bus">low-floor buses</a> with wheelchair ramps, while older high-floor buses have lifts. All buses have amber (orange) colored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> exterior <a href="/wiki/Destination_sign" title="Destination sign">headsigns</a> displaying route and destination, with automated audio/visual stop announcements for passengers.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MBTA_Bus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 29, 2015, the MassDOT board approved the purchase of 325 new 40-foot buses (175 CNG-powered and 150 hybrid) from New Flyer, with options for an additional 200 hybrid buses and 200 diesel buses. The 325 buses, costing a total of $222.2 million, will be delivered in 2016 and 2017 following the acceptance of a production test model. They will replace the remaining C40LF and 40-LFW fleets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roster_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-roster-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Six additional hybrid buses will be used for privately operated routes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/712_(MBTA_bus)#712" title="712 (MBTA bus)">712</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/713_(MBTA_bus)#713" title="713 (MBTA bus)">713</a> when a new contract begins on July 1, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roster_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-roster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 5, 2015, the MBTA Fiscal Control Board approved the purchase of 44 new 60-foot articulated hybrid buses from New Flyer to replace the CNG-powered Neoplan AN460LF fleet. The contract will include an option for an additional hybrid bus with extended range electric-only operation for Silver Line Waterfront use. If tested successfully, an additional option for 45 hybrid buses with extended range electric operation would be exercised to replace the dual-mode AN460LF fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roster_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-roster-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2015, the MBTA was awarded a $4.14 million <a href="/wiki/Federal_Transit_Administration" title="Federal Transit Administration">FTA</a> grant to purchase five 60-foot articulated battery electric buses from New Flyer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roster_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-roster-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2016, the MBTA will place a prototype 40-foot hydrogen fuel cell bus provided by the FTA into service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roster_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-roster-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_buses">Private buses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MBTA_Bus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Private buses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1150" data-file-width="1800" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg/220px-Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg/330px-Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg/440px-Blue_Hill_Bus_Lines_24_at_Mattapan_Terminal_in_1967.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_MBTA_bus_routes#Privately_operated_routes" title="List of MBTA bus routes">List of MBTA bus routes § Privately operated routes</a></div>
<p>Most local bus routes in Massachusetts outside the immediate MBTA operating area are operated by the state's other regional transit authorities (RTAs). However, some routes that connect with MBTA bus or subway service are operated by outside private contractors with partial subsidy by the MBTA. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five routes – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/710_(MBTA_bus)#710" title="710 (MBTA bus)">710</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/712_(MBTA_bus)#712" title="712 (MBTA bus)">712</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/713_(MBTA_bus)#713" title="713 (MBTA bus)">713</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/714_(MBTA_bus)#714" title="714 (MBTA bus)">714</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/716_(MBTA_bus)#716" title="716 (MBTA bus)">716</a> – are numbered like other MBTA buses; their operators accept MBTA passes on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CharlieTicket" title="CharlieTicket">CharlieTickets</a>, but do not have CharlieCard readers. The five routes are primarily commuter routes which connect with other MBTA services at their inbound terminals. They were taken over from various private operators (Hudson Bus Lines for the 710 and 716, Rapid Transit Inc. for the 712/713, and Nantasket Transportation for the 714).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netransit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-netransit-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five suburban municipalities contract with outside operators for local circulator routes, most with partial MBTA subsidy. <a href="/wiki/Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="Bedford, Massachusetts">Bedford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beverly,_Massachusetts" title="Beverly, Massachusetts">Beverly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dedham,_Massachusetts" title="Dedham, Massachusetts">Dedham</a> run single routes; <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Massachusetts" title="Burlington, Massachusetts">Burlington</a> runs five routes; and <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Massachusetts" title="Lexington, Massachusetts">Lexington</a> runs six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netransit_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-netransit-9">[9]</a></sup> Most are run by private operators, except for the Beverly Shuttle, which is part of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Ann_Transportation_Authority" title="Cape Ann Transportation Authority">Cape Ann Transportation Authority</a> system. Additionally, a nonprofit shuttle is run in Boston's <a href="/wiki/Mission_Hill,_Boston" title="Mission Hill, Boston">Mission Hill</a> neighborhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netransit_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-netransit-9">[9]</a></sup> Those 15 routes appear on MBTA system maps and connect with MBTA services at designated transfer points, but are numbered separately and do not accept MBTA passes.</p>
