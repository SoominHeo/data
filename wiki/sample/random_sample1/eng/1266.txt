<p><b>MCA, Inc.</b> (or <b>Music Corporation of America</b>) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> media company. Initially starting in the music business, the company next became a dominant force in the film business, and later expanded into the television business. MCA published music, booked acts, ran a record company, represented film, television and radio stars, and eventually produced and sold television programs to the three major <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> networks, but had an especially good relationship with <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>. MCA, Inc., is the legal predecessor of <a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a>, which since March 2013 is a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>. MCA's other legal successor is Universal Studios Holding I Corp, an <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a>, who also owns MCA's former music assets, now known as <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a> (which has absorbed <a href="/wiki/PolyGram" title="PolyGram">PolyGram</a> and <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Publishing_Group" title="Universal Music Publishing Group">Universal Music Publishing Group</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MCA_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MCA_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MCA was formed in 1924 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jules_Stein" title="Jules Stein">Jules Stein</a> and William R. Goodheart, Jr., as <b>Music Corporation of America</b>, a music booking agency based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>. MCA helped pioneer modern practices of touring bands and name acts. Early on, MCA booked such prominent artists as <a href="/wiki/King_Oliver" title="King Oliver">King Oliver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jelly_Roll_Morton" title="Jelly Roll Morton">Jelly Roll Morton</a> for clubs and <a href="/wiki/Speakeasy" title="Speakeasy">speakeasies</a> run by legendary notorious Chicago mobsters such as <a href="/wiki/Al_Capone" title="Al Capone">Al Capone</a> and others.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lew_Wasserman" title="Lew Wasserman">Lew Wasserman</a> joined MCA in 1936 at the age of 23 and rose through the ranks of MCA for more than four decades, with <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Werblin" title="Sonny Werblin">Sonny Werblin</a> as his right-hand man. Wasserman helped create MCA's radio show, <i><a href="/wiki/Kay_Kyser" title="Kay Kyser">Kay Kyser and His Kollege of Musical Knowledge</a></i>, which debuted on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Radio" title="NBC Radio">NBC Radio</a> that same year. Following that success, Stein installed Wasserman in New York City in 1937, but Wasserman convinced him that Hollywood was the best place for the company's growth.</p>
<p>The company was guided by a codification of Stein's pet policies known as "The Rules of The Road". The Rules were passed down from the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> era, Chicago–area MCA (referenced in <i>Citizen Cohn</i> and <i>The King and Queen of Hollywood</i> books) to the 1940s Los Angeles–area firm, which focused on representing movie actors. The Rules were next passed to the 1950s generation of MCA talent agents, including A. Jerold Perenchio, who later owned and headed a number of businesses including the 1992 to 2007 version of <a href="/wiki/Univision" title="Univision">Univision</a>. Perenchio is well known for his version of the Rules (now up to twenty rules), which vary from year to year and have some internal contradictions (Perenchio pointed out that while there is a "no nepotism" rule, he is aware his son was on the board of directors at the time).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1939, MCA's headquarters moved from Chicago to <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_California" title="Beverly Hills, California">Beverly Hills, California</a>, creating a movie division and beginning to acquire talent agencies and represent established actors such as <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">James Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fonda" title="Henry Fonda">Henry Fonda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bette_Davis" title="Bette Davis">Bette Davis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, whom Wasserman became very close with personally. In later decades, Wasserman became a guiding force in Reagan's political ambition by helping Reagan to win the presidency of the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">Screen Actors Guild</a> (SAG), then election as Governor of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 1966, and finally <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> in 1980.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1930s, MCA had become the largest <a href="/wiki/Talent_agent" title="Talent agent">talent agency</a> in the world, with over 700 clients, including movie stars, recording artists, Broadway actors, radio stars, producers, and directors. Its aggressive acquisition of its clientele in all entertainment sectors earned MCA the nickname of <i>The Octopus</i>. This behavior led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Justice" title="U.S. Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> agents to investigate not only whether MCA was a <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> breaking anti-trust laws, but also its suspected connections to underworld criminal activities. This investigation continued for the next few decades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revue_Productions">Revue Productions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MCA_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Revue Productions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1948, Jules Stein moved up as MCA's first chairman, giving Lew Wasserman charge of day-to-day operations of the company as president. That year, Stein and Wasserman decided to get into a new medium that would, decades later, change the entertainment landscape forever: <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>. Although many motion picture studios would not touch this new medium, thinking it was just a fad and would fade away, MCA decided to embrace it. First, however, the company needed to get a waiver from the Screen Actors Guild, which ruled at the time that talent agencies such as MCA were prohibited from producing TV shows or films. Thanks to the newly elected SAG president, Ronald Reagan, MCA was granted a waiver to start producing TV shows.</p>
<p>After the waiver was granted, the company formed <b>MCA Television Limited</b> for <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_syndication" title="Broadcast syndication">syndication</a>. In 1950 <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revue_Productions" title="Revue Productions">Revue Productions</a></b>, once a live concert subsidiary that produced "Stage Door Canteen" live events for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USO" title="USO">USO</a> during World War II, was re-launched as MCA's television production subsidiary. By 1956, Revue became the top supplier of television for all broadcast networks, spanning three decades of television programs such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Armour_Theater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Armour Theater (page does not exist)">Armour Theater</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/General_Electric_Theater" title="General Electric Theater">General Electric Theater</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Leave_It_to_Beaver" title="Leave It to Beaver">Leave It to Beaver</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wagon_Train" title="Wagon Train">Wagon Train</a></i>, and many others. Prior to 1958, all Revue's shows were filmed at the old <a href="/wiki/Republic_Pictures" title="Republic Pictures">Republic Pictures</a> studio lot in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_City,_California" title="Studio City, California">Studio City, California</a>.</p>
<p>In February 1958,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> MCA acquired <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>' pre-1950 sound feature film library through a newly created MCA subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/EMKA,_Ltd." title="EMKA, Ltd.">EMKA, Ltd.</a></p>
<p>Also in 1958, MCA bought the 423-acre (1.71 km<sup>2</sup>) Universal Studios lot from <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Pictures</a> for $11 million and renamed it, as well as the actual television unit, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revue_Studios" title="Revue Studios">Revue Studios</a>. As part of the deal, MCA leased the studios back to Universal for $2 million a year, plus unlimited access to MCA's clients such as Jimmy Stewart, <a href="/wiki/Rock_Hudson" title="Rock Hudson">Rock Hudson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doris_Day" title="Doris Day">Doris Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> to make films for Universal.</p>
<p>Stein, who by this time was the sole owner of MCA, decided to take the company public by giving 51% of his ownership of MCA to his employees, which included a 20% stake for Wasserman. The company went <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">public</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> and was incorporated as <b>MCA Inc.</b> on November 10, 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A couple of years later, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> gave MCA his rights to <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho_(1960_film)" title="Psycho (1960 film)">Psycho</a></i> and his television anthology in exchange for 150,000 shares, making him the third largest investor in MCA, and his own boss at Universal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Takeover_of_Universal_Pictures_and_Decca_Records">Takeover of Universal Pictures and Decca Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MCA_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Takeover of Universal Pictures and Decca Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1962, MCA entered a <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">merger of equals</a> with New York-based American <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca Records</a>, with MCA as the surviving company. Decca at the time owned <a href="/wiki/Coral_Records" title="Coral Records">Coral Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_Records" title="Brunswick Records">Brunswick Records</a>, as well as an 89% controlling stake in Universal Pictures. In order to acquire Universal, MCA was forced by <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>'s Department of Justice to dissolve its talent agency—which represented most of the industry's biggest names—because owning both the movie studio and a talent agency would violate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antitrust" title="Antitrust">antitrust</a> laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The agents quickly formed several dozen new firms, representing talent. Many of these are woven into the corporate fabric of today's talent management agencies. Jerry Perenchio's firm, UTM (United Talent Management) represented <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Elizabeth Taylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">Muhammad Ali</a>. By the end of the year, MCA had assumed full ownership of Universal upon the completion of the merger.</p>
<p>In 1964, MCA formed Universal City Studios, to merge under one umbrella, both Universal Pictures and Revue Productions division, which was reincorporated as <a href="/wiki/Universal_Television" title="Universal Television">Universal Television</a> in 1966.</p>
<p>In 1966, MCA formed <a href="/wiki/Uni_Records" title="Uni Records">Uni Records</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in 1967, MCA bought New York-based <a href="/wiki/Kapp_Records" title="Kapp Records">Kapp Records</a>. That same year MCA also acquired guitar maker <a href="/wiki/Danelectro" title="Danelectro">Danelectro</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MCA_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a> label was established outside the United States and Canada to issue releases by the MCA group of labels. Decca, Kapp and Uni were merged into MCA Records at <a href="/wiki/Universal_City,_California" title="Universal City, California">Universal City, California</a> in 1971; the three labels maintained their identities for a short time but were soon retired in favor of the MCA label. The first MCA Records release in the US was former Uni artist <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Crocodile_Rock" title="Crocodile Rock">Crocodile Rock</a>" in 1972. In 1973, the final Decca pop label release was issued.</p>
<p>In 1973, Stein stepped down from the company he founded and Wasserman took over as chairman and chief executive officer, while <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Sheinberg" title="Sidney Sheinberg">Sidney Sheinberg</a> was appointed president and chief operating officer of MCA. Other executives within MCA were Lawrence R. Barnett, who ran the agency's live acts division during its glory agency years in the 1950s and 1960s, and <a href="/wiki/Ned_Tanen" title="Ned Tanen">Ned Tanen</a>, head of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>. Tanen was behind Universal hits such as <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_House" title="Animal House">Animal House</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hughes_(filmmaker)" title="John Hughes (filmmaker)">John Hughes</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Candles" title="Sixteen Candles">Sixteen Candles</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Breakfast_Club" title="The Breakfast Club">The Breakfast Club</a></i>.</p>
<p>MCA issued soundtrack albums for most films released by Universal Pictures.</p>
<p>In 1975, the company entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_publisher" title="Book publisher">book publishing</a> business with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/G._P._Putnam%27s_Sons" title="G. P. Putnam's Sons">G. P. Putnam's Sons</a>. In 1979, it acquired <a href="/wiki/ABC_Records" title="ABC Records">ABC Records</a> along with its subsidiaries <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Records_(1969)" title="Paramount Records (1969)">Paramount Records</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impulse_Records" title="Impulse Records">Impulse Records</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dot_Records" title="Dot Records">Dot Records</a>. ABC had acquired the Paramount and Dot labels when it purchased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf%2BWestern" title="Gulf+Western">Gulf+Western</a>'s record labels.</p>
<p>From 1983 to 1989, <a href="/wiki/Irving_Azoff" title="Irving Azoff">Irving Azoff</a> was chairman of MCA Records and is credited for turning around the fortunes of the label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">Chess Records</a> catalog was acquired from the remnants of Sugarhill in 1985. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motown_Records" title="Motown Records">Motown Records</a> was bought in 1988 (and sold to <a href="/wiki/PolyGram" title="PolyGram">PolyGram</a> in 1993). <a href="/wiki/GRP_Records" title="GRP Records">GRP Records</a> (which became MCA's label for <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> music and thus began managing the company's jazz catalogue) and <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a> (which served as another mainstream music subsidiary) were acquired in 1990. In the same year, MCA was purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panasonic_Corporation" title="Panasonic Corporation">Matsushita Electric</a>.</p>
<p>MCA also acquired other assets outside of the music industry. It became a shareholder in <a href="/wiki/USA_Network" title="USA Network">USA Network</a> in 1981, eventually owning 50% of the network (the other half was owned by Paramount). In 1982, its publishing division, G. P. Putnam's Sons, bought <a href="/wiki/Grosset_%26_Dunlap" title="Grosset &amp; Dunlap">Grosset &amp; Dunlap</a> from <a href="/wiki/Filmways" title="Filmways">Filmways</a>. In 1984, MCA bought <a href="/wiki/Walter_Lantz_Productions" title="Walter Lantz Productions">Walter Lantz Productions</a> and its characters. It also bought a TV station in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/WWOR-TV" title="WWOR-TV">WWOR-TV</a> (renamed from WOR-TV), in 1987, from <a href="/wiki/RKO_General" title="RKO General">RKO General</a>, which was in the midst of a licensing scandal.</p>
<p>In November 1990, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panasonic_Corporation" title="Panasonic Corporation">Matsushita Electric</a> of Japan agreed to acquire MCA for US$6.59 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> MCA was forced to sell WWOR in 1991 by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a>, because foreign companies could not own over 25% of a US TV station.</p>
<p>In 1995, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seagram_Company_Ltd." title="Seagram Company Ltd.">Seagram Company Ltd.</a> acquired 80% of MCA from Matsushita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On December 9, 1996, the new owners dropped the MCA name; the company became <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios, Inc.</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and its music division, MCA Music Entertainment Group, was renamed <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a>. MCA Records continued to live on as a label within the Universal Music Group. The following year, G. P. Putnam's Sons was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Group" title="Penguin Group">Penguin Group</a>.</p>
<p>In 1996, MCA filed a lawsuit against <a href="/wiki/Viacom" title="Viacom">Viacom</a> over the latter's launch of the <a href="/wiki/TV_Land" title="TV Land">TV Land</a> cable network. Viacom had purchased Paramount in 1994, and the contract for USA Networks prohibited either of their owners from owning cable channels outside the joint venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Viacom had owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Networks" title="MTV Networks">MTV Networks</a> (the parent of TV Land) since 1985. The suit was settled when Viacom sold MCA its half of the joint venture. TV Land eventually added shows from the MCA/Universal library in 1999.</p>
<p>In 1998, Seagram acquired <a href="/wiki/PolyGram" title="PolyGram">PolyGram</a> from <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> and merged it with its music holdings. When Seagram's drinks business was bought by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Pernod_Ricard" title="Pernod Ricard">Pernod Ricard</a>, its media holdings (including Universal) were sold to <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a>, which became Vivendi Universal in 2000.</p>
<p>In the spring of 2003, MCA Records was folded into <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> label, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group_Nashville" title="Universal Music Group Nashville">MCA Nashville Records</a>, is still in operation. MCA's classical music catalog is managed by <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Grammophon" title="Deutsche Grammophon">Deutsche Grammophon</a>.</p>
<p>MCA's non-music assets at the time of the company's renaming, including Universal Studios and the 50% interest in <a href="/wiki/USA_Network" title="USA Network">USA Networks</a>, are now owned by NBC Universal (now full owner of USA), which was 80% owned by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>, and 20% owned by <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a>. In 2009, a deal was struck in which GE would buy out Vivendi's interest, and sold a controlling 51% interest to <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> afterwards. The sale was completed on January 28, 2011, with General Electric owning 49% of the newly formed <b><a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a></b>. Comcast bought GE's stake two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The landmark <a href="/wiki/Paul_Williams_(architect)" title="Paul Williams (architect)">Paul Williams</a>-designed Beverly Hills property that MCA once occupied is now the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_Talent_Agency" title="Paradigm Talent Agency">Paradigm Talent Agency</a>. Conference rooms inside the building are named after Jules Stein and Lew Wasserman in honor of the legendary occupants.</p>
