<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">MMX (instruction set)</h1>

<p><b>MMX</b> is a <a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">single instruction, multiple data</a> (<a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">SIMD</a>) <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, introduced in 1997 with its <a href="/wiki/P5_(microarchitecture)" title="P5 (microarchitecture)">P5</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentium_(brand)" title="Pentium (brand)">Pentium</a> line of <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a>, designated as "Pentium with MMX Technology".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It developed out of a similar unit introduced on the <a href="/wiki/Intel_i860" title="Intel i860">Intel i860</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and earlier the <a href="/wiki/Intel_i750" title="Intel i750">Intel i750</a> video pixel processor. MMX is a <a href="/wiki/Processor_supplementary_capability" title="Processor supplementary capability">processor supplementary capability</a> that is supported on recent <a href="/wiki/IA-32" title="IA-32">IA-32</a> processors by Intel and other vendors.</p>
<p>MMX has subsequently been extended by several programs by Intel and others: <a href="/wiki/3DNow!" title="3DNow!">3DNow!</a> and ongoing revisions of <a href="/wiki/Streaming_SIMD_Extensions" title="Streaming SIMD Extensions">Streaming SIMD Extensions</a> (SSE).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MMX is officially a meaningless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initialism" title="Initialism">initialism</a> <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarked</a> by Intel;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> unofficially, the initials have been variously explained as standing for <i>MultiMedia eXtension</i>, <i>Multiple Math eXtension</i>, or <i>Matrix Math eXtension</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">AMD</a>, during one of its numerous court battles with Intel, produced marketing material from Intel indicating that MMX stood for "Matrix Math Extensions". Since an initialism cannot be trademarked, this was an attempt to invalidate Intel's trademark. In 1995, Intel filed suit against AMD and Cyrix Corp. for misuse of its trademark MMX. AMD and Intel settled, with AMD acknowledging MMX as a trademark owned by Intel, and with Intel granting AMD rights to use the MMX trademark as a technology name, but not a processor name.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_details">Technical details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technical details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pentium_II.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2666" data-file-width="3444" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Pentium_II.jpg/220px-Pentium_II.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Pentium_II.jpg/330px-Pentium_II.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Pentium_II.jpg/440px-Pentium_II.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pentium_II.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pentium II processor with MMX technology</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>MMX defines eight <a href="/wiki/Processor_register" title="Processor register">registers</a>, called MM0 through MM7, and operations that operate on them. Each register is 64 bits wide and can be used to hold either 64-bit integers, or multiple smaller integers in a "packed" format: a single instruction can then be applied to two 32-bit integers, four 16-bit integers, or eight 8-bit integers at once.</p>
<p>MMX provides only integer operations. When originally developed, for the <a href="/wiki/Intel_i860" title="Intel i860">Intel i860</a>, the use of integer math made sense (both 2D and 3D calculations required it), but as graphics cards that did much of this became common, <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> <a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">SIMD</a> in the CPU became somewhat redundant for graphical applications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Saturation_arithmetic" title="Saturation arithmetic">saturation arithmetic</a> operations in MMX could<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2016)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> significantly speed up some <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">digital signal processing</a> applications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>To avoid compatibility problems with the <a href="/wiki/Context_switch" title="Context switch">context switch</a> mechanisms in existing operating systems, the MMX registers are aliases for the existing <a href="/wiki/X87" title="X87">x87</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_point_unit" title="Floating point unit">FPU</a> registers, which context switches would already save and restore. Unlike the x87 registers, which behave like a <a href="/wiki/Stack_(abstract_data_type)" title="Stack (abstract data type)">stack</a>, the MMX registers are each directly addressable (random access).</p>
<p>Any operation involving the floating point stack might also affect the MMX registers and vice versa, so this aliasing makes it difficult to work with floating point and SIMD operations in the same application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conte_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-conte-4">[4]</a></sup> To maximize performance, programmers often used the processor exclusively in one mode or the other, deferring the relatively slow switch between them as long as possible.</p>
<p>Each 64-bit MMX register corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Significand" title="Significand">mantissa</a> part of an 80-bit x87 register. The upper 16 bits of the x87 registers thus go unused in MMX, and these bits are all set to ones, making them <a href="/wiki/NaN" title="NaN">NaNs</a> or infinities in the floating point representation. This can be used by applications to decide whether a particular register's content is intended as floating point or SIMD data.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_support">Software support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Software support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Software support for MMX was slow in coming. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_C_Compiler" title="Intel C Compiler">Intel's C Compiler</a> and related development tools obtained <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_function" title="Intrinsic function">intrinsics</a> for invoking MMX instructions and Intel released <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_library" title="Software library">libraries</a> of common vectorized algorithms using MMX. Both Intel and <a href="/wiki/Metrowerks" title="Metrowerks">Metrowerks</a> attempted <a href="/wiki/Automatic_vectorization" title="Automatic vectorization">automatic vectorization</a> in their compilers, but the operations in the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> programming language mapped poorly onto the MMX instruction set and custom algorithms as of 2000 typically still had to be written in <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conte_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-conte-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Successor">Successor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Successor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMD" title="AMD">AMD</a>, a competing x86 microprocessor vendor, enhanced Intel's MMX with their own <a href="/wiki/3DNow!" title="3DNow!">3DNow!</a> instruction set. 3DNow is best known for adding single-precision (32-bit) floating-point support to the SIMD instruction-set, among other integer and more general enhancements.</p>
<p>Following MMX, Intel's next major x86 extension was the <a href="/wiki/Streaming_SIMD_Extensions" title="Streaming SIMD Extensions">SSE</a>, introduced with the Pentium-III family (roughly a year after AMD's 3DNow! was introduced).</p>
<p>SSE addressed the core shortcomings of MMX (inability to mix integer-SIMD ops with any floating-point ops) by creating a new 128-bit wide register file (XMM0–XMM7) and new SIMD instructions for it. Like 3DNow!, SSE focused exclusively on single-precision floating-point operations (32-bit); integer SIMD operations were still performed using the MMX register and instruction set. However, the new XMM register-file allowed SSE SIMD-operations to be freely mixed with either MMX or x87 FPU ops.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/SSE2" title="SSE2">SSE2</a>, introduced with the Pentium 4, further extended the x86 SIMD instruction set with integer (8/16/32 bit) and double-precision floating-point data support for the XMM register file. SSE2 also allowed the MMX opcodes to use XMM register operands, but ended this support with SSE4 (and recently with <a href="/wiki/SSE4#SSE4.2" title="SSE4">SSE4.2</a>, introduced in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(microarchitecture)" title="Core (microarchitecture)">Core microarchitecture</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, since processor support for any SSE revision also implies support for MMX, the removal does not limit the data types usable by x86 SIMD.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MMX_in_embedded_applications">MMX in embedded applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MMX_(instruction_set)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: MMX in embedded applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Intel's and <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell</a>'s <a href="/wiki/XScale" title="XScale">XScale</a> microprocessor core starting with PXA270 include an <a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">SIMD</a> <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> extension to the ARM core called <b>iwMMXt</b> whose functions are similar to those of the <a href="/wiki/IA-32" title="IA-32">IA-32</a> MMX extension. iwMMXt stands for "Intel Wireless MMX Technology". It provides arithmetic and logic operations on 64-bit integer numbers (the software may choose to instead perform two 32-bit, four 16-bit or eight 8-bit operations in a single instruction). The extension contains 16 data registers of 64-bits and eight control registers of 32-bits. All registers are accessed through standard <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM architecture</a> coprocessor mapping mechanism. iwMMXt occupies coprocessors 0 and 1 space, and some of its <a href="/wiki/Opcode" title="Opcode">opcodes</a> clash with the opcodes of the earlier floating-point extension, FPA.</p>
<p>Later versions of <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell</a>'s ARM processors supports both WMMX (Wireless MMX) and WMMX2 (Wireless MMX2) support.</p>
