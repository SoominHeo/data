<p>The <b>MP 40</b> (<i>Maschinenpistole 40</i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Submachine_gun" title="Submachine gun">submachine gun</a> chambered for the <a href="/wiki/9%C3%9719mm_Parabellum" title="9×19mm Parabellum">9×19mm Parabellum</a> cartridge. It was developed in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and used extensively by the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a> during <span class="nowrap">World War II</span>.</p>
<p>Designed in 1938 by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Vollmer" title="Heinrich Vollmer">Heinrich Vollmer</a> with inspiration from its predecessor the MP 38, it was heavily used by infantrymen, <a href="/wiki/Fallschirmj%C3%A4ger" title="Fallschirmjäger">paratroopers</a>, platoon and squad leaders on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Western Front (World War II)">Western Front</a>. Its advanced and modern features made it a favorite among soldiers and popular in countries from various parts of the world after the war. It was often erroneously called "Schmeisser" by the Allies, despite <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schmeisser" title="Hugo Schmeisser">Hugo Schmeisser</a>'s non-involvement in the weapon's design and production. From 1940 to 1945, an estimated 1.1 million were produced by <a href="/wiki/Erma_Werke" title="Erma Werke">Erma Werke</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <i>Maschinenpistole 40</i> ("Machine pistol 40") descended from its predecessor the MP 38, which was in turn based on the MP 36, a prototype made of machined steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201413_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201413-5">[5]</a></sup> The MP 36 was developed independently by <a href="/wiki/Erma_Werke" title="Erma Werke">Erma Werke</a>'s Berthold Geipel with funding from the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">German Army</a>. It took design elements from <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Vollmer" title="Heinrich Vollmer">Heinrich Vollmer</a>'s VPM 1930 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ERMA_EMP-35" title="ERMA EMP-35">EMP</a>. Vollmer then worked on Berthold Geipel's MP 36 and in 1938 submitted a prototype to answer a request from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heereswaffenamt" title="Heereswaffenamt">Heereswaffenamt</a></i> (Army Weapons Office) for a new submachine gun, which was adopted as MP 38. The MP 38 was a simplification of the MP 36, and the MP 40 was a further simplification of the MP 38, with certain cost-saving alterations, most notably in the more extensive use of stamped steel rather than machined parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201413_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201413-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MP 40 was often called the "Schmeisser" by the Allies, after the weapon designer <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schmeisser" title="Hugo Schmeisser">Hugo Schmeisser</a>. Schmeisser had designed the <a href="/wiki/MP_18" title="MP 18">MP 18</a>, which was the first mass-produced submachine gun in the world, and carried some resemblance to the MP 40. He did not, however, have anything to do with the design or development of the MP 40, although he held a patent on the magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERottman20129_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERottman20129-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The MP 40 submachine guns are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-bolt" title="Open-bolt">open-bolt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blowback_(firearms)" title="Blowback (firearms)">blowback</a>-operated <a href="/wiki/Automatic_firearm" title="Automatic firearm">automatic</a> arms. The only mode of fire was fully automatic, but the relatively low rate of fire enabled single shots with controlled trigger pulls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeterson2011314_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeterson2011314-7">[7]</a></sup> The bolt features a telescoping return spring guide which serves as a pneumatic recoil buffer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeterson2011314_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeterson2011314-7">[7]</a></sup> The cocking handle was permanently attached to the bolt on early MP 38s, but on late production MP 38s and MP 40s, the bolt handle was made as a separate part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBishop2002259_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBishop2002259-8">[8]</a></sup> It also served as a safety by pushing the head of handle into one of two separate notches above the main opening; this action locked the bolt either in the cocked (rear) or uncocked (forward) position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBishop2002259_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBishop2002259-8">[8]</a></sup> The absence of this feature on early MP 38s resulted in field expedients such as leather harnesses with a small loop, used to hold the bolt in forward position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MP 38 receiver was made of machined steel, but this was a time-consuming and expensive process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417-10">[10]</a></sup> To save time and materials, and thus increase production, construction of the MP 40 receiver was simplified by using stamped steel and electro-spot welding as much as possible. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417-10">[10]</a></sup> The MP 38 also features longitudinal grooving on the receiver and bolt, as well as a circular opening on the magazine housing. These features were eliminated on the MP 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Quesada201417-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>One unique feature found on most MP 38 and MP 40 submachine guns was an aluminum, steel, or <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">bakelite</a> resting bar or support under the barrel. This was used to steady the weapon when firing over the side of open-top <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_personnel_carrier" title="Armored personnel carrier">armored personnel carriers</a> such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sdkfz_251" title="Sdkfz 251">Sdkfz 251</a> <a href="/wiki/Half-track" title="Half-track">half-track</a>. A handguard, made of a synthetic material derived from bakelite, was located between the magazine housing and the pistol grip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHobart197595.E2.80.9398_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobart197595.E2.80.9398-11">[11]</a></sup> The barrel lacked any form of insulation, which often resulted in burns on the supporting hand if it was incorrectly positioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHobart197595.E2.80.9398_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobart197595.E2.80.9398-11">[11]</a></sup> The MP 40 also had a forward-folding metal stock, the first for a submachine gun, resulting in a shorter overall weapon when folded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197790_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197790-12">[12]</a></sup> However, this stock design was at times insufficiently durable for hard combat use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197790_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197790-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the MP 40 was generally reliable, a major weakness was its 32-round magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733-13">[13]</a></sup> Unlike the double-column, dual-feed magazine insert found on the <a href="/wiki/Thompson_submachine_gun" title="Thompson submachine gun">Thompson M1921-28 variants</a>, the MP 40 used a double-column, single-feed insert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733-13">[13]</a></sup> The single-feed insert resulted in increased friction against the remaining cartridges moving upwards towards the feed lips, occasionally resulting in feed failures; this problem was exacerbated by the presence of dirt or other debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoggWeeks197733-13">[13]</a></sup> Another problem was that the magazine was also sometimes misused as a handhold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381-14">[14]</a></sup> This could cause the weapon to <a href="/wiki/Firearm_malfunction" title="Firearm malfunction">malfunction</a> when hand pressure on the magazine body caused the magazine lips to move out of the line of feed, since the magazine well did not keep the magazine firmly locked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381-14">[14]</a></sup> German soldiers were trained to grasp either the handhold on the underside of the weapon or the magazine housing with the supporting hand to avoid feed malfunctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunlap196680.E2.80.9381-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>At the outbreak of World War II, the majority of German soldiers carried either <a href="/wiki/Karabiner_98k" title="Karabiner 98k">Karabiner 98k</a> rifles or MP 40s, both of which were regarded as the standard weapons of choice for an infantryman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeil201565_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeil201565-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, later experience with Soviet tactics, such as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stalingrad" title="Battle of Stalingrad">Battle of Stalingrad</a> where entire Russian units armed with submachine guns outgunned their German counterparts in short range urban combat, caused a shift in tactics, and by the end of the war the MP 40 and its derivatives were being issued to entire assault platoons on a limited basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPriestleyCavatore2014105.E2.80.93108_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPriestleyCavatore2014105.E2.80.93108-16">[16]</a></sup> Starting in 1943, the German Army moved to replace both the Karabiner 98k rifle and MP 40 with the new, revolutionary <a href="/wiki/StG_44" title="StG 44">StG 44</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPriestleyCavatore2014105.E2.80.93108_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPriestleyCavatore2014105.E2.80.93108-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeil201565_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeil201565-15">[15]</a></sup> By the end of World War II (which ended in 1945), an estimated 1.1 million MP 40s had been produced of all variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWillbanks200482_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWillbanks200482-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_usage">Post-war usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-war usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During and after the end of World War II, many MP 40s were captured or surrendered (upwards of 200,000) to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> and were then redistributed to the paramilitary and irregular forces of some developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngram200175_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngram200175-18">[18]</a></sup> The Norwegian army withdrew the MP 38 in 1975, MP 40 was used for some years more, the territorials (<a href="/wiki/Home_Guard_(Norway)" title="Home Guard (Norway)">Heimevernet</a>) used it until about 1990, when it was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch_MP5" title="Heckler &amp; Koch MP5">Heckler &amp; Koch MP5</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lanchester">Lanchester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lanchester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evacuation_of_Dunkirk" title="Evacuation of Dunkirk">evacuation of Dunkirk</a> in 1940, the British Armed Forces wanted to adopt a submachine gun similar to the MP 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHobart197580.E2.80.9381_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobart197580.E2.80.9381-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> sent a request to the Ministry of Supply for 10,000 British-made copies of the MP 40, but eventually they settled on a copy of the <a href="/wiki/MP_18" title="MP 18">MP 28</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Lanchester_submachine_gun" title="Lanchester submachine gun">Lanchester</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHobart197580.E2.80.9381_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobart197580.E2.80.9381-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MP_40.2FI">MP 40/I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: MP 40/I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A modified version of the standard MP 40 (sometimes erroneously called <i>MP 40/II</i>) with a dual side-by-side magazine holder (for a theoretical ammunition total of 64 rounds), designed for special operations troops on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> to compensate for the enemies' <a href="/wiki/PPSh-41" title="PPSh-41">PPSh-41</a> larger magazine capacity. However, the design proved unsuccessful due to weight and reliability issues.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MP_41">MP 41</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP_40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: MP 41">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="764" data-file-width="2910" height="79" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg/300px-German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg/450px-German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg/600px-German_MP_wooden_stock.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>In 1943, Schmeisser designed the MP 41, which was in reality an MP 40 with a wooden rifle stock and a selector, identical to those found on the earlier <a href="/wiki/MP_18#Evolution" title="MP 18">MP 28</a> submachine gun. It saw limited service, however, and was only issued to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a> and police units in 1944. The MP 41 was also supplied to Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">Axis ally</a> Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAxworthy199142_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAxworthy199142-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>During World War II, <a href="/wiki/Resistance_during_World_War_II" title="Resistance during World War II">anti-Nazi resistance fighters</a> and Allied soldiers sometimes captured MP40s to replace or supplement their own weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakaida_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sakaida-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornish_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MP 40 was used for several decades following World War II by many countries around the world in armed conflicts. Captured MP40s found their way into guerrilla groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Viet Cong</a> or African guerrillas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKatz19889_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKatz19889-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Guns-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
