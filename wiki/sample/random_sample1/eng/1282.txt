<p>"<b>Mean Mr. Mustard</b>" is a song written by <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn1988182_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn1988182-1">[1]</a></sup> (credited to <a href="/wiki/Lennon%E2%80%93McCartney" title="Lennon–McCartney">Lennon–McCartney</a>) and performed by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a> on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road" title="Abbey Road">Abbey Road</a></i>. The <i>Abbey Road</i> version was recorded with "<a href="/wiki/Sun_King_(song)" title="Sun King (song)">Sun King</a>" in one continuous piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn1988182_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn1988182-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Mr._Mustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song was written in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>; Lennon said that it was inspired by a newspaper story about a <a href="/wiki/Miser" title="Miser">miser</a> who concealed his cash wherever he could in order to prevent people from forcing him to spend it. On reflection, Lennon did not think highly of the composition, dismissing it in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_Anthology" title="The Beatles Anthology">Anthology</a></i> as "a bit of crap I wrote in India."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurner2005195_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurner2005195-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A demo version of the song was recorded in May 1968 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinfauns_(George_Harrison)" title="Kinfauns (George Harrison)">Kinfauns</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a>'s home in <a href="/wiki/Esher" title="Esher">Esher</a>. It appears on <i><a href="/wiki/Anthology_3" title="Anthology 3">Anthology 3</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn19967_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn19967-3">[3]</a></sup> In this version, Mustard's sister is named Shirley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000337_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000337-4">[4]</a></sup> Lennon changed it to Pam when he saw the opportunity to ease the segue into the song "<a href="/wiki/Polythene_Pam" title="Polythene Pam">Polythene Pam</a>", which follows "Mean Mr. Mustard" on the album. According to Lennon, "In 'Mean Mr. Mustard', I said 'his sister Pam'—originally it was 'his sister Shirley' in the lyric. I changed it to Pam to make it sound like it had something to do with it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000337_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000337-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As eventually recorded, "Mustard" originally was to end in the chord of D major—this would have led into the next track in the climactic medley, "<a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty_(song)" title="Her Majesty (song)">Her Majesty</a>". However, since the latter song was moved to the end of the album, "Mustard" instead hard-edits into "<a href="/wiki/Polythene_Pam" title="Polythene Pam">Polythene Pam</a>", and thus the final note of "Mustard" would open "Her Majesty" as a stand-alone track at the album's conclusion. The complete version of "Mustard" (with its original clean ending) can be heard on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Beatles_Rock_Band" title="The Beatles Rock Band">The Beatles: Rock Band</a></i> video game.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel">Personnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Mr._Mustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions">Cover versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mean_Mr._Mustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cover versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booker_T._%26_the_MGs" title="Booker T. &amp; the MGs">Booker T. &amp; the MGs</a> covered the track on their 1970 album <i><a href="/wiki/McLemore_Avenue" title="McLemore Avenue">McLemore Avenue</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lazlo_Bane" title="Lazlo Bane">Lazlo Bane</a>'s cover of the song was added as the Japanes exclusive bonus track to the <i><a href="/wiki/11_Transistor" title="11 Transistor">11 Transistor</a></i> album.</li>
<li>The version in the <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band_(film)" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (film)">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i> film was performed by Mean Mr. Mustard (played by <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Howerd" title="Frankie Howerd">Frankie Howerd</a>) and his evil robot companions, the Computerettes. As with the performance of "<a href="/wiki/She%27s_Leaving_Home" title="She's Leaving Home">She's Leaving Home</a>" (also in the film), the computerised singing of the Computerettes was performed by the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>.</li>
<li>When <a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a> released <i>Abbey Road Now!</i> in 2009, part of a continuing series of CDs of Beatles albums covered track-by-track by modern artists, "Mean Mr. Mustard" was covered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornershop" title="Cornershop">Cornershop</a> alongside "<a href="/wiki/Polythene_Pam" title="Polythene Pam">Polythene Pam</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beatallica" title="Beatallica">Beatallica</a> did a version that was originally titled "Mean Mr. Mustaine" in reference to former <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a> guitarist and <a href="/wiki/Megadeth" title="Megadeth">Megadeth</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Dave_Mustaine" title="Dave Mustaine">Dave Mustaine</a>, before their label forced to use the original lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The music is based on "The Four Horsemen", a track Mustaine helped write under the title "Mechanix" and recorded the way he had composed with Megadeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
