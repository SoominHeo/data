<p>"<b>Moon River</b>" is a song composed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mancini" title="Henry Mancini">Henry Mancini</a> with lyrics by <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mercer" title="Johnny Mercer">Johnny Mercer</a>. It received an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Academy Award for Best Original Song</a> for its performance by <a href="/wiki/Audrey_Hepburn" title="Audrey Hepburn">Audrey Hepburn</a> in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_(film)" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's (film)">Breakfast at Tiffany's</a></i> (1961).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-1">[1]</a></sup> It also won Mancini the 1962 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Record_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Record of the Year">Grammy Award for Record of the Year</a> and won Mancini and Mercer the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Grammy Award for Song of the Year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The song has been covered by many other artists.</p>
<p>It became the theme song for <a href="/wiki/Andy_Williams" title="Andy Williams">Andy Williams</a>, who first recorded it in 1961 and performed it at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a> ceremony in 1962. He sang the first eight bars of the song at the beginning of each episode of <a href="/wiki/The_Andy_Williams_Show" title="The Andy Williams Show">his eponymous television show</a> and named his production company and venue in <a href="/wiki/Branson,_Missouri" title="Branson, Missouri">Branson, Missouri</a> after it. His autobiography is called <i>"Moon River" and Me</i>. Williams' version was never released as a single, but it charted as an LP track that he recorded for Columbia on a hit album of 1962. Cadence Records' president <a href="/wiki/Archie_Bleyer" title="Archie Bleyer">Archie Bleyer</a> disliked Williams' version, as Bleyer believed it had little or no appeal to teenagers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Forty years later in 2002, a 74-year-old Williams sang the song at the conclusion of the live telecast of the NBC 75th Anniversary Special to a standing ovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song's success was responsible for relaunching Mercer's career as a songwriter, which had stalled in the mid-1950s because <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> had replaced <a href="/wiki/Jazz_standard" title="Jazz standard">jazz standards</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a> of the time. The song's popularity is such that it has been used as a test sample in a study on people's memories of popular songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartlett_551.E2.80.93560_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bartlett_551.E2.80.93560-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Comments about the lyrics have noted that they are particularly reminiscent of Mercer's youth in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> and his longing to expand his horizons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wright_(journalist)" title="Robert Wright (journalist)">Robert Wright</a> wrote in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" title="The Atlantic Monthly">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i>, "This is a love sung to wanderlust. Or a romantic song in which the romantic partner is the idea of romance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An inlet near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, Johnny Mercer's hometown, was named Moon River in honor of him and this song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartlett_551.E2.80.93560_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bartlett_551.E2.80.93560-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Versions">Versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original">Original</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Original">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mercer and Mancini wrote the song for Audrey Hepburn to fit her vocal range. The lyrics, written by Mercer, are reminiscent of his childhood in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, including its waterways. As a child, he had picked <a href="/wiki/Huckleberry" title="Huckleberry">huckleberries</a> in summer, and connected them with a carefree childhood and Mark Twain's <a href="/wiki/Huckleberry_Finn" title="Huckleberry Finn">Huckleberry Finn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-times-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cryer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cryer-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although an instrumental version is played over the film's opening titles, the lyrics are first heard in a scene where Paul "Fred" Varjak (<a href="/wiki/George_Peppard" title="George Peppard">George Peppard</a>) discovers Holly Golightly (Hepburn) singing them, accompanied by her guitar, on the fire escape outside their apartments.</p>
<p>There was an eruption of behind-the-scenes consternation when a <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> executive, Martin Rackin, suggested removing the song from the film after a tepid Los Angeles preview. Hepburn's reaction was described by Mancini and others in degrees varying from her saying, "over my dead body", to her using somewhat more colorful language to make the same point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Hepburn's version was uncredited in the original <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s:_Music_from_the_Motion_Picture" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's: Music from the Motion Picture">movie soundtrack</a>.</p>
<p>An album version recorded by Mancini and his chorus was released as a single and became a number 11 hit in December 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to unpublished charts in <i>Billboard</i>, Joel Whitburn's <i>Top Adult (Contemporary) Songs</i> variously reported the song as a #3 or #1 <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a> hit. Mancini's original version was also featured in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Born_on_the_Fourth_of_July_(film)" title="Born on the Fourth of July (film)">Born on the Fourth of July</a></i> (1989). In 1993, following Hepburn's death, her version was released on an album titled <i>Music from the Films of Audrey Hepburn</i>. In 2004, Hepburn's version finished at #4 on <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Songs" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Songs">AFI's 100 Years...100 Songs</a> survey of top tunes in American cinema.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Moon River" was a hit single for <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Butler" title="Jerry Butler">Jerry Butler</a> in late 1961; it reached number 11 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart in December,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> two weeks before Mancini's recording reached the same spot. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danny_Williams_(musician)" title="Danny Williams (musician)">Danny Williams</a> had a hit version of the song that reached number one in the UK in the final week of 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-1">[1]</a></sup> Although Andy Williams never released the song as a single, his LP <i><a href="/wiki/Moon_River_and_Other_Great_Movie_Themes" title="Moon River and Other Great Movie Themes">Moon River and Other Great Movie Themes</a></i> (1962), was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_record" title="Gold record">certified gold</a> in 1963 for sales grossing over $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Finn" title="Neil Finn">Neil Finn</a> and <a performance at the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House" title="Sydney Opera House">Sydney Opera House Concert Hall</a> was recorded for the live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Goin%27_Your_Way" title="Goin' Your Way">Goin' Your Way</a></i>, released the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_Kelly_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paul_Kelly-19">[19]</a></sup> The title of the album comes from a 6</a>, episode 13, "In Care Of" (2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A version of the song was featured in <a href="/wiki/Asif_Kapadia" title="Asif
