<p>The <b>1831 general election in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></b> saw a landslide win by supporters of <a href="/wiki/Electoral_reform" title="Electoral reform">electoral reform</a>, which was the major election issue. As a result, it was the last unreformed election, as the Parliament which resulted ensured the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a>. Polling was held from 28 April to 1 June 1831. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whig_(British_political_party)" title="Whig (British political party)">Whigs</a> won a majority of 136 over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tory_(British_political_party)" title="Tory (British political party)">Tories</a>, which was as near to a landslide as the unreformed electoral system could deliver. As the Government obtained a dissolution of Parliament once the new electoral system had been enacted, the resulting Parliament was a short one and there was <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1832" title="United Kingdom general election, 1832">another election the following year</a>. The election was the first since <a href="/wiki/British_general_election,_1715" title="British general election, 1715">1715</a> to see a victory by a party previously in minority.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_situation">Political situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_general_election,_1831&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ninth United Kingdom Parliament, which had been elected in 1830, did not have a stable majority for the Tory government of the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a>: the best estimate is that it had 310 supporters, 225 opponents and 121 doubtful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1830_Parliament_partisan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-1830_Parliament_partisan-1">[1]</a></sup> After a series of defeats, on 15 November 1830 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Parnell,_1st_Baron_Congleton" title="Henry Parnell, 1st Baron Congleton">Henry Parnell</a>'s motion for an inquiry into the <a href="/wiki/Civil_list" title="Civil list">civil list</a> was carried by 233 to 204; this defeat surprised Wellington and his cabinet and forced their resignation. Wellington went into opposition, with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Peel" title="Robert Peel">Sir Robert Peel</a> as the Tory <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Leader of the Opposition (United Kingdom)">Leader of the Opposition in the House of Commons</a>. A Whig government under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">Earl Grey</a> was appointed on 22 November 1830, the first predominantly Whig administration since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_all_the_Talents" title="Ministry of all the Talents">Ministry of all the Talents</a> in 1806-1807. The government <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_House_of_Commons" title="Leader of the House of Commons">Leader of the House of Commons</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Spencer,_3rd_Earl_Spencer" title="John Spencer, 3rd Earl Spencer">Viscount Althorp</a>, who also served as <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>.</p>
<p>Grey was determined to bring in reform to the traditional electoral system, which had been discussed for many decades. With aristocratic colleagues he produced a surprisingly bold scheme of reform; the second reading of the Reform Bill was carried by only one vote (302-301) on 22 March 1831. The Tory opposition was determined to stop the scheme going ahead, and when the Bill went into committee on 18 April, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Gascoyne" title="Isaac Gascoyne">General Gascoyne</a> moved an amendment which required that the total number of MPs representing England and Wales ought not to be reduced. This proposal was a skilfully drafted '<a href="/wiki/Wrecking_amendment" title="Wrecking amendment">wrecking amendment</a>' and when it was passed by 299-291 on 19 April, the Grey government knew it would not get its legislation. In truth Grey had been ready to ask for a dissolution immediately the Committee stage began, and King <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">William IV</a> reluctantly agreed; the King dissolved Parliament in person (amid a great political tumult) on 22 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Story_of_the_1830_Parliament_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Story_of_the_1830_Parliament-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new Parliament was summoned to meet on 14 June 1831, for a maximum seven-year term from that date.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dates_of_election">Dates of election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_general_election,_1831&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dates of election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At this period there was not one election day. After receiving a writ (a royal command) for the election to be held, the local <a href="/wiki/Returning_officer" title="Returning officer">returning officer</a> fixed the election timetable for the particular constituency or constituencies he was concerned with. Polling in seats with contested elections could continue for many days.</p>
<p>The general election took place between the first contest on 28 April and the last contest on 1 June 1831.</p>
