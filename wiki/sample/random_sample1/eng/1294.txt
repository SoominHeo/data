<p><b>Victor Santiago, Jr.</b> (born September 6, 1977), best known by his <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> <b>N.O.R.E.</b>, formerly <b>Noreaga</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reggaeton" title="Reggaeton">reggaeton</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recording_artist" title="Recording artist">recording artist</a>. Santiago first rose to prominence as one half of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Capone-N-Noreaga" title="Capone-N-Noreaga">Capone-N-Noreaga</a> (C-N-N), alongside fellow Queens-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Capone_(rapper)" title="Capone (rapper)">Capone</a>. He would also have success as a solo artist with the singles "<a href="/wiki/Superthug" title="Superthug">Superthug</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Banned_from_T.V." title="Banned from T.V.">Banned from T.V.</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Nothin%27_(song)" title="Nothin' (song)">Nothin'</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Oye_Mi_Canto" title="Oye Mi Canto">Oye Mi Canto</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mas_Maiz" title="Mas Maiz">Mas Maiz</a>".</p>
<p>Santiago has released music under various record labels, including <a href="/wiki/Penalty_Recordings" title="Penalty Recordings">Penalty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roc-La-Familia" title="Roc-La-Familia">Roc-La-Familia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a>. In June 2011, it was announced Santiago partnered with fellow American rapper <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>' <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(record_label)" title="Conglomerate (record label)">Conglomerate</a> label, where he released his 2013 album <i><a href="/wiki/Student_of_the_Game" title="Student of the Game">Student of the Game</a></i>, issued under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E1_Music" title="E1 Music">E1 Music</a>. Santiago is also <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> (CEO) of his own imprint, Militainment Business.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N.O.R.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995.E2.80.9397:_Career_beginnings_with_Capone">1995–97: Career beginnings with Capone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N.O.R.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1995–97: Career beginnings with Capone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Capone-N-Noreaga" title="Capone-N-Noreaga">Capone-N-Noreaga</a></div>
<p>Santiago's career began to take off in 1995, while under the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> Noreaga (after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panamanian" title="Panamanian">Panamanian</a> dictator <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Noriega" title="Manuel Noriega">Manuel Noriega</a>), as part of the duo <a href="/wiki/Capone-N-Noreaga" title="Capone-N-Noreaga">Capone-N-Noreaga</a> (C-N-N), alongside friend and fellow <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>-based rapper <a href="/wiki/Capone_(rapper)" title="Capone (rapper)">Capone</a> (whom he met while serving time in prison). They signed to <a href="/wiki/Penalty_Recordings" title="Penalty Recordings">Penalty Recordings</a> in 1996, and released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_Report" title="The War Report">The War Report</a></i>, in 1997. <i>The War Report</i> was a commercial and critical success which made the duo well known among hip hop audiences. The album received praise for using the "traditional grimey boom-bap" sound of classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Hip-Hop" title="New York Hip-Hop">New York City hip hop</a>, which at that time was falling out in favor of a more futuristic sound. The album peaked at #21 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, and the singles "Illegal Life", "Closer", and "T.O.N.Y. (Top of New York)" all charted on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_and_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles and Tracks">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles and Tracks</a> chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1997.E2.80.932000:_Solo_career_and_debut_album">1997–2000: Solo career and debut album</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N.O.R.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1997–2000: Solo career and debut album">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the release of the album, Capone was again sentenced to a jail term, which left Noreaga to take on a solo career and make an album on his own. Noreaga's solo debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E._(album)" title="N.O.R.E. (album)">N.O.R.E</a></i>, was released in 1998. The title is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Fugitive" title="Fugitive">Niggaz on (the) Run</a> <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">Eatin'</a>". The album became even more successful than the duo's debut, charting at #3 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 and being <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA). The album features <a href="/wiki/Guest_appearance" title="Guest appearance">guest appearances</a> from fellow NYC rappers <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Pun" title="Big Pun">Big Pun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>. It also features in many cases a different production style than <i>The War Report</i>, including the modern futuristic sound, with tracks produced by then-up-and-coming producers <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swizz_Beatz" title="Swizz Beatz">Swizz Beatz</a>. The Neptunes produced the hit "<a href="/wiki/Superthug" title="Superthug">Superthug</a>", which reached #36 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and #1 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks">Hot Rap Tracks</a> chart. The song, along with <a href="/wiki/Mase" title="Mase">Mase</a>'s hit "Lookin' At Me", was one of the Neptunes' first high-profile productions and made them one of the most sought after production teams in hip hop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It also started their relationship with Noreaga as they would continue to produce more hits for him in the future.</p>
<p>In 1999, Noreaga would go on to release his second solo album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melvin_Flynt_-_Da_Hustler" title="Melvin Flynt - Da Hustler">Melvin Flynt - Da Hustler</a></i>, which was a moderate success, reaching the top 10 of the <i>Billboard</i> 200 and becoming <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified gold</a> by the RIAA. The album's most successful single (produced by The Neptunes) was "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oh_No_(Noreaga_song)" title="Oh No (Noreaga song)">Oh No"</a>, which reached #49 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_and_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles and Tracks">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles and Tracks</a> chart. Capone was released from prison in 1999. The two subsequently began recording music together again and released their second C-N-N album in the year 2000. The album, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Reunion_(album)" title="The Reunion (album)">The Reunion</a></i>, was not well received critically and failed to match the commercial success of the duo's debut or Noreaga's solo albums. <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> reviewer Matt Conaway summed up the album by stating "With <i>The Reunion</i>, Capone-N-Noreaga take a step backward." The group would then go on a hiatus and not release another album until 2009.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001.E2.80.9307:_Name_change_and_reggaeton_era">2001–07: Name change and reggaeton era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N.O.R.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2001–07: Name change and reggaeton era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Noreaga continued to pursue his career as a solo artist. He officially changed his stage name to N.O.R.E.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, and after some delays, his third solo studio album <i><a href="/wiki/God%27s_Favorite_(album)" title="God's Favorite (album)">God's Favorite</a></i>, was released in 2002. The album peaked at #3 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 and was later certified platinum. It contained the major hit "<a href="/wiki/Nothin%27_(song)" title="Nothin' (song)">Nothin'</a>" (produced by The Neptunes), which reached #10 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100. It became N.O.R.E.'s highest charting hit.</p>
<p>Following <i>God's Favorite</i>, N.O.R.E. changed his musical focus. He began recording <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish-language</a> songs to reflect his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" title="Puerto Rican people">Puerto Rican</a> background and made <a href="/wiki/Reggaeton" title="Reggaeton">reggaeton</a> music, rather than traditional <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>. The reggaeton single "<a href="/wiki/Oye_Mi_Canto" title="Oye Mi Canto">Oye Mi Canto</a>", was released in 2004 and became a major hit, peaking at #12 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100. The song was originally supposed to be a single from an upcoming album titled <i>1 Fan a Day</i>, but the album was never released. Instead, N.O.R.E. released a reggaeton/hip hop album in 2006, titled <i><a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E._y_la_Familia...Ya_T%C3%BA_Sabe" title="N.O.R.E. y la Familia...Ya Tú Sabe">N.O.R.E. y la Familia...Ya Tú Sabe</a></i>, which included the lead singles "Oye Mi Canto" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mas_Maiz" title="Mas Maiz">Mas Maiz</a>". The album, released under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Roc-La-Familia" title="Roc-La-Familia">Roc-La-Familia</a>, has both English and Spanish language tracks. It did not chart nearly as well as any of N.O.R.E.'s previous solo albums, as it only reached #82 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200.</p>
<p>N.O.R.E. went on to release two more solo albums. The first, his fifth album <i><a href="/wiki/Noreality" title="Noreality">Noreality</a></i>, was released in 2007 and supported by the single "Set It Off", featuring vocals and production from Swizz Beatz. N.O.R.E. released his CD/DVD, <i>Noreality</i>, in September via a joint venture with his own Thugged Out Militainment label and <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a>. The album features guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Three_6_Mafia" title="Three 6 Mafia">Three 6 Mafia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharrell" title="Pharrell">Pharrell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>'s Prodigy, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tru_Life" title="Tru Life">Tru Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Banner" title="David Banner">David Banner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a> and Capone. Swizz Beatz is among the producers. The DVD, which was available in limited quantities, is based on the rapper's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second album <i>S.O.R.E.</i>, was controversially released in 2009. N.O.R.E. would go on to denounce <i>S.O.R.E.</i>, calling it an album he had nothing to do with<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and asked fans not to purchase it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The album is a reference to his <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> and his <a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E._(album)" title="N.O.R.E. (album)">first album</a> which is a <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a> for "Niggaz on (the) Run Eatin'", which makes "S.O.R.E.", "Still on (the) Run Eatin'". The term was first used in late 2007, when N.O.R.E. released a song entitled "Still on the Run Eatin'", featuring <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>. N.O.R.E. said in a video blog that he didn't approve of this album. He also stated the album's <a href="/wiki/Cover_art" title="Cover art">artwork</a> was of him at 310 pounds, adding he had lost 62 pounds since then. He also went on to mention five songs on the album were actually handed in, while the rest were pulled off the internet, some of which coming from 2007, probably stemming from label financial issues. He later stated the official album he's working on is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N.O.R.E._Pt._2:_Born_Again" title="N.O.R.E. Pt. 2: Born Again">N.O.R.E. Pt. 2: Born Again</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009.E2.80.93present:_Student_of_the_Game">2009–present: <i>Student of the Game</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N.O.R.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2009–present: Student of the Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, N.O.R.E. collaborated with Capone once again, to release their third studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Channel_10_(album)" title="Channel 10 (album)">Channel 10</a></i>, which charted at #136 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200. In 2010, a fourth Capone-N-Noreaga album titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_War_Report_2" title="The War Report 2">The War Report 2</a></i>, was released. Its lead single was "Hood Pride", featuring <a href="/wiki/Faith_Evans" title="Faith Evans">Faith Evans</a>. In June 2011, fellow American rapper <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, with whom N.O.R.E. has collaborated with several times in the past, stated on his Twitter account that he had officially signed N.O.R.E. to his newly founded record label <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conglomerate_Records" title="Conglomerate Records">Conglomerate Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twitter1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-twitter1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 2009 and 2011, N.O.R.E. teamed up with <a href="/wiki/DJ_EFN" title="DJ EFN">DJ EFN</a> to host a satellite radio show for Sirius XM called Militainment Crazy Raw Radio, a title that gave equal weight to N.O.R.E.'s Militainment brand, to EFN's Crazy Hood, and to 66 Raw, the channel on Sirius XM which carried the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 2013, Santiago stated that he would yet again change his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moniker" title="Moniker">moniker</a>, this time to P.A.P.I. (Acronym for <b>P</b>ower <b>A</b>lways <b>P</b>roves <b>I</b>ntelligence),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p.a.p.i._10-0"><a href="#cite_note-p.a.p.i.-10">[10]</a></sup> which stirred mixed reactions from his fans. On April 16, 2013 he released <i><a href="/wiki/Student_of_the_Game" title="Student of the Game">Student of the Game</a></i>, and shortly after announced a sequel would be released as his next album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 27, 2013, he told MTV that his next album, <i>Melvin Flynt II: Da Final Hustle</i> will be his final album, as he will stop touring and releasing solo albums to focus on being a music executive and CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xxlmagii_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmagii-12">[12]</a></sup> On October 30, 2015, Santiago tweeted: "New and last album in July!!!",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> followed by "<i>Melvin Flynt 2</i>? Or <i>N.O.R.E. 2</i>?".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xxlmagii_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmagii-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
