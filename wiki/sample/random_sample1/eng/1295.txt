<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">NASA Clean Air Study</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>NASA Clean Air Study</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> has been led by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>) in association with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associated_Landscape_Contractors_of_America" title="Associated Landscape Contractors of America">Associated Landscape Contractors of America</a> (ALCA). Its results suggest that certain common indoor plants may provide a natural way of removing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> agents such as <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> from the air, helping neutralize the effects of <a href="/wiki/Sick_building_syndrome" title="Sick building syndrome">sick building syndrome</a>.</p>
<p>The first list of <b>air-filtering plants</b> was compiled by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> as part of a clean air study published in 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pot_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pot-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which researched ways to clean air in <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space stations</a>. As well as absorbing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and releasing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, as all <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> do, these plants also eliminate significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a>. The second and third lists are from <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wolverton" title="Bill Wolverton">B. C. Wolverton's</a> book<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolverton1996_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolverton1996-5">[5]</a></sup> and paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolverton1993_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolverton1993-6">[6]</a></sup> and focus on removal of specific chemicals.</p>
<p>NASA researchers suggest efficient air cleaning is accomplished with at least one plant per 100 square feet of home or office space. Other research has shown that micro-organisms in the potting mix (soil) of a potted plant remove benzene from the air, and that some plant species also contribute to removing benzene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foliage">Foliage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_Clean_Air_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foliage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the plants on the list evolved in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> environments. Due to their ability to flourish on reduced <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, their leaf composition allows them to <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesize</a> well in household light.</p>
