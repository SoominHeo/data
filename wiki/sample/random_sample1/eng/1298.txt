<p><b>NBC News</b> is a division of the American broadcast network <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>. The division operates under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBCUniversal_News_Group" title="NBCUniversal News Group">NBCUniversal News Group</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a>, in turn a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>. The group's various operations report to the president of NBC News, <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Turness" title="Deborah Turness">Deborah Turness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turness_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turness-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>NBC News aired the first news program in American broadcast television history on February 21, 1940. The group's broadcasts are produced and aired from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30_Rockefeller_Center" title="30 Rockefeller Center">30 Rockefeller Center</a>, NBC's headquarters in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>
<p>The division presides over America's number-one-rated newscast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_Nightly_News" title="NBC Nightly News">NBC Nightly News</a></i>, and the longest-running television series in American history, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meet_The_Press" title="Meet The Press">Meet The Press</a></i>. NBC News also offers 70 years of rare historic footage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> from the NBCUniversal Archives online.</p>
<p>NBC News operates a 24-hour cable news network known as <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a>, which includes the organization's flagship daytime news operation, <a href="/wiki/MSNBC_Live" title="MSNBC Live">MSNBC Live</a>. The cable network shares staff and editorial control with NBC News.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caravan_era">Caravan era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caravan era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first American television newscast in history was made by NBC News on February 21, 1940, anchored by <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Thomas" title="Lowell Thomas">Lowell Thomas</a> and airing weeknights at 6:45 p.m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In June 1940, NBC, through its flagship station in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, W2XBS (renamed commercial WNBT in 1941, now <a href="/wiki/WNBC" title="WNBC">WNBC</a>) operating on channel one, televised 30¼ hours of coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_National_Convention" title="Republican National Convention">Republican National Convention</a> live and direct from <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>. The station used a series of relays from Philadelphia to New York and on to upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a>, for rebroadcast on W2XB in <a href="/wiki/Schenectady,_New_York" title="Schenectady, New York">Schenectady</a> (now <a href="/wiki/WRGB" title="WRGB">WRGB</a>), making this among the first "network" programs of NBC Television. Due to wartime restrictions, there were no live telecasts of the 1944 conventions, although films of the events were reportedly shown over WNBT the next day.</p>
<p>In 1948, NBC teamed up with <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i> magazine to provide election night coverage of President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a>'s surprising victory over New York governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._Dewey" title="Thomas E. Dewey">Thomas E. Dewey</a>. The television audience was small, but NBC's share in New York was double that of any other outlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The following year, the <i><a href="/wiki/Camel_News_Caravan" title="Camel News Caravan">Camel News Caravan</a></i>, anchored by <a href="/wiki/John_Cameron_Swayze" title="John Cameron Swayze">John Cameron Swayze</a>, debuted on NBC. Lacking the graphics and technology of later years, it nonetheless contained many of the elements of modern newscasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup> NBC hired its own film crews and in the program's early years, it dominated CBS's competing program, which did not hire its own film crews until 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup> (by contrast, CBS spent lavishly on <a href="/wiki/Edward_R._Murrow" title="Edward R. Murrow">Edward R. Murrow</a>'s weekly series, <i><a href="/wiki/See_It_Now" title="See It Now">See It Now</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup>). In 1950, <a href="/wiki/David_Brinkley" title="David Brinkley">David Brinkley</a> began serving as the program's <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> correspondent, but attracted little attention outside the network until paired with <a href="/wiki/Chet_Huntley" title="Chet Huntley">Chet Huntley</a> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitworth_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitworth-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1955, the <i>Camel News Caravan</i> fell behind <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_Evening_News" title="CBS Evening News">Douglas Edwards with the News</a></i>, and Swayze lost the already tepid support of NBC executives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup> The following year, NBC replaced the program with the <i><a href="/wiki/Huntley-Brinkley_Report" title="Huntley-Brinkley Report">Huntley-Brinkley Report</a></i>.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1951, NBC News was managed by director of news Bill McAndrew, who reported to vice president of news and public affairs J. Davidson Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Huntley-Brinkley_era">Huntley-Brinkley era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Huntley-Brinkley era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="918" data-file-width="713" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG/180px-NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG/270px-NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG/360px-NBC_News_promotional_photo_1961.JPG 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

NBC News had close to 700 correspondents and cameramen in 1961 who were stationed throughout the world. Film was received in the United States by plane or by the jointly operated NBC-<a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> transatlantic film cable.</div>

</div>
<p>Television assumed an increasingly prominent role in American family life in the late 1950s, and NBC News was called television's "champion of news coverage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> NBC president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Kintner" title="Robert Kintner">Robert Kintner</a> provided the news division with ample amounts of both financial resources and air time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1956, the network paired anchors <a href="/wiki/Chet_Huntley" title="Chet Huntley">Chet Huntley</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Brinkley" title="David Brinkley">David Brinkley</a> and the two became celebrities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitworth_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitworth-8">[8]</a></sup> supported by reporters including <a href="/wiki/John_Chancellor" title="John Chancellor">John Chancellor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_McGee_(journalism)" title="Frank McGee (journalism)">Frank McGee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Newman" title="Edwin Newman">Edwin Newman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sander_Vanocur" title="Sander Vanocur">Sander Vanocur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Dickerson" title="Nancy Dickerson">Nancy Dickerson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Pettit" title="Tom Pettit">Tom Pettit</a>, and Ray Scherer.</p>
<p>Created by producer <a href="/wiki/Reuven_Frank" title="Reuven Frank">Reuven Frank</a>, NBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Huntley-Brinkley_Report" title="Huntley-Brinkley Report">Huntley-Brinkley Report</a></i> had its debut on October 29, 1956. During much of its 14-year run, it exceeded the viewership levels of its CBS News competition, anchored initially by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Edwards" title="Douglas Edwards">Douglas Edwards</a> and, beginning in April 1962, by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">Walter Cronkite</a>.</p>
<p>NBC's vice president of news and public affairs, J. Davidson Taylor, was a Southerner who, with producer Reuven Frank, was determined that NBC would lead television's coverage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RobertsAndKlibanoff_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsAndKlibanoff-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1955, NBC provided national coverage of Rev. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>'s leadership of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montgomery_Bus_Boycott" title="Montgomery Bus Boycott">bus boycott</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery, Alabama</a>, airing reports from Frank McGee, then news director of NBC's Montgomery affiliate <a href="/wiki/WSFA" title="WSFA">WSFA-TV</a>, who would later join the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halberstam_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halberstam-12">[12]</a></sup> A year later, John Chancellor's coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock_Nine" title="Little Rock Nine">admission of black students</a> to <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock_Central_High_School" title="Little Rock Central High School">Central High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a> was the first occasion when the key news story came from television rather than print<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halberstam_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Halberstam-12">[12]</a></sup> and prompted a prominent U.S. senator to observe later, "When I think of Little Rock, I think of John Chancellor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-9">[9]</a></sup> Other reporters who covered the movement for the network included Sander Vanocur, Herbert Kaplow, Charles Quinn, and Richard Valeriani,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RobertsAndKlibanoff_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsAndKlibanoff-11">[11]</a></sup> who was hit with an ax handle at a demonstration in <a href="/wiki/Marion,_Alabama" title="Marion, Alabama">Marion, Alabama</a> in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Walter Cronkite's enthusiasm for the space race eventually won the anchorman viewers for CBS, NBC News, with the work of correspondents such as Frank McGee, Roy Neal, <a href="/wiki/Jay_Barbree" title="Jay Barbree">Jay Barbree</a>, and Peter Hackes, also provided ample coverage of American manned space missions in the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Project Gemini</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Project Apollo</a> programs. In an era when space missions rated continuous coverage, NBC configured its largest studio, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Radio_City_Studios" title="NBC Radio City Studios">Studio 8H</a>, for space coverage. It utilized models and mockups of rockets and spacecraft, maps of the earth and moon to show orbital trackage, and stages on which animated figures created by puppeteer <a href="/wiki/Bil_Baird" title="Bil Baird">Bil Baird</a> were used to depict movements of astronauts before on-board spacecraft television cameras were feasible. (Studio 8H had been home to the <a href="/wiki/NBC_Symphony_Orchestra" title="NBC Symphony Orchestra">NBC Symphony Orchestra</a> and is now the home of <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a>.</i>) NBC's coverage of the first moon landing in 1969 earned the network an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1950s, Kintner reorganized the chain of command at the network, making Bill McAndrew president of NBC News, reporting directly to Kintner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-9">[9]</a></sup> McAndrew served in that position until his death in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-9">[9]</a></sup> McAndrew was succeeded by his executive vice president, producer Reuven Frank, who held the position until 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 22, 1963, NBC interrupted various programs on its affiliate stations at 1:45 p.m. to announce that <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">President John F. Kennedy had been shot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Eight minutes later, at 1:53:12 p.m., NBC broke into programming with a network bumper slide and <a href="/wiki/Chet_Huntley" title="Chet Huntley">Chet Huntley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Ryan_(journalist)" title="Bill Ryan (journalist)">Bill Ryan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_McGee_(journalist)" title="Frank McGee (journalist)">Frank McGee</a> informing the viewers what was going on as it happened; but since a camera was not in service, the reports were audio only. However, NBC did not begin broadcasting over the air until 1:57 p.m. ET. About 40 minutes later, after word came that JFK was pronounced dead, NBC suspended regular programming and carried 71 hours of uninterrupted news coverage of the assassination and the <a href="/wiki/State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="State funeral of John F. Kennedy">funeral</a> of the president—including the only live broadcast of the fatal shooting of Kennedy's assassin, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Harvey_Oswald" title="Lee Harvey Oswald">Lee Harvey Oswald</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ruby" title="Jack Ruby">Jack Ruby</a> as Oswald was being led in handcuffs by law-enforcement officials through the basement of Dallas Police Headquarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NBC_Nightly_News_era"><i>NBC Nightly News</i> era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NBC Nightly News era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NBC's ratings lead began to slip toward the end of the 1960s and fell sharply when Chet Huntley retired in 1970 (Huntley died of cancer in 1974). The loss of Huntley, along with a reluctance by RCA to fund NBC News at a similar level as CBS was funding its news division, left NBC News in the doldrums. NBC's primary news show gained its present title, <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_Nightly_News" title="NBC Nightly News">NBC Nightly News</a></i>, on August 3, 1970.</p>
<p>The network tried a platoon of anchors (Brinkley, McGee, and <a href="/wiki/John_Chancellor" title="John Chancellor">John Chancellor</a>) during the early months of <i>Nightly News</i>. Despite the efforts of the network's eventual lead anchor, the articulate, even-toned Chancellor, and an occasional first-place finish in the <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">Nielsens</a>, <i>Nightly News</i> in the 1970s was primarily a strong second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matusow_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Matusow-7">[7]</a></sup> By the end of the decade, NBC had to contend not only with a powerful CBS but also a surging <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Roone_Arledge" title="Roone Arledge">Roone Arledge</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tom_Brokaw" title="Tom Brokaw">Tom Brokaw</a> became sole anchor in 1983, after co-anchoring with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Mudd" title="Roger Mudd">Roger Mudd</a> for a year, and began leading NBC's efforts. In 1986 and 1987, NBC won the top spot in the <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">Nielsens</a> for the first time in years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> only to fall back when Nielsen's ratings methodology changed. In late 1996, <i>Nightly News</i> again moved into first place,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> a spot it has held onto in most of the succeeding years. <a href="/wiki/Brian_Williams" title="Brian Williams">Brian Williams</a> assumed primary anchor duties when Brokaw retired in December 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In February 2015, NBC suspended Williams for six months for telling an inaccurate story about his experience in the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-19">[19]</a></sup> He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lester_Holt" title="Lester Holt">Lester Holt</a> on an interim basis. On June 18, 2015, it was announced that Holt would become the permanent anchor and Williams would be moved to MSNBC as an anchor of breaking news and special reports beginning in August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Dateline_NBC" title="Dateline NBC">Dateline NBC</a></i> broadcast an investigative report about the safety of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> (GM) trucks. GM discovered the "actual footage" utilized in the broadcast had been rigged by the inclusion of explosive incendiaries attached to the gas tanks and the use of improper sealants for those tanks. GM subsequently filed an anti-defamation lawsuit against NBC, which publicly admitted the results of the tests were rigged and settled the lawsuit with GM on the very same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_news">Company news</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Company news">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 22, 2007, <i>Nightly News</i> moved into its new high definition studios, at Studio 3C at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Studios_(New_York)" title="NBC Studios (New York)">NBC Studios</a> in 30 Rockefeller Plaza in New York City. The network's 24-hour cable network, <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a>, joined the network in New York on that day as well. The new studios/headquarters for NBC News and MSNBC are now located in one area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2010">financial crisis of 2007–2008</a>, NBC News was urged to save $500 million by NBC Universal. On that occasion, NBC News laid off several of its in-house reporters such as <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Corke" title="Kevin Corke">Kevin Corke</a>, Jeannie Ohm and Don Teague. This was the largest layoff in NBC News history.</p>
<p>After the sudden death of the influential moderator <a href="/wiki/Tim_Russert" title="Tim Russert">Tim Russert</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Meet_the_Press" title="Meet the Press">Meet the Press</a></i> in June 2008, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Brokaw" title="Tom Brokaw">Tom Brokaw</a> took over as an interim host; and on December 14, 2008, <a href="/wiki/David_Gregory_(journalist)" title="David Gregory (journalist)">David Gregory</a> became the new moderator of the show until August 14, 2014, when NBC announced that NBC News Political Director <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Todd" title="Chuck Todd">Chuck Todd</a> would take over as the 12th moderator of <a href="/wiki/Meet_the_Press" title="Meet the Press">Meet the Press</a> starting September 7, 2014. David Gregory's last broadcast was August 10, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2009, NBC had established leadership in network news, airing the highest-rated morning, evening, and Sunday interview news programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Its ability to share costs with MSNBC and share in the cable network's advertising and subscriber revenue made it far more profitable than its network rivals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia_Tech_massacre">Virginia Tech massacre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Virginia Tech massacre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_massacre" title="Virginia Tech massacre">Virginia Tech massacre</a></div>
<p>On April 16, 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cho_Seung-hui" title="Cho Seung-hui">Cho Seung-hui</a>, gunman in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_massacre" title="Virginia Tech massacre">Virginia Tech massacre</a>, took time between the two shooting episodes to prepare and mail a large multimedia package to NBC News in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> containing messages about his anger at the wealthy and alluding to the slaughter that was about to take place. Although the package was sent overnight mail, it was not received until 11 a.m. on April 18 because of Cho's confusion over the zip code of NBC's headquarters at <a href="/wiki/30_Rockefeller_Plaza" title="30 Rockefeller Plaza">30 Rockefeller Plaza</a>. The package contained a DVD showing video clips of Cho speaking and more than two dozen photos of Cho, including 11 of him thrusting pistols at the camera. A postal worker delivering the parcel to the network's Rockefeller Center offices recognized the sender and alerted NBC security personnel. They immediately reported the package to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a>. Meanwhile, NBC made copies of the contents and aired carefully edited pieces on its evening news and cable programs. Snippets from the package, including still photos, videos and voice narration, were also made available to competing news outlets who agreed to credit the network as the source. Then-NBC News president <a href="/wiki/Steve_Capus" title="Steve Capus">Steve Capus</a> defended use of the material, but the frequency of its broadcast was cut dramatically.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shooting_of_Trayvon_Martin">Shooting of Trayvon Martin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shooting of Trayvon Martin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright">

<p>On March 27, 2012, NBC News broadcast an edited segment from a 911 call placed by <a href="/wiki/George_Zimmerman" title="George Zimmerman">George Zimmerman</a> before he <a href="/wiki/Shooting_of_Trayvon_Martin" title="Shooting of Trayvon Martin">shot Trayvon Martin</a>. The editing made it appear that Zimmerman volunteered that Martin was black, rather than merely responding to the dispatcher's inquiry, which would support a view that the shooting was racially motivated. A media watchdog organization accused NBC News of engaging in "an all-out falsehood." While NBC News initially declined to comment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> the news agency did issue an apology to viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zimmerman_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerman-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> called the statement "skimpy on the details on just how the mistake unfolded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zimmerman_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerman-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NBC_News_team_kidnapping">NBC News team kidnapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: NBC News team kidnapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NBC_News_team_kidnapping_in_Syria" title="NBC News team kidnapping in Syria">NBC News team kidnapping in Syria</a></div>
<p>On 13 December 2012, NBC News reporter <a href="/wiki/Richard_Engel" title="Richard Engel">Richard Engel</a> and his five crew members, Aziz Akyavaş, Ghazi Balkiz, John Kooistra, Ian Rivers and Ammar Cheikh Omar, were abducted in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>. Having escaped after five days in captivity, Engel said he believed that a <a href="/wiki/Shabiha" title="Shabiha">Shabiha</a> group loyal to <a href="/wiki/Bashar_al-Assad" title="Bashar al-Assad">al-Assad</a> was behind the abduction, and that the crew was freed by the <a href="/wiki/Ahrar_al-Sham" title="Ahrar al-Sham">Ahrar al-Sham</a> group five days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Engel's account was however challenged from early on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In April 2015, NBC had to revise the kidnapping account, following further investigations by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, which suggested that the NBC team "was almost certainly taken by a Sunni criminal element affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Free_Syrian_Army" title="Free Syrian Army">Free Syrian Army</a>," rather than by a loyalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a> group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidents">Presidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Presidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nine men have served as president of NBC News during its history: <a href="/wiki/Reuven_Frank" title="Reuven Frank">Reuven Frank</a> (1968–73, 1981–85), Richard Wald (1973–77), Lester M. Crystal (1977–79), William J. Small (1979–81), Lawrence Grossman (1985–88), <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gartner" title="Michael Gartner">Michael Gartner</a> (1988–93), <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lack_(executive)" title="Andrew Lack (executive)">Andrew Lack</a> (1993–2001), <a href="/wiki/Neal_Shapiro" title="Neal Shapiro">Neal Shapiro</a> (2001–05), and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Capus" title="Steve Capus">Steve Capus</a> (2005–March 5, 2013). In August 2013, <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Turness" title="Deborah Turness">Deborah Turness</a> assumed the role as president of NBC News, becoming the first woman to head the division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turness_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Turness-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_News_Radio" title="NBC News Radio">NBC News Radio</a> has been distributed by <a href="/wiki/IHeartMedia" title="IHeartMedia">iHeartMedia</a> and its TTWN Networks since July 2016. It is provided to the network's 24/7 News Source affiliates and includes a top of the hour newscast along with other audio content which is heard on over 1000 radio stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Radio_Network" title="NBC Radio Network">NBC Radio Network</a> was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Westwood_One_(1976%E2%80%932011)" title="Westwood One (1976–2011)">Westwood One</a> in 1987 as <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>, which acquired NBC's parent company <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">RCA</a>, divested most properties not pertaining to the NBC television network. NBC Radio's news operation was merged into the <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Broadcasting_System" title="Mutual Broadcasting System">Mutual Broadcasting System</a>, then into Westwood One's then-corporate sibling <a href="/wiki/CBS_Radio" title="CBS Radio">CBS Radio</a>, and eventually assimilated into the syndicator itself. Initially just a service limited to one-hour reports from 6 a.m. to 10 p.m. ET, on March 5, 2012, Dial Global – who had acquired Westwood One – announced NBC News Radio would expand to a full-time 24-hour radio news network, replacing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNN_Radio" title="CNN Radio">CNN Radio</a> (that itself replaced both NBC Radio and Mutual in 1999).</p>
<p>As of 2016, <a href="/wiki/WOR_(AM)" title="WOR (AM)">WOR</a> serves as NBC News Radio's <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> flagship station, while <a href="/wiki/KOGO_(AM)" title="KOGO (AM)">KOGO</a> serves as the network's <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a> flagship station.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It broadcasts in a 15-minute cycle, with the latest news in business, politics, health, technology, sport, weather,...</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NBC_News_Overnight.2FNightside"><i>NBC News Overnight</i>/<i>Nightside</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: NBC News Overnight/Nightside">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982, NBC News began production on <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_News_Overnight" title="NBC News Overnight">NBC News Overnight</a></i> with anchors <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ellerbee" title="Linda Ellerbee">Linda Ellerbee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Dobyns" title="Lloyd Dobyns">Lloyd Dobyns</a>, and Bill Schechner. That program was cancelled in December 1983, but in 1991, NBC News launched another overnight news show called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_News_Nightside" title="NBC News Nightside">NBC Nightside</a></i>. During its run, the show's anchors included Sara James,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Bruce Hall, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Mora" title="Antonio Mora">Antonio Mora</a>, Tom Miller, <a href="/wiki/Campbell_Brown" title="Campbell Brown">Campbell Brown</a>, Kim Hindrew, Tom Donavan, and Tonya Strong.</p>
<p><i>NBC Nightside</i> lasted until 1998 and was replaced by "NBC All Night", composed of reruns of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_with_Jay_Leno" title="The Tonight Show with Jay Leno">The Tonight Show with Jay Leno</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Conan_O%27Brien" title="Late Night with Conan O'Brien">Late Night with Conan O'Brien</a></i>, and later from January 1, 2007 to September 23, 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/Poker_After_Dark" title="Poker After Dark">Poker After Dark</a></i>. NBC now airs same day repeats of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Today_with_Kathie_Lee_and_Hoda" title="Today with Kathie Lee and Hoda">fourth hour of <i>Today</i></a> and CNBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Money" title="Mad Money">Mad Money</a></i> on weekdays, <a href="/wiki/LXTV" title="LXTV">LXTV</a> programs on early Sunday mornings, and <i>Meet the Press</i> and <i>Dateline</i> encores on early Monday mornings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NBC_News_Channel">NBC News Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: NBC News Channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NBC News Channel is a news video and report feed service, similar to a wire service, providing pre-produced international, national and regional stories some with fronting reporters customized for NBC network affiliates. It is based in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a> and is connected to the studios of Charlotte NBC affiliate <a href="/wiki/WCNC-TV" title="WCNC-TV">WCNC-TV</a>. NBC News Channel also served as the production base of <i>NBC Nightside</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noted_coverage">Noted coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Noted coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NBC News got the first American news interviews from two Russian presidents (<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>), and Brokaw was the only American television news correspondent to witness the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a> in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShalesBerlin_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShalesBerlin-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_broadcasts">International broadcasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NBC_News&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: International broadcasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MSNBC is not shown outside the Americas on a channel in its own right. However, both NBC News and MSNBC are shown for a few hours a day on <a href="/wiki/OSN_News" title="OSN News">OSN News</a> in <a href="/wiki/MENA" title="MENA">MENA</a> Region.</p>
<p>MSNBC is also shown occasionally on sister network <a href="/wiki/CNBC_Europe" title="CNBC Europe">CNBC Europe</a> during <a href="/wiki/Breaking_news" title="Breaking news">breaking news</a>, and Nightly News and Meet the Press are also shown regularly. <i>NBC Nightly News</i> is shown in the Philippines on <a href="/wiki/9TV" title="9TV">9TV</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Talk_TV_(Philippines)" title="Talk TV (Philippines)">Talktv</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_News_Channel" title="Solar News Channel">Solar News Channel</a>), while <i>Early Today</i> was officially dropped from the network in December 2013, but they replaced by the repeats of <a href="/wiki/Inside_Edition" title="Inside Edition">Inside Edition</a>. <i>NBC Nightly News</i>, along with the full program lineup of NBC, was carried by affiliate <a href="/wiki/VSB-TV" title="VSB-TV">VSB-TV</a> in Bermuda.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Seven_Network" title="Seven Network">Seven Network</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> has close ties with NBC and has used a majority of the network's imaging and slogans since the 1970s. <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_News" title="Seven News">Seven News</a></i> has featured <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mission_(theme_music)" title="The Mission (theme music)">The Mission</a></i> as its news theme since the mid-1980s. Local newscasts were named <i>Seven Nightly News</i> from the mid-1980s until around 2000. NBC and Seven will often share news recourses between the two countries. NBC News has been known to use Seven News reporters for live reports on a developing news story in Australia. Seven News will sometimes also incorporate an NBC News report into its national bulletins. <i>Today</i>, <i>Weekend Today</i> and <i>Meet The Press</i> are all broadcast on the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Network" title="Seven Network">Seven Network</a> during the early morning hours from 3-5 a.m., just before Seven's own morning show <i><a href="/wiki/Sunrise_(TV_program)" title="Sunrise (TV program)">Sunrise</a></i>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_Nightly_News" title="NBC Nightly News">NBC Nightly News</a></i> is <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">live digital television broadcast transmission</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_delay" title="Broadcast delay">delayed</a>) on <a href="/wiki/TVB_Pearl" title="TVB Pearl">TVB Pearl</a> daily from 07:00 until 08:00 <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Time" title="Hong Kong Time">Hong Kong Time</a> (18:00 until 19:00 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Standard_Time_(North_America)" title="Eastern Standard Time (North America)">New York City Time</a>).</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> network, used to air segments from <i>NBC Nightly News</i> on their <i><a href="/wiki/ITV_News_at_5:30" title="ITV News at 5:30">ITV News at 5:30</a></i> morning newscast before it was cancelled in December 2012. NBC News share facilities and crew in the UK with <a href="/wiki/ITN" title="ITN">ITN</a>, who is the news provider for ITV.</p>
