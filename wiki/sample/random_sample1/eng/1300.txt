<p>The <b>PC-9800 Series</b> is a lineup of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/16-bit" title="16-bit">16-bit</a> and <a href="/wiki/32-bit" title="32-bit">32-bit</a> personal computers manufactured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_Corporation" title="NEC Corporation">NEC</a> from 1982 through 2000. The platform established NEC's dominance in the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a> market, and by 1999, more than 18 million PC-98 units had been sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PC-9800_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The first model, the PC-9801, was launched on October 1982,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-3">[3]</a></sup> and employed an <a href="/wiki/Intel_8086" title="Intel 8086">8086</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a>. It ran at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clock_speed" title="Clock speed">clock speed</a> of 5 MHz, with two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9CPD7220" title="ΜPD7220">µPD7220</a> display controllers (one for text, the other for video graphics), and shipped with 128 KB of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" title="Random access memory">RAM</a>, expandable to 640 KB. Its 8-color display had a maximum resolution of 640×400 pixels. Its successor, the PC-9801E, which appeared in 1983, employed an <a href="/wiki/Intel_8086" title="Intel 8086">8086</a>-2 CPU, which could selectively run at a speed of either 5 or 8 MHz. The NEC PC-9801VM used <a href="/wiki/NEC_V20" title="NEC V20">NEC V30</a> CPU.</p>
<p>When the PC-9801 was launched in 1982, it was initially priced at 298,000 yen (about US$1,200 in 1982 dollars).</p>
<p>In the 1980s and early 1990s, NEC dominated the Japan domestic PC market with more than 60% of the PCs sold as PC-9801 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC-8801" title="PC-8801">PC-8801</a>. In 1990, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> Japan introduced the <a href="/wiki/DOS/V" title="DOS/V">DOS/V</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> which enabled displaying Japanese text on standard <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer/AT" title="IBM Personal Computer/AT">IBM PC/AT</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_Graphics_Array" title="Video Graphics Array">VGA</a> adapters. After that, the decline of the PC-98 began. The PC-9801's last successor was the <a href="/wiki/Celeron" title="Celeron">Celeron</a>-based PC-9821Ra43 (with a clock frequency of 433 MHz, using a 440FX chipset-based motherboard design from 1998), which appeared in 2000.</p>
<p>While NEC did not market these specific machines in the West, it did sell the NEC <a href="/wiki/APC_III" title="APC III">APC III</a>, which has similar hardware as early PC-98 models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Old-computers_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Old-computers-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PC-9800_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The PC-98 is different from the IBM PC in many ways; for instance, it uses its own 16-bit C-Bus instead of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industry_Standard(s)_Architecture" title="Industry Standard(s) Architecture">ISA</a> bus; <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memory-mapped_I/O" title="Memory-mapped I/O">I/O port</a> addressing, memory management, and graphics output are also different. However, localized <a href="/wiki/MS-DOS" title="MS-DOS">MS-DOS</a> or <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> will still run on PC-9801s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clones">Clones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PC-9800_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seiko_Epson_Corporation" title="Seiko Epson Corporation">Seiko Epson</a> manufactured PC-9801 clones, as well as compatible peripherals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PC-9800_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Software for the PC-98 generally ran from program and data disks (Disk 0 &amp; 1 or A &amp; B), and NEC did not have a strong <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">GUI</a> to go up against <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a> when it took Japan's PC market by storm. NEC's decision to work with Microsoft to offer a PC-98 compatible version of Windows 95 could be seen as the first step towards the PC-98's downfall, as consumers were no longer required to have an NEC-built system to run software designed for Windows.</p>
<p>The PC9801 had thousands of game titles designed for it, many of which made creative use of the system's limitations (it was originally designed as a business machine) to great commercial success. Despite having hardware specifications far inferior to the <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a> <a href="/wiki/FM_Towns" title="FM Towns">FM Towns</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharp_X68000" title="Sharp X68000">Sharp X68000</a> personal computers, the massive install base and steady flow of game titles (in particular "<a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin" title="Dōjin">dōjin</a>" style <a href="/wiki/Dating_sim" title="Dating sim">dating sims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">RPGs</a>, as well as early games of the <i><a href="/wiki/Touhou_Project" title="Touhou Project">Touhou Project</a></i> franchise) kept it as the favored platform for PC game developers in Japan until the rise of the DOS/V clones.</p>
<p>NEC kept much of its hardware and platform proprietary or under license, so while it had a virtual monopoly in the Japanese market, later IBM PC clones with DOS/V and Windows from companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitachi,_Ltd." title="Hitachi, Ltd.">Hitachi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a> that did not require such license fees (like Epson's 98 clones) flooded the market and displaced NEC. The proprietary technology that was NEC's strength turned into its weakness as its competitors could use off-the-shelf technology to build cheaper IBM PC clones at a time when NEC was charging much steeper prices for its PC-98s.</p>
