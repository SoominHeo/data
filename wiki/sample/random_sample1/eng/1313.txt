<p><b>NGC 6357</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse_nebula" title="Diffuse nebula">diffuse nebula</a> near <a href="/wiki/NGC_6334" title="NGC 6334">NGC 6334</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scorpius_(constellation)" title="Scorpius (constellation)">Scorpius</a>. The nebula contains many proto-stars shielded by dark disks of gas, and young stars wrapped in expanding "cocoons" or expanding gases surrounding these small stars. It is also known as the <b>Lobster Nebula</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This nebula was also given the name <b>War and Peace Nebula</b> by the <a href="/wiki/Midcourse_Space_Experiment" title="Midcourse Space Experiment">Midcourse Space Experiment</a> scientists because of its appearance as well as the name "Madokami Nebula" as it resembles one of the main characters, Madoka Kaname from the Japanese anime Puella Magi Madoka Magica. They said that in infrared images the bright, western part resembles a dove, while the eastern part looks like a skull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is located about 5500 light years away from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pismis_24">Pismis 24</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6357&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pismis 24">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5057" data-file-width="3140" height="354" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg/220px-EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg/330px-EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg/440px-EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EmissionNebula_NGC6357.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> (HST) image of <a href="/wiki/Pismis_24-1" title="Pismis 24-1">Pismis 24-1</a>, the "core" of NGC 6357.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This nebula includes the open cluster <b>Pismis 24</b>, which is home to several <a href="/wiki/OB_star" title="OB star">massive stars</a>. One of the brightest stars in the cluster, <a href="/wiki/Pismis_24-1" title="Pismis 24-1">Pismis 24-1</a>, was thought possibly to be the most massive on record, approaching 300 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>, until it was discovered to be a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">multiple system</a> of at least three stars; component stars would still remain near 100 solar masses each, making them among the more massive stars on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="G353.2.2B0.7">G353.2+0.7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6357&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: G353.2+0.7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The young stellar cluster G353.2+0.7 lies east of Pismis 24 and was revealed by a <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra</a> X-ray image showing approximately 800 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-townsley14_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-townsley14-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="G353.1.2B0.36">G353.1+0.36</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6357&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: G353.1+0.36">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The young stellar cluster G353.1+0.36 lies southeast of Pismis 24 and also contains approximately 800 stars detected by X-ray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-townsley14_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-townsley14-10">[10]</a></sup> The region includes several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O-type_stars" title="O-type stars">O-type stars</a>, including [BDSB2003] 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Damke06_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Damke06-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Massive_stars">Massive stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_6357&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Massive stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NGC 6357 is one of the most prominent sites of massive-star formation our neighborhood of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. A variety of early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O_star" title="O star">O-type stars</a> reside in this nebula, blowing the bubbles around the stars clusters that can be seen in the molecular cloud.</p>
