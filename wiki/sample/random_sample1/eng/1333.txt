<p><b>5</b> (<b>five</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">number</a>, <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numeral</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Natural_number" title="Natural number">natural number</a> following <a href="/wiki/4_(number)" title="4 (number)">four</a> and preceding <a href="/wiki/6_(number)" title="6 (number)">six</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mathematics">In mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Five is the third <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">prime number</a>. Because it can be written as <span class="nowrap">2<sup>2<sup>1</sup></sup> + 1</span>, five is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Fermat_number" title="Fermat number">Fermat prime</a>; therefore a <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular polygon</a> with 5 sides (a regular <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a>) is <a href="/wiki/Constructible_polygon" title="Constructible polygon">constructible</a> with compass and unmarked straightedge. 5 is the third <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Germain_prime" title="Sophie Germain prime">Sophie Germain prime</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Safe_prime" title="Safe prime">safe prime</a>, the third <a href="/wiki/Catalan_number" title="Catalan number">Catalan number</a>, and the third <a href="/wiki/Mersenne_prime" title="Mersenne prime">Mersenne prime</a> exponent. Five is the first <a href="/wiki/Wilson_prime" title="Wilson prime">Wilson prime</a> and the third <a href="/wiki/Factorial_prime" title="Factorial prime">factorial prime</a>, also an <a href="/wiki/Alternating_factorial" title="Alternating factorial">alternating factorial</a>. Five is the first <a href="/wiki/Good_prime" title="Good prime">good prime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is an <a href="/wiki/Eisenstein_prime" title="Eisenstein prime">Eisenstein prime</a> with no imaginary part and real part of the form <span class="nowrap">3<i>n</i> − 1</span>. It is also the only number that is part of more than one pair of <a href="/wiki/Twin_prime" title="Twin prime">twin primes</a>. Five is a <a href="/wiki/Congruent_number" title="Congruent number">congruent number</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five is conjectured to be the only odd <a href="/wiki/Untouchable_number" title="Untouchable number">untouchable number</a> and if this is the case then five will be the only odd prime number that is not the base of an aliquot tree.</p>
<p>Five is also the only prime that is the sum of two consecutive primes, namely 2 and 3.</p>
<p>The number 5 is the fifth <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci_number" title="Fibonacci number">Fibonacci number</a>, being <a href="/wiki/2_(number)" title="2 (number)">2</a> plus <a href="/wiki/3_(number)" title="3 (number)">3</a>. 5 is also a <a href="/wiki/Pell_number" title="Pell number">Pell number</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Markov_number" title="Markov number">Markov number</a>, appearing in solutions to the Markov Diophantine equation: (1, 2, 5), (1, 5, 13), (2, 5, 29), (5, 13, 194), (5, 29, 433), ... (<span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="OEIS"><img alt="OEIS" data-file-height="556" data-file-width="409" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/OEISicon_light.svg/11px-OEISicon_light.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/OEISicon_light.svg/17px-OEISicon_light.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/OEISicon_light.svg/22px-OEISicon_light.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> <a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A030452" title="oeis:A030452">A030452</a></span> lists Markov numbers that appear in solutions where one of the other two terms is 5). Whereas 5 is unique in the Fibonacci sequence, in the Perrin sequence 5 is both the fifth and sixth <a href="/wiki/Perrin_number" title="Perrin number">Perrin numbers</a>.</p>
<p>5 is the length of the <a href="/wiki/Hypotenuse" title="Hypotenuse">hypotenuse</a> of the smallest <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_triple" title="Pythagorean triple">integer-sided right triangle</a>.</p>
<p>In bases 10 and 20, 5 is a 1-<a href="/wiki/Automorphic_number" title="Automorphic number">automorphic number</a>.</p>
<p>5 and 6 form a <a href="/wiki/Ruth%E2%80%93Aaron_pair" title="Ruth–Aaron pair">Ruth–Aaron pair</a> under either definition.</p>
<p>There are five solutions to <a href="/wiki/Zn%C3%A1m%27s_problem" title="Znám's problem">Znám's problem</a> of length 6.</p>
<p>Five is the second <a href="/wiki/Sierpinski_number" title="Sierpinski number">Sierpinski number</a> of the first kind, and can be written as S2=(2<sup>2</sup>)+1</p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomial</a> equations of degree <a href="/wiki/4_(number)" title="4 (number)">4</a> and below can be solved with radicals, equations of degree 5 and higher cannot generally be so solved. This is the <a href="/wiki/Abel%E2%80%93Ruffini_theorem" title="Abel–Ruffini theorem">Abel–Ruffini theorem</a>. This is related to the fact that the <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_group" title="Symmetric group">symmetric group</a> <i>S</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> is a <a href="/wiki/Solvable_group" title="Solvable group">solvable group</a> for <i>n</i> ≤ 4 and not solvable for <i>n</i> ≥ 5.</p>
<p>While all <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graphs</a> with 4 or fewer vertices are <a href="/wiki/Planar_graph" title="Planar graph">planar</a>, there exists a graph with 5 vertices which is not planar: <i>K</i><sub>5</sub>, the <a href="/wiki/Complete_graph" title="Complete graph">complete graph</a> with 5 vertices.</p>
<p>Five is also the number of <a href="/wiki/Platonic_solid" title="Platonic solid">Platonic solids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> with five sides is a <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Figurate_number" title="Figurate number">Figurate numbers</a> representing pentagons (including five) are called <a href="/wiki/Pentagonal_number" title="Pentagonal number">pentagonal numbers</a>. Five is also a <a href="/wiki/Square_pyramidal_number" title="Square pyramidal number">square pyramidal number</a>.</p>
<p>Five is the only prime number to end in the digit 5, because all other numbers written with a 5 in the ones-place under the decimal system are multiples of five. As a consequence of this, 5 is in base 10 a 1-<a href="/wiki/Automorphic_number" title="Automorphic number">automorphic number</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)#Vulgar.2C_proper.2C_and_improper_fractions" title="Fraction (mathematics)">Vulgar fractions</a> with 5 or <a href="/wiki/2_(number)" title="2 (number)">2</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" title="Fraction (mathematics)">denominator</a> do not yield infinite <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal</a> expansions, unlike expansions with all other prime denominators, because they are prime factors of <a href="/wiki/10_(number)" title="10 (number)">ten</a>, the base. When written in the decimal system, all multiples of 5 will end in either 5 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/0_(number)" title="0 (number)">0</a>.</p>
<p>There are five <a href="/wiki/Simple_Lie_group#Exceptional_cases" title="Simple Lie group">Exceptional Lie groups</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_the_glyph">Evolution of the glyph</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolution of the glyph">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evolution5glyph.png"><img alt="Evolution5glyph.png" data-file-height="71" data-file-width="311" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Evolution5glyph.png/220px-Evolution5glyph.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Evolution5glyph.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a></div>
<p>The evolution of the modern Western <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a> for the numeral five cannot be traced back to the Indian system as for the numbers 1 to 4. The <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">Gupta</a> empires in what is now <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> had among themselves several different glyphs which bear no resemblance to the modern glyph. The <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Nagari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a> took these glyphs and all came up with glyphs that are similar to a lowercase "h" rotated 180°. The Ghubar Arabs transformed the glyph in several different ways, producing glyphs that were more similar to the numbers 4 or 3 than to the number 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was from those characters that Europeans finally came up with the modern 5, though from purely graphical evidence, it would be much easier to conclude that the modern symbol came from the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_numerals" title="Khmer numerals">Khmer</a>. The Khmer glyph develops from the Kushana/Ândhra/Gupta numeral, its shape looking like the modern version with an extended swirled 'tail' <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the shape of the 5 character has an <a href="/wiki/Ascender_(typography)" title="Ascender (typography)">ascender</a> in most modern <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>, in typefaces with <a href="/wiki/Text_figures" title="Text figures">text figures</a> the character usually has a <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descender</a>, as, for example, in <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Text_figures_256.svg"><img alt="Text figures 256.svg" data-file-height="37" data-file-width="60" height="37" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Text_figures_256.svg/60px-Text_figures_256.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Text_figures_256.svg/90px-Text_figures_256.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Text_figures_256.svg/120px-Text_figures_256.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a>.</p>

<li>The most destructive known <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> rate an F-5 on the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">Fujita scale</a> or EF-5 on the <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_scale" title="Enhanced Fujita scale">Enhanced Fujita scale</a>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Messier_object" title="Messier object">Messier object</a> <a href="/wiki/Messier_5" title="Messier 5">M5</a>, a magnitude 7.0 <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Serpens" title="Serpens">Serpens</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/New_General_Catalogue" title="New General Catalogue">New General Catalogue</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.ngcic.org/" rel="nofollow">object</a> <a href="/wiki/NGC_5" title="NGC 5">NGC 5</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> 13 <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(constellation)" title="Andromeda (constellation)">Andromeda</a>.</li>
<li>The Roman numeral V stands for dwarfs (<a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> stars) in the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">Yerkes spectral classification scheme</a>.</li>
<li>The Roman numeral V (usually) stands for the fifth-discovered satellite of a planet or minor planet (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">Jupiter V</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)" title="Saros (astronomy)">Saros</a> <a class="external text" href="http://sunearth.gsfc.nasa.gov/eclipse/LEsaros/LEsaros1-175.html" rel="nofollow">number</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a> series which began on October 8, 2581 BC and ended on March 24, 1084 BC. The duration of Saros series 5 was 1496.5 years, and it contained 84 lunar eclipses.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)" title="Saros (astronomy)">Saros</a> <a class="external text" href="http://sunearth.gsfc.nasa.gov/eclipse/SEsaros/SEsaros1-175.html" rel="nofollow">number</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> series which began on April 4, <a href="/wiki/28th_century_BC" title="28th century BC">2720 BC</a> and ended on May 24, 1422 BC. The duration of Saros series 5 was 1298.1 years, and it contained 73 solar eclipses.</li>
<li>There are five <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian points</a> in a two-body system.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discordianism">Discordianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Discordianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Discordianism" title="Discordianism">Discordianism</a>, 5 is seen as a very important number. This is demonstrated in the <a href="/wiki/Discordianism#Law_of_Fives" title="Discordianism">Law of Fives</a>, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Discordianism#Pentabarf" title="Discordianism">Pentabarf</a>, which contains five rules.</li>

</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish">Jewish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jewish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> contains five books—<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leviticus" title="Book of Leviticus">Leviticus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a>—which are collectively called the Five Books of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, the Pentateuch (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> for "five containers," referring to the scroll cases in which the books were kept), or <a href="/wiki/Chumash_(Judaism)" title="Chumash (Judaism)">Humash</a> (<span lang="he" xml:lang="he">חומש</span>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> for "fifth").</li>
<li>The book of <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> is arranged into five books, paralleling the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Five_Books_of_Moses" title="Five Books of Moses">Five Books of Moses</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hamsa" title="Hamsa">Khamsa</a>, an ancient symbol shaped like a hand with four fingers and one thumb, is used as a protective amulet by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>; that same symbol is also very popular in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arabic</a> culture, known to protect from envy and the <a href="/wiki/Evil_eye" title="Evil eye">evil eye</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sikh">Sikh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sikh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The five sacred <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> symbols prescribed by Guru Gobind Singh are commonly known as <i>panj kakars</i> or the 'Five Ks' because they start with letter K representing <i>kakka</i> (<span lang="pn" xml:lang="pn">ਕ</span>) in the <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi language</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurmukhi_script" title="Gurmukhi script">Gurmukhi script</a>. They are: <i>kesh</i> (unshorn hair), <i>kangha</i> (the comb), <i>kara</i> (the steel bracelet), <i>kachhehra</i> (the soldiers shorts), and <i>kirpan</i> (the sword) (in Gurmukhi: <span lang="pn" xml:lang="pn">ਕੇਸ, ਕੰਘਾ, ਕੜਾ, ਕਛਹਰਾ, ਕਿਰਪਾਨ</span>). Also, there are five deadly evils: <i>kam</i> (lust), <i>krodh</i> (anger), <i>moh</i> (attachment), <i>lobh</i> (greed), and <i>ankhar</i> (ego).</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_religions_and_cultures">Other religions and cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other religions and cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>According to ancient Greek philosophers such as <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, the universe is made up of five <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">classical elements</a>: <a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">ether</a>. This concept was later adopted by Medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alchemists" title="Alchemists">alchemists</a> and more recently by practitioners of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Pagan" title="Neo-Pagan">Neo-Pagan</a> religions such as <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a>, or five-pointed star, bears religious significance in various faiths including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baha%27i" title="Baha'i">Baha'i</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">Satanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thelema" title="Thelema">Thelema</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a>, "five" sounds like the word "not" (character: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">唔</span>). When five appears in front of a lucky number, e.g. "58", the result is considered unlucky.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> tradition, there are <a href="/wiki/Wu_Xing" title="Wu Xing">five elements</a>: (<a href="/wiki/Water_(Wu_Xing)" title="Water (Wu Xing)">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_(Wu_Xing)" title="Fire (Wu Xing)">fire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_(Wu_Xing)" title="Earth (Wu Xing)">earth</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_(Wu_Xing)" title="Tree (Wu Xing)">wood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metal_(Wu_Xing)" title="Metal (Wu Xing)">metal</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> names for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Week-day_names" title="Week-day names">days of the week</a>, Tuesday through <a href="/wiki/Saturday" title="Saturday">Saturday</a>, come from these elements via the identification of the elements with the <a href="/wiki/Classical_planet" title="Classical planet">five planets visible with the naked eye</a>. Also, the traditional Japanese calendar has a five-day weekly cycle that can be still observed in printed mixed calendars combining Western, Chinese-Buddhist, and Japanese names for each weekday.</li>
<li>Members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Nation_of_Gods_and_Earths" title="The Nation of Gods and Earths">The Nation of Gods and Earths</a>, a primarily African American religious organization, call themselves the "Five-Percenters" because they believe that only 5% of mankind is truly enlightened.</li>
</ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fifth" title="Perfect fifth">perfect fifth</a> is the most consonant harmony, and is the basis for most western tuning systems.</li>
<li>Modern musical notation uses a <a href="/wiki/Staff_(music)" title="Staff (music)">musical staff</a> made of five horizontal lines.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">harmonics</a> – the fifth <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_series_(music)" title="Harmonic series (music)">partial</a> (or 4th <a href="/wiki/Overtone" title="Overtone">overtone</a>) of a <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental</a> has a frequency ratio of 5:1 to the frequency of that fundamental. This ratio corresponds to the interval of 2 octaves plus a pure major third. Thus, the interval of 5:4 is the interval of the pure third. A <a href="/wiki/Major_and_minor" title="Major and minor">major</a> <a href="/wiki/Triad_(music)" title="Triad (music)">triad</a> <a href="/wiki/Chord_(music)" title="Chord (music)">chord</a> when played in <a href="/wiki/Just_intonation" title="Just intonation">just intonation</a> (most often the case in <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> vocal ensemble singing), will contain such a pure major third.</li>
<li>The number of completed, numbered piano concertos of <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>.</li>
<li>Using the Latin root, five musicians are called a quintet.</li>
<li>A scale with five notes per octave is called a <a href="/wiki/Pentatonic_scale" title="Pentatonic scale">pentatonic scale</a>.</li>
<li>Five is the lowest possible number that can be the top number of a <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">time signature</a> with an asymmetric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" title="Meter (music)">meter</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> have five interlocked rings as their symbol, representing the number of inhabited <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> represented by the Olympians (Europe, Asia, Africa, Australia and Oceania, and the Americas).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Five-a-side_football" title="Five-a-side football">Five-a-side football</a> is a variation of <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> in which each team fields five players.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squad_number" title="Squad number">jersey number</a> 5 has been retired by several <a href="/wiki/Major_professional_sports_leagues_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Major professional sports leagues in the United States and Canada">North American sports</a> teams in honor of past playing greats or other key figures:
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>:
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>, for <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Hall of Famer</a> <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Robinson" title="Brooks Robinson">Brooks Robinson</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> have retired the number twice. The first was in 1940 for <a href="/wiki/Willard_Hershberger" title="Willard Hershberger">Willard Hershberger</a>, who committed suicide during the season. The number was returned to service in 1942, and was later retired a second time for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Bench" title="Johnny Bench">Johnny Bench</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a>, for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Lou_Boudreau" title="Lou Boudreau">Lou Boudreau</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a>, for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bagwell" title="Jeff Bagwell">Jeff Bagwell</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a>, for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/George_Brett" title="George Brett">George Brett</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>, for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Miami_Marlins" title="History of the Miami Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> retired the number for their first president <a href="/wiki/Carl_Barger" title="Carl Barger">Carl Barger</a>, who died in December 1992, four months before the team's first game. The number was chosen because DiMaggio was Barger's favorite player. When the team renamed itself the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> in advance of its 2012 move to <a href="/wiki/Marlins_Park" title="Marlins Park">a new stadium</a>, it decided to honor Barger with a plaque at the new park and placed the number into circulation.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a>:
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Suns" title="Phoenix Suns">Phoenix Suns</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Dick_Van_Arsdale" title="Dick Van Arsdale">Dick Van Arsdale</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a>:
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Hockey_Hall_of_Fame" title="Hockey Hall of Fame">Hall of Famer</a> <a href="/wiki/Dit_Clapper" title="Dit Clapper">Dit Clapper</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a>, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicklas_Lidstrom" title="Nicklas Lidstrom">Nicklas Lidstrom</a>.</li>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Islanders" title="New York Islanders">New York Islanders</a>, for Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Denis_Potvin" title="Denis Potvin">Denis Potvin</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs" title="Toronto Maple Leafs">Toronto Maple Leafs</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Bill_Barilko" title="Bill Barilko">Bill Barilko</a>. The Leafs have a unique policy of not retiring numbers unless the player honoured either died or suffered a career-ending incident while a member of the team. Barilko disappeared while on a fishing trip in 1951; his presumed death was confirmed when the wreckage of the plane he was on was discovered in a remote section of Ontario in 1962.</li>

</ul>
</li>

<li>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball_scorekeeping#Defensive_positions" title="Baseball scorekeeping">baseball scorekeeping</a>, the number 5 represents the <a href="/wiki/Third_baseman" title="Third baseman">third baseman</a>'s position.</li>
<li>In basketball:
<ul>
<li>The number 5 is used to represent the position of <a href="/wiki/Center_(basketball)" title="Center (basketball)">center</a>.</li>
<li>The number of players of a <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> team on the court at a given time. Thus, the phrase five on five is commonly used to describe standard competitive basketball.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Five-second_rule_(basketball)" title="Five-second rule (basketball)">"5-second rule"</a> refers to several related rules designed to promote continuous play. In all cases, violation of the rule results in a turnover.
<ul>
<li>In all major rulesets, a team has 5 seconds to release the ball toward the court on a throw-in.</li>
<li>In all major rulesets except that of the NBA, a "closely guarded" player has 5 seconds to shoot, pass, or penetrate past the defender.</li>
<li>In the NBA only, a player cannot dribble with his back or side to the basket for more than 5 seconds.</li>
<li>Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Basketball_Federation" title="International Basketball Federation">FIBA</a> rules, a player has 5 seconds to attempt a free throw. (North American rulesets allow 10 seconds.)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> racing, the number 5 &amp; 6 cars traditionally belonged to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WilliamsF1" title="WilliamsF1">Williams</a> team, until the end of the <a href="/wiki/1995_Formula_One_season" title="1995 Formula One season">1995 Formula One season</a>. It was most synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> in the late 1980s and early 1990s.

</ul>
</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestling</a>, if a wrestler grabs the ropes when he is in a submission hold, the attacking wrestler has up to a 5 count to break the hold until a disqualification is made. This is also the case for choking.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a>:
<ul>

</ul>
</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a>:
<ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=5_(number)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg" title="5 as a resin identification code, used in recycling."><img alt="5 as a resin identification code, used in recycling." data-file-height="103" data-file-width="107" height="48" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg/50px-U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg/75px-U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg/100px-U%2B2677_DejaVu_Sans.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
<ul>
<li>5 is the most common number of gears for automobiles with <a href="/wiki/Manual_transmission" title="Manual transmission">manual transmission</a>.</li>
<li>In radio communication, the term "<a href="/wiki/Five_by_five" title="Five by five">Five by five</a>" is used to indicate perfect signal strength and clarity.</li>
<li>On almost all devices with a <a href="/wiki/Numeric_keypad" title="Numeric keypad">numeric keypad</a> such as telephones, computers, etc., the 5 key has a raised dot or raised bar to make dialing easier. Persons who are blind or have low vision find it useful to be able to feel the keys of a telephone. All other numbers can be found with their relative position around the 5 button (on computer keyboards, the 5 key of the <a href="/wiki/Numeric_keypad" title="Numeric keypad">numpad</a> has the raised dot or bar, but the 5 key that shifts with % does not).</li>
<li>On most <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephones</a>, the 5 key is associated with the letters <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">J</a>, <a href="/wiki/K" title="K">K</a>, and <a href="/wiki/L" title="L">L</a>, but on some of the <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry" title="BlackBerry">BlackBerry</a> phones, it is the key for <a href="/wiki/G" title="G">G</a> and <a href="/wiki/H" title="H">H</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Pentium" title="Pentium">Pentium</a>, coined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_Corporation" title="Intel Corporation">Intel Corporation</a>, is a fifth-generation <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a> architecture <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a> used in recycling to identify <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Pentamer" title="Pentamer">pentamer</a> is an oligomer composed of five sub-units.</li>
</ul>
