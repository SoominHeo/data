<p><i><b>No Code</b></i> is the fourth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by the American <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam" title="Pearl Jam">Pearl Jam</a>, released on August 27, 1996 through <a href="/wiki/Epic_Records" title="Epic Records">Epic Records</a>. Following a troubled tour for its previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/Vitalogy" title="Vitalogy">Vitalogy</a></i> (1994), in which Pearl Jam engaged in a much-publicized boycott of <a href="/wiki/Ticketmaster" title="Ticketmaster">Ticketmaster</a>, the band went into the studio to record its follow-up. The music on the record was more diverse than what the band had done on previous releases, incorporating elements of <a href="/wiki/Garage_rock" title="Garage rock">garage rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worldbeat" title="Worldbeat">worldbeat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelia</a>.</p>
<p>Although <i>No Code</i> debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, it left a large section of the band’s fanbase unsatisfied and quickly fell down the charts. Critical reviews were also mixed, with praise to the musical variety but the album being considered inconsistent. The album became the first Pearl Jam album to not reach multi-platinum status, receiving a single <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> certification by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a> in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For its fourth album, Pearl Jam again worked with producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brendan_O%27Brien_(music_producer)" title="Brendan O'Brien (music producer)">Brendan O'Brien</a>, with whom they had worked on predecessors <i><a href="/wiki/Vs._(Pearl_Jam_album)" title="Vs. (Pearl Jam album)">Vs.</a></i> (1993) and <i><a href="/wiki/Vitalogy" title="Vitalogy">Vitalogy</a></i> (1994). <i>No Code</i> was the band's first album with drummer <a href="/wiki/Jack_Irons" title="Jack Irons">Jack Irons</a>, who had joined the band as <i>Vitalogy</i> was being completed. Following the summer U.S. leg of the band's <a href="/wiki/Vitalogy_Tour" title="Vitalogy Tour">Vitalogy Tour</a>, the band began work on <i>No Code</i> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> in July 1995 during the infamous <a href="/wiki/1995_Chicago_heat_wave" title="1995 Chicago heat wave">Chicago heat wave</a>. The Chicago sessions lasted a week at the Chicago Recording Company. During a break in a string of make-up dates for the 1995 tour the band went into the studio for a week-long session in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, where the band recorded "<a href="/wiki/Off_He_Goes" title="Off He Goes">Off He Goes</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostdogs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lostdogs-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The rest of the recording took place in the first half of 1996 in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> at Studio Litho, which is owned by guitarist <a href="/wiki/Stone_Gossard" title="Stone Gossard">Stone Gossard</a>. The album was then mixed by O'Brien at his mixing facility at Southern Tracks in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sessions for the album began with strife and tension. Bassist <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Ament" title="Jeff Ament">Jeff Ament</a> wasn't made aware that the band was recording until three days into the sessions, and said that he "wasn't super involved with that record on any level."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup> Guitarist <a href="/wiki/Mike_McCready" title="Mike McCready">Mike McCready</a> said, "I'm sure Jeff was pissed, but it was more about separating, because if we played all together nothing would get done. We'd all just get pissed off at each other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup> At one point Ament even walked out of the recording sessions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garbarini_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garbarini-5">[5]</a></sup> and considered quitting the band due to lead vocalist <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Vedder" title="Eddie Vedder">Eddie Vedder</a>'s control of the creation process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Due to Pearl Jam balancing recording and touring, Irons commented that the band was "more on-the-fly during the making of <i>No Code</i>, and some good things happened out of that, but we were also really tired. It was difficult to tour and play these shows that were two or three hours long and then force ourselves to produce something in a studio."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peiken_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peiken-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCready said that a lot of the songs were developed out of <a href="/wiki/Jam_session" title="Jam session">jam sessions</a>, and said "I think we kind of rushed it a little bit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Ament said that the band members would bring in fragments of songs, and it would take hours before Vedder could have music to which he could add vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garbarini_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Garbarini-5">[5]</a></sup> He added that "Ed's typically the guy who finishes off the songs...But by the end of <i>No Code</i>, he was so burnt, it was so much work for him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philadelphia_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-philadelphia-9">[9]</a></sup> The band's mood had gotten better by the time the album was finished, and the bandmembers gave credit for this to Irons. Regarding Irons, O'Brien stated that "everybody was on their best musical behavior around him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup> McCready said that Irons urged the band members to discuss their problems, and called him "a big spiritual influence, if not the biggest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garbarini_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Garbarini-5">[5]</a></sup> Vedder said, "Making <i>No Code</i> was all about gaining perspective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-spin-10">[10]</a></sup> Commenting upon the sessions as a whole, O'Brien said, "It was really a transitional record. We had a good time making it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outtakes">Outtakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outtakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two outtakes from the recording sessions ended up as <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-sides</a>, "Black, Red, Yellow" on the "<a href="/wiki/Hail,_Hail" title="Hail, Hail">Hail, Hail</a>" <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">single</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostdogs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lostdogs-1">[1]</a></sup> and "Dead Man", which was originally intended for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Man_Walking_(film)" title="Dead Man Walking (film)">Dead Man Walking</a>,</i> on "Off He Goes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Both songs were included on the 2003 <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Dogs_(album)" title="Lost Dogs (album)">Lost Dogs</a></i> collection of rarities, along with four other tracks from the <i>No Code</i> sessions, two of which ended up in compilations - "<a href="/wiki/Leaving_Here" title="Leaving Here">Leaving Here</a>", which appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Alive" title="Home Alive">Home Alive</a>,</i> and "Gremmie Out of Control," featured on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_for_Our_Mother_Ocean_Vol._1" title="Music for Our Mother Ocean Vol. 1">Music for Our Mother Ocean Vol. 1</a>.</i> The other rejected songs were "All Night" and "Don't Gimme No Lip."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostdogs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-lostdogs-1">[1]</a></sup> "Olympic Platinum", written by the album's mixer <a href="/wiki/Nick_DiDia" title="Nick DiDia">Nick DiDia</a> around the time of the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996 Summer Olympics</a>, was recorded by the band and released on its 1996 fan club Christmas single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>While <i>Vitalogy</i> had shifted away from the earlier albums' accessible compositions and polished production, <i>No Code</i> represented a deliberate break from <i><a href="/wiki/Ten_(Pearl_Jam_album)" title="Ten (Pearl Jam album)">Ten</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">stadium</a> sound, favoring <a href="/wiki/Experimental_rock" title="Experimental rock">experimental</a> ballads and noisy <a href="/wiki/Garage_rock" title="Garage rock">garage rock</a> songs. It stood out with its emphasis on subtle harmony ("<a href="/wiki/Off_He_Goes" title="Off He Goes">Off He Goes</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Asia" title="Music of Asia">Eastern</a> influences ("<a href="/wiki/Who_You_Are_(Pearl_Jam_song)" title="Who You Are (Pearl Jam song)">Who You Are</a>"), and <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> ("I'm Open"). Irons lends a tribal drum sound on the songs "Who You Are" and "In My Tree". Irons stated, "To turn my drum music into a song is pretty challenging, but the guys have been really supportive of me doing it, and we've worked some things into a few songs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peiken_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peiken-7">[7]</a></sup> Vedder said, "We realized that we had an opportunity to experiment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-spin-10">[10]</a></sup> David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> stated that "<i>No Code</i> displays a wider range of moods and instrumentation than on any previous Pearl Jam album."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entertainmentweekly_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-entertainmentweekly-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lyrical themes on the album deal with issues of spirituality, morality, and self-examination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayout_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-wayout-15">[15]</a></sup> Vedder said, "I think there's a little self-examination in those songs, something that a lot of my friends are going through too, as they approach 30."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayout_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-wayout-15">[15]</a></sup> Ament said, "In some ways, it's like the band's story. It's about growing up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayout_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-wayout-15">[15]</a></sup> The lyrics of "<a href="/wiki/Hail,_Hail" title="Hail, Hail">Hail, Hail</a>" refer to two people in a troubled relationship struggling to hold it together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thenewyorktimes_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-thenewyorktimes-16">[16]</a></sup> Vedder has said that he wrote the song "Off He Goes" about himself and how he is a "shit friend," adding, "I'll show up and everything's great and then all of the sudden I'm outta there..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup> "Lukin" is about the "pretty intense stalker problem," Vedder faced during the mid-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secondcoming_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-secondcoming-17">[17]</a></sup> "Around the Bend" was written by Vedder as a <a href="/wiki/Lullaby" title="Lullaby">lullaby</a> that Irons could sing to his son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lyrics to "Smile" are taken from a note that <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Flemion" title="Dennis Flemion">Dennis Flemion</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Frogs_(band)" title="The Frogs (band)">The Frogs</a> hid inside Vedder's notebook while he was onstage performing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The words used in the note are taken from the Frogs songs "This Is How I Feel" and "Now I Wanna Be Dead". Flemion is given credit in the <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a>, but the credit is absent from the CD version. The lyrics to "<a href="/wiki/Red_Mosquito" title="Red Mosquito">Red Mosquito</a>" were inspired by the events surrounding Pearl Jam's June 24, 1995 concert at <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park" title="Golden Gate Park">Golden Gate Park</a>, which happened on the same day Vedder was hospitalized due to <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">food poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vedder_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vedder-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Vedder only made it through seven songs and the band was forced to cancel the remaining dates of the short tour that it was on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayout_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-wayout-15">[15]</a></sup> For the first time on a Pearl Jam album a band member other than Vedder contributed lyrics, with Gossard writing the lyrics to "Mankind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-spin-10">[10]</a></sup> Gossard also sang lead vocals on the track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging_and_title">Packaging and title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Packaging and title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nocode_-_triangle.jpg"><img alt="A triangle with an eye figure inside is seen out of a montage of photographs." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="435" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Nocode_-_triangle.jpg/220px-Nocode_-_triangle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Nocode_-_triangle.jpg/330px-Nocode_-_triangle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a7/Nocode_-_triangle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nocode_-_triangle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The album cover unfolded, creating the <i>No Code</i> logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The album package consists of 156 <a href="/wiki/Instant_film#Polaroid" title="Instant film">Polaroid</a> photos that unfold into a 2x2 square. The Polaroid photos are seemingly random. One of the photos featured on the front cover is the eyeball of <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Rodman" title="Dennis Rodman">Dennis Rodman</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Bulls" title="Chicago Bulls">Chicago Bulls</a> player and friend of the band,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostdogs_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-lostdogs-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> while another photo is of Vedder's foot after he had been stung by a <a href="/wiki/Stingray" title="Stingray">stingray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The pictures, when viewed from afar, blend to form the <i>No Code</i> triangle/eyeball logo that is the theme throughout the album. The CD and vinyl came with lyrics printed on the back of replica Polaroids. Only nine Polaroids came in a set, leaving one to have to obtain another set to accumulate all thirteen songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>When discussing the album's title, Vedder said "it's called <i>No Code</i> because it's full of code. It's misinformation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In medical terminology, a "no code" order is a medical order to withhold CPR on a patient. It is also known as a "do not resuscitate" order. In another interview, Vedder said that "if the record is a complete failure you've kind of owned up to it in a subliminal way. <i>No Code</i> was the same thing. For me, <i>No Code</i> meant 'Do Not Resuscitate'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tour">Tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/No_Code_Tour" title="No Code Tour">No Code Tour</a></div>
<p>Pearl Jam promoted the album with tours in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in the fall of 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The short tour of North America focused on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>. As with <i>Vitalogy</i>, very little touring was done in the United States to promote <i>No Code</i> because of the band's refusal to play in <a href="/wiki/Ticketmaster" title="Ticketmaster">Ticketmaster</a>'s venues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The band chose to use alternate ticketing companies for the shows. A European tour followed in the fall of 1996, of which the band's November 3, 1996 show in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> at <a href="/wiki/Deutschlandhalle" title="Deutschlandhalle">Deutschlandhalle</a> was broadcast on many radio stations worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the North American tour fans complained about the difficulty in obtaining tickets and the use of non-Ticketmaster venues, which were judged to be remote and impersonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Gossard stated that there was "a lot of stress associated with trying to tour at that time" and that "it was growing more and more difficult to be excited about being part of the band." He added, "Ticketmaster, as monopolistic as it may be, is very efficient so we weren't playing the venues we wanted to play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tenpast_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-tenpast-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 17, 2014 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_wireless_Center" title="I wireless Center">i wireless Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moline,_Illinois" title="Moline, Illinois">Moline, Illinois</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Lightning_Bolt_Tour" title="Lightning Bolt Tour">Lightning Bolt Tour</a>, Pearl Jam played the entire album in order as part of their set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moline_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moline-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NoCodeRS_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NoCodeRS-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>No Code</i> sold 366,500 copies in its first week of release, topping the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, but falling short of analysts' predictions of at least 535,000 copies. This was significantly less than what the band's previous two albums sold in their respective first weeks of release. It was, however, the seventh biggest-selling debut of what was a slow year for the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayout_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-wayout-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> It stayed at number one for two weeks, and was Pearl Jam's last album to debut at number one on the album chart until <i><a href="/wiki/Backspacer" title="Backspacer">Backspacer</a></i> was released in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <i>No Code</i> was the band's first album to not reach multi-platinum status, being certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RIAA_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-35">[35]</a></sup> and, as of 2013, has sold 1.75 million copies in the United States according to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan">Nielsen SoundScan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three singles were released from <i>No Code</i>. The lead single "<a href="/wiki/Who_You_Are_(Pearl_Jam_song)" title="Who You Are (Pearl Jam song)">Who You Are</a>" peaked at number 31 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Songs" title="Alternative Songs">Modern Rock</a> charts and number five on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Mainstream_Rock_Tracks" title="Hot Mainstream Rock Tracks">Mainstream Rock</a> charts. Neither of the album's other singles, "<a href="/wiki/Hail,_Hail" title="Hail, Hail">Hail, Hail</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Off_He_Goes" title="Off He Goes">Off He Goes</a>", charted on the Hot 100, but both placed on the Mainstream Rock and Modern Rock charts, as did album track "Red Mosquito".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>No Code</i> received a mixed to positive critical reception. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> staff writer <a href="/wiki/David_Fricke" title="David Fricke">David Fricke</a> gave <i>No Code</i> four out of five stars, saying that the album "is abrupt in its mood swings almost to the point of vertigo." He praised the album as "the kind of impulsive, quixotic, provocative ruckus that has become rare in a modern-rock mainstream" and added that "<i>No Code</i> basically means no rule books, no limits and, above all, no fear."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-42">[42]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> gave the album four out of five stars. The review said that the album "constantly adds unexpected and facinating  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] details....A solid attraction amid intriguing oddities is the powerful array of guitar sounds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-q_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-q-40">[40]</a></sup> Critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> described the album as "slowly winning a heartwarming battle against constitutional melancholia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christgau_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> staff writer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> gave the album three and a half out of five stars, saying, "While a bit too incoherent, <i>No Code</i> is Pearl Jam's richest and most rewarding album to date as well as their most human."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-14">[14]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> gave <i>No Code</i> a seven out of ten. In the review, it is stated that "Vedder is still preoccupied with his own mortality, but now he appears more quasi-mystical than miserable....for all its relative placidity, <i>No Code</i> is still a difficult beast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-nme-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Referring to the songs on the album, <a href="/wiki/Jon_Pareles" title="Jon Pareles">Jon Pareles</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said "about half are worth the effort." He observed that "too often, [Vedder] falls into American culture's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a> syndrome, idealizing childhood innocence above all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thenewyorktimes_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-thenewyorktimes-16">[16]</a></sup> David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the album a C, saying that while <i>No Code</i> "cracks open their sound," it "becomes a collection of fragments that don't add up to much of anything, except a portrait of a musically disjointed band." On the change in mood compared with the band’s previous releases, he said that "the album leaves you with the vaguely unsettling feeling that Pearl Jam without pain are like a pretzel without salt, or Seattle without rain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entertainmentweekly_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-entertainmentweekly-13">[13]</a></sup> Ryan Schreiber of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> called it "a nice listen," but stated that "there's a ton of filler here. In fact, it's almost <i>all</i> filler."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitchforkmedia_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-pitchforkmedia-39">[39]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_magazine" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> reviewer <a href="/wiki/Christopher_John_Farley" title="Christopher John Farley">Christopher John Farley</a> said that the album "makes it sound as if they're having a midlife crisis." Farley added that "too few of the songs on the Pearl Jam CD explore the musical possibilities they suggest in any kind of definitive or provocative manner."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-time-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
