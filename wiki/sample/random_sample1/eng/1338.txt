<p>The <b>O3b Satellite Constellation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">satellite constellation</a> designed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backhaul_(telecommunications)" title="Backhaul (telecommunications)">data backhaul</a> from remote locations. The first four satellites were launched on 25 June 2013, and eight more in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Launch_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Launch-1">[1]</a></sup> There are plans to extend this to twenty satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The constellation is owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/O3b_Networks" title="O3b Networks">O3b Networks</a>, which began offering service in March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellites">Satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O3b_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The satellites were deployed in a circular orbit along the equator at an altitude of 8063 km (<a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">medium Earth orbit</a>) at a velocity of approximately 11,755 mph (18,918 km/h), each making 5 orbits a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The constellation currently has twelve satellites operational in orbit, after three launches of four satellites each. Due to problems with a component of the first four satellites launched, two of those four have been placed on standby for the other two, so ten are used operationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each satellite is equipped with twelve fully steerable <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">Ka band</a> antennas (two beams for gateways, ten beams for remotes) that use 4.3 GHz of spectrum (2×216 MHz per beam) with a proposed throughput of 1.2 Gbit/s per beam (600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbit/s#Megabit_per_second" title="Mbit/s">MBit/s</a> per direction),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITU_presentation_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITU_presentation-6">[6]</a></sup> resulting in a total capacity of 12 GBit/s per satellite. Each beam's footprint measures 700 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-o3b_our-technology_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-o3b_our-technology-7">[7]</a></sup> O3b claims a mouth-to-ear one-way <a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">latency</a> of 179 milliseconds for voice communication, and an end-to-end round-trip latency of 238ms for data services. The maximum throughput per TCP connection is 2.1 Mbit/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For maritime applications, O3b claims a round-trip latency of 130ms, and connectivity speeds of over 500 Mbit/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The satellites are powered by <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> solar arrays and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_ion" title="Lithium ion">lithium ion</a> batteries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and weigh approximately 700 kilograms (1,500 lb) each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Launch_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Launch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The satellites were constructed by <a href="/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space" title="Thales Alenia Space">Thales Alenia Space</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/Thales_Group" title="Thales Group">Thales Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first satellite (PFM) was built in the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Mandelieu_Space_Center" title="Cannes Mandelieu Space Center">Cannes Mandelieu Space Center</a>, while the rest of the constellation was assembled, integrated and tested in Thales Alenia Space Italy's Roman facilities.</p>
