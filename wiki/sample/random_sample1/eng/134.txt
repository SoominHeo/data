<p>"<b>(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction</b>" is a song by the English rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a>, released in 1965. It was written by <a href="/wiki/Jagger/Richards" title="Jagger/Richards">Mick Jagger and Keith Richards</a> and produced by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Loog_Oldham" title="Andrew Loog Oldham">Andrew Loog Oldham</a>. Richards' three-note guitar riff‍—‌intended to be replaced by horns‍—‌opens and drives the song. The lyrics refer to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_frustration" title="Sexual frustration">sexual frustration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercialism" title="Commercialism">commercialism</a>.</p>
<p>The song was first released as a single in the United States in June 1965 and was also featured on the American version of the Rolling Stones' fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_Our_Heads" title="Out of Our Heads">Out of Our Heads</a></i>, released that July. "Satisfaction" was a hit, giving the Stones their first number one in the US. In the UK, the song initially was played only on <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio" title="Pirate radio">pirate radio stations</a>, because its lyrics were considered too sexually suggestive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nuzum_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nuzum-3">[3]</a></sup> It later became the Rolling Stones' fourth number one in the United Kingdom.</p>
<p>In 2004, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine placed "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" in the second spot on its list of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Songs_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Songs of All Time">The 500 Greatest Songs of All Time</a>". The song was added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Recording_Registry" title="National Recording Registry">National Recording Registry</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> in 2006.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Richards recorded the rough version of the riff in a hotel room at the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Harrison_Hotel" title="Fort Harrison Hotel">Fort Harrison Hotel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Clearwater,_Florida" title="Clearwater, Florida">Clearwater, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He ran through it once before falling asleep. He said when he listened back to it in the morning, there was about two minutes of <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a> before you could hear him drop the pick and "then me snoring for the next forty minutes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Rolling Stones first recorded the track on 10 May 1965 at <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">Chess Studios</a> in Chicago, Illinois<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzentgraf_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nzentgraf-6">[6]</a></sup> – a version featuring <a href="/wiki/Brian_Jones" title="Brian Jones">Brian Jones</a> on harmonica. The Stones lip-synched to a dub of this version the first time they debuted the song on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Shindig!" title="Shindig!">Shindig!</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The group re-recorded it two days later at <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Studios</a> in Hollywood, Los Angeles, California, with a different beat and the <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">Gibson</a> <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(music)" title="Distortion (music)">Maestro fuzzbox</a> adding sustain to the sound of the guitar riff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tioos-satisfaction_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tioos-satisfaction-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingwithp187_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollingwithp187-9">[9]</a></sup> Richards envisioned redoing the track later with a <a href="/wiki/Horn_section" title="Horn section">horn section</a> playing the riff: "this was just a little sketch, because, to my mind, the fuzz tone was really there to denote what the horns would be doing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tioos-satisfaction_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-tioos-satisfaction-8">[8]</a></sup> The other Rolling Stones, as well as producer and manager <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Loog_Oldham" title="Andrew Loog Oldham">Andrew Loog Oldham</a> and sound engineer <a href="/wiki/David_Hassinger" title="David Hassinger">David Hassinger</a> eventually outvoted Richards and Jagger so the track was selected for release as a single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingwithp187_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-rollingwithp187-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-songfacts_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-songfacts-10">[10]</a></sup> The song's success boosted sales of the Gibson fuzzbox so that the entire available stock sold out by the end of 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOLDONSONG_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOLDONSONG-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author Gary West cites a different source for the release of "Satisfaction" in interviewing WTRY radio (Troy, NY) DJ Joe Condon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the interview, Condon clearly states that his radio station began playing "Satisfaction" on 29 April 1965, making the above recording date impossible. It can be assumed that "Satisfaction" was probably recorded earlier in April, and that WTRY was playing a test pressing.</p>
<p>Like most of the Stones' pre-1966 recordings, "Satisfaction" was originally released in mono only. In the mid-1980s, a true stereo version of the song was released on German and Japanese editions of the CD reissue of <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rocks_1964%E2%80%931971" title="Hot Rocks 1964–1971">Hot Rocks 1964–1971</a></i>. The stereo mix features a piano (played by session player <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nitzsche" title="Jack Nitzsche">Jack Nitzsche</a>, who also provides the song's iconic tambourine) and acoustic guitar that are barely audible in the original mono release (both instruments are also audible on a <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootleg recording</a> of the instrumental track). This stereo mix of "Satisfaction" also appeared on a radio-promo CD of rare stereo tracks provided to US radio stations in the mid-1980s, but has not yet been featured on a worldwide commercial CD; even later pressings of the German and Japanese <i>Hot Rocks</i> CDs feature the mono mix, making the earlier releases with the stereo mix collectors' items. For the worldwide 2002 reissue of <i>Hot Rocks</i>, an alternative quasi-stereo mix was used featuring the lead guitar, bass, drums, and vocals in the center channel and the acoustic guitar and piano "split" left and right via a <a href="/wiki/Delay_(audio_effect)" title="Delay (audio effect)">delay</a> effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics_and_melody">Lyrics and melody</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lyrics and melody">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Satisfaction_riff.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="105" data-file-width="675" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/57/Satisfaction_riff.png/450px-Satisfaction_riff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/57/Satisfaction_riff.png 1.5x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Satisfaction_riff.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Guitar riff from "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The song opens with the guitar riff, which is joined by the bass halfway through. It is repeated three times with the drums and acoustic guitar before the vocal enters with the line: "I can't get no satisfaction". The key is E major, but with the 3rd and 7th <a href="/wiki/Degree_(music)" title="Degree (music)">degree</a> occasionally lowered, creating - in the first part of the verses ("I can't get no ...") - a distinctive mellow sound. The accompanying chords (i.e. E major, D major and A major) are <a href="/wiki/Borrowed_chord" title="Borrowed chord">borrowed</a> from the E <a href="/wiki/Mixolydian_mode" title="Mixolydian mode">mixolydian</a> scale, which is often used in <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>.</p>
<p>The title line is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Double_negative" title="Double negative">negative concord</a>. Jagger sings the verses in a tone hovering between cynical commentary and frustrated protest, and then leaps half singing and half yelling into the <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a>, where the guitar riff reappears. The lyrics outline the singer's irritation with the increasing commercialism of the modern world, where the radio broadcasts "useless information" and a man on television tells him "how white my shirts can be – but he can't be a man 'cause he doesn't smoke the same cigarettes as me." Jagger also describes the stress of being a celebrity, and the tensions of touring. The reference in the verse to not getting any "girl reaction" was fairly controversial in its day, interpreted by some listeners (and radio programmers) as meaning a girl willing to have sex. Jagger commented that they "didn't understand the dirtiest line", as afterwards the girl asks him to return the following week as she is "on a losing streak,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> an apparent reference to <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstruation</a>. The song closes with a fairly subdued repetition of the song's title, followed suddenly by a full shout of the line, with the final words repeated into the fade-out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its day the song was perceived as disturbing because of both its sexual connotations and the negative view of commercialism and other aspects of modern culture; critic Paul Gambaccini stated: "The lyrics to this were truly threatening to an older audience. This song was perceived as an attack on the status quo".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOLDONSONG_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SOLDONSONG-11">[11]</a></sup> When the Rolling Stones performed the song on <i><a href="/wiki/Shindig!" title="Shindig!">Shindig!</a></i> in 1965, the line "trying to make some girl" was censored,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shindig052665_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-shindig052665-16">[16]</a></sup> although a performance on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i> on 13 February 1966 was uncensored. Forty years later, when the band performed three songs during the February 2006 <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XL" title="Super Bowl XL">Super Bowl XL</a> halftime show, "Satisfaction" was the only one of the three songs not censored as it was broadcast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_success">Release and success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release and success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Satisfaction" was released as a single in the US by <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">London Records</a> on 6 June 1965, with "The Under-Assistant West Coast Promotion Man" as its <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-side</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliottp69_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-elliottp69-18">[18]</a></sup> The single entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> charts in America in the week ending 12 June 1965, remaining there for 14 weeks, reaching the top on 10 July by displacing the <a href="/wiki/Four_Tops" title="Four Tops">Four Tops</a>' "<a href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Help_Myself_(Sugar_Pie_Honey_Bunch)" title="I Can't Help Myself (Sugar Pie Honey Bunch)">I Can't Help Myself (Sugar Pie Honey Bunch)</a>". "Satisfaction" held the number one spot for four weeks, being knocked off on 7 August by "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Henery_the_Eighth,_I_Am" title="I'm Henery the Eighth, I Am">I'm Henery the Eighth, I Am</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Herman%27s_Hermits" title="Herman's Hermits">Herman's Hermits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charts_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-charts-19">[19]</a></sup> While in its eighth week on the American charts, the single was <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified</a> a <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">gold record award</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a> for shipping over a million copies across the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldrecord_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldrecord-20">[20]</a></sup> giving the band their first of many gold disc awards in America. Later the song was also released by London Records on <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_Our_Heads" title="Out of Our Heads">Out of Our Heads</a></i> in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-songfacts_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-songfacts-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Billboard</i> ranked the record as the No. 3 song of 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Satisfaction" was not immediately released by <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca Records</a> in Great Britain. Decca was already in the process of preparing a <a href="/wiki/Got_Live_If_You_Want_It!_(EP)" title="Got Live If You Want It! (EP)">live Rolling Stones EP</a> for release, so the new single did not come out in Britain until 20 August,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliottp69_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-elliottp69-18">[18]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/The_Spider_and_the_Fly_(song)" title="The Spider and the Fly (song)">"The Spider and the Fly"</a> on the B-side. The song peaked at number one for two weeks, replacing <a href="/wiki/Sonny_%26_Cher" title="Sonny &amp; Cher">Sonny &amp; Cher's</a> "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_You_Babe" title="I Got You Babe">I Got You Babe</a>", between 11 and 25 September, before being toppled by <a href="/wiki/The_Walker_Brothers" title="The Walker Brothers">the Walker Brothers</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Make_It_Easy_on_Yourself" title="Make It Easy on Yourself">Make It Easy on Yourself</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charts_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-charts-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the decades since its release, "Satisfaction" has repeatedly been acclaimed by the music industry. In 1976, Britain's <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">New Musical Express</a></i> listed "Satisfaction" 7th among the top 100 singles of all time. There was a resurgence of interest in the song after it was prominently featured in the 1979 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Now" title="Apocalypse Now">Apocalypse Now</a>.</i> In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Vox_(musical_equipment)" title="Vox (musical equipment)">Vox</a> listed "Satisfaction" among "100 records that shook the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top100shook_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-top100shook-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1999, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music_Incorporated" title="Broadcast Music Incorporated">BMI</a> named "Satisfaction" as the 91st-most performed song of the 20th century. In 2000, <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a> listed "Satisfaction" first among its "Top 100 Greatest Rock Songs";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vh1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-vh1-23">[23]</a></sup> the same year, "Satisfaction" also finished runner-up to "Yesterday" in a list jointly compiled by <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> and MTV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top100pop_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-top100pop-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> placed the song 68th out of its "1001 Best Songs Ever". In 2004, <i>Rolling Stone</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s panel of judges named "Satisfaction" as the second-greatest song of all time, coming in second to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan's</a> "<a href="/wiki/Like_a_Rolling_Stone" title="Like a Rolling Stone">Like a Rolling Stone</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rollingstone_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rollingstone-25">[25]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> magazine has called the opening riff "five notes that shook the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jagger said of "Satisfaction":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"It was the song that really made the Rolling Stones, changed us from just another band into a huge, monster band... It has a very catchy title. It has a very catchy guitar riff. It has a great guitar sound, which was original at that time. And it captures a spirit of the times, which is very important in those kinds of songs... Which was <a href="/wiki/Social_alienation" title="Social alienation">alienation</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-songfacts_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-songfacts-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The song has become a staple at Rolling Stones shows. They have performed it on nearly every tour since its release, and concert renditions have been included on the albums <i><a href="/wiki/Got_Live_If_You_Want_It!_(album)" title="Got Live If You Want It! (album)">Got Live If You Want It!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Still_Life_(The_Rolling_Stones_album)" title="Still Life (The Rolling Stones album)">Still Life</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Flashpoint_(album)" title="Flashpoint (album)">Flashpoint</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Live_Licks" title="Live Licks">Live Licks</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shine_a_Light_(The_Rolling_Stones_album)" title="Shine a Light (The Rolling Stones album)">Shine a Light</a></i>, and <i>Sweet Summer Sun: <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park_Live" title="Hyde Park Live">Hyde Park Live</a></i>. One unusual rendition is included in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frank" title="Robert Frank">Robert Frank's</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Cocksucker_Blues" title="Cocksucker Blues">Cocksucker Blues</a></i> from the 1972 tour, when the song was performed by both the Rolling Stones and <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder's</a> band as the second half of a medley with Wonder's "Uptight".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions">Cover versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cover versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Otis_Redding">Otis Redding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Otis Redding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Otis_Redding" title="Otis Redding">Otis Redding</a> recorded a rendition of "Satisfaction" for his album <i><a href="/wiki/Otis_Blue/Otis_Redding_Sings_Soul" title="Otis Blue/Otis Redding Sings Soul">Otis Blue/Otis Redding Sings Soul</a></i>, released in 1965. Redding claimed that he did not know the lyrics of the song. "I use a lot of words different than the Stones' version," he noted. "That's because I made them up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Of that session, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Cropper" title="Steve Cropper">Steve Cropper</a> said, "...if you ever listened to the record you can hardly understand the lyrics, right? I set down to a record player and copied down what I thought the lyrics were and I handed Otis a piece of paper and before we got through with the cut, he threw the paper on the floor and that was it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Music writer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> described it as an "anarchic reading" of the Stones' original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christgau_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-46">[46]</a></sup> Redding's <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>-style arrangement featured horns playing the main riff,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc51_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-pc51-47">[47]</a></sup> as Keith Richards had originally intended. In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Wood" title="Ronnie Wood">Ronnie Wood</a> noted that the Rolling Stones' later concert renditions of the number reflect Redding's interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tioos-satisfaction_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-tioos-satisfaction-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bubblerock">Bubblerock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bubblerock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_King" title="Jonathan King">Jonathan King</a> covered the song on his 1972 album Bubblerock Is Here To Stay as a country and western version which <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> loved. It was released as a single and became a UK Top 30 hit in 1974.</p>

<p>The American avant-garde / <a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a> collective <a href="/wiki/The_Residents" title="The Residents">The Residents</a> recorded and released their own performance of "Satisfaction" in 1976. Originally released in an edition of only 200 copies, the cover quickly became a cult sensation, thanks in part to the success of <a href="/wiki/Devo" title="Devo">Devo</a>'s cover the following year, necessitating a re-press in 1978 of 30,000 copies. <a href="/wiki/Brad_Laner" title="Brad Laner">Brad Laner</a>, writing for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dangerous_Minds_(website)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dangerous Minds (website) (page does not exist)">Dangerous Minds</a>, states the cover "is nearly everything the better known version by Devo from a year later is not: Loose, belligerant, violent, truly fucked up. A real stick in the eye of everything conventionally tasteful in 1976 America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p>The American <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> band <a href="/wiki/Devo" title="Devo">Devo</a> released their rendition of "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" as a single in 1977. Steve Huey of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> stated that the cover version "reworks the original's alienation into a spastic freak-out that's nearly unrecognizable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This version of the song was featured prominently in the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> epic crime film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_(film)" title="Casino (film)">Casino</a></i>.</p>
<p>The quirky music video for the song and several others from this album received significant airplay on the upstart <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>. A notable feature of the video was dancer Craig Allen Rothwell, known as Spazz Attack, whose signature dance move, a forward flip onto his back, drew him significant attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britney_Spears">Britney Spears</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(I_Can%27t_Get_No)_Satisfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Britney Spears">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Oops!..._I_Did_It_Again_(album)" title="Oops!... I Did It Again (album)">Oops!... I Did It Again</a>.</div>
<p>Britney Spears recorded the song with producer <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Jerkins" title="Rodney Jerkins">Rodney "Darkchild" Jerkins</a> for her second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Oops!..._I_Did_It_Again_(album)" title="Oops!... I Did It Again (album)">Oops!... I Did It Again</a></i>, on 24 February 2000 at Pacifique Recording Studios in Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spears' cover received mixed reviews from critics. While reviewing <i>Oops!</i>, Stephen Thomas Erlewine of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> selected the song as Track Pick, describing "the clenched-funk revision of the Stones' deathless 'Satisfaction'" as emblematic of a "bewildering magpie aesthetic" on Spears' early albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> declared the song a 'choice cut,' meaning a good song on an otherwise lackluster album,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> while a <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">New Musical Express</a></i> review gave the cover a negative review, saying, "the long-awaited [...] [Spears'] cover of the Stones' '(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction' is a letdown".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spears first performed the cover on her 2000's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oops!..._I_Did_It_Again_World_Tour" title="Oops!... I Did It Again World Tour">Oops!... I Did It Again World Tour</a>. The performance ended with a dance sequence set to the familiar Richards guitar lick that was omitted from her recorded version (played here by her guitarist "Skip").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitch_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-pitch-56">[56]</a></sup> Spears also performed "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" on the <a href="/wiki/2000_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="2000 MTV Video Music Awards">2000 MTV Video Music Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vma1_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-vma1-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk" title="Björk">Björk</a> and <a href="/wiki/PJ_Harvey" title="PJ Harvey">PJ Harvey</a> duetted on a live cover of the song as a special performance at the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cat_Power" title="Cat Power">Cat Power</a> released an acoustic folk cover of the song on her 2000 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Covers_Record" title="The Covers Record">The Covers Record</a></i>. Her version omits all three of the song's chorus refrains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>

<li>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_(musician)" title="Diesel (musician)">Diesel</a> recorded a version for his [EP] <i><a href="/wiki/7_Axes" title="7 Axes">7 Axes</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
