<p><b>OGLE-TR-111</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_dwarf" title="Yellow dwarf">yellow dwarf</a> star approximately 5,000 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carina_(constellation)" title="Carina (constellation)">Carina</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">Keel</a>). Having an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of about 17, this distant and dim star has not yet been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_catalog" title="Star catalog">cataloged</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMBAD_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-SIMBAD-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_system">Planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OGLE-TR-111&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002 the <a href="/wiki/Optical_Gravitational_Lensing_Experiment" title="Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment">Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment</a> (OGLE) survey detected that the light from the star periodically dimmed very slightly every 4 <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a>, indicating a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>-sized body <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transiting</a> the star. But since the mass of the object had not been measured, it was not clear that it was a true planet, low-mass <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> or something else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Udalski2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Udalski2002-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2004 <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a> measurements showed unambiguously that the transiting body is indeed a planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pont2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pont2004-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The planet is probably very similar to the other "<a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">hot Jupiters</a>" orbiting nearby stars. Its mass is about half that of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and it orbits the star at a distance less than 1/20th that of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005, evidence of another transit was announced. Planet "OGLE-TR-111c" is a possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> orbiting the star. It was first proposed in 2005 based on preliminary evidence from the <a href="/wiki/Optical_Gravitational_Lensing_Experiment" title="Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment">Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment</a> (OGLE) survey. More data is required to confirm this planet candidate. If it is confirmed, OGLE-TR-111 would become one of the first stars with a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transiting planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minniti2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minniti2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
