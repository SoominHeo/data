<p>The <b>P-270 Moskit</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">П-270 «Москит»</span>; English: <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">Mosquito</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> powered <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">Anti-Ship missile</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> designation is <b>3M80</b>, air launched variant is the <b>Kh-41</b> and its <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> is <i><b><a href="/wiki/SS-N-22" title="SS-N-22">SS-N-22 Sunburn</a></b></i>. The missile system was designed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raduga_Design_Bureau" title="Raduga Design Bureau">Raduga Design Bureau</a> during the 1970s as a follow up to the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-N-9" title="SS-N-9">SS-N-9 Siren</a>". The Moskit was originally designed to be ship-launched, but variants have been adapted to be launched from land (modified trucks), underwater (<a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>) and air (reportedly the <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-33" title="Sukhoi Su-33">Sukhoi Su-33</a>, a naval variant of the <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-27" title="Sukhoi Su-27">Sukhoi Su-27</a>), as well as on the <a href="/wiki/Lun-class_ekranoplan" title="Lun-class ekranoplan">Lun-class ekranoplan</a>. The missile can carry conventional and nuclear <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warheads</a>. The exact classification of the missile is unknown, with varying types reported. This uncertainty is due to the secrecy surrounding an active military weapon. The Moskit is one of the missiles known by the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> codename SS-N-22 Sunburn.</p>
<p>The missile has been purchased and exported to the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Navy" title="People's Liberation Army Navy">People's Liberation Army Navy</a> (<a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a> (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-270_Moskit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It reaches a speed of Mach 3 at high altitude and Mach 2.2 at low-altitude. This speed is 4.25 to 3 times more than speed of the subsonic American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_AGM-84_Harpoon" title="Boeing AGM-84 Harpoon">Harpoon</a>. When slower missiles, like the Harpoon or the French <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a> are used, the maximum theoretical response time for the defending ship is 120 to 150 seconds. This long response time provides time to launch countermeasures and employ jamming before deploying "hard" defense systems such as missiles and <a href="/wiki/Close-in_weapon_system" title="Close-in weapon system">close-in weapon systems</a>. But the high speed of the 3M82 "Mosquito" missiles reduce the maximum theoretical response time for the defending ship to 25 to 30 seconds. This short response time makes jamming and countermeasures very difficult, and firing missiles and quick-firing artillery even more difficult. The Moskit was designed to be employed against smaller NATO naval groups in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> (Danish and German) and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> (Turkish) and non-NATO vessels in the Pacific (Japanese, South Korean, etc.), and to defend the Russian mainland against NATO amphibious assault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The missile can perform intensive anti-defense maneuvers with overloads in excess of 10g, which completed for 9 km before the target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new-factoria.ru_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-new-factoria.ru-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Variants of the missile have been designated 3M80M, 3M82 (Moskit M).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The P-270 designation is believed to be the initial product codename for the class of missile, with the Russian Ministry of Defense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_Agency_of_Missiles_and_Artillery_of_the_Ministry_of_Defense_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Main Agency of Missiles and Artillery of the Ministry of Defense of the Soviet Union">GRAU indices</a> (starting with 3M) designating the exact variant of the missile. The 3M80 was its original model. The 3M80M model (also termed 3M80E for export) was a 1984 longer range version of the missile, with the latest version with the longest range being the 3M82 Moskit M. The ASM-MSS / Kh-41 variant is the 1993 air-launched version of the missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 3M80MVE variant has an optional longer 240 km range through a second, high-altitude flight profile setting, however using the higher altitude profile would make the missile detectable at much greater distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
