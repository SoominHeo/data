<p><b>Peer-to-peer</b> (<b>P2P</b>) computing or networking is a distributed application architecture that partitions tasks or workloads between peers. Peers are equally privileged, equipotent participants in the application. They are said to form a peer-to-peer network of nodes.</p>
<p>Peers make a portion of their resources, such as processing power, disk storage or network bandwidth, directly available to other network participants, without the need for central coordination by servers or stable hosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Peers are both suppliers and consumers of resources, in contrast to the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Client-server" title="Client-server">client-server</a> model in which the consumption and supply of resources is divided. Emerging collaborative P2P systems are going beyond the era of peers doing similar things while sharing resources, and are looking for diverse peers that can bring in unique resources and capabilities to a virtual community thereby empowering it to engage in greater tasks beyond those that can be accomplished by individual peers, yet that are beneficial to all the peers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP2P_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CP2P-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While P2P systems had previously been used in many application domains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D._Barkai.2C_2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-D._Barkai.2C_2002-3">[3]</a></sup> the architecture was popularized by the file sharing system <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a>, originally released in 1999. The concept has inspired new structures and philosophies in many areas of human interaction. In such social contexts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_(meme)" title="Peer-to-peer (meme)">peer-to-peer as a meme</a> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networking</a> that has emerged throughout society, enabled by <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> technologies in general.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While P2P systems had previously been used in many application domains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D._Barkai.2C_2002_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-D._Barkai.2C_2002-3">[3]</a></sup> the concept was popularized by file sharing systems such as the music-sharing application <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a> (originally released in 1999).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The peer-to-peer movement allowed millions of Internet users to connect "directly, forming groups and collaborating to become user-created search engines, virtual supercomputers, and filesystems." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oram.2C_A._2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oram.2C_A._2001-4">[4]</a></sup> The basic concept of peer-to-peer computing was envisioned in earlier software systems and networking discussions, reaching back to principles stated in the first <a href="/wiki/Request_for_Comments" title="Request for Comments">Request for Comments</a>, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1" rel="nofollow">RFC 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>'s vision for the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> was close to a P2P network in that it assumed each user of the web would be an active editor and contributor, creating and linking content to form an interlinked "web" of links. The early Internet was more open than present day, where two machines connected to the Internet could send packets to each other without firewalls and other security measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oram.2C_A._2001_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oram.2C_A._2001-4">[4]</a></sup> This contrasts to the <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcasting</a>-like structure of the web as it has developed over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As a precursor to the Internet, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arpanet" title="Arpanet">ARPANET</a> was a successful client-server network where "every participating node could request and serve content." However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arpanet" title="Arpanet">ARPANET</a> was not self-organized, and it lacked the ability to "provide any means for context or content-based routing beyond 'simple' address-based routing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Therefore, a distributed messaging system that is often likened as an early peer-to-peer architecture was established: <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">USENET</a>. <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">USENET</a> was developed in 1979 and is a system that enforces a decentralized model of control. The basic model is a client-server model from the user or client perspective that offers a self-organizing approach to newsgroup servers. However, <a href="/wiki/News_server" title="News server">news servers</a> communicate with one another as peers to propagate <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet news</a> articles over the entire group of network servers. The same consideration applies to <a href="/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol" title="Simple Mail Transfer Protocol">SMTP</a> email in the sense that the core email-relaying network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_transfer_agent" title="Mail transfer agent">mail transfer agents</a> has a peer-to-peer character, while the periphery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail_client" title="E-mail client">e-mail clients</a> and their direct connections is strictly a client-server relationship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In May 1999, with millions more people on the Internet, <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Fanning" title="Shawn Fanning">Shawn Fanning</a> introduced the music and file-sharing application called Napster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16-7">[7]</a></sup> Napster was the beginning of peer-to-peer networks, as we know them today, where "participating users establish a virtual network, entirely independent from the physical network, without having to obey any administrative authorities or restrictions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Steinmetz.2C_R._2005_pp._9-16-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A peer-to-peer network is designed around the notion of equal <i><a href="/wiki/Peer_group_(computer_networking)" title="Peer group (computer networking)">peer</a></i> nodes simultaneously functioning as both "clients" and "servers" to the other nodes on the network. This model of network arrangement differs from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Client%E2%80%93server" title="Client–server">client–server</a> model where communication is usually to and from a central server. A typical example of a file transfer that uses the client-server model is the <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">File Transfer Protocol</a> (FTP) service in which the client and server programs are distinct: the clients initiate the transfer, and the servers satisfy these requests.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Routing_and_resource_discovery">Routing and resource discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Routing and resource discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peer-to-peer networks generally implement some form of virtual <a href="/wiki/Overlay_network" title="Overlay network">overlay network</a> on top of the physical network topology, where the nodes in the overlay form a <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subset</a> of the nodes in the physical network. Data is still exchanged directly over the underlying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TCP/IP" title="TCP/IP">TCP/IP</a> network, but at the <a href="/wiki/Application_layer" title="Application layer">application layer</a> peers are able to communicate with each other directly, via the logical overlay links (each of which corresponds to a path through the underlying physical network). Overlays are used for indexing and peer discovery, and make the P2P system independent from the physical network topology. Based on how the nodes are linked to each other within the overlay network, and how resources are indexed and located, we can classify networks as <i>unstructured</i> or <i>structured</i> (or as a hybrid between the two).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unstructured_networks">Unstructured networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unstructured networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="723" data-file-width="887" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fa/Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/300px-Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fa/Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/450px-Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fa/Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/600px-Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unstructured_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Overlay network diagram for an <b>unstructured P2P network</b>, illustrating the ad hoc nature of the connections between nodes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Unstructured peer-to-peer networks</i> do not impose a particular structure on the overlay network by design, but rather are formed by nodes that randomly form connections to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Gnutella" title="Gnutella">Gnutella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gossip_protocol" title="Gossip protocol">Gossip</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kazaa" title="Kazaa">Kazaa</a> are examples of unstructured P2P protocols).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because there is no structure globally imposed upon them, unstructured networks are easy to build and allow for localized optimizations to different regions of the overlay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Also, because the role of all peers in the network is the same, unstructured networks are highly robust in the face of high rates of "churn"—that is, when large numbers of peers are frequently joining and leaving the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jin-Unstructured-2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jin-Unstructured-2010-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lv-2002_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-lv-2002-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>However the primary limitations of unstructured networks also arise from this lack of structure. In particular, when a peer wants to find a desired piece of data in the network, the search query must be flooded through the network to find as many peers as possible that share the data. Flooding causes a very high amount of signaling traffic in the network, uses more CPU/memory (by requiring every peer to process all search queries), and does not ensure that search queries will always be resolved. Furthermore, since there is no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlation</a> between a peer and the content managed by it, there is no guarantee that flooding will find a peer that has the desired data. Popular content is likely to be available at several peers and any peer searching for it is likely to find the same thing. But if a peer is looking for rare data shared by only a few other peers, then it is highly unlikely that search will be successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Structured_networks">Structured networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structured networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Structured_(DHT)_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="718" data-file-width="996" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/300px-Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/450px-Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png/600px-Structured_%28DHT%29_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Structured_(DHT)_peer-to-peer_network_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Overlay network diagram for a <b>structured P2P network</b>, using a <a href="/wiki/Distributed_hash_table" title="Distributed hash table">distributed hash table</a> (DHT) to identify and locate nodes/resources</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <i>structured peer-to-peer networks</i> the overlay is organized into a specific topology, and the protocol ensures that any node can efficiently<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> search the network for a file/resource, even if the resource is extremely rare.</p>
<p>The most common type of structured P2P networks implement a <a href="/wiki/Distributed_hash_table" title="Distributed hash table">distributed hash table</a> (DHT),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in which a variant of <a href="/wiki/Consistent_hashing" title="Consistent hashing">consistent hashing</a> is used to assign ownership of each file to a particular peer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P2P_API_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-P2P_API-21">[21]</a></sup> This enables peers to search for resources on the network using a <a href="/wiki/Hash_table" title="Hash table">hash table</a>: that is, (<i>key</i>, <i>value</i>) pairs are stored in the DHT, and any participating node can efficiently retrieve the value associated with a given key.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DHT_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="352" data-file-width="840" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/DHT_en.svg/250px-DHT_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/DHT_en.svg/375px-DHT_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/DHT_en.svg/500px-DHT_en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DHT_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Distributed hash tables</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>However, in order to route traffic efficiently through the network, nodes in a structured overlay must maintain lists of neighbors that satisfy specific criteria. This makes them less robust in networks with a high rate of <i>churn</i> (i.e. with large numbers of nodes frequently joining and leaving the network).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lv-2002_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-lv-2002-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> More recent evaluation of P2P resource discovery solutions under real workloads have pointed out several issues in DHT-based solutions such as high cost of advertising/discovering resources and static and dynamic load imbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable distributed networks that use DHTs include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(protocol)" title="BitTorrent (protocol)">BitTorrent's</a> distributed tracker, the <a href="/wiki/Kad_network" title="Kad network">Kad network</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Storm_botnet" title="Storm botnet">Storm botnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/YaCy" title="YaCy">YaCy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Content_Distribution_Network" title="Coral Content Distribution Network">Coral Content Distribution Network</a>. Some prominent research projects include the <a href="/wiki/Chord_(peer-to-peer)" title="Chord (peer-to-peer)">Chord project</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kademlia" title="Kademlia">Kademlia</a>, <a href="/wiki/PAST_storage_utility" title="PAST storage utility">PAST storage utility</a>, <a href="/wiki/P-Grid" title="P-Grid">P-Grid</a>, a self-organized and emerging overlay network, and <a href="/wiki/CoopNet_content_distribution_system" title="CoopNet content distribution system">CoopNet content distribution system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> DHT-based networks have also been widely utilized for accomplishing efficient resource discovery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Grid_computing" title="Grid computing">grid computing</a> systems, as it aids in resource management and scheduling of applications.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_models">Hybrid models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hybrid models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Hybrid models are a combination of peer-to-peer and client-server models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A common hybrid model is to have a central server that helps peers find each other. <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a> is an example of a hybrid model. There are a variety of hybrid models, all of which make trade-offs between the centralized functionality provided by a structured server/client network and the node equality afforded by the pure peer-to-peer unstructured networks. Currently, hybrid models have better performance than either pure unstructured networks or pure structured networks because certain functions, such as searching, do require a centralized functionality but benefit from the decentralized aggregation of nodes provided by unstructured networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_and_trust">Security and trust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Security and trust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peer-to-peer systems pose unique challenges from a <a href="/wiki/Computer_security" title="Computer security">computer security</a> perspective.</p>
<p>Like any other form of <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>, P2P applications can contain <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability_(computing)" title="Vulnerability (computing)">vulnerabilities</a>. What makes this particularly dangerous for P2P software, however, is that peer-to-peer applications act as servers as well as clients, meaning that they can be more vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Exploit_(computer_security)" title="Exploit (computer security)">remote exploits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vu-p2p-principles-p8_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-vu-p2p-principles-p8-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Routing_attacks">Routing attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Routing attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Also, since each node plays a role in routing traffic through the network, malicious users can perform a variety of "routing attacks", or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denial_of_service" title="Denial of service">denial of service</a> attacks. Examples of common routing attacks include "incorrect lookup routing" whereby malicious nodes deliberately forward requests incorrectly or return false results, "incorrect routing updates" where malicious nodes corrupt the routing tables of neighboring nodes by sending them false information, and "incorrect routing network partition" where when new nodes are joining they bootstrap via a malicious node, which places the new node in a partition of the network that is populated by other malicious nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrupted_data_and_malware">Corrupted data and malware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Corrupted data and malware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Data_validation" title="Data validation">Data validation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">Malware</a></div>
<p>The prevalence of <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> varies between different peer-to-peer protocols. Studies analyzing the spread of malware on P2P networks found, for example, that 63% of the answered download requests on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limewire" title="Limewire">Limewire</a> network contained some form of malware, whereas only 3% of the content on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OpenFT" title="OpenFT">OpenFT</a> contained malware. In both cases, the top three most common types of malware accounted for the large majority of cases (99% in Limewire, and 65% in OpenFT). Another study analyzing traffic on the <a href="/wiki/Kazaa" title="Kazaa">Kazaa</a> network found that 15% of the 500,000 file sample taken were infected by one or more of the 365 different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_viruses" title="Computer viruses">computer viruses</a> that were tested for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corrupted data can also be distributed on P2P networks by modifying files that are already being shared on the network. For example, on the <a href="/wiki/FastTrack" title="FastTrack">FastTrack</a> network, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> managed to introduce faked chunks into downloads and downloaded files (mostly <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> files). Files infected with the RIAA virus were unusable afterwards and contained malicious code. The RIAA is also known to have uploaded fake music and movies to P2P networks in order to deter illegal file sharing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Consequently, the P2P networks of today have seen an enormous increase of their security and file verification mechanisms. Modern <a href="/wiki/Hash_chain" title="Hash chain">hashing</a>, <a href="/wiki/File_verification" title="File verification">chunk verification</a> and different encryption methods have made most networks resistant to almost any type of attack, even when major parts of the respective network have been replaced by faked or nonfunctional hosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resilient_and_scalable_computer_networks">Resilient and scalable computer networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Resilient and scalable computer networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wireless_mesh_network" title="Wireless mesh network">Wireless mesh network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">Distributed computing</a></div>
<p>The decentralized nature of P2P networks increases robustness because it removes the <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">single point of failure</a> that can be inherent in a client-server based system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ms-overlay-survey_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ms-overlay-survey-36">[36]</a></sup> As nodes arrive and demand on the system increases, the total capacity of the system also increases, and the likelihood of failure decreases. If one peer on the network fails to function properly, the whole network is not compromised or damaged. In contrast, in a typical client–server architecture, clients share only their demands with the system, but not their resources. In this case, as more clients join the system, fewer resources are available to serve each client, and if the central server fails, the entire network is taken down.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributed_storage_and_search">Distributed storage and search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Distributed storage and search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yacy-resultados.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="736" data-file-width="907" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Yacy-resultados.png/300px-Yacy-resultados.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Yacy-resultados.png/450px-Yacy-resultados.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Yacy-resultados.png/600px-Yacy-resultados.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yacy-resultados.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Search results for the query "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_libre" title="Software libre">software libre</a>", using <a href="/wiki/YaCy" title="YaCy">YaCy</a> a free <a href="/wiki/Distributed_search_engine" title="Distributed search engine">distributed search engine</a> that runs on a peer-to-peer network instead making requests to centralized index servers (like <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yahoo" title="Yahoo">Yahoo</a>, and other corporate search engines)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are both advantages and disadvantages in P2P networks related to the topic of data backup, recovery, and availability. In a centralized network, the system administrators are the only forces controlling the availability of files being shared. If the administrators decide to no longer distribute a file, they simply have to remove it from their servers, and it will no longer be available to users. Along with leaving the users powerless in deciding what is distributed throughout the community, this makes the entire system vulnerable to threats and requests from the government and other large forces. For example, YouTube has been pressured by the RIAA, MPAA, and entertainment industry to filter out copyrighted content. Although server-client networks are able to monitor and manage content availability, they can have more stability in the availability of the content they choose to host. A client should not have trouble accessing obscure content that is being shared on a stable centralized network. P2P networks, however, are more unreliable in sharing unpopular files because sharing files in a P2P network requires that at least one node in the network has the requested data, and that node must be able to connect to the node requesting the data. This requirement is occasionally hard to meet because users may delete or stop sharing data at any point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In this sense, the community of users in a P2P network is completely responsible for deciding what content is available. Unpopular files will eventually disappear and become unavailable as more people stop sharing them. Popular files, however, will be highly and easily distributed. Popular files on a P2P network actually have more stability and availability than files on central networks. In a centralized network a simple loss of connection between the server and clients is enough to cause a failure, but in P2P networks the connections between every node must be lost in order to cause a data sharing failure. In a centralized system, the administrators are responsible for all data recovery and backups, while in P2P systems, each node requires its own backup system. Because of the lack of central authority in P2P networks, forces such as the recording industry, RIAA, MPAA, and the government are unable to delete or stop the sharing of content on P2P systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Content_delivery">Content delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Content delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In P2P networks, clients both provide and use resources. This means that unlike client-server systems, the content serving capacity of peer-to-peer networks can actually <i>increase</i> as more users begin to access the content (especially with protocols such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bittorrent" title="Bittorrent">Bittorrent</a> that require users to share, refer a performance measurement study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>). This property is one of the major advantages of using P2P networks because it makes the setup and running costs very small for the original content distributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="File-sharing_networks">File-sharing networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: File-sharing networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many file <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer file sharing</a> networks, such as <a href="/wiki/Gnutella" title="Gnutella">Gnutella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gnutella2" title="Gnutella2">G2</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/EDonkey_network" title="EDonkey network">eDonkey network</a> popularized peer-to-peer technologies.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Content_delivery_network#Peer-to-peer_CDNs" title="Content delivery network">Peer-to-peer content delivery networks</a>.</li>
<li>Peer-to-peer content services, e.g. caches for improved performance such as Correli Caches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>Software publication and distribution (<a href="/wiki/Linux_distribution" title="Linux distribution">Linux distribution</a>, several games); via <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> networks.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_infringements">Copyright infringements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Copyright infringements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Peer-to-peer networking involves data transfer from one user to another without using an intermediate server. Companies developing P2P applications have been involved in numerous legal cases, primarily in the United States, over conflicts with <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-43">[43]</a></sup> Two major cases are <i><a href="/wiki/Grokster" title="Grokster">Grokster</a> vs RIAA</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/MGM_Studios,_Inc._v._Grokster,_Ltd." title="MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.">MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.cnet.com_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.cnet.com-44">[44]</a></sup> In both of the cases the file sharing technology was ruled to be legal as long as the developers had no ability to prevent the sharing of the copyrighted material.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_implications">Social implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Social implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_peer-to-peer_processes" title="Social peer-to-peer processes">Social peer-to-peer processes</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incentivizing_resource_sharing_and_cooperation">Incentivizing resource sharing and cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Incentivizing resource sharing and cooperation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uberisation" title="Uberisation">Uberisation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torrentcomp_small.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="357" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Torrentcomp_small.gif/300px-Torrentcomp_small.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/Torrentcomp_small.gif 1.5x" width="300"/></a>

<b>The <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a> protocol</b>: In this animation, the colored bars beneath all of the 7 clients in the upper region above represent the file being shared, with each color representing an individual piece of the file. After the initial pieces transfer from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_(BitTorrent)" title="Seed (BitTorrent)">seed</a> (large system at the bottom), the pieces are individually transferred from client to client. The original seeder only needs to send out one copy of the file for all the clients to receive a copy.</div>

<p>Cooperation among a community of participants is key to the continued success of P2P systems aimed at casual human users; these reach their full potential only when large numbers of nodes contribute resources. But in current practice P2P networks often contain large numbers of users who utilize resources shared by other nodes, but who do not share anything themselves (often referred to as the "freeloader problem"). Freeloading can have a profound impact on the network and in some cases can cause the community to collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In these types of networks "users have natural disincentives to cooperate because cooperation consumes their own resources and may degrade their own performance." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feldman.2C_M._2004.2C_pp._102-111_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feldman.2C_M._2004.2C_pp._102-111-47">[47]</a></sup> Studying the social attributes of P2P networks is challenging due to large populations of turnover, asymmetry of interest and zero-cost identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feldman.2C_M._2004.2C_pp._102-111_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feldman.2C_M._2004.2C_pp._102-111-47">[47]</a></sup> A variety of incentive mechanisms have been implemented to encourage or even force nodes to contribute resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some researchers have explored the benefits of enabling virtual communities to self-organize and introduce incentives for resource sharing and cooperation, arguing that the social aspect missing from today's P2P systems should be seen both as a goal and a means for self-organized virtual communities to be built and fostered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Ongoing research efforts for designing effective incentive mechanisms in P2P systems, based on principles from game theory, are beginning to take on a more psychological and information-processing direction.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_and_anonymity">Privacy and anonymity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Privacy and anonymity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some peer-to-peer networks (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Freenet" title="Freenet">Freenet</a>) place a heavy emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymity</a>—that is, ensuring that the contents of communications are hidden from eavesdroppers, and that the identities/locations of the participants are concealed. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_key_cryptography" title="Public key cryptography">Public key cryptography</a> can be used to provide <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_validation" title="Data validation">data validation</a>, authorization, and authentication for data/messages. <a href="/wiki/Onion_routing" title="Onion routing">Onion routing</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mix_network" title="Mix network">mix network</a> protocols (e.g. Tarzan) can be used to provide anonymity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellectual_property_law_and_illegal_sharing">Intellectual property law and illegal sharing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Intellectual property law and illegal sharing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although peer-to-peer networks can be used for legitimate purposes, rights holders have targeted peer-to-peer over the involvement with sharing copyrighted material. Peer-to-peer networking involves data transfer from one user to another without using an intermediate server. Companies developing P2P applications have been involved in numerous legal cases, primarily in the United States, primarily over issues surrounding <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-43">[43]</a></sup> Two major cases are <i><a href="/wiki/Grokster" title="Grokster">Grokster</a> vs RIAA</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/MGM_Studios,_Inc._v._Grokster,_Ltd." title="MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.">MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.cnet.com_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.cnet.com-44">[44]</a></sup> In both of the cases the file sharing technology was ruled to be legal as long as the developers had no ability to prevent the sharing of the copyrighted material. To establish criminal liability for the copyright infringement on peer-to-peer systems, the government must prove that the defendant infringed a copyright willingly for the purpose of personal financial gain or commercial advantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">Fair use</a> exceptions allow limited use of copyrighted material to be downloaded without acquiring permission from the rights holders. These documents are usually news reporting or under the lines of research and scholarly work. Controversies have developed over the concern of illegitimate use of peer-to-peer networks regarding public safety and national security. When a file is downloaded through a peer-to-peer network, it is impossible to know who created the file or what users are connected to the network at a given time. Trustworthiness of sources is a potential security threat that can be seen with peer-to-peer systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_neutrality">Network neutrality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peer-to-peer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Network neutrality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peer-to-peer applications present one of the core issues in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_neutrality" title="Network neutrality">network neutrality</a> controversy. Internet service providers (<a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">ISPs</a>) have been known to throttle P2P file-sharing traffic due to its high-<a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newteevee.com_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-newteevee.com-53">[53]</a></sup> Compared to Web browsing, e-mail or many other uses of the internet, where data is only transferred in short intervals and relative small quantities, P2P file-sharing often consists of relatively heavy bandwidth usage due to ongoing file transfers and swarm/network coordination packets. In October 2007, <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>, one of the largest broadband Internet providers in the United States, started blocking P2P applications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(protocol)" title="BitTorrent (protocol)">BitTorrent</a>. Their rationale was that P2P is mostly used to share illegal content, and their infrastructure is not designed for continuous, high-bandwidth traffic. Critics point out that P2P networking has legitimate legal uses, and that this is another way that large providers are trying to control use and content on the Internet, and direct people towards a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Client-server" title="Client-server">client-server</a>-based application architecture. The client-server model provides financial barriers-to-entry to small publishers and individuals, and can be less efficient for sharing large files. As a reaction to this <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_throttling" title="Bandwidth throttling">bandwidth throttling</a>, several P2P applications started implementing protocol obfuscation, such as the <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_protocol_encryption" title="BitTorrent protocol encryption">BitTorrent protocol encryption</a>. Techniques for achieving "protocol obfuscation" involves removing otherwise easily identifiable properties of protocols, such as deterministic byte sequences and packet sizes, by making the data look as if it were random.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breaking_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-breaking-54">[54]</a></sup> The ISP's solution to the high bandwidth is <a href="/wiki/P2P_caching" title="P2P caching">P2P caching</a>, where an ISP stores the part of files most accessed by P2P clients in order to save access to the Internet.</p>

<p>Researchers have used computer simulations to aid in understanding and evaluating the complex behaviors of individuals within the network. "Networking research often relies on simulation in order to test and evaluate new ideas. An important requirement of this process is that results must be reproducible so that other researchers can replicate, validate, and extend existing work." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basu.2C_A._2013_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basu.2C_A._2013-55">[55]</a></sup> If the research cannot be reproduced, then the opportunity for further research is hindered. "Even though new simulators continue to be released, the research community tends towards only a handful of open-source simulators. The demand for features in simulators, as shown by our criteria and survey, is high. Therefore, the community should work together to get these features in open-source software. This would reduce the need for custom simulators, and hence increase repeatability and reputability of experiments." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basu.2C_A._2013_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Basu.2C_A._2013-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
