<p>The <b>PC Engine GT</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">PCエンジンGT</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handheld_video_game_console" title="Handheld video game console">handheld video game console</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_Home_Electronics" title="NEC Home Electronics">NEC Home Electronics</a>, released in Japan in late 1990 and later in the United States as the <b>TurboExpress</b>. It is essentially a portable version of the <a href="/wiki/TurboGrafx-16" title="TurboGrafx-16">TurboGrafx-16/PC Engine</a> home console that came two to three years earlier. Its launch price in Japan was ¥44,800, whereas in the U.S. was $249.99, briefly raising to $299.99, soon dropping back to $249.99, and $199.99 by 1992.</p>
<p>The TurboExpress was technically advanced at the time, able to play all the TurboGrafx-16's <a href="/wiki/HuCard" title="HuCard">HuCard</a> games, and also featuring a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_tuner" title="TV tuner">TV tuner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It has a 66 mm (2.6 in.) screen, the same size as the original <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a>, and can display 64 <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(computer_graphics)" title="Sprite (computer graphics)">sprites</a> at once, 16 per scanline, in up to 481 colors from a palette of 512.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has 8 kilobytes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_Access_Memory" title="Random Access Memory">RAM</a>. The Turbo runs its HuC6280 <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> at 1.79 or 7.16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a>.</p>
<p>The TurboExpress primarily competed with <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Game_Gear" title="Game Gear">Game Gear</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atari_Lynx" title="Atari Lynx">Atari Lynx</a>. However, with 1.5 million units sold, far behind its two main competitors, NEC failed to gain significant sales or market share in the handheld market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamepro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamepro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TurboExpress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PC_Engine_GT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/PC_Engine_GT.jpg/220px-PC_Engine_GT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/PC_Engine_GT.jpg/330px-PC_Engine_GT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/PC_Engine_GT.jpg/440px-PC_Engine_GT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PC_Engine_GT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The PC Engine GT</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to a problem with cheap capacitors, sound failure is a frequent problem with the TurboExpress, sometimes even in new systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The screen used in the TurboExpress was another source for problems, though it was state of the art when it was released. The LCD technology used was still fairly new and the rate of pixel failure was very high. Brand-new TurboExpress systems often had several bad pixels. Text is also difficult or impossible to read in certain circumstances, as many times fonts were written to be seen on a television screen, not on a small LCD screen. As a result, certain RPGs and adventure games can be difficult to play on the unit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some TurboGrafx-16 HuCards save game data to the internal memory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TurboGrafx-CD" title="TurboGrafx-CD">TurboGrafx-CD</a> unit, <a href="/wiki/TurboDuo" title="TurboDuo">TurboDuo</a>, or TurboBooster Plus (a peripheral for the core TG-16 console). The TurboExpress lacks this internal memory, and as a result it is not capable of saving in this manner. Most games provide a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Password_(video_games)" title="Password (video games)">password save</a> mechanism as an alternative.</p>
<p>The battery life is about three hours for 6 AA batteries. This is also a problem for other color and backlit or sidelit handhelds of the period, such as the <a href="/wiki/Game_Gear" title="Game Gear">Game Gear</a> at 5–6 hours, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Nomad" title="Sega Nomad">Sega Nomad</a> at 2–3 hours, and the <a href="/wiki/Atari_Lynx" title="Atari Lynx">Atari Lynx</a> at more than 4 hours.</p>

<ul>
<li>CPU: HuC6280</li>
<li>CPU speed: 7.16 MHz or 1.79 MHz (switchable in software)</li>

<li>Max colors: 512</li>

</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="TurboLink">TurboLink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TurboExpress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: TurboLink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>TurboLink</i> allows two-player play. <i>Falcon</i>, a flight simulator, includes a "head-to-head" dogfight mode that can only be accessed via TurboLink, same as Bomberman 93 Com-Link multiplayer. However, very few TG-16 games offer co-op play modes especially designed with the TurboExpress in mind.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="TurboVision">TurboVision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TurboExpress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: TurboVision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turbo_express.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="300" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Turbo_express.jpg/200px-Turbo_express.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/be/Turbo_express.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turbo_express.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A TurboExpress with the TurboVision TV tuner, along with some games</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>TurboVision is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_tuner" title="TV tuner">TV tuner</a> adapter for the TurboExpress. It allowed a player to either watch television, or go back to playing games with the flip of a switch; in other words, one may use the TurboExpress as a video monitor. It includes an RCA audio/video input for external <a href="/wiki/Composite_video" title="Composite video">composite video</a> signals. However, due to the adoption of the High Definition Television <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> digital standard, this adapter will no longer function as a television due to the lack of any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> digital processing circuitry (this tuner can only process an <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">analog signal</a> for television). Due to this limitation, the TV tuner adapter is now relegated to a collectible for most people although its RCA audio/video input function (albeit very limited with its low screen resolution) is still operational with the correct cables.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TurboExpress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Gaming_World" title="Computer Gaming World">Computer Gaming World</a></i> favorably compared TurboExpress to Game Boy, but stated that the NEC handheld "gobbles power like crazy ... almost forcing players to <i>immediately</i> purchase an AC adapter". The magazine nonetheless praised its compatibility with TurboGrafx games, and concluded that "to see this machine in action is to fall in love with it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams199101_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams199101-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regardless of its technical advantages upon its release, the Turbo Express was not widely recognized or adopted by gamers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In addition to NEC's marketing issues, the handheld was initially released for $299.99, significantly higher than popular competitors. Because of this price tag it was labeled as the "Rolls Royce of handheld systems".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TurboExpress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The TurboExpress appears in the movies <i><a href="/wiki/3_Ninjas" title="3 Ninjas">3 Ninjas</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Enemy_of_the_State_(film)" title="Enemy of the State (film)">Enemy of the State</a></i>, with the latter appearance taking place well after the system's demise. It also appears in the television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doogie_Howser,_M.D" title="Doogie Howser, M.D">Doogie Howser, M.D</a></i>.</p>
