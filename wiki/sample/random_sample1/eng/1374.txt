<p><b>PRS Guitars</b> (also known as <b>Paul Reed Smith Guitars</b>) is an American guitar manufacturer headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Stevensville,_Maryland" title="Stevensville, Maryland">Stevensville, Maryland</a>, founded by guitarist and master <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthier</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Reed_Smith" title="Paul Reed Smith">Paul Reed Smith</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>PRS has a reputation as a manufacturer of high end <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitars</a> made in the US, and they are known for their custom shop instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-1">[1]</a></sup> However, since the 1990s they have expanded production to Asia, where they manufacture the more affordable 'SE' line of instruments. As of 2013 they have begun making more affordable guitars in the US with their 'S2' line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> PRS Guitars also manufactures <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guitar_amplifiers" title="Guitar amplifiers">guitar amplifiers</a>. Two of PRS's most notable endorsers are <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustavo_Cerati" title="Gustavo Cerati">Gustavo Cerati</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2002-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition to this, many other artists endorse the company, including <a href="/wiki/Mike_Oldfield" title="Mike Oldfield">Mike Oldfield</a>, who has used a PRS Artist Custom 24 for studio recordings and live shows since the late 1980s; <a href="/wiki/Clint_Lowery" title="Clint Lowery">Clint Lowery</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mikael_%C3%85kerfeldt" title="Mikael Åkerfeldt">Mikael Åkerfeldt</a>; <a href="/wiki/Paul_Allender" title="Paul Allender">Paul Allender</a>; <a href="/wiki/Dave_Navarro" title="Dave Navarro">Dave Navarro</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ted_Nugent" title="Ted Nugent">Ted Nugent</a>; <a href="/wiki/Al_Di_Meola" title="Al Di Meola">Al Di Meola</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mark_Tremonti" title="Mark Tremonti">Mark Tremonti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Wilson" title="Steven Wilson">Steven Wilson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orianthi" title="Orianthi">Orianthi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Paul Reed Smith was a guitar player who began building guitars while he was in college in the mid-1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Early guitar players to use his hand-built guitars were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanley_Whitaker" title="Stanley Whitaker">Stanley Whitaker</a> (of Happy The Man), <a href="/wiki/Derek_St._Holmes" title="Derek St. Holmes">Derek St. Holmes</a> (of Ted Nugent's band) and <a href="/wiki/Howard_Leese" title="Howard Leese">Howard Leese</a> (then with <a href="/wiki/Heart_(band)" title="Heart (band)">Heart</a>), but Smith's big break came when <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a> began playing the still hand-built guitars, which at the time looked like a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Gibson Les Paul</a> with a double cut-away and were made of expensive woods like <a href="/wiki/Flame_maple" title="Flame maple">curly maple</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marten2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marten2009-5">[5]</a></sup> He used the custom as a <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototype</a> to raise orders on the road worth nearly $300,000. Smith set up a limited partnership with his wife Barbara and business man Warren Esanu, and set up a factory in Virginia Avenue, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> Smith produced 20 guitars for the 1985 <a href="/wiki/NAMM_Show" title="NAMM Show">NAMM Show</a> (those guitars are now known as the "NAMM 20"), and managed to find a niche in the guitar market: the mid-1980s was the time of the <a href="/wiki/Superstrat" title="Superstrat">Superstrat</a>, and there was little competition for the higher-quality, upscale guitars PRS was building, guitars with "an elegant, modern, vintage-inspired design--the kind of guitar one might have expected Fenders and Gibsons to evolve into".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gruhn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gruhn-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At first there were eight workers taking up one third of the building which they shared with a furniture-stripping shop. After three years PRS had taken over most of the building, with 45 people producing 15 guitars every day, and as time progressed a separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodshop" title="Woodshop">woodshop</a> was added. By 1995 the factory was making 25-30 guitars daily and employed 80 people. In 1996 production moved to a new factory in <a href="/wiki/Stevensville,_Maryland" title="Stevensville, Maryland">Stevensville</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent_Island" title="Kent Island">Kent Island</a>, just across a bridge from Annapolis. By the end of 1998 Prs was producing 700 guitars a month with a staff of 110 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bodies of most PRS guitars are crafted of <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> top on most models. They often feature highly figured tops, including <a href="/wiki/Flame_maple" title="Flame maple">flame maple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quilt_maple" title="Quilt maple">quilt maple</a> and figured maple creating the effect of tiger stripes. A small number of bodies feature white Limba, referred to as Korina by luthiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> PRS necks are usually made from mahogany, although some models feature <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> or Indian or Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Rosewood" title="Rosewood">rosewood</a> necks; <a href="/wiki/Fingerboard" title="Fingerboard">fingerboards</a> on production models are normally made of rosewood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fingerboard_inlays">Fingerboard inlays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fingerboard inlays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PRS's signature fret markers include the standard moon inlays and the optional and sought after birds, often fashioned from <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearl</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paua" title="Paua">paua</a> (<a href="/wiki/Abalone" title="Abalone">abalone</a>) shell. The moons appear similar to standard dot inlays, but have a crescent more prominent than the rest of the dot. The bird inlays feature representations of the following species; <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a> (3rd fret), marsh hawk (5th fret), ruby throated hummingbird (7th fret), <a href="/wiki/Common_tern" title="Common tern">common tern</a> (9th fret), Coopers hawk (12th fret), <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a> (15th fret), <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrow</a> (17th fret), <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> (19th fret), a landing <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawk</a> (21st fret) and a <a href="/wiki/Screech_owl" title="Screech owl">screech owl</a> on a branch (24-fret models only).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 20th and 25th anniversary edition production models feature updated and stylized versions of this standard inlay.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".2210-Top.22_guitars">"10-Top" guitars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "10-Top" guitars'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very small percentage of maple trees cut in North America are actually figured. According to their website, to make "10-Top" status, a PRS top must have "clearly defined figure across its entire top with no 'dead' spots".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As such, the term "10 Top" refers to specimens of maple with distinctive and notable figure, as opposed to a specific grade of timber.</p>
<p>PRS guitars which feature a "10 Top" feature a written or stamped "10" on the upper right headstock (when viewed from behind).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Close-up of the 5th, 7th and 9th fret bird inlays.</div>

<p>Nuts are synthetic and tuners are of PRS's own design, although some models feature Korean-made <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kluson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kluson (page does not exist)">Kluson</a>-style tuners. PRS guitars feature three original bridge designs: a one-piece pre-intonated stoptail, vibrato, and wrapover tailpiece.The Vibrato was designed with the help of guitar engineer John Mann. It was an update on the classic Fender vibrato and used cam-locking tuners, which offered wide pitch bending with exceptional tuning stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> The pre-intonated stoptail is unique to PRS, however, this design does not allow intonation to be adjusted to compensate for variations in string thickness or drop tuning. The PRS <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> resembles a vintage <a href="/wiki/Fender_Stratocaster" title="Fender Stratocaster">Fender Stratocaster</a> unit, and the more recent compensated wrapover tailpiece allows for minimal intonation adjustment. An adjustable wrapover bridge is available as an extra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pickups">Pickups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pickups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pick_up_(music_technology)" title="Pick up (music technology)">Pickups</a> are designed and wound in-house. While most of the pickups are humbuckers, some are actually a pair of single coils wound in opposing directions, one intended for the neck and one for the bridge position. Through the use of a unique rotary pickup selector switch, PRS pickups offer 5 different sounds: a combination of thick <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humbucking" title="Humbucking">humbucking</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibson" title="Gibson">Gibson</a>-like tones, and thinner single-coil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocaster" title="Stratocaster">Stratocaster</a>-like tones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> The standard <a href="/wiki/Treble_(sound)" title="Treble (sound)">treble</a> and standard bass pick ups use magnetic pole pieces in the non-adjustable inner coil, and a rear-placed feeder magnet in order to achieve a more authentic single-coil tone when split by the rotary switch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>PRS developed pickups for the aggressive rock market, offering pick ups such as the chainsaw, and the Hot-Fat-Screams (HFS) initially used on the Special model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> The Vintage Treble and Vintage Bass humbuckers were used on the Classic Electric Model, and a combination of HFS and Vintage bass pick ups were used on the CE maple top models, the Standard, and The Custom models.</p>
<p>In 1998 an electronic upgrade kit was released for pre-1993 instruments which included, lighter weight tuner buttons, nickel-plated brass screws for saddles and <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(music)" title="Intonation (music)">intonation</a>, a simulated tone control, and a high capacitance hook up wire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2012 PRS released the 408 pickups used on the 408 and Paul's Guitar models. These pickups include innovations that allow no loss of volume when in coil split mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRS_408_Pickups_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRS_408_Pickups-12">[12]</a></sup> They have an exclusive agreement to use wire drawn from the same machine that made wire for Les Paul and Stratocaster pickups in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRS_Pickup_Wire_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRS_Pickup_Wire-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SE_models">SE models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: SE models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PRS introduced a new, affordable, line of guitars in 2000 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> referred to as the "SE" which are manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> by World Musical Instrument Co. Contrary to popular belief,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> SE does not stand for student edition. The incorrect assumption was even published in the PRS Book. PRS produce a large range of models in the SE series including the Custom 24, SE245, SE Kestrel &amp; Kingfisher bass guitars as well as signature guitars such as the Bernie Marsden, Tremonti, Zach Myers &amp; Santana amongst others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="S2_Series">S2 Series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: S2 Series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, PRS added the <a class="external text" href="http://prsguitars.com/s2series/about/" rel="nofollow">S2 Series</a>, which added a more affordable American PRS that are built in their Maryland factory. The S2 series features proprietary pickups and hardware and a more beveled and flat top, as opposed to the arched top of the Private Stock. Currently the S2 lineup has a Standard 22, 24 and Singlecut, the Custom 22, 24 and 22 Semi-hollow, Singlecut and Singlecut Semi-hollow, Starla, Mira and Mira Semi-hollow. Also in 2015, PRS added the S2 Vela.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dragon_models">The Dragon models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Dragon models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992 PRS introduced the Dragon 1 model. Only 50 units were produced. It featured an intricate dragon <a href="/wiki/Inlay" title="Inlay">inlay</a> which ran down the finger board, a wide 22 fret neck, and a non-vibrato Stop-tail bridge and a new pick up design. The changes in design from previous models added a noticeable tonal improvement which led the company to use the same characteristics in later models such as the PRS Custom 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> The Dragon 2 was released in 1993, and the Dragon 3 in 1994. Both featured dragon inlays which became more extreme with every year. Only 100 of each of the 2 models were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1999 PRS released the Dragon 2000, which featured complex body curves, and a 3 dimensional dragon inlay. Just 50 Dragon 2000's were ever produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electric_Guitars_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Guitars-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_Stock">Private Stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Private Stock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, PRS began producing highly customized versions of the guitars dubbed, "Guitars of the Month." Each featured select ultra-grade woods and custom finishes. In 1996, PRS released the first "Private Stock" guitar. Today, the Private Stock team produces one-of-a-kind pieces costing upwards of $10,000. Each guitar is built with unique finishes, custom inlays, and unparalleled detail. Whereas a standard production guitar has an average build time of six hours, one Private Stock acoustic guitar can take up to three weeks to complete. Many premium exotic woods, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Rosewood" title="Brazilian Rosewood">Brazilian Rosewood</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quilted_Maple&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quilted Maple (page does not exist)">Quilted Maple</a>, are commonly used. Notable Private Stock players include <a href="/wiki/Dweezil_Zappa" title="Dweezil Zappa">Dweezil Zappa</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Mayer" title="John Mayer">John Mayer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neal_Schon" title="Neal Schon">Neal Schon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factory_Tours">Factory Tours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Factory Tours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PRS Guitars conduct an hour long factory tour all year long. This tour is free of charge, and covers the electric guitar manufacturing line, amplifier department and the acoustic shop. Tours are limited to groups of eight and are reserved on a first-come, first-served basis. There is also a museum and the 'West Street East' store where one can buy PRS apparel, parts and accessories. Tours are offered on Tuesdays and Thursdays at 10:00 AM and 1:00 PM EST. The factory is located at their headquarters on Kent Island, Stevensville, MD, United States. The factory also has a store full of their current offerings where one can actually play the guitars. Group tours are also available on request. More details can be found <a class="external text" href="http://www.prsguitars.com/tours/" rel="nofollow">here</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PRS_Guitars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001, PRS released their "Singlecut" guitar, which bore some resemblance to the venerable <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Les Paul</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">Gibson Guitar Corporation</a> filed a trademark infringement lawsuit against Paul Reed Smith. An injunction was ordered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-injunction_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-injunction-16">[16]</a></sup> and PRS ceased manufacturing of the Singlecut at the end of 2004. Federal District Court Judge William J. Haynes, in a 57-page decision ruled "that PRS [Paul Reed Smith] was imitating the Les Paul" and gave the parties ninety days "to complete any discovery on damages or disgorgement of PRS's profits on the sales of its offending Singlecut guitar."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-injunction_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-injunction-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed the lower court decision and ordered the dismissal of Gibson's suit against PRS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The decision also immediately vacated the injunction prohibiting the sale and production of PRS’s Singlecut Guitar. PRS announced that it would immediately resume production of its Singlecut guitars.</p>
<p>Gibson subsequently tried and failed to have the case reheard by all sixteen active Sixth Circuit judges (denied in December 2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and then by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> (denied June 2006),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which was their last chance to have their original injunction upheld.</p>
<p>While no changes to the design of the Singlecut occurred as a result of the lawsuit (given that Gibson lost), some Singlecut owners and sellers have adopted the term 'pre-lawsuit' to differentiate their Singlecut from others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
