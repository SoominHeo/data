<p>The <b>United States presidential election of 1884</b> was the 25th quadrennial <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election" title="United States presidential election">presidential election</a>, held on Tuesday, November 4, 1884. It saw the first election of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Democratic_Party" title="United States Democratic Party">Democrat</a> as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> since the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1856" title="United States presidential election, 1856">election of 1856</a>. The campaign was marred by exceptional political acrimony and personal invective.</p>
<p>New York Governor <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> narrowly defeated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican</a> former United States Senator <a href="/wiki/James_G._Blaine" title="James G. Blaine">James G. Blaine</a> of Maine to break the longest losing streak for any major party in American political history: six consecutive presidential elections.</p>
<p>New York decided the election, awarding Governor Cleveland the state's 36 electors by a margin of just 1,047 votes <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> out of 1,171,312 cast. It was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_narrow_elections" title="List of narrow elections">narrowest U.S. Presidential election</a> until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2000_U.S._Presidential_election" title="2000 U.S. Presidential election">2000</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominations">Nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republican_Party_nomination">Republican Party nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Republican Party nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1884_Republican_National_Convention" title="1884 Republican National Convention">1884 Republican National Convention</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1884RepublicanPoster.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="1000" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/1884RepublicanPoster.png/220px-1884RepublicanPoster.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/1884RepublicanPoster.png/330px-1884RepublicanPoster.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/1884RepublicanPoster.png/440px-1884RepublicanPoster.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1884RepublicanPoster.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/James_G._Blaine" title="James G. Blaine">Blaine</a>/<a href="/wiki/John_A._Logan" title="John A. Logan">Logan</a> campaign poster</div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/1884_Republican_National_Convention" title="1884 Republican National Convention">1884 Republican National Convention</a> was held in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Illinois, on June 3–6, with former Speaker of the House James G. Blaine from Maine, <a href="/wiki/Chester_A._Arthur" title="Chester A. Arthur">President Arthur</a>, and Senator <a href="/wiki/George_F._Edmunds" title="George F. Edmunds">George F. Edmunds</a> from Vermont as the frontrunners. Though he was still popular, Arthur did not make a serious bid for a full-term nomination, knowing that his increasing health problems meant he would probably not survive a second term (he ultimately died in November 1886). Blaine led on the first ballot, with Arthur second, and Edmunds third. This order did not change on successive ballots as Blaine increased his lead, and he won a majority on the fourth ballot. After nominating Blaine, the convention chose Senator <a href="/wiki/John_A._Logan" title="John A. Logan">John A. Logan</a> from Illinois as the vice-presidential nominee. Blaine remains the only Presidential nominee ever to come from Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Famed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Civil_war" title="American Civil war">Civil War</a> general <a href="/wiki/William_Tecumseh_Sherman" title="William Tecumseh Sherman">William Tecumseh Sherman</a> was considered a possible Republican candidate, but ruled himself out with what has become known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sherman_pledge" title="Sherman pledge">Sherman pledge</a>: "If drafted, I will not run; if nominated, I will not accept; if elected, I will not serve." <a href="/wiki/Robert_Todd_Lincoln" title="Robert Todd Lincoln">Robert Todd Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War of the United States</a>, and son of the past President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>, was also strongly courted by politicians and the media of the day to seek the presidential or vice-presidential nomination. Lincoln however was as averse to the nomination as Sherman was.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Democratic_Party_nomination">Democratic Party nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Democratic Party nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1884_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1884 Democratic National Convention">1884 Democratic National Convention</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1884DemocraticPoster.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="612" data-file-width="915" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1884DemocraticPoster.png/220px-1884DemocraticPoster.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1884DemocraticPoster.png/330px-1884DemocraticPoster.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1884DemocraticPoster.png/440px-1884DemocraticPoster.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1884DemocraticPoster.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Cleveland</a>/<a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Hendricks" title="Thomas A. Hendricks">Hendricks</a> campaign poster</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Democrats convened in Chicago on July 8–11, 1884, with New York Governor Grover Cleveland as clear frontrunner, the candidate of northern reformers and sound-money men (as opposed to inflationists). Although <a href="/wiki/Tammany_Hall" title="Tammany Hall">Tammany Hall</a> bitterly opposed his nomination, the machine represented a minority of the New York delegation. Its only chance to block Cleveland was to break the unit rule, which mandated that the votes of an entire delegation be cast for only one candidate, and this it failed to do. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_N._Lockwood" title="Daniel N. Lockwood">Daniel N. Lockwood</a> from New York placed Cleveland's name in nomination. But this rather lackluster address was eclipsed by the seconding speech of <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Bragg" title="Edward S. Bragg">Edward S. Bragg</a> from Wisconsin, who roused the delegates with a memorable slap at Tammany. "They love him, gentlemen," Bragg said of Cleveland, "and they respect him, not only for himself, for his character, for his integrity and judgment and iron will, but they love him most of all for the enemies he has made." As the convention rocked with cheers, Tammany boss <a href="/wiki/John_Kelly_(U.S._politician)" title="John Kelly (U.S. politician)">John Kelly</a> lunged at the platform, screaming that he welcomed the compliment.</p>
<p>On the first ballot, Cleveland led the field with 392 votes, more than 150 votes short of the nomination. Trailing him were <a href="/wiki/Thomas_F._Bayard" title="Thomas F. Bayard">Thomas F. Bayard</a> from Delaware, 170; <a href="/wiki/Allen_G._Thurman" title="Allen G. Thurman">Allen G. Thurman</a> from Ohio, 88; <a href="/wiki/Samuel_J._Randall" title="Samuel J. Randall">Samuel J. Randall</a> from Pennsylvania, 78; and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_E._McDonald" title="Joseph E. McDonald">Joseph E. McDonald</a> from Indiana, 56; with the rest scattered. Randall then withdrew in Cleveland's favor. This move, together with the Southern bloc scrambling aboard the Cleveland bandwagon, was enough to put him over the top of the second ballot, with 683 votes, to 81.5 for Bayard and 45.5 for <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Hendricks" title="Thomas A. Hendricks">Thomas A. Hendricks</a> from Indiana. Hendricks was nominated unanimously for vice-president on the first ballot after <a href="/wiki/John_C._Black" title="John C. Black">John C. Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Rosecrans" title="William Rosecrans">William Rosecrans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Glick" title="George Washington Glick">George Washington Glick</a> withdrew their names from consideration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Anti-Monopoly National Convention assembled in the Hershey Music Hall in Chicago, Illinois. The party had been formed to express opposition to the business practices of the emerging nationwide companies. There were around 200 delegates present from 16 states, but 61 of those delegates had come from Michigan and Illinois.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alson_Streeter" title="Alson Streeter">Alson Streeter</a> was the temporary chairman and John F. Henry was the permanent chairmen.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Butler_(politician)" title="Benjamin Butler (politician)">Benjamin F. Butler</a> was nominated for president on the first ballot. Delegates from New York, Washington D.C. and Maryland bolted the convention when it appeared that no discussion of other candidates would be allowed. <a href="/wiki/Allen_G._Thurman" title="Allen G. Thurman">Allen Thurman</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_B._Weaver" title="James B. Weaver">James Weaver</a> were put forward as alternatives to Butler, but Weaver declined, not wishing to run another national campaign for political office, and Thurman's name failed to generate much enthusiasm. Butler, while far from opposed to the nomination, hoped to be nominated by the Democratic or Republican parties, or at least in the case of the former, to influence the party platform into being more favorable to greenbacks. Ultimately however, only the <a href="/wiki/Greenback_Party" title="Greenback Party">Greenback Party</a> would endorse his candidacy.</p>
<p>The convention chose not to nominate a candidate for vice-president, hoping that other conventions would endorse a similar platform and name a suitable vice-presidential nominee.</p>

<p>The 3rd Greenback Party National Convention assembled in English's Opera House in Indianapolis, Indiana. Delegates from 28 states and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> were in attendance. The convention nominated Benjamin F. Butler for president over its Party Chairman Jesse Harper on the first ballot. <a href="/wiki/Absolom_M._West" title="Absolom M. West">Absolom M. West</a> was nominated unanimously for vice-president, and subsequently was also endorsed by the Anti-Monopoly Party.</p>
<p>Butler had initially hoped to form a number of fusion slates with the <i>"minority party"</i> in each state, Democratic or Republican, and for his supporters of various parties to come together under a single <i>"People's Party"</i>. Many in the two major parties however, while maybe agreeing with Butler's message and platform, were unwilling to place their support beyond the party line. In a number of places however, Iowa in particular, fusion slates were nominated; essentially, Butler's and Cleveland's votes would both be added together for the total vote of the fusion slate, allowing them to carry the state even if neither were to carry a plurality, with the electoral vote being divided according to the percentage of the vote each party net.</p>
<p>Interestingly however, even if Fusion were carried out in every state in which it were considered possible <i>(Indiana, Nebraska, Wisconsin, Illinois)</i>, it would not have changed the end result, none of the states flipping from Blaine to Cleveland, with Butler winning a single electoral vote from Indiana.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Prohibition_Party_nomination">American Prohibition Party nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: American Prohibition Party nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The American Prohibition Party held its national convention in the YMCA building in Chicago, Illinois. There were 150 delegates, including many non-voting delegates. The party sought to merge the reform movements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-masonry" title="Anti-masonry">anti-masonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">prohibition</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-polygamy" title="Anti-polygamy">anti-polygamy</a>, and direct election of the president into a new party. <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Blanchard_(abolitionist)" title="Jonathan Blanchard (abolitionist)">Jonathan Blanchard</a> was a major figure within the party. He traveled throughout northern states in the spring and gave an address entitled "The American Party - Its Principles and Its Claims."</p>
<p>During the convention, the party name was changed from the American Party to the American Prohibition Party. The party had been known as the Anti-Masonic Party in 1880. Many of the delegates at the convention were initially interested in nominating <a href="/wiki/John_St._John_(American_politician)" title="John St. John (American politician)">John St. John</a>, the former Governor of Kansas, but it was feared that such a nomination might cost him that of the Prohibition Party, which he was actively seeking. Party leaders met with <a href="/wiki/Samuel_C._Pomeroy" title="Samuel C. Pomeroy">Samuel C. Pomeroy</a>, a former Senator from the same state who was the convention's runner-up for the nomination, and at Pomeroy's suggestion they agreed to withdraw the ticket from the race should St. John win the Prohibition Party nomination. Nominated alongside Pomeroy was John A. Conant from Connecticut.</p>
<p>John St. John would later unanimously win the Prohibition Party nomination, with Pomeroy and Conant withdrawing from the Presidential Contest and endorsing his candidacy. The New York Times speculated that the endorsement would <i>"give him 40,000 votes"</i>.</p>

<p>The fourth Prohibition Party National Convention assembled in Lafayette Hall, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania. There were 505 delegates from 31 states and territories at the convention. The national ticket was nominated unanimously: John St. John for president and <a href="/wiki/William_Daniel_(Maryland_politician)" title="William Daniel (Maryland politician)">William Daniel</a> for vice-president. The straightforward single-issue <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_Party" title="Prohibition Party">Prohibition Party</a> platform advocated the criminalization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Equal_Rights_Party_nomination">Equal Rights Party nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Equal Rights Party nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Dissatisfied with resistance by the men of the major parties to women's suffrage, a small group of women announced the formation in 1884 of the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_Party_(United_States)" title="Equal Rights Party (United States)">Equal Rights Party</a>.</p>
<p>The Equal Rights Party held its national convention in San Francisco, California on September 20. The convention nominated <a href="/wiki/Belva_Ann_Lockwood" title="Belva Ann Lockwood">Belva Ann Lockwood</a>, an attorney in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, for President. Chairman <a href="/wiki/Marietta_Stow" title="Marietta Stow">Marietta Stow</a>, the first woman to preside over a national nominating convention, was nominated for vice-president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lockwood agreed to be the party's presidential candidate, even though most women in the United States did not yet have the right to vote. She said, "I cannot vote but I can be voted for." She was the first woman to run a full campaign for the office (<a href="/wiki/Victoria_Woodhull" title="Victoria Woodhull">Victoria Woodhull</a> conducted a more limited campaign in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1872" title="United States presidential election, 1872">1872</a>). The Equal Rights Party had no treasury, but Lockwood gave lectures to pay for campaign travel. She received approximately 4,194 votes nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_election">General election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: General election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_presidential_election,_1884&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The issue of personal character was paramount in the 1884 campaign. Blaine had been prevented from getting the Republican presidential nomination during the previous two elections because of the stigma of the "Mulligan letters": in 1876, a Boston bookkeeper named James Mulligan had located some letters showing that Blaine had sold his influence in Congress to various businesses. One such letter ended with the phrase "burn this letter", from which a popular chant of the Democrats arose - "Burn, burn, burn this letter!" In just one deal, he had received $110,150 (over $1.5 million in 2010 dollars) from the Little Rock and Fort Smith Railroad for securing a federal land grant, among other things. Democrats and anti-Blaine Republicans made unrestrained attacks on his integrity as a result. Cleveland, on the other hand, was known as "Grover the Good" for his personal integrity; in the space of the three previous years he had become successively the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>, and then the governor of the state of New York, cleaning up large amounts of Tammany Hall's graft.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="9300" data-file-width="6600" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg/220px-How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg/330px-How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg/440px-How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:How_The_Public_Domain_Has_Been_Squandered_1884_Cornell_CUL_PJM_1088.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Commentator <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Jacoby_(columnist)" title="Jeff Jacoby (columnist)">Jeff Jacoby</a> notes that, "Not since George Washington had a candidate for president been so renowned for his rectitude."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In July the Republicans found a refutation buried in Cleveland's past. Aided by sermons from a minister named George H. Ball, they charged that Cleveland had fathered an illegitimate child while he was a lawyer in Buffalo. When confronted with the scandal, Cleveland's immediately instructed his supporters to "Above all, tell the truth." Cleveland admitted to paying child support in 1874 to Maria Crofts Halpin, the woman who claimed he fathered her child, named Oscar Folsom Cleveland. Halpin was involved with several men at the time, including Cleveland's friend and law partner, Oscar Folsom, for whom the child was named. Cleveland did not know which man was the father; he assumed responsibility because he was the only bachelor among them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_F._Graff_2002_61.E2.80.9363_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henry_F._Graff_2002_61.E2.80.9363-11">[11]</a></sup> Shortly before election day, The Republican media published an affidavit from Halpin in which she stated that until she met Cleveland her "life was pure and spotless", and "there is not, and never was, a doubt as to the paternity of our child, and the attempt of Grover Cleveland, or his friends, to couple the name of Oscar Folsom, or any one else, with that boy, for that purpose is simply infamous and false."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Republican cartoonists across the land had a field day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleveland's campaign decided that candor was the best approach to this scandal: it admitted that Cleveland had formed an "illicit connection" with the mother and that a child had been born and given the Cleveland surname. They also noted that there was no proof that Cleveland was the father, and claimed that, by assuming responsibility and finding a home for the child, he was merely doing his duty. Finally, they showed that the mother had not been forced into an asylum; her whereabouts were unknown. Blaine's supporters condemned Cleveland in the strongest of terms, singing "Ma, Ma, Where's my Pa?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (After Cleveland's victory, Cleveland supporters would respond to the taunt with: "Gone to the White House, Ha, Ha, Ha.") However, the Cleveland campaign's damage control worked well enough and the race remained a tossup through Election Day. The greatest threat to the Republicans came from reformers called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mugwump" title="Mugwump">Mugwumps</a>" who were angrier at Blaine's public corruption than at Cleveland's private affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the final week of the campaign, the Blaine campaign suffered a catastrophe. At a Republican meeting attended by Blaine, a group of New York preachers castigated the Mugwumps. Their spokesman, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_D._Burchard_(minister)" title="Samuel D. Burchard (minister)">Reverend Dr. Samuel Burchard</a>, made this fatal statement: "We are Republicans, and don't propose to leave our party and identify ourselves with the party whose antecedents have been <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanism" title="Romanism">Romanism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">rebellion</a>." Blaine did not notice Burchard's anti-Catholic slur, nor did the assembled newspaper reporters, but a Democratic operative did, and Cleveland's campaign managers made sure that it was widely publicized. The statement energized the Irish and Catholic vote in New York City heavily against Blaine, costing him New York state and the election by the narrowest of margins.</p>
<p>In addition to Rev. Dr. Samuel Burchard's statement, it is also believed that John St. John's campaign was responsible for winning Cleveland the election in New York. Since Prohibitionists tended to ally more with Republicans, the Republican Party attempted to convince John St. John to drop out. When they failed, they resorted to slandering him. Because of this, he redoubled his efforts in upstate New York, where Blaine was vulnerable on his prohibition stance, and took votes away from the Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
