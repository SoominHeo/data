<p>"<b>Puff, the Magic Dragon</b>" (or "<b>Puff</b>") is a song written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonard_Lipton" title="Leonard Lipton">Leonard Lipton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Yarrow" title="Peter Yarrow">Peter Yarrow</a> and made popular by Yarrow's group <a href="/wiki/Peter,_Paul_and_Mary" title="Peter, Paul and Mary">Peter, Paul and Mary</a> in a 1963 recording.</p>
<p>Lipton wrote the complete lyrics, Yarrow found and used them, and later gave Lipton the credits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puff,_the_Magic_Dragon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lyrics for "Puff, the Magic Dragon" were based on a 1959 poem by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonard_Lipton" title="Leonard Lipton">Leonard Lipton</a>, a 19-year-old <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Lipton was inspired by an <a href="/wiki/Ogden_Nash" title="Ogden Nash">Ogden Nash</a> poem titled "<a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Custard_the_Dragon" title="The Tale of Custard the Dragon">Custard the Dragon</a>", about a "realio, trulio little pet dragon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lyrics tell a story of the ageless <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">dragon</a> Puff and his playmate, Jackie Paper, a little boy who grows up and loses interest in the imaginary adventures of childhood and leaves Puff to be with himself. (The line "A dragon lives forever, but not so little boys" is generally thought to imply only that "little Jackie Paper" grew up.) The story of the song takes place "by the sea" in the fictional land of "Honalee".</p>
<p>Lipton was friends with Peter Yarrow's housemate when they were all students at Cornell. He used Yarrow's typewriter to get the poem out of his head. He then forgot about it until years later, when a friend called and told him Yarrow was looking for him, to give him credit for the lyrics. On making contact Yarrow gave Lipton half the songwriting credit, and he still gets royalties from the song.</p>
<p>In an effort to be <a href="/wiki/Gender_neutrality" title="Gender neutrality">gender-neutral</a>, Yarrow now sings the line "A dragon lives forever, but not so little boys" as "A dragon lives forever, but not so girls and boys." The original poem also had a verse that did not make it into the song. In it, Puff found another child and played with him after returning. Neither Yarrow nor Lipton remembers the verse in any detail, and the paper that was left in Yarrow's typewriter in 1958 has since been lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, Yarrow joined <a href="/wiki/Paul_Stookey" title="Paul Stookey">Paul Stookey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Travers_(singer)" title="Mary Travers (singer)">Mary Travers</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Peter,_Paul_and_Mary" title="Peter, Paul and Mary">Peter, Paul and Mary</a>. The group incorporated the song into their live performances before recording it in 1962; their 1962 recording of "Puff" reached number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart and spent two weeks atop the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Adult_Contemporary_(chart)" title="Adult Contemporary (chart)">easy listening</a> chart in early 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It also reached number ten on Billboard's R&amp;B chart. More importantly, however, "Puff" joined several other PP&amp;M hits of the same era ("If I Had a Hammer", "Where Have All The Flowers Gone," and "Blowin' In the Wind" among them [the last composed and first recorded by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>]) to become standards of American musical culture, the best-known and most widely loved examples of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk-song" title="Folk-song">folk-song</a> <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genre</a> that virtually all Americans know by heart and can sing along to.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="A claim such this needs to be supported by a reliable source; otherwise, it's just original research and puffery (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speculation_about_drug_references">Speculation about drug references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puff,_the_Magic_Dragon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Speculation about drug references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the song's initial success, speculation arose â as early as a 1964 article in <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> â that the song contained veiled references to smoking <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">marijuana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The word "paper" in the name of Puff's human friend (Jackie Paper) was said to be a reference to <a href="/wiki/Rolling_paper" title="Rolling paper">rolling papers</a>, and the word "dragon" was interpreted as "draggin'," i.e. inhaling smoke; similarly, the name "Puff" was alleged to be a reference to taking a "puff" on a joint. The supposition was claimed to be common knowledge in a letter by a member of the public to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The authors of the song have repeatedly rejected this interpretation and have strongly and consistently denied that they intended any references to <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">drug use</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Peter Yarrow has frequently explained that the song is about the hardships of growing older and has no relationship to drug-taking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He has also said of the song that it "never had any meaning other than the obvious one" and is about the "loss of innocence in children",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and dismissed the suggestion of association with drugs as "sloppy research"."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976, Yarrow's bandmate <a href="/wiki/Paul_Stookey" title="Paul Stookey">Paul Stookey</a> of Peter, Paul and Mary also upheld the song's innocence. He recorded a version of the song at the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House" title="Sydney Opera House">Sydney Opera House</a> in March 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in which he set up a <a href="/wiki/Mock_trial" title="Mock trial">fictitious trial scene</a>. The Prosecutor accused the song of being about marijuana, but Puff and Jackie protested. The judge finally left the case to the jury (the Opera House audience) and said if they will sing along with the song, it would be acquitted. The audience joined in with Stookey, and at the end of their sing-along, the judge declared: "case dismissed."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Content describing the fictitious trial performance should be supported by a reliable source. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puff,_the_Magic_Dragon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 1978 animated television special, <i><a href="/wiki/Puff_the_Magic_Dragon_(film)" title="Puff the Magic Dragon (film)">Puff the Magic Dragon</a></i>, adapted the song. It was followed by two sequels, <i><a href="/wiki/Puff_the_Magic_Dragon_in_the_Land_of_the_Living_Lies" title="Puff the Magic Dragon in the Land of the Living Lies">Puff the Magic Dragon in the Land of the Living Lies</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Puff_and_the_Incredible_Mr._Nobody" title="Puff and the Incredible Mr. Nobody">Puff and the Incredible Mr. Nobody</a></i>. In all three films <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Meredith" title="Burgess Meredith">Burgess Meredith</a> voiced Puff. In December 2016, it was announced that <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fox_Animation" title="Fox Animation">Fox Animation</a> will produce a live-action/animation film based on the song with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Mitchell_(director)" title="Mike Mitchell (director)">Mike Mitchell</a> as director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song was adapted for a children's pantomime, which played at Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Centre" title="Seymour Centre">Seymour Centre</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2007 book adaptation of the song's lyrics by Yarrow, Lipton, and illustrator <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Puybaret&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eric Puybaret (page does not exist)">Eric Puybaret</a> gives the story a happier ending with a young girl (presumed by reviewers to be Jackie Paper's daughter)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> seeking out Puff to become her new companion. The lyrics remain unchanged from the Peter Paul and Mary version; the young girl is only seen in the pictures by illustrator Puybaret. On the last page of the book, she is introduced to Puff by an older Jackie Paper.</p>
<p>Both tune and elements of the lyrics were adapted in the controversial <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_the_Magic_Negro" title="Barack the Magic Negro">Barack the Magic Negro</a>", written and recorded by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Shanklin" title="Paul Shanklin">Paul Shanklin</a> for <a href="/wiki/Rush_Limbaugh" title="Rush Limbaugh">Rush Limbaugh</a>'s radio program, after the term was first applied to then presidential candidate Obama by movie and culture critic, <a href="/wiki/David_Ehrenstein" title="David Ehrenstein">David Ehrenstein</a>, in a <i>Los Angeles Times</i> op ed column of March 19, 2007. Yarrow condemned the act as "shocking and saddening in the extreme," stating that "taking a children's song and twisting it in such vulgar, mean-spirited way, is a slur to our entire country and our common agreement to move beyond racismâ¦ Puff, himself, if asked, would certainly agree."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid 1970s an American <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> band named Ruach created a parody version of the song entitled "Puff the Kosher Dragon". In the course of the song, Kosher Puff eats <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a> food, has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_Mitzvah" title="Bar Mitzvah">Bar Mitzvah</a>, fights <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-semites" title="Anti-semites">anti-semites</a> and finally marries and brings up his children as loyal members of the faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Ruach song has been noted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as one of the first examples of a modern Jewish band using a popular secular tune.</p>
<p>The tune was used in the promotional LP <i>Push the Magic Button</i> for the track with the same name by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Versatec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Versatec (page does not exist)">Versatec</a>, a computer printer company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_War">Vietnam War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puff,_the_Magic_Dragon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vietnam War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC-47_Spooky" title="AC-47 Spooky">AC-47 Spooky</a> gunship was nicknamed the "Dragon" or "Dragon ship" by the Americans because of its armament and firepower â the nickname soon caught on, and one website without primary citations indicates that the American troops began to call the AC-47 "Puff the Magic Dragon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In another Vietnam reference, Robert Mason's <a href="/wiki/Chickenhawk_(book)" title="Chickenhawk (book)">Chickenhawk</a> states, in reference to the Peter, Paul and Mary song playing on a turntable: <i>âPuff the Magic Dragonâ was making me uncomfortable. It was the saccharine song that had inspired the naming of the murderous Gatling-gun-armed C-47s. I couldnât listen.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puff,_the_Magic_Dragon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>, said his <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">Dragon spacecraft</a> was named after "Puff the Magic Dragon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Part of the song was used in a scene from <i><a href="/wiki/Meet_the_Parents" title="Meet the Parents">Meet the Parents</a></i> where Greg Focker is uncomfortably trying to explain to Jack Byrnes how the song is supposedly about smoking marijuana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Hill" title="King of the Hill">King of the Hill</a></i> titled "The Bluegrass is Always Greener", Hank scolds Bill for singing the song; as a <a href="/wiki/Bait-and-switch" title="Bait-and-switch">bait-and-switch</a> joke, the conservative and uptight Hank isn't aware of the alleged drug subtext, and is scolding Bill for being a grown man singing a song about a dragon.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Magic" title="Orlando Magic">Orlando Magic</a> mascot is named "<a href="/wiki/Stuff_the_Magic_Dragon" title="Stuff the Magic Dragon">Stuff the Magic Dragon</a>".</p>
<p>In Marvel Studios' <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_2" title="Iron Man 2">Iron Man 2</a></i>, the character Justin Hammer, who owns Hammer Industries, introduces a machine gun nicknamed Puff the Magic Dragon to the U.S. military, thereby combining allusions to both PP&amp;M's "If I Had a Hammer" and "Puff".</p>
<p>There is a comedic magician who calls himself <a href="/wiki/Piff_the_Magic_Dragon" title="Piff the Magic Dragon">Piff the Magic Dragon</a>. He always opens his show with, "You might know my brother... Steve."</p>
