<p><b>P-waves</b> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Linear_elasticity#Elastic_wave" title="Linear elasticity">elastic wave</a>, and are one of the two main types of elastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_wave_(seismology)" title="Body wave (seismology)">body waves</a>, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic_waves" title="Seismic waves">seismic waves</a> in seismology, that travel through a <a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">continuum</a> and are the first waves from an earthquake to arrive at a seismograph. The <a href="/wiki/Continuum_(measurement)" title="Continuum (measurement)">continuum</a> is made up of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> waves), liquids, or solids, including the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. P-waves can be produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a> and recorded by <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismographs</a>. The name P-wave can stand for either <b>pressure wave</b> as it is formed from alternating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compressions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rarefaction" title="Rarefaction">rarefactions</a> or <b>primary wave</b>, as it has the highest velocity and is therefore the first wave to be recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-John-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> and homogeneous solids, the mode of propagation of a P-wave is always <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">longitudinal</a>; thus, the particles in the solid vibrate along the axis of propagation (the direction of motion) of the wave energy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seismic_waves_in_the_Earth">Seismic waves in the Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Seismic waves in the Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core-mantle_boundary" title="Core-mantle boundary">Core-mantle boundary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohorovi%C4%8Di%C4%87_discontinuity" title="Mohorovičić discontinuity">Mohorovičić discontinuity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low-velocity_zone" title="Low-velocity zone">Low-velocity zone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lehmann_discontinuity" title="Lehmann discontinuity">Lehmann discontinuity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="901" data-file-width="1321" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG/300px-Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG/450px-Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG/600px-Speeds_of_seismic_waves.PNG 2x" width="300"/></a>

</div>
<p>Primary and secondary waves are body waves that travel within the Earth. The motion and behavior of both <i>P</i>-type and <i>S</i>-type in the Earth are monitored to probe the interior <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth" title="Structure of the Earth">structure of the Earth</a>. Discontinuities in velocity as a function of depth are indicative of changes in phase or composition. Differences in arrival times of waves originating in a seismic event like an earthquake as a result of waves taking different paths allow mapping of the Earth's inner structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Negendank_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Negendank-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="P-wave_shadow_zone">P-wave shadow zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: P-wave shadow zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="379" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg/220px-Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg/330px-Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg/440px-Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earthquake_wave_shadow_zone.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
P-wave shadow zone (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USGS" title="USGS">USGS</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Almost all the information available on the structure of the Earth's deep interior is derived from observations of the travel times, <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refractions</a> and phase transitions of seismic body waves, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_modes" title="Normal modes">normal modes</a>. P-waves travel through the fluid layers of the <a href="/wiki/Earth#Interior" title="Earth">Earth's interior</a>, and yet they are refracted slightly when they pass through the transition between the semisolid <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and the liquid <a href="/wiki/Earth#The_core" title="Earth">outer core</a>. As a result, there is a P-wave "<a href="/wiki/Shadow_zone" title="Shadow zone">shadow zone</a>" between 103° and 142°<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> from the earthquake's focus, where the initial P-waves are not registered on seismometers. In contrast, S-waves do not travel through liquids, rather, they are <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuated</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_earthquake_warning">As an earthquake warning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As an earthquake warning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Earthquake advance warning is possible by detecting the non-destructive primary waves that travel more quickly through the Earth's crust than do the destructive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-waves" title="S-waves">secondary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_wave" title="Rayleigh wave">Rayleigh waves</a>, in the same way that lightning flashes reach our eyes before we hear the thunder during a storm. The amount of advance warning depends on the delay between the arrival of the P-wave and other destructive waves, generally on the order of seconds up to about 60–90 seconds for deep, distant, large quakes such as Tokyo would have received before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Tohoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tohoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tohoku earthquake</a>. The effectiveness of advance warning depends on accurate detection of the P-waves and rejection of <a href="/wiki/Ground_vibrations" title="Ground vibrations">ground vibrations</a> caused by local activity (such as trucks or construction) as otherwise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False-positive" title="False-positive">false-positive</a> warnings will result. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_Early_Warning" title="Earthquake Early Warning">Earthquake Early Warning</a> systems can be automated to allow for immediate safety actions such as issuing alerts, stopping elevators at the nearest floors or switching gas utilities off.</p>
