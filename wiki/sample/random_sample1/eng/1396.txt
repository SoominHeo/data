<p>"<b>Que Sera, Sera (Whatever Will Be, Will Be)</b>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FrontCover_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FrontCover-1">[1]</a></sup> first published in 1956, is a <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular</a> <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">song</a> written by the <a href="/wiki/Jay_Livingston" title="Jay Livingston">Jay Livingston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Evans" title="Ray Evans">Ray Evans</a> songwriting team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_2006_135_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2006_135-2">[2]</a></sup> The song was introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Knew_Too_Much_(1956_film)" title="The Man Who Knew Too Much (1956 film)">The Man Who Knew Too Much</a></i> (1956),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leigh_2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leigh_2001-3">[3]</a></sup> starring <a href="/wiki/Doris_Day" title="Doris Day">Doris Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">James Stewart</a> in the lead roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_2006_135_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2006_135-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Day's <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">recording</a> of the song for <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catalog_numbering_systems_for_single_records" title="Catalog numbering systems for single records">catalog number</a> 40704) made it to number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and number one in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_2006_135_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2006_135-2">[2]</a></sup> From 1968 to 1973, it was the <a href="/wiki/Theme_music" title="Theme music">theme song</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situation_comedy" title="Situation comedy">situation comedy</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Doris_Day_Show" title="The Doris Day Show">The Doris Day Show</a>,</i> becoming her <a href="/wiki/Signature_song" title="Signature song">signature song</a>. The three verses of the song progress through the life of the narrator—from childhood, through young adulthood and falling in love, to parenthood—and each asks "What will I be?" or "What lies ahead?" The chorus repeats the answer: "What will be, will be." It reached the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts#A_variety_of_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard magazine charts</a> in July 1956. The song received the 1956 <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Academy Award for Best Original Song</a> with the alternative title "Whatever Will Be, Will Be (Que Sera, Sera)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leigh_2001_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leigh_2001-3">[3]</a></sup> It was the third <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards#The_Oscar" title="Academy Awards">Oscar</a> in this category for Livingston and Evans, who previously won in 1948 and 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_2006_135_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2006_135-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2004 it finished at #48 in <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Songs" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Songs">AFI's 100 Years...100 Songs</a> survey of top tunes in American cinema.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Title_sequence" title="Title sequence">title sequence</a> of the Hitchcock film gives the song title as "Whatever Will Be". It was a #1 hit in Australia for pop singer <a href="/wiki/Normie_Rowe" title="Normie Rowe">Normie Rowe</a> in September 1965.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_in_title_and_lyrics">Language in title and lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Que_Sera,_Sera_(Whatever_Will_Be,_Will_Be)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language in title and lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The popularity of the song has led to curiosity about the origins of the saying and the identity of its language. Both the Spanish-like spelling used by Livingston and Evans and an Italian-like form ("che sarà sarà") are first documented in the 16th century as an English heraldic <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The "Spanish" form appears on a brass plaque in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St._Nicholas,_Thames_Ditton" title="Church of St. Nicholas, Thames Ditton">Church of St. Nicholas, Thames Ditton</a>, Surrey, dated 1559.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The "Italian" form was first adopted as a family motto by either <a href="/wiki/John_Russell,_1st_Earl_of_Bedford" title="John Russell, 1st Earl of Bedford">John Russell, 1st Earl of Bedford</a>, or his son, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Russell,_2nd_Earl_of_Bedford" title="Francis Russell, 2nd Earl of Bedford">Francis Russell, 2nd Earl of Bedford</a>. It is said by some sources to have been adopted by the elder Russell after his experience at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">Battle of Pavia</a> (1525), and to be engraved on his tomb (1555 <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">N.S.</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The 2nd Earl's adoption of the motto is commemorated in a manuscript dated 1582.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Their successors—Earls and, later, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Dukes of Bedford ("Sixth Creation")</a>, as well as other aristocratic families—continued to use the motto. Soon after its adoption as a heraldic motto, it appeared in <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Faustus_(play)" title="Doctor Faustus (play)">Doctor Faustus</a></i> (written ca. 1590; published 1604), whose text<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (Act 1, Scene 1) contains a line with the archaic Italian spelling "Che sera, sera / What will be, shall be".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Early in the 17th century the saying begins to appear in the speech and thoughts of fictional characters as a spontaneous expression of a fatalistic attitude, always in an English-speaking context.</p>
<p>The saying has no history in Spain, Italy, or France, and in fact is ungrammatical in all three of these Romance languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is composed of Spanish or Italian words superimposed on English syntax. It was evidently formed by a word-for-word mistranslation of English "What will be will be", merging the free relative pronoun <i>what</i> (= "that which") with the interrogative <i>what?</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Livingston and Evans had some knowledge of Spanish, and early in their career they worked together as musicians on cruise ships to the Caribbean and South America. Composer Jay Livingston had seen the 1954 Hollywood film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Barefoot_Contessa" title="The Barefoot Contessa">The Barefoot Contessa</a></i>, in which a fictional Italian family has the motto "Che sarà sarà" carved in stone at their ancestral mansion. He immediately wrote it down as a possible song title, and he and lyricist Ray Evans later gave it a Spanish spelling "because there are so many Spanish-speaking people in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In modern times, thanks to the popularity of the song and its many translations, the phrase has been adopted in countries around the world to name a variety of entities, including books, movies, restaurants, vacation rentals, airplanes, and race horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_song_and_phrase">Other uses of the song and phrase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Que_Sera,_Sera_(Whatever_Will_Be,_Will_Be)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other uses of the song and phrase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song is regularly sung at English <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> matches when a team is progressing to the next round of a competition that will ultimately lead them to <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley Stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wembley_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wembley-18">[18]</a></sup> The chorus's second line is changed to ‘Que Sera, Sera, whatever will be, will be, we're going to Wembley, Que Sera, Sera’. For many years now, it has been very common for the fans to intentionally mispronouce the words as "Que seras seras" so that they sound like "kiss 'er arse", i.e. "kiss her arse" with a dropped "h". This is done for humoristic effect.</p>
<p>In 1956 "Que Sera, Sera" was the name given to a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">US Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-47_Skytrain" title="C-47 Skytrain">C-47 Skytrain</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkytrainCentennialGov_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-SkytrainCentennialGov-19">[19]</a></sup> which, on October 31, 1956, was the first aircraft to land on the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Deep_Freeze" title="Operation Deep Freeze">Operation Deep Freeze</a> II), commanded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral" title="Rear Admiral">Rear Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/George_J._Dufek" title="George J. Dufek">George Dufek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, the song was used in the <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">Simpsons</a> episode "<a href="/wiki/Bart%27s_Comet" title="Bart's Comet">Bart's Comet</a>". It is sung by several characters as they wait for a comet to destroy Springfield, only to have it burn up and be no bigger than a Chihuahua's head.</p>
<p>The 2000 <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> film <a href="/wiki/Pukar_(2000_film)" title="Pukar (2000 film)">Pukar</a> has a song, "Kay Sera Sera", based on this.</p>
<p>The 2009 <a href="/wiki/Said_the_Whale" title="Said the Whale">Said the Whale</a> song "Goodnight Moon" directly references the song's second verse, with the lyrics "Oh que sera sera/ Whatever will be will be/ The future belongs to me/ And I belong to you".</p>
<p>In 2015, the song was used in an anti-smoking <a href="/wiki/Public_service_announcement" title="Public service announcement">public service announcement</a> for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Health" title="New York State Department of Health">New York State Department of Health</a>.</p>
<p>The 2015 Canadian musical animated short <i><a href="/wiki/Carface" title="Carface">Carface</a></i> is also set to the song, as performed by a 1957 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Bel_Air" title="Chevrolet Bel Air">Chevy Bel Air</a> cartoon character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amidi_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amidi-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Appears in the 2009 indie film "Mary and Max".</p>

<p>Australian pop singer <a href="/wiki/Normie_Rowe" title="Normie Rowe">Normie Rowe</a>'s 1965 recording of "Que Sera, Sera", which was produced by <a href="/wiki/Pat_Aulton" title="Pat Aulton">Pat Aulton</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sunshine_Records_(Australia)" title="Sunshine Records (Australia)">Sunshine Record label</a> (Sunshine QK 1103), was the biggest hit of his career, "the biggest Australian rock 'n roll hit of 1965",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Normie_Rowe_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Normie_Rowe-22">[22]</a></sup> and is reputed to be the biggest-selling Australian single of the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Donnell_2010_228_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Donnell_2010_228-23">[23]</a></sup> The song was "done in the style of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louie,_Louie" title="Louie, Louie">Louie, Louie</a>" and the manner of "<a href="/wiki/Hang_On_Sloopy" title="Hang On Sloopy">Hang On Sloopy</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Normie_Rowe_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Normie_Rowe-22">[22]</a></sup> and given a "<a href="/wiki/Beat_music" title="Beat music">Merseybeat</a>" treatment (in the manner of The Beatles' "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_%26_Shout" title="Twist &amp; Shout">Twist &amp; Shout</a>"), and was backed by Rowe's band The Playboys. It was paired with a powerful version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnny_Kidd_%26_The_Pirates" title="Johnny Kidd &amp; The Pirates">Johnny Kidd &amp; The Pirates</a>' classic "<a href="/wiki/Shakin%27_All_Over" title="Shakin' All Over">Shakin' All Over</a>", and the single became a double-sided No. 1 hit in most capitals (#1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sydney,_Australia" title="Sydney, Australia">Sydney</a>, #1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Australia" title="Melbourne, Australia">Melbourne</a>, #1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brisbane,_Australia" title="Brisbane, Australia">Brisbane</a>, #1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adelaide,_Australia" title="Adelaide, Australia">Adelaide</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pop_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pop-25">[25]</a></sup> in September 1965, charting for 28 weeks and selling in unprecedented numbers, with Rock historian Ian McFarlane reporting sales of 80,000 copies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Donnell_2010_228_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Donnell_2010_228-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> while 1970s encyclopedist Noel McGrath claimed sales of 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Rowe scored another first in October 1965 when "Que Sera Sera" became his third hit single in the Melbourne Top 40 simultaneously. In 1965 Rowe received a gold record for "Que Sera, Sera" at Sydney's prestigious Chevron Hotel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In December 1965 the master of Rowe's version was purchased by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jay-Gee_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jay-Gee Records (page does not exist)">Jay-Gee Records</a> for release in the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In April 1966 Rowe received a second gold record for the sales of "Que Sera, Sera".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In August 1966 Rowe won Radio 5KA's annual best male vocal award for "Que Sera, Sera".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2006 Rowe released a newly recorded version, which was released by ABC via <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>, and later adding "the whole digital mix with a radio mix and a dance mix".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<li>A Yiddish version (Barclay 86034) was recorded in 1958 by comedian <a href="/wiki/Dave_Cash_(Yiddish_comedian)" title="Dave Cash (Yiddish comedian)">Dave Cash</a> with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Didier_Boland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Didier Boland (page does not exist)">Didier Boland</a> and his orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
