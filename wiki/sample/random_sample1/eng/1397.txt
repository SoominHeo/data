<p><i><b>Queen of the Clouds</b></i> is the debut studio album by Swedish singer <a href="/wiki/Tove_Lo" title="Tove Lo">Tove Lo</a>. It was released on 24 September 2014 by <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a>. The album follows her first <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Truth_Serum_(EP)" title="Truth Serum (EP)">Truth Serum</a></i> (2014) and includes the successful singles "<a href="/wiki/Habits_(Stay_High)" title="Habits (Stay High)">Habits (Stay High)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Talking_Body" title="Talking Body">Talking Body</a>". Lo worked with several writers and producers such as The Struts, <a href="/wiki/Klas_%C3%85hlund" title="Klas Åhlund">Klas Åhlund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kronlund" title="Alexander Kronlund">Alexander Kronlund</a>, Alx Reuterskiöld, and <a href="/wiki/Captain_Cuts" title="Captain Cuts">Captain Cuts</a>. Lyrically, the album's themes center on the stages of a relationship, including passion, love and break-ups.</p>
<p>The album garnered generally positive reviews from music critics who praised its production and lyrical content. On 25 September 2015, a Blueprint edition of the album was announced, including tracks from <i>Truth Serum</i>, a new version of "<a href="/wiki/Moments_(Tove_Lo_song)" title="Moments (Tove Lo song)">Moments</a>", as well as the song "Not Made for This World" originally a <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a> only bonus track, and an explicit version of the song "Scream My Name" from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games:_Mockingjay,_Part_1_%E2%80%93_Original_Motion_Picture_Soundtrack" title="The Hunger Games: Mockingjay, Part 1 – Original Motion Picture Soundtrack">The Hunger Games: Mockingjay – Part 1</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thematic_background_and_development">Thematic background and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen_of_the_Clouds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Thematic background and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Queen of the Clouds</i> is the follow-up to Lo's debut <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Truth_Serum_(EP)" title="Truth Serum (EP)">Truth Serum</a></i>. The album is divided into three sections: "The Sex", "The Love" and "The Pain". Similar to theme of a poisoned relationship in <i>Truth Serum</i>, the album centers on a breakup and provides a complete story of her romantic struggles. She described her music as like a therapist where she could sing about things she would not normally dare to speak about.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bg-2">[2]</a></sup> The title for the album came from her song "Not on Drugs" and describes her new lifestyle touring the world after the success of her first EP. Lo said that it represented the feeling of "floating on top of the world" and that it was important that the album title represented her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen_of_the_Clouds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Electropop" title="Electropop">electropop</a> musical styles, <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> is predominantly a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> which divides the record into three sections: "The Sex", "The Love" and "The Pain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bg_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bg-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stereo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stereo-4">[4]</a></sup> Similar to the theme of poisoned relationships explored on Lo's debut <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Truth_Serum_(EP)" title="Truth Serum (EP)">Truth Serum</a></i> (2014), the album centers on a breakup and provides a complete narration of her romantic struggles. She described her music as "like a therapist" where Lo could sing about things she would not normally feel comfortable speaking about.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bg_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-bg-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> blends multiple styles of music into a "monogenre" sound, containing heavy influences of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">EDM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance-pop" title="Dance-pop">dance-pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, combining them into a "seamless compendium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stereo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stereo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Madonna,_Rotterdam,_26-8-1987.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3162" data-file-width="1958" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg/170px-Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg/255px-Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg/340px-Madonna%2C_Rotterdam%2C_26-8-1987.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Madonna,_Rotterdam,_26-8-1987.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The musical styles explored on "The Sex" section of <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> have drawn comparisons to the works of early <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> (<i>pictured</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globe_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Globe-5">[5]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first section, "The Sex", opens the record with four songs about the "lecherous and reckless" beginning of a relationship, and are composed in a "infectious and tongue-in-cheek stampede of uptempo <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> Album opener, "My Gun", has been described as an urban-influenced pop song textured with elements of <a href="/wiki/Dancehall" title="Dancehall">dancehall</a>. It begins with a "mournful"-sounding choir before building to an upbeat pop groove. Lo’s vocals are low and seductive, before gliding into her higher range. The track explores the "flirty" stage before delving into more sexual themes, found later on "Talking Body", and has been compared to the works of <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sound_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sound-7">[7]</a></sup> Lyrically, Lo compares her lover to "her gun" and sings of feeling a release of pleasure: "Boy, if you're gonna shoot me down/Do it gently".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> The next track, "Like Em' Young", is driven by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribal_rhythms" title="Tribal rhythms">tribal-influenced rhythm</a> "coated" with a "parade" of <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music">dub</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bass_music" title="Bass music">bass</a>. Its lyrics are satirical in nature, as she criticizes other women for judging her taste in younger men: "Hey girl, why you judging me/When your, your guy is turning 53?/ I don't know what really gets you more/Is it that my guy's gonna live out yours?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> "Talking Body" is a heavily sexual song described as "salivating with carnal lust", and according to Ken Capobianco of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>, evokes "the rush of early <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globe_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Globe-5">[5]</a></sup> Lyrically, the verses see Lo in a state of idée fixe towards her partner, as she finds herself revolving her life around him. The chorus is musically armed with heavy propulsion combined with rising, subdued synths and sees her "objectifying" his model physique. Rounding out the first section is "Timebomb", a frantic <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance</a> <a href="/wiki/Piano_ballad" title="Piano ballad">piano ballad</a> hybrid described as a "sonic bombardment" of "euphoric" chords, drums, and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a> synths, and features an "explosive" chorus containing crashing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cymbals" title="Cymbals">cymbals</a> and pounding bass. Lo's vocals in the track are sung out of time to the beat, rushing her lyrics into a single phrase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sound_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-sound-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second section, "The Love", has been described as "a thunderous yearning through gigantic mid tempo, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a> balladry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> It opens with the "volcanic" ballad "Moments", which boasts atmospherics and influences of "trippy" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samba_music" title="Samba music">samba</a>. Lyrically, it sees Lo confessing her flaws before declaring "On good days, I am charming as fuck!".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> "The Way That I Am" is a <a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">dubstep</a> song filled with "pain and passion" as Lo sings in a soulful rasp and lyrically cries out to her partner to love her as she is; including flaws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> "Got Love" is one of the albums "pure" pop moments, and contains an unwaveringly positive attitude. Lyrically, she rejoices exultant feelings of first falling in love as she sings "Good enough to make the ocean look like it's a pond/Good enough to turn the valleys into mountain tops", and discusses the theme of immortality "We live like legends now, know that would never die/Oh, we Got Love".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> Closing out the section is the <a href="/wiki/Power_pop" title="Power pop">power pop</a> song "Not on Drugs". Primarily a guitar-driven song that explores the topic of a feeling of love so bewildering and overpowering that it affects your behavior, as if you were on drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The final section is "The Pain" which explores the difficulty piecing together one's life after the destruction and demise of a relationship through "distraught" lyricism and melancholic pop. The section opens with the "thunderous" <a href="/wiki/Electropop" title="Electropop">electropop</a> power-ballad "Thousand Miles", which lyrically depicts Lo's obsession with reuniting with her lost love at all costs, even if it means running "a thousand miles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> Musically, the mid-tempo track tells a story of a girl "struggling to forget a rocky relationship with an ex," and is sonically amped up by the singer’s "emotionally distraught" vocals and "thundering" drum rhythms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leak_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leak-8">[8]</a></sup> The following song, "<a href="/wiki/Habits_(Stay_High)" title="Habits (Stay High)">Habits (Stay High)</a>" sees her emotionally unravel even more, as she talks about self-medicating by smoking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a> to cope to live without her lover. It's "<a href="/wiki/Snap_music" title="Snap music">snappy</a>" verses are filled with "quietly distinct, often strange imagery", including Lo eating her dinner in the bathtub, getting drunken munchies, and seducing dads on the playground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitchfork_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pitchfork-9">[9]</a></sup> Musically, it is a <a href="/wiki/Purity_Ring_(band)" title="Purity Ring (band)">Purity Ring</a>-inspired song, and creates a calming atmosphere compared to that of <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> "This Time Around" is a dark <a href="/wiki/Charli_XCX" title="Charli XCX">Charli XCX</a>-inspired track which reflects on the lessons learned from a heartbreak. Lo's vocal performance on the song has been described as particularly yearning and broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup> The final song of the section and album is "Run on Love", an "easy-going" reworked <a href="/wiki/Nu-disco" title="Nu-disco">nu-disco</a> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">dance</a> collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">EDM</a> producer Lucas Nord. Lyrically, Lo sings of picking up her heart and choosing to no longer be a victim by living in the moment enjoy time with her lover before it runs out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globe_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Globe-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fort_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Fort-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen_of_the_Clouds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lo announced the release of <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> on 19 August 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Shortly after, on 9 September 2014, the album was made available to pre-order online through <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On 16 September 2014, the album tracks "Moments", "Timebomb" and "Thousand Miles" were also made available to download alongside the iTunes pre-order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On 24 September 2014, Lo performed "Habits" on <i><a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Kimmel_Live!" title="Jimmy Kimmel Live!">Jimmy Kimmel Live!</a></i> to promote her album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen_of_the_Clouds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Habits_(Stay_High)" title="Habits (Stay High)">Habits (Stay High)</a>", was originally released as "Habits" on 25 March 2013 prior to the release of <i>Truth Serum</i> in March 2014. It was later re-released as "Habits (Stay High)" on 6 December 2013. "Habits (Stay High)" peaked at number three on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A remix of "Habits" by record production duo Hippie Sabotage, alternatively titled "Stay High", was released on 3 March 2014 and peaked at number 13 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Singles_Chart" title="Swedish Singles Chart">Swedish Singles Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It performed even more successfully elsewhere, peaking within the top 10 of the charts in Norway, the Netherlands, France, the United Kingdom, New Zealand and Australia. .</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Not_on_Drugs" title="Not on Drugs">Not on Drugs</a>" was originally revealed as the album's second single with its lyric video of the premiering in June 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and its music video premiering in August of the same year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but a different single, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talking_Body_(Tove_Lo_song)" title="Talking Body (Tove Lo song)">Talking Body</a>", was chosen to replace it, impacting pop radio on 20 January 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talking_Body_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talking_Body-18">[18]</a></sup> "Talking Body" has since peaked at number 12 on the Hot 100 as of 20 May 2015.</p>
<p>On May 23, 2015 at the Boston Calling Music Festival, <a href="/wiki/Tove_Lo" title="Tove Lo">Tove Lo</a> announced that "<a href="/wiki/Timebomb_(Tove_Lo_song)" title="Timebomb (Tove Lo song)">Timebomb</a>" will serve as the third single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timebomb_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timebomb-19">[19]</a></sup> It was digitally released on 12 August 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Republic_Records" title="Republic Records">Republic Records</a>, which handles Lo's American radio promotions, opted not to push "Timebomb" to radio. Therefore, "<a href="/wiki/Moments_(Tove_Lo_song)" title="Moments (Tove Lo song)">Moments</a>" impacted pop radio on October 13, 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moments_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moments-21">[21]</a></sup> as the fourth single from the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Upon release, <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> received positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the album received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average</a> score of 73/100 based on 4 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MC_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MC-32">[32]</a></sup> Sam Lansky from <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i> praised the lyrical content of the album, stating that "the tracks all have a satisfying stomp and crash, but it’s her lyrics that shine brightest, trading in pop clichés but flipping them in the same breath." He described the <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a> of the tracks as commercial enough to give Lo a "fighting chance at stateside stardom".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-1">[1]</a></sup> Carrie Battan from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork</a> gave the album a 7.2 out of 10 and described her music as "bruised, brightly arranged pop songs that feel grand but not excessive". She also noted that her "disarming honesty" and "well-phrased lyrics" are what sticks to the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitchfork_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-pitchfork-9">[9]</a></sup> Evan Ross from andPOP gave the album 3.5 out of 5 stars described <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> as "a really enjoyable album that pretty accurately describes what it’s like to be young and in love" and particularly liked that the album was an entire concept instead of randomly thrown in tracks. He praised the production and enjoyable filler songs. He also noted that Lo's "EDM-inspired album" had more substance than other similar albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andpop_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-andpop-33">[33]</a></sup> Maxie Molotov-Smith from <i>Fortitude Magazine</i> gave the album 4.5 out of 5 stars, praising the "stark heart-on-sleeve" honesty in Lo's lyrics and described the music as "expertly-crafted electronic pop music" with infectious hooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortitude_Magazine_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fortitude_Magazine-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jon O'Brien from Music Is My Oxygen gave the album 4 out of 5 stars and praised Lo's standing out from the crowd, describing her album as a "refreshingly raw debut which casts Tove Lo as pop’s premier hot mess".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mimo_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-mimo-35">[35]</a></sup> Kathy Iandoli from <a href="/wiki/Idolator_(website)" title="Idolator (website)">Idolator</a> gave the album 3.5 out of 5 and praised the honesty of the lyrics and noted that Lo "has become masterfully adept at jamming thoughtful words into dancey songs through her past songwriting, but she takes it a step further with <i>Queen Of The Clouds</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idolator_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-idolator-36">[36]</a></sup> A reviewer from PressPLAY gave the album a 4 out of 5 and praised the album's lyrical content and that Lo "goes into the dark places that modern pop tends to avoid", although they also wrote that there was a "slight need for variety and a tighter edit of the tracklist". The critic also described Lo as having a "frantic, drunken energy <i>[...]</i> that keeps you hooked on her songs unlike any of her peers"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pressplay_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-pressplay-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen_of_the_Clouds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Lo's native Sweden, <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> debuted at number six on the <a href="/wiki/Sverigetopplistan" title="Sverigetopplistan">Sverigetopplistan</a> charts. In the United States, the album entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> at number 14 with first-week sales of 19,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> As of August 2016, <i>Queen of the Clouds</i> had sold 190,000 copies in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US-sales_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-US-sales-39">[39]</a></sup> The album also peaked within the top 50 in Australia, Finland, New Zealand and Norway.</p>
