<p>The <b>R-21</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru"><i><a href="/wiki/Er_(Cyrillic)" title="Er (Cyrillic)">Р</a>-21</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO</a>: <b>SS-N-5 'Sark/Serb'</b>; <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a>: <b>4K55</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missile</a> in service with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> between 1963 and 1989. It was the first Soviet nuclear missile that could be launched from a submerged submarine, and also had twice the range of earlier missiles. It replaced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-11FM" title="R-11FM">R-11FM</a> and <a href="/wiki/R-13_(missile)" title="R-13 (missile)">R-13</a> (SS-N-4) on many <a href="/wiki/Golf-class_submarine" title="Golf-class submarine">Golf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hotel-class_submarine" title="Hotel-class submarine">Hotel-class submarines</a>, and was in turn superseded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-27_(missile)" title="R-27 (missile)">R-27</a> (SS-N-6 'Serb') missile carried by <a href="/wiki/Yankee-class_submarine" title="Yankee-class submarine">Yankee-class submarines</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-21_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Development of the <a href="/wiki/R-15_(missile)" title="R-15 (missile)">R-15</a> and R-21 was initially assigned to <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Yangel" title="Mikhail Yangel">Mikhail Yangel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yuzhnoye_Design_Office" title="Yuzhnoye Design Office">Yuzhnoye Design Office</a> (OKB-586) on 20 March 1958. The project was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Makeyev" title="Viktor Makeyev">Viktor Makeyev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Makeyev_Rocket_Design_Bureau" title="Makeyev Rocket Design Bureau">SKB-385</a> on 17 March 1959. It was intended for the <a href="/wiki/Golf-class_submarine" title="Golf-class submarine"><i>Golf</i>-class submarines</a> (Project 629B); the complete missile system was called the <b>D-4</b> weapon complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 4th Research Institute had been conducting trials of underwater missile launch systems since 1955<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup> with modified Scuds<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but the first successful launch was in 1960<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup> and the first flight of a standard R-21 was in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup> Over the lifetime of the missile, 193 out of 228 launches were successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The R-21 was probably the basis for the design of the North Korean No Dong (<a href="/wiki/Rodong-1" title="Rodong-1">Rodong-1</a>) missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-21_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike Western designs, the R-21 used a cold launch solid rocket to eject the missile from the flooded launch tube before the main motor ignited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup> This allowed missiles to be launched in a water depth of 40-60m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bukharin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bukharin-5">[5]</a></sup> The propulsion system used an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRFNA" title="IRFNA">IRFNA</a>-amine fuel, <i>AK-27I/TG-02</i>. AK-27I was a mixture of 73% nitric acid, 27% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> passivant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astronautix_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Astronautix-6">[6]</a></sup> The fuel was originally used in the <a href="/wiki/Wasserfall" title="Wasserfall">Wasserfall</a> rocket under the name <a href="/wiki/Tonka_(fuel)" title="Tonka (fuel)">TONKA-250</a> and consisted of 50% <a href="/wiki/Triethylamine" title="Triethylamine">triethylamine</a> and 50% <a href="/wiki/Xylidine" title="Xylidine">xylidine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astronautix_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Astronautix-6">[6]</a></sup> This gave the R-21 a range of 1,400 km (760 nmi), double that of first-generation sub-launched missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bukharin_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bukharin-5">[5]</a></sup> The Naval Institute Guide suggests that the range was initially 1300 km, and extended to 1650 km later in the life of the missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIGSN_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-NIGSN-1">[1]</a></sup> There was a single warhead of approximately 800 kilotons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIGSN_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-NIGSN-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-21_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>R-21</b> (4K55) - original design</li>
<li><b>R-21A</b> (4K55A) - subsequent modification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There was some confusion about the SS-N-4/5/6 series of missiles in the West, the SS-N-5 is normally given the <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> 'Sark' like the SS-N-4 first carried by the Golf submarines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but some variants were assigned the name 'Serb' normally used for the SS-N-6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIGSN_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-NIGSN-1">[1]</a></sup> Jane's uses 'Sark'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-21_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The missiles replaced first-generation R-11FM and R-13 missiles on some Golf (Project 629) and Hotel (Project 658) class SSBNs, with three missiles per submarine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSWS_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-JSWS-2">[2]</a></sup> between 1963 and 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bukharin_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bukharin-5">[5]</a></sup> They were succeeded from 1967 by <a href="/wiki/Yankee-class_submarine" title="Yankee-class submarine">Yankee-class submarines</a> carrying 2400 km-range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-27_(missile)" title="R-27 (missile)">R-27</a> (SS-N-6 'Serb') missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Seven of the eight Hotel I (Project 658) subs were upgraded to Hotel II (Project 658M) standard, which were retired by 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
