<p><b>Robin George Collingwood</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 22 February 1889 – 9 January 1943) was an English <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, historian, and archaeologist. He is best known for his philosophical works including <i>The Principles of Art</i> (1938) and the posthumously published <i>The Idea of History</i> (1946).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Collingwood was born in <a href="/wiki/Cartmel" title="Cartmel">Cartmel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grange-over-Sands" title="Grange-over-Sands">Grange-over-Sands</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, the son of the artist and archaeologist <a href="/wiki/W._G._Collingwood" title="W. G. Collingwood">W. G. Collingwood</a>, who had acted as <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin's</a> private secretary in the final years of Ruskin's life. Collingwood's mother was also an artist and a talented pianist. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Rugby_School" title="Rugby School">Rugby School</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/University_College,_Oxford" title="University College, Oxford">University College, Oxford</a>, where he gained a First in Classical Moderations (Greek and Latin) in 1910 and a congratulatory First in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greats" title="Greats">Greats</a> (Ancient History and Philosophy) in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Prior to graduation he was elected a fellow of <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_College,_Oxford" title="Pembroke College, Oxford">Pembroke College, Oxford</a>.</p>
<p>Collingwood was a fellow of <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_College,_Oxford" title="Pembroke College, Oxford">Pembroke College, Oxford</a>, for some 15 years until becoming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waynflete_Professor_of_Metaphysical_Philosophy" title="Waynflete Professor of Metaphysical Philosophy">Waynflete Professor of Metaphysical Philosophy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_College,_Oxford" title="Magdalen College, Oxford">Magdalen College, Oxford</a>. He was the only pupil of <a href="/wiki/Francis_J._Haverfield" title="Francis J. Haverfield">F. J. Haverfield</a> to survive World War I. Important influences on Collingwood were the Italian Idealists <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Croce" title="Benedetto Croce">Benedetto Croce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gentile" title="Giovanni Gentile">Giovanni Gentile</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guido_de_Ruggiero" title="Guido de Ruggiero">Guido de Ruggiero</a>, the last of whom was also a close friend. Other important influences were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G._W._F._Hegel" title="G. W. F. Hegel">Hegel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">Giambattista Vico</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._H._Bradley" title="F. H. Bradley">F. H. Bradley</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Alexander_Smith" title="John Alexander Smith">J. A. Smith</a>.</p>
<p>After several years of increasingly debilitating strokes Collingwood died at <a href="/wiki/Coniston,_Cumbria" title="Coniston, Cumbria">Coniston</a>, Lancashire, in 1943. He was a practising Anglican throughout his life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosopher">Philosopher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosopher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_history">Philosophy of history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Philosophy of history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Collingwood is widely noted for <i>The Idea of History</i> (1946), a work collated from various sources soon after his death by his pupil T. M. Knox. It came to be a major inspiration for philosophy of history in the English-speaking world and is extensively cited, leading commentator <a href="/wiki/Louis_Mink" title="Louis Mink">Louis Mink</a> to ironically remark that Collingwood is coming to be "the best known neglected thinker of our time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Collingwood held that history could not be studied in the same way as natural science because the internal thought processes of historical persons could not be perceived with the physical senses, and past historical events could not be directly observed. He suggested that a historian must "reconstruct" history by using "historical imagination" to "re-enact" the thought processes of historical persons based on information and evidence from historical sources.</p>
<p>Collingwood pointed out a fundamental difference between knowing things in the present (or in the natural sciences) and knowing history. To come to know things in the present or about things in the natural sciences, “real” things can be observed, as they are in existence or that have substance right now.</p>
<p>The problem with coming to know things about history is that while past human actions actually or really happened, they took place in the past. The actions, then, have no real existence or substance at the point in time that the historian is studying them. Based on the understanding that the events and actions that historians study have already happened, they are finished and so cannot actually be observed. Collingwood maintained that historians must use their imaginations to reconstruct and understand the past. Because human events that have already taken place cannot be observed, he argued that they must be imagined.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_art">Philosophy of art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Philosophy of art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>The Principles of Art</i> (1938) comprises Collingwood's most developed treatment of <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetic</a> questions. Collingwood held (following Croce) that works of art are essentially expressions of emotion. For Collingwood, an important social role of the artist is to clarify and articulate emotions from his or her community.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_philosophy">Political philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In politics Collingwood defended the ideals of what he called liberalism "in its Continental sense":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The essence of this conception is .. the idea of a community as governing itself by fostering the free expression of all political opinions that take shape within it, and finding some means of reducing this multiplicity of opinions to a unity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeologist">Archaeologist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Archaeologist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Collingwood was not just a philosopher of history but also a practising historian and archaeologist. He was, during his time, a leading authority on Roman Britain: he spent his term time at Oxford teaching philosophy but devoted his long vacations to archaeology.</p>
<p>He began work along <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a>. The family home was at Coniston in the Lake District and his father was a leading figure in the Cumberland and Westmorland Archaeological Society. Collingwood was drawn in on a number of excavations and put forward the theory that Hadrian’s Wall was not so much a fighting platform but an elevated sentry walk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also put forward the suggestion that Hadrian's defensive system also included a number of forts along the Cumberland coast.</p>
<p>He was very active in the 1930 Wall Pilgrimage for which he prepared the ninth edition of Bruce's Handbook.</p>
<p>His final and most controversial excavation in Cumbria was that of a circular ring ditch near Penrith known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Arthur%27s_Round_Table_(henge)" title="King Arthur's Round Table (henge)">King Arthur's Round Table (henge)</a> in 1937. It appeared to be a Neolithic henge monument, and Collingwood’s excavations, failing to find conclusive evidence of Neolithic activity, nevertheless found the base of two stone pillars, a possible cremation trench and some post holes. Sadly, his subsequent ill health prevented him undertaking a second season so the work was handed over to the German prehistorian <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Bersu" title="Gerhard Bersu">Gerhard Bersu</a>, who queried some of Collingwood’s findings. However, recently, Grace Simpson, the daughter of the excavator F.G. Simpson, has queried Bersu's work and largely rehabilitated Collingwood as an excavator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also began what was to be the major work of his archaeological career, preparing a corpus of the <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_Inscriptions_of_Britain" title="Roman Inscriptions of Britain">Roman Inscriptions of Britain</a></i>, which involved travelling all over Britain to see the inscriptions and draw them; he eventually prepared drawings of nearly 900 inscriptions. It was finally published in 1965 by his student R.P. Wright.</p>
<p>He also published two major archaeological works. The first, somewhat surprisingly for a philosopher was <i>The Archaeology of Roman Britain</i>, a handbook in sixteen chapters covering first the archaeological sites (fortresses, towns and temples and portable antiquities) inscriptions, coins, pottery and brooches. Mortimer Wheeler in a review,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> remarked that "it seemed at first a trifle off beat that he should immerse himself in so much museum-like detail… but I felt sure that this was incidental to his primary mission to organise his own thinking".</p>
<p>However, his most important work was his contribution to the first volume of the Oxford History of England, <i>Roman Britain and the English Settlements</i> of which he wrote the major part, <a href="/wiki/Nowell_Myres" title="Nowell Myres">Nowell Myres</a> adding the second smaller part on English settlements. The book was in many ways revolutionary for it set out to write the story of Roman Britain from an archaeological rather than a historical viewpoint, putting into practice his own belief in 'Question and Answer' archaeology.</p>
<p>The result was alluring and influential. However, as Ian Richmond wrote: 'The general reader may discover too late that it has one major defect. It does not sufficiently distinguish between objective and subjective and combines both in a subtle and apparently objective presentation'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most notorious passage is that on Romano-British art; he says: "The impression that constantly haunts the archaeologist, like a bad smell, is that of an ugliness that plagues the place like a London fog".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Collingwood’s most important contribution to British archaeology was his insistence on Question and Answer archaeology: excavations should not take place unless there is a question to be answered. It is a philosophy which, as <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Birley" title="Anthony Birley">Anthony Birley</a> points out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> has been incorporated by English Heritage into the conditions for Scheduled Monuments Consent. Still, it has always been surprising that the proponents of the "new" archaeology in the 1960s and the 70s have entirely ignored the work of Collingwood, the one major archaeologist who was also a major professional philosopher.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Author">Author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outside archaeology and philosophy, he also published the travel book <i>The First Mate's Log of a Voyage to Greece</i> (1940), an account of a yachting voyage in the Mediterranean, in the company of several of his students.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Ransome" title="Arthur Ransome">Arthur Ransome</a> was a family friend, and learned to sail in their boat, subsequently teaching his sibling's children to sail. Ransome loosely based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_characters_in_Arthur_Ransome_books#The_Swallows" title="List of characters in Arthur Ransome books">the Swallows</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Swallows_and_Amazons_series" title="Swallows and Amazons series">Swallows and Amazons series</a></i> on his sibling's children.</p>
