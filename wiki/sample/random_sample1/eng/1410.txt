<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Request for Comments</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note"><span class="plainlinks selfreference noprint">For the Wikipedia process, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Requests_for_comment" title="Wikipedia:Requests for comment">Wikipedia:Requests for comment</a>.</span></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For help automatically creating an external link to a specific IETF Request for Comment, see <a href="/wiki/Help:Magic_links#RFC" title="Help:Magic links">Help:Magic links § RFC</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>Request for Comments</b> (<b>RFC</b>) is a type of publication from the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a> (IETF) and the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Society" title="Internet Society">Internet Society</a> (ISOC), the principal technical development and standards-setting bodies for the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.</p>
<p>An RFC is authored by engineers and <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientists</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum" title="Memorandum">memorandum</a> describing methods, behaviors, research, or innovations applicable to the working of the Internet and Internet-connected systems. It is submitted either for <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a> or simply to convey new concepts, information, or (occasionally) engineering humor. The IETF adopts some of the proposals published as RFCs as <a href="/wiki/Internet_Standard" title="Internet Standard">Internet Standards</a>.</p>
<p>Request for Comments documents were invented by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Crocker" title="Steve Crocker">Steve Crocker</a> in 1969 to help record unofficial notes on the development of <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a>. RFCs have since become official documents of Internet <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specifications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" title="Communications protocol">communications protocols</a>, procedures, and events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inception of the RFC format occurred in 1969 as part of the seminal <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a> project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Today, it is the official publication channel for the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF), the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Architecture_Board" title="Internet Architecture Board">Internet Architecture Board</a> (IAB), and –  to some extent –  the global community of computer network researchers in general.</p>
<p>The authors of the first RFCs <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewrote</a> their work and circulated <a href="/wiki/Hard_copy" title="Hard copy">hard copies</a> among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">ARPA</a> researchers. Unlike the modern RFCs, many of the early RFCs were actual requests for comments and were titled as such to avoid sounding too declarative and to encourage discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The RFC leaves questions open and is written in a less formal style. This less formal style is now typical of <a href="/wiki/Internet_Draft" title="Internet Draft">Internet Draft</a> documents, the precursor step before being approved as an RFC.</p>
<p>In December 1969, researchers began distributing new RFCs via the newly operational ARPANET. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1" rel="nofollow">RFC 1</a>, entitled "Host Software", was written by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Crocker" title="Steve Crocker">Steve Crocker</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a> (UCLA), and published on April 7, 1969. Although written by Steve Crocker, the RFC emerged from an early <a href="/wiki/Working_group" title="Working group">working group</a> discussion between Steve Crocker, Steve Carr and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Rulifson" title="Jeff Rulifson">Jeff Rulifson</a>.</p>
<p>In <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3" rel="nofollow">RFC 3</a>, which first defined the RFC series, Crocker started attributing the RFC series to the Network Working Group. Rather than being a formal committee, it was a loose association of researchers interested in the ARPANET project. In effect, it included anyone who wanted to join the meetings and discussions about the project.</p>
<p>Many of the subsequent RFCs of the 1970s also came from UCLA, because UCLA was one of the first <a href="/wiki/Interface_Message_Processor" title="Interface Message Processor">Interface Message Processors</a> (IMPs) on ARPANET. The <a href="/wiki/Augmentation_Research_Center" title="Augmentation Research Center">Augmentation Research Center</a> (ARC) at <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">Stanford Research Institute</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Engelbart" title="Douglas Engelbart">Douglas Engelbart</a>, was another of the four first ARPANET <a href="/wiki/Node_(networking)" title="Node (networking)">nodes</a> and the source of early RFCs. The ARC became the first network information center (<a href="/wiki/InterNIC" title="InterNIC">InterNIC</a>), which was managed by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_J._Feinler" title="Elizabeth J. Feinler">Elizabeth J. Feinler</a> to distribute the RFCs along with other network information. From 1969 until 1998, <a href="/wiki/Jon_Postel" title="Jon Postel">Jon Postel</a> served as the RFC <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>. On his death in 1998, his obituary was published as <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2468" rel="nofollow">RFC 2468</a>.</p>
<p>Following the expiration of the original ARPANET contract with the U.S. federal government, the Internet Society, acting on behalf of the IETF, contracted with the Networking Division of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> (USC) <a href="/wiki/Information_Sciences_Institute" title="Information Sciences Institute">Information Sciences Institute</a> (ISI) to assume the editorship and publishing responsibilities under the direction of the IAB. Sandy Ginoza joined USC/ISI in 1999 to work on RFC editing, and Alice Hagens in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Braden" title="Bob Braden">Bob Braden</a> took over the role of RFC project lead, while <a href="/wiki/Joyce_K._Reynolds" title="Joyce K. Reynolds">Joyce K. Reynolds</a> continued to be part of the team until October 13, 2006.</p>
<p>In July 2007, <i>streams</i> of RFCs were defined, so that the editing duties could be divided. IETF documents came from IETF working groups or submissions sponsored by an IETF area director from the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Steering_Group" title="Internet Engineering Steering Group">Internet Engineering Steering Group</a>. The IAB can publish its own documents. A research stream of documents comes from the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Research_Task_Force" title="Internet Research Task Force">Internet Research Task Force</a> (IRTF), and an independent stream from other outside sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A new model was proposed in 2008, refined, and published in August 2009, splitting the task into several roles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> including the RFC Series Advisory Group (RSAG). The model was updated in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The streams were also refined in December 2009, with standards defined for their style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfc5741_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rfc5741-8">[8]</a></sup> In January 2010 the RFC editor function was moved to a contractor, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Association_Management_Solutions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Association Management Solutions (page does not exist)">Association Management Solutions</a>, with Glenn Kowack serving as interim series editor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In late 2011, Heather Flanagan was hired as the permanent RFC Series Editor. Also at that time, an RFC Series Oversight Committee (RSOC) was created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_evolution">Production and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The RFC Editor assigns each RFC a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_number" title="Serial number">serial number</a>. Once assigned a number and published, an RFC is never rescinded or modified; if the document requires amendments, the authors publish a revised document. Therefore, some RFCs supersede others; the superseded RFCs are said to be <i>deprecated</i>, <i>obsolete</i>, or <i>obsoleted by</i> the superseding RFC. Together, the serialized RFCs compose a continuous historical record of the evolution of Internet standards and practices. The RFC process is documented in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">RFC 2026</a> (<i>The Internet Standards Process, Revision 3</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RFC_Index_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RFC_Index-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The RFC production process differs from the <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standardization</a> process of formal standards organizations such as <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>. Internet technology experts may submit an <a href="/wiki/Internet_Draft" title="Internet Draft">Internet Draft</a> without support from an external institution. Standards-track RFCs are published with approval from the IETF, and are usually produced by experts participating in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IETF_Working_Group" title="IETF Working Group">working groups</a>, which first publish an Internet Draft. This approach facilitates initial rounds of peer review before documents mature into RFCs.</p>
<p>The RFC tradition of pragmatic, experience-driven, after-the-fact standards authorship accomplished by individuals or small working groups can have important advantages over the more formal, committee-driven process typical of ISO and national standards bodies.</p>
<p>Most RFCs use a common set of terms such as "MUST" and "NOT RECOMMENDED" (as defined by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2119" rel="nofollow">RFC 2119</a>), <a href="/wiki/Augmented_Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Augmented Backus–Naur form">augmented Backus–Naur form</a> (ABNF) (<a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5234" rel="nofollow">RFC 5234</a>) as a meta-language, and simple text-based formatting, in order to keep the RFCs consistent and easy to understand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RFC_Index_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-RFC_Index-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-series">Sub-series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sub-series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RFC series contains three sub-series for <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">IETF</a> RFCs:</p>
<dl>
<dt>BCP</dt>
<dd>Best Current Practice; mandatory IETF RFCs not on standards track, see <a href="#Status_.22best_current_practice.22">below</a>.</dd>
<dt>FYI</dt>
<dd>For Your Information; informational RFCs promoted by the IETF as specified in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1150" rel="nofollow">RFC 1150</a> (FYI 1). In 2011, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6360" rel="nofollow">RFC 6360</a> obsoleted FYI 1 and concluded this sub-series.</dd>
<dt>STD</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Internet_Standard" title="Internet Standard">Standard</a>; this used to be the third and highest maturity level of the IETF standards track specified in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">RFC 2026</a> (BCP 9). In 2011 <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6410" rel="nofollow">RFC 6410</a> (a new part of BCP 9) reduced the standards track to two maturity levels.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Streams">Streams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Streams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are four streams of RFCs: (1) <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">IETF</a>, (2) <a href="/wiki/Internet_Research_Task_Force" title="Internet Research Task Force">IRTF</a>, (3) <a href="/wiki/Internet_Architecture_Board" title="Internet Architecture Board">IAB</a>, and (4) <i>independent submission</i>. Only the IETF creates BCPs and RFCs on standards track. An <i>independent submission</i> is checked by the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Steering_Group" title="Internet Engineering Steering Group">IESG</a> for conflicts with IETF work; the quality is assessed by an <i>independent submission editorial board</i>. In other words, IRTF and <i>independent </i> RFCs are supposed to contain relevant info or experiments for the Internet at large not in conflict with IETF work; compare <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc4846" rel="nofollow">RFC 4846</a>, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5742" rel="nofollow">RFC 5742</a>, and <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5744" rel="nofollow">RFC 5744</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Obtaining_RFCs">Obtaining RFCs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Obtaining RFCs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The official source for RFCs on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> is the <a class="external text" href="//www.rfc-editor.org/rfc.html" rel="nofollow">RFC Editor</a>. Almost any published RFC can be retrieved via a <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URL</a> of the form http://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc5000.txt, shown for <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5000" rel="nofollow">RFC 5000</a>.</p>
<p>Every RFC is submitted as plain <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> text and is published in that form, but may also be available in other <a href="/wiki/File_format" title="File format">formats</a>.</p>
<p>For easy access to the metadata of an RFC, including abstract, keywords, author(s), publication date, errata, status, and especially later updates, the RFC Editor site offers a search form with many features. A redirection sets some efficient parameters, example: <a class="extiw" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5000" title="rfc:5000">rfc:5000</a>.</p>
<p>The official <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Serial_Number" title="International Standard Serial Number">International Standard Serial Number</a> (ISSN) of the RFC series is 2070-1721.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfc5741_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-rfc5741-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Not all RFCs are standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Each RFC is assigned a designation with regard to status within the Internet standardization process. This status is one of the following: <i>Informational</i>, <i>Experimental</i>, <i>Best Current Practice</i>, <i>Standards Track</i>, or <i>Historic</i>.</p>
<p>Each RFC is static; if the document is changed, it is submitted again and assigned a new RFC number.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Standards_Track.22">"Standards Track"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: "Standards Track"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Internet_Standard#Internet_Standards" title="Internet Standard">Internet Standard § Internet Standards</a></div>
<p>Standards-track documents are further divided into <i>Proposed Standard</i>, <i>Draft Standard</i>, and <i>Internet Standard</i> documents.</p>
<p>Only the IETF, represented by the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Steering_Group" title="Internet Engineering Steering Group">Internet Engineering Steering Group</a> (IESG), can approve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_standard" title="Internet standard">standards-track</a> RFCs.</p>
<p>If an RFC becomes an Internet Standard (STD), it is assigned an STD number but retains its RFC number. The definitive list of Internet Standards is the <a class="external text" href="http://www.rfc-editor.org/standards" rel="nofollow">Official Internet Protocol Standards</a>. Previously STD 1 used to maintain a snapshot of the list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>When an Internet Standard is updated, its STD number stays the same, now referring to a new RFC or set of RFCs. A given Internet Standard, STD <i>n</i>, may be RFCs <i>x</i> and <i>y</i> at a given time, but later the same standard may be updated to be RFC <i>z</i> instead. For example, in 2007 <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3700" rel="nofollow">RFC 3700</a> was an Internet Standard—STD 1—and in May 2008 it was replaced with <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5000" rel="nofollow">RFC 5000</a>, so <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3700" rel="nofollow">RFC 3700</a> changed to <i>Historic</i>, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5000" rel="nofollow">RFC 5000</a> became an Internet Standard, and as of May 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> STD 1 is <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5000" rel="nofollow">RFC 5000</a>.</p>
<p>(Best Current Practices work in a similar fashion; BCP <i>n</i> refers to a certain RFC or set of RFCs, but which RFC or RFCs may change over time).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Informational.22">"Informational"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Informational"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <i>informational</i> RFC can be nearly anything from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Fools%27_Day_RFC" title="April Fools' Day RFC">April 1 jokes</a> to widely recognized essential RFCs like <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> Structure and Delegation (<a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1591" rel="nofollow">RFC 1591</a>). Some informational RFCs formed the <abbr title="For Your Information">FYI</abbr> sub-series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Experimental.22">"Experimental"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title='Edit section: "Experimental"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <i>experimental</i> RFC can be an IETF document or an individual submission to the 'RFC Editor'. A draft is designated experimental if it is unclear the proposal will work as intended or unclear if the proposal will be widely adopted. An experimental RFC may be promoted to standards track if it becomes popular and works well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Best_Current_Practice.22">"Best Current Practice"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: "Best Current Practice"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_Current_Practice" title="Best Current Practice">Best Current Practice</a> subseries collects administrative documents and other texts which are considered as official rules and not only <i>informational</i>, but which do not affect <i>over the wire data</i>. The border between standards track and BCP is often unclear. If a document only affects the Internet Standards Process, like BCP 9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> or IETF administration, it is clearly a BCP. If it only defines rules and regulations for <a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">Internet Assigned Numbers Authority</a> (IANA) registries it is less clear; most of these documents are BCPs, but some are on the standards track.</p>
<p>The BCP series also covers technical recommendations for how to practice Internet standards; for instance the recommendation to use source filtering to make DoS attacks more difficult (<a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2827" rel="nofollow">RFC 2827</a>: "<i>Network Ingress Filtering: Defeating Denial of Service Attacks which employ IP Source Address Spoofing</i>") is <a class="external text" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/bcp38" rel="nofollow">BCP 38</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Historic.22">"Historic"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title='Edit section: "Historic"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>historic</i> RFC is one that the technology defined by the RFC is no longer recommended for use, which differs from "Obsoletes" header in a replacement RFC. For example, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc821" rel="nofollow">RFC 821</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMTP" title="SMTP">SMTP</a>) itself is obsoleted by various newer RFCs, but SMTP itself is still "current technology", so it is not in "Historic" status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On the other hand, since <a href="/wiki/Border_Gateway_Protocol" title="Border Gateway Protocol">BGP version 4</a> has entirely superseded earlier BGP versions, the RFCs describing those earlier versions (e.g. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1267" rel="nofollow">RFC 1267</a>) have been designated historic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Unknown.22">"Unknown"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Request_for_Comments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: "Unknown"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Status <i>unknown</i> is used for some very old RFCs, where it is unclear which status the document would get if it were published today. Some of these RFCs would not be published at all today; an early RFC was often just that: a simple request for comments, not intended to specify a protocol, administrative procedure, or anything else for which the RFC series is used today.</p>
