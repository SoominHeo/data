<p>The <b>RIM-66 Standard MR</b> (SM-1MR/SM-2MR) is a medium-range <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM), with a secondary role as <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">anti-ship missile</a>, originally developed for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> (USN). The SM-1 was developed as a replacement for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIM-2" title="RIM-2">RIM-2 Terrier</a> and <a href="/wiki/RIM-24_Tartar" title="RIM-24 Tartar">RIM-24 Tartar</a> that were deployed in the 1950s on a variety of USN ships. The <a href="/wiki/RIM-67_Standard" title="RIM-67 Standard">RIM-67 Standard</a> (SM-1ER/SM-2ER) is an extended range version of this missile with a <a href="/wiki/Solid_rocket_booster" title="Solid rocket booster">solid rocket booster</a> stage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Standard missile program was started in 1963 to produce a family of missiles to replace existing guided missiles used by the Terrier, Talos, and Tartar guided missile launch systems. The intention was to produce a new generation of guided missiles that could be retrofit to existing guided missile systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Missile_1">Standard Missile 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standard Missile 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66A is the medium ranged version of the Standard missile and was initially developed as a replacement for the earlier RIM-24C as part of the <a href="/wiki/Tartar_Guided_Missile_Fire_Control_System" title="Tartar Guided Missile Fire Control System">Mk74 "Tartar" Guided Missile Fire Control System</a>. It used the same fuselage as the earlier Tartar missile, for easier use with existing launchers and magazines for that system. The RIM-66A/B while looking like the earlier RIM-24C on the exterior is a different missile internally with redesigned electronics and a more reliable homing system and fuse that make it more capable than its predecessor. The RIM-66A/B Standard MR, (SM-1MR Block I to V) was used during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. The only remaining version of the Standard missile 1 in service is the RIM-66E (SM-1MR Block VI). While no longer in service with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USN" title="USN">USN</a>, the RIM-66E is still in service with many navies globally and is expected to remain in service until 2020.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Missile_2">Standard Missile 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standard Missile 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66C/D Standard MR (SM-2MR Block I) was developed in the 1970s and was a key part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegis_combat_system" title="Aegis combat system">Aegis combat system</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Threat_Upgrade" title="New Threat Upgrade">New Threat Upgrade</a> (NTU). The SM-2MR introduced inertial and command mid-course guidance. The missile's autopilot is programmed to fly the most efficient path to the target and can receive course corrections from the ground. Target illumination for semi-active homing is needed only for a few seconds in the terminal phase of the interception. This capability enables the Aegis combat system and New Threat Upgrade equipped vessels to time share illumination radars, greatly increasing the number of targets that can be engaged in quick succession. Mk 41 VLS adopts modular design concept, which result in different versions that vary in size and weight. The length comes in three sizes: 209 in (530 cm) for the self-defense version, 266 in (680 cm) for the tactical version, and 303 in (770 cm) for the strike version. The empty weight for an 8-cell module is 26,800 lb (12,200 kg) for the self-defense version, 29,800 lb (13,500 kg) for the tactical version, and 32,000 lb (15,000 kg) for the strike version.</p>
<p>In the middle 1980s, the SM-2MR was deployed via Mk 41 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_Launching_System" title="Vertical Launching System">Vertical Launch System</a> (VLS) aboard <a href="/wiki/USS_Bunker_Hill_(CG-52)" title="USS Bunker Hill (CG-52)">USS <i>Bunker Hill</i></a>, the first U.S. Navy ship to deploy a vertical launcher. VLS has, since 2003, been the only launcher used for the Standard missile in the U.S. Navy aboard <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga-class_cruiser" title="Ticonderoga-class cruiser"><i>Ticonderoga</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arleigh_Burke-class_destroyer" title="Arleigh Burke-class destroyer"><i>Arleigh Burke</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>.</p>
<p>The United States Navy is committed to keeping the Standard Missile 2 medium-range viable until 2035.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SM-1 and SM-2 were continuously upgraded through Blocks (see below).</p>
<p>The Standard can also be used against ships, either at line-of-sight range using its semi-active homing mode, or over the horizon using inertial guidance and terminal infrared homing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contractors">Contractors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contractors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Standard missiles were constructed by <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a> Pomona Division until 1992, when it became part of the Hughes Missile Systems Company. Hughes formed a joint venture with Raytheon called Standard Missile Company (SMCo). Hughes Missile Systems was eventually sold to Raytheon making it the sole contractor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Standard missile one became operational in 1968. The missile was utilized by ships equipped with the <a href="/wiki/Tartar_Guided_Missile_Fire_Control_System" title="Tartar Guided Missile Fire Control System">Tartar Guided Missile Fire Control System</a>. The missile saw its first combat use in the early 1970s in the Vietnam war.</p>
<p>The Standard Missile Two became operational in the late 1970s and was deployed operationally with the Aegis Combat System in 1983. Both Standard one and two were used against both surface and air targets during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Praying_Mantis" title="Operation Praying Mantis">Operation Praying Mantis</a>. On July 3, 1988, <a href="/wiki/USS_Vincennes_(CG-49)" title="USS Vincennes (CG-49)">USS <i>Vincennes</i></a> mistakenly shot down <a href="/wiki/Iran_Air_Flight_655" title="Iran Air Flight 655">Iran Air Flight 655</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">A300B2</a>, using two SM-2MR missiles from her forward launcher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Report_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Report-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1988 the Iranian <a href="/wiki/La_Combattante_IIa-class_fast_attack_craft" title="La Combattante IIa-class fast attack craft"><i>Kaman</i>-class</a> missile boat <i>Joshan</i> was disabled by RIM-66 Standard missiles during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Praying_Mantis" title="Operation Praying Mantis">Operation Praying Mantis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 9, 2016 <a href="/wiki/USS_Mason_(DDG-87)" title="USS Mason (DDG-87)">USS <i>Mason</i></a> fired two SM-2 (RIM-66 variant) Standard missiles at incoming <a href="/wiki/Houthi_insurgency_in_Yemen" title="Houthi insurgency in Yemen">Houthi</a> anti-ship missiles. It is unknown if the SM-2 Standards were responsible for intercepting the cruise missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment_history">Deployment history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Deployment history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Standard missile is designated by blocks depending upon their technological package.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-1_Medium_Range_Block_I.2FII.2FIII.2FIV.2C_RIM-66A">SM-1 Medium Range Block I/II/III/IV, RIM-66A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: SM-1 Medium Range Block I/II/III/IV, RIM-66A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The First Standard missiles entered service in the USN in 1967. Blocks I, II, and III were preliminary versions. Block IV was the production version. This missile was a replacement for the earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIM-24" title="RIM-24">RIM-24C</a> Tartar missile.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-1_Medium_Range_Block_V.2C_RIM-66B">SM-1 Medium Range Block V, RIM-66B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: SM-1 Medium Range Block V, RIM-66B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66B introduced changes that resulted in higher reliability. A new faster reacting autopilot, a more powerful dual thrust rocket motor, and a new warhead were added. Many RIM-66A missiles were re-manufactured into RIM-66B.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-1_Medium_Range_Blocks_VI.2FVIA.2FVIB.2C_RIM-66E">SM-1 Medium Range Blocks VI/VIA/VIB, RIM-66E</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: SM-1 Medium Range Blocks VI/VIA/VIB, RIM-66E">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66E was the last version of the standard missile one medium-range. This version entered service in 1983<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raytheon_RIM-66_Standard_MR_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_RIM-66_Standard_MR-10">[10]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> and export customers. The RIM-66E was used by all remaining <a href="/wiki/Tartar_Guided_Missile_Fire_Control_System" title="Tartar Guided Missile Fire Control System">Tartar vessels</a> that were not modified to use the <a href="/wiki/New_Threat_Upgrade" title="New Threat Upgrade">New Threat Upgrade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hazard_Perry-class_frigate" title="Oliver Hazard Perry-class frigate"><i>Oliver Hazard Perry</i>-class frigates</a> which controlled it with the Mk92 fire control system. Production of this missile ended in 1987. The missile was retired from USN service in 2003; however there are a large number of this model in service abroad and it is expected to remain viable until 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNI_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNI-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-2_Medium_Range_Block_I.2C_RIM-66C.2FD">SM-2 Medium Range Block I, RIM-66C/D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: SM-2 Medium Range Block I, RIM-66C/D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66C was the first version of the Standard missile two. The missile became operational in 1978 with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegis_combat_system" title="Aegis combat system">Aegis combat system</a> fitted to the <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga-class_cruiser" title="Ticonderoga-class cruiser"><i>Ticonderoga</i>-class cruiser</a>. The RIM-66D was the SM-2 medium-range block I version for the New Threat Upgrade. The SM-2 incorporates a new autopilot giving it inertial guidance in all phases of flight except for the terminal intercept where semi-active radar homing is still used. This version is no longer in service; remaining missiles have either been remanufactured into later models or have been put in storage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-2_Medium_Range_Block_II.2C_RIM-66G.2FH.2FJ">SM-2 Medium Range Block II, RIM-66G/H/J</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: SM-2 Medium Range Block II, RIM-66G/H/J">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Block II missile was introduced in 1983 with a new rocket motor for longer range and a new warhead. The RIM-66G is for the Aegis combat system and the Mk26 missile launcher. The RIM-66H is for Aegis and the Mk41 vertical launcher. The RIM-66J is the version for the New Threat Upgrade. Block II missiles are no longer manufactured, and have been withdrawn from service. The remainder have either been put in storage, scrapped for spare parts, or remanufactured into later models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SM-2_Medium_Range_Block_III.2FIIIA.2FIIIB.2C_RIM-66K.2FL.2FM">SM-2 Medium Range Block III/IIIA/IIIB, RIM-66K/L/M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: SM-2 Medium Range Block III/IIIA/IIIB, RIM-66K/L/M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The RIM-66M is the version of the Standard missile two medium-range (SM-2MR) currently in service with the USN aboard <i>Ticonderoga</i>-class cruisers and <a href="/wiki/Arleigh_Burke-class_destroyer" title="Arleigh Burke-class destroyer"><i>Arleigh Burke</i>-class destroyers</a>. The missile is specifically designed for the Aegis Combat System and the <a href="/wiki/Mark_41_Vertical_Launching_System" title="Mark 41 Vertical Launching System">Mk41 Vertical launch system</a>. The Block III missiles differ from earlier blocks by the addition of the MK 45 MOD 9 target detecting device, for improved performance against low altitude targets. The Block IIIB missile additionally has a dual semi-active/infrared seeker for terminal homing. The dual seeker is intended for use in high-<a href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasure" title="Electronic countermeasure">ECM</a> environments, against targets over the horizon or with a small radar cross section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNI_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-USNI-11">[11]</a></sup> The seeker was originally developed for the canceled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIM-7" title="AIM-7">AIM-7R Sparrow air-to-air missile</a>. All USN Block III and IIIA missiles are to be upgraded to Block IIIB. Block IIIA missiles are operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Maritime_Self-Defense_Force" title="Japanese Maritime Self-Defense Force">Japanese Maritime Self-Defense Force</a> on its <a href="/wiki/Kong%C5%8D-class_destroyer" title="Kongō-class destroyer"><i>Kongō</i>-class</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atago-class_destroyer" title="Atago-class destroyer"><i>Atago</i>-class</a> Aegis destroyers. Aegis equipped vessels in the Spanish and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KDX-III" title="KDX-III">South Korean</a> navies use it as well. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Navy" title="Royal Netherlands Navy">Dutch</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Navy" title="German Navy">German</a> Navies have added it to the Anti-Air Warfare system, which uses the <a href="/wiki/Thales_Nederland" title="Thales Nederland">Thales Nederland</a> <a href="/wiki/Active_Phased_Array_Radar" title="Active Phased Array Radar">Active Phased Array Radar</a> and Smart-L radar. South Korean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KDX-II" title="KDX-II">KDX-II</a> destroyers use the block IIIA with a New Threat Upgrade compatible guided missile fire control system. Block III variants for Aegis and arm launchers are designated RIM-66L. Block III missiles for New Threat Upgrade systems are designated RIM-66K. Block IIIB missiles were not produced for the New Threat Upgrade. Blocks IIIA and IIIB are the current production versions. The Thales Nederland STIR 1.8 and 2.4 fire control systems are also supported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raytheon_Web_site_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_Web_site-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ticonderoga_class_cruiser" title="Ticonderoga class cruiser"><i>Ticonderoga</i>-class cruiser</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aegis_Combat_System" title="Aegis Combat System">Aegis Combat System</a> )</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arleigh_Burke_class_destroyer" title="Arleigh Burke class destroyer"><i>Arleigh Burke</i>-class destroyer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aegis_Combat_System" title="Aegis Combat System">Aegis Combat System</a> )</li>
</ul>
<p>RIM-66 has also been widely exported and is in service in other navies worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_Attack_Standard_Missile">Land Attack Standard Missile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RIM-66_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Land Attack Standard Missile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The RGM-165 LASM, also given the designation SM-4, was intended as means to give long-range precision fires in support of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marine_Corps" title="US Marine Corps">US Marine Corps</a>. Intended as an adaptation of the RIM-66, it retained the original MK 125 warhead and MK 104 rocket motor, with the radar seeker replaced by GPS/INS guidance. While test fired in 1997 using three modified RIM-66K SM-2MR Block III missiles, with 800 missiles set for replacement and IOC expected for 2003/2004, it was cancelled in 2002 due to limited capabilities against mobile or hardened targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
