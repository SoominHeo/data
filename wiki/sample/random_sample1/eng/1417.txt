<p><b><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Radio Nacional de España</span> <i>(</i>RNE</b>) (<i>National Radio of Spain</i>) is Spain's national <a href="/wiki/Public_broadcasting" title="Public broadcasting">public radio</a> service. Since 1973 it has formed, together with <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a href="/wiki/Televisi%C3%B3n_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Televisión Española">Televisión Española</a></span> (TVE), a part of <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Radiotelevisión Española</span> (<a href="/wiki/RTVE" title="RTVE">RTVE</a>), the corporation responsible for managing national public-service broadcasting in Spain.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_RNE">Origins of RNE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins of RNE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Radio Nacional de España</span></i> officially came into existence in <a href="/wiki/Salamanca" title="Salamanca">Salamanca</a> on 19 January 1937, at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (1936–1939), and was dependent upon the recently created <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Delegación de Estado para Prensa y Propaganda</span></i> (State Delegation for Press and Propaganda). The station's studios were in Palacio de Anaya, the headquarters of the <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Oficina de Prensa y Propaganda</span></i> (Office for Press and Propaganda), whose first directors were also those of RNE.</p>
<p>RNE's first <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a>, which had a broadcasting power of 20 kW and was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Telefunken" title="Telefunken">Telefunken</a>, was a gift from the government of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francoist <i>Estado Nuevo</i></a> (New State).</p>
<p>It was at this time that the immense propaganda potential of radio became apparent, and from 14 June 1937 RNE became the nationalists' leading radio station. That distinction had until then been held by <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Radio Castilla de Burgos</span></i>, which produced the information and propaganda that all of the radio stations that had fallen into the hands of the nationalist forces were obliged to carry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_post_Spanish_Civil_War_era">The post Spanish Civil War era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The post Spanish Civil War era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 6 October 1939, at the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, the leader of the victorious Nationalist forces, General <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>, issued an order subjecting private radio broadcasting to censorship by the official political party of the state, <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falange" title="Falange">FET y de las JONS</a></span></i>, and furthermore granting RNE the exclusive right to transmit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/News_bulletin" title="News bulletin">news bulletins</a>.</p>
<p>In consequence, all broadcasters (private as well as public) were obliged to connect twice daily with RNE and re-transmit the government-approved news bulletins produced by the official radio channel. These bulletins, normally broadcast in the early afternoon at 14.30 and in the late evening at 22.00, were officially entitled <i>Diario Hablado</i>, although – given their origin in the war dispatches (<i>partes de guerra</i>) of 1936–39 and their continued militaristic tone – they were long popularly referred to as <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">El parte</span></i>.</p>
<p>The only other sources of information available to radio listeners in Spain at that time were the Spanish-language bulletins broadcast by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and by French Radio from <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, as well as the programmes of Radio España Independiente, which was a radio station established by the Communist Party of Spain in exile in Moscow (although known as <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">La Pirenaica</span></i> since it was believed to broadcast from a location somewhere in the Pyrenees<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>Although from the time of the Civil War there had already been <a href="/wiki/International_broadcasting" title="International broadcasting">foreign broadcasts</a> in various languages, it was not until April 1945 that the installation of the central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_wave" title="Short wave">short wave</a> transmitter at Arganda del Rey (Madrid) would provide 40 kW of broadcasting power, which was very strong for this period. Foreign broadcasting thus acquired a great importance, with transmissions (in Spanish as much as in English) directed especially at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">America</a>.</p>
<p>In 1940, RNE's headquarters were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> During this post-<a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> and early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> era – before the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> arrival in Italy in 1943 and the German retreat from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalingrad" title="Stalingrad">Stalingrad</a> – RNE collaborated with the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a> in retransmitting in Spanish news from the official radio stations of Germany and Italy.</p>
<p>It was from this moment on that the slow journey of Spanish public radio began, motivated by the poor quality of the media on the one hand, and the international block on the other which impeded, until 1955, the entry of RNE into the <a href="/wiki/European_Broadcasting_Union" title="European Broadcasting Union">European Broadcasting Union</a>.</p>
<p>The end of the 1950s and beginning of the 1960s saw the introduction of advanced technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">frequency modulation</a> (FM) and transmissions in stereo. A parallel commercial channel, <i>Radio Peninsular</i>, was also created.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_60s_and_70s:_a_time_of_growth">The 60s and 70s: a time of growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 60s and 70s: a time of growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>1964 was the first year of a major restructuring exercise at RNE which was to see the establishment of a network of regional broadcasting centres equipped with powerful 250 to 500 kW <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediumwave" title="Mediumwave">mediumwave</a> transmitters. These gave RNE full coverage of not just the national territory but a good part, too, of the rest of Europe (especially at night). The regional transmitters normally all broadcast the same signal, relayed from the main studios in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, although provision was made for them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opt_out" title="Opt out">opt out</a> at certain times of day and transmit regional news from their own local studios. This was the foundation of today's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RNE_Radio_1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RNE Radio 1 (page does not exist)">RNE Radio 1</a>.</p>
<p>In November 1965 RNE opened a second network, using <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">FM</a> transmitters and specializing in music – taking advantage of the superior sound quality offered by this method of <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">transmission</a>. This network eventually became the RNE channel which is today known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_Cl%C3%A1sica" title="RNE Radio Clásica">Radio Clásica</a>.</p>
<p>In 1971 a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> transmitter was inaugurated at <a href="/wiki/Noblejas" title="Noblejas">Noblejas</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toledo_(province)" title="Toledo (province)">province of Toledo</a>. Intended for the broadcasting of RNE's external services (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Exterior_de_Espa%C3%B1a" title="Radio Exterior de España">Radio Exterior de España</a>), this transmitter was much more powerful than its predecessor sited at <a href="/wiki/Arganda_del_Rey" title="Arganda del Rey">Arganda del Rey</a>. These services were to undergo a far-reaching reform in 1975 when broadcasts in Russian and other <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, directed at audiences behind the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_curtain" title="Iron curtain">iron curtain</a>", were withdrawn in favour of programmes intended for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spaniards</a> and other Spanish-speaking people abroad.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_democratic_era">The democratic era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The democratic era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The arrival of democracy to Spain after the death of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a> in 1975 produced several changes. One of these was the end, on 25 October 1977, of the private broadcasters' obligation to connect with RNE for the transmission of daily news bulletins. From then on, each broadcaster was free to determine the content of its own news programmes.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1970s, the broadcasts of <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Tercer Programa</span></i> (RNE 3), which until then had only been transmitted in Madrid, were extended to the whole of Spain. RNE 3 offered educational and cultural programming, which was enlarged to include programmes on musical themes.</p>
<p>Throughout the Franco dictatorship a number of semi-official radio stations (<i>autorizadas</i>) had functioned in parallel with the private broadcasters and RNE, and belonged to organisations such as <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Confederación Nacional de Sindicatos</span></i> (National Confederation of Trade Unions), <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Movimiento</span></i>, and <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Organización Juvenil Espanola</span></i> (The Spanish Youth Organisation). These stations were merged in 1978 into <i>Radiocadena Española</i> (Spanish Radio Network). However, some of the transmitters had to be closed down because their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a> were not included in those assigned to Spain in the international agreements covering the distribution of the radio broadcasting spectrum. <i>Radiocadena Española</i> was merged in 1988 into Radio Nacional de España to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_R%C3%A0dio_4" title="RNE Ràdio 4">RNE Ràdio 4</a> (broadcasting in <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_5" title="RNE Radio 5">RNE Radio 5</a> (All-news radio station broadcasting in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RNE_today">RNE today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: RNE today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1989, <i>Radiocadena Española</i> and <i>Radio Nacional</i> were combined to produce the current format of six themed radio channels:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_Nacional" title="RNE Radio Nacional">Radio Nacional</a> (previously Radio 1) – <a href="/wiki/Generalist_channel" title="Generalist channel">Generalist channel</a> with a broad spectrum of mostly speech-based programming.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_Cl%C3%A1sica" title="RNE Radio Clásica">Radio Clásica</a> (previously Radio 2) – Concerts and classical music in general.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_3" title="RNE Radio 3">Radio 3</a> – RNE's "youth station", concentrating on pop, rock, world music, folk, and allied cultural events.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_R%C3%A0dio_4" title="RNE Ràdio 4">Ràdio 4</a> – Regional broadcasting in the <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan language</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNE_Radio_5" title="RNE Radio 5">Radio 5</a> – 24-hour news.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Exterior_de_Espa%C3%B1a" title="Radio Exterior de España">Radio Exterior de España</a> – <a href="/wiki/International_broadcasting" title="International broadcasting">International broadcasting</a> service on short wave, with an audience of 80 million listeners (surpassed only by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Radio" title="Vatican Radio">Vatican Radio</a>). This station is also transmitted via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcasting" title="Digital Audio Broadcasting">DAB</a> for Spain and by <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite</a>. Transmissions are in Spanish, French, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">Ladino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, Russian and English.</li>
</ul>
<p>These stations are also available <a class="external text" href="http://www.rtve.es/rne/RNE_Envivo/index.html" rel="nofollow">online</a> and via <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcast</a> (see External Links below).</p>
<p>Integrated into the state public broadcasting body RTVE (Radiotelevisión Española) in 1973, RNE today has been assigned the role of "state public radio service, which is an essential service for the community and for the cohesion of democratic societies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like its television broadcasting sister organisation, <a href="/wiki/Televisi%C3%B3n_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Televisión Española">TVE</a>, <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Radio Nacional</span></i> is wholly financed by public funds and does not air commercials in its programming.</p>
<p>In January 2012, RNE celebrated its 75th anniversary in the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felipe,_Prince_of_Asturias" title="Felipe, Prince of Asturias">Felipe, Prince of Asturias</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letizia,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Letizia, Princess of Asturias">Princess Letizia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
