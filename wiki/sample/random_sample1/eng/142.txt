<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Panic of 1896</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Panic of 1896</b> was an acute <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">economic depression</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> that was less serious than other panics of the era precipitated by a drop in <a href="/wiki/Free_silver" title="Free silver">silver reserves</a> and market concerns on the effects it would have on the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deflation" title="Deflation">Deflation</a> of commodities prices drove the <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock market</a> to new lows in a trend that began to reverse only after the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1896" title="United States presidential election, 1896">1896 election</a> of <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>. The failure of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Bank_of_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Bank of Illinois (page does not exist)">National Bank of Illinois</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> is remembered as one of the motivating factors in the sensational <a href="/wiki/Adolph_Luetgert" title="Adolph Luetgert">Adolph Luetgert</a> murder case. During the panic, <a href="/wiki/Call_money" title="Call money">call money</a> would reach 125 percent, the highest level since the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panic_of_1896&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Panic of 1896 had roots in the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1893" title="Panic of 1893">Panic of 1893</a>, and is seen as a continuation of that economic depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The drop in American gold reserves worsened the effects of the Panic of 1893, and the Panic of 1896 was given its own distinction. The <a href="/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1873" title="Coinage Act of 1873">Coinage Act of 1873</a> demonetized the use of silver in America, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resumption_Act_of_1875" title="Resumption Act of 1875">Resumption Act of 1875</a> further established the gold standard. This period of deflation was met with some resistance, as the agrarian <a href="/wiki/Populist_Party_(United_States,_1984)" title="Populist Party (United States, 1984)">Populist Party</a> formed to protest the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>, and reinstate the <a href="/wiki/Bimetallism" title="Bimetallism">bimetallic standard</a>, due to farmers’ inability to repay debts at lower prices, and silver miners loss of market share. Farmers also wanted to adopt the bimetallic standard because they could sell their crops at higher prices. The <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Silver_Purchase_Act" title="Sherman Silver Purchase Act">Sherman Silver Purchase of 1890</a> allowed limited use of silver in the American economy, but did not allow unlimited coinage as supporters of the “<a href="/wiki/Free_silver" title="Free silver">Free Silver</a>” movement wanted. The Silver Purchase did not work as most of the backers has intended, as a large portion of the buyers redeemed their coins with gold, causing the already pressured American gold reserves to deplete. The American gold reserves dropped to just $60 million in January 1895, which, combined with the subsequent <a href="/wiki/J._P._Morgan" title="J. P. Morgan">J. P. Morgan</a> bond episode, in which Morgan, in cooperation with the European <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschilds</a>, sold gold directly to the U. S. treasury, caused the public’s worry for the gold standard to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_of_1896">Election of 1896</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panic_of_1896&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Election of 1896">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Jennings_Bryan">William Jennings Bryan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panic_of_1896&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: William Jennings Bryan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populist_Party_(United_States,_1984)" title="Populist Party (United States, 1984)">Populist</a> party candidate <a href="/wiki/William_Jennings_Bryan" title="William Jennings Bryan">William Jennings Bryan</a> ran on the platform of "<a href="/wiki/Free_silver" title="Free silver">free silver</a>," in which he implored Americans to drop the gold standard and reinstate the coinage of silver in order to inflate the American economy. Bryan was an orator that gave many speeches on the issue, with the most famous one being his "<a href="/wiki/Cross_of_Gold_speech" title="Cross of Gold speech">Cross of Gold</a>" speech delivered on July 9, 1896. In this speech, Bryan laid out his belief that the existence of multiple forms of legal tender was necessary for the health of the national economy, as farmers would benefit from being able to sell their crops at a higher price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Bryan became both the Democratic and Populist party nominee due in part to his stances on the issues of the gold standard, which he believed could alleviate the Panic of 1896. Bryan was a dark horse candidate prior to his Cross of Gold speech, but his intense rhetoric resonated among many Americans that felt that a second, less valuable, form of legal tender was necessary for less wealthy Americans, and he quickly became the front-runner of both the Populist and Democratic parties.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_McKinley">William McKinley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panic_of_1896&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: William McKinley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a> wished to remain on the gold standard, unlike his opponent, Bryan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many bankers and businessmen were disturbed by Bryan's silver rhetoric, and McKinley was able to win the election. McKinley kept the United States on the gold standard by signing the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Standard_Act" title="Gold Standard Act">Gold Standard Act</a> in 1900, lessening the strength of both the <a href="/wiki/Bimetallism" title="Bimetallism">bimetallism</a> movement and public fears about the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">Gold Standard</a> by his successful navigation out of the Panic of 1896, as the economy recovered by 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Gold Standard Act formerly adopted gold as the lone standard for redeeming <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">paper money</a> in the United States, effectively stopping bimetallism in its tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panic_of_1896&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The economic hard times caused debates over whether America should remain on the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a> or use a <a href="/wiki/Bimetallism" title="Bimetallism">bimetallic standard</a>. The continued economic hardships after the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1893" title="Panic of 1893">Panic of 1893</a> and the 1895 Morgan Bonds into the Panic of 1896 increased American worry about the strength of the American economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Many members of the <a href="/wiki/Populist_Party_(United_States,_1984)" title="Populist Party (United States, 1984)">Populist Party</a> took the Jewish ancestry of the <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschilds</a> as a negative and a wave of <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitism</a> emerged within the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> During the Panic, the national unemployment rate increased from 13.7% in 1895 to 14.5% in 1896, which persisted until 1898.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A series of high-profile banker suicides took place in December 1896 and January 1897 in Chicago, Illinois due to the failure of the National Bank of Illinois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
