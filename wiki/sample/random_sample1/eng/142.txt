<p><b>Elisabeth of Austria</b> (24 December 1837 – 10 September 1898) was the wife of Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph of Austria">Franz Joseph I</a>, and thus <a href="/wiki/List_of_Austrian_consorts" title="List of Austrian consorts">Empress of Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Hungarian_consorts" title="List of Hungarian consorts">Queen of Hungary</a> and Queen consort of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia-Slavonia" title="Kingdom of Croatia-Slavonia">Croatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>.</p>
<p>Born into <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavarian</a> royalty, Elisabeth ("Sisi") enjoyed an informal upbringing before marrying Franz Joseph at the age of sixteen. The marriage thrust her into the much more formal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> court life, for which she was ill-prepared and which she found uncongenial. Early in the marriage she was at odds with her mother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Sophie_of_Bavaria" title="Princess Sophie of Bavaria">Princess Sophie</a>, who took over the rearing of Elisabeth's daughters, one of whom, <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Sophie_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Sophie of Austria">Sophie</a>, died in infancy. The birth of a male heir, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf,_Crown_Prince_of_Austria" title="Rudolf, Crown Prince of Austria">Rudolf</a>, improved her standing at court considerably, but her health suffered under the strain, and she would often visit Hungary for its more relaxed environment. She came to develop a deep kinship with Hungary, and helped to bring about the <a href="/wiki/Dual_monarchy" title="Dual monarchy">dual monarchy</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austria%E2%80%93Hungary" title="Austria–Hungary">Austria–Hungary</a> in 1867.</p>
<p>The death of her only son Rudolf, and his mistress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Vetsera" title="Mary Vetsera">Mary Vetsera</a>, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayerling_Incident" title="Mayerling Incident">murder–suicide tragedy</a> at his hunting lodge at <a href="/wiki/Mayerling" title="Mayerling">Mayerling</a> in 1889 was a blow from which Elisabeth never recovered. She withdrew from court duties and travelled widely, unaccompanied by her family. She was obsessively concerned with maintaining her youthful figure and beauty, demanding to be sewn into her leather corsets and spending two or three hours a day on her coiffure. While travelling in Geneva in 1898, she was stabbed to death by an Italian anarchist named <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Lucheni" title="Luigi Lucheni">Luigi Lucheni</a> who selected her because he had missed his chance to assassinate <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philippe,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans_(1869%E2%80%931926)" title="Prince Philippe, Duke of Orléans (1869–1926)">Prince Philippe, Duke of Orléans</a>, and wanted to kill the next member of royalty that he saw. Elisabeth was the longest serving Empress-consort of Austria, at 44 years.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duchess_in_Bavaria">Duchess in Bavaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Duchess in Bavaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Born <i>Elisabeth Amalie Eugenie</i> on 24 December 1837 in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, she was the fourth child of <a href="/wiki/Duke_Maximilian_Joseph_in_Bavaria" title="Duke Maximilian Joseph in Bavaria">Duke Maximilian Joseph in Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ludovika_of_Bavaria" title="Princess Ludovika of Bavaria">Princess Ludovika of Bavaria</a>, the half-sister of King <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a>. Maximilian was considered to be rather peculiar; he had a childish love of circuses and traveled the Bavarian countryside to escape his duties. The family home was at <a href="/wiki/Possenhofen_Castle" title="Possenhofen Castle">Possenhofen Castle</a>, far from the protocols of court. "Sisi" and her siblings grew up in a very unrestrained and unstructured environment; she often skipped her lessons to go riding about the countryside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1853, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Sophie_of_Bavaria" title="Princess Sophie of Bavaria">Princess Sophie of Bavaria</a>, the domineering mother of 23-year-old Emperor <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph</a>, preferring to have <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">a niece as a daughter-in-law</a> rather than a stranger, <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">arranged a marriage</a> between her son and her sister Ludovika's eldest daughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helene_in_Bavaria" title="Helene in Bavaria">Helene</a> ("Néné"). Although the couple had never met, Franz Joseph's obedience was taken for granted by the archduchess, who was once described as "the only man in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hofburg_Imperial_Palace" title="Hofburg Imperial Palace">Hofburg</a>" for her authoritarian manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Duchess and Helene were invited to journey to the resort of <a href="/wiki/Bad_Ischl" title="Bad Ischl">Bad Ischl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upper_Austria" title="Upper Austria">Upper Austria</a> to receive his formal proposal of marriage. Fifteen-year-old Sisi accompanied her mother and sister and they traveled from Munich in several coaches. They arrived late as the Duchess, prone to migraine, had to interrupt the journey; the coach with their gala dresses never did arrive. The family was still in mourning over the death of an aunt so they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mourning_dress" title="Mourning dress">dressed in black</a> and unable to change to more suitable clothing before meeting the young Emperor. While black did not suit eighteen-year-old Helene's dark coloring, it made her younger sister's blonder looks more striking by contrast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Helene was a pious, quiet young woman, and she and Franz Joseph felt ill at ease in each other's company, but he was instantly infatuated with her younger sister. He did not propose to Helene, but defied his mother and informed her that if he could not have Elisabeth, he would not marry at all. Five days later their betrothal was officially announced. The couple were married eight months later in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Augustinian_Church,_Vienna" title="Augustinian Church, Vienna">Augustinerkirche</a> on 24 April 1854. The marriage was finally consummated three days later, and Elisabeth received a <a href="/wiki/Dower" title="Dower">dower</a> equal to today's 240,000 USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Empress">Empress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Empress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="483" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg/220px-Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg/330px-Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg/440px-Amanda_Bergstedt_001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>After enjoying an informal and unstructured childhood, Elisabeth, who was shy and introverted by nature, and more so among the stifling formality of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> court life, had difficulty adapting to the Hofburg and its rigid protocols and strict <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a>. Within a few weeks, Elisabeth started to display health problems: she had fits of coughing and became anxious and frightened whenever she had to descend a narrow steep staircase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was surprised to find she was pregnant and gave birth to her first child, a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Sophie_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Sophie of Austria">Archduchess Sophie of Austria</a> (1855–1857), just ten months after her wedding. The elder Archduchess Sophie, who often referred to Elisabeth as a "silly young mother",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> not only named the child (after herself) without consulting the mother, but took complete charge of the baby, refusing to allow Elisabeth to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breastfeed" title="Breastfeed">breastfeed</a> or otherwise care for her own child. When a second daughter, <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Gisela_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Gisela of Austria">Archduchess Gisela of Austria</a> (1856–1932), was born a year later, the Archduchess took the baby away from Elisabeth as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fact that she had not produced a male heir made Elisabeth increasingly unwanted in the palace. One day she found a pamphlet on her desk with the following words underlined:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>...The natural destiny of a Queen is to give an heir to the throne. If the Queen is so fortunate as to provide the State with a <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">Crown-Prince</a> this should be the end of her ambition – she should by no means meddle with the government of an Empire, the care of which is not a task for women... If the Queen bears no sons, she is merely a foreigner in the State, and a very dangerous foreigner, too. For as she can never hope to be looked on kindly here, and must always expect to be sent back whence she came, so will she always seek to win the King by other than natural means; she will struggle for position and power by intrigue and the sowing of discord, to the mischief of the King, the nation, and the Empire...</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Her mother-in-law is generally considered to be the source of the malicious pamphlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The accusation of political meddling referred to Elisabeth's influence on her husband regarding his Italian and Hungarian subjects. When she traveled to Italy with him she persuaded him to show mercy toward political prisoners. In 1857 Elisabeth visited <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> for the first time with her husband and two daughters, and it left a deep and lasting impression upon her, probably because in Hungary she found a welcome respite from the constraints of Austrian court life. It was "the first time that Elisabeth had met with men of character in Franz Joseph's realm, and she became acquainted with an aristocratic independence that scorned to hide its sentiments behind courtly forms of speech... She felt her innermost soul reach out in sympathy to the proud, steadfast people of this land..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Unlike the archduchess, who despised the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" title="Hungarian people">Hungarians</a>, Elisabeth felt such an affinity for them that she began to learn Hungarian; the country reciprocated in its adoration of her.</p>
<p>This same trip proved tragic as both of Elisabeth's children became ill with <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>. While Gisela recovered quickly, two-year-old Sophie grew steadily weaker, then died. It is generally assumed today that she died of typhus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-7">[7]</a></sup> Her death pushed Elisabeth, who was already prone to bouts of melancholy, into periods of heavy <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, which would haunt her for the rest of her life. She turned away from her living daughter, began neglecting her, and their relationship never recovered.</p>
<p>In December 1857 Elisabeth became pregnant for the third time in as many years, and her mother, who had been concerned about her daughter's physical and mental health, hoped that this new pregnancy would help her recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_regimen">Physical regimen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical regimen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>At 172 cm (5 feet 8 inches), Elisabeth was unusually tall. Even after four pregnancies she maintained her weight at approximately 50 kg (110 pounds, 7 st 12 lbs) for the rest of her life. She achieved this through fasting and exercise.</p>
<p>Elisabeth was strongly attached to her parents, especially to her mother, and was still a child in search of an identity of her own when an adult role with unusual obligations and restrictions was imposed upon her. She had no control in her new life and was unable to identify herself as both the spouse of the emperor and a young mother. As a result, she attempted to recreate her childhood with its lack of obligations. The only quality for which she felt herself appreciated, and over which she had control, was her physical appearance, so she started cultivating this as the primary source of her self-esteem. Obsessively achievement-oriented and almost compulsively perfectionistic in her attitudes, she became a slave to her own beauty and image.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In deep mourning after her daughter Sophie's death, Elisabeth refused to eat for days; a behavior that would reappear in later periods of melancholy and depression. Whereas she previously had supper with the family, she now began to avoid this; and if she did eat with them, she ate quickly and very little. Whenever her weight threatened to exceed fifty kilos, a "fasting cure" or "hunger cure" would follow, which involved almost complete fasting. Meat itself often filled her with disgust, so she either had the juice of half-raw beefsteaks squeezed into a thin soup, or else adhered to a diet of milk and eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elisabeth emphasised her extreme slenderness through the practice of <a href="/wiki/Tightlacing" title="Tightlacing">"tight-lacing"</a>. During the peak period of 1859–60, which coincided with Franz-Joseph's political and military defeats in <a href="/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence" title="Second Italian War of Independence">Italy</a>, her sexual withdrawal from her husband after three pregnancies in rapid succession, and her losing battle with her mother-in-law for dominance in rearing her children, she reduced her waist to 16 inches in circumference. Corsets of the time were split-<a href="/wiki/Busk" title="Busk">busk</a> types, fastening up the front with hooks and eyes, but Elisabeth had more rigid, solid-front ones made in Paris out of leather, "like those of Parisian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courtesans" title="Courtesans">courtesans</a>", probably to hold up under the stress of such strenuous lacing, "a proceeding which sometimes took quite an hour". The fact that "she only wore them for a few weeks" may indicate that even leather proved inadequate for her needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Elisabeth's defiant flaunting of this exaggerated dimension angered her mother-in-law, who expected her to be pregnant continuously.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although on her return to Vienna in August 1862, a lady-in-waiting reported that “she eats properly, sleeps well, and does not tight-lace any more”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> her clothing from this time until her death still measured only 18 1/2 – 19 1/2 inches around the waist, which prompted the Prince of Hesse to describe her as “almost inhumanly slender.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> She developed a horror of fat women and transmitted this attitude to her youngest daughter, who was terrified when, as a little girl, she first met Queen Victoria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her youth Elisabeth followed the fashions of the age, which for many years were <a href="/wiki/Crinoline" title="Crinoline">cage-crinolined</a> hoop skirts, but when fashion began to change, she was at the forefront of abandoning the hoop skirt for a tighter and leaner silhouette. She disliked both expensive accoutrements and the protocol that dictated constant changes of clothing, preferring simple, monochromatic <a href="/wiki/Riding_habit" title="Riding habit">riding habit</a>-like attire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> She never wore petticoats or any other "underlinen", as they added bulk, and was often literally sewn into her clothes, to bypass waistbands, creases, and wrinkles and to further emphasize the "<a href="/wiki/Wasp_waist" title="Wasp waist">wasp waist</a>" that became her hallmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The empress developed extremely rigorous and disciplined exercise habits. Every castle she lived in was equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gymnasium</a>, the Knights' Hall of the Hofburg was converted into one, mats and balance beams were installed in her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedchamber" title="Bedchamber">bedchamber</a> so that she could practise on them each morning, and the imperial villa at Ischl was fitted with gigantic mirrors so that she could correct every movement and position. She took up <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">fencing</a> in her 50s with equal discipline. A fervent horsewoman, she rode every day for hours on end, becoming probably the world's best, as well as best-known, female equestrian at the time. When, due to <a href="/wiki/Sciatica" title="Sciatica">sciatica</a>, she could no longer endure long hours in the saddle, she substituted walking, subjecting her attendants to interminable marches and hiking tours in all weather.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the last years of her life, Elisabeth became even more restless and obsessive, weighing herself up to three times a day. She regularly took steam baths to prevent weight gain; by 1894 she had wasted away to near <a href="/wiki/Emaciation" title="Emaciation">emaciation</a>, reaching her lowest point of 95.7 lbs (43.5 kg). There were some aberrations in Elisabeth's diet that appear to be signs of <a href="/wiki/Binge_eating" title="Binge eating">binge eating</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup> On one occasion in 1878 the Empress astonished her travelling companions when she unexpectedly visited a restaurant incognito, where she drank champagne, ate a broiled chicken and an Italian salad, and finished with a "considerable quantity of cake". She may have satisfied her urge to binge in secret on other occasions; in 1881 she purchased an English country house and had a spiral staircase built from her living room into the kitchen, so that she could reach it in private.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beauty">Beauty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Beauty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In addition to her rigorous exercise routines Elisabeth practised what could be called a true beauty cult, but one that was highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascetic" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a>, solitary, and prone to bizarre, eccentric, and almost mystic routines. Daily care of her abundant and extremely long hair, which in time turned from the dark blonde of her youth to chestnut brunette, took at least three hours.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Her hair was so long and heavy that she often complained that the weight of the elaborate double braids and pins gave her headaches. Her hairdresser, Franziska Feifalik, was originally a stage hairdresser at the Wiener Burgtheater. Responsible for all of Elisabeth's ornate hairstyles, she always accompanied her on her wanderings. Feifalik was forbidden to wear rings and required to wear white gloves; after hours of dressing, braiding, and pinning up the Empress' tresses, the hairs that fell out had to be presented in a silver bowl to her reproachful empress for inspection. When her hair was washed with special "essences" of eggs and cognac once every two weeks, all activities and obligations were cancelled for that day. Before her son's death, she tasked Feifalik with tweezing gray hairs away,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-17">[17]</a></sup> but at the end of her life her hair was described as "abundant, though streaked with silver threads."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p.58_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p.58-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elisabeth used these captive hours during grooming to learn languages; she spoke fluent English and French, and added <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">modern Greek</a> to her Hungarian studies. Her Greek tutor, Constantin Christomanos, described the ritual:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Hairdressing takes almost two hours, she said, and while my hair is busy, my mind stays idle. I am afraid that my mind escapes through the hair and onto the fingers of my hairdresser. Hence my headache afterwards. The Empress sat at a table which was moved to the middle of the room and covered with a white cloth. She was shrouded in a white, laced <a href="/wiki/Peignoir" title="Peignoir">peignoir</a>, her hair, unfastened and reaching to the floor, enfolded her entire body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Unlike other women of her time, Elisabeth used little cosmetics or perfume, as she wished to showcase her natural beauty, but she tested countless beauty products prepared in the court pharmacy, or prepared by a lady-in-waiting in her own apartments, to preserve it. Although one favorite, "Crème Céleste", was compounded from white wax, <a href="/wiki/Spermaceti" title="Spermaceti">spermaceti</a>, sweet almond oil, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosewater" title="Rosewater">rosewater</a>; she attached far less importance to creams and emollients, and experimented with a wide variety of facial tonics and waters, from which she apparently expected more results. Elisabeth slept without a pillow on a metal bedstead, all the better to retain her upright posture, with either raw veal or crushed strawberries lining her nightly leather facial mask.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> She was heavily massaged and often slept with cloths soaked in either violet- or cider-vinegar above her hips to preserve her slim waist, and her neck was wrapped with cloths soaked in Kummerfeld-toned washing water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> To further preserve her skin tone, she took both a cold shower every morning (which in later years aggravated her arthritis) and an olive oil bath in the evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After age thirty-two, she did not sit for any more portraits, and would not allow any photographs of her to be taken, so that her public image of the eternal beauty would not be challenged. The few photographs that were taken without her knowledge show a woman who was “graceful, but almost too slender”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p.58_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p.58-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="762" data-file-width="1026" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg/220px-The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg/330px-The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg/440px-The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Austrian_Imperial_family_in_G%C3%B6ddollo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Engraving depicting the Hungarian royal family at <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6d%C3%B6ll%C5%91_Palace" title="Gödöllő Palace">Gödöllő Palace</a> (circa 1870)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Franz Joseph was passionately in love with his wife, but she did not reciprocate his feelings fully and felt increasingly stifled by the rigidness of court life. He was an unimaginative and sober man, a political reactionary who was still guided by his mother and her adherence to the strict <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish Court</a> Ceremonial (<i>Spanisches Hofzeremoniell</i>) regarding both his public and domestic life, whereas Elisabeth inhabited a different world altogether. Restless to the point of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a>, naturally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Introverted" title="Introverted">introverted</a>, and emotionally distant from her husband, she fled him as well as her duties of life at court, avoiding them both as much as she could. He indulged her wanderings, but constantly and unsuccessfully tried to tempt her into a more domestic life with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elisabeth slept very little and spent hours reading and writing at night, and even took up smoking, a shocking habit for women which made her the further subject of already avid gossip. She had a special interest in history, philosophy, and literature, and developed a profound reverence for the German lyric poet and radical political thinker, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heinrich Heine</a>, whose letters she collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>She tried to make a name for herself by writing Heine-inspired poetry. Referring to herself as <a href="/wiki/Titania" title="Titania">Titania</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s Fairy Queen, Elisabeth expressed her intimate thoughts and desires in a large number of romantic poems, which served as a type of secret diary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Most of her poetry relates to her journeys, <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">classical Greek</a> and romantic themes, and ironic commentary on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg_dynasty" title="Habsburg dynasty">Habsburg dynasty</a>. Her wanderlust is defined by her own work:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>O'er thee, like thine own sea birds// I'll circle without rest//For me earth holds no corner//To build a lasting nest.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Elisabeth was an emotionally complex woman, and perhaps due to the melancholy and eccentricity that was considered a given characteristic of her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> lineage (the best-known member of the family being her favorite cousin, the eccentric <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_II_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig II of Bavaria">Ludwig II of Bavaria</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> she was interested in the treatment of the mentally ill. In 1871, when the Emperor asked her what she would like as a gift for her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint%27s_Day" title="Saint's Day">Saint's Day</a>, she listed a young tiger and a medallion, but: "...a fully equipped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunatic_asylum" title="Lunatic asylum">lunatic asylum</a> would please me most".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_a_son">Birth of a son</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Birth of a son">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Achilleion_1_123.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2056" data-file-width="2944" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Achilleion_1_123.JPG/220px-Achilleion_1_123.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Achilleion_1_123.JPG/330px-Achilleion_1_123.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Achilleion_1_123.JPG/440px-Achilleion_1_123.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Achilleion_1_123.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Empress Elisabeth with Emperor Franz Joseph</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 21 August 1858, Elisabeth finally gave birth to an heir, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Prince_Rudolf_of_Austria" title="Crown Prince Rudolf of Austria">Rudolf</a> (1858–1889). The 101-gun salute announcing the welcome news to Vienna also signaled an increase in her influence at court. This, combined with her sympathy toward Hungary, made Elisabeth an ideal mediator between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magyars" title="Magyars">Magyars</a> and the emperor. Her interest in politics had developed as she matured; she was liberal-minded, and placed herself decisively on the Hungarian side in the increasing conflict of nationalities within the empire.</p>
<p>Elisabeth was a personal advocate for Hungarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_Gyula_Andr%C3%A1ssy" title="Count Gyula Andrássy">Count Gyula Andrássy</a>, who also was rumored to be her lover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Whenever difficult negotiations broke off between the Hungarians and the court, they were resumed with her help. During these protracted dealings, Elisabeth suggested to the emperor that Andrássy be made the <a href="/wiki/Premier" title="Premier">Premier</a> of Hungary as part of a compromise, and in a forceful attempt to bring the two men together, strongly admonished her husband:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I have just had an interview with Andrássy. He set forth his views clearly and plainly. I quite understood them and arrived at the conclusion that if you would trust him – and trust him entirely – we might still be saved, not only Hungary, but the monarchy, too.... I can assure you that you are not dealing with a man desirous of playing a part at any price or striving for a position; on the contrary, he is risking his present position, which is a fine one. But approaching shipwreck, he, too, is prepared to do all in his power to save it; what he possesses – his understanding and influence in the country – he will lay at your feet. For the last time I beg you in Rudolf's name not to lose this, at the last moment...</p>
<p>...If you say 'No,' if at the last moment you are no longer willing to listen to disinterested counsels. then... you will be relieved forever from my future... and nothing will remain to me but the consciousness that whatever may happen, I shall be able to say honestly to Rudolf one day; "I did everything in my power. Your misfortunes are not on my conscience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisa.2C_Stephan_1995.2C_p._172_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sisa.2C_Stephan_1995.2C_p._172-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>When Elisabeth was still blocked from controlling her son's upbringing and education, she openly rebelled. Due to her nervous attacks, fasting cures, severe exercise regime, and frequent fits of coughing, the state of her health had become so alarming that in October 1860 she was reported to suffer not only from "green-sickness" (<a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>), but also from physical exhaustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> A serious lung complaint of “Lungenschwindsucht" (<a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>) was feared by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_%C5%A0koda" title="Joseph Škoda">Dr. Skoda</a>, a lung specialist, who advised a stay on <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weindel.2C_Henri_1909.2C_p.141_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weindel.2C_Henri_1909.2C_p.141-25">[25]</a></sup> During this time the court was rife with malicious rumors that Franz Joseph was having a <a href="/wiki/Affair" title="Affair">liaison</a> with an actress named Frau Roll,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weindel.2C_Henri_1909.2C_p.141_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weindel.2C_Henri_1909.2C_p.141-25">[25]</a></sup> leading to speculation today that Elisabeth's symptoms could have been anything from psychosomatic to a result of venereal disease.</p>
<p>Elisabeth seized on the excuse and left her husband and children, to spend the winter in seclusion. Six months later, a mere four days after her return to Vienna, she again experienced coughing fits and fever. She ate hardly anything and slept badly, and Dr. Skoda observed a recurrence of her lung disease. A fresh rest cure was advised, this time on <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a>, where she improved almost immediately. If her illnesses were psychosomatic, abating when she was removed from her husband and her duties, her eating habits were causing physical problems as well. In 1862 she had not seen Vienna for a year when her family physician, Dr. Fischer of Munich, examined her and observed serious anemia and signs of "dropsy" (<a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a>). Her feet were sometimes so swollen that she could walk only laboriously, and with the support of others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On medical advice, she went to <a href="/wiki/Bad_Kissingen" title="Bad Kissingen">Bad Kissingen</a> for a cure. Elisabeth recovered quickly at the spa, but instead of returning home to assuage the gossip about her absence she spent more time with her own family in Bavaria. In August 1862, after a two-year absence, she returned shortly before her husband's birthday, but immediately suffered from a violent "<a href="/wiki/Migraine" title="Migraine">migraine</a>" and vomited four times en route, which supports the theory that her primary complaints were stress-related and psychosomatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rudolf was now four years old, and Franz Joseph hoped for another son to safeguard the succession. Dr. Fischer claimed that the health of the empress would not permit another pregnancy, and she would regularly have to go to Kissingen for a cure. Elisabeth fell into her old pattern of escaping boredom and dull court protocol through frequent walking and riding, using her health as an excuse to avoid both official obligations and sexual intimacy. Her successful avoidance of further pregnancies would have been a natural reaction to having been assigned the role of an imperial brood mare, bearing a child a year only to have it taken away from her, but the importance of preserving her youthful appearance was an important influence in her decision:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Children are the curse of a woman, for when they come, they drive away Beauty, which is the best gift of the gods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>She was now more assertive in her defiance of her husband and mother-in-law than before, openly opposing them on the subject of the military education of Rudolf, who, like his mother, was extremely sensitive and not suited to the life at court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>After having used every excuse to avoid pregnancy, Elisabeth later decided that she wanted a fourth child. Her decision was at once a deliberate personal choice and a political negotiation: by returning to the marriage, she ensured that Hungary, with which she felt an intense emotional alliance, would gain an equal footing with Austria.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Compromise_of_1867" title="Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867">Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867</a> created the dual monarchy of Austria–Hungary. Andrassy was made the first Hungarian prime minister and in return, he saw that Franz Joseph and Elisabeth were officially crowned King and Queen of Hungary in June.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As a coronation gift, Hungary presented the royal couple with a country residence in <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6d%C3%B6ll%C5%91" title="Gödöllő">Gödöllő</a>, twenty miles east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buda-Pest" title="Buda-Pest">Buda-Pest</a>. In the next year, Elisabeth lived primarily in Gödöllő and Buda-Pest, leaving her neglected and resentful Austrian subjects to trade rumors that if the infant she was expecting were a son, she would name him Stephen, after the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_I_of_Hungary" title="Stephen I of Hungary">patron saint and first king of Hungary</a>. The issue was avoided when she gave birth to a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Marie_Valerie_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Marie Valerie of Austria">Marie Valerie</a> (1868–1924). Dubbed the "Hungarian child", she was born in Buda-Pest ten months after her parents' coronation and baptised there in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisa.2C_Stephan_1995.2C_p._172_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sisa.2C_Stephan_1995.2C_p._172-24">[24]</a></sup> Determined to bring this last child up by herself, Elisabeth finally had her way. She poured all her repressed maternal feelings on her youngest daughter to the point of nearly smothering her. Sophie's influence over Elisabeth's children and the court faded, and she died in 1872.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Travels">Travels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Travels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Elisabeth's desk at the <a href="/wiki/Achilleion_(Corfu)" title="Achilleion (Corfu)">Achilleion</a> in Corfu</div>

<p>After having achieved this victory, Elisabeth did not stay to enjoy it, but instead embarked on a life of travel, and saw little of her children. “If I arrived at a place and knew that I could never leave it again, the whole stay would become hell despite being paradise”. After her son's death, she commissioned the building of a palace in Corfu which she named the <i><a href="/wiki/Achilleion_(Corfu)" title="Achilleion (Corfu)">Achilleion</a></i>, after <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s hero <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Iliad" title="The Iliad">The Iliad</a></i>. After her death, the building was purchased by <a href="/wiki/German_Emperor" title="German Emperor">German Emperor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II_of_Germany" title="Wilhelm II of Germany">Wilhelm II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup> Later it was acquired by the nation of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and converted to a museum.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Newspapers published articles on her passion for <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">riding sports</a>, diet and exercise regimens, and fashion sense. She often shopped at the Budapest fashion house, <i>Antal Alter</i> (now <a href="/wiki/Alter_%C3%A9s_Kiss" title="Alter és Kiss">Alter és Kiss</a>), which had become very popular with the fashion-crazed crowd. Newspapers also reported on a series of reputed lovers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although there is no verifiable evidence of her having an affair, one of her alleged lovers was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Middleton" title="Bay Middleton">George "Bay" Middleton</a>, a dashing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Scot" title="Anglo-Scot">Anglo-Scot</a>. He had been named as the probable lover of Lady Henrietta Blanche Hozier and father of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_Churchill,_Baroness_Spencer-Churchill" title="Clementine Churchill, Baroness Spencer-Churchill">Clementine Ogilvy Hozier</a> (the wife of <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>). To prevent him from becoming lonely during her long absences, Elisabeth encouraged her husband <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph" title="Franz Joseph">Franz Joseph</a>'s close relationship with actress <a href="/wiki/Katharina_Schratt" title="Katharina Schratt">Katharina Schratt</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayerling_incident">Mayerling incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mayerling incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mayerling_1889.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1356" data-file-width="2208" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Mayerling_1889.jpg/220px-Mayerling_1889.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Mayerling_1889.jpg/330px-Mayerling_1889.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Mayerling_1889.jpg/440px-Mayerling_1889.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mayerling_1889.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photograph of the imperial hunting lodge at Mayerling, in which Crown Prince Rudolf committed suicide in 1889</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1889 Elisabeth's life was shattered by the death of her only son <a href="/wiki/Rudolf,_Crown_Prince_of_Austria" title="Rudolf, Crown Prince of Austria">Rudolf</a>, who was found dead together with his young lover <a href="/wiki/Baroness_Mary_Vetsera" title="Baroness Mary Vetsera">Baroness Mary Vetsera</a>, in what was suspected to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murder-suicide" title="Murder-suicide">murder-suicide</a> on Rudolf's part. The scandal was known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayerling_Incident" title="Mayerling Incident">Mayerling Incident</a> after the name of Rudolf's hunting lodge in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>, where they were found.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Elisabeth never recovered from the tragedy, sinking further into melancholy. Within a few years, she had lost her father (in 1888), her only son (1889), her sister (1890) and her mother (1892). After Rudolf's death she was thought to have dressed only in black for the rest of her life, although a light blue and cream gown discovered by The Hofburg's Sisi Museum dates to this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup> To compound her losses, Count <a href="/wiki/Gyula_Andr%C3%A1ssy" title="Gyula Andrássy">Gyula Andrássy</a> died a year later, on 18 February 1890. "My last and only friend is dead," she lamented. Marie Valerie declared, "...she clung to him with true and steadfast friendship as she did perhaps, to no other person."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Whether their personal relationship was an intimate one or not, her feelings for him were ones she also felt for his country, and that she knew were wholeheartedly reciprocated by the Magyars.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Mayerling scandal increased public interest in Elisabeth, and she continued to be an icon and a sensation in her own right wherever she went. She wore long black gowns that could be buttoned up at the bottom, and carried a white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasol" title="Parasol">parasol</a> made of leather in addition to a concealing fan to hide her face from the curious.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Elisabeth spent little time in Vienna with her husband. Their correspondence increased during their last years, however, and their relationship became a warm friendship. On her imperial steamer, <i>Miramar</i>, Empress Elisabeth travelled through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. Her favourite places were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_Martin" title="Cap Martin">Cap Martin</a> on the <a href="/wiki/French_Riviera" title="French Riviera">French Riviera</a>, and also Sanremo on the Ligurian Riviera, where tourism had started only in the second half of the nineteenth century; <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bad_Ischl" title="Bad Ischl">Bad Ischl</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, where the imperial couple would spend the summer; and <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a>. The Empress also visited countries not usually visited by European royals at the time: <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. The endless travels became a means of escape for the empress from her life and her misery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1898, despite warnings of possible assassination attempts, the sixty-year-old Elisabeth traveled incognito to <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, although someone from the <a href="/wiki/Beau-Rivage_Geneva" title="Beau-Rivage Geneva">Hôtel Beau-Rivage</a> revealed that the Empress of Austria was their guest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 1:35 p.m. on Saturday 10 September 1898, Elisabeth and <a href="/wiki/Countess_Irma_Szt%C3%A1ray_de_Szt%C3%A1ra_et_Nagymih%C3%A1ly" title="Countess Irma Sztáray de Sztára et Nagymihály">Countess Irma Sztáray de Sztára et Nagymihály</a>, her <a href="/wiki/Lady-in-waiting" title="Lady-in-waiting">lady-in-waiting</a>, left the hotel on the shore of Lake Geneva on foot to catch the <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamship</a> <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer_Gen%C3%A8ve" title="Paddle steamer Genève"><i>Genève</i></a> for Montreux. Since the empress "did not like <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">processions</a>," her servants had already been ordered to leave by train for neighboring <a href="/wiki/Territet" title="Territet">Territet</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>They were walking along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promenade" title="Promenade">promenade</a> when the 25-year-old Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarchist" title="Anarchist">anarchist</a> <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Lucheni" title="Luigi Lucheni">Luigi Lucheni</a> approached them, attempting to peer underneath the empress's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasol" title="Parasol">parasol</a>. According to Sztáray, as the <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_bell" title="Ship's bell">ship's bell</a> announced the departure, Lucheni seemed to stumble and made a movement with his hand as if he wanted to maintain his balance. In reality, in an act of "<a href="/wiki/Propaganda_of_the_deed" title="Propaganda of the deed">propaganda of the deed</a>", he had stabbed Elisabeth with a sharpened needle file that was 4 inches (100 mm) long (used to file the eyes of industrial needles) that he had inserted into a wooden handle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Lucheni originally planned to kill the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philippe,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans_(1869%E2%80%931926)" title="Prince Philippe, Duke of Orléans (1869–1926)">Duke of Orléans</a>; but the <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">Pretender</a> to France’s throne had left Geneva earlier for the <a href="/wiki/Valais" title="Valais">Valais</a>. Failing to find him, the assassin selected Elisabeth when a Geneva newspaper revealed that the elegant woman traveling under the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> of "Countess of Hohenembs" was the Empress Elisabeth of Austria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>I am an anarchist by conviction...I came to Geneva to kill a sovereign, with object of giving an example to those who suffer and those who do nothing to improve their social position; it did not matter to me who the sovereign was whom I should kill...It was not a woman I struck, but an Empress; it was a crown that I had in view.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>After Lucheni struck her, the empress collapsed. A coach driver helped her to her feet and alerted the Austrian concierge of the Beau-Rivage, a man named Planner, who had been watching the empress's progress toward the <i>Genève</i>. The two women walked roughly 100 yards (91 m) to the gangway and boarded, at which point Sztáray relaxed her hold on Elisabeth's arm. The empress then lost consciousness and collapsed next to her. Sztáray called for a doctor, but only a former nurse, a fellow passenger, was available. The boat’s captain, Captain Roux, was ignorant of Elisabeth's identity and since it was very hot on deck, advised the countess to disembark and take her companion back to her hotel. Meanwhile, the boat was already sailing out of the harbor. Three men carried Elisabeth to the top deck and laid her on a bench. Sztáray opened her gown, cut Elisabeth's corset laces so she could breathe. Elisabeth revived somewhat and Sztáray asked her if she was in pain, and she replied, "No". She then asked, "What has happened?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-32">[32]</a></sup> and lost consciousness again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Countess Sztáray noticed a small brown stain above the empress's left breast. Alarmed that Elisabeth had not recovered consciousness, she informed the captain of her identity, and the boat turned back to Geneva. Elisabeth was carried back to the Hotel Beau-Rivage by six sailors on a stretcher improvised from a sail, cushions and two oars. Fanny Mayer, the wife of the hotel director, a visiting nurse, and the countess undressed Elisabeth and removed her shoes, when Sztáray noticed a few small drops of blood and a small wound. When they then removed her from the stretcher to the bed she was clearly dead; Frau Mayer believed the two audible breaths she heard the Empress take as she was brought into the room were her last. Two doctors, Dr. Golay and Dr. Mayer arrived, along with a priest, who was too late to grant her absolution. Mayer incised the artery of her left arm to ascertain death, and found no blood. She was pronounced dead at 2:10 p.m. Everyone knelt down and prayed for the repose of her soul, and Countess Sztáray closed Elisabeth's eyes and joined her hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p._333_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p._333-34">[34]</a></sup> No matter how reluctant or resentful she was of the title, Elisabeth had been the Empress of Austria for 44 years.</p>
<p>When Franz Joseph received the telegram informing him of Elisabeth's death, his first fear was that she had committed suicide. It was only when a third message arrived, detailing the assassination, that he was relieved of that notion. The telegram asked permission to perform an autopsy, and answer was that whatever procedures were prescribed by Swiss Law should be adhered to.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The autopsy was performed the next day by Golay, who discovered that the weapon, which had not yet been found, had penetrated 3.33 inches (85 mm) into Elisabeth's thorax, fractured the fourth rib, pierced the lung and <a href="/wiki/Pericardium" title="Pericardium">pericardium</a>, and penetrated the heart from the top before coming out the base of the left <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricle</a>. Because of the sharpness and thinness of the file the wound was very narrow and, due to pressure from Elisabeth's extremely tight corseting, the hemorrhage of blood into the pericardial sac around the heart was slowed to mere drops. Until this sac filled, the beating of her heart was not impeded, which is why Elisabeth had been able to walk from the site of the assault and up the boat’s boarding ramp. Had the weapon not been removed, she would have lived a while longer, as it would have acted like a plug to stop the bleeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Golay photographed the wound, but turned the photograph over to the Swiss Procurator-General, who had it destroyed, on the orders of Franz Joseph, along with the autopsy instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Geneva shuttered itself in mourning, Elisabeth’s body was placed in a triple coffin: two inner ones of lead, the third exterior one in bronze, reposing on lion claws. On Tuesday, before the coffins were sealed, Franz Joseph's official representatives arrived to identify the body. The coffin was fitted with two glass panels, covered with doors, which could be slid back to allow her face to be seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p._333_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burgh.2C_Edward_Morgan_Alborough_1899.2C_p._333-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Wednesday morning, Elisabeth's body was carried back to Vienna aboard a funeral train. The inscription on her coffin read, “Elisabeth, Empress of Austria”. The Hungarians were outraged and the words, “and Queen of Hungary” were hastily added. The entire Austro-Hungarian Empire was in deep mourning; 82 sovereigns and high-ranking nobles followed her funeral cortege on the morning of 17 September to the tomb in the Church of the Capuchins.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Empress Elisabeth's tomb next to that of her husband Franz Joseph in Vienna's <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Crypt" title="Imperial Crypt">Imperial Crypt</a>, on the other side of Franz Josef's tomb is that of their son, Crown Prince Rudolf</div>

<p>After the attack, Lucheni fled down the Rue des Alpes, where he threw the file into the entrance to No. 3. He was caught by two cabdrivers and a sailor, then secured by a gendarme. The weapon was found the next day by the concierge during his morning cleaning; he thought it belonged to a laborer who had moved the day before and did not notify the police of his discovery until the following day. There was no blood on the file and the tip was broken off, which occurred when Lucheni threw it away. The file was so dull in appearance it was speculated that it had been deliberately selected because it would be less noticeable than a shiny knife, which would have given Lucheni away as he approached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Lucheni had planned to purchase a stiletto, but lacking the price of 12 francs he had simply sharpened an old file into a homemade dagger and cut down a piece of firewood into a handle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Lucheni boasted that he acted alone, because many political refugees found a haven in Switzerland, the possibility that he was part of a plot and that the life of the emperor was also in danger, was considered. Once it was discovered that an Italian was responsible for Elisabeth's murder, unrest swept Vienna and reprisals were threatened against Italians. The intensity of shock, mourning, and outrage far exceeded that which occurred at the news of Rudolf's death. An outcry also immediately erupted over the lack of protection for the empress. The Swiss police were well aware of her presence, and telegrams to the appropriate authorities advising them to take all precautions had been dispatched. Police Chief Virieux of the Canton of Vaud had organized Elisabeth's protection, but she had detected his officers outside the hotel the day before the assassination and protested that the surveillance was disagreeable, so Virieux had no choice but to withdraw them. It is also possible that if Elisabeth had not dismissed her other attendants that day, an entourage larger than one lady-in-waiting could have discouraged Lucheni, who had been following the Empress for several days, awaiting an opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lucheni was brought before the Geneva Court in October. Furious that the death sentence had been abolished in Geneva, he demanded that he be tried according to the laws of the Canton of <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, which still had the death penalty, signing the letter: “Luigi Lucheni, anarchist, and one of the most dangerous".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since Elisabeth was famous for preferring the common man to courtiers, known for her charitable works, and considered such a blameless target, Lucheni's sanity was questioned initially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Elisabeth's will stipulated that a large part of her jewel collection should be sold and the proceeds, then estimated at over £600,000, were to be applied to various religious and charitable organizations. Franz Joseph remarked to Prince Liechtenstein, who was the couple's devoted equerry, "That a man could be found to attack such a woman, whose whole life was spent in doing good and who never injured any person, is to me incomprehensible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Everything outside of the crown jewels and state property that Elisabeth had the power to bequeath was left to her granddaughter, the <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Elisabeth_Marie_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Elisabeth Marie of Austria">Archduchess Elisabeth</a>, Rudolf's child.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Lucheni was declared to be sane, but was tried as a common murderer, not a political criminal. Incarcerated for life, and denied the opportunity to make a political statement by his action, he attempted to kill himself with the sharpened key from a tin of sardines on 20 February 1900. Ten years later, he hanged himself with his belt in his cell on the evening of 16 October 1910, after a guard confiscated and destroyed his uncompleted memoirs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Upon her death, Franz Joseph founded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Elizabeth" title="Order of Elizabeth">Order of Elizabeth</a> in memory of her.</p>
<p>On the promenade in Territet, there is a monument to Empress Elisabeth of Austria. This town is between Montreux and Chateau Chillon.</p>
<p>In 1988, historian <a href="/wiki/Brigitte_Hamann" title="Brigitte Hamann">Brigitte Hamann</a> wrote <i>The Reluctant Empress</i>, a biography of Elisabeth, reviving interest in Franz Joseph's consort. Unlike previous portrayals of Elisabeth as a one-dimensional fairy tale princess, <a href="/wiki/Hamann" title="Hamann">Hamann</a> portrayed her as a bitter, unhappy woman full of self-loathing and various emotional and mental disorders. She was seen to have searched for happiness, but died a broken woman who never found it. Hamann's portrayal explored new facets of the legend of Sisi, as well as contemplating the role of women in high-level politics and dynasties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="3000" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg/220px-ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg/330px-ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg/440px-ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ElizabethOfHungary_MatthiasChurch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Queen Elizabeth of Hungary, 1837-1898, Sculpture located in St Matthias Church in Budapest, Hungary.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A large number of chapels were named in her honour, connecting her to Saint Elisabeth. Various parks were named after her, such as the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Elisabeth_Park" title="Empress Elisabeth Park">Empress Elisabeth Park</a> in Meran, South Tyrol.</p>
<p>Various residences that Elisabeth frequented, including her apartments in the Hofburg and the <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nbrunn_Palace" title="Schönbrunn Palace">Schönbrunn Palace</a> in Vienna, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_villa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Imperial villa (page does not exist)">imperial villa</a> in Ischl, the Achilleion in Corfu, and her summer residence in Gödöllő, Hungary are preserved and open to the public.</p>
<p>Several sites in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> are named after her: two of <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>'s districts, <a href="/wiki/Erzs%C3%A9betv%C3%A1ros" title="Erzsébetváros">Erzsébetváros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesterzs%C3%A9bet" title="Pesterzsébet">Pesterzsébet</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Bridge" title="Elisabeth Bridge">Elisabeth Bridge</a>.</p>
<p>Empress Elisabeth and the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Elisabeth_Railway" title="Empress Elisabeth Railway">Empress Elisabeth Railway</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_railway_(Austria)" title="West railway (Austria)">West railway</a>) named after her were recently selected as a main motif for a high value collector coin, the <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Austria)#2008_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Austria)">Empress Elisabeth Western Railway commemorative coin</a>.</p>
<p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Blanchard" title="Gerald Blanchard">Gerald Blanchard</a> stole the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koechert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koechert (page does not exist)">Koechert</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._E._K%C3%B6chert" title="de:A. E. Köchert">de</a>)</span> Diamond Pearl known as the Sisi Star, a 10-pointed star of diamonds fanning out around one enormous pearl from an exhibit commemorating the 100th anniversary of her assassination at the Schönbrunn Palace in Vienna. It was one of approximately 27 jewel-encrusted pieces designed and made by court jeweler Jakob Heinrich Köchert for her to wear in her hair,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-43">[43]</a></sup> which appears in the famous portrait of her by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Xaver_Winterhalter" title="Franz Xaver Winterhalter">Franz Xaver Winterhalter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The Star was recovered by Canadian Police in 2007 and eventually returned to Austria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-43">[43]</a></sup> Although Blanchard possessed the priceless jewel, no one was ever formally charged with stealing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowers_Bahney_2015_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bowers_Bahney_2015-4">[4]</a></sup> Two versions of the famous stars were created: a second type without a pearl center, was designed by court jeweller Rozet &amp; Fischmeister. Some stars were given to ladies of the court. One set of 27 diamond stars was kept in the Imperial family; they are seen in a photograph that shows the dowry of Rudolf's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Elisabeth_Marie_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Elisabeth Marie of Austria">Archduchess Elisabeth</a>, known as "Erzsi", on the occasion of her wedding to Prince Otto of Windisch-Graetz in 1902.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal_of_Elisabeth_in_the_arts">Portrayal of Elisabeth in the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Portrayal of Elisabeth in the arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage">Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1932 the comic operetta <i>Sissi</i> premiered in Vienna. Composed by <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kreisler" title="Fritz Kreisler">Fritz Kreisler</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> was written by Ernst and Hubert Marischka, with orchestrations by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Russell_Bennett" title="Robert Russell Bennett">Robert Russell Bennett</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Although the pet name of the empress was always spelled, "Sisi," never "Sissi," this incorrect version of her name persisted in the works about her that followed.</p>
<p>In 1943 <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a> wrote a play about an imagined meeting between Elisabeth and her assassin, <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Aigle_%C3%A0_deux_t%C3%AAtes" title="L'Aigle à deux têtes">L'Aigle à deux têtes</a></i> (The Eagle with Two Heads). It was first staged in 1946.</p>
<p>In 1992, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_theater" title="Musical theater">musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_(musical)" title="Elisabeth (musical)">Elisabeth</a></i> premièred at the <a href="/wiki/Theater_an_der_Wien" title="Theater an der Wien">Theater an der Wien</a> in Vienna. With libretto by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Kunze_(writer)" title="Michael Kunze (writer)">Michael Kunze</a> and music by <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Levay" title="Sylvester Levay">Sylvester Levay</a>, this is probably the darkest portrayal of the Empress' life. It portrayed Elisabeth bringing a physical manifestation of death with her to the imperial court, thus destroying the Habsburg dynasty. The leading role in the premiere was played by Dutch musical singer <a href="/wiki/Pia_Douwes" title="Pia Douwes">Pia Douwes</a>. <i>Elisabeth</i> went on to become the most successful German-language musical of all time and has enjoyed numerous productions around the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballet">Ballet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ballet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1978 ballet, <i><a href="/wiki/Mayerling_(ballet)" title="Mayerling (ballet)">Mayerling</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_MacMillan" title="Kenneth MacMillan">Kenneth MacMillan</a> portrayed Elisabeth in a <i><a href="/wiki/Pas_de_deux" title="Pas de deux">pas de deux</a></i> with her son Prince Rudolf, the principal character in the ballet.</p>
<p>In 1993 French ballerina <a href="/wiki/Sylvie_Guillem" title="Sylvie Guillem">Sylvie Guillem</a> appeared in a piece entitled, <i>Sissi, l'impératice anarchiste</i> (Sissi, Anarchist Empress), choreographed by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_B%C3%A9jart" title="Maurice Béjart">Maurice Béjart</a> to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Strauss_II" title="Johann Strauss II">Strauss's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Kaiser-Walzer" title="Kaiser-Walzer">Emperor Waltz</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Romy_Scheider_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="380" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Romy_Scheider_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Romy_Scheider_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Romy_Scheider_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Romy_Scheider_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Romy_Scheider_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Romy_Scheider_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Actress <a href="/wiki/Romy_Schneider" title="Romy Schneider">Romy Schneider</a> portrayed Elisabeth in what is known as the <i>Sissi trilogy</i>, three Austrian films in the late 1950s.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The 1921 film <i>Kaiserin Elisabeth von Österreich</i> was one of the first films to focus entirely on Elisabeth. It was co-written by Elisabeth's niece, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie_Larisch" title="Marie Larisch">Marie Larisch</a> (who played her younger self at the age of 62), and starred Carla Nelsen as the title character. The film later achieved notoriety when a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Con-artists" title="Con-artists">con-artists</a> started selling stills from the murder scene as actual photographs of the crime.</p>
<p>In 1936, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> released <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_Steps_Out" title="The King Steps Out">The King Steps Out</a></i>, a film version of the operetta "Sissi", directed by <a href="/wiki/Josef_von_Sternberg" title="Josef von Sternberg">Josef von Sternberg</a>. It starred opera diva <a href="/wiki/Grace_Moore" title="Grace Moore">Grace Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franchot_Tone" title="Franchot Tone">Franchot Tone</a>.</p>
<p>Jean Cocteau directed the 1948 film version of his play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Eagle_with_Two_Heads" title="The Eagle with Two Heads">The Eagle with Two Heads</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonioni" title="Antonioni">Antonioni</a>'s 1981 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mystery_of_Oberwald" title="The Mystery of Oberwald">The Mystery of Oberwald</a></i> is another adaptation of the play.</p>
<p>In the German-speaking world, Elisabeth's name is often associated with a trilogy of romantic films about her life directed by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Marischka" title="Ernst Marischka">Ernst Marischka</a> which starred a teenage <a href="/wiki/Romy_Schneider" title="Romy Schneider">Romy Schneider</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sissi_(film)" title="Sissi (film)">Sissi</a></i> (1955)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sissi_%E2%80%93_The_Young_Empress" title="Sissi – The Young Empress">Sissi — die junge Kaiserin</a></i> (1956) (<i>Sissi — The Young Empress</i>)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sissi_%E2%80%93_Fateful_Years_of_an_Empress" title="Sissi – Fateful Years of an Empress">Sissi — Schicksalsjahre einer Kaiserin</a></i> (1957) (<i>Sissi — Fateful Years of an Empress</i>)</li>
<li><i>Forever My Love</i> is a condensed version, with the three films edited down into one feature and dubbed in English. This version was released in North America in 1962.</li>
</ul>
<p>In early dramatizations, Elisabeth appears as peripheral to her husband and son, and so is always shown as a mature character. Schneider's characterization of Elisabeth as a young woman is the first time the "young" empress is seen on screen. The trilogy was the first to explicitly depict the romantic myth of Sissi, and ends abruptly with her determination to live a private life. Any further exploration of the topic would have been at odds with the accepted image of the loving wife, devoted mother, and benevolent empress. The three films, newly restored, are shown every <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> on <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a>, German, Dutch, and French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV" title="TV">television</a>. In 2007, the films were released as <i>The Sissi Collection</i> with English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtitles" title="Subtitles">subtitles</a>. Schneider came to loathe the role, claiming, "Sissi sticks to me like porridge (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Grießbrei</i></span>)." Later she appeared as a much more realistic and fascinating Elisabeth in <a href="/wiki/Luchino_Visconti" title="Luchino Visconti">Luchino Visconti</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_(film)" title="Ludwig (film)">Ludwig</a>,</i> a 1972 film about Elisabeth's cousin, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_II_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig II of Bavaria">Ludwig II of Bavaria</a>. A portrait of Schneider in this film was the only one, taken from her roles, which is displayed in her home.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ava_Gardner" title="Ava Gardner">Ava Gardner</a> played the empress in the 1968 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mayerling_(1968_film)" title="Mayerling (1968 film)">Mayerling</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Omar_Sharif" title="Omar Sharif">Omar Sharif</a> starred as Crown Prince Rudolf.</p>
<p>The 1991 German film called <i>Sisi/Last Minute</i> (original <i>Sisi und der Kaiserkuß,</i> (<i>Sisi and the emperor's kiss</i>) starred French actress Vanessa Wagner as Sisi, Nils Tavernier as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Franz_Joseph" title="Emperor Franz Joseph">Emperor Franz Joseph</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonja_Kirchberger" title="Sonja Kirchberger">Sonja Kirchberger</a> as Helene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the film version of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lloyd_Webber" title="Andrew Lloyd Webber">Andrew Lloyd Webber</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phantom_of_the_Opera_(2004_film)" title="The Phantom of the Opera (2004 film)">The Phantom of the Opera</a></i>, the character Christine is wearing a gown inspired by the famous portrait of Elisabeth by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Xaver_Winterhalter" title="Franz Xaver Winterhalter">Winterhalter</a>.</p>
<p>In 2007, German comedian and director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Herbig" title="Michael Herbig">Michael Herbig</a> released a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer-animated" title="Computer-animated">computer-animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Parody_film" title="Parody film">parody film</a> based on Elisabeth under the title <i><a href="/wiki/Lissi_und_der_wilde_Kaiser" title="Lissi und der wilde Kaiser">Lissi und der wilde Kaiser</a></i> (lit.: "Lissi and the Wild Emperor"). It is based on his Sissi <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> sketches featured in his television show <i><a href="/wiki/Bullyparade" title="Bullyparade">Bullyparade</a>.</i></p>
<p>A recent appearance of Sisi was in the new 2012 biopic about Ludwig II of Bavaria titled <i>Ludwig II</i>, where she was played by <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Herzsprung" title="Hannah Herzsprung">Hannah Herzsprung</a>.</p>
<p>In December 2014, to coincide with the presentation of the Pre-Fall 2015 'Metier d'arts' collection by luxury fashion house <a href="/wiki/Chanel" title="Chanel">Chanel</a>, shown in the <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Leopoldskron" title="Schloss Leopoldskron">Schloss Leopoldskron</a> palace, creative director <a href="/wiki/Karl_Lagerfeld" title="Karl Lagerfeld">Karl Lagerfeld</a> directed a short film featuring <a href="/wiki/Cara_Delevingne" title="Cara Delevingne">Cara Delevingne</a> as Empress Elisabeth accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Pharrell_Williams" title="Pharrell Williams">Pharrell Williams</a>. During a dream sequence, the duo sing a song written by Williams entitled <i>CC the World</i>, playing on the iconic interlocking logo of the fashion house, the initials of its founder <a href="/wiki/Coco_Chanel" title="Coco Chanel">Coco Chanel</a>, as well as the Empress's nickname 'Sisi'. Lagerfeld recreated the iconic gown worn by Elisabeth in the portrait by Winterhalter, whilst Pharrell takes on attire similar to Franz Joseph.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, Elisabeth was portrayed in the British television series <i><a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Eagles" title="Fall of Eagles">Fall of Eagles</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Diane_Keen" title="Diane Keen">Diane Keen</a> played the young Elisabeth and <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Gurney" title="Rachel Gurney">Rachel Gurney</a> portrayed the empress at the time of Rudolf's death.</p>
<p>The 1992 WGBH-TV adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>'s Miss Marple mystery <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mirror_Crack%27d_from_Side_to_Side" title="The Mirror Crack'd from Side to Side">The Mirror Crack'd from Side to Side</a></i> centers around the shooting of a fictitious film about Elisabeth. The role of the actress portraying the empress was played by <a href="/wiki/Claire_Bloom" title="Claire Bloom">Claire Bloom</a>.</p>
<p>The season five finale of the Austrian detective television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kommissar_Rex" title="Kommissar Rex">Kommissar Rex</a></i> (1994) revolves around a deluded woman affected by myth of the empress. The episode, appropriately, is entitled, "Sisi."</p>
<p>A heavily fictionalized version of Elisabeth's younger years is portrayed in a 1997 animated children's series, <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Sissi" title="Princess Sissi">Princess Sissi</a>.</i></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arielle_Dombasle" title="Arielle Dombasle">Arielle Dombasle</a> portrayed Elisabeth in the 2004 television film <i>Sissi, l'impératrice rebelle</i>, detailing the last five days of her life.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sandra_Ceccarelli" title="Sandra Ceccarelli">Sandra Ceccarelli</a> portrayed an older Elisabeth in the 2006 television dramatization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayerling_Incident" title="Mayerling Incident">Mayerling Incident</a>, <i>The Crown Prince</i>. Her son and his lover were played by <a href="/wiki/Max_von_Thun" title="Max von Thun">Max von Thun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vittoria_Puccini" title="Vittoria Puccini">Vittoria Puccini</a>.</p>
<p>In December 2009, <i>Sisi</i>, a two-part mini-series, premiered on <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> television, produced by a German, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Partnership" title="Partnership">partnership</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Cristiana_Capotondi" title="Cristiana Capotondi">Cristiana Capotondi</a> as Elisabeth and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Rott&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Rott (page does not exist)">David Rott</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Franz_Joseph" title="Emperor Franz Joseph">Emperor Franz Joseph</a>. Like the 1997 animated series, this film portrays the romantic mythology surrounding the unhappy <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> of Elisabeth and Franz Joseph, but the political problems of the empire and the personal troubles of the main characters are dealt with in much better detail than in many other dramas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Constantin <a class="extiw" href="https://el.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9A%CF%89%CE%BD%CF%83%CF%84%CE%B1%CE%BD%CF%84%CE%AF%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82_%CE%A7%CF%81%CE%B7%CF%83%CF%84%CE%BF%CE%BC%CE%AC%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82" title="el:Κωνσταντίνος Χρηστομάνος">Christomanos</a> (1867-1911) who served as Elisabeth's modern Greek language tutor from 1891 to 1893 and escorted her during her stay in Corfu, published his memoirs of her shortly after her death, in his 1899 <a class="external text" href="http://www.amazon.com/Tagebuchbl%C3%A4tter-Constantin-Christomanos/dp/3707601781/ref=la_B00J9B70I6_1_2?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1458947246&amp;sr=1-2" rel="nofollow">Tagebuchblätter</a> (Diary Pages). Although he portrayed Elisabeth in an idealistic favourable manner as a fairytale princess come to life, his book greatly displeased the Imperial Court that declared him persona non grata and forced him to resign his University teaching position in Vienna and leave Austria.</p>
<p>The story of Elisabeth is told in Susan Appleyard's ebook, <i>In a Gilded Cage</i>.</p>
<p>Elisabeth's youth and early adult life are dramatized in the novel <i>Imperial Waltz</i> by William S. Abrahams (Dial Press, 1954).</p>
<p>Elisabeth appears as a significant character in <a href="/wiki/Gary_Jennings" title="Gary Jennings">Gary Jennings</a>' 1987 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Spangle_(novel)" title="Spangle (novel)">Spangle</a></i>. The novel concerns a circus traveling through <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> at the close of the 1800s, and portrays Elisabeth's interest in circuses and <a href="/wiki/Stunt_performer" title="Stunt performer">daredevil</a> riding.</p>
<p>Her story inspired the 2003 children's book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Diaries" title="The Royal Diaries">The Royal Diaries: Elisabeth, The Princess Bride</a></i>.</p>
<p>The empress appears in the romantic fiction novel <i>Stars in my Heart</i> by <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Cartland" title="Barbara Cartland">Barbara Cartland</a>.</p>
<p>Appears in Alexander Lernet-Holenia's novel <i>Mayerling</i> (1960).</p>
<p>She appears in a cameo in the short story "The Road to Charing Cross" in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Flashman_and_the_Tiger" title="Flashman and the Tiger">Flashman and the Tiger</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/George_MacDonald_Fraser" title="George MacDonald Fraser">George MacDonald Fraser</a> (1999). She dances with the anti-hero, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Flashman" title="Harry Flashman">Harry Flashman</a> at a ball at the end of the story, in which Flashman has helped prevent her husband the Emperor from being assassinated.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> (a.k.a. Samuel Clemens) wrote about the assassination of Empress of Austria in an article entitled "The Memorable Assassination," which he did not submit for publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Empress haunts a deadly Christmas house party in the form of a chatty biography, <i>Life of the Empress Elizabeth of Austria</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Georgette_Heyer" title="Georgette Heyer">Georgette Heyer</a>’s mystery, <i>Envious Casca</i> (1941). The book and its disappearance form part of the goings-on that drive the various family members and guests to distraction.</p>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Allison_Pataki" title="Allison Pataki">Allison Pataki</a> wrote a historical fiction novel about Elisabeth and her marriage to Emperor Franz Joseph entitled <i>The Accidental Empress</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> in February 2015. Its sequel, <i>Sisi, Empress on Her Own</i>, was published in March 2016.</p>
<p>Journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jennifer_Bowers_Bahney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jennifer Bowers Bahney (page does not exist)">Jennifer Bowers Bahney</a> wrote the narrative non-fiction true story of the theft of the Koechert Diamond Pearl titled Stealing Sisi's Star: How a master thief nearly got away with Austria's most famous jewel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> published by McFarland &amp; Co., June 2015.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dutch singer Petra Berger's album <i>Eternal Woman</i> includes "If I Had a Wish", a song about Elisabeth.</p>
<p>The song "Sisi" by the Scottish band Washington Irving is inspired by Elisabeth's life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Elisabeth_of_Austria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Elizabeth Church in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lviv,_Ukraine" title="Lviv, Ukraine">Lviv, Ukraine</a> (now the Greek Catholic Church of St. Olha and Elizabeth), was founded in 1903 by the Emperor in memory of Elisabeth.</p>
