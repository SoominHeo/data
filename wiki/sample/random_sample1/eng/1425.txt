<p>The <b>Bulava</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Булава</span>, lit. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mace_(club)" title="Mace (club)">mace</a>"; designation <b>RSM-56</b>, <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> <b>SS-NX-30</b> or <b>SS-N-32</b> , <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> index <b>3M30</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missile</a> (SLBM) developed for the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy" title="Russian Navy">Russian Navy</a> and deployed in 2013 on the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borei-class_submarine" title="Borei-class submarine">Borei class</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile nuclear submarines</a>. It is intended as the future cornerstone of Russia's <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_triad" title="Nuclear triad">nuclear triad</a>, and is the most expensive weapons project in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foxnews_7oct_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews_7oct-5">[5]</a></sup> The weapon takes its name from <i><a href="/wiki/Bulawa" title="Bulawa">bulava</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> word for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mace_(club)" title="Mace (club)">mace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bulava has slightly greater range but a 30%+ lower <a href="/wiki/Throw-weight" title="Throw-weight">throw-weight</a> than the 1979 American <a href="/wiki/Trident_(missile)" title="Trident (missile)">Trident</a> C4 missile.</p>
<p>Designed by <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Institute_of_Thermal_Technology" title="Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology">Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology</a>, development of the missile was launched in the late 1990s as a replacement for the <a href="/wiki/R-39_Rif" title="R-39 Rif">R-39 Rif</a> solid-fuel SLBM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wru201004_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wru201004-7">[7]</a></sup> It is expected that the first three Borei-class submarines will carry 16 missiles, while the following five vessels will carry 20 missiles. Development and deployment of the Bulava missile within the Russian Navy is not affected by the enforcement of the <a href="/wiki/New_START" title="New START">new START</a> treaty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The missile's flight test programme was problematic. Until 2009, there were 6 failures in 13 flight tests and one failure during ground test, blamed mostly on substandard components. After a failure in December 2009, further tests were put on hold and a probe was conducted to find out the reasons for the failures. Testing was resumed on 7 October 2010 with a launch from the <a href="/wiki/Typhoon-class_submarine" title="Typhoon-class submarine">Typhoon-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_TK-208_Dmitri_Donskoi" title="Russian submarine TK-208 Dmitri Donskoi"><i>Dmitri Donskoi</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>; the warheads successfully hit their targets at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kura_Test_Range" title="Kura Test Range">Kura Test Range</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_7oct_success_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_7oct_success-9">[9]</a></sup> Seven launches have been conducted since the probe, all successful. On 28 June 2011, the missile was launched for the first time from its standard carrier, Borei-class submarine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Yury_Dolgorukiy" title="Russian submarine Yury Dolgorukiy"><i>Yury Dolgorukiy</i></a>, and on 27 August 2011 the first full-range (over 9,000 km (5,600 mi)) flight test was conducted. After this successful launch, the start of serial production of Bulava missiles in the same configuration was announced on 28 June 2011. A successful <a href="/wiki/Salvo" title="Salvo">salvo</a> launch on 23 December 2011 concluded the flight test programme. The missile was officially approved for service on 27 December 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and was reported to be commissioned aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Yury_Dolgorukiy" title="Russian submarine Yury Dolgorukiy">Yuri Dolgorukiy</a> on 10 January 2013. The missile did however continue to fail in the summer of 2013 and was not operational as of November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Bulava is finally operational aboard Yury Dolgorukiy as of October 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, recent developments put this in question. In November 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Vladimir_Monomakh" title="Russian submarine Vladimir Monomakh">Vladimir Monomakh</a> sub fired two missiles while submerged. One of the missiles self-destructed during the boost phase and the other failed to deliver its warheads to the specified target. After being sent back to the manufacturer, it was determined that the missiles failed due to manufacturing defects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pravdareport.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pravdareport.com-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bulava missile was developed by Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology under the leadership of chief designer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yury_Solomonov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yury Solomonov (page does not exist)">Yury Solomonov</a>. Although it utilizes some engineering solutions used for the recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topol-M" title="Topol-M">Topol-M</a> ICBM, the new missile has been developed virtually from scratch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kramnik2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kramnik2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Bulava is both lighter and more sophisticated than the Topol-M. The two missiles are expected to have comparable ranges, and similar <a href="/wiki/Circular_error_probable" title="Circular error probable">CEP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warhead</a> configurations. Bulava has a declared START throw weight of 1150 kg to 9,500 kilometers.</p>
<p>The missile has three stages; the first and second stages use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid fuel</a> propellant, while the third stage uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket_fuel" title="Liquid rocket fuel">liquid fuel</a> to allow high maneuverability during <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warhead</a> separation. The missile can be launched from an inclined position, allowing a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> to fire them while moving. It has a low flight trajectory, and due to this could be classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasiballistic_missile" title="Quasiballistic missile">quasi-ballistic missile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_infograph_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_infograph-15">[15]</a></sup> The missile possesses advanced defense capabilities making it resistant to missile-defense systems.</p>
<p>Borei-class submarines carrying Bulava missiles are expected to be an integral part of the Russian nuclear triad until 2040.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_7oct_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt_7oct-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bulava can be loaded on TEL road mobile launchers, on railway BZhRK trains, other various displacement ground sea sub.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_history">Development history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inception">Inception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1990s, Russia had two submarine-launched ICBMs, the solid-fuel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-39_(missile)" title="R-39 (missile)">R-39</a> and the liquid-fuel <a href="/wiki/R-29_Vysota" title="R-29 Vysota">R-29 Vysota</a> family, both developed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Makeyev_Design_Bureau" title="Makeyev Design Bureau">Makeyev Design Bureau</a>. A new missile, designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-39UTTH_Bark" title="R-39UTTH Bark">R-39UTTH Bark</a> was under development to replace the R-39. The Bark was planned to become the only submarine-launched ballistic missile of the Russian nuclear arsenal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russianforces_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-russianforces-17">[17]</a></sup> However, its development was plagued with problems, and after three test failures the Bark programme was canceled in 1998. Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology was now tasked with developing a new advanced missile. The institute promised that it would able to quickly develop a new naval missile based on its recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topol-M" title="Topol-M">Topol-M</a> land-based ICBM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_saga_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_saga-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new missile would be deployed per 16 missiles on the Borei I-<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_saga_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-rian_saga-18">[18]</a></sup> and per 20 missiles on Borei II-class submarines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As the new submarines would not be ready in time for flight tests, the <a href="/wiki/Typhoon-class_submarine" title="Typhoon-class submarine">Typhoon-class submarine</a> <i>Dmitry Donskoy</i> was upgraded to carry Bulavas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Key people involved in the decision to develop Bulava included the institute director and Bulava's chief designer Yury Solomonov; director of the Defense Ministry's Fourth Central Research Institute, Major-General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Dvorkin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vladimir Dvorkin (page does not exist)">Vladimir Dvorkin</a>; Navy Commander, Fleet Admiral <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Kuroyedov" title="Vladimir Kuroyedov">Vladimir Kuroyedov</a>; Defense Minister, Marshal <a href="/wiki/Igor_Sergeyev" title="Igor Sergeyev">Igor Sergeyev</a>; Economics Minister <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Urinson" title="Yakov Urinson">Yakov Urinson</a> and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Chernomyrdin" title="Viktor Chernomyrdin">Viktor Chernomyrdin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_saga_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-rian_saga-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_tests">First tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The missile completed the first stage launch-tests at the end of 2004. Although it was initially planned to base the Bulava design on the Topol-M, the first tests showed that the new missile was completely different in terms of appearance, dimensions and warhead lay-out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_saga_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-rian_saga-18">[18]</a></sup> It was later acknowledged that the Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology had developed Bulava virtually from scratch, reusing only a few engineering solutions from the Topol-M.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kramnik2010_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-kramnik2010-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Troubles">Troubles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Troubles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2009, the missile had experienced 5 failures in 11 tests. This led to the missile's chief designer, Yury Solomonov resigning from his post in July 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aleksandr_Sukhodolskiy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aleksandr Sukhodolskiy (page does not exist)">Aleksandr Sukhodolskiy</a> was appointed as the new general designer of sea-based ballistic missiles at the Moscow Institute of Thermal Technology; Solomonov however retained his post of general designer of land-based missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations_for_the_failures">Explanations for the failures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Explanations for the failures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Chief designer Solomonov has blamed the failures on the poor state of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_industry_of_Russia" title="Defense industry of Russia">Russian defense industry</a> and problems in the design-technology-production chain.</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Sometimes [the problem] is poor-quality materials, sometimes it is the lack of necessary equipment to exclude the 'human' factor in production, sometimes it is inefficient quality control"</dd>
</dl>
<p>According to Solomonov, the industry is unable to manufacture 50 of the necessary components for the missile, forcing designers to improvise and look for alternative solutions, which seriously complicates the testing process. Solomonov further said that despite the failures, there was no need for changes in the design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_az_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-news_az-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sergei Kovalyov, the designer of three generations of Russian strategic submarines said that due to lack of funding, the developers had been unable to conduct test launches from a floating pad to test the underwater segment of the missile's trajectory. He also said that there were insufficient funds to conduct ground-based test launches. Both types of testing had been standard procedure during Soviet times. Kovalyov also criticised the poor quality of missile components provided by a large number of sub-contractors and the absence of military representatives at manufacturing plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2009_Norwegian_spiral_anomaly" title="2009 Norwegian spiral anomaly">2009 Norwegian spiral anomalies</a>, a temporary strange light phenomenon over vast areas of northern Norway have been explained with a failed stage of a Bulava missile test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Scientist_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Scientist-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-26">[26]</a></sup> According to a spokesman, "The missile's first two stages worked as normal, but there was a technical malfunction at the next, third, stage of the trajectory".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moskowitz_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-moskowitz-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieees201001_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieees201001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_the_military">Effects on the military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects on the military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Due to the delays in Bulava's development, the launch of the fourth Borei-class submarine, <i>Svyatitel Nikolay</i>, has been pushed back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Russia was planning to build eight of Borei-class submarines by 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only one Typhoon-class submarine, <i>Dmitry Donskoy</i>, has been modified to launch Bulavas. The Bulava program is the most expensive weapons project in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foxnews_7oct_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews_7oct-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_about_the_program">Debate about the program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Debate about the program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite continued test failures, the Russian defense minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatoly_Serdyukov" title="Anatoly Serdyukov">Anatoly Serdyukov</a>, has stated that the project will not be abandoned. "We will certainly not give up the Bulava. I think that despite all the failures, the missile will fly," he said in an interview in late December 2009. The Russian military has been adamant that there is no alternative to Bulava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been discussion among analysts about the possibility of re-equipping the Borei-class submarines with the more reliable liquid-propellant <a href="/wiki/R-29RMU_Sineva" title="R-29RMU Sineva">R-29RMU Sineva</a> missiles. The Sineva is an upgrade of the <a href="/wiki/R-29RM_Shtil" title="R-29RM Shtil">R-29RM Shtil</a> and entered service in 2007. According to <i><a href="/wiki/RIA_Novosti" title="RIA Novosti">RIA Novosti</a></i> military analyst Ilya Kramnik, this would have been an attractive option, given that the less advanced Sineva missiles already have "virtually the same impressive specifications as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trident_II" title="Trident II">Trident II</a> (D5) SLBMs wielded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>." However, the work needed to redesign and modify the Borei-class submarines to carry Sinevas is regarded as too expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kramnik_alternative_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-kramnik_alternative-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Probe">Probe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Probe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After a launch failure in December 2009 caused by a defective engine nozzle and which led to the <a href="/wiki/2009_Norwegian_spiral_anomaly" title="2009 Norwegian spiral anomaly">2009 Norwegian spiral anomaly</a>, further tests were put on hold and a review of the missile program was initiated. The results of the probe were delivered to the Russian government in May 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_probe_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_probe-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_tests">2010 tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010 tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Testing was resumed for the first time after the probe on 7 October 2010. The missile was launched from the submerged <i>Dmitry Donskoy</i>, in the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>, and the warheads successfully hit their targets at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kura_testing_range" title="Kura testing range">Kura testing range</a>, 380 kilometres (240 mi) to the north of <a href="/wiki/Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky" title="Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky">Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_7oct_success_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-rian_7oct_success-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_7oct_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-rt_7oct-16">[16]</a></sup> The launch reportedly took place at 07:15 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-podvig_7oct_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-podvig_7oct-34">[34]</a></sup> The missile travelled over 6,000 kilometres (3,700 mi), and the rocket's trajectory was within the normal parameters, according to a Navy official.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alertnet_7oct_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-alertnet_7oct-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second test launch in 2010 from <i>Dmitry Donskoy</i> was set to 29 October<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flot.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-flot.com-3">[3]</a></sup> and was successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_29oct_success_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_29oct_success-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next test to be performed from <i>Yuriy Dolgorukiy</i> was initially planned to December 2010, but was postponed to mid-summer 2011 due to ice conditions in White Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each of following new test missiles needed to be built under nearly identical conditions, in order to determine the cause of potential failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_tests_and_deployment">2011 tests and deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2011 tests and deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Russian defense sources have stated that the Bulava missile will not enter service until it is 98-99% reliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> According to the Russian Vice Premier <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Ivanov" title="Sergei Ivanov">Sergei Ivanov</a> another six successful launches (one planned in 2010, other five in 2011) will be required before the missile could be commissioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-40">[40]</a></sup> After a successful test salvo launch in December 2011, Russian president Dmitry Medvedev announced that the Bulava missile was ready and it would now be adopted for service with the Russian Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_tests_and_deployment">2012 tests and deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2012 tests and deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 2012 a high-ranking official of Russia's United Shipbuilding Corporation said in 2012 Russia will test fire its Bulava missile only once, in November, specifically from the nuclear-powered submarine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Alexander_Nevsky" title="Russian submarine Alexander Nevsky">Alexander Nevsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_deployment">2013 deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RSM-56_Bulava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2013 deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bulava was finally commissioned with its lead carrier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Yury_Dolgorukiy" title="Russian submarine Yury Dolgorukiy">Yuri Dolgorukiy</a> on 10 January 2013. The official ceremony of raising the Russian Navy colors on the submarine was led by Russian Defense Minister Sergey Shoigu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt10j_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rt10j-2">[2]</a></sup> After another failed launch in September, Shoigu announced a pause in the state trials of the next two submarines and five more test launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The entire production run of the missiles was then recalled for factory inspections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Russian Deputy Defense Minister Yury Borisov told reporters on Wednesday 20 November 2013: "The commission has completed its work. The causes have been determined. They are technological and are related to the production of the nozzle," Borisov told a roundtable meeting on state defense contracts. The cause of the failure does not call into question "the correctness of the production of the product in general," he said. The flaw that has been revealed has been corrected on the three remaining missiles in this batch, Borisov said. Borisov said Russian Defense Minister Sergei Shoigu has made a decision to hold another five Bulava launches. "These launches will be planned next year," Borisov said. The intercontinental ballistic missile Bulava was launched at the Kura testing ground in Kamchatka during the state testing of the strategic nuclear submarine Alexander Nevsky in the White Sea on September 6. "The missile left the launch container, but the its board system failed three minutes into the launch," the Russian Defense Ministry earlier said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
