<p>"<b>Radio Ga Ga</b>" is a 1984 song performed and recorded by the British <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>, written by their drummer <a href="/wiki/Roger_Taylor_(Queen_drummer)" title="Roger Taylor (Queen drummer)">Roger Taylor</a>. It was released as a single with "<a href="/wiki/The_Works_(Queen_album)#.22I_Go_Crazy.22" title="The Works (Queen album)">I Go Crazy</a>" by Brian May on the original B-side (3:42) and was included on the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Works_(Queen_album)" title="The Works (Queen album)">The Works</a></i>. The song also features on the band's compilation albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_II_(Queen_album)" title="Greatest Hits II (Queen album)">Greatest Hits II</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Queen" title="Classic Queen">Classic Queen</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The single was a worldwide success for the band, reaching number one in 19 countries, number 2 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">Australian ARIA Chart</a>, and number 16 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BL_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BL-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The band performed the song at every concert from 1984 to their last concert with lead singer <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> in 1986, with their most notable performance at <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning">Meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recorded in 1983 and released in January 1984, the song was a commentary on television overtaking <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio's</a> popularity and how one would listen to radio in the past for a favourite comedy, drama, or science fiction programme. It also pertained to the advent of the music video and <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>, which was now competing with radio as an important medium for promoting records. Ironically, Queen had done much to popularize the music video with "<a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Rhapsody" title="Bohemian Rhapsody">Bohemian Rhapsody</a>" in 1975 and the video for "Radio Ga Ga" would become a regular staple on MTV in 1984. It was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/1984_MTV_Video_Music_Awards#Best_Art_Direction_in_a_Video" title="1984 MTV Video Music Awards">MTV Video Music Award</a> that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Roger Taylor was quoted:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">â</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><i>That's part of what the song's about, really. The fact that they [music videos] seem to be taking over almost from the aural side, the visual side seems to be almost more important.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">â</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Taylor originally conceived of it as "Radio <a href="/wiki/Latin_profanity#Cac.C4.81re:_to_defecate" title="Latin profanity">caca</a>" (from something his toddler son once said),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which doubled as a criticism of radio for the decrease in variety of programming and the type of music being played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BL_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BL-5">[5]</a></sup> "Radio Ga Ga" in one of the original cassette boxes is called 'radio ca ca'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song makes reference to two important radio events of the 20th century; <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a>' 1938 broadcast of <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H.G. Wells</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(radio_drama)" title="The War of the Worlds (radio drama)">The War of the Worlds</a></i> in the lyric "<i>through wars of worlds/invaded by Mars</i>", and <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>'s 18 June 1940 "<a href="/wiki/This_was_their_finest_hour" title="This was their finest hour">This was their finest hour</a>" speech from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>, in the lyric "<i>You've yet to have your finest hour</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inspiration for this song came when Roger Taylor heard his son utter the words "radio ca-ca" while listening to a bad song on the radio while they were in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. After hearing the phrase, Taylor began writing the song when he locked himself in a room with a <a href="/wiki/Roland_Jupiter-8" title="Roland Jupiter-8">Roland Jupiter-8</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Drum_machine" title="Drum machine">drum machine</a>. He thought it would fit his solo album, but when the band heard it, <a href="/wiki/John_Deacon" title="John Deacon">John Deacon</a> wrote a <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> reconstructed the track, thinking it could be a big hit. Taylor then took a skiing holiday and let Mercury polish the lyrics, harmony, and arrangements of the song. Recording sessions began at <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant" title="Record Plant">Record Plant Studios</a> and included <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> session keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Fred_Mandel" title="Fred Mandel">Fred Mandel</a>, who later on would work with <a href="/wiki/Supertramp" title="Supertramp">Supertramp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>. Mandel programmed the Jupiter's arpeggiated synth-bass parts. The recording features prominent use of the <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland</a> VP330+ <a href="/wiki/Vocoder" title="Vocoder">vocoder</a>. The bassline was produced by a <a href="/wiki/Roland_Jupiter-8" title="Roland Jupiter-8">Roland Jupiter-8</a>, using the built-in arpeggiator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video">Video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Mallet_(director)" title="David Mallet (director)">David Mallet</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the song features scenes from <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">Fritz Lang</a>'s 1927 <a href="/wiki/German_Expressionism" title="German Expressionism">German expressionist</a> <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PV_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-PV-17">[17]</a></sup> and was filmed at Carlton TV Studios and Shepperton Studios, London, in November 1983. It features the band in a car flying over the <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)#Plot" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">title city</a>, and later performing the song in front of the city's working class. Freddie Mercury's solo song "Love Kills" was used in <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Moroder" title="Giorgio Moroder">Giorgio Moroder</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)#Restorations" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">restored version</a> of the film, and in exchange Queen were granted the rights to use footage from it in their "Radio Ga Ga" video. However, Queen had to buy performance rights to the film from the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East German</a> government, which was the copyright holder at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> At the end of the music video, the words "Thanks To Metropolis" appear. The video also features footage from earlier Queen promo videos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PV_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-PV-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_versions">Live versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Live versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Queen finished their sets before the encores on <a href="/wiki/The_Works_Tour" title="The Works Tour">The Works Tour</a> with "Radio Ga Ga" and Mercury would normally sing "you had your time" in a lower octave and modify the deliveries of "you had the power, you've yet to have your finest hour" while Roger Taylor sang the pre-chorus in the high octave. Live versions from the 1984/85 tour were recorded and filmed on the concert films Queen <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_in_Rio" title="Rock in Rio">Rock in Rio</a></i> 1985, and <i><a href="/wiki/We_Are_the_Champions:_Final_Live_in_Japan" title="We Are the Champions: Final Live in Japan">Final Live in Japan</a></i> 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As heard on bootleg recordings, Deacon can be heard providing backing vocals to the song; it is one of the very few occasions he sang in concert.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:10px;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"I remember thinking 'oh great, they've picked it up' and then I thought 'this is not a Queen audience'. This is a general audience who've bought tickets before they even knew we were on the bill. And they all did it. How did they know? Nobody told them to do it."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">â<a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a> on the audience participation in clapping to "Radio Ga Ga" at <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Queen played a shorter, up-tempo version of "Radio Ga Ga" during the <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> concert in July 1985 at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium_(1923)" title="Wembley Stadium (1923)">Wembley Stadium</a>, where Queen's "show-stealing performance" had 72,000 people clapping in unison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It was the second song the band performed at Live Aid after opening with "<a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Rhapsody" title="Bohemian Rhapsody">Bohemian Rhapsody</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-PS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> "Radio Ga Ga" became a live favourite thanks largely to the audience participation potential of the clapping sequence prompted by the rhythm of the chorus (copied from the video). Mercury sang all high notes in this version. The song was played for the <a href="/wiki/Magic_Tour_(Queen)" title="Magic Tour (Queen)">Magic Tour</a> a year later, including twice more at Wembley Stadium; it was recorded for the live album <i><a href="/wiki/Live_at_Wembley_%2786" title="Live at Wembley '86">Live at Wembley '86</a></i>, VHS Video and DVD on 12 July 1986, the second night in the venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-MT-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Young" title="Paul Young">Paul Young</a> performed the song with Queen at <a href="/wiki/The_Freddie_Mercury_Tribute_Concert" title="The Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert">the Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert</a> again at Wembley Stadium on 20 April 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> At the "<a href="/wiki/Party_at_the_Palace" title="Party at the Palace">Party at the Palace</a>" concert, celebrating Queen Elizabeth II's Golden Jubilee in 2002, "Radio Ga Ga" opened up Queen's set with Roger Taylor on vocals and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Collins" title="Phil Collins">Phil Collins</a> on the drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>This song was played on the <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Paul_Rodgers_Tour" title="Queen + Paul Rodgers Tour">Queen + Paul Rodgers Tour</a> in 2005â2006 and sung by Roger Taylor and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rodgers" title="Paul Rodgers">Paul Rodgers</a>. It was recorded officially at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorpoint_Arena_Sheffield" title="Motorpoint Arena Sheffield">Hallam FM Arena</a> in Sheffield on 5 May 2005. The result, <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Champions" title="Return of the Champions">Return of the Champions</a></i>, was released on CD and DVD on 19 September 2005 and 17 October 2005. It was also played on the <a href="/wiki/Rock_the_Cosmos_Tour" title="Rock the Cosmos Tour">Rock the Cosmos Tour</a> during late 2008, this time with only Rodgers on lead vocals. The concert album <i><a href="/wiki/Live_in_Ukraine" title="Live in Ukraine">Live in Ukraine</a></i> came as a result of this tour, yet the song is not available on the CD or DVD versions released 15 June 2009. This performance of "Radio Ga Ga" is only available as a digital download from iTunes. It was again played on the <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Adam_Lambert" title="Queen + Adam Lambert">Queen + Adam Lambert</a> Tour with Adam Lambert on lead vocals.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions">Cover versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cover versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">Musical theatre</a> actress <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Paige" title="Elaine Paige">Elaine Paige</a> recorded the song on her album of Queen covers <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_Album" title="The Queen Album">The Queen Album</a></i> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>Roger Taylor performed the song with <a href="/wiki/Spike_Edney" title="Spike Edney">Spike Edney</a>'s SAS Band.</li>
<li>A heavily modified version of the song serves as the introductory number for <i><a href="/wiki/We_Will_Rock_You_(musical)" title="We Will Rock You (musical)">We Will Rock You</a></i>, a musical composed of Queen songs.</li>
<li>The song was covered in 2004 by <a href="/wiki/Electric_Six" title="Electric Six">Electric Six</a>. The band released the song as a single against their wishes under pressure from their label at the time, although the song had been in their repertoire during the band's early Wildbunch days. The video depicts Electric Six frontman, Dick Valentine, as the ghost of <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> dancing near his own grave. It was widely misinterpreted that Valentine (as Mercury) was dancing on his grave. He explains on his website's video section "Though some have claimed this video portrays me dancing on Freddie Mercury's grave, actually it's more like we are resurrecting Mr. Mercury for the duration of the song and his grave is the logical starting point."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sophie_Ellis-Bextor" title="Sophie Ellis-Bextor">Sophie Ellis-Bextor</a> has made a live-cover of this single at the <i><a href="/wiki/Al_Murray%27s_Happy_Hour" title="Al Murray's Happy Hour">Al Murray's Happy Hour</a></i>, on <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> rock band <a href="/wiki/Tai_Chi_(band)" title="Tai Chi (band)">Tai Chi</a> released a <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> version of the song called "æçæäºº" (English: "My Lover") in 1989.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Ga_Ga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>American pop singer <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a> credits her stage name to this song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> She stated that she "adored" Queen, and that they had a hit called 'Radio Gaga'. "That's why I love the name", she said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
