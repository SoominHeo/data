<p>The <b>S-125 <i>Neva/Pechora</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">С-125 "Нева"/"Печора"</span>, <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> <b>SA-3 <i>Goa</i></b>) Soviet <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> system was designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksei_Mihailovich_Isaev" title="Aleksei Mihailovich Isaev">Aleksei Mihailovich Isaev</a> to complement the <a href="/wiki/S-25_Berkut" title="S-25 Berkut">S-25</a> and <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">S-75</a>. It has a shorter effective range and lower engagement <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> than either of its predecessors and also flies slower, but due to its two-stage design it is more effective against more maneuverable targets. It is also able to engage lower flying targets than the previous systems, and being more modern it is much more resistant to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasures" title="Electronic countermeasures">ECM</a> than the S-75. The 5V24 (V-600) missiles reach around <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 3 to 3.5 in flight, both stages powered by <a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">solid fuel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_motor" title="Rocket motor">rocket motors</a>. The S-125, like the <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">S-75</a>, uses radio command guidance. The naval version of this system has the NATO reporting name <b>SA-N-1 Goa</b> and original designation <b>M-1 Volna</b> (Russian Волна – <i>wave</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The S-125 was first deployed between 1961 and 1964 around <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, augmenting the <a href="/wiki/S-25_Berkut" title="S-25 Berkut">S-25</a> and <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">S-75</a> sites already ringing the city, as well as in other parts of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>. In 1964, an upgraded version of the system, the <b>S-125M "Neva-M"</b> and later <b>S-125M1 "Neva-M1"</b> was developed. The original version was designated SA-3A by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">US DoD</a> and the new Neva-M named <b>SA-3B</b> and (naval) <b>SA-N-1B</b>. The Neva-M introduced a redesigned booster and an improved guidance system. The SA-3 was not used against U.S. forces in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, because the Soviets feared that China (after the souring of Sino-Soviet relations in 1960), through which most, if not all of the equipment meant for North Vietnam had to travel, would try to copy the missile.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angola">Angola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Angola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Air_Force_of_Angola" title="National Air Force of Angola">FAPA-DAA</a> acquired a significant number of SA-3s, and these were encountered during the first strike flown by <a href="/wiki/South_African_Air_Force" title="South African Air Force">SAAF</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirage_F.1" title="Mirage F.1">Mirage F.1s</a> against targets in Angola ever - in June 1980. While the SAAF reported two aircraft were damaged by SAMs during this action, Angola claimed to have shot down four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 June 1980, while attacking <a href="/wiki/SWAPO" title="SWAPO">SWAPO</a>'s Tobias Haneko Training Camp during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Sceptic" title="Operation Sceptic">Operation Sceptic (Smokeshell)</a>, SAAF Major Frans Pretorius and Captain IC du Plessis, both flying Mirage F.1s, were hit by SA-3s. Pretorius's aircraft was hit in a fuel line and he had to perform a <a href="/wiki/Deadstick_landing" title="Deadstick landing">deadstick landing</a> at AFB Ondangwa. Du Plessis's aircraft sustained heavier damage and had to divert to Ruacana forward airstrip, where he landed with only the main undercarriage extended. Both aircraft were repaired and returned to service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vlamgat_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vlamgat-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Soviets supplied several SA-3s to the Arab states in the late 1960s and 1970s, most notably Egypt and Syria. The SA-3 saw extensive action during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Attrition" title="War of Attrition">War of Attrition</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War" title="Yom Kippur War">Yom Kippur War</a>. During the latter, the SA-3, along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-2" title="SA-2">SA-2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-6" title="SA-6">SA-6</a>, formed the backbone of the Egyptian air defence network. In Egypt, March–July 1970 Soviet battalions of S-125 17 Shooting (35 missiles) shot down 9 Israeli and Egyptian planes 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvo.guns.ru_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pvo.guns.ru-7">[7]</a></sup> Israel recognized the 5 in 1970 and in 1973 another 6<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvo.guns.ru_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pvo.guns.ru-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Syria deployed it for the first time during the 1973 Yom Kippur War and also during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1982_Lebanon_war" title="1982 Lebanon war">1982 Lebanon war</a>. In fighting over the <a href="/wiki/Beqaa_Valley" title="Beqaa Valley">Beqaa Valley</a>, however, the IAF managed to neutralize the SAM threat by launching <a href="/wiki/Operation_Mole_Cricket_19" title="Operation Mole Cricket 19">Operation Mole Cricket 19</a>, in which several SA-3 batteries, along with SA-2s and SA-6s, were destroyed in a single day.</p>

<p>A USAF <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-16" title="F-16">F-16</a> (serial 87-257) was shot down on January 19, 1991, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Storm" title="Operation Desert Storm">Operation Desert Storm</a>. The aircraft was struck by an SA-3 just south of Baghdad. The pilot, Major Jeffrey Scott Tice, ejected safely but became a POW as the ejection took place over Iraq. It was the 8th combat loss and the first daylight raid over Baghdad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the opening night of Desert Storm, on 17 January 1991, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52" title="B-52">B-52</a>G was damaged by a missile. Different versions of this engagement are told. It could have been a S-125 or a <a href="/wiki/2K12_Kub" title="2K12 Kub">2K12 Kub</a> while other versions report a MiG-29 allegedly fired a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vympel_R-27" title="Vympel R-27">Vympel R-27</a>R missile and damaged the B-52G.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, the U.S. Air Force disputes these claims, stating the bomber was actually hit by friendly fire, an <a href="/wiki/AGM-88_HARM" title="AGM-88 HARM">AGM-88</a> High-speed, Anti-Radiation Missile (HARM) that homed on the fire-control radar of the B-52's tail gun; the jet was subsequently renamed <i>In HARM's Way</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Shortly following this incident, General <a href="/wiki/George_Lee_Butler" title="George Lee Butler">George Lee Butler</a> announced that the gunner position on B-52 crews would be eliminated, and the gun turrets permanently deactivated, commencing on 1 October 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FR_Yugoslavia">FR Yugoslavia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FR Yugoslavia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Yugoslav Army <a href="/wiki/250th_Air_Defense_Missile_Brigade" title="250th Air Defense Missile Brigade">250th Air Defense Missile Brigade</a> 3rd battery equipped with S-125 system managed <a href="/wiki/1999_F-117A_shootdown" title="1999 F-117A shootdown">to shoot down</a> an <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk" title="Lockheed F-117 Nighthawk">F-117 Nighthawk</a> stealth attack aircraft on March 27, 1999 during the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a> (the only recorded downing of a stealth aircraft) near village Budjanovci, about 45 km from Belgrade. It was also used to shoot down a NATO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-16" title="F-16">F-16</a> fighter on May 2 (its pilot; Lt. Col <a href="/wiki/David_L._Goldfein" title="David L. Goldfein">David L. Goldfein</a>, the commander of <a href="/wiki/555th_Fighter_Squadron" title="555th Fighter Squadron">555th Fighter Squadron</a>, managed to eject and was later rescued by a <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">combat search-and-rescue</a> (CSAR) mission).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holloman_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-holloman-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the war, different Yugoslav SAM sites and possibly the SA-3 also shot down some NATO <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">UAVs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>"The war (in Kosovo) proved that a competent opponent can improvise ways to overcome superior weaponry because every technology has weaknesses that can be identified and exploited," the jury is still out even on real damage to Serbian military infrastructure, the fact remains that SAM sites forced NATO planes to fly higher and be less effective than they would have been without these defences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndrewMartin_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AndrewMartin-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syrian_Civil_War">Syrian Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Syrian Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 March 2015, a US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MQ-1_Predator" title="MQ-1 Predator">MQ-1 Predator</a> drone was shot down by a <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Air_Defense_Force" title="Syrian Air Defense Force">Syrian Air Defense Force</a> S-125 missile while on intelligence flight near the coastal town of Latakia. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2016, ISIS forces captured three SA-3 launchers after they retook Palmyra from Russian and Syrian government troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The S-125 is somewhat mobile, an improvement over the <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">S-75</a> system. The missiles are typically deployed on fixed turrets containing two or four but can be carried ready-to-fire on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zavod_Imeni_Likhacheva" title="Zavod Imeni Likhacheva">ZIL</a> trucks in pairs. Reloading the fixed launchers takes a few minutes.</p>

<p>The S-125 system uses 2 different missile versions. The V-600 (or 5V24) had the smallest warhead with only 60 kg of high explosive. It had a range of about 15 km.</p>
<p>The later version is named V-601 (or 5V27). It has a length of 6.09 m, a wing span of 2.2 m and a body diameter of 0.375 m. This missile weighs 953 kg at launch, and has a 70 kg warhead containing 33 kg of HE and 4,500 fragments. The minimum range is 3.5 km, and the maximum is 35 km (with the Pechora 2A). The intercept altitudes are between 100 m and 18 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janes_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janes-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The S-125M (1970) system uses 5V27. The intercept altitudes are between 20 m and 14 km. The minimum range is 2.5 km, and the maximum is 22 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvo.guns.ru_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-pvo.guns.ru-7">[7]</a></sup> The S-125M1 (1978) system uses 5V27D. In the early 1980s established for each system 1-2 radar simulator (against antiradar missiles assigned)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvo.guns.ru_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-pvo.guns.ru-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radars">Radars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Radars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The launchers are accompanied by a command building or truck and three primary <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> systems:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/P-15_radar" title="P-15 radar">P-15 "Flat Face"</a></b> or <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=P-15M_Squat_Eye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="P-15M Squat Eye (page does not exist)">P-15M(2) "Squat Eye"</a></b> 380 kW C-band target acquisition radar (also used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-6_Gainful" title="SA-6 Gainful">SA-6</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-8_Gecko" title="SA-8 Gecko">SA-8</a>, range 250 km/155 miles)</li>
<li><b>SNR-125 "Low Blow"</b> 250 kW I/D-band tracking, <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_radar" title="Fire-control radar">fire control</a> and guidance radar (range 40 km/25 miles, second mode 80 km/50 miles)</li>
<li><b>PRV-11 "Side Net"</b> E-band height finder (also used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-2_Guideline" title="SA-2 Guideline">SA-2</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-4_Ganef" title="SA-4 Ganef">SA-4</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-5_Gammon" title="SA-5 Gammon">SA-5</a>, range 28 km/17 miles, max height 32 km/105,000 ft)</li>
</ul>
<p><b>"Flat Face"</b>/<b>"Squat Eye"</b> is mounted on a van (<b>"Squat Eye"</b> on a taller mast for better performance against low-altitude targets also an IFF [Identifies Friend or Foe]), <b>"Low Blow"</b> on a trailer and <b>"Side Net"</b> on a box-bodied trailer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_and_upgrades">Variants and upgrades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Variants and upgrades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_version">Naval version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-125_Neva/Pechora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Naval version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SA-N-1_launcher.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/SA-N-1_launcher.jpg/220px-SA-N-1_launcher.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/SA-N-1_launcher.jpg/330px-SA-N-1_launcher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/SA-N-1_launcher.jpg/440px-SA-N-1_launcher.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Work on a naval version <b>M-1 Volna</b> (SA-N-1) started in 1956, along with work on a land version. It was first mounted on a rebuilt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kotlin_class_destroyer" title="Kotlin class destroyer">Kotlin class destroyer</a> (Project 56K) <i>Bravyi</i> and tested in 1962. In the same year, the system was accepted. The basic missile was a V-600 (or 4K90) (range: from 4 to 15 km, altitude: from 0.1 to 10 km). Fire control and guidance is carried out by 4R90 Yatagan radar, with five <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antennas</a> on a common head. Only one target can be engaged at a time (or two, for ships fitted with two Volna systems). In case of emergency, Volna could be also used against naval targets, due to short response time.</p>
<p>The first launcher type was the two-missile ZIF-101, with a magazine for 16 missiles. In 1963 an improved two-missile launcher, ZIF-102, with a magazine for 32 missiles, was introduced to new ship classes. In 1967 Volna systems were upgraded to <b>Volna-M</b> (SA-N-1B) with V-601 (4K91) missiles (range: 4–22 km, altitude: 0.1–14 km).</p>
<p>In 1974 - 1976 some systems were modernized to <b>Volna-P</b> standard, with an additional TV target tracking channel and better resistance to jamming. Later, improved <b>V-601M</b> missiles were introduced, with lower minimal attack altitude against aerial targets (system <b>Volna-N</b>).</p>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_navy" title="Indian navy">Indian</a> frigates also carry the M-1 Volna system.</p>

<p>Since Russia replaced all of its S-125 sites with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-10_Grumble" title="SA-10 Grumble">SA-10</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-12" title="SA-12">SA-12</a> systems, they decided to upgrade the S-125 systems being removed from service to make them more attractive to export customers.</p>
<ul>
<li>Released in 2000, the <b>Pechora-2</b> version features better range, multiple target engagement ability and a higher <a href="/wiki/Probability_of_kill" title="Probability of kill">probability of kill</a> (PK). The launcher is moved onto a truck allowing much shorter relocation times.</li>
<li>It is also possible to fire the <b>Pechora-2M</b> system against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_missiles" title="Cruise missiles">cruise missiles</a>. Deployment time 25 minutes, protected from the active interference, and anti-radiation missiles (total in practical shooting)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Early warning radar is replaced by anti-stealth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> radar Caste 2e2, defeat the purpose of by range 2.5–32 km, defeat the purpose of in height - 0.02–20 km, distance rocket launchers from the control center 10 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Speed up to 1000 m/s (target), Used rocket 5V27DE,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> by weight the warhead + 50% range of flight splinters + 350%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Probability of hitting the target 1st rocket: at a distance up to 25 km - 0,72-0,99, detection range with EPR = 2 m. sq about 100 km, with the objectives of the EPR = 0.15 m. sq about 50 km, with no interference. When using a of active jamming - 40 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rusarmy.com_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rusarmy.com-26">[26]</a></sup> ADMS "Pechora-2M" has the ability to interfacing with higher level command post and radar remote using telecode channels. Is equally effective at any time during the day and at night (optical location, daytime and nighttime, and also thermal imager [up to 30 km of night and 60 km of day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-6">[6]</a></sup>]),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> for such a system, the detection range of an aircraft such as F-16 is 30 km away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Is possible to use two radar pointing missiles, it allows the client to simultaneously work on two goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rusarmy.com_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-rusarmy.com-26">[26]</a></sup> For the purpose with a height of 350 meters detection range of 40 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, a Russian-Belarusian financial-industrial consortium called Oboronitelnye Sistemy (<a href="/wiki/Defense_Systems" title="Defense Systems">Defense Systems</a>) was awarded a contract to overhaul <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>'s S-125 SAM system. These refurbished weapons have been reintroduced as the S-125 Pechora 2M.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>In 2001, Poland began offering an upgrade to the S-125 known as the <b>Newa SC</b>. This replaced many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_circuit" title="Analog circuit">analogue</a> components with <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital</a> ones for improved reliability and accuracy. This upgrade also involves mounting the missile launcher on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WZT-1" title="WZT-1">WZT-1</a> tank chassis (a <a href="/wiki/Transporter_erector_launcher" title="Transporter erector launcher">TEL</a>), greatly improving mobility and also adds <a href="/wiki/Identification_friend_or_foe" title="Identification friend or foe">IFF</a> capability and data links. Radar is mounted on an 8-wheeled heavy truck chassis (formerly used for <a href="/wiki/Scud" title="Scud">Scud</a> launchers).</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbian</a> modifications include terminal/camera homing from radar base.</p>
<p>Cuba also developed a similar upgrade to the Polish one, which was displayed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Habana" title="La Habana">La Habana</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Later the same year, the Russian version was upgraded again to the <b>Pechora-M</b> which upgraded almost all aspects of the system - the rocket motor, radar, guidance, <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(explosives)" title="Fuse (explosives)">fuse</a> and electronics. There is an added <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red" title="Infra-red">infra-red</a> tracking device to allow launching of missiles without the use of the radar.</li>
</ul>
<p>There is also a version of the S-125 available from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> with the warhead replaced with <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> instrumentation, for use as target drones.</p>
<ul>
<li>In October 2010, Ukrainian <b>Aerotechnica</b> announced a modernized version of S-125 named <b>S-125-2D Pechora</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janes2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janes2-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
