<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Almaz" title="NPO Almaz">NPO Almaz</a> <b>S-200 <i>Angara/Vega/Dubna</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> Ангара\Вега\Дубна), <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> <b>SA-5 <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gammon" title="wiktionary:gammon">Gammon</a></i></b>, is a very long range, medium-to-high <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM) system designed in the 1960s to defend large areas from <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a> attack or other strategic aircraft. Each <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalion</a> has 6 single-rail missile launchers for the 10.8 m (35 ft) long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missiles" title="Missiles">missiles</a> and a fire control radar. It can be linked to other, longer-range <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> systems.</p>

<p>The S-200 surface-to-air missile system was designed for the defense of the most important administrative, industrial and military installations from all types of air attack. S-200 provides defeat of modern and advanced aircraft, including air command and control centers, AWACS aircraft, aircraft jamming creation and other manned and unmanned aerial vehicles. The S-200 is an all-weather system that can be operated in various climatic conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvo.guns.ru_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pvo.guns.ru-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1966, the S-200 was officially accepted into service in order to replace the failed <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">anti-ballistic missile</a> <b>RZ-25/5V11 "Dal"</b>. The Dal was assigned the NATO reporting name SA-5 "Griffon" before it was cancelled.</p>
<p>The first S-200 operational regiments were deployed in 1966 with 18 sites and 342 launchers in service by the end of the year. By 1968 there were 40 sites, and by 1969 there were 60 sites. The growth in numbers then gradually increased throughout the 1970s (1100 launchers)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup> and early 1980s until the peak of 130<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janes-1">[1]</a></sup> sites and 2030 launchers was reached in 1980-1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Each missile is launched by 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_rocket" title="Solid rocket">solid-fueled</a> strap-on <a href="/wiki/Booster_(rocketry)" title="Booster (rocketry)">rocket boosters</a>. After they burn out and drop away (between 3 and 5.1 seconds from launch) it fires a 5D67 liquid fueled sustainer rocket engine (for 51–150 seconds) which burns a fuel called TG-02 Samin (50% <a href="/wiki/Xylidine" title="Xylidine">xylidine</a> and 50% <a href="/wiki/Triethylamine" title="Triethylamine">triethylamine</a>), oxidized by an agent called AK-27P Melange (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuming_nitric_acid" title="Fuming nitric acid">fuming nitric acid</a> enriched with nitrogen oxides, phosphoric acid and hydrofluoric acid).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Maximum range is between 150 km (81 nmi) and 300 km (160 nmi), depending on the model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni-ulm.de_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-uni-ulm.de-6">[6]</a></sup> The missile uses radio illumination mid-course correction to fly towards the target with a terminal <a href="/wiki/Semi-active_radar_homing" title="Semi-active radar homing">semi-active radar homing</a> phase. Maximum target speed is around <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 4. Effective altitude is 300 m (980 ft) to 20,000 m (66,000 ft) for early models and up to 35,000 m (115,000 ft) for later models. The warhead is either 217 kg (478 lb) high-explosive fragmentation (16,000 × 2 g fragmentation pellets and 21,000 × 3.5 g pellets) triggered by radar proximity fuse or command signal, or a 25 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiloton" title="Kiloton">kt</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a> warhead triggered by command signal only. Each missile weighs around 7,108 kg (15,670 lb) at takeoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni-ulm.de_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-uni-ulm.de-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system utilises radio <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi_active_radar_guidance" title="Semi active radar guidance">semi active guidance</a> throughout the missile's flight, which is far more accurate at long range than the command guidance method used by the earlier <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">S-75 Dvina</a> and other missiles. The existence of an optional terminal passive radar homing mode for use against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AEW" title="AEW">AEW</a> aircraft remains unconfirmed. Peak missile speed is around <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 8 and the single-shot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kill_probability" title="Kill probability">kill probability</a> is quoted as 0.85, presumably against a high altitude bomber-type target.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_radar_system">Main radar system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-200_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main radar system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fire control radar of the S-200 system is the <b>5N62</b> (NATO: <b>Square Pair</b>) <a href="/wiki/Continuous_wave" title="Continuous wave">CW</a> <a href="/wiki/H_band_(NATO)" title="H band (NATO)">H band</a> radar, whose range is 270 km (170 mi). It is used for both the tracking of targets and their illumination.</p>

<p>Command post S - 300 (SA-20/SA-20A/SA-20B) can manage in any combination the elements of S - 200 and S-300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kap-yar.ru_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kap-yar.ru-11">[11]</a></sup> Missiles complex S - 200 Dubna can be controlled command post system S - 300,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kap-yar.ru_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-kap-yar.ru-11">[11]</a></sup> command post S - 300 may also be controlled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> by the command post S - 400 (S - 200 Dubna still have in service)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soldiering.ru_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-soldiering.ru-9">[9]</a></sup> Or through a higher-level command post (Organize Use PVO 73N6 "Baikal-1").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iranian air defense force has implemented several improvements on their S-200 systems such as using solid state parts and removing restrictions on working time. They destroyed a UAV target beyond 100 km range in military drill in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> They use two new solid propellant missile named <a href="/wiki/Sayyad-2" title="Sayyad-2">Sayyad-2</a> and Sayyad-3 via interface systems Talash-2 and Talash-3 in cooperation with S-200 system. These missiles can cover medium and long ranges at high altitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syria">Syria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S-200_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Syria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Syrian Army constructed an S-200 site at Kweires airport, near Aleppo in July 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On 12 September 2016, <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a> confirmed that two Syrian S-200 missiles were fired at Israeli attack planes while they were on a mission inside the Syrian airspace. The Syrian Defense Ministry claimed that an Israeli jet and drone were shot down. According to IDF spokesman's office, the claims are "total lies," and "at no point was the safety of IDF aircraft compromised." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Siberia_Airlines_Flight_1812" title="Siberia Airlines Flight 1812">Siberia Airlines Flight 1812</a>: a Ukrainian S-200 accidentally locked on to a <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-154" title="Tupolev Tu-154">Tupolev Tu-154</a> passenger aircraft when the target <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">drone</a> the missile was aimed at was destroyed by another missile.
