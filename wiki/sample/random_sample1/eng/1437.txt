<p><b>S4C</b> (<small>Welsh pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Welsh" title="Help:IPA for Welsh">[ɛs ˈpɛdwar ɛk]</a></span>, from the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i><span lang="cy">Sianel Pedwar Cymru</span></i>, meaning "Channel Four Wales") is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh-language" title="Welsh-language">Welsh-language</a> <a href="/wiki/Public_broadcasting" title="Public broadcasting">public-service</a> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> channel based in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> and broadcast throughout <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.</p>
<p>The first television channel to be aimed specifically at a Welsh-speaking audience, S4C is (after <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC One</a>, <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a>) the fourth-oldest television channel in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (<a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> was launched in the rest of the country one day later.) Its chief executive since April 2012 is <a href="/wiki/Ian_Jones_(television_executive)" title="Ian Jones (television executive)">Ian Jones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When first established, the channel—initially broadcast on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analogue_television" title="Analogue television">analogue television</a>—was bilingual (Welsh and English) outside peak hours, with English-language content consisting of the simultaneous or deferred transmission of programmes from <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> (analogue reception of which was unavailable in most of Wales). When <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">digital television</a> arrived several years later, S4C added a second, 100% Welsh-language service, called <span lang="cy">S4C Digidol</span> ("digital"). With the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Digital_switchover_dates_in_the_United_Kingdom#Wales" title="Digital switchover dates in the United Kingdom">digital switchover in Wales</a> on 31 March 2010—which made English-language Channel 4 available across Wales—S4C's bilingual analogue channel closed, and what had been S4C Digidol became the default S4C channel, available on <a href="/wiki/Freeview_(UK)" title="Freeview (UK)">Freeview</a>, satellite and cable, and broadcasting entirely in Welsh. S4C does not commission programming in English, but when English is used on the channel it is left untranslated.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition</a> service called <span lang="cy">S4C Clirlun</span> ("clear picture"), <a href="/wiki/Simulcast" title="Simulcast">simulcasting</a> S4C's main channel, began transmission on 30 April 2010 on Freeview channel 53 in Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clirlun_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clirlun-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, it was announced on 11 July 2012 that, as part of cost-saving measures designed to deal with the impact of cuts to S4C's public funding, the Clirlun channel would close before the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4 HD</a> began broadcasting in Wales from 2 December 2012 in its place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C4HD_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-C4HD-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-history">Pre-history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the launch of S4C on Monday 1 November 1982 (one day before Channel 4 in the rest of the UK), Welsh speakers had been served by occasional programmes in Welsh, broadcast as regional opt-outs on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Wales" title="BBC Wales">BBC Wales</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harlech_Television" title="Harlech Television">HTV Cymru Wales</a> (the <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> franchise in Wales), usually at off-peak or inconvenient times. This was unsatisfactory for Welsh speakers, who saw the arrangement as a sop, and also an annoyance for non-Welsh speakers, who found the English programmes seen in the rest of the UK often rescheduled or not transmitted at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962 the <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> network had created a licence area for North and West Wales, which was awarded to <i>Wales (West and North) Limited</i>. This traded as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teledu_Cymru" title="Teledu Cymru">Teledu Cymru</a> and provided significant levels of Welsh-language programming. However, problems with transmission infrastructure and poor market research led to financial difficulties within two years and the station was taken over by its neighbour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_Wales_and_West" title="Television Wales and West">Television Wales and West</a>.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, Welsh-language activists had campaigned for a TV service in the language, which already had its own radio station, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Cymru" title="BBC Radio Cymru">BBC Radio Cymru</a>. Both the Conservative and Labour parties promised a Welsh-language fourth channel, if elected to government in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979 general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Shortly after the Conservatives won a majority in the election, the new home secretary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Whitelaw" title="William Whitelaw">William Whitelaw</a> decided against a Welsh fourth channel, and suggested that, except for an occasional opt-out, the service should be the same as that offered in the rest of the UK. This led to acts of civil disobedience, including refusals to pay the <a href="/wiki/Television_licence" title="Television licence">television licence</a> fee, thereby running the risk of prosecution or even a prison sentence, and sit-ins in BBC and HTV studios. Some took more extreme measures, including attacking television transmitters in Welsh-speaking areas.</p>
<p>In 1980, the former president of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Plaid_Cymru" title="History of Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwynfor_Evans" title="Gwynfor Evans">Gwynfor Evans</a>, threatened to go on hunger strike if the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> did not honour its commitment to provide a Welsh-language TV service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The channel started broadcasting on 1 November 1982, the night before Channel 4's opening. S4C appointed its first female CEO, <a href="/wiki/Iona_Jones" title="Iona Jones">Iona Jones</a> in 2005.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming">Programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg/220px-Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg/330px-Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg/440px-Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Headquarters_of_S4C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The headquarters of S4C in <a href="/wiki/Llanishen" title="Llanishen">Llanishen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>S4C's remit is to provide a service which features a wide range of programmes in the Welsh language. Like Channel 4, S4C does not produce programmes of its own; instead, it commissions programmes from <a href="/wiki/BBC_Cymru_Wales" title="BBC Cymru Wales">BBC Cymru Wales</a> and independent producers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (although the quantity purchased from <a href="/wiki/ITV_Wales_%26_West" title="ITV Wales &amp; West">ITV Cymru Wales</a> has greatly reduced since the early years of S4C), and it has particularly developed a reputation for commissioning animation, such as <i><a href="/wiki/SuperTed" title="SuperTed">SuperTed</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fireman_Sam" title="Fireman Sam">Sam Tân</a></i> (which became <i>Fireman Sam</i> in its English version on the BBC), <i><a href="/wiki/Gogs" title="Gogs">Gogs</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shakespeare:_The_Animated_Tales" title="Shakespeare: The Animated Tales">Shakespeare: The Animated Tales</a></i>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Wales" title="BBC Wales">BBC Wales</a> fulfils its public service requirement by producing programmes in Welsh, including <span lang="cy"><i><a href="/wiki/Newyddion" title="Newyddion">Newyddion</a></i></span>, S4C's news bulletin, and a soap opera, <span lang="cy"><i><a href="/wiki/Pobol_y_Cwm" title="Pobol y Cwm">Pobol y Cwm</a></i></span>, and providing them to S4C free of charge. It has also provided (or licensed) Welsh-language versions of English-language programmes, e.g., <i>The Tweenies</i>. On the analogue service, S4C showed programmes produced for Channel 4 in the rest of the United Kingdom ― either <a href="/wiki/Simulcast" title="Simulcast">simultaneously</a> or time-shifted ― outside of peak hours. These programmes were provided to S4C by Channel 4, free of charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>To make content more accessible to English speakers, all Welsh-language programming carries English subtitles. Originally these were on <a href="/wiki/Teletext" title="Teletext">Teletext</a> page 888, with Welsh subtitles on page 889, with both subtitle languages now also available on digital television platforms. For speakers of English who are learning Welsh, certain programmes, particularly children's programmes <span lang="cy"><i>Planed Plant Bach</i></span> and <span lang="cy"><i>Planed Plant</i></span>, carry subtitles featuring Welsh subtitles with additional English translations in brackets next to more difficult Welsh-language words. TV films produced for S4C have received some good foreign reviews; <i><a href="/wiki/Hedd_Wyn_(film)" title="Hedd Wyn (film)">Hedd Wyn</a></i> was nominated for the Best Foreign Language Oscar in 1994<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Solomon_%26_Gaenor" title="Solomon &amp; Gaenor">Solomon &amp; Gaenor</a></i> was nominated in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The S4C analogue signal also spilled over on to the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. In the past it was rebroadcast in a number of areas there on <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> terrestrial signals by so-called 'deflectors', however Channel 4 is now re-transmitted from satellite by the few remaining deflectors. Up until the 1990s, S4C was also carried by some Irish <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMDS" title="MMDS">MMDS</a> providers before being replaced by Channel 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The S4C signal also continues to overspill into the Republic of Ireland via the Freesat satellite service.</p>
<p>Up until 2009, S4C ran its own <a href="/wiki/Teletext" title="Teletext">teletext</a> service, <a href="/wiki/Sbectel" title="Sbectel">Sbectel</a> (<span lang="cy"><i>"Sbec"</i></span>, Welsh for "a peek" or "a glimpse", and a reference to an S4C schedule insert formerly included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_Times" title="TV Times">TV Times</a> issues for the HTV Wales region).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_channels">Digital channels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Digital channels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the switch-off of analogue terrestrial signals on 31 March 2010, Wales became the first fully digital region in the UK, with both S4C and Channel 4 now available to all homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As a result, S4C now broadcasts solely in the Welsh language and, as well as on <a href="/wiki/Freeview_(UK)" title="Freeview (UK)">Freeview</a> in Wales, is available throughout Britain, Ireland and the rest of western Europe on <a href="/wiki/Freesat" title="Freesat">Freesat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_(UK_%26_Ireland)" title="Sky (UK &amp; Ireland)">Sky</a>. A review commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" title="Department for Culture, Media and Sport">Department for Culture, Media and Sport</a> in 2004 suggested that "S4C should operate a single core service after digital switchover".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laughton_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laughton-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S4C2logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="185" data-file-width="707" height="58" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/S4C2logo.svg/220px-S4C2logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/S4C2logo.svg/330px-S4C2logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/S4C2logo.svg/440px-S4C2logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S4C2logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Logo of the former channel S4C2</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition, S4C also operated a sister channel, <a href="/wiki/S4C2" title="S4C2">S4C2</a> until 2010. It formerly broadcast coverage of the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> when in session. The programme content was provided by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> who, from January 2010, now make it available online and via <a href="/wiki/BBC_Parliament" title="BBC Parliament">BBC Parliament</a>. Like the main channel, S4C2 was available within Wales on Freeview and throughout the UK and Ireland on Freesat and Sky. S4C2 had two audio feeds, allowing viewers to select between an untranslated version and an English-only version where all Welsh spoken is translated into English. Delayed coverage of Assembly proceedings is now broadcast overnight on S4C's main channel on Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Thursday. In addition to the analogue TV signal transmitted throughout Wales, S4C, along with <a href="/wiki/UBM_plc" title="UBM plc">United News &amp; Media</a>, owned the company <a href="/wiki/S4C_Digital_Networks" title="S4C Digital Networks">S4C Digital Networks</a> (SDN). SDN was awarded the UK-wide contract to provide half a digital multiplex worth of programming. The other half continues to belong to the broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Channel_5_(UK)" title="Channel 5 (UK)">Channel 5</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 27 April 2005 S4C sold its share of SDN to <a href="/wiki/ITV_plc" title="ITV plc">ITV plc</a> for approximately £34 million, though it still has the half-multiplex as of right in Wales. ITV already owned some of SDN due to the consolidation of the ITV industry: Granada bought UNM's stake in SDN, and this was then incorporated into the united ITV plc. In January 2007, S4C announced plans to launch a Welsh-language children's service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S4C_announce_children.27s_channel_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-S4C_announce_children.27s_channel-16">[16]</a></sup> The new service, in the form of a programming block, launched on 23 June 2008. Under the name "<a href="/wiki/Cyw" title="Cyw">Cyw</a>" (English: Chick), it brings together a wide range of programmes for nursery-age children, and S4C plans eventually to extend the service to include the "<a href="/wiki/Stwnsh" title="Stwnsh">Stwnsh</a>" strand for older children and a third service for teenagers and young people. The service currently airs on weekdays from 7am to 1.30pm on S4C.</p>
<p>S4C launched a high-definition simulcast of S4C called 'Clirlun' on 19 July 2010 to coincide with terrestrial digital switchover in Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S4C_Press_Release:_S4C_Clirlun_now_available_throughout_Wales_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-S4C_Press_Release:_S4C_Clirlun_now_available_throughout_Wales-17">[17]</a></sup> Clirlun was broadcast on Freeview channel 53 only, and not via other platforms. However, following funding cuts and a review of core services it was announced on 11 July 2012 that Clirlun would close before the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S4C_Press_Release:_S4C_efficiency_measures_on_course_to_meet_targets_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-S4C_Press_Release:_S4C_efficiency_measures_on_course_to_meet_targets-18">[18]</a></sup> Clirlun closed at midnight on the evening of 1 December 2012, with Channel 4 HD taking over its transmission capacity with effect from the next day, 2 December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C4HD_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-C4HD-5">[5]</a></sup> It was announced on 20 May 2016 that S4C would relaunch a high-definition service S4C HD on Freesat and Sky in Wales and across the UK from 7 June onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2014 S4C became available on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_iPlayer" title="BBC iPlayer">BBC iPlayer</a> website, both live and on demand, as part of an 18-month trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1982.E2.80.931988">1982–1988</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1982–1988">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S4C_1982_logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="62" data-file-width="413" height="33" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/S4C_1982_logo.svg/220px-S4C_1982_logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/S4C_1982_logo.svg/330px-S4C_1982_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/S4C_1982_logo.svg/440px-S4C_1982_logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S4C_1982_logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
S4C's first logo, used from 1 November 1982 to 30 September 1988</div>

</div>
<p>On 1 November 1982, S4C's on-air appearance has always been a representation of the Welsh society and people, but this representation has changed several times. Initial idents featured clips from the natural landscapes of Wales with a basic logo animation and fanfare, with the logo forming as <i>WALE<b>S4C</b>YMRU</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> There were several clock idents for the news and closedown.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1987.E2.80.931993">1987–1993</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1987–1993">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="935" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg/200px-S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg/300px-S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg/400px-S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S4C_logo_1988-1995.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
S4C's second logo, used from 8 September 1987 to 9 February 1995</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 8 September 1987, the ident changed to a computer-generated ident featuring an animation of the streamlined S4C logo, the colours of the logo were blue, green and red and the font was Bodoni. There was also a clock ident. On 7 September 1990, the new ident was introduced, depicting a piece of Welsh slate with colour blue, green and red washing over the letters S4C. There were several versions for this ident. From 1 November 1991 onwards, the music had a sombre violin and a bombastic fanfare. This ident with sombre music was used for S4C Clasuron Fideo in 1991 until 1993. There were two different clock idents, first the grey coloured one until 1992, and the final with in gold colour until 4 September 1993.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1993.E2.80.932007">1993–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1993–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 5 September 1993 until 9 February 1995, S4C introduced a new series of idents, which depicted inanimate objects as having characteristics of dragons (such as flight or breathing fire), as a reference to the red dragon on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Wales" title="Flag of Wales">flag of Wales</a>. S4C's logo was also refreshed in 10 February 1995, now using a red <a href="/wiki/Futura_(typeface)" title="Futura (typeface)">Futura</a> typeface, and a <a href="/wiki/Tilde" title="Tilde">tilde</a> representing a dragon, In 1993, there were six normal idents and a clockwork dragon schools ident. From around 1996 the company adopted a Lambie-Nairn identity and five more idents were made. In 1998 and 1999, there were last five more idents and also a schools ident. In 2000 onwards, further idents were added to the original set and some of the original idents were discontinued. The dragon idents were withdrawn in December 2006. From 1 to 16 January 2007, temporary idents were called <i>a new direction</i> and the S4C 1995 logo was withrawn on 17 January 2007.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007.E2.80.932014">2007–2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2007–2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S4C_logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="123" data-file-width="323" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/S4C_logo.svg/220px-S4C_logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/S4C_logo.svg/330px-S4C_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/S4C_logo.svg/440px-S4C_logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S4C_logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
S4C's fourth logo, used from 18 January 2007 to 9 April 2014</div>

<p>In 18 January 2007, S4C announced that their digital channels would be refreshed with a new corporate logo and brand. The new branding was implemented online on 17 January, with S4C's television channels adopting it the next day. The new branding, developed by the London-based firm Proud Creative, was intended to portray S4C as a more "contemporary" multi-platform broadcaster, and downplayed "traditional" Welsh imagery such as dragons. Its idents were filmed around various parts of the country, and themed around magnetism—representing the "uncontrollable attraction" of Welsh people and their "emotional affinity to the homeland, whether near or far."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-s4crevamp_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-s4crevamp-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S4C_announce_rebrand_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-S4C_announce_rebrand-23">[23]</a></sup> The magnetism-themed idents were later replaced by a new set developed in collaboration with the agency Minivegas, consisting of live-action scenes with dynamic, animated elements that can react to the voice of the continuity announcer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mv-idents_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mv-idents-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014.E2.80.93present">2014–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2014–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 10 April 2014 To Present, A new S4C logo and brand developed by <a class="external text" href="http://www.sugarcs.com" rel="nofollow">Sugar Creative Studio</a> was introduced in early 2014; the new design was developed around a concept of providing "context" to S4C's target audience and programming. The design revolves heavily around a trapezoidal shape used within the channel's new logo, which is prominently used within aspects of the channel's overall marketing and branding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sugar-news4c_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sugar-news4c-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to S4C's latest annual report, 9.9 million people viewed the channel throughout the UK during 2015-2016, with 629,000 viewers throughout the UK in an average week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>S4C has faced criticism for poor viewing figures since its launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Leaked internal reports in March 2010 showed that "over the 20-day period from February 15 to last Saturday, March 6, as many as 196 of the 890 programmes put out by S4C were rated as having zero viewers". The story was widely reported across the UK and referenced in parliament by the then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_Secretary" title="Culture Secretary">Culture Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Hunt" title="Jeremy Hunt">Jeremy Hunt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In response, an S4C spokesperson stated that 90% of the programmes were aimed at pre-school children, and that BARB (the organisation that compiles television ratings in the UK) only takes into account viewers aged four years and over. The remaining 10% consisted of repeats and daytime news bulletins which did not attract the 1000 viewers necessary to register on a UK-wide analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 July 2010, S4C's chief executive Iona Jones left her post without explanation. Assembly members and Members of Parliament requested an independent investigation into the circumstances leading up to her departure. The S4C Authority refused to comment further and commissioned a review into how the broadcaster was governed in August 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On 3 February 2011, it was announced that issues between Iona Jones and S4C had been settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On 11 February 2011, the Shortridge Report on corporate governance was made public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_and_regulation">Funding and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Funding and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From its inception S4C was part publicly-financed: funding came both from its advertising revenue and a fixed annual grant from the UK <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" title="Department for Culture, Media and Sport">Department for Culture, Media and Sport</a> (DCMS), receiving £90m of funding in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Additionally, some Welsh-language programming (including <span lang="cy"><i><a href="/wiki/Newyddion" title="Newyddion">Newyddion</a></i></span> and <span lang="cy"><i><a href="/wiki/Pobol_y_Cwm" title="Pobol y Cwm">Pobol y Cwm</a></i></span>) was produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Wales" title="BBC Wales">BBC Wales</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Public_service_broadcasting_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Public service broadcasting in the United Kingdom">public service</a> remit, and provided to S4C free of charge.</p>
<p>From 2013, responsibility for funding S4C began to transfer to the BBC, with the DCMS reducing its funding by 94% by 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The BBC will provide around £76m of funding to S4C by this date, resulting in a cut of around 25% to S4C's annual budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>S4C is controlled by the <a href="/wiki/S4C_Authority" title="S4C Authority">S4C Authority</a> (<span lang="cy"><i>Awdurdod S4C</i></span>), an independent body unconnected to <a href="/wiki/Ofcom" title="Ofcom">Ofcom</a>, the regulator of other UK television channels such as ITV and Channel 4.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clic">Clic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S4C&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Clic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S4C_Catch_Up.png" title="Clic"><img alt="Clic" data-file-height="434" data-file-width="480" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/S4C_Catch_Up.png/200px-S4C_Catch_Up.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/S4C_Catch_Up.png/300px-S4C_Catch_Up.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/S4C_Catch_Up.png/400px-S4C_Catch_Up.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div>
<p><b>Clic</b> is a free online <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">video on demand</a> service provided by S4C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Clic offers a live-streaming, <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">signed</a> programming, a 35-day catch-up service and archive programming. Clic is available across the UK but also contains a limited selection of worldwide programming. Clic's catch-up service is split into seven categories: Drama, Entertainment, Factual and Arts, Music, Sport, and two Children's categories, <a href="/wiki/Cyw" title="Cyw">Cyw</a> (ages 3–6) and <a href="/wiki/Stwnsh" title="Stwnsh">Stwnsh</a> (ages 7–13). A Clic app was released for <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IOS_(Apple)" title="IOS (Apple)">iOS</a> devices on 18 August 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
