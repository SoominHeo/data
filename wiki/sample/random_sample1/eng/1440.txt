<p>The <b>SA80</b> (Small Arms for the 1980s) is a British family of <a href="/wiki/5.56%C3%9745mm_NATO" title="5.56×45mm NATO">5.56×45mm NATO</a> small arms. It is a <a href="/wiki/Selective_fire" title="Selective fire">selective fire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas-operated_reloading" title="Gas-operated reloading">gas-operated</a> <a href="/wiki/Assault_rifle" title="Assault rifle">assault rifle</a>. Mechanically, it has been derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armalite_AR-18" title="Armalite AR-18">Armalite AR-18</a>, which had been manufactured under licence in the UK by <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Armaments_Company" title="Sterling Armaments Company">Sterling Armaments Company</a>. The first prototypes were tried in 1976, and production ended in 1994. It is due to remain in service until 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The L85 rifle variant of the SA80 family has been the standard issue <a href="/wiki/Service_rifle" title="Service rifle">service rifle</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a> since 1987, replacing the <a href="/wiki/L1A1_Self-Loading_Rifle" title="L1A1 Self-Loading Rifle">L1A1</a> variant of the <a href="/wiki/FN_FAL" title="FN FAL">FN FAL</a>. The A1 variant was upgraded in the early 2000s by <a href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch" title="Heckler &amp; Koch">Heckler &amp; Koch</a> as the SA80A2 and remains in service as of 2016. In mid-2016 a prototype A3 variant was showcased which further improved on the weapon and is reportedly being considered to extend the out of service date beyond 2025. The remainder of the family comprises the L86 Light Support Weapon, the short-barrelled L22 <a href="/wiki/Carbine" title="Carbine">carbine</a> and the L98 Cadet rifle.</p>
<p>The SA80 was the last in a long line of British weapons (including the <a href="/wiki/Lee%E2%80%93Enfield" title="Lee–Enfield">Lee–Enfield</a> family) to come from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Small_Arms_Factory" title="Royal Small Arms Factory">Royal Small Arms Factory</a>, the national arms development and production facility at <a href="/wiki/Enfield_Lock" title="Enfield Lock">Enfield Lock</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The system's history dates back to the late 1940s, when an ambitious programme to develop a new cartridge and new class of rifle was launched in the United Kingdom based on combat experience drawn from <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Two <a href="/wiki/.280_British" title=".280 British">7mm</a> prototypes were built in a <a href="/wiki/Bullpup" title="Bullpup">bullpup</a> configuration, designated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EM-2_Bullpup_Rifle" title="EM-2 Bullpup Rifle">EM-1</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EM-2_Bullpup_Rifle" title="EM-2 Bullpup Rifle">EM-2</a>. When <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> adopted the <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> rifle cartridge as the standard calibre for its <a href="/wiki/Service_rifle" title="Service rifle">service rifles</a>, further development of these rifles was discontinued (the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> chose to adopt the 7.62 mm <a href="/wiki/L1A1_Self-Loading_Rifle" title="L1A1 Self-Loading Rifle">L1A1</a> SLR <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_rifle" title="Semi-automatic rifle">semi-automatic rifle</a>, which is a licence-built version of the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/FN_FAL" title="FN FAL">FN FAL</a>).</p>
<p>In 1969, the Enfield factory began work on a brand new family of weapons, chambered in a newly designed British <a href="/wiki/4.85%C3%9749mm" title="4.85×49mm">4.85×49mm</a> intermediate cartridge. While the experimental weapon family was very different from the EM-2 in internal design and construction methods, its bullpup configuration with an optical sight was a clear influence on the design of what was to become the SA80. The system was to be composed of two weapons: the <a href="/wiki/L64/65" title="L64/65">XL64E5</a> rifle (also called the <i>Enfield Individual Weapon</i>) and a light support weapon known as the <a href="/wiki/L64/65" title="L64/65">XL65E4</a> <a href="/wiki/Light_machine_gun" title="Light machine gun">light machine gun</a>.</p>
<p>The sheet metal construction, and the design of the bolt, bolt carrier, guide rods, gas system and the weapon's disassembly showed strong similarities to the Armalite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AR-18" title="AR-18">AR-18</a> which was manufactured under licence from 1975 to 1983 by the <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Armaments_Company" title="Sterling Armaments Company">Sterling Armaments Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dagenham" title="Dagenham">Dagenham</a>, Essex,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OffTarget_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OffTarget-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolazell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolazell-6">[6]</a></sup> and which had been tested by the UK MoD in 1966 and 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolazell_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dolazell-6">[6]</a></sup> During the development of the SA-80 a bullpup conversion was made of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AR-18" title="AR-18">AR-18</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Stoner_63" title="Stoner 63">Stoner 63</a> at Enfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Technically, in the mid-1970s, the <a href="/wiki/4.85%C3%9749mm" title="4.85×49mm">4.85×49mm</a> round was seen as superior to the then existing version of 5.56mm M193 round in use by the US (for the <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle" title="M16 rifle">M16/M16A1</a>) and by other forces. (This was the expressed view of trials team members whilst demonstrating the XL64E5 prototype at the British Army School of Infantry at <a href="/wiki/Warminster" title="Warminster">Warminster</a>.) It should be noted that development of small-arms munitions have a long and continuous life and it was estimated by the trials specialists from Enfield that this weapon would ultimately be superior in the 4.85mm configuration. For the 4.85 mm round, both propellant and projectile were at the beginning of their respective development curves. Also, weight for weight, more rounds of ammunition could be carried by an individual soldier – a considerable advantage on the battlefield. It was regarded as probable at the time that the argument for the 5.56 mm standard within NATO had more to do with the economics involved. Over the lifetime of a small-arms weapon type, far more money is spent on the munitions than the weapons themselves. If the 5.56 mm supporters had lost the argument in favour of a British 4.85 mm round, the economic impact would have been very large and political pressure undoubtedly played a part in the final decision.</p>
<p>In 1976, the prototypes were ready to undergo trials. However, after NATO's decision to standardise ammunition among its members, Enfield engineers re-chambered the rifles to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/5.56%C3%9745mm_NATO" title="5.56×45mm NATO">5.56×45mm NATO</a> M193 cartridge. The newly redesigned 5.56 mm version of the XL64E5 became known as the XL70E3. The left-handed XL68 was also re-chambered in 5.56×45mm as the XL78. The 5.56mm light support weapon variant, the XL73E3, developed from the XL65E4, was noted for the full length receiver extension with the bipod under the muzzle now indicative of the type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watters-Chronology_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watters-Chronology-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further development out of the initial so-called "Phase A"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watters-Chronology_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watters-Chronology-10">[10]</a></sup> pre-production series led to the XL85 and XL86. While the XL85E1 and XL86E1 were ultimately adopted as the L85 and L86 respectively, a number of additional test models were produced. The XL85E2 and XL86E2 were designed to an alternate build standard with 12 components different from E1 variants, including parts of the gas system, bolt, and magazine catch. Three series of variants were created for "Environmental User Trials". XL85E3 and XL86E3 variants were developed with 24 modified parts, most notably a plastic safety plunger. The E4's had 21 modified parts, no modification to the pistol grip, and an <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> safety plunger, unlike the E3 variants. Lastly, the E5 variants had 9 modified parts in addition to those from the E3/E4 variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watters-Chronology_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Watters-Chronology-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>SA80 development was complicated from the start. One complication was at least three project staffing changes at the Royal Small Arms Factory, which resulted in repetition of testing several times. One problem with the design of the gun itself was that the cases would be ejected at constantly varying angles as it heated up and the rate of fire changed, resulting in a large ejection port. The conversion from 4.85 mm to 5.56 mm also caused a complication, as the rate of fire dropped dramatically as the gas port was left in the same position, but the pressure and time curve of the rounds were different. The 4.85 mm round was based on the 5.56 mm case in anticipation of the need to convert calibres. The barrel was changed easily, but the gas ports had to be enlarged considerably. This was made worse by the production of ammunition with power that gave a lower port pressure and rate-of-fire. Pressure problems had less of an effect on the LSW due to its longer barrel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>After receiving feedback from users and incorporating the various design changes requested, including adapting the rifle for use with the heavier Belgian SS109 version of the 5.56×45mm round and improving reliability, the weapon system was accepted into service with the British Armed Forces in 1985 as the SA80. The SA80 family originally consisted of the <b>L85A1 IW</b> (Individual Weapon) and the <b>L86A1 LSW</b> (Light Support Weapon). The first rifle was issued on 2 October 1985 to Sergeant Gary Gavin, a 26-year-old in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worcestershire_and_Sherwood_Foresters" title="Worcestershire and Sherwood Foresters">Worcestershire and Sherwood Foresters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OffTarget_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-OffTarget-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SA80 family was designed and produced (until 1988) by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Small_Arms_Factory" title="Royal Small Arms Factory">Royal Small Arms Factory</a> at <a href="/wiki/Enfield_Lock" title="Enfield Lock">Enfield Lock</a>. In 1988, production of the rifle was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ordnance" title="Royal Ordnance">Royal Ordnance</a>'s Nottingham Small Arms Facility (later <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace" title="British Aerospace">British Aerospace</a>, Royal Ordnance; now <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Land_%26_Armaments" title="BAE Systems Land &amp; Armaments">BAE Systems Land &amp; Armaments</a>).</p>

<p>In 1994, production was officially completed. More than 350,000 L85A1 rifles and L86A1 light machine guns had been manufactured for the United Kingdom. As responsibility for the funding and supply of the home defence regiments of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territory" title="British Overseas Territory">British Overseas Territories</a> (which are only loosely integrated within the British Army) has been handed to the local governments of the territories (although the regiments themselves come under command of the national (British) government), the SA80 was not automatically supplied to these units. The <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands_Defence_Force" title="Falkland Islands Defence Force">Falkland Islands Defence Force</a> instead adopted the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Steyr_AUG" title="Steyr AUG">Steyr AUG</a> while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_Regiment" title="Bermuda Regiment">Bermuda Regiment</a> (since 2015 the <i>Royal Bermuda Regiment</i>) adopted the American <a href="/wiki/Ruger_Mini-14" title="Ruger Mini-14">Ruger Mini-14</a> in 1983 (although it has held small stocks of the L85A1 and L85A2 since the 1980s for the purpose of familiarisation training as many of its personnel attend courses or attachments in Britain). The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Gibraltar_Regiment" title="Royal Gibraltar Regiment">Royal Gibraltar Regiment</a>, which is more closely integrated into British Army, adopted the SA80. In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bermuda_Regiment" title="Royal Bermuda Regiment">Royal Bermuda Regiment</a> replaced the Mini-14 with the L85A2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalgazette.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-royalgazette.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The L85 is also in use with the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Defence_Force" title="Jamaica Defence Force">Jamaica Defence Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MJ_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MJ-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_mechanism">Operating mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operating mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the exception of the L98A1, the SA80 system is a <a href="/wiki/Selective_fire" title="Selective fire">selective fire</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas-operated_reloading" title="Gas-operated reloading">gas-operated</a> design that uses ignited powder gases bled through a port in the barrel to provide the weapon's automation. The rifle uses a short-stroke gas piston system located above the barrel, which is fed gas through a three-position adjustable gas regulator. The first gas setting is used for normal operation, the second is for use in difficult environmental conditions, while the third setting prevents any gas from reaching the piston and is used to launch <a href="/wiki/Rifle_grenade" title="Rifle grenade">rifle grenades</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The weapon uses a rotating cylindrical bolt that contains seven radially mounted locking splines, an extractor and casing ejector. The bolt's rotation is controlled by a cam stud that slides inside a <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">helical</a> camming guide machined into the bolt carrier.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The family is built in a <a href="/wiki/Bullpup" title="Bullpup">bullpup</a> layout (the action is behind the trigger group), with a forward-mounted <a href="/wiki/Pistol_grip" title="Pistol grip">pistol grip</a>. The main advantage of this type of arrangement is the overall compactness of the weapon, which can be achieved without compromising the barrel length, hence the overall length of the L85 rifle is shorter than a carbine, but the barrel length is that of an assault rifle. However, the adoption of this layout also means that the rifle must be used exclusively right-handed since the ejection port and cocking handle (which reciprocates during firing) are on the right side of the receiver, making aimed fire from the left shoulder difficult. This can also give rise to a tactical disadvantage when firing around the left side of cover, where the shooter must expose the majority of his body. However left shoulder firing can be achieved by tilting the right hand side of the rifle downwards, reducing the impediments of the cocking handle and the ejection port.</p>
<p>The SA80 family is hammer-fired and has a trigger mechanism with a fire-control selector that enables semi-automatic fire and fully automatic fire (the fire selector lever is located at the left side of the receiver, just aft of the magazine). A cross bolt type safety prevents accidental firing and is located above the trigger; the "safe" setting blocks the movement of the trigger.</p>
<p>The L85 rifle features a barrel with a slotted <a href="/wiki/Flash_suppressor" title="Flash suppressor">flash suppressor</a>, which also serves as a mounting base for attaching and launching rifle grenades, attaching a <a href="/wiki/Blank-firing_adaptor" title="Blank-firing adaptor">blank-firing adaptor</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a>.</p>
<p>The weapons are fed from a <a href="/wiki/STANAG_magazine" title="STANAG magazine">STANAG magazine</a>, usually with a 30-round capacity. The magazine release button is placed above the magazine housing, on the left side of the receiver. When the last cartridge is fired from the magazine, the bolt and bolt carrier assembly lock to the rear.</p>
<p>The weapon's receiver is made from <a href="/wiki/Stamping_(metalworking)" title="Stamping (metalworking)">stamped</a> <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet</a> <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, reinforced with <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">riveted</a> <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machined</a> steel inserts. Synthetics were also used (i.e. the handguards, pistol grip, buttpad and cheek rest were all fabricated from <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Picatinny_rail" title="Picatinny rail">Picatinny</a> railed handguard was also developed for the type.</p>

<p>Rifles used by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> infantry soldiers (and other soldiers with a dismounted close combat role) and the <a href="/wiki/RAF_Regiment" title="RAF Regiment">RAF Regiment</a> are equipped with a <a href="/wiki/SUSAT" title="SUSAT">SUSAT</a> (Sight Unit Small Arms, Trilux) optical sight, with a fixed 4× magnification and an illuminated aiming pointer powered by a variable <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> light source (as of 2006 almost all British Army personnel deployed on operations have been issued SUSATs). Mounted on the SUSAT's one-piece, pressure <a href="/wiki/Die_casting" title="Die casting">die-cast</a> <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> body are a set of back-up <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_sight" title="Iron sight">iron sights</a> that consist of a front blade and small rear aperture. Rifles used with other branches of the armed forces when not on operations are configured with fixed iron sights, consisting of a flip rear aperture housed inside the carrying handle and a forward post vertical blade foresight, installed on a bracket above the gas block. The rear sight can be adjusted for windage, and the foresight—elevation. In place of the SUSAT, a passive night vision CWS scope can be used, and also—independent of the SUSAT—a laser pointer.</p>

<p>Weapons used by some Royal Marines, Infantry, RAF Regiment, <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_Police" title="Ministry of Defence Police">Ministry of Defence Police</a> and other soldiers with a dismounted close combat role in operations in Afghanistan have had the SUSAT replaced with the <a href="/wiki/Trijicon" title="Trijicon">Trijicon</a> <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Combat_Optical_Gunsight" title="Advanced Combat Optical Gunsight">Advanced Combat Optical Gunsight</a> (ACOG).</p>
<p>In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> began issuing <a href="/wiki/ELCAN_Optical_Technologies" title="ELCAN Optical Technologies">ELCAN</a> SpecterOS 4× Lightweight Day Sights (LDS) in an effort to replace ageing SUSAT units across the British Armed Forces, forming the first stage of the <a href="/wiki/Future_Integrated_Soldier_Technology" title="Future Integrated Soldier Technology">FIST</a> infantry enhancement project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In order to mount the new sight, the weapon has been provided with an adapter to convert the existing sight rail to the <a href="/wiki/Picatinny_rail" title="Picatinny rail">Picatinny</a> standard, in keeping with the updated handguard. The FIST project has also seen upgrades to the existing <a href="/wiki/Thales_Optronics" title="Thales Optronics">Qioptiq</a> CWS (4×) and Maxi-Kite (6×) night vision scopes, and the introduction of the FIST Thermal Sight, following operational experience with the VIPIR-2+ thermal weapon sight in Afghanistan. All of the new FIST weapon sights have the capacity to accept Shield's Close Quarter Battlesight reflex sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>The L85 is supplied with a sling, <a href="/wiki/Blank-firing_adaptor" title="Blank-firing adaptor">blank-firing adaptor</a>, cleaning kit and a blade-type bayonet, which coupled with the sheath can double as a wire cutter (the sheath contains a small saw and sharpening stone). The rifle can be adapted to use <a href="/wiki/.22_Long_Rifle" title=".22 Long Rifle">.22 Long Rifle</a> training ammunition with a special conversion kit. The <a href="/wiki/Small_Arms_Weapons_Effects_Simulator" title="Small Arms Weapons Effects Simulator">Small Arms Weapons Effects Simulator</a> can be used on the L85 when in training with blank ammunition. The rifle variant also accommodates a 40 mm under-barrel <a href="/wiki/Grenade_launcher" title="Grenade launcher">grenade launcher</a> such as Heckler &amp; Koch AG-36 40 mm grenade launcher variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four main variants that make up the SA80 family: the <b>L85 IW Rifle</b>, the <b>L86 Light Support Weapon</b>, the <b>L22 Carbine</b> and the <b>L98 Cadet rifle</b>. The family has currently undergone two major models, Lxx<b>A1</b> being the first issue weapons, and the Lxx<b>A2</b> to distinguish weapons which have undergone H&amp;K upgrades. The L designation is for "Land Service".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L85_Rifle">L85 Rifle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: L85 Rifle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <b>L85 IW</b> ("Individual Weapon"; full name: Rifle, 5.56 mm, L85A2), in its improved A2 version, is the standard rifle for the British armed forces.</p>
<p>On operations with ground close combat units (Infantry, Royal Marines, RAF Regiment), the rifle is often fitted with an <a href="/wiki/LLM01" title="LLM01">LLM01</a> Laser Light Module.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The L85A2 can also mount the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AG36" title="AG36">L123A2 UGL</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/40_mm" title="40 mm">40 mm</a> underbarrel grenade launcher (British variant of the H&amp;K AG36). The addition of the underbarrel grenade launcher adds another 3.30 lb (1.49 kg) to the L85A2's weight.</p>

<p>Magazines issued with the L85A1 were aluminium,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and not very robust. There are now three types of magazine issued with the L85A2, the most recent being the plastic <a href="/wiki/Magpul_Industries" title="Magpul Industries">Magpul</a> EMAG purchased as an <a href="/wiki/Urgent_Operational_Requirement" title="Urgent Operational Requirement">Urgent Operational Requirement</a> (UOR),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> the other two are of steel construction with a stainless steel follower. The main variant is for live ammunition, and the other is exclusively used for blank ammunition. The blank variant is identified by yellow stripes on the magazine, and is designed to prevent the loading of live rounds. As blank rounds are shorter than live rounds, live rounds will not physically fit into the blank magazine. Blank rounds will fit into the normal magazine, but their slightly shorter length creates problems with jamming.</p>
<p>From 2007, an upgrade including the provision of ACOGs, a new handguard incorporating <a href="/wiki/Picatinny_rail" title="Picatinny rail">Picatinny rails</a> (with optional hand grip/bipod),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and a new vortex style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_hider" title="Flash hider">flash eliminator</a> is being introduced for use by selected units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L86_LSW">L86 LSW</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: L86 LSW">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kozacki_Step_83.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="682" data-file-width="1024" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Kozacki_Step_83.jpg/220px-Kozacki_Step_83.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Kozacki_Step_83.jpg/330px-Kozacki_Step_83.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Kozacki_Step_83.jpg/440px-Kozacki_Step_83.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kozacki_Step_83.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
LSW during training</div>

<p>The <b>L86A1 LSW</b> ("Light Support Weapon") is a magazine-fed automatic weapon originally intended to provide fire support at a <a href="/wiki/Fireteam" title="Fireteam">fireteam</a> level. It has a longer barrel than the L85A1 rifle and a bipod, shoulder strap and rear pistol grip, together with a shorter handguard. The extended barrel provides an increased muzzle velocity and further stabilizes the bullet, giving a greater effective range. The weapon is otherwise identical to the L85 version on which it is based, and the same 30-round magazines and sighting systems are used. Like the L85 rifle, it has a rate-of-fire selector on the left side behind the magazine housing, enabling either single shots or automatic fire.</p>
<p>The increased barrel length, bipod and the optical performance of the SUSAT give the weapon excellent accuracy. From its inception, the L86 was a target of criticism on much the same basis as the L85. The LSW has the additional issue (shared by any light support weapon derived from a rifle, for example the heavy-barrel <a href="/wiki/FN_FAL" title="FN FAL">FN FAL</a>) of its inability to deliver sustained automatic fire as it does not have a quick-change barrel, and is not belt fed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a time, the primary use of the LSW has shifted to that of a marksman's weapon within many infantry sections, capable of providing precision fire at ranges of over 600 m;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> however, it was replaced in this role by the Rifle, 7.62 mm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L129A1" title="L129A1">L129A1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The role of a light support weapon is instead filled by the L110A2 Light Machine Gun <a href="/wiki/FN_Minimi" title="FN Minimi">FN Minimi</a>, which is a belt fed weapon with a quick-change barrel.</p>
<p>The L86A1 was upgraded to the L86A2 at the same time as L85A1 rifles were upgraded to L85A2 standards, undergoing the same set of modifications.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L22_Carbine">L22 Carbine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: L22 Carbine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brits_soldiers_in_Basra_03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>There have been three attempts at a carbine version of the L85. The first was in 1989 (length overall 556 mm, barrel length 289 mm). The second attempt, which was in 1994, used the standard L86 LSW handguard and a 17.4-inch barrel (length overall 709 mm, barrel length 442 mm). The third attempt (2003–2004) is also the only one to officially be adopted – the L22. This resembles the 1989 model but has all the necessary A2 upgrades, and has a 318 mm (12.5 in) barrel and an overall length of 585 mm. Around 1,500 were "manufactured" from surplus L86 LSWs; more were built with the increased demand. Due to the shortened barrel (12.5 in), it is less accurate and less powerful, especially at long ranges. Because there is no handguard, these guns are outfitted with a vertical front grip. It exists in A1 and A2 variants. Initially issued to tank and armoured vehicle crews for emergency action out of vehicle, the L22 has been seen in the hands of the Royal Marines Fleet Protection Group and pilots of all three services due to its compact size.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L98_Cadet_General_Purpose_Rifles">L98 Cadet General Purpose Rifles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: L98 Cadet General Purpose Rifles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <i>L98A1 Cadet GP Rifle</i> was a general-purpose (GP) <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifle</a> used by the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Cadet_Force" title="Combined Cadet Force">Combined Cadet Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_Cadets" title="Sea Cadets">Sea</a>, Royal Marine, <a href="/wiki/Army_Cadet_Force" title="Army Cadet Force">Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_Training_Corps" title="Air Training Corps">Air Cadets</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It was introduced in 1987 to replace the <a href="/wiki/.303_British" title=".303 British">.303</a> <a href="/wiki/Lee%E2%80%93Enfield#Rifle_No_4_Mk_I" title="Lee–Enfield">Lee–Enfield No 4 rifles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bren_gun" title="Bren gun">.303 Bren guns</a> used for weapons training. The L98A1 rifle began a phased decommission in early 2009 and is now no longer in use. UK cadet forces have now received the updated L98A2 rifles. The L98A1 was similar to the L85A1, but lacked the gas components. It was a manually operated, <a href="/wiki/Single-shot" title="Single-shot">single-shot</a> rifle, with a cocking handle extension piece mounted on the right side of the weapon, and was cocked with the right hand. It was also fitted with adjustable iron sights.</p>
<p>The L98A1 had a number of design features that caused problems. A stoppage occurred if the <a href="/wiki/Cocking_handle" title="Cocking handle">cocking handle</a> was not fully retracted and released because the spent round failed to eject cleanly, fouling the breech and preventing the loading of the next cartridge. This fault was often caused by poor cleaning as dirt, grit and rain easily foul and remove the oil from the exposed cocking handle slide making the action harder to cycle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The absence of the <a href="/wiki/Flash_suppressor" title="Flash suppressor">flash suppressor</a> also prevented the fitting of a <a href="/wiki/Blank-firing_adaptor" title="Blank-firing adaptor">blank firing attachment (BFA)</a> thus increasing the safety distance from 5 m to 50 m.</p>
<p>A conversion kit existed that enabled the L85A1, L86A1 and L98A1 to fire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/.22_LR" title=".22 LR">.22 LR</a> <a href="/wiki/Rimfire_ammunition" title="Rimfire ammunition">rimfire</a> cartridges instead of the standard 5.56×45mm NATO cartridge. This was designated the L41A1. This allowed the weapon to fire live rounds on .22 ranges when full size military ranges are unavailable. The kit consisted of modified working parts (springs etc.), a special magazine that is the same size and shape as the standard 5.56 mm magazine and a breech insert, shaped like a 5.56 mm cartridge, which was fitted into the weapons breech. This adapter contained a smaller breech into which the modified bolt inserts the .22 cartridge. The modified magazine locked into the magazine housing exactly like a normal one would. It allowed .22 rounds to be fired semi-automatically using direct blow back against the bolt to cycle the next round. If the kit was fitted to the L98A1 a standard L85 cocking handle had to be fitted to allow semi automatic fire. The conversion was not permanent and could be removed from the weapon in the time it took to normally strip and reassemble the weapon. This kit was not compatible with the A2 upgrade and was removed from service, however a quantity have been modified to work in A2 weapons and have been approved for use in the L98A2, this kit has been designated as the L41A2.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drill_Purpose_Rifle" title="Drill Purpose Rifle">Drill Purpose</a> (DP) version of the L98A1, known as the L103A1. It was similar to the GP rifle, but modifications had been made in order to deactivate it: the barrel was sealed by filling it with lead, the firing pin was cut and welded down to the bolt face and the hammer was filed down, making reactivation uneconomical. The weapons were used by cadets for weapons training. The DP could be identified by a white stripe on the hand guard and near the butt of the weapon with the letters "DP" in the stripe. The bolt carrier assembly was painted red and this can be seen from the breech on the right hand side of the weapon.</p>
<p>The <i>L98A2 GP Rifle</i> was introduced in 2009, as a replacement for the L98A1 Cadet GP Rifle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Unlike the L98A1 the A2 has the same cocking handle and operation as the L85A2; the L98A2 can also be distinguished from the A1 via the presence of an "HK A2" stamp on their gas cylinders. The L98A2 can be fitted with the Safe Blank Firing System (SBFS) incorporating a <a href="/wiki/Blank-firing_adaptor" title="Blank-firing adaptor">Blank Firing Attachment (BFA)</a> and a blank-only magazine. It can only be distinguished from the L85A2 by the absence of a selector switch meaning it is locked on semi-automatic fire.</p>

<p>The L103A2 Cadet DP (Drill Purpose) Rifle is a deactivated L98A2 used by cadets for practicing rifle drill and weapons handling tests. The L103A2 contains similar working and gas parts to the standard live firing weapon but has been extensively modified so it is impossible to convert it back to a functioning firearm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment">Deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SA80 has been used in all conflicts in which the British Armed Forces have been involved since its introduction in the mid-1980s. Deployments include Northern Ireland, the First Gulf War, Bosnia, Kosovo, Sierra Leone, Afghanistan and Iraq. The British went into battle with fixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayonets" title="Bayonets">bayonets</a> on the SA80 in Iraq, the first time fixed bayonets had been used since the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On several occasions, fixed bayonets have been used during the Afghanistan conflict also.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_and_modification">Service and modification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SA80&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Service and modification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after being adopted for service, problems began to surface:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>…the first five years of this rifle's service have been disastrous. A number of manufacturing defects showed up in service conditions, and it was not until the closure of the RSAF at Enfield and the setting up of an entirely new production line, with new computer-controlled machine tools, at the new RSAF Nottingham, that the quality of the production weapons began to improve. It will take some time for the poor reputation gained by the initial issue weapons to be overcome; the only consolation is that the same sort of thing has happened to other military rifles in the past, and they have managed to live down their early reputation and prove their innate reliability. It is to be hoped that the L85A1 will do so as well.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Ian_V._Hogg" title="Ian V. Hogg">Ian V. Hogg</a>, 1990<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>When the L85A1 and L86A1 were first sent into major combat during the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, individual performance was appalling. The L85A1 proved unreliable in semi-auto mode, and slightly better in full-auto, while the L86A1 performed the opposite. Specific complaints included: the poor quality plastic furniture fell apart and the gun was damaged easily; the magazine release catch was easily knocked accidentally and dropped the magazine; the catch on the housing over the gas mechanism was too weak and constantly popped open, so it had to be taped down; only 26–28 rounds could be loaded in a magazine because the springs were weak, and it also had to be kept very clean and the lips checked for dents; the LSW had a small magazine capacity for its role and overheated after 120–150 rounds fired in bursts; the weapons were difficult to strip and reassemble, with the gas plug easily jamming in place and requiring an armorer to remove; and ergonomic issues related to the safety catch, cocking lever, and the location and stiffness of the fire selector switch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SA80 initially gained a poor reputation amongst British soldiers as being unreliable and fragile, a fact picked up by the UK media,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2-27">[27]</a></sup> entertainment industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and members of the House of Lords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The writer and former soldier <a href="/wiki/Andy_McNab" title="Andy McNab">Andy McNab</a> said in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bravo_Two_Zero_(1993_book)" title="Bravo Two Zero (1993 book)">Bravo Two Zero</a></i> that the British Army procured a "Rolls-Royce in the SA80, albeit a prototype Rolls-Royce".</p>
<p>Immediately after the first Gulf war (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Granby" title="Operation Granby">Operation Granby</a>), the UK <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> (MoD) commissioned the LANDSET Report (officially entitled "Equipment Performance (SA80) During Operation Granby (The Gulf War)"), into the effectiveness of the L85A1 IW and L86A1 LSW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This report criticised the acceptance of the weapon into service. Neither weapon had managed to pass the sand trials and both frequently jammed. The mechanism of both weapons needed to be well lubricated as the weapon became prone to seizure if fired "dry", yet in sandy condition the lubricated weapon became unreliable due to the lubricant attracting sand into the moving parts. The LANDSET report identified in excess of 50 faults, most notably the magazine release catch, which could easily be caught on clothing and therefore accidentally release the magazine; the plastic safety plunger which became brittle in cold climates; and firing pins that were not up to repeated use, and prone to fracture if used in automatic fire mode. Although this report identified over 50 faults, and some of the rifle's problems were corrected as a result (e.g. the magazine release guard and trigger), these modifications addressed only seven of these issues and complaints over reliability in service continued.</p>
<p>The Ministry of Defence applied various minor fixes to problems in the guns or simply denied that a problem existed. The MoD finally began to address issues with the SA80 family in 1992 but procuring entirely new weapons was considered too expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>As a result, a more extensive modification programme was executed. In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch" title="Heckler &amp; Koch">Heckler &amp; Koch</a>, at that time owned by the British defence conglomerate BAE Systems, was contracted to upgrade the SA80 family of weapons. Two hundred thousand SA80s were re-manufactured at a cost of £400 each, producing the A2 variant. Changes focused primarily on improving reliability and include: a redesigned cocking handle, modified bolt, extractor and a redesigned hammer assembly that produces a slight delay in the hammer's operation in continuous fire mode, improving reliability and stability. There were equivalent LSW and carbine modifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-22">[22]</a></sup> The British <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> describes the L85A2 revision as "modified in light of operational experience ... the most reliable weapons of their type in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-modsa80_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-modsa80-31">[31]</a></sup> Armed Forces trials indicated extremely good reliability over a range of climates for various operational scenarios, though with a decline in reliability in hot, and especially hot and dry conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armytrials_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-armytrials-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The L85A2 has achieved an average reliability rate of 25,200 <a href="/wiki/Mean_time_between_failures" title="Mean time between failures">mean rounds between failure</a>, and the L86A2 achieved 12,897 mean rounds between failures. Both weapons have higher reliability rates in cold/dry, temperate, and hot/wet conditions (over 31,500 MRBF for L85A2), but lower rates in hot/dry environments. The minimum expected life of A2 components is 10,000 rounds, meaning they may never suffer stoppages during their lifetimes. The L85A1 was required to be able to fire 120 rounds over 24 hours, and the L86A1 was required to fire 800 rounds in 24 hours. The L85A2 is required to fire 150 rounds in 8 minutes 40 seconds, and the L86A2 is required to fire 960 rounds in 36 minutes. The first A2-style SA80 weapons were rushed into action in Afghanistan in December 2001, and all 200,000 were converted by February 2006. Three to four thousand weapons were converted per month. Despite the modifications, reports started to emerge that the L85A2 was still jamming. In reality, there were few jams and problems that were much less serious than they were made out to be, stemming from isolated cases of soldiers not cleaning their weapons correctly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modified A2 variants are distinguished by the "HK A2" marking on the top of the weapon just forward of the buttplate, and the distinctive comma shaped cocking handle (shaped to aid the ejection of the empty round casing and prevent stoppages). A forward Picatinny accessories rail supplied by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defense" title="Daniel Defense">Daniel Defense</a> was incorporated from 2008. The <a href="/wiki/Magpul_Industries" title="Magpul Industries">Magpul Industries</a> polymer EMAG magazine was introduced from 2011 to replace the Heckler &amp; Koch steel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STANAG_4179" title="STANAG 4179">STANAG 4179</a> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Continued testing of the L85A2 in adverse conditions demonstrates its reliability over contemporary rifles, including the M16. Although it is heavier than most conventional and more modern bullpup rifles, its full-length barrel gives higher muzzle velocities and better terminal performance than both the M4 and the M16. Rounds from an M4 will only reliably fragment out to 50–100 metres, while the L85A2 and M16 allowed fragmentation out to 150–200 metres, and the L86A2 has an even longer fragmentation range. Despite these modifications, the L86A2 did not overcome efforts to replace it with a belt-fed machine gun. British troops were issued with <a href="/wiki/FN_Minimi" title="FN Minimi">L110A1</a> machine guns to add suppressive fire out to 300 metres; despite these officially being supplementary weapons, they all but replaced the L86.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nildram_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-nildram-11">[11]</a></sup> The L129 was issued to provide increased terminal effect at long range, to compensate for the limitations of both L85 and L86 weapons.</p>
