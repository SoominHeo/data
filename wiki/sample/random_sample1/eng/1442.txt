<p><b>Super Audio CD</b> (<b>SACD</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">read-only</a> <a href="/wiki/Optical_disc" title="Optical disc">optical disc</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_storage" title="Audio storage">audio storage</a>, introduced in 1999. It was developed jointly by <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips Electronics</a>, and intended to be the successor to their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Disc" title="Compact Disc">Compact Disc</a> (CD) format. While the SACD format can offer more channels (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Surround_sound" title="Surround sound">surround sound</a>), and a longer playing time than CD, research published in 2007 found no significant difference in audio quality between SACD and standard CD at ordinary volume levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AES_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AES-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having made little impact in the consumer audio market, by 2007 SACD was deemed to be a failure by the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2007-2">[2]</a></sup> A small market for SACD has remained, serving the <a href="/wiki/Audiophile" title="Audiophile">audiophile</a> community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-availability_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-availability-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Super Audio CD format was introduced in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2007-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Royal Philips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crest_Digital" title="Crest Digital">Crest Digital</a> partnered in May 2002 to develop and install the first SACD hybrid disc production line in the USA, with a production capacity of 3 million discs per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> SACD did not achieve the same level of growth that Compact discs enjoyed in the 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitaltrends_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-digitaltrends-5">[5]</a></sup> and was not accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet2009-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereophile2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-stereophile2006-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avrev2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-avrev2006-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_SACD_artists" title="List of SACD artists">List of SACD artists</a></div>
<p>By October 2009, record companies had published more than 6,000 SACD releases, slightly more than half of which were <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">Jazz</a> and popular music albums, mainly remastered previous releases, were the next two most numerous genres represented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many popular artists have released some or all of their back catalog on SACD. <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Side_of_the_Moon" title="The Dark Side of the Moon">The Dark Side of the Moon</a></i> (1973) sold over 800,000 copies by June 2004 in its SACD Surround Sound edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SACDGold_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SACDGold-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>'s rock opera <i><a href="/wiki/Tommy_(album)" title="Tommy (album)">Tommy</a></i> (1969), and <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music" title="Roxy Music">Roxy Music</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Avalon_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Avalon (Roxy Music album)">Avalon</a></i> (1982), were released on SACD to take advantage of the format's multi-channel capability. All three albums were remixed in <a href="/wiki/5.1_surround_sound" title="5.1 surround sound">5.1 surround</a>, and released as hybrid SACDs with a stereo mix on the standard CD layer.</p>
<p>Some popular artists have released new recordings on SACD. Sales figures for <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sacred_Love" title="Sacred Love">Sacred Love</a></i> (2003) album reached number one on SACD sales charts in four European countries in June 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SACDGold_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SACDGold-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 2007 and 2008, <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> re-released all of their studio albums across three box sets. Each album in these sets contains the original album on SACD in both stereo and 5.1 mixes. The US &amp; Canada versions do not use SACD but CD instead.</p>
<p>By August 2009 443 labels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> had released one or more SACDs. Instead of depending on major label support, some orchestras and artists have released SACDs on their own. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Chicago Symphony Orchestra">Chicago Symphony Orchestra</a> started the Chicago Resound label to provide full support for high-resolution SACD hybrid discs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> established their own 'LSO Live' label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the SACD discs that were released from 2000-2005 are now out of print and are available only on the used market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-availability_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-availability-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-availability_2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-availability_2-15">[15]</a></sup> By 2009, the major record companies were no longer regularly releasing discs in the format, with new releases confined to the smaller labels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-availability_3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-availability_3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>SACD is a disc of identical physical dimensions as a standard compact disc; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memory_storage_density" title="Memory storage density">density</a> of the disc is the same as a <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>. There are three types of disc:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech2001_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech2001-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Hybrid</b>: Hybrid SACDs are encoded with a 4.7 GB DSD layer (also known as the <i>HD layer</i>), as well as a PCM (Red Book) audio layer readable by most conventional Compact Disc players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Single-layer</b>: A DVD-5 encoded with one 4.7 GB DSD layer.</li>
<li><b>Dual-layer</b>: A DVD-9 encoded with two DSD layers, totaling 8.5 GB, and no PCM layer. Dual-layer SACDs can store nearly twice as much data as a single-layer SACD.</li>
</ul>
<p>Unlike hybrid discs, both single- and dual-layer SACD's are incompatible with conventional CD players and cannot be played on them.</p>
<p>A stereo SACD recording has an uncompressed rate of 5.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbit/s" title="Mbit/s">Mbit/s</a>, four times the rate for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_CD_standard)" title="Red Book (audio CD standard)">Red Book</a> CD stereo audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech2001_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech2001-17">[17]</a></sup> Other technical parameters are as follows:</p>

<p>Commercial releases commonly included both surround sound (five full-range plus <a href="/wiki/Low-frequency_effects" title="Low-frequency effects">LFE</a> multi-channel) and stereo (dual-channel) mixes on the SACD layer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some reissues however, retained the mixes of earlier multi-channel formats (examples include the 1973 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadraphonic" title="Quadraphonic">quadraphonic</a> mix of <a href="/wiki/Mike_Oldfield" title="Mike Oldfield">Mike Oldfield</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tubular_Bells" title="Tubular Bells">Tubular Bells</a></i> and the 1957 three-channel stereo recording by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Chicago Symphony Orchestra">Chicago Symphony Orchestra</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mussorgsky" title="Mussorgsky">Mussorgsky</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pictures_at_an_Exhibition" title="Pictures at an Exhibition">Pictures at an Exhibition</a></i>, reissued on SACD in 2001 and 2004 respectively).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disc_reading">Disc reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disc reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:251px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sacd.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="249" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sacd.svg/249px-Sacd.svg.png" width="249"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">A Super Audio CD uses two layers and the standardized <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a> of conventional CD players to enable both types of player to read the data.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Objective lenses in conventional CD players have a longer working distance, or <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a>, than lenses designed for SACD players. This means that when a hybrid SACD is placed into a conventional CD player, the laser beam passes the high-resolution layer and is reflected by the conventional layer at the standard 1.2 mm distance, and the high-density layer is out of focus. When the disc is placed into an SACD player, the laser is reflected by the high-resolution layer (at 0.6 mm distance) before it can reach the conventional layer. Conversely, if a conventional CD is placed into an SACD player, the laser will read the disc as a CD since there is no high-resolution layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech2001_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech2001-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muszeroldal_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-muszeroldal-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DSD">DSD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: DSD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">Direct Stream Digital</a></div>
<p>SACD audio is stored in a format called <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">Direct Stream Digital</a> (DSD), which differs from the conventional <a href="/wiki/Pulse-code_modulation" title="Pulse-code modulation">Pulse-code modulation</a> (PCM) used by the <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> or conventional computer audio systems.</p>
<p>DSD is 1-<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a>, has a sampling rate of 2.8224 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHz" title="MHz">MHz</a>, and makes use of <a href="/wiki/Noise_shaping" title="Noise shaping">noise shaping</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantization_(sound_processing)" title="Quantization (sound processing)">quantization</a> techniques in order to push 1-bit quantization noise up to inaudible ultrasonic frequencies. This gives the format a greater <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> and wider frequency response than the CD. The SACD format is capable of delivering a dynamic range of 120 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a> from 20 Hz to 20 kHz and an extended frequency response up to 100 kHz, although most currently available players list an upper limit of 70–90 kHz,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and practical limits reduce this to 50 kHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremetech2001_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech2001-17">[17]</a></sup> Because of the nature of <a href="/wiki/Delta-sigma_modulation" title="Delta-sigma modulation">sigma-delta converters</a>, one cannot make a direct technical comparison between DSD and PCM. DSD's frequency response can be as high as 100 kHz, but frequencies that high compete with high levels of ultrasonic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantization_error" title="Quantization error">quantization noise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ambisonic2001_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ambisonic2001-23">[23]</a></sup> With appropriate <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">low-pass filtering</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Frequency_response" title="Frequency response">frequency response</a> of 20 kHz can be achieved along with a dynamic range of nearly 120 dB, which is about the same dynamic range as PCM audio with a resolution of 20 bits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DST">DST</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: DST">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To reduce the space and bandwidth requirements of <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">Direct Stream Digital</a> (DSD), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">lossless data compression</a> method called Direct Stream Transfer (DST) is used. DST compression is compulsory for multi-channel regions and optional for stereo regions. This typically compresses by a factor of between two and three, allowing a disc to contain 80 minutes of both 2-channel and 5.1-channel sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Direct Stream Transfer compression was also standardized as an amendment to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPEG-4_Audio" title="MPEG-4 Audio">MPEG-4 Audio</a> standard (<a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> 14496-3:2001/Amd 6:2005 – Lossless coding of oversampled audio) in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-iso_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-iso-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-iso2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-iso2-26">[26]</a></sup> It contains the DSD and DST definitions as described in the Super Audio CD Specification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-iso-ipsj_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-iso-ipsj-27">[27]</a></sup> The MPEG-4 DST provides lossless coding of oversampled audio signals. Target applications of DST is archiving and storage of 1-bit oversampled audio signals and SA-CD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-chiariglione_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-chiariglione-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-chiariglione-old_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-chiariglione-old-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpeg4audio-version4-2009_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpeg4audio-version4-2009-30">[30]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_implementation_(computing)" title="Reference implementation (computing)">reference implementation</a> of MPEG-4 DST was published as ISO/IEC 14496-5:2001/Amd.10:2007 in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-reference-implementation_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-reference-implementation-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dst-reference-implementation-iso_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-dst-reference-implementation-iso-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copy_protection">Copy protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Copy protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SACD has several <a href="/wiki/Copy_protection" title="Copy protection">copy protection</a> features at the physical level, which made the digital content of SACD discs difficult to copy until the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Jailbreak" title="PlayStation Jailbreak">jailbreak</a> of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>. The content may be copyable without SACD quality by resorting to the <a href="/wiki/Analog_hole" title="Analog hole">analog hole</a>, or ripping the conventional 700 MB layer on hybrid discs. Copy protection schemes include physical pit modulation and 80-bit <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> of the audio data, with a key encoded on a special area of the disc that is only readable by a licensed SACD device. The HD layer of an SACD disc cannot be played back on computer CD/DVD drives, and SACDs can only be manufactured at the disc replication facilities in <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka_Prefecture" title="Shizuoka Prefecture">Shizuoka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> However, PlayStation 3 with a SACD drive and appropriate firmware can use specialized software to extract a <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">DSD</a> copy of the HD stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_CD">Comparison with CD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparison with CD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the audiophile community, the sound from the SACD format is thought to be significantly better than that of CD. For example, one supplier claims that "The DSD process used for producing SACDs captures more of the nuances from a performance and reproduces them with a clarity and transparency not possible with CD."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Audio_Engineering_Society" title="Audio Engineering Society">Audio Engineering Society</a> published the results of a year-long trial, in which a range of subjects including professional recording engineers were asked to discern the difference between SACD and a <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> audio (44.1 kHz/16 bit) conversion of the same source material under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blind_experiment#Double-blind_trials" title="Blind experiment">double blind</a> test conditions. Out of 554 trials, there were 276 correct answers, a 49.8% success rate corresponding almost exactly to the 50% that would have been expected by <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">chance guessing</a> alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> When the level of the signal was elevated by 14 dB or more, the test subjects were able to detect the higher <a href="/wiki/Noise_floor" title="Noise floor">noise floor</a> of the CD quality loop easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AES_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AES-1">[1]</a></sup> The authors commented:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Now, it is very difficult to use negative results to prove the inaudibility of any given phenomenon or process. There is always the remote possibility that a different system or more finely attuned pair of ears would reveal a difference. But we have gathered enough data, using sufficiently varied and capable systems and listeners, to state that the burden of proof has now shifted. Further claims that careful 16/44.1 encoding audibly degrades high resolution signals must be supported by properly controlled double-blind tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AES_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AES-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Following criticism that the original published results of the study were not sufficiently detailed, the AES published a list of the audio equipment and recordings used during the tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mixonline_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-mixonline-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_DVD-A">Comparison with DVD-A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comparison with DVD-A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/ABX_test" title="ABX test">Double-blind listening tests</a> in 2004 between DSD and 24-bit, 176.4 kHz PCM recordings reported that among test subjects no significant differences could be heard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blech_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blech-43">[43]</a></sup> DSD advocates and equipment manufacturers continue to assert an improvement in sound quality above PCM 24-bit 176.4 kHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Despite both formats' extended frequency responses, it has been shown people cannot distinguish audio with information above 21 kHz from audio without such high-frequency content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHK_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHK-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playback_hardware">Playback hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Audio_CD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Playback hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sony_SCD-1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sony SCD-1 (page does not exist)">Sony SCD-1</a> player was introduced concurrently with the SACD format in 1999, at a price of approximately US$5,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It weighed over 26 kilograms (57 lb) and played two-channel SACDs and <a href="/wiki/Compact_Disc_Digital_Audio" title="Compact Disc Digital Audio">Red Book</a> CDs only. Electronics manufacturers, including <a href="/wiki/Onkyo" title="Onkyo">Onkyo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onkyo_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Onkyo-47">[47]</a></sup> Denon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denon_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Denon-48">[48]</a></sup> Marantz,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marantz_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marantz-49">[49]</a></sup> Pioneer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pioneer_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pioneer-50">[50]</a></sup> and Yamaha<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamaha_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamaha-51">[51]</a></sup> offer or offered SACD players. <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> has made in-car Super Audio CD players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>SACD players are not permitted to offer an output carrying an unencrypted stream of <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">Direct Stream Digital</a> (DSD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first two generations of Sony's <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> game console were capable of reading SACD discs. Starting with the third generation (introduced October 2007), SACD playback was removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> All PS3 models however will play <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital#DSD_disc_format" title="Direct Stream Digital">DSD Disc format</a>. PS3 was capable of converting multi-channel DSD to <a href="/wiki/Lossy_compression" title="Lossy compression">lossy</a> 1.5 Mbit/s <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS</a> for playback over <a href="/wiki/S/PDIF" title="S/PDIF">S/PDIF</a> using the 2.00 <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3_system_software" title="PlayStation 3 system software">system software</a>. The subsequent revision removed the feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several brands have introduced (mostly <a href="/wiki/High-end_audio" title="High-end audio">high-end</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> players that can play SACD discs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unofficial playback of SACD <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disc_image" title="Disc image">disc images</a> on a PC is possible through <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_player_(software)" title="Audio player (software)">audio player</a> <a href="/wiki/Foobar2000" title="Foobar2000">foobar2000</a> for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> using an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> plug-in extension called SACDDecoder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">Mac OS X</a> music software <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Audirvana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Audirvana (page does not exist)">Audirvana</a> also supports playback of SACD disc images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
