<p><a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a> first appeared in <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programming</a> in the late 1930s, during what is called the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Science_Fiction" title="Golden Age of Science Fiction">Golden Age of Science Fiction</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">Special effects</a> and other production techniques allow creators to present a living visual image of an imaginary world not limited by the constraints of reality.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fiction_television_production_process_and_methods">Science fiction television production process and methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science fiction television production process and methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The need to portray imaginary settings or characters with properties and abilities beyond the reach of current reality obliges producers to make extensive use of specialized techniques of television production.</p>
<p>Through most of the 20th century, many of these techniques were expensive and involved a small number of dedicated craft practitioners, while the reusability of props, models, effects, or animation techniques made it easier to keep using them. The combination of high initial cost and lower maintenance cost pushed producers into building these techniques into the basic concept of a series, influencing all the artistic choices.</p>
<p>By the late 1990s, improved technology and more training and cross-training within the industry made all of these techniques easier to use, so that directors of individual episodes could make decisions to use one or more methods, so such artistic choices no longer needed to be baked into the series concept.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">Special effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Special effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">Special effects</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:303px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magicam-patent.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="561" data-file-width="800" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Magicam-patent.png/301px-Magicam-patent.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Magicam-patent.png/452px-Magicam-patent.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Magicam-patent.png/602px-Magicam-patent.png 2x" width="301"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magicam-patent.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
For the series <a href="/wiki/The_Starlost" title="The Starlost">The Starlost</a>, the Magicam, a servo controlled dolly along with a secondary periscope camera filming a model background, was designed by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Trumbull" title="Douglas Trumbull">Douglas Trumbull</a>, however, the system did not work reliably and <a href="/wiki/Chroma_key" title="Chroma key">blue screen</a> effects were used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sf_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sf-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Special effects (or "SPFX") have been an essential tool throughout the history of science fiction on television: small explosives to simulate the effects of various <a href="/wiki/Raygun" title="Raygun">rayguns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squib_(explosive)" title="Squib (explosive)">squibs</a> of blood and gruesome prosthetics to simulate the monsters and victims in horror series, and the <a href="/wiki/Wire-flying" title="Wire-flying">wire-flying</a> entrances and exits of <a href="/wiki/George_Reeves" title="George Reeves">George Reeves</a> as <a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Superman_(TV_series)" title="Adventures of Superman (TV series)">Superman</a>.</p>
<p>The broad term "special effects" includes all the techniques here, but more commonly there are two categories of effects. <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">Visual effects</a> ("VFX") involve photographic or digital manipulation of the onscreen image, usually done in <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a>. Mechanical or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_effects" title="Physical effects">physical effects</a> involve props, pyrotechnics, and other physical methods used during <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">principal photography</a> itself. Some effects involved a combination of techniques; a ray gun might require a pyrotechnic during filming, and then an optical glowing line added to the film image in post-production. <a href="/wiki/Stunt" title="Stunt">Stunts</a> are another important category of physical effects. In general, all kinds of special effects must be carefully planned during <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">pre-production</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer-generated_imagery">Computer-generated imagery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Computer-generated imagery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">Computer-generated imagery</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Babylon_5" title="Babylon 5">Babylon 5</a></i> was the first series to use <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a>, or "CGI", for all exterior space scenes, even those with characters in space suits. The technology has made this more practical, so that today models are rarely used. In the 1990s, CGI required expensive processors and customized applications, but by the 2000s (decade), computing power has pushed capabilities down to personal laptops running a wide array of software.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Models_and_puppets">Models and puppets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Models and puppets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">Scale model</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">Puppet</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">Models</a> have been an essential tool in science fiction television since the beginning, when <a href="/wiki/Buck_Rogers" title="Buck Rogers">Buck Rogers</a> took flight in spark-scattering spaceships wheeling across a matte backdrop sky. The original <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i> required a staggering array of models; the <a href="/wiki/Starship_Enterprise" title="Starship Enterprise">USS <i>Enterprise</i></a> had to be built in several different scales for different needs. Models fell out of use in filming in the 1990s as CGI became more affordable and practical, but even today, designers sometimes construct scale models which are then digitized for use in animation software.</p>
<p>Models of characters are <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppets</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Anderson" title="Gerry Anderson">Gerry Anderson</a> created a series of shows using puppets living in a universe of models and miniature sets, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Thunderbirds_(TV_series)" title="Thunderbirds (TV series)">Thunderbirds</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/ALF_(TV_series)" title="ALF (TV series)">ALF</a></i> depicted an alien living in a family, while <i><a href="/wiki/Farscape" title="Farscape">Farscape</a></i> included two puppets as regular characters. In <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_SG-1" title="Stargate SG-1">Stargate SG-1</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Asgard_(Stargate)" title="Asgard (Stargate)">Asgard</a> characters are puppets in scenes where they are sitting, standing, or lying down.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animation">Animation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Animation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">Animation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">Anime</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kuha2053108.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Kuha2053108.JPG/220px-Kuha2053108.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Kuha2053108.JPG/330px-Kuha2053108.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Kuha2053108.JPG/440px-Kuha2053108.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>As animation is completely free of the constraints of gravity, momentum, and physical reality, it is an ideal technique for science fiction and fantasy on television. In a sense, virtually all animated series allow characters and objects to perform in unrealistic ways, so they are almost all considered to fit within the broadest category of speculative fiction (in the context of awards, criticism, marketing, etc.) The artistic affinity of animation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_books" title="Comic books">comic books</a> has led to a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a>-themed animation, much of this adapted from comics series, while the impossible characters and settings allowed in animation made this a preferred medium for both <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> and for series aimed at young audiences.</p>
<p>Originally, animation was all hand-drawn by artists, though in the 1980s, beginning with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Power" title="Captain Power">Captain Power</a></i>, computers began to automate the task of creating repeated images; by the 1990s, hand-drawn animation became defunct.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Animation_in_live-action">Animation in live-action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Animation in live-action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In recent years as technology has improved, this has become more common, notably since the development of the Massive software application permits producers to include hordes of non-human characters to storm a city or space station. The robotic <a href="/wiki/Cylon_(reimagining)" title="Cylon (reimagining)">Cylons</a> in the new version of <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">Battlestar Galactica</a></i> are usually animated characters, while the <a href="/wiki/Asgard_(Stargate)" title="Asgard (Stargate)">Asgard</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_SG-1" title="Stargate SG-1">Stargate SG-1</a></i> are animated when they are shown walking around or more than one is on screen at once.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fiction_television_economics_and_distribution">Science fiction television economics and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Science fiction television economics and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, science fiction series are subject to the same financial constraints as other television shows. However, high production costs increase the financial risk, while limited audiences further complicate the business case for continuing production. <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i> was the first television series to cost more than $100,000 per episode, while <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i> was the first to cost more than $1 million per episode.</p>
<p>The innovative nature of science fiction means that new shows cannot rely on predictable market-tested formulas like <a href="/wiki/Legal_drama" title="Legal drama">legal dramas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcoms</a>; the involvement of creative talent outside the Hollywood mainstream introduces more variables to the budget forecasts.</p>
<p>In the past, science fiction television shows have maintained a family friendly format that rendered them suitable for all ages, especially children, as the majority of them were of the action-adventure format. This enabled merchandising such as toy lines, animated cartoon adaptations, and other licensing. However, many modern shows include a significant amount of adult themes (such as sexual situations, nudity, profanity and graphic violence) rendering them unsuitable for young audiences, and severely limiting the remaining audience demographic and the potential for merchandising.</p>
<p>The perception, more than the reality, of science fiction series being cancelled unreasonably is greatly increased by the attachment of fans to their favorite series, which is much stronger in <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">science fiction fandom</a> than it is in the general population. While mainstream shows are often more strictly episodic, where ending shows can allow viewers to imagine that characters live happily, or at least normally, ever after, science fiction series generate questions and loose ends that, when unresolved, cause dissatisfaction among devoted viewers. Creative settings also often call for broader story arcs than is often found in mainstream television, requiring science fiction series many episodes to resolve an ongoing major conflict. Science fiction television producers will sometimes end a season with a dramatic <a href="/wiki/Cliffhanger" title="Cliffhanger">cliffhanger</a> episode to attract viewer interest, but the short-term effect rarely influences financial partners. <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Angel_(TV_series)" title="Dark Angel (TV series)">Dark Angel</a></i> is one of many shows ending with a cliffhanger scene that left critical questions open when the series was cancelled.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_fandom">Media fandom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Media fandom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_fandom" title="Media fandom">Media fandom</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">science fiction fandom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trekkie" title="Trekkie">Trekkie</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg/220px-Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg/330px-Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg/440px-Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Star_Trek_Federation_Officers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fans at a science fiction convention dressed as characters from <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the earliest forms of media fandom was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Trek_fandom" title="Star Trek fandom">Star Trek fandom</a>. Fans of the series became known to each other through the <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">science fiction fandom</a>. In 1968, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> decided to cancel <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Bjo_Trimble" title="Bjo Trimble">Bjo Trimble</a> wrote letters to contacts in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Fantasy_Fan_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Fantasy Fan Foundation (page does not exist)">National Fantasy Fan Foundation</a>, asking people to organize their local friends to write to the network to demand the show remain on the air. Network executives were overwhelmed by an unprecedented wave of correspondence, and they kept the show on the air. Although the series continued to receive low ratings and was canceled a year later, the enduring popularity of the series resulted in <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount</a> creating a set of movies, and then a new series <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i>, which by the early 1990s had become one of the most popular dramas on American television.</p>
<p>Although somewhat smaller, <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i> fandom considerably predates <i>Star Trek</i> fandom. Meanwhile, <i>Star Trek</i> fans continued to grow in numbers, and began organizing conventions in the 1970s. No other show attracted a large organized following until the 1990s, when <i><a href="/wiki/Babylon_5" title="Babylon 5">Babylon 5</a></i> attracted both <i>Star Trek</i> fans and a large number of literary SF fans who previously had not been involved in media fandom. Other series began to attract a growing number of followers.</p>
<p>In the late 1990s, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_(TV_series)" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer (TV series)">Buffy the Vampire Slayer</a></i> drew a large mainstream audience into fandom; greater demand allowed (even obliged, for the sake of time management) <i>Buffy</i> actors to charge much higher appearance fees than the <i>Star Trek</i> actors had. This pushed appearances out of the reach of some volunteer non-profit fan groups towards commercial event promoters. At the same time, a market for celebrity autographs emerged on <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, which created a new source of income for actors, who began to charge money for autographs that they had previously been doing for free. This became significant enough that lesser-known actors would come to conventions without requesting any appearance fee, simply to be allowed to sell their own autographs (commonly on publicity photos). Today most events with actor appearances are organized by commercial promoters, though a number of fan-run conventions still exist, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toronto_Trek" title="Toronto Trek">Toronto Trek</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shore_Leave_(convention)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shore Leave (convention) (page does not exist)">Shore Leave</a>.</p>
<p>The 1985 series <i><a href="/wiki/Robotech" title="Robotech">Robotech</a></i> is most often credited as the catalyst for the Western interest in anime. The series inspired a few fanzines such as <i>Protoculture Addicts</i> and <i>Animag</i> both of which in turn promoted interest in the wide world of anime in general. Anime's first notable appearance at SF or comic book conventions was in the form of video showings of popular anime, untranslated and often low quality VHS bootlegs. Starting in the 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> fans began organizing conventions. These quickly grew to sizes much larger than other science fiction and media conventions in the same communities; many cities now have anime conventions attracting five to ten thousand attendees. Many anime conventions are a hybrid between non-profit and commercial events, with volunteer organizers handling large revenue streams and dealing with commercial suppliers and professional marketing campaigns.</p>
<p>For decades, the majority of science fiction media fandom has been represented by males of all ages and for most of its modern existence, a fairly diverse racial demographic. The most highly publicized demographic for science fiction fans is the male adolescent; roughly the same demographic for American <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>. Female fans, while always present, were far fewer in number and less conspicuously present in fandom. With the rising popularity of fanzines, female fans became increasingly vocal. Starting in the 2000s (decade), genre series began to offer more prominent female characters. Many series featured women as the main characters with males as supporting characters. <i><a href="/wiki/True_Blood" title="True Blood">True Blood</a></i> is an example. Also, such shows premises moved away from heroic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_(fiction)" title="Action (fiction)">action-adventure</a> and focused more on characters and their relationships. This has caused the rising popularity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fanfiction" title="Fanfiction">fanfiction</a>, a large majority of which is categorized as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_fanfiction" title="Slash fanfiction">slash fanfiction</a>. Female fans comprise the majority of fanfiction writers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fiction_television_history_and_culture">Science fiction television history and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Science fiction television history and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._television_science_fiction">U.S. television science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: U.S. television science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U.S._television_science_fiction" title="U.S. television science fiction">U.S. television science fiction</a></div>
<p>U.S. television science fiction has produced <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i> and its various spin-off shows of the <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek franchise</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">The Twilight Zone</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a>,</i> and many others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_television_science_fiction">British television science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: British television science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_television_science_fiction" title="British television science fiction">British television science fiction</a></div>
<p><b>British television science fiction</b> began in 1938 when the broadcast medium was in its infancy with the transmission of a partial adaptation of Karel Čapek's <a href="/wiki/R.U.R." title="R.U.R.">R.U.R.</a>. Despite an occasionally chequered history, popular programmes in the genre have been produced by both the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and the largest commercial channel, <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i> is listed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> as the longest-running <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> television show in the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and as the "most successful" science fiction series of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_science_fiction_television">Canadian science fiction television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canadian science fiction television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_science_fiction_television" title="Canadian science fiction television">Canadian science fiction television</a></div>
<p>Science fiction in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">CBC</a> as early as the 1950s. In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">CTV</a> produced <i><a href="/wiki/The_Starlost" title="The Starlost">The Starlost</a></i>. In the 1980s, Canadian animation studios including <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">Nelvana</a>, began producing a growing proportion of the world market in animation.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, Canada became an important player in live action speculative fiction on television, with dozens of series like <i><a href="/wiki/Forever_Knight" title="Forever Knight">Forever Knight</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robocop:_The_Series" title="Robocop: The Series">Robocop</a></i>, and most notably <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_SG-1" title="Stargate SG-1">Stargate SG-1</a></i>. Many series have been produced for youth and children's markets, including <i><a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Black" title="Deepwater Black">Deepwater Black</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/MythQuest" title="MythQuest">MythQuest</a></i>.</p>
<p>In the first decade of the 21st century, changes in provincial tax legislation prompted many production companies to move from Toronto to <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>. Recent popular series produced in Vancouver include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dead_Zone_(TV_series)" title="The Dead Zone (TV series)">The Dead Zone</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Smallville" title="Smallville">Smallville</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(TV_series)" title="Andromeda (TV series)">Andromeda</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_Atlantis" title="Stargate Atlantis">Stargate Atlantis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_Universe" title="Stargate Universe">Stargate Universe</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_4400" title="The 4400">The 4400</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_(TV_series)" title="Sanctuary (TV series)">Sanctuary</a></i> and the reimagined <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">Battlestar Galactica</a></i>.</p>
<p>Because of the small size of the domestic television market, most Canadian productions involve partnerships with production studios based in the United States and Europe. However, in recent years, new partnership arrangements are allowing Canadian investors a growing share of control of projects produced in Canada and elsewhere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_science_fiction_television">Australian science fiction television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Australian science fiction television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Australian_science_fiction_television" title="Australian science fiction television">Australian science fiction television</a></div>
<p>Australia's best known Science Fiction series was <i><a href="/wiki/Farscape" title="Farscape">Farscape</a></i>; made with American co-production, it ran from 1999 to 2003. Early series made in the 1960s included <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Interparis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Interparis (page does not exist)">The Interparis</a></i> (1968) <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vega_4_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vega 4 (TV series) (page does not exist)">Vega 4</a></i> (1967), and <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Five" title="Phoenix Five">Phoenix Five</a></i> (1970). A significant proportion of Australian produced Science Fiction programmes are made for the teens/young Adults market, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girl_from_Tomorrow" title="The Girl from Tomorrow">The Girl from Tomorrow</a></i>, the long-running <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Squiggle" title="Mr. Squiggle">Mr. Squiggle</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Halfway_Across_the_Galaxy_and_Turn_Left" title="Halfway Across the Galaxy and Turn Left">Halfway Across the Galaxy and Turn Left</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ocean_Girl" title="Ocean Girl">Ocean Girl</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crash_Zone" title="Crash Zone">Crash Zone</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Watch_This_Space" title="Watch This Space">Watch This Space</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spellbinder_(TV_series)" title="Spellbinder (TV series)">Spellbinder</a></i>.</p>
<p>Other series like <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Trax" title="Time Trax">Time Trax</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Roar_(TV_series)" title="Roar (TV series)">Roar</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Space:_Above_and_Beyond" title="Space: Above and Beyond">Space: Above and Beyond</a></i> were filmed in Australia, but used mostly US crew and actors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_television_science_fiction">Japanese television science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Japanese television science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_anime" title="History of anime">History of anime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokusatsu" title="Tokusatsu">Tokusatsu</a></div>
<p>Japan has a long history of producing science fiction series for television. Some of the most famous are <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Osamu_Tezuka" title="Osamu Tezuka">Osamu Tezuka</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Robot" title="Super Robot">Super Robots</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mitsuteru_Yokoyama" title="Mitsuteru Yokoyama">Mitsuteru Yokoyama</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tetsujin_28-go" title="Tetsujin 28-go">Tetsujin 28-go</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Gigantor" title="Gigantor">Gigantor</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Go_Nagai" title="Go Nagai">Go Nagai</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mazinger_Z" title="Mazinger Z">Mazinger Z</a></i>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Robot" title="Real Robot">Real Robots</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Yoshiyuki_Tomino" title="Yoshiyuki Tomino">Yoshiyuki Tomino</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gundam" title="Gundam">Gundam</a></i> series and <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dji_Kawamori" title="Shōji Kawamori">Shōji Kawamori</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Macross" title="Macross">Macross</a></i> series.</p>
<p>Other primary aspects of Japanese science fiction television are the <a href="/wiki/Superhero_fiction" title="Superhero fiction">superhero</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tokusatsu" title="Tokusatsu">tokusatsu</a></i> (a term literally meaning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">special effects</a>) series, pioneered by programs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Moonlight_Mask" title="Moonlight Mask">Moonlight Mask</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_Prince" title="Planet Prince">Planet Prince</a></i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suitmation" title="Suitmation">suitmation</a> technique has been used in long running franchises include <a href="/wiki/Eiji_Tsuburaya" title="Eiji Tsuburaya">Eiji Tsuburaya</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ultra_Series" title="Ultra Series">Ultra Series</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shotaro_Ishinomori" title="Shotaro Ishinomori">Shotaro Ishinomori</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kamen_Rider_Series" title="Kamen Rider Series">Kamen Rider Series</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Super_Sentai" title="Super Sentai">Super Sentai</a> Series.</p>
<p>In addition, several <a href="/wiki/Japanese_television_drama" title="Japanese television drama">dramas</a> utilize science fiction elements as framing devices, but are not labeled as "tokusatsu" as they do not utilize actors in full body suits and other special effects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_European_science_fiction_series">Continental European science fiction series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Continental European science fiction series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_European_series">Northern European series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Northern European series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Among the notable German language productions is <i><a href="/wiki/Lexx" title="Lexx">Lexx</a></i> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raumpatrouille" title="Raumpatrouille">Raumpatrouille</a></i></span>, a German series first broadcast in 1966. Mmovies by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rainer_Erler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rainer Erler (page does not exist)">Rainer Erler</a>, include the miniseries <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="/wiki/Das_Blaue_Palais" title="Das Blaue Palais">Das Blaue Palais</a></i></span>.<br/>
<i><a href="/wiki/Star_Maidens" title="Star Maidens">Star Maidens</a></i> (1975, aka "Medusa" or "Die Mädchen aus dem Weltraum") was a British-German coproduction of pure SF. Danish television broadcast the children's TV-series <i><a href="/wiki/Crash_(1984_TV_series)" title="Crash (1984 TV series)">Crash</a></i> in 1984 about a boy who finds out that his room is a space ship.</p>
<p>Early Dutch television series were <i><a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morgen_gebeurt_het" title="nl:Morgen gebeurt het">Morgen gebeurt het</a></i> (tomorrow it will happen), broadcast from 1957 to 1959, about a group of Dutch space explorers and their adventures, <i>De duivelsgrot</i> (the devil's cave), broadcast from 1963 to 1964, about a scientist who finds the map of a cave that leads to the center of the earth and <i>Treinreis naar de Toekomst</i> (train journey to the future) about two young children who are taken to the future by robots who try to recreate humanity, but are unable to give the cloned humans a soul. All three of these television series where aimed mostly at children.</p>
<p>Later television series were <i>Professor Vreemdeling</i> (1977) about a strange professor who wants to make plants speak and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeuws_Meisje_(televisie)" title="nl:Zeeuws Meisje (televisie)">Zeeuws Meisje</a></i> (1997) a nationalistic post-apocalyptic series where the Netherlands has been built full of housing and the highways are filled with traffic jams. The protagonist, a female superhero, wears traditional folkloric clothes and tries to save traditional elements of Dutch society against the factory owners.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_series">Italian series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Italian series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Italian TV shows include <i><a href="/wiki/A_come_Andromeda" title="A come Andromeda">A come Andromeda</a></i> (1972) which was a remake of 1962 <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/A_for_Andromeda" title="A for Andromeda">A for Andromeda</a></i> (from the novels of Hoyle and Elliott), <i>Geminus</i> (1968), <i><a href="/wiki/Il_segno_del_comando" title="Il segno del comando">Il segno del comando</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/Gamma_(miniseries)" title="Gamma (miniseries)">Gamma</a></i> (1974) and <i>La traccia verde</i> (1975).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="French_series">French series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: French series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>French series are <i><a href="/wiki/Highlander:_The_Series" title="Highlander: The Series">Highlander: The Series</a></i> <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> science-fiction/fantasy television series (both co-produced with Canada) and a number of smaller fiction/fantasy television series, including <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tang_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tang (TV series) (page does not exist)">Tang</a></i> in 1971, about a secret organization that attempts to control the world with a new super weapon, "Les atomistes" and 1970 miniseries "La brigade des maléfices".</p>
<p>Another French-produced science fiction series was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_age" title="New age">new age</a> animated series <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Il était une fois... l'espace</i></span> (English: Once upon a time...space). <a href="/wiki/Anime-influenced_animation" title="Anime-influenced animation">Anime-influenced animation</a> includes a series of French-Japanese <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>/<a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, including such titles as <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_31" title="Ulysses 31">Ulysses 31</a></i> (1981), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mysterious_Cities_of_Gold" title="The Mysterious Cities of Gold">The Mysterious Cities of Gold</a></i> (1982), and <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cban_Star-Racers" title="Ōban Star-Racers">Ōban Star-Racers</a></i> (2006).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_series">Spanish series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Spanish series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first Spanish SF series was <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Valor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diego Valor (page does not exist)">Diego Valor</a></i>, a 22 episode TV adaption of a radio show hero of the same name based on <a href="/wiki/Dan_Dare" title="Dan Dare">Dan Dare</a>, aired weekly between 1958 and 1959. Nothing was survived of this series, not a single still; it is not known if the show was even recorded or just a live broadcast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 60s were dominated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chicho_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Serrador&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chicho Ibáñez Serrador (page does not exist)">Chicho Ibáñez Serrador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narciso_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Menta" title="Narciso Ibáñez Menta">Narciso Ibáñez Menta</a>, who adapted SF works from Golden Age authors and others to a series titled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ma%C3%B1ana_puede_ser_verdad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mañana puede ser verdad (page does not exist)">Mañana puede ser verdad</a></i>. Only 11 episodes were filmed. The 70s saw three important television films, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Los_pajaritos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Los pajaritos (page does not exist)">Los pajaritos</a></i> (1974), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Gioconda_est%C3%A1_triste&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Gioconda está triste (page does not exist)">La Gioconda está triste</a></i> (1977), and <i><a href="/wiki/La_cabina" title="La cabina">La cabina</a></i> (1972), this last one, about a man who becomes trapped in a telephone booth, while passersby seem unable to help him, won the 1973 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Emmy_Award" title="International Emmy Award">International Emmy Award</a> for Fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jimenez2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jimenez2012-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plut%C3%B3n_B.R.B._Nero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plutón B.R.B. Nero (page does not exist)">Plutón B.R.B. Nero</a></i> (2008) was a brutal SF comedy by <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lex_de_la_Iglesia" title="Álex de la Iglesia">Álex de la Iglesia</a>, in the line of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy">The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Dwarf" title="Red Dwarf">Red Dwarf</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i>, with 26 episodes of 35 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Other series of the 2010s were <i><a href="/wiki/Los_protegidos" title="Los protegidos">Los protegidos</a></i> (2010-2012), <i><a href="/wiki/El_Barco_(TV_series)" title="El Barco (TV series)">El barco</a></i> (2011-2013), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_internado" title="El internado">El internado</a></i> (2007-2010), all three inspired by northamerican productions, with minor SF elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecartelera_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecartelera-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The latest success is <i><a href="/wiki/El_ministerio_del_tiempo" title="El ministerio del tiempo">El ministerio del tiempo</a></i> (<i>The ministry of time</i>), premiered on February 24, 2015 on <a href="/wiki/Televisi%C3%B3n_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Televisión Española">TVE</a>'s main channel <a href="/wiki/La_1_(Spanish_TV_channel)" title="La 1 (Spanish TV channel)">La 1</a>. The series follows the exploits of a patrol of the fictional Ministry of Time, which deals with incidents caused by time travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elperiodico_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-elperiodico-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It has garnered several national prizes in 2015, like the <a href="/wiki/Premios_Ondas" title="Premios Ondas">Ondas Prize</a>, and has a thick following on-line, called <i>los ministéricos</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20minutos_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-20minutos-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_European_series">Eastern European series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_on_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Eastern European series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> produced <i><a href="/wiki/The_Collector_(Serbian_TV_series)" title="The Collector (Serbian TV series)">The Collector</a></i> (<span lang="sr" xml:lang="sr"><i>Sakupljač</i></span>), a science fiction television series based upon <a href="/wiki/Zoran_%C5%BDivkovi%C4%87_(writer)" title="Zoran Živković (writer)">Zoran Živković's</a> story, winner of a <a href="/wiki/World_Fantasy_Award" title="World Fantasy Award">World Fantasy Award</a>. Several science-fiction series were also produced in various European countries, and never translated into English.</p>
