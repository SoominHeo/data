<p><b>Science fiction film</b> (or <b>sci-fi</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Film_genre" title="Film genre">genre</a> that uses <a href="/wiki/Speculative_fiction" title="Speculative fiction">speculative</a>, fictional <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>-based depictions of phenomena that are not fully accepted by mainstream science, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life_in_fiction" title="Extraterrestrial life in fiction">extraterrestrial lifeforms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planets_in_science_fiction" title="Planets in science fiction">alien worlds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">extrasensory perception</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time_travel_in_fiction" title="Time travel in fiction">time travel</a>, along with futuristic elements such as <a href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_spacecraft" title="List of fictional spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cyborgs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">interstellar travel</a> or other technologies. Science fiction films have often been used to focus on <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_issue" title="Social issue">social issues</a>, and to explore philosophical issues like the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>. In many cases, <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">tropes</a> derived from written <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> may be used by filmmakers ignorant of or at best indifferent to the standards of scientific plausibility and plot logic to which written science fiction is traditionally held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genre</a> has existed since the early years of silent cinema, when Georges Melies' <i><a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">A Trip to the Moon</a></i> (1902) employed <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">trick photography</a> effects. The next major example in the genre was the film <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i> (1927) - being the first <a href="/wiki/Feature_length" title="Feature length">feature length</a> science fiction movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> From the 1930s to the 1950s, the genre consisted mainly of low-budget <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">B movies</a>. After <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>'s landmark <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i> (1968), the science fiction film genre was taken more seriously. In the late 1970s, big-budget science fiction films filled with special effects became popular with audiences after the success of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">Star Wars</a></i> and paved the way for the blockbuster hits of subsequent decades.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_the_genre">Characteristics of the genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics of the genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Sobchack" title="Vivian Sobchack">Vivian Sobchack</a>, an American cinema and media theorist and cultural critic:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Science fiction film is <i>a film genre which emphasizes actual, extrapolative, or speculative <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical_methods" title="Empirical methods">empirical method</a>, interacting in a social context with the lesser emphasized, but still present, <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">transcendentalism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, in an attempt to reconcile man with the unknown</i> (Sobchack 63).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This definition suggests a continuum between (real-world) <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a> and (<a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>) <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">transcendentalism</a>, with science fiction film on the side of empiricism, and <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy film</a> on the side of transcendentalism. However, there are numerous well-known examples of science fiction horror films, epitomized by such pictures as <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_(1931_film)" title="Frankenstein (1931 film)">Frankenstein</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(film)" title="Alien (film)">Alien</a></i>.</p>
<p>The visual style of science fiction film can be characterized by a clash between alien and familiar images. This clash is implemented when alien images become familiar, as in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clockwork_Orange_(film)" title="A Clockwork Orange (film)">A Clockwork Orange</a></i>, when the repetitions of the Korova Milkbar make the alien decor seem more familiar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As well, familiar images become alien, as in the films <i><a href="/wiki/Repo_Man_(film)" title="Repo Man (film)">Repo Man</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Sky" title="Liquid Sky">Liquid Sky</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, in <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Strangelove" title="Dr. Strangelove">Dr. Strangelove</a></i>, the ,distortion of the humans make the familiar images seem more alien.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Finally, alien and familiar images are juxtaposed, as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deadly_Mantis" title="The Deadly Mantis">The Deadly Mantis</a></i>, when a giant <a href="/wiki/Mantis" title="Mantis">praying mantis</a> is shown climbing the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a>.</p>
<p>Cultural theorist <a href="/wiki/Scott_Bukatman" title="Scott Bukatman">Scott Bukatman</a> has proposed that science fiction film allows contemporary culture to witness an expression of the <a href="/wiki/Sublime_(philosophy)" title="Sublime (philosophy)">sublime</a>, be it through exaggerated scale, apocalypse or transcendence.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_before_1920" title="List of science fiction films before 1920">List of science fiction films before 1920</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1920s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1920s">List of science fiction films of the 1920s</a></div>
<p>Science fiction films appeared early in the <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent film</a> era, typically as short films shot in black and white, sometimes with colour tinting. They usually had a technological theme and were often intended to be humorous. In <a href="/wiki/1902_in_film" title="1902 in film">1902</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">Georges Méliès</a> released <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Voyage_dans_la_Lune" title="Le Voyage dans la Lune">Le Voyage dans la Lune</a></i>, generally considered the first science fiction film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and a film that used early trick photography to depict a spacecraft's journey to the moon. Several early films merged the science fiction and <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror</a> genres. Examples of this are <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_(1910_film)" title="Frankenstein (1910 film)">Frankenstein</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1910_in_film" title="1910 in film">1910</a>), a film adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">Mary Shelley</a>'s novel, and <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Jekyll_and_Mr._Hyde_(1920_film)" title="Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1920 film)">Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde</a></i> (1920), based on the psychological tale by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>. Taking a more adventurous tack, <i><a href="/wiki/20,000_Leagues_Under_the_Sea_(1916_film)" title="20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1916 film)">20,000 Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1916_in_film" title="1916 in film">1916</a>) is a film based on <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>’s famous novel of a wondrous submarine and its vengeful captain. In the 1920s, European filmmakers tended to use science fiction for prediction and social commentary, as can be seen in German films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1927_in_film" title="1927 in film">1927</a>) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frau_im_Mond" title="Frau im Mond">Frau im Mond</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1929_in_film" title="1929 in film">1929</a>). Other notable science fiction films of the silent era include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Impossible_Voyage" title="The Impossible Voyage">The Impossible Voyage</a></i> (1904), <i>The Motorist</i> (1906), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquest_of_the_Pole" title="Conquest of the Pole">Conquest of the Pole</a></i> (1912), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himmelskibet_(film)" title="Himmelskibet (film)">Himmelskibet</a></i> (1918; which with its runtime of 97 minutes generally is considered the first feature-length science fiction film in history),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cabinet_of_Dr._Caligari" title="The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari">The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari</a></i> (1920), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mechanical_Man" title="The Mechanical Man">The Mechanical Man</a></i> (1921), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Qui_Dort" title="Paris Qui Dort">Paris Qui Dort</a></i> (1923), <i><a href="/wiki/Aelita" title="Aelita">Aelita</a></i> (1924), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luch_Smerti" title="Luch Smerti">Luch Smerti</a></i> (1925) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(1925_film)" title="The Lost World (1925 film)">The Lost World</a></i> (1925).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s.E2.80.931950s">1930s–1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1930s–1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1930s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1930s">List of science fiction films of the 1930s</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1940s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1940s">List of science fiction films of the 1940s</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1950s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1950s">List of science fiction films of the 1950s</a></div>
<p>In the 1930s, there were several big budget science fiction films, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Just_Imagine" title="Just Imagine">Just Imagine</a></i> (1930), <i><a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(1933_film)" title="King Kong (1933 film)">King Kong</a></i> (1933), <i><a href="/wiki/Things_to_Come" title="Things to Come">Things to Come</a></i> (1936) and <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Horizon_(1937_film)" title="Lost Horizon (1937 film)">Lost Horizon</a></i> (1937). Starting in 1936, a number of science fiction <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strips</a> were adapted as <a href="/wiki/Serial_film" title="Serial film">serials</a>, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Flash_Gordon" title="Flash Gordon">Flash Gordon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Buck_Rogers" title="Buck Rogers">Buck Rogers</a></i>, both starring <a href="/wiki/Buster_Crabbe" title="Buster Crabbe">Buster Crabbe</a>. These serials, and the comic strips they were based on, were very popular with the general public. Other notable science fiction films of the 1930s include <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_(1931_film)" title="Frankenstein (1931 film)">Frankenstein</a></i> (1931), <i><a href="/wiki/Bride_of_Frankenstein" title="Bride of Frankenstein">Bride of Frankenstein</a></i> (1935), <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_X_(film)" title="Doctor X (film)">Doctor X</a></i> (1932), <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Jekyll_and_Mr._Hyde_(1931_film)" title="Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1931 film)">Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde</a></i> (1931), <i><a href="/wiki/F.P.1" title="F.P.1">F.P.1</a></i> (1932), <i><a href="/wiki/Island_of_Lost_Souls_(1932_film)" title="Island of Lost Souls (1932 film)">Island of Lost Souls</a></i> (1932), <i><a href="/wiki/Deluge_(film)" title="Deluge (film)">Deluge</a></i> (1933), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Invisible_Man_(film)" title="The Invisible Man (film)">The Invisible Man</a></i> (1933), <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_World_(1934_film)" title="Master of the World (1934 film)">Master of the World</a></i> (1934), <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Love_(1935_film)" title="Mad Love (1935 film)">Mad Love</a></i> (1935), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tunnel_(1935_film)" title="The Tunnel (1935 film)">Trans-Atlantic Tunnel</a></i> (1935), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil-Doll" title="The Devil-Doll">The Devil-Doll</a></i> (1936), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Invisible_Ray_(1936_film)" title="The Invisible Ray (1936 film)">The Invisible Ray</a></i> (1936), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Changed_His_Mind" title="The Man Who Changed His Mind">The Man Who Changed His Mind</a></i> (1936), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Walking_Dead_(1936_film)" title="The Walking Dead (1936 film)">The Walking Dead</a></i> (1936), <i><a href="/wiki/Non-Stop_New_York" title="Non-Stop New York">Non-Stop New York</a></i> (1937) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_Doctor_X" title="The Return of Doctor X">The Return of Doctor X</a></i> (1939). The 1940s brought us <i><a href="/wiki/Before_I_Hang" title="Before I Hang">Before I Hang</a></i> (1940), <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(1940_film)" title="Black Friday (1940 film)">Black Friday</a></i> (1940), <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Cyclops" title="Dr. Cyclops">Dr. Cyclops</a></i> (1940), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil_Commands" title="The Devil Commands">The Devil Commands</a></i> (1941), <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Jekyll_and_Mr._Hyde_(1941_film)" title="Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1941 film)">Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde</a></i> (1941), <i><a href="/wiki/Man_Made_Monster" title="Man Made Monster">Man Made Monster</a></i> (1941), <i><a href="/wiki/It_Happened_Tomorrow" title="It Happened Tomorrow">It Happened Tomorrow</a></i> (1944), <i><a href="/wiki/It_Happens_Every_Spring" title="It Happens Every Spring">It Happens Every Spring</a></i> (1949) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Woman" title="The Perfect Woman">The Perfect Woman</a></i> (1949). The release of <i><a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">Destination Moon</a></i> (1950) and <i><a href="/wiki/Rocketship_X-M" title="Rocketship X-M">Rocketship X-M</a></i> (1950) brought us to what many people consider "the golden age of the science fiction film".</p>
<p>In the 1950s, public interest in space travel and new technologies was great. While many 1950s science fiction films were low-budget <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">B movies</a>, there were several successful films with larger budgets and impressive special effects. These include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still" title="The Day the Earth Stood Still">The Day the Earth Stood Still</a></i> (1951), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thing_from_Another_World" title="The Thing from Another World">The Thing from Another World</a></i> (1951), <i><a href="/wiki/When_Worlds_Collide_(1951_film)" title="When Worlds Collide (1951 film)">When Worlds Collide</a></i> (1951), <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(1953_film)" title="The War of the Worlds (1953 film)">The War of the Worlds</a></i> (1953), <i><a href="/wiki/20,000_Leagues_Under_the_Sea_(1954_film)" title="20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1954 film)">20,000 Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> (1954), <i><a href="/wiki/This_Island_Earth" title="This Island Earth">This Island Earth</a></i> (1955), <i><a href="/wiki/Forbidden_Planet" title="Forbidden Planet">Forbidden Planet</a></i> (1956), <i><a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_the_Body_Snatchers" title="Invasion of the Body Snatchers">Invasion of the Body Snatchers</a></i> (1956), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Curse_of_Frankenstein" title="The Curse of Frankenstein">The Curse of Frankenstein</a></i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_Center_of_the_Earth_(1959_film)" title="Journey to the Center of the Earth (1959 film)">Journey to the Center of the Earth</a></i> (1959) and <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Beach_(1959_film)" title="On the Beach (1959 film)">On the Beach</a></i> (1959). There is often a close connection between films in the science fiction genre and the so-called "monster movie". Examples of this are <i><a href="/wiki/Them!" title="Them!">Them!</a></i> (1954), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beast_from_20,000_Fathoms" title="The Beast from 20,000 Fathoms">The Beast from 20,000 Fathoms</a></i> (1953) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blob" title="The Blob">The Blob</a></i> (1958). During the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Harryhausen" title="Ray Harryhausen">Ray Harryhausen</a>, protege of master King Kong animator Willis O'Brien, used <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop-motion animation</a> to create special effects for the following notable science fiction films: <i><a href="/wiki/It_Came_from_Beneath_the_Sea" title="It Came from Beneath the Sea">It Came from Beneath the Sea</a></i> (1955), <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_vs._the_Flying_Saucers" title="Earth vs. the Flying Saucers">Earth vs. the Flying Saucers</a></i> (1956) and <i><a href="/wiki/20_Million_Miles_to_Earth" title="20 Million Miles to Earth">20 Million Miles to Earth</a></i> (1957).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1960s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1960s">List of science fiction films of the 1960s</a></div>
<p>There were relatively few science fiction films in the 1960s, but some of the films transformed science fiction cinema. <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1968_in_film" title="1968 in film">1968</a>) brought new realism to the genre, with its groundbreaking visual effects and realistic portrayal of space travel and influenced the genre with its epic story and transcendent philosophical scope. Other 1960s films included <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Vampires" title="Planet of the Vampires">Planet of the Vampires</a></i> (1965) by Italian filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Mario_Bava" title="Mario Bava">Mario Bava</a>, that is regarded as one of the best movies of the period, <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes_(1968_film)" title="Planet of the Apes (1968 film)">Planet of the Apes</a></i> (1968) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451_(1966_film)" title="Fahrenheit 451 (1966 film)">Fahrenheit 451</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1966_in_film" title="1966 in film">1966</a>), which provided social commentary, and the campy <i><a href="/wiki/Barbarella_(film)" title="Barbarella (film)">Barbarella</a></i> (1968), which explored the sillier side of earlier science fiction. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard" title="Jean-Luc Godard">Jean-Luc Godard</a>'s French "new wave" film <i><a href="/wiki/Alphaville_(film)" title="Alphaville (film)">Alphaville</a></i> (1965) posited a futuristic Paris commanded by an artificial intelligence which has outlawed all emotion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s.E2.80.931980s">1970s–1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1970s–1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1970s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1970s">List of science fiction films of the 1970s</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1980s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1980s">List of science fiction films of the 1980s</a></div>
<p>The era of manned trips to the moon in 1969 and the 1970s saw a resurgence of interest in the science fiction film. <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Tarkovsky" title="Andrei Tarkovsky">Andrei Tarkovsky</a>'s slow-paced <i><a href="/wiki/Solaris_(1972_film)" title="Solaris (1972 film)">Solaris</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1972_in_film" title="1972 in film">1972</a>) and philosophical <i><a href="/wiki/Stalker_(1979_film)" title="Stalker (1979 film)">Stalker</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1979_in_film" title="1979 in film">1979</a>) are two widely acclaimed examples of the renewed interest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auteur_theory" title="Auteur theory">film <i>auteurs</i></a> in science fiction. Science fiction films from the early 1970s explored the theme of paranoia, in which humanity is depicted as under threat from sociological, ecological or technological adversaries of its own creation, such as <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a>'s directional debut <i><a href="/wiki/THX_1138" title="THX 1138">THX 1138</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1971_in_film" title="1971 in film">1971</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain" title="The Andromeda Strain">The Andromeda Strain</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1971_in_film" title="1971 in film">1971</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Running" title="Silent Running">Silent Running</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1972_in_film" title="1972 in film">1972</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Soylent_Green" title="Soylent Green">Soylent Green</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1973_in_film" title="1973 in film">1973</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Westworld_(film)" title="Westworld (film)">Westworld</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1973_in_film" title="1973 in film">1973</a>) and its sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Futureworld" title="Futureworld">Futureworld</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1976_in_film" title="1976 in film">1976</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Logan%27s_Run_(film)" title="Logan's Run (film)">Logan's Run</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1976_in_film" title="1976 in film">1976</a>). The science fiction comedies of the 1970s included <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sleeper_(1973_film)" title="Sleeper (1973 film)">Sleeper</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1973_in_film" title="1973 in film">1973</a>), and <a href="/wiki/John_Carpenter" title="John Carpenter">John Carpenter</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Star_(film)" title="Dark Star (film)">Dark Star</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1974_in_film" title="1974 in film">1974</a>).</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">Star Wars</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1977_in_film" title="1977 in film">1977</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind" title="Close Encounters of the Third Kind">Close Encounters of the Third Kind</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1977_in_film" title="1977 in film">1977</a>) were box-office hits that brought about a huge increase in science fiction films. In <a href="/wiki/1979_in_film" title="1979 in film">1979</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Motion_Picture" title="Star Trek: The Motion Picture">Star Trek: The Motion Picture</a></i> brought the television series to the big screen for the first time. It was also in this period that the Walt Disney Company released many science fiction films for family audiences such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Island_at_the_Top_of_the_World" title="The Island at the Top of the World">The Island at the Top of the World</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_to_Witch_Mountain_(1975_film)" title="Escape to Witch Mountain (1975 film)">Escape to Witch Mountain</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Hole" title="The Black Hole">The Black Hole</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Navigator" title="Flight of the Navigator">Flight of the Navigator</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Honey,_I_Shrunk_the_Kids" title="Honey, I Shrunk the Kids">Honey, I Shrunk the Kids</a></i>. The sequels to <i>Star Wars</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Empire_Strikes_Back" title="The Empire Strikes Back">The Empire Strikes Back</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1980_in_film" title="1980 in film">1980</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Jedi" title="Return of the Jedi">Return of the Jedi</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1983_in_film" title="1983 in film">1983</a>), also saw worldwide box office success. <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>'s films, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(film)" title="Alien (film)">Alien</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1979_in_film" title="1979 in film">1979</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1982_in_film" title="1982 in film">1982</a>), along with <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1984_in_film" title="1984 in film">1984</a>), presented the future as dark, dirty and chaotic, and depicted aliens and androids as hostile and dangerous. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1982_in_film" title="1982 in film">1982</a>), one of the most successful films of the 1980s, presented aliens as benign and friendly, a theme already present in Spielberg's own <i>Close Encounters of the Third Kind</i>.</p>
<p>The big budget adaptations of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(film)" title="Dune (film)">Dune</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Raymond" title="Alex Raymond">Alex Raymond</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Flash_Gordon_(film)" title="Flash Gordon (film)">Flash Gordon</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>'s sequel to <i>2001</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/2010_(film)" title="2010 (film)">2010</a></i>, were box office failures that dissuaded producers from investing in science fiction literary properties. Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/Tron" title="Tron">Tron</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1982_in_film" title="1982 in film">1982</a>) turned out to be a moderate success. The strongest contributors to the genre during the second half of the 1980s were James Cameron and Paul Verhoeven with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop" title="RoboCop">RoboCop</a></i> entries. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Zemeckis" title="Robert Zemeckis">Robert Zemeckis</a>' film <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future" title="Back to the Future">Back to the Future</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1985_in_film" title="1985 in film">1985</a>) and its <a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_(franchise)" title="Back to the Future (franchise)">sequels</a> were critically praised and became box office successes, not to mention international phenomena. James Cameron's sequel to <i>Alien</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aliens_(film)" title="Aliens (film)">Aliens</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1986_in_film" title="1986 in film">1986</a>), was very different from the original film, falling more into the action/science fiction genre, it was both a critical and commercial success and <a href="/wiki/Sigourney_Weaver" title="Sigourney Weaver">Sigourney Weaver</a> was nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_Actress_in_a_Leading_Role" title="Best Actress in a Leading Role">Best Actress in a Leading Role</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a>. The Japanese anime film <i><a href="/wiki/Akira_(1988_film)" title="Akira (1988 film)">Akira</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1988_in_film" title="1988 in film">1988</a>) also had a big influence outside Japan when released.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s.E2.80.932000s">1990s–2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s–2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_1990s" title="List of science fiction films of the 1990s">List of science fiction films of the 1990s</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_2000s" title="List of science fiction films of the 2000s">List of science fiction films of the 2000s</a></div>
<p>In the 1990s, the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> genre spawned several movies on the theme of the computer-human interface, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Terminator_2:_Judgment_Day" title="Terminator 2: Judgment Day">Terminator 2: Judgment Day</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1991_in_film" title="1991 in film">1991</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Recall_(1990_film)" title="Total Recall (1990 film)">Total Recall</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1990_in_film" title="1990 in film">1990</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lawnmower_Man_(film)" title="The Lawnmower Man (film)">The Lawnmower Man</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1992_in_film" title="1992 in film">1992</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1999_in_film" title="1999 in film">1999</a>). Other themes included disaster films (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Armageddon_(1998_film)" title="Armageddon (1998 film)">Armageddon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(film)" title="Deep Impact (film)">Deep Impact</a></i> (both <a href="/wiki/1998_in_film" title="1998 in film">1998</a>), alien invasion (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(1996_film)" title="Independence Day (1996 film)">Independence Day</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1996_in_film" title="1996 in film">1996</a>)) and genetic experimentation (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1993_in_film" title="1993 in film">1993</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Gattaca" title="Gattaca">Gattaca</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1997_in_film" title="1997 in film">1997</a>)). Also, the <i>Star Wars</i> prequel trilogy began with the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_I_%E2%80%93_The_Phantom_Menace" title="Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace">Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace</a></i>, which eventually grossed over one billion dollars.</p>
<p>As the decade progressed, computers played an increasingly important role in both the addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">special effects</a> (thanks to <i><a href="/wiki/Terminator_2:_Judgment_Day" title="Terminator 2: Judgment Day">Terminator 2: Judgment Day</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>) and the production of films. As software developed in sophistication it was used to produce more complicated effects. It also enabled filmmakers to enhance the visual quality of animation, resulting in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell_(1995_film)" title="Ghost in the Shell (1995 film)">Ghost in the Shell</a></i> (1995) from Japan, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Iron_Giant" title="The Iron Giant">The Iron Giant</a></i> (1999) from the United States.</p>
<p>During the first decade of the 2000s, <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero films</a> abounded, as did earthbound science fiction such as the <i>Matrix</i> trilogy. In <a href="/wiki/2005_in_film" title="2005 in film">2005</a>, the <i>Star Wars</i> saga was completed with the darkly themed <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_III_%E2%80%93_Revenge_of_the_Sith" title="Star Wars: Episode III – Revenge of the Sith">Star Wars: Episode III – Revenge of the Sith</a></i>. Science-fiction also returned as a tool for political commentary in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/A.I._Artificial_Intelligence" title="A.I. Artificial Intelligence">A.I. Artificial Intelligence</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sunshine_(2007_film)" title="Sunshine (2007 film)">Sunshine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/District_9" title="District 9">District 9</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_Men" title="Children of Men">Children of Men</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Serenity_(film)" title="Serenity (film)">Serenity</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sleep_Dealer" title="Sleep Dealer">Sleep Dealer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pandorum" title="Pandorum">Pandorum</a></i>. The 2000s also saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers_(film)" title="Transformers (film)">Transformers</a></i> (2007) and <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers:_Revenge_of_the_Fallen" title="Transformers: Revenge of the Fallen">Transformers: Revenge of the Fallen</a></i> (2009), both of which resulted in worldwide box office success. In 2009, <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> garnered worldwide box office success, and would later become the highest-grossing movie of all time. This movie was also an example of political commentary. It depicted humans destroying the environment on another planet by mining for a special metal called unobtainium. That same year, <i><a href="/wiki/Terminator_Salvation" title="Terminator Salvation">Terminator Salvation</a></i> was released and garnered only moderate success.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_films_of_the_2010s" title="List of science fiction films of the 2010s">List of science fiction films of the 2010s</a></div>
<p>The 2010s has seen new entries in several classic science fiction franchises, including <i><a href="/wiki/Predators_(film)" title="Predators (film)">Predators</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2010_in_film" title="2010 in film">2010</a>),<i><a href="/wiki/Tron:_Legacy" title="Tron: Legacy">Tron: Legacy</a></i> (2010), a resurgence of the <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a> series, and entries into the <a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Planet of the Apes">Planet of the Apes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">Godzilla</a> franchises. Several more cross-genre films have also been produced, including comedies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Tub_Time_Machine" title="Hot Tub Time Machine">Hot Tub Time Machine</a></i> (2010), <i><a href="/wiki/Seeking_a_Friend_for_the_End_of_the_World" title="Seeking a Friend for the End of the World">Seeking a Friend for the End of the World</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2012_in_film" title="2012 in film">2012</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Safety_Not_Guaranteed" title="Safety Not Guaranteed">Safety Not Guaranteed</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2013_in_film" title="2013 in film">2013</a>); romance films such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_(2013_film)" title="Her (2013 film)">Her</a></i> (2013) and <i><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Monsters_(film)" title="Monsters (film)">Monsters</a></i> (2010), and action films including <a href="/wiki/Real_Steel" title="Real Steel">Real Steel</a> (2010), <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_of_Tomorrow" title="Edge of Tomorrow">Edge of Tomorrow</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2014_in_film" title="2014 in film">2014</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i> (2013). The <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a> boom has also continued, into films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_2" title="Iron Man 2">Iron Man 2</a></i> (2010) and <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">3</a></i> (2013), several entries into the <a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film_series)" title="X-Men (film series)">X-Men film series</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Avengers_(2012_film)" title="The Avengers (2012 film)">The Avengers</a></i> (2012), which became the fourth-highest grossing film of all time. New franchises such as <i><a href="/wiki/Deadpool_(film)" title="Deadpool (film)">Deadpool</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Guardians_of_the_Galaxy" title="Guardians of the Galaxy">Guardians of the Galaxy</a></i> have also begun in this decade.</p>
<p>Further into the decade, more realistic science fiction <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epic films</a> have also become prevalent, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_(2013_film)" title="Gravity (2013 film)">Gravity</a></i> (2013), <i><a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">Interstellar</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2014_in_film" title="2014 in film">2014</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max:_Fury_Road" title="Mad Max: Fury Road">Mad Max: Fury Road</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2015_in_film" title="2015 in film">2015</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Arrival_(film)" title="Arrival (film)">Arrival</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/2016_in_film" title="2016 in film">2016</a>). Many of these films have gained widespread accolades, including several <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a> wins and nominations. These films have addressed recent matters of scientific interest, including space travel, climate change, and artificial intelligence.</p>
<p>Alongside these original films, many adaptations have been produced, especially within the <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian_fiction" title="Dystopian fiction">dystopian fiction</a> subgenre, popular in the early part of the decade. These include the <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(film_series)" title="The Hunger Games (film series)"><i>Hunger Games</i> film series</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games" title="The Hunger Games">trilogy of novels</a> by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Collins" title="Suzanne Collins">Suzanne Collins</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Divergent_Series" title="The Divergent Series">The Divergent Series</a></i> based on <a href="/wiki/Veronica_Roth" title="Veronica Roth">Veronica Roth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Divergent_trilogy" title="Divergent trilogy">Divergent trilogy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maze_Runner_(film_series)" title="Maze Runner (film series)"><i>Maze Runner</i> series</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/James_Dashner" title="James Dashner">James Dashner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Maze_Runner_(series)" title="The Maze Runner (series)"><i>The Maze Runner</i> novels</a>. Several adult adaptations have also been produced, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Martian_(film)" title="The Martian (film)">The Martian</a></i> (2015), based on <a href="/wiki/Andy_Weir" title="Andy Weir">Andy Weir</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/The_Martian_(novel)" title="The Martian (novel)">2013 novel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(film)" title="Cloud Atlas (film)">Cloud Atlas</a></i> (2012), based on <a href="/wiki/David_Mitchell_(author)" title="David Mitchell (author)">David Mitchell</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(novel)" title="Cloud Atlas (novel)">2004 novel</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/World_War_Z_(film)" title="World War Z (film)">World War Z</a></i>, based on <a href="/wiki/Max_Brooks" title="Max Brooks">Max Brooks</a>' <a href="/wiki/World_War_Z" title="World War Z">2006 novel</a>.</p>
<p>Independent productions have also increased in the 2010s, with the rise of <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinematography" title="Digital cinematography">digital filmmaking</a> making it easier for amateur filmmakers to produce movies. These films include <i><a href="/wiki/Attack_the_Block" title="Attack the Block">Attack the Block</a></i> (2011), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Source_Code_(film)" title="Source Code (film)">Source Code</a></i> (2011), <i><a href="/wiki/Looper_(film)" title="Looper (film)">Looper</a></i> (2012), <i><a href="/wiki/Upstream_Color" title="Upstream Color">Upstream Color</a></i> (2013), and <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_Machina_(film)" title="Ex Machina (film)">Ex Machina</a></i> (2015), which won the Academy Award for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Visual Effects</a>, in a surprising upset over the much higher-budget <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Force_Awakens" title="Star Wars: The Force Awakens">Star Wars: The Force Awakens</a></i> (2015).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes.2C_imagery.2C_and_visual_elements">Themes, imagery, and visual elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Themes, imagery, and visual elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Science fiction films are often speculative in nature, and often include key supporting elements of science and technology. However, as often as not the "science" in a <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> science fiction movie can be considered pseudo-science, relying primarily on atmosphere and quasi-scientific artistic fancy than facts and conventional scientific theory. The definition can also vary depending on the viewpoint of the observer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many science fiction films include elements of mysticism, <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a>, magic, or the <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>, considered by some to be more properly elements of fantasy or the occult (or religious) film.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This transforms the movie genre into a science fantasy with a religious or quasi-religious philosophy serving as the driving motivation. The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Forbidden_Planet" title="Forbidden Planet">Forbidden Planet</a></i> employs many common science fiction elements, but the film carries a profound message - that the evolution of a species toward technological perfection (in this case exemplified by the disappeared alien civilization called the "Krell") does not ensure the loss of primitive and dangerous urges.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the film, this part of the primitive mind manifests itself as monstrous destructive force emanating from the freudian subconscious, or "Id".</p>
<p>Some films blur the line between the genres, such as films where the protagonist gains the extraordinary powers of the <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a>. These films usually employ quasi-plausible reason for the hero gaining these powers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Not all <a href="/wiki/List_of_science_fiction_themes" title="List of science fiction themes">science fiction themes</a> are equally suitable for movies. In addition to science fiction horror, <a href="/wiki/Space_opera" title="Space opera">space opera</a> is most common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Often enough, these films could just as well pass as <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">Westerns</a> or <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> films if the science fiction props were removed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Common motifs also include voyages and expeditions to other planets, and <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopias</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopias</a> are rare.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imagery">Imagery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Imagery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Film theorist <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Sobchack" title="Vivian Sobchack">Vivian Sobchack</a> argues that science fiction films differ from fantasy films in that while science fiction film seeks to achieve our belief in the images we are viewing, fantasy film instead attempts to suspend our disbelief. The science fiction film displays the unfamiliar and alien in the context of the familiar. Despite the alien nature of the scenes and science fictional elements of the setting, the imagery of the film is related back to mankind and how we relate to our surroundings. While the science fiction film strives to push the boundaries of the human experience, they remain bound to the conditions and understanding of the audience and thereby contain prosaic aspects, rather than being completely alien or abstract.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Genre films such as westerns or war movies are bound to a particular area or time period. This is not true of the science fiction film. However, there are several common visual elements that are evocative of the genre. These include the spacecraft or space station, alien worlds or creatures, robots, and futuristic gadgets. Examples include movies like <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_in_Space_(film)" title="Lost in Space (film)">Lost in Space</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Serenity_(film)" title="Serenity (film)">Serenity</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(2012_film)" title="Prometheus (2012 film)">Prometheus</a></i>. More subtle visual clues can appear with changes of the human form through modifications in appearance, size, or behavior, or by means a known environment turned eerily alien, such as an empty city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_elements">Scientific elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scientific elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>While science is a major element of this genre, many movie studios take significant liberties with scientific knowledge. Such liberties can be most readily observed in films that show <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> maneuvering in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> should preclude the transmission of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> or maneuvers employing wings, yet the sound track is filled with inappropriate flying noises and changes in flight path resembling an aircraft banking. The filmmakers, unfamiliar with the specifics of <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">space travel</a>, focus instead on providing acoustical atmosphere and the more familiar maneuvers of the aircraft.</p>
<p>Similar instances of ignoring science in favor of art can be seen when movies present environmental effects as portrayed in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film_series)" title="Star Trek (film series)">Star Trek</a></i>. Entire <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> are destroyed in titanic explosions requiring mere seconds, whereas an actual event of this nature takes many hours.</p>
<p>The role of the scientist has varied considerably in the science fiction film genre, depending on the public perception of science and advanced technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Starting with Dr. <a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mad_scientist" title="Mad scientist">mad scientist</a> became a <a href="/wiki/Stock_character" title="Stock character">stock character</a> who posed a dire threat to society and perhaps even civilization. Certain portrayals of the "mad scientist", such as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sellers" title="Peter Sellers">Peter Sellers</a>'s performance in <i>Dr. Strangelove</i>, have become iconic to the genre.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the monster films of the 1950s, the scientist often played a heroic role as the only person who could provide a <a href="/wiki/Technological_fix" title="Technological fix">technological fix</a> for some impending doom. Reflecting the distrust of government that began in the 1960s in the United States, the brilliant but rebellious scientist became a common theme, often serving a <a href="/wiki/Cassandra" title="Cassandra">Cassandra</a>-like role during an impending disaster.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloning</a>) is a popular scientific element in films as depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> (cloning of extinct species), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Island_(2005_film)" title="The Island (2005 film)">The Island</a></i> (cloning of humans), and (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_modification" title="Genetic modification">genetic modification</a>) in some superhero movies and in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alien_(film_series)" title="Alien (film series)">Alien</a></i> series. <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">Cybernetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holography" title="Holography">holographic projections</a> as depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop" title="RoboCop">RoboCop</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/I,_Robot_(film)" title="I, Robot (film)">I, Robot</a></i> are also popularized. <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">Interstellar travel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teleportation" title="Teleportation">teleportation</a> is a popular theme in the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film_series)" title="Star Trek (film series)">Star Trek</a></i> series that is achieved through <a href="/wiki/Warp_drive" title="Warp drive">warp drives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transporter_(Star_Trek)" title="Transporter (Star Trek)">transporters</a> while <a href="/wiki/Intergalactic_travel" title="Intergalactic travel">intergalactic travel</a> is popular in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_(film)" title="Stargate (film)">Stargate</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> that is achieved through <a href="/wiki/Hyperspace_(science_fiction)" title="Hyperspace (science fiction)">hyperspace</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wormhole" title="Wormhole">wormholes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</a> is also featured in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film_franchise)" title="Star Trek (film franchise)">Star Trek</a></i> series in the form of <a href="/wiki/Replicator_(Star_Trek)" title="Replicator (Star Trek)">replicators</a> (utopia), in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still_(2008_film)" title="The Day the Earth Stood Still (2008 film)">The Day the Earth Stood Still</a></i> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">grey goo</a> (dystopia), and in <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">Iron Man 3</a></i> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Extremis#extremis" title="Extremis">extremis (nanotubes)</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Force_shield" title="Force shield">Force fields</a> is a popular theme in <i>Independence Day</i> while <a href="/wiki/Invisibility" title="Invisibility">invisibility</a> is also popular in <i>Star Trek</i>. Arc reactor technology, featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_(2008_film)" title="Iron Man (2008 film)">Iron Man</a></i>, is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Cold_fusion" title="Cold fusion">cold fusion</a> device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The late <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Clarke%27s_three_laws" title="Clarke's three laws">third law</a> states that "any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic". Past science fiction films have depicted "fictional" ("magical") technologies that became present reality. For example, the PADD (Personal Access Display Device) from <i>Star Trek</i> was a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gesture_recognition" title="Gesture recognition">Gesture recognition</a> in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i> is part of current <a href="/wiki/Motion_controller" title="Motion controller">game consoles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence" title="Artificial general intelligence">Human-level artificial intelligence</a> is also fast approaching with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_personal_assistant" title="Intelligent personal assistant">smartphone A.I.</a> while a <a href="/wiki/Cloaking_device" title="Cloaking device">working cloaking device / material</a> is the main goal of <a href="/wiki/Stealth_technology" title="Stealth technology">stealth technology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_car" title="Autonomous car">Autonomous cars</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/KITT" title="KITT">KITT</a> from the <i><a href="/wiki/Knight_Rider_(2008_film)" title="Knight Rider (2008 film)">Knight Rider</a></i> series) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computer" title="Quantum computer">quantum computers</a>, like in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Stealth_(film)" title="Stealth (film)">Stealth</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(2014_film)" title="Transcendence (2014 film)">Transcendence</a></i>, also will be available eventually. Furthermore, although Clarke's laws does not classify <a href="/wiki/Disruptive_innovation" title="Disruptive innovation">"sufficiently advanced" technologies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kardashev_scale" title="Kardashev scale">Kardashev scale</a> measures a civilization's level of technological advancement into types. For example, an interstellar <i>Star Trek</i> can be described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_IV_civilization" title="Type IV civilization">Type II civilization</a> while an intergalactic <i>Star Wars</i> can be described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_IV_civilization" title="Type IV civilization">Type III civilization</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alien_lifeforms">Alien lifeforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alien lifeforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrials_in_fiction" title="Extraterrestrials in fiction">Extraterrestrials in fiction</a></div>
<p>The concept of life, particularly intelligent life, having an extraterrestrial origin is a popular staple of science fiction films. Early films often used alien life forms as a threat or peril to the human race, where the invaders were frequently fictional representations of actual military or political threats on Earth as observed in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mars_Attacks!" title="Mars Attacks!">Mars Attacks!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Starship_Troopers_(film)" title="Starship Troopers (film)">Starship Troopers</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_(franchise)" title="Predator (franchise)">Predator</a></i> series, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Riddick_(franchise)" title="The Chronicles of Riddick (franchise)">The Chronicles of Riddick</a></i> series. Some aliens were represented as benign and even beneficial in nature in such films as <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_to_Witch_Mountain_(1975_film)" title="Escape to Witch Mountain (1975 film)">Escape to Witch Mountain</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind" title="Close Encounters of the Third Kind">Close Encounters of the Third Kind</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Element" title="The Fifth Element">The Fifth Element</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy_(film)" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy (film)">The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Men_in_Black_(film_series)" title="Men in Black (film series)">Men in Black</a></i> series.</p>
<p>In order to provide subject matter to which audiences can relate, the large majority of intelligent alien races presented in films have an <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphic</a> nature, possessing human emotions and motivations. In films like <i><a href="/wiki/Cocoon_(film)" title="Cocoon (film)">Cocoon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Stepmother_Is_an_Alien" title="My Stepmother Is an Alien">My Stepmother Is an Alien</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Species_(film)" title="Species (film)">Species</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Contact_(1997_American_film)" title="Contact (1997 American film)">Contact</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Box_(2009_film)" title="The Box (2009 film)">The Box</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Knowing_(film)" title="Knowing (film)">Knowing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still" title="The Day the Earth Stood Still">The Day the Earth Stood Still</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Watch_(2012_film)" title="The Watch (2012 film)">The Watch</a></i>, the aliens were nearly human in physical appearance, and communicated in a common earth language. However, the aliens in <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate_(film)" title="Stargate (film)">Stargate</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(2012_film)" title="Prometheus (2012 film)">Prometheus</a></i> were human in physical appearance but communicated in an alien language. A few films have tried to represent intelligent aliens as something utterly different from the usual humanoid shape (e.g. An intelligent life form surrounding an entire planet in <i><a href="/wiki/Solaris_(1972_film)" title="Solaris (1972 film)">Solaris</a></i>, the ball shaped creature in <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Star_(film)" title="Dark Star (film)">Dark Star</a></i>, microbial-like creatures in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Invasion_(film)" title="The Invasion (film)">The Invasion</a></i>, shape-shifting creatures in <i><a href="/wiki/Evolution_(2001_film)" title="Evolution (2001 film)">Evolution</a></i>). Recent trends in films involve <a href="/wiki/Kaiju" title="Kaiju">building-size alien creatures</a> like in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i> where the <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> has tremendously improved over the previous decades as compared in previous films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Godzilla_(1998_film)" title="Godzilla (1998 film)">Godzilla</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disaster_films">Disaster films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Disaster films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Disaster_film" title="Disaster film">Disaster film</a></div>
<p>A frequent theme among science fiction films is that of impending or actual disaster on an epic scale. These often address a particular concern of the writer by serving as a vehicle of warning against a type of activity, including technological research. In the case of alien invasion films, the creatures can provide as a stand-in for a feared foreign power.</p>
<p>Disaster films typically fall into the following general categories:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Alien invasion</b> — hostile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life_in_popular_culture" title="Extraterrestrial life in popular culture">extraterrestrials</a> arrive and seek to supplant humanity. They are either overwhelmingly powerful or very insidious. Typical examples include <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(1953_film)" title="The War of the Worlds (1953 film)">The War of the Worlds</a></i> (1953), <i><a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_the_Body_Snatchers" title="Invasion of the Body Snatchers">Invasion of the Body Snatchers</a></i> (1956) <i><a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(1996_film)" title="Independence Day (1996 film)">Independence Day</a></i> (1996), <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Worlds_(2005_film)" title="War of the Worlds (2005 film)">War of the Worlds</a></i> (2005), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still_(2008_film)" title="The Day the Earth Stood Still (2008 film)">The Day the Earth Stood Still</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/Skyline_(film)" title="Skyline (film)">Skyline</a></i> (2010), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Darkest_Hour_(film)" title="The Darkest Hour (film)">The Darkest Hour</a></i> (2011), <i><a href="/wiki/Battle:_Los_Angeles" title="Battle: Los Angeles">Battle: Los Angeles</a></i> (2011), <i><a href="/wiki/Battleship_(film)" title="Battleship (film)">Battleship</a></i> (2012), <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i> (2013) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ender%27s_Game_(film)" title="Ender's Game (film)">Ender's Game</a></i> (2013).</li>
<li><b>Environmental disaster</b> — such as major climate change, or an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> strike. Movies that have employed this theme include <i><a href="/wiki/Soylent_Green" title="Soylent Green">Soylent Green</a></i> (1973), <i><a href="/wiki/Waterworld" title="Waterworld">Waterworld</a></i> (1995), <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(film)" title="Deep Impact (film)">Deep Impact</a></i> (1998), <i><a href="/wiki/Armageddon_(1998_film)" title="Armageddon (1998 film)">Armageddon</a></i> (1998), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Core" title="The Core">The Core</a></i> (2003), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Day_after_Tomorrow" title="The Day after Tomorrow">The Day after Tomorrow</a></i> (2004), <i><a href="/wiki/2012_(film)" title="2012 (film)">2012</a></i> (2009) and <i><a href="/wiki/Snowpiercer" title="Snowpiercer">Snowpiercer</a></i> (2013).</li>
<li><b>Man supplanted by technology</b> — typically in the form of an all-powerful <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a>, advanced <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cyborgs</a>, or else genetically modified humans or animals. Among the films in this category are <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i> (1984), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/I,_Robot_(film)" title="I, Robot (film)">I, Robot</a></i> (2004) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers_(film_series)" title="Transformers (film series)">Transformers</a></i> series.</li>
<li><b>Nuclear war</b> — usually in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopic</a>, post-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">holocaust</a> tale of grim survival. Examples of such a storyline can be found in the movies <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Strangelove" title="Dr. Strangelove">Dr. Strangelove</a></i> (1964), <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes_(1968_film)" title="Planet of the Apes (1968 film)">Planet of the Apes</a></i> (1968), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Boy_and_His_Dog" title="A Boy and His Dog">A Boy and His Dog</a></i> (1975), <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max" title="Mad Max">Mad Max</a></i> (1979), <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_Ember" title="City of Ember">City of Ember</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Eli" title="The Book of Eli">The Book of Eli</a></i> (2010), and <i><a href="/wiki/Oblivion_(2013_film)" title="Oblivion (2013 film)">Oblivion</a></i> (2013).</li>
<li><b>Pandemic</b> — a highly lethal <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>, often one created by man, threatens or wipes out most of humanity in a massive <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">plague</a>. This topic has been treated in such films as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain_(film)" title="The Andromeda Strain (film)">The Andromeda Strain</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Omega_Man" title="The Omega Man">The Omega Man</a></i> (1971), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12_Monkeys_(film)" title="12 Monkeys (film)">12 Monkeys</a></i> (1995), <i><a href="/wiki/28_Weeks_Later" title="28 Weeks Later">28 Weeks Later</a></i> (2007), <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am_Legend_(film)" title="I Am Legend (film)">I am Legend</a></i> (2007) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_(film_series)" title="Resident Evil (film series)">Resident Evil</a></i> series.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monster_films">Monster films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Monster films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monster_movie" title="Monster movie">Monster movie</a></div>
<p>While monster films do not usually depict danger on a global or epic scale, science fiction film also has a long tradition of movies featuring monster attacks. These differ from similar films in the horror or fantasy genres because science fiction films typically rely on a scientific (or at least pseudo-scientific) rationale for the monster's existence, rather than a supernatural or magical reason. Often, the science fiction film monster is created, awakened, or "evolves" because of the machinations of a mad scientist, a nuclear accident, or a scientific experiment gone awry. Typical examples include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beast_from_20,000_Fathoms" title="The Beast from 20,000 Fathoms">The Beast from 20,000 Fathoms</a></i> (1953), <i><a href="/wiki/Cloverfield" title="Cloverfield">Cloverfield</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Godzilla_(franchise)" title="Godzilla (franchise)">Godzilla</a></i> series of films.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mind_and_identity">Mind and identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mind and identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The core <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mental aspects</a> of what makes us human has been a staple of science fiction films, particularly since the 1980s. <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> examined what made an organic-creation a human, while the <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop_(franchise)" title="RoboCop (franchise)">RoboCop</a></i> series saw an <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">android</a> mechanism fitted with the brain and reprogrammed mind of a human to create a <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cyborg</a>. The idea of brain transfer was not entirely new to science fiction film, as the concept of the "<a href="/wiki/Mad_scientist" title="Mad scientist">mad scientist</a>" transferring the human mind to another body is as old as <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i> while the idea of corporations behind mind transfer technologies is observed in later films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gamer_(film)" title="Gamer (film)">Gamer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrogates_(film)" title="Surrogates (film)">Surrogates</a></i>.</p>
<p>Films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Recall_(1990_film)" title="Total Recall (1990 film)">Total Recall</a></i> have popularized a thread of films that explore the concept of reprogramming the human mind. The theme of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brainwashing" title="Brainwashing">brainwashing</a> in several films of the sixties and seventies including <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clockwork_Orange_(film)" title="A Clockwork Orange (film)">A Clockwork Orange</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Manchurian_Candidate_(1962_film)" title="The Manchurian Candidate (1962 film)">The Manchurian Candidate</a></i> coincided with secret real-life government experimentation during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_MKULTRA" title="Project MKULTRA">Project MKULTRA</a>. Voluntary erasure of memory is further explored as themes of the films <i><a href="/wiki/Paycheck_(film)" title="Paycheck (film)">Paycheck</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eternal_Sunshine_of_the_Spotless_Mind" title="Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind">Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind</a></i>. Some films like <i><a href="/wiki/Limitless_(film)" title="Limitless (film)">Limitless</a></i> explore the concept of mind enhancement. The anime series <i><a href="/wiki/Serial_Experiments_Lain" title="Serial Experiments Lain">Serial Experiments Lain</a></i> also explores the idea of reprogrammable reality and memory.</p>
<p>The idea that a human could be entirely represented as a program in a computer was a core element of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Tron" title="Tron">Tron</a></i>. This would be further explored in the film version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lawnmower_Man_(film)" title="The Lawnmower Man (film)">The Lawnmower Man</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(2014_film)" title="Transcendence (2014 film)">Transcendence</a></i>, and the idea reversed in <i><a href="/wiki/Virtuosity" title="Virtuosity">Virtuosity</a></i> as computer programs sought to become real persons. In the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_(franchise)" title="The Matrix (franchise)">Matrix</a></i> series, the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> world became a real world prison for humanity, managed by intelligent machines. In movies such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EXistenZ" title="EXistenZ">eXistenZ</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thirteenth_Floor" title="The Thirteenth Floor">The Thirteenth Floor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Inception" title="Inception">Inception</a></i>, the nature of reality and virtual reality become intermixed with no clear distinguishing boundary.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Psychokinesis" title="Psychokinesis">Telekinesis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Telepathy" title="Telepathy">telepathy</a></i> are featured in movies like <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Mimzy" title="The Last Mimzy">The Last Mimzy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Race_to_Witch_Mountain" title="Race to Witch Mountain">Race to Witch Mountain</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_(film)" title="Chronicle (film)">Chronicle</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lucy_(2014_film)" title="Lucy (2014 film)">Lucy</a></i> while <a href="/wiki/Precognition" title="Precognition">precognition</a> is featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robots">Robots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Robots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">Robots</a> have been a part of science fiction since the Czech playwright <a href="/wiki/Karel_%C4%8Capek" title="Karel Čapek">Karel Čapek</a> coined the word in 1921. In early films, robots were usually played by a human actor in a boxy metal suit, as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phantom_Empire" title="The Phantom Empire">The Phantom Empire</a></i>, although the female robot in <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i> is an exception. The first depiction of a sophisticated robot in a United States film was <a href="/wiki/Gort_(The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still)" title="Gort (The Day the Earth Stood Still)">Gort</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Stood_Still" title="The Day the Earth Stood Still">The Day the Earth Stood Still</a></i>.</p>
<p>Robots in films are often sentient and sometimes sentimental, and they have filled a range of roles in science fiction films. Robots have been supporting characters, such as Robby the Robot in <i><a href="/wiki/Forbidden_Planet" title="Forbidden Planet">Forbidden Planet</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Data_(Star_Trek)" title="Data (Star Trek)">Data</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film_series)" title="Star Trek (film series)">Star Trek</a></i>, sidekicks (e.g., <a href="/wiki/C-3PO" title="C-3PO">C-3PO</a> and <a href="/wiki/R2-D2" title="R2-D2">R2-D2</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i>, JARVIS from <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_(2008_film)" title="Iron Man (2008 film)">Iron Man</a></i>), and extras, visible in the background to create a futuristic setting (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_II" title="Back to the Future Part II">Back to the Future Part II</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Recall_(2012_film)" title="Total Recall (2012 film)">Total Recall</a></i> (2012), <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop_(2014_film)" title="RoboCop (2014 film)">RoboCop</a></i> (2014)). As well, robots have been formidable movie villains or monsters (e.g., the robot Box in the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logan%27s_Run_(1976_film)" title="Logan's Run (1976 film)">Logan's Run</a></i> (1976), <a href="/wiki/HAL_9000" title="HAL 9000">HAL 9000</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i>, ARIIA in <i><a href="/wiki/Eagle_Eye" title="Eagle Eye">Eagle Eye</a></i>, robot <a href="/wiki/Sentinel_(comics)" title="Sentinel (comics)">Sentinels</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_Days_of_Future_Past" title="X-Men: Days of Future Past">X-Men: Days of Future Past</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_droid" title="Battle droid">battle droids</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i>). In some cases, robots have even been the leading characters in science fiction films; in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> (1982), many of the characters are <a href="/wiki/Biological_engineering" title="Biological engineering">bioengineered</a> <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">android</a> "<a href="/wiki/Replicant" title="Replicant">replicants</a>" and in the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astro_Boy_(2009_film)" title="Astro Boy (2009 film)">Astro Boy</a></i> (2009), the leading character is a boy who became a robot.</p>
<p>Films like <i><a href="/wiki/Bicentennial_Man_(film)" title="Bicentennial Man (film)">Bicentennial Man</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/A.I._Artificial_Intelligence" title="A.I. Artificial Intelligence">A.I. Artificial Intelligence</a></i> depicted the emotional fallouts of robots that are self-aware. Other films like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Second_Renaissance" title="The Second Renaissance">The Animatrix (The Second Renaissance)</a></i> present the consequences of mass-producing self-aware androids as humanity succumbs to their robot overlords.</p>
<p>One popular theme in science fiction film is whether robots will someday replace humans, a question raised in the film adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/I,_Robot_(film)" title="I, Robot (film)">I, Robot</a></i> (in jobs) and in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Real_Steel" title="Real Steel">Real Steel</a></i> (in sports), or whether intelligent robots could develop a conscience and a motivation to protect, take over, or destroy the human race (as depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/Terminator_(franchise)" title="Terminator (franchise)">The Terminator</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers_(film_series)" title="Transformers (film series)">Transformers</a></i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/Avengers:_Age_of_Ultron" title="Avengers: Age of Ultron">Avengers: Age of Ultron</a></i>). Another theme is remote <a href="/wiki/Telepresence" title="Telepresence">telepresence</a> via <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">androids</a> as depicted in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrogates_(film)" title="Surrogates (film)">Surrogates</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">Iron Man 3</a></i>. As <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">computer power increases exponentially</a>, some sci-fi dreams have already been realized as computers become adept in logic reasoning and language understanding. For example, the computer <a href="/wiki/Deep_Blue_(chess_computer)" title="Deep Blue (chess computer)">Deep Blue</a> beat the world chess champion in 1997 and a documentary film, <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Over:_Kasparov_and_the_Machine" title="Game Over: Kasparov and the Machine">Game Over: Kasparov and the Machine</a></i>, was released in 2003. Another famous computer called <a href="/wiki/Watson_(computer)" title="Watson (computer)">Watson</a> defeated the two best human <i>Jeopardy</i> (game show) players in 2011 and a NOVA documentary film, <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Nova_episodes#Season_38:_2010.E2.80.932011" title="List of Nova episodes">Smartest Machine on Earth</a></i>, was released in the same year.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Robot" title="Super Robot">Building-size robots</a> are also becoming a popular theme in movies as featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i>. Future live action films include popular television series like <i><a href="/wiki/Voltron" title="Voltron">Voltron</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Robotech" title="Robotech">Robotech</a></i> and may include an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion" title="Neon Genesis Evangelion">Neon Genesis Evangelion</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> robots of <i>Pacific Rim</i> was greatly improved over the past decades as compared in previous films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mighty_Morphin_Power_Rangers:_The_Movie" title="Mighty Morphin Power Rangers: The Movie">Mighty Morphin Power Rangers: The Movie</a></i>. While "size does matter", a famous tagline of the movie <i>Godzilla</i>, incredibly small robots, called <a href="/wiki/Nanorobotics" title="Nanorobotics">nanobots</a>, do matter as well (e.g. Borg <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borg_assimilation" title="Borg assimilation">nanoprobes</a> in <i>Star Trek</i> and nanites in <i>I, Robot</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_travel">Time travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Time travel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Time_travel_in_fiction" title="Time travel in fiction">Time travel in fiction</a></div>
<p>The concept of <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a>—travelling backwards and forwards through time—has always been a popular staple of science fiction film and science fiction television series. Time travel usually involves the use of some type of advanced technology, such as H. G. Wells' classic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine" title="The Time Machine">The Time Machine</a></i>, the commercially successful 1980s-era <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_(franchise)" title="Back to the Future (franchise)">Back to the Future</a></i> trilogy, <i><a href="/wiki/Bill_%26_Ted%27s_Excellent_Adventure" title="Bill &amp; Ted's Excellent Adventure">Bill &amp; Ted's Excellent Adventure</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Terminator_(franchise)" title="Terminator (franchise)">Terminator</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_Vu_(2006_film)" title="Déjà Vu (2006 film)">Déjà Vu</a></i> (2006), <i><a href="/wiki/Source_Code" title="Source Code">Source Code</a></i> (2011), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_of_Tomorrow_(film)" title="Edge of Tomorrow (film)">Edge of Tomorrow</a></i> (2014). Other movies, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes_(1968_film)" title="Planet of the Apes (1968 film)">Planet of the Apes</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Timeline_(film)" title="Timeline (film)">Timeline</a></i> (2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Mimzy" title="The Last Mimzy">The Last Mimzy</a></i> (2007), explained their depictions of time travel by drawing on physics concepts such as the <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> phenomenon of time dilation (which could occur if a spaceship was travelling near the speed of light) and <a href="/wiki/Wormhole" title="Wormhole">wormholes</a>. Some films show time travel not being attained from advanced technology, but rather from an inner source or personal power, such as the 2000s-era films <i><a href="/wiki/Donnie_Darko" title="Donnie Darko">Donnie Darko</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Nobody_(film)" title="Mr. Nobody (film)">Mr. Nobody</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Butterfly_Effect_(film)" title="The Butterfly Effect (film)">The Butterfly Effect</a></i>.</p>
<p>More conventional time travel movies use technology to bring the past to life in the present, or in a present that lies in our future. The film <i><a href="/wiki/Iceman_(1984_film)" title="Iceman (1984 film)">Iceman</a></i> (1984) told the story of the reanimation of a frozen <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a>. The film <i><a href="/wiki/Freejack" title="Freejack">Freejack</a></i> (1992) shows time travel used to pull victims of horrible deaths forward in time a split-second before their demise, and then use their bodies for spare parts.</p>
<p>A common theme in time travel film is the paradoxical nature of travelling through time. In the <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">French New Wave</a> film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_jet%C3%A9e" title="La jetée">La jetée</a></i> (1962), director <a href="/wiki/Chris_Marker" title="Chris Marker">Chris Marker</a> depicts the self-fulfilling aspect of a person being able to see their future by showing a child who witnesses the death of his future self. <i>La Jetée</i> was the inspiration for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12_Monkeys_(film)" title="12 Monkeys (film)">12 Monkeys</a></i>, (1995) director <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a>'s film about time travel, memory and madness. The <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_(franchise)" title="Back to the Future (franchise)">Back to the Future</a></i> series and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine_(2002_film)" title="The Time Machine (2002 film)">The Time Machine</a></i> goes one step further and explores the result of altering the past, while in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_First_Contact" title="Star Trek: First Contact">Star Trek: First Contact</a></i> (1996) and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film)" title="Star Trek (film)">Star Trek</a></i> (2009) the crew must rescue the Earth from having its past altered by time-travelling <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cyborgs</a> and alien races.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_as_commentary_on_social_issues">Genre as commentary on social issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Genre as commentary on social issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The science fiction film genre has long served as useful means of discussing sensitive topical issues without arousing controversy, and it often provides thoughtful social commentary on potential unforeseen future issues. The fictional setting allows for a deeper examination and reflection of the ideas presented, with the perspective of a viewer watching remote events. Most controversial issues in science fiction films tend to fall into two general storylines, <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">Utopian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopian</a>. Either a society will become better or worse in the future. Because of controversy, most science fiction films will fall into the <a href="/wiki/List_of_dystopian_films" title="List of dystopian films">dystopian film</a> category rather than the Utopian category.</p>
<p>The types of commentary and controversy presented in science fiction films often illustrate the particular concerns of the periods in which they were produced. Early science fiction films expressed fears about automation replacing workers and the dehumanization of society through science and technology. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_in_the_White_Suit" title="The Man in the White Suit">The Man in the White Suit</a></i> (1951) used a science fiction concept as a means to satirize postwar British "establishment" conservatism, industrial capitalists, and trade unions. Another example is <i><a href="/wiki/HAL_9000" title="HAL 9000">HAL 9000</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey" title="2001: A Space Odyssey">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i> (1968). He controls the shuttle, and later harms its crew. "Kubrick's vision reveals technology as a competitive force that must be defeated in order for humans to evolve."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Later films explored the fears of environmental catastrophe, technology-created disasters, or overpopulation, and how they would impact society and individuals (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Soylent_Green" title="Soylent Green">Soylent Green</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Elysium_(film)" title="Elysium (film)">Elysium</a></i>).</p>
<p>The monster movies of the 1950s—like <i><a href="/wiki/Godzilla_(1954_film)" title="Godzilla (1954 film)">Godzilla</a></i> (1954)—served as stand-ins for fears of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> and views on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_war" title="Cold war">cold war</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 1970s, science fiction films also became an effective way of satirizing contemporary social mores with <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Running" title="Silent Running">Silent Running</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Star_(film)" title="Dark Star (film)">Dark Star</a></i> presenting hippies in space as a riposte to the militaristic types that had dominated earlier films.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clockwork_Orange_(film)" title="A Clockwork Orange (film)">A Clockwork Orange</a></i> presented a horrific vision of youth culture, portraying a youth gang engaged in <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, along with disturbing scenes of forced <a href="/wiki/Classical_conditioning" title="Classical conditioning">psychological conditioning</a> serving to comment on societal responses to <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>.</p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logan%27s_Run_(1976_film)" title="Logan's Run (1976 film)">Logan's Run</a></i> depicted a futuristic <a href="/wiki/Swinging_(sexual_practice)" title="Swinging (sexual practice)">swingers</a>' utopia that practiced euthanasia as a form of population control and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stepford_Wives_(1975_film)" title="The Stepford Wives (1975 film)">The Stepford Wives</a></i> anticipated a reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_liberation_movement" title="Women's liberation movement">women's liberation movement</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Enemy_Mine_(film)" title="Enemy Mine (film)">Enemy Mine</a></i> demonstrated that the foes we have come to hate are often just like us, even if they appear alien.</p>
<p>Contemporary science fiction films continue to explore social and political issues. One recent example is <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i> (2002), debuting in the months after the terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001 and focused on the issues of police powers, privacy and civil liberties in a near-future United States. Some like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Island_(2005_film)" title="The Island (2005 film)">The Island</a></i> (2005) and <i><a href="/wiki/Never_Let_Me_Go_(2010_film)" title="Never Let Me Go (2010 film)">Never Let Me Go</a></i> (2010) explore the issues surrounding cloning.</p>
<p>More recently, the headlines surrounding events such as the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_terrorism" title="International terrorism">international terrorism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> scare, and United States <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_immigration" title="Opposition to immigration">anti-immigration laws</a> have found their way into the consciousness of contemporary filmmakers. The film <i><a href="/wiki/V_for_Vendetta_(film)" title="V for Vendetta (film)">V for Vendetta</a></i> (2006) drew inspiration from controversial issues such as the <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Act" title="Patriot Act">Patriot Act</a> and the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> while science fiction thrillers such as <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_Men" title="Children of Men">Children of Men</a></i> (also 2006) and <i><a href="/wiki/District_9" title="District 9">District 9</a></i> (2009) commented on diverse social issues such as <a href="/wiki/Xenophobia" title="Xenophobia">xenophobia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_dissonance" title="Cognitive dissonance">cognitive dissonance</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> (2009) had remarkable resemblance to colonialism of native land, mining by multinational-corporations and the Iraq War.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_noir">Future noir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Future noir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lancaster_University" title="Lancaster University">Lancaster University</a> professor Jamaluddin Bin Aziz argues that as science fiction has evolved and expanded, it has fused with other film genres such as <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thrillers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a>. When science fiction integrates film noir elements, Bin Aziz calls the resulting hybrid form "future noir", a form which "... encapsulates a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodern" title="Postmodern">postmodern</a> encounter with generic persistence, creating a mixture of irony, pessimism, prediction, extrapolation, bleakness and nostalgia." Future noir films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Brazil_(1985_film)" title="Brazil (1985 film)">Brazil</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12_Monkeys_(film)" title="12 Monkeys (film)">12 Monkeys</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_City_(1998_film)" title="Dark City (1998 film)">Dark City</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_Men" title="Children of Men">Children of Men</a></i> use a <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> who is "...increasingly dubious, alienated and fragmented", at once "dark and playful like the characters in Gibson's <i><a href="/wiki/Neuromancer" title="Neuromancer">Neuromancer</a></i>, yet still with the "... shadow of <a href="/wiki/Philip_Marlowe" title="Philip Marlowe">Philip Marlowe</a>..."</p>
<p>Future noir films that are set in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic" title="Post-apocalyptic">post-apocalyptic</a> world "...restructure and re-represent society in a parody of the atmospheric world usually found in noir’s construction of a city—dark, bleak and beguiled." Future noir films often intermingle elements of the gothic thriller genre, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i>, which makes references to <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a> practices, and <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(film)" title="Alien (film)">Alien</a></i>, with its tagline "In space, no one can hear you scream", and a space vessel, Nostromo, "that hark[s] back to images of the haunted house in the gothic horror tradition". Bin Aziz states that films such as <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i> are a subgenre of "techno noir" that create "...an atmospheric feast of noir darkness and a double-edged world that is not what it seems."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_versus_literature">Film versus literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Film versus literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When compared to science fiction <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, science fiction films often rely less on the human imagination and more upon action scenes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">special effect</a>-created alien creatures and exotic backgrounds. Since the 1970s, film audiences have come to expect a high standard for special effects in science fiction films. In some cases, science fiction-themed films superimpose an exotic, futuristic setting onto what would not otherwise be a science-fiction tale. Nevertheless, some critically acclaimed science fiction movies have followed in the path of science fiction literature, using story development to explore abstract concepts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_science_fiction_authors">Influence of science fiction authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fiction_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Influence of science fiction authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> was the first major science fiction author to be adapted for the screen with Melies <i><a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">Le Voyage dans la Lune</a></i> (1902) and <i><a href="/wiki/Under_the_Seas" title="Under the Seas">20,000 lieues sous les mers</a></i> (1907), which used Verne's scenarios as a framework for fantastic visuals. By the time Verne's work fell out of copyright in 1950, the adaptations were treated as period pieces. His works have been adapted a number of times since then, including <i><a href="/wiki/20,000_Leagues_Under_the_Sea_(1954_film)" title="20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1954 film)">20,000 Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> (1954), <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(film)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (film)">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i> (1958), and two film versions of <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_Center_of_the_Earth" title="Journey to the Center of the Earth">Journey to the Center of the Earth</a></i> in 1959 and 2008.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> novels <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Invisible_Man_(1933_film)" title="The Invisible Man (1933 film)">The Invisible Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Things_to_Come" title="Things to Come">Things to Come</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Island_of_Doctor_Moreau" title="The Island of Doctor Moreau">The Island of Doctor Moreau</a></i> were all adapted into films during his lifetime while <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(novel)" title="The War of the Worlds (novel)">The War of the Worlds</a></i> was updated in 1953 and again in 2005, adapted to film at least four times altogether. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine" title="The Time Machine">The Time Machine</a></i> has had two film versions (1961 and 2002) while <i><a href="/wiki/Sleeper_(1973_film)" title="Sleeper (1973 film)">Sleeper</a></i> in part is a pastiche of Wells' 1910 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sleeper_Awakes" title="The Sleeper Awakes">The Sleeper Awakes</a></i>.</p>
<p>With the drop-off in interest in science fiction films during the 1940s, few of the 'golden age' science fiction authors made it to the screen. A novella by <a href="/wiki/John_W._Campbell" title="John W. Campbell">John W. Campbell</a> provided the basis for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thing_from_Another_World" title="The Thing from Another World">The Thing from Another World</a></i> (1951). <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a> contributed to the screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">Destination Moon</a></i> (1950), but none of his major works were adapted for the screen until the 1990s: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Puppet_Masters_(film)" title="The Puppet Masters (film)">The Puppet Masters</a></i> (1994) and <i><a href="/wiki/Starship_Troopers_(film)" title="Starship Troopers (film)">Starship Troopers</a></i> (1997). <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>'s fiction influenced the <i>Star Wars</i> and <i>Star Trek</i> films, but it was not until 1988 that a film version of one of his short stories (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nightfall_(Asimov)" title="Nightfall (Asimov)">Nightfall</a></i>) was produced. The first major motion picture adaptation of a full-length Asimov work was <i><a href="/wiki/Bicentennial_Man_(film)" title="Bicentennial Man (film)">Bicentennial Man</a></i> (1999) (based on the short stories <i>Bicentennial Man</i> and <i>The Positronic Man</i>, the latter co-written with Robert Silverberg), although <i><a href="/wiki/I,_Robot_(film)" title="I, Robot (film)">I, Robot</a></i> (2004), a film loosely based on <a href="/wiki/I,_Robot" title="I, Robot">Asimov's book of short stories</a> by the same name, drew more attention.</p>
<p>The adaptation of science fiction author <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>'s novel as <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i> won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Visual Effects">Academy Award for Visual Effects</a> and offered thematic complexity not typically associated with the science fiction genre at the time. Its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/2010_(film)" title="2010 (film)">2010</a></i>, was commercially successful but less highly regarded by critics. Reflecting the times, two earlier science fiction works by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">Ray Bradbury</a> were adapted for cinema in the 1960s with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451_(1966_film)" title="Fahrenheit 451 (1966 film)">Fahrenheit 451</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Illustrated_Man" title="The Illustrated Man">The Illustrated Man</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut" title="Kurt Vonnegut">Kurt Vonnegut</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse-Five_(film)" title="Slaughterhouse-Five (film)">Slaughterhouse-Five</a></i> was filmed in 1971 and <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_of_Champions" title="Breakfast of Champions">Breakfast of Champions</a></i> in 1998.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>'s fiction has been used in a number of science fiction films, in part because it evokes the paranoia that has been a central feature of the genre. Films based on Dick's works include <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> (1982), <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Recall_(1990_film)" title="Total Recall (1990 film)">Total Recall</a></i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/Impostor_(film)" title="Impostor (film)">Impostor</a></i> (2001), <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i> (2002), <i><a href="/wiki/Paycheck_(film)" title="Paycheck (film)">Paycheck</a></i> (2003), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Scanner_Darkly_(film)" title="A Scanner Darkly (film)">A Scanner Darkly</a></i> (2006) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adjustment_Bureau" title="The Adjustment Bureau">The Adjustment Bureau</a></i> (2011). These films are loose adaptations of the original story, with the exception of <i>A Scanner Darkly</i>, which is close to Dick's book.</p>
