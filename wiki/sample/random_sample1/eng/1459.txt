<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Definitions of science fiction</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>There have been many attempts at defining <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This is a list of definitions that have been offered by authors, editors, critics and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">fans</a> over the years since science fiction became a genre. Definitions of related terms such as "<a href="/wiki/Science_fantasy" title="Science fantasy">science fantasy</a>", "speculative fiction", and "fabulation" are included where they are intended as definitions of aspects of science fiction or because they illuminate related definitions—see e.g. Robert Scholes's definitions of "fabulation" and "structural fabulation" below. Some definitions of sub-types of science fiction are included, too; for example see David Ketterer's definition of "philosophically-oriented science fiction". In addition, some definitions are included that define, for example, a science fiction story, rather than science fiction itself, since these also illuminate an underlying definition of science fiction.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Encyclopedia_of_Science_Fiction" title="The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction">The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction</a></i>, edited by <a href="/wiki/John_Clute" title="John Clute">John Clute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Nicholls_(writer)" title="Peter Nicholls (writer)">Peter Nicholls</a>, contains an extensive discussion of the problem of definition, under the heading "Definitions of SF". The authors regard <a href="/wiki/Darko_Suvin" title="Darko Suvin">Darko Suvin</a>'s definition as having been most useful in catalysing academic debate, though they consider disagreements to be inevitable as science fiction is not homogeneous. Suvin's cited definition, dating from 1972, is: "a literary genre whose necessary and sufficient conditions are the presence and interaction of estrangement and cognition, and whose main formal device is an imaginative framework alternative to the author's empirical environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The authors of the <i>Encyclopedia</i> article—<a href="/wiki/Brian_Stableford" title="Brian Stableford">Brian Stableford</a>, Clute, and Nicholls—explain that, by "cognition", Suvin refers to the seeking of rational understanding, while his concept of estrangement is similar to the idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alienation_effect" title="Alienation effect">alienation</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Bertolt_Brecht" title="Bertolt Brecht">Bertolt Brecht</a>, that is, a means of making the subject matter recognizable while also seeming unfamiliar.</p>
<p>The order of the quotations is chronological; quotations without definite dates are listed last. The list below omits Hugo Gernsback's later redefining of the term "science fiction". According to anthologist, populist and historian of the genre <a href="/wiki/Sam_Moskowitz" title="Sam Moskowitz">Sam Moskowitz</a> (1920 – 1997), Gernback final words on the matter were: "Science fiction is a form of popular entertainment which contains elements of known, extrapolation of known or logical theoretical science". The list also omits John W. Campbell's infamous "Science fiction is what I say it is".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Definitions_of_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_chronological_order">In chronological order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Definitions_of_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In chronological order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Gernsback" title="Hugo Gernsback">Hugo Gernsback</a>. 1926. "By 'scientifiction' I mean the <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> type of story—a charming romance intermingled with scientific fact and prophetic vision... Not only do these amazing tales make tremendously interesting reading—they are always instructive. They supply knowledge... in a very palatable form... New adventures pictured for us in the scientifiction of today are not at all impossible of realization tomorrow... Many great science stories destined to be of historical interest are still to be written... Posterity will point to them as having blazed a new trail, not only in literature and fiction, but progress as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/J._O._Bailey" title="J. O. Bailey">J. O. Bailey</a>. 1947. "A piece of scientific fiction is a narrative of an imaginary invention or discovery in the natural sciences and consequent adventures and experiences... It must be a scientific discovery—something that the author at least rationalizes as possible to science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jakubowski_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jakubowski-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>. 1947. "Let's gather up the bits and pieces and define the Simon-pure science fiction story: 1. The conditions must be, in some respect, different from here-and-now, although the difference may lie only in an invention made in the course of the story. 2. The new conditions must be an essential part of the story. 3. The problem itself—the "plot"—must be a <i>human</i> problem. 4. The human problem must be one which is created by, or indispensably affected by, the new conditions. 5. And lastly, no established fact shall be violated, and, furthermore, when the story requires that a theory contrary to present accepted theory be used, the new theory should be rendered reasonably plausible and it must include and explain established facts as satisfactorily as the one the author saw fit to junk. It may be far-fetched, it may seem fantastic, but it must <i>not</i> be at variance with observed facts, i.e., if you are going to assume that the human race descended from Martians, then you've <i>got</i> to explain our apparent close relationship to terrestrial anthropoid apes as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eshbach_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-eshbach-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_W._Campbell,_Jr." title="John W. Campbell, Jr.">John W. Campbell, Jr.</a>. 1947. "To be science fiction, not fantasy, an honest effort at prophetic extrapolation from the known must be made."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eshbach_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-eshbach-7">[7]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>―. "Scientific methodology involves the proposition that a well-constructed theory will not only explain every known phenomenon, but will also predict new and still undiscovered phenomena. Science-fiction tries to do much the same—and write up, in story form, what the results look like when applied not only to machines, but to human society as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[notes 1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Damon_Knight" title="Damon Knight">Damon Knight</a>. 1952. At the start of a series of book review columns, Knight stated the following as one of his assumptions: "That the term 'science fiction' is a misnomer, that trying to get two enthusiasts to agree on a definition of it leads only to bloody knuckles; that better labels have been devised (Heinlein's suggestion, 'speculative fiction', is the best, I think), but that we're stuck with this one; and that it will do us no particular harm if we remember that, like 'The Saturday Evening Post', it means what we point to when we say it." This definition is now usually seen in abbreviated form as "Science fiction is [or means] what we point to when we say it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theodore_Sturgeon" title="Theodore Sturgeon">Theodore Sturgeon</a>. 1952. "A science fiction story is a story built around human beings, with a human problem, and a human solution, which would not have happened at all without its scientific content."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAH_14_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAH_14-11">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basil_Davenport" title="Basil Davenport">Basil Davenport</a>. 1955. "Science fiction is fiction based upon some imagined development of science, or upon the extrapolation of a tendency in society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davenport_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davenport-12">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edmund_Crispin" title="Edmund Crispin">Edmund Crispin</a>. 1955. A science fiction story "is one that presupposes a technology, or an effect of technology, or a disturbance in the natural order, such as humanity, up to the time of writing, has not in actual fact experienced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wyndham_Seeds_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wyndham_Seeds-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gokce_defs_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-gokce_defs-14">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>. 1959. "Realistic speculation about possible future events, based solidly on adequate knowledge of the real world, past and present, and on a thorough understanding of the nature and significance of the scientific method. To make this definition cover all science fiction (instead of 'almost all') it is necessary only to strike out the word 'future'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_TP_9_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knight_TP_9-15">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kingsley_Amis" title="Kingsley Amis">Kingsley Amis</a>. 1960. "Science fiction is that class of prose narrative treating of a situation that could not arise in the world we know, but which is hypothesized on the basis of some innovation in science or technology, or pseudo-science or pseudo-technology, whether human or extra-terrestrial in origin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Blish" title="James Blish">James Blish</a>. 1960 or 1964. Science fantasy is "a kind of hybrid in which plausibility is specifically invoked for most of the story, but may be cast aside in patches at the author's whim and according to no visible system or principle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a>. 1962. "Fantasy is the impossible made probable. Science Fiction is the improbable made possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Serling_def_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Serling_def-18">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Judith_Merril" title="Judith Merril">Judith Merril</a>. 1966. "Speculative fiction: stories whose objective is to explore, to discover, to <i>learn</i>, by means of projection, extrapolation, analogue, hypothesis-and-paper-experimentation, something about the nature of the universe, of man, or 'reality'... I use the term 'speculative fiction' here specifically to describe the mode which makes use of the traditional 'scientific method' (observation, hypothesis, experiment) to examine some postulated approximation of reality, by introducing a given set of changes—imaginary or inventive—into the common background of 'known facts', creating an environment in which the responses and perceptions of the characters will reveal something about the inventions, the characters, or both".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[notes 2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Darko_Suvin" title="Darko Suvin">Darko Suvin</a>. 1972. Science fiction is "a literary genre whose necessary and sufficient conditions are the presence and interaction of estrangement and cognition, and whose main formal device is an imaginative framework alternative to the author's empirical environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_M._Disch" title="Thomas M. Disch">Thomas M. Disch</a>. 1973. "The basic premise of all s-f—that Absolutely Anything Can Happen and Should—has never been so handsomely and hilariously realized as in <i>An Alien Heat</i>." (Cover blurb for the 1973 Harper and Row edition of the novel by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moorcock" title="Michael Moorcock">Michael Moorcock</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brian_Aldiss" title="Brian Aldiss">Brian Aldiss</a>. 1973. "Science fiction is the search for a definition of man and his status in the universe which will stand in our advanced but confused state of knowledge (science), and is characteristically cast in the Gothic or post-Gothic mould".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jakubowski_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-jakubowski-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> Revised 1986. "a definition of mankind...", "...post-Gothic mode".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">Ray Bradbury</a>. 1974. Science fiction is "the one field that reached out and embraced every sector of the human imagination, every endeavor, every idea, every technological development, and every dream." "I called us a nation of Ardent Blasphemers. We ran about measuring not only how things <i>were</i> but how they <i>ought to be</i>. ... We Americans are better than we hope and worse than we think, which is to say, we are the most paradoxical of all of the paradoxical nations in time. Which is what science fiction is all about. For science fiction runs out with tapes to measure Now against Then against Tomorrow Breakfast. It triangulates mankind amongst these geometrical threads, praising him, warning him." "For, above all, science fiction, as far back as Plato trying to figure out a proper society, has always been a fable teacher of morality...There is no large problem in the world this afternoon that is not a science-fictional problem." "Science fiction then is the fiction of revolutions. Revolutions in time, space, medicine, travel, and thought...Above all, science fiction is the fiction of warm-blooded human men and women sometimes elevated and sometimes crushed by their machines." "So science fiction, we now see, is interested in more than sciences, more than machines. That <i>more</i> is always men and women and children themselves, how they behave, how they hope to behave. Science fiction is apprehensive of future modes of behavior as well as future constructions of metal." "Science fiction guesses at sciences before they are sprung out of the brows of thinking men. More, the authors in the field try to guess at machines which are the fruit of these sciences. Then we try to guess at how mankind will react to these machines, how use them, how grow with them, how be destroyed by them. All, all of it fantastic." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factfiction_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-factfiction-25">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Ketterer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Ketterer (page does not exist)">David Ketterer</a>. 1974. "Philosophically-oriented science fiction, extrapolating on what we know in the context of our vaster ignorance, comes up with a startling <i>donnée</i>, or rationale, that puts humanity in a radically new perspective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Norman_Spinrad" title="Norman Spinrad">Norman Spinrad</a>. 1974. "Science fiction is anything published as science fiction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jakubowski_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-jakubowski-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinrad_MSF_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinrad_MSF-26">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>. 1975. "Science fiction can be defined as that branch of literature which deals with the reaction of human beings to changes in science and technology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANH_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-IANH-27">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Scholes" title="Robert Scholes">Robert Scholes</a>. 1975. Fabulation is "fiction that offers us a world clearly and radically discontinuous from the one we know, yet returns to confront that known world in some cognitive way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholes_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholes-28">[26]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>―. 1975. In structural fabulation, "the tradition of speculative fiction is modified by an awareness of the universe as a system of systems, a structure of structures, and the insights of the past century of science are accepted as fictional points of departure. Yet structural fabulation is neither scientific in its methods nor a substitute for actual science. It is a fictional exploration of human situations made perceptible by the implications of recent science. Its favourite themes involve the impact of developments or revelations derived from the human or physical sciences upon the people who must live with those revelations or developments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholes_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-scholes-28">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>― and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Rabkin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eric Rabkin (page does not exist)">Eric Rabkin</a>. 1977. "...science fiction could begin to exist as a literary form only when a different future became conceivable by human beings―specifically a future in which new knowledge, new discoveries, new adventures, new mutations, would make life radically different from the familiar patterns of the past and present." "The worlds of <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">Milton</a> remain separate from science fiction because they are constructed on a plan derived from religious tradition rather than scientific speculation or imagination based, however loosely, on science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholes-rabkin_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholes-rabkin-29">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gunn_(author)" title="James Gunn (author)">James Gunn</a>. 1977. "Science Fiction is the branch of literature that deals with the effects of change on people in the real world as it can be projected into the past, the future, or to distant places. It often concerns itself with scientific or technological change, and it usually involves matters whose importance is greater than the individual or the community; often civilization or the race itself is in danger."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunn_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunn-30">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Darko_Suvin" title="Darko Suvin">Darko Suvin</a>. 1979. "SF is distinguished by the narrative dominance or hegemony of a fictional "<a href="/wiki/Novum" title="Novum">novum</a>" (novelty, innovation) validated by cognitive logic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suvin_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-suvin-31">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Parrinder" title="Patrick Parrinder">Patrick Parrinder</a>. 1980. "<a href="/wiki/Hard_science_fiction" title="Hard science fiction">'Hard' SF</a> is related to 'hard facts' and also to the 'hard' or engineering sciences. It does not necessarily entail realistic speculation about a future world, though its bias is undoubtedly realistic. Rather, this is the sort of SF that most appeals to scientists themselves—and is often written by them. The typical 'hard' SF writer looks for new and unfamiliar scientific theories and discoveries which could provide the occasion for a story, and, at its more didactic extreme, the story is only a framework for introducing the scientific concept to the reader."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parrinder_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-parrinder-32">[30]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>―. 1980. "In 'space opera' (the analogy is with the Western 'horse opera' rather than the 'soap opera') the reverse [Parrinder is referring to his definition of "hard sf"] is true; a melodramatic adventure-fantasy involving stock themes and settings is evolved on the flimsiest scientific basis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parrinder_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-parrinder-32">[30]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Pringle" title="David Pringle">David Pringle</a>. 1985. "Science fiction is a form of fantastic fiction which exploits the imaginative perspectives of modern science".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kim_Stanley_Robinson" title="Kim Stanley Robinson">Kim Stanley Robinson</a>. 1987. Sf is "an historical literature... In every sf narrative, there is an explicit or implicit fictional history that connects the period depicted to our present moment, or to some moment in our past."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stableford_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-stableford-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Evans_(author)" title="Christopher Evans (author)">Christopher Evans</a>. 1988. "Perhaps the crispest definition is that science fiction is a literature of 'what if?' What if we could travel in time? What if we were living on other planets? What if we made contact with alien races? And so on. The starting point is that the writer supposes things are different from how we know them to be."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>. 1990. "'[H]ard science fiction' [is] stories that feature authentic scientific knowledge and depend upon it for plot development and plot resolution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>. 2000. "Science fiction is something that <i>could</i> happen - but you usually wouldn't want it to. Fantasy is something that <i>couldn't</i> happen - though you often only wish that it could." (emphasis original) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jeff_Prucher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jeff Prucher (page does not exist)">Jeff Prucher</a>. 2006. Science fiction is "a genre (of literature, film, etc.) in which the setting differs from our own world (e.g. by the invention of new technology, through contact with aliens, by having a different history, etc.), and in which the difference is based on extrapolations made from one or more changes or suppositions; hence, such a genre in which the difference is explained (explicitly or implicitly) in scientific or rational, as opposed to supernatural, terms."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Orson_Scott_Card" title="Orson Scott Card">Orson Scott Card</a> listed five types of stories that generally fall into sci-fi. September 28, 2010 "1. All stories set in the future, because the future can’t be known. This includes all stories speculating about future technologies, which is, for some people, the only thing that science fiction is good for. Ironically, many stories written in the 1940s and 1950s that were set in what was then the future—the 1960s, 1970s and 1980s—are no longer “futuristic.” Yet they aren’t “false,” either, because few science fiction writers pretend to be writing what will happen. Rather, they write what might happen. So those out-of-date futures, like that depicted in the novel 1984, simply shift from the “future” category to:</li>
</ul>
<p>2. All stories set in the historical past that contradict known facts of history. Within the field of science fiction, these are called “alternate world” stories. For instance, what if the Cuban Missile Crisis had led to nuclear war? What if Hitler had died in 1939? In the real world, of course, these events did not happen—so stories that take place in such false pasts are the purview of sci-fi and fantasy.</p>
<p>3. All stories set in other worlds, because we’ve never gone there. Whether “future humans” take part in the story or not, if it isn’t Earth, it belongs to this genre.</p>
<p>4. All stories supposedly set on Earth, but before recorded history and contradicting the known archaeological record—stories about visits from ancient aliens, or ancient civilizations that left no trace, or “lost kingdoms” surviving into modern times.</p>
<p>5. All stories that contradict some known or supposed law of nature. Obviously, fantasy that uses magic falls into this category, but so does much sci-fi: time travel stories, for instance, or invisible "man stories."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Milner" title="Andrew Milner">Andrew Milner</a>. 2012. Science fiction "is a selective tradition, continuously reinvented in the present, through which the boundaries of the genre are continuously policed, challenged and disrupted, and the cultural identity of the SF community continuously established, preserved and transformed. It is thus essentially and necessarily a site of contestation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li class="mw-empty-elt"></li>
</ul>
