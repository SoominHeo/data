<p><b>SK Sturm Graz</b> is an Austrian <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> club, based in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a>, playing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_Bundesliga" title="Austrian Bundesliga">Austrian Bundesliga</a>. The club was founded in 1909. Its colours are black and white.</p>
<p>In its history, Sturm Graz has won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_Football_Championship" title="Austrian Football Championship">Austrian Football Championship</a> three times—1998, 1999 and 2011—and participated several times in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> and <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Europa League</a>. Their biggest rivals are Graz neighbours <a href="/wiki/Grazer_AK" title="Grazer AK">Grazer AK</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SK Sturm Graz was founded in 1909 as a workers team, as opposed to its neighbours <a href="/wiki/Grazer_AK" title="Grazer AK">Grazer AK</a>, founded in 1902. Between 1921 and 1949, the team enjoyed considerable success in winning the regional Styrian championship 11 times.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a> in 1938 made Austria part of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German Third Reich</a> and Austrian clubs became part of German football competition. Sturm played in the opening round of the 1940 Tschammerpokal, predecessor to the modern-day <a href="/wiki/DFB-Pokal" title="DFB-Pokal">DFB-Pokal</a> (German Cup). They then qualified to play in the <a href="/wiki/Gauliga_Ostmark" title="Gauliga Ostmark">Gauliga Ostmark</a>, one of Germany's top-flight regional leagues, in 1941. The team withdrew part way through the 1941–42 season and was relegated after an 11th-place result in the following campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1949, Sturm entered the Austrian national league as the first non-<a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>-based team.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1981.E2.80.94First_success">1981—First success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1981—First success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first great success came under manager <a href="/wiki/Otto_Bari%C4%87" title="Otto Barić">Otto Barić</a>, when the club finished runners-up in the league in the 1980–81 season. In 1983–84, the club battled through to the quarter-finals of the <a href="/wiki/1983%E2%80%9384_UEFA_Cup" title="1983–84 UEFA Cup">UEFA Cup</a>, beaten only by <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Forest_F.C." title="Nottingham Forest F.C.">Nottingham Forest</a> through a penalty in extra-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992.E2.80.94Start_of_a_new_era">1992—Start of a new era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1992—Start of a new era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 1992, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hannes_Kartnig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hannes Kartnig (page does not exist)">Hannes Kartnig</a> was installed as president, naming his close friend <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Schilcher" title="Heinz Schilcher">Heinz Schilcher</a> as new manager. At the time, Sturm was languishing under enormous debts. Sturm qualified for the newly formed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zehnerliga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zehnerliga (page does not exist)">Zehnerliga</a>, and Kartnig and Schilcher decided the best course of action would be to abstain from big-name signings, opting instead for a new start using young players from the club's youth setup. In 1993, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Milan_Djuricic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Milan Djuricic (page does not exist)">Milan Djuricic</a> became manager.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1994_to_2002.E2.80.94Osim_and_European_football">1994 to 2002—Osim and European football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1994 to 2002—Osim and European football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, the Bosnian <a href="/wiki/Ivica_Osim" title="Ivica Osim">Ivica Osim</a> took control of the up-to-now unsuccessful Sturm; this proved to be a crucial turning-point in the club's history. Osim succeeded in producing an effective and powerful team using the young and inexperienced players at his disposal, strengthened with a few experienced leading players. The team's first success was as runners-up in the league in 1995. One year later, they won their first title, beating <a href="/wiki/FC_Admira_Wacker_M%C3%B6dling" title="FC Admira Wacker Mödling">Admira Wacker</a> in the cup final, but wobbling in the league to finish runners-up yet again.</p>
<p>In 1998, Sturm won its first Austrian Bundesliga title, pulling away from the field early on and winning the title with seven games in hand. Sturm set two records during this season; they remained unbeaten in their first 12 matches, and then for another 19 matches later in the season. At the end of the season, they amassed 81 points, an Austrian record total, winning the title with 19 points ahead of <a href="/wiki/SK_Rapid_Wien" title="SK Rapid Wien">Rapid Wien</a>. This season also saw the development of the "magic triangle" of <a href="/wiki/Mario_Haas" title="Mario Haas">Mario Haas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Reinmayr" title="Hannes Reinmayr">Hannes Reinmayr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivica_Vasti%C4%87" title="Ivica Vastić">Ivica Vastić</a>.</p>
<p>The year 1999 saw Sturm Graz retain the title, securing the treble as they did so (league, cup and super cup), in addition to appearing in the qualification for the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a>. Here, however, a scoreless draw with <a href="/wiki/FC_Spartak_Moscow" title="FC Spartak Moscow">Spartak Moscow</a> proved to be the team's only success. The 1999–2000 season saw Sturm in the Champions League for a second time, finishing third in its group. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FC_Tirol" title="FC Tirol">FC Tirol</a> wrested the domestic title from Sturm's grasp, but the runners-up spot achieved was sufficient for a third trip into the following season's Champions League.</p>
<p>Sensationally, Sturm Graz won its Champions League Group D (against <a href="/wiki/Galatasaray_S.K._(football)" title="Galatasaray S.K. (football)">Galatasaray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rangers_F.C." title="Rangers F.C.">Rangers</a> and <a href="/wiki/AS_Monaco_FC" title="AS Monaco FC">AS Monaco</a>), reaching the second round for the first time. The league campaign was less successful – a fourth-place finish, the worst under Osim.</p>
<p>After the Champions League exploits, several key players out of the 12 who later left were not suitably replaced. Worse still, this hasty squad redevelopment devoured almost all the profit made from the European campaign. Only a small fraction of the money was invested in youth development to establish an academy. Despite this, the newly assembled team again finished in second place in the league, but failed at the qualification hurdle for the Champions League. This, together with increasing criticism from the club President, precipitated the departure of Osim after eight years at the helm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_to_2009.C2.A0.E2.80.93_Consolidation">2002 to 2009 – Consolidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2002 to 2009 – Consolidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Franco_Foda" title="Franco Foda">Franco Foda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Gress" title="Gilbert Gress">Gilbert Gress</a> (seven defeats in nine games) both enjoyed short and fruitless stints as coach, before former sweeper <a href="/wiki/Mihailo_Petrovi%C4%87_(footballer)" title="Mihailo Petrović (footballer)">Michael Petrović</a> took control in autumn 2003. He presided over a gradual introduction of young talent, securing the team's place in the top flight in both 2004 and 2005, finishing in the seventh spot.</p>
<p>Since 2005, Sturm has been facing financial problems and, on 1 September 2006, a petition of bankruptcy was filed by the tax authorities. Because of the financial situation, Sturm was forced to use young players who were soon sold to reconsole the club. Also in 2006, coach Michael Petrović left the club, replaced by Franco Foda.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_to_present_day.C2.A0.E2.80.93_New_successes">2009 to present day – New successes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2009 to present day – New successes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sturm_Graz.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="214" data-file-width="350" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/79/Sturm_Graz.png/220px-Sturm_Graz.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/79/Sturm_Graz.png/330px-Sturm_Graz.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/79/Sturm_Graz.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>After a fourth-place finish in 2009, the Blackies qualified for the group stage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UEFA_Europe_League" title="UEFA Europe League">UEFA Europe League</a> in 2009–10. Their opponents were <a href="/wiki/Galatasaray_S.K._(football)" title="Galatasaray S.K. (football)">Galatasaray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panathinaikos_F.C." title="Panathinaikos F.C.">Panathinaikos</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FC_Dinamo_Bucure%C5%9Fti" title="FC Dinamo Bucureşti">Dinamo București</a>. In 2010, the Blackies won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96FB-Cup" title="ÖFB-Cup">ÖFB-Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a> in front of 25,000 of its own fans against <a href="/wiki/SC_Wiener_Neustadt" title="SC Wiener Neustadt">Wiener Neustadt</a>. That was the highest number of fans ever travelling to a match in a different <a href="/wiki/States_of_Austria" title="States of Austria">state</a>.</p>
<p>In 2010–11 Sturm won the Austrian Championship. A highlight of the season was a qualifying game against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juventus_Turin" title="Juventus Turin">Juventus Turin</a> in the Europe League.</p>
<p>In 2011–12, Sturm played in the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2011–12 UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> qualification rounds and managed to defeat Hungarian club <a href="/wiki/Videoton_FC" title="Videoton FC">Videoton</a> and <a href="/wiki/FC_Zestafoni" title="FC Zestafoni">Zestafoni</a> of Georgia. In the play-off, however, Sturm Graz lost against <a href="/wiki/FC_BATE_Borisov" title="FC BATE Borisov">BATE Borisov</a>, thus ensuring qualification to the group stages of the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_UEFA_Europa_League" title="2011–12 UEFA Europa League">Europe League</a>, where they were grouped with <a href="/wiki/R.S.C._Anderlecht" title="R.S.C. Anderlecht">Anderlecht</a>, <a href="/wiki/FC_Lokomotiv_Moscow" title="FC Lokomotiv Moscow">Lokomotiv Moscow</a> and <a href="/wiki/AEK_Athens_F.C." title="AEK Athens F.C.">AEK Athens</a>. At the end of the season, Sturm finished in fifth in the Bundesliga and coach Franco Foda was fired after six years. With his replacement <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hyballa" title="Peter Hyballa">Peter Hyballa</a>, Sturm played strong during the autumn months, but a poor spring resulted in Hyballa's dismissal before the end of the season. Luckily, Sturm managed to fourth in the final league table, albeit with the lowest amount of points ever sufficed for fourth place. This ensured Europa League qualification for the subsequent year. <a href="/wiki/Darko_Milani%C4%8D" title="Darko Milanič">Darko Milanič</a>, who won several titles with <a href="/wiki/NK_Maribor" title="NK Maribor">Maribor</a> in Slovenia, took the reins of the club for the 2013–14 campaign.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium">Stadium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stadium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The traditional home of the team for many years was the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gruabn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gruabn (page does not exist)">Gruabn</a></i>, which held over 12,000 people – almost exclusively standing – and which was characterised by its narrow playing field, and the proximity of the fans to the players. From 1997 to 2005, Gruabn was used just as a training ground and for youth and amateur matches. In 2005, the ground was sold to the city of Graz to relieve the club's financial difficulties. The year 1997 saw the club's move to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger_Stadium" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger Stadium">Arnold Schwarzenegger Stadium</a>, shared between Sturm and its local rivals, <a href="/wiki/Grazer_AK" title="Grazer AK">Grazer AK</a>. Since February 2006, the stadium is called <a href="/wiki/UPC-Arena" title="UPC-Arena">UPC-Arena</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fans_and_the_Graz_Derby">Fans and the Graz Derby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SK_Sturm_Graz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fans and the Graz Derby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study published in 2008 by the German market research institute <i>Sport + Markt</i> showed that Sturm have around 360,000 fans across Austria, which is only second to the number of <a href="/wiki/SK_Rapid_Wien" title="SK Rapid Wien">Rapid Wien</a> supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Europe, there are estimated to be 410,000 fans, which ranks them as the 117th-most supported club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are several organised <a href="/wiki/Ultras" title="Ultras">fan groups</a> – the biggest and most well-known are Jewels Sturm and the Brigata Graz, which were both founded in 1994, and Grazer Sturmflut, founded two years later in 1996.</p>
<p>Sturm fans have a very strong friendship with fans of German <a href="/wiki/2._Bundesliga" title="2. Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a> club <a href="/wiki/Karlsruher_SC" title="Karlsruher SC">Karlsruhe</a>. They have also contacts with fans of <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a>, <a href="/wiki/SV_Werder_Bremen" title="SV Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a> and fans from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pisa_Calcio" title="Pisa Calcio">Pisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrarese_Calcio" title="Carrarese Calcio">Carrara</a> in the Italian league. More recently, they have also had contacts with a group of <a href="/wiki/NK_Maribor" title="NK Maribor">Maribor</a> ultras.</p>
<p>Sturm have a big rivalry with cross-town rivals <a href="/wiki/Grazer_AK" title="Grazer AK">Grazer AK</a>, with whom they compete the Graz Derby. In 1974, there was big opposition from both sets of fans against a proposed merger to become "FC Graz." Since 1920, excluding the friendly matches (especially before the first official Styrian Cup in 1920), 197 matches have been played between the two, of which there were 185 encounters in the <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Austria" title="Football in Austria">league</a> (130 at the professional level and 55 at amateur level in the Styrian League); an additional five encounters in the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Cup" title="Austrian Cup">Austrian Cup</a>; one match in the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Supercup" title="Austrian Supercup">Austrian Supercup</a>; two meetings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tschammerpokal" title="Tschammerpokal">Tschammerpokal</a> and four games in the Styrian Cup. The very first Derby took place in 1911, the last was dated 17 May 2007. So far, Sturm have won more derby matches than Grazer.</p>
<p>Other rivalries are with the two Vienna clubs (<a href="/wiki/FK_Austria_Wien" title="FK Austria Wien">Austria Wien</a> and <a href="/wiki/SK_Rapid_Wien" title="SK Rapid Wien">Rapid Wien</a>) due to the history of competition for trophies between the three clubs, and as with most <a href="/wiki/Ultras" title="Ultras">ultras</a> the fans have a strong dislike of <a href="/wiki/FC_Red_Bull_Salzburg" title="FC Red Bull Salzburg">Red Bull Salzburg</a>, unhappy with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/SV_Austria_Salzburg" title="SV Austria Salzburg">Austria Salzburg</a> by Austrian <a href="/wiki/Energy_drink" title="Energy drink">energy drink</a> company <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull" title="Red Bull">Red Bull</a>.</p>
