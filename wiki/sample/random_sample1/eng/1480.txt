<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_typology" title="Linguistic typology">linguistic typology</a>, <b>subject–verb–object</b> (<b>SVO</b>) is a sentence structure where the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> comes first, the <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> second, and the <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> third. Languages may be classified according to the dominant sequence of these elements. The label is often used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergative_language" title="Ergative language">ergative languages</a> which do not have subjects, but have an <b><a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">agent</a>–verb–object</b> order.</p>
<p>SVO is the second most common order by number of known languages, after <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" title="Subject–object–verb">SOV</a>. Together, SVO and SOV account for more than 75% of the world's languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also the most common order developed in <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">Creole languages</a>, suggesting that it may be somehow more initially 'obvious' to human psychology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Languages regarded as SVO include: <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish href="/wiki/Modern_Greek_grammar#Syntax" title="Modern Greek grammar">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> (with the <a href="/wiki/V2_word_order" title="V2 word order">V2</a> restriction), <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian (Bahasa Indonesia)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_language" title="Kashubian language">Kashubian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay (Bahasa Melayu)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quich%C3%A9_language" title="Quiché language">Quiche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotuman_language" title="Rotuman language">Rotuman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> (but see below), <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili <p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> has free syntactic order, although SOV tended to be preferred by <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical Greeks</a>. Many famous phrases are SVO, however.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Subject–verb–object languages almost always place <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">relative clauses</a> after the nouns they modify and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverbial_subordinator" title="Adverbial subordinator">adverbial subordinators</a> before the clause modified, with <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a> being notable exceptions.</p>
<p>Although some subject–verb–object languages in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, the best known being <a href="/wiki/Ewe_language" title="Ewe language">Ewe</a>, use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postposition" title="Postposition">postpositions</a> in noun phrases, the vast majority of them have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">prepositions</a> as in English. Most subject–verb–object languages place genitives after the noun, but a significant minority, including the postpositional SVO languages of West Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Hmong%E2%80%93Mien_languages" title="Hmong–Mien languages">Hmong–Mien languages</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan languages</a>, and European languages such as Swedish, Danish, Lithuanian and Latvian, have <i>prenominal</i> genitives<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (as would be expected in a <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" title="Subject–object–verb">SOV language</a>).</p>
<p>Outside Europe, subject–verb–object languages have a strong tendency to place <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">demonstratives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">numerals</a> <i>after</i> the noun they modify, but Chinese, Vietnamese, Indonesian, and Malay place numerals before nouns, as in English. Some linguists have come to view the numeral as the head in this relationship to fit the rigid right-branching of these languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a strong tendency for SO languages to have auxiliaries precede main verbs: I <b>am</b> thinking. He <b>should</b> reconsider. Etc.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_sentences">Example sentences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Example sentences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An example of SVO order in English is:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Andy ate cereal.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The situation is more complex in languages that have no word order imposed by their grammar; example: the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> languages have both the <a href="/wiki/VO_language" title="VO language">VO</a> and <a href="/wiki/OV_language" title="OV language">OV</a> constructs in their common word order uses. In some languages, some word orders are considered more "natural" than others. In some the order is the matter of emphasis. For ex., the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> allows the use of subject-verb-object in any order and "shuffles" parts to bring up a slightly different contextual meaning each time. E.g. "любит она его" (Loves she him) may be used to point out "she acts this way because she LOVES him", or "его она любит" (Him she loves) is used in context "if you pay attention, you'll see that He is the one she truly loves", or "его любит она" (him loves She) may appear along the lines "I agree that cat is a disaster, but since my wife adores it and I adore her...". In <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> an SVO order is basic in an affirmative sentence and different order is used to either emphasize some part of it or to adapt it to a broader context logic. E.g. "<i>Roweru</i> ci nie kupię" (I won't buy you <i>a bicycle</i>), "<i>Od piątej</i> czekam" (I've been waiting <i>since five</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> where normally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject-object-verb" title="Subject-object-verb">SOV</a> is used, SVO can be used to emphasize the verb in some instances. For example, "John terketti Mary'yi" (Lit. <i>John/left/Mary</i>: John left Mary) is the answer to the question "What did John do with Mary?" instead of the regular [SOV] sentence "John Mary'yi terketti" (Lit. <i>John/Mary/left</i>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kashmiri_language" title="Kashmiri language">Kashmiri</a>, SOV with V2 in main clauses coexists with SOV in subordinate clauses, as given in Example 1 below; and a change in syntax – for instance, by bringing an adpositional phrase to the front of the sentence for emphasis – may also dictate the use of VSO, as in Example 2. (See <a href="/wiki/V2_word_order" title="V2 word order">V2 word order</a>.) In Kashmiri the word order in embedded clauses is conditioned by the category of the subordinating conjunction, as in Example 3.</p>
<ol>
<li>"Er weiß, dass ich jeden Sonntag das Auto wasche" (German: "He knows that I wash the car every Sunday", lit. "He knows, that I every Sunday the car wash"). Cf. the simple sentence "Ich wasche das Auto jeden Sonntag", "I wash the car every Sunday".</li>
<li>"Elke zondag was ik de auto" (Dutch: "Every Sunday I wash the car", lit. "Every Sunday wash I the car"). "Ik was de auto elke zondag" translates perfectly into English "I wash the car every Sunday", but, as a result of changing the syntax, <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(linguistics)" title="Inversion (linguistics)">inversion</a> SV-&gt;VS takes place.</li>
<li>"mye ees phyikyir yithi.ni tsi temyis ciThy <b>dyikh</b>" (Kashmiri: "I was afraid you might give him the letter", lit. "to.me was worry lest you to.him letter <b>will.give</b>"). If the embedded clause is introduced by the transparent conjunction <i>zyi</i> the SOV order changes to SVO. "mye ees phyikyir (zyi) tsi maa <b>dyikh</b> temyis ciThy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>English developed from such a reordering language and still bears traces of this word order, for example in locative inversion ("In the garden <b>sat</b> a cat.") and some clauses beginning with negative expressions: "only" ("Only then <b>do</b> we find X."), "not only" ("Not only <b>did</b> he storm away but also slammed the door."), "under no circumstances" ("under no circumstances <b>are</b> the students allowed to use a mobile phone"), "never" ("Never <b>have</b> I done that."), "on no account" and the like. In these cases <a href="/wiki/Do-support" title="Do-support"><i>do</i>-support</a> may or may not be required, depending on the construction.</p>
