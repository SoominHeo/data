<p><b>SWOT analysis</b> (alternatively <b>SWOT matrix</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym#Nomenclature" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for <i>strengths</i>, <i>weaknesses</i>, <i>opportunities</i>, and <i>threats</i> and is a structured <a href="/wiki/Plan" title="Plan">planning</a> method that evaluates those four elements of a <a href="/wiki/Project" title="Project">project</a> or <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> venture. A SWOT analysis can be carried out for a company, product, place, industry, or person. It involves specifying the objective of the business venture or project and identifying the internal and external factors that are favorable and unfavorable to achieve that objective. Some authors credit SWOT to <a href="/wiki/Albert_S._Humphrey" title="Albert S. Humphrey">Albert Humphrey</a>, who led a convention at the Stanford Research Institute (now <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>) in the 1960s and 1970s using data from <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, Humphrey himself did not claim the creation of SWOT, and the origins remain obscure. The degree to which the internal environment of the firm matches with the external environment is expressed by the concept of <a href="/wiki/Strategic_fit" title="Strategic fit">strategic fit</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>Strengths: characteristics of the business or project that give it an advantage over others</li>
<li>Weaknesses: characteristics of the business that place the business or project at a disadvantage relative to others</li>
<li>Opportunities: elements in the environment that the business or project could exploit to its advantage</li>
<li>Threats: elements in the environment that could cause trouble for the business or project</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Identification of SWOTs is important because they can inform later steps in planning to achieve the objective. First, decision-makers should consider whether the objective is attainable, given the SWOTs. If the objective is <i>not</i> attainable, they must select a different objective and repeat the process.</p>
<p>Users of SWOT analysis must ask and answer questions that generate meaningful information for each category (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats) to make the analysis useful and find their competitive advantage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_and_external_factors">Internal and external factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Internal and external factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>So it is said that if you know your enemies and know yourself, you can win a hundred battles without a single loss. If you only know yourself, but not your opponent, you may win or may lose. If you know neither yourself nor your enemy, you will always endanger yourself.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_War" title="The Art of War">The Art of War</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sun_Tzu" title="Sun Tzu">Sun Tzu</a></cite></div>
<p>SWOT analysis aims to identify the key internal and external factors seen as important to achieving an objective. SWOT analysis groups key pieces of information into two main categories:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ol>
<li>Internal factors – the <i>strengths</i> and <i>weaknesses</i> internal to the organization</li>
<li>External factors – the <i>opportunities</i> and <i>threats</i> presented by the environment external to the organization</li>
</ol>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Analysis may view the internal factors as strengths or as weaknesses depending upon their effect on the organization's objectives. What may represent strengths with respect to one objective may be weaknesses (distractions, competition) for another objective. The factors may include all of the <a href="/wiki/Marketing_mix" title="Marketing mix">4Ps</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personnel" title="Personnel">personnel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> capabilities, and so on.</p>
<p>The external factors may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> matters, <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a>, and sociocultural changes, as well as changes in the marketplace or in competitive position. The results are often presented in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Table_(information)" title="Table (information)">matrix</a>.</p>
<p>SWOT analysis is just one method of <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">categorization</a> and has its own weaknesses. For example, it may tend to persuade its users to compile lists rather than to think about actual important factors in achieving objectives. It also presents the resulting lists uncritically and without clear prioritization so that, for example, weak opportunities may appear to balance strong threats.</p>
<p>It is prudent not to eliminate any candidate SWOT entry too quickly. The importance of individual SWOTs will be revealed by the value of the strategies they generate. A SWOT item that produces valuable strategies is important. A SWOT item that generates no strategies is not important.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The usefulness of SWOT analysis is not limited to profit-seeking organizations. SWOT analysis may be used in any decision-making situation when a desired end-state (objective) is defined. Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organizations" title="Non-profit organizations">non-profit organizations</a>, governmental units, and individuals. SWOT analysis may also be used in pre-crisis planning and preventive <a href="/wiki/Crisis_management" title="Crisis management">crisis management</a>. SWOT analysis may also be used in creating a recommendation during a <a href="/wiki/Viability_study" title="Viability study">viability study</a>/survey.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy_building">Strategy building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Strategy building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SWOT analysis can be used effectively to build organizational or personal strategy. Steps necessary to execute strategy-oriented analysis involve identification of internal and external factors (using popular 2x2 matrix), selection and evaluation of the most important factors, and identification of relations existing between internal and external features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>For instance, strong relations between strengths and opportunities can suggest good conditions in the company and allow using an <i>aggressive</i> strategy. On the other hand, strong interactions between weaknesses and threats could be analyzed as a potential warning and advice for using a <i>defensive</i> strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matching_and_converting">Matching and converting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Matching and converting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One way of utilizing SWOT is matching and converting. Matching is used to find <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a> by matching the strengths to opportunities. Another tactic is to convert weaknesses or threats into strengths or opportunities. An example of a conversion strategy is to find new markets. If the threats or weaknesses cannot be converted, a company should try to minimize or avoid them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_planning">Corporate planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corporate planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As part of the development of strategies and plans to enable the organization to achieve its objectives, that organization will use a systematic/rigorous process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_planning" title="Corporate planning">corporate planning</a>. SWOT alongside <a href="/wiki/PEST_analysis" title="PEST analysis">PEST</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PESTLE" title="PESTLE">PESTLE</a> can be used as a basis for the analysis of business and environmental factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Set objectives</b> – defining what the organization is going to do</li>
<li><b>Environmental scanning</b>
<ul>
<li>Internal appraisals of the organization's SWOT, this needs to include an assessment of the present situation as well as a portfolio of products/services and an analysis of the product/service life cycle</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Analysis of existing strategies</b>, this should determine relevance from the results of an internal/external appraisal. This may include <a href="/wiki/Gap_analysis" title="Gap analysis">gap analysis</a> of environmental factors</li>
<li><b>Strategic Issues</b> defined – key factors in the development of a corporate plan that the organization must address</li>
<li><b>Develop</b> new/revised strategies – revised analysis of strategic issues may mean the objectives need to change</li>
<li><b>Establish</b> <a href="/wiki/Critical_success_factor" title="Critical success factor">critical success factors</a> – the achievement of objectives and strategy implementation</li>
<li><b>Preparation</b> of operational, resource, projects plans for strategy implementation</li>
<li><b>Monitoring</b> results – mapping against plans, taking corrective action, which may mean amending objectives/strategies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arms_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-arms-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marketing_management" title="Marketing management">Marketing management</a></div>
<p>In many competitor analyses, marketers build detailed profiles of each competitor in the market, focusing especially on their relative competitive strengths and weaknesses using SWOT analysis. Marketing managers will examine each competitor's cost structure, sources of profits, resources and competencies, competitive positioning and product differentiation, degree of <a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">vertical integration</a>, historical responses to industry developments, and other factors.</p>
<p>Marketing management often finds it necessary to invest in research to collect the data required to perform accurate marketing analysis. Accordingly, management often conducts market research (alternately marketing research) to obtain this information. Marketers employ a variety of techniques to conduct market research, but some of the more common include:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>Qualitative marketing research such as focus groups</li>
<li>Quantitative marketing research such as statistical surveys</li>
<li>Experimental techniques such as test markets</li>
<li>Observational techniques such as ethnographic (on-site) observation</li>
<li>Marketing managers may also design and oversee various environmental scanning and competitive intelligence processes to help identify trends and inform the company's marketing analysis.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_community_organization">In community organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In community organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SWOT analysis has been utilized in community work as a tool to identify positive and negative factors within organizations, communities, and the broader society that promote or inhibit successful implementation of social services and social change efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> It is used as a preliminary resource, assessing strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats in a community served by a nonprofit or community organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-social_work_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-social_work-9">[9]</a></sup> This organizing tool is best used in collaboration with community workers and/or community members before developing goals and objectives for a program design or implementing an organizing strategy.The SWOT analysis is a part of the planning for social change process and will not provide a strategic plan if used by itself. After a SWOT analysis is completed, a social change organization can turn the SWOT list into a series of recommendations to consider before developing a strategic plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-our_community_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-our_community-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Strengths and Weaknesses</b>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>These are the internal factors within an organization.</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Human resources - staff, volunteers, board members, target population</li>
<li>Physical resources - your location, building, equipment</li>
<li>Financial - grants, funding agencies, other sources of income</li>
<li>Activities and processes - programs you run, systems you employ</li>
<li>Past experiences - building blocks for learning and success, your reputation in the community</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Opportunities and Threats</b>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>These are external factors stemming from community or societal forces.</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Future trends in your field or the culture</li>
<li>The economy - local, national, or international</li>
<li>Funding sources - foundations, donors, legislatures</li>
<li>Demographics - changes in the age, race, gender, culture of those you serve or in your area</li>
<li>The physical environment (Is your building in a growing part of town? Is the bus company cutting routes?)</li>
<li>Legislation (Do new federal requirements make your job harder...or easier?)</li>
<li>Local, national, or international events</li>
</ul>
<p>Although the SWOT analysis was originally designed as an organizational method for business and industries, it has been replicated in various community work as a tool for identifying external and internal support to combat internal and external opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> The SWOT analysis is necessary to provide direction to the next stages of the change process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOOK_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOOK-11">[11]</a></sup> It has been utilized by community organizers and community members to further social justice in the context of Social Work practice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_in_community_organization">Application in community organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Application in community organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements_to_consider">Elements to consider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elements to consider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Elements to consider in a SWOT analysis include understanding the community that a particular organization is working with. This can be done via public forums, listening campaigns, and informational interviews. Data collection will help inform the community members and workers when developing the SWOT analysis. A needs and assets assessment are tooling that can be used to identify the needs and existing resources of the community. When these assessments are done and data has been collected, an analysis of the community can be made that informs the SWOT analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps_for_implementation">Steps for implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Steps for implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A SWOT analysis is best developed in a group setting such as a work or community meeting. A facilitator can conduct the meeting by first explaining what a SWOT analysis is as well as identifying the meaning of each term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>One way of facilitating the development of a SWOT analysis includes developing an example SWOT with the larger group then separating each group into smaller teams to present to the larger group after set amount of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> This allows for individuals, who may be silenced in a larger group setting, to contribute. Once the allotted time is up, the facilitator may record all the factors of each group onto a large document such as a poster board, and then the large group, as a collective, can go work through each of the threats and weaknesses to explore options that may be used to combat negative forces with the strengths and opportunities present within the organization and community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup> A SWOT meeting allows participants to creatively brainstorm, identify obstacles, and possibly strategize solutions/way forward to these limitations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="When_to_use_SWOT_analysis">When to use SWOT analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: When to use SWOT analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The uses of a SWOT analysis by a community organization are as follows: to organize information, provide insight into barriers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-use_and_misuse_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-use_and_misuse-12">[12]</a></sup> that may be present while engaging in social change processes, and identify strengths available that can be activated to counteract these barriers.</p>
<p><i>A SWOT analysis can be used to:</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Explore new solutions to problems <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Identify barriers that will limit goals/objectives <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Decide on direction that will be most effective <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Reveal possibilities and limitations for change<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-community_tool_box_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-community_tool_box-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>To revise plans to best navigate systems, communities, and organizations</li>
<li>As a brainstorming and recording device as a means of communication<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-use_and_misuse_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-use_and_misuse-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>To enhance “credibility of interpretation” to be utilized in presentation to leaders or key supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-social_work_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-social_work-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The SWOT analysis in social work practice framework is beneficial because it helps organizations decide whether or not an objective is obtainable and therefore enables organizations to set achievable goals, objectives, and steps to further the social change or community development effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benefits_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-benefits-13">[13]</a></sup> It enables organizers to take visions and produce practical and efficient outcomes that effect long-lasting change, and it helps organizations gather meaningful information to maximize their potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benefits_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-benefits-13">[13]</a></sup> Completing a SWOT analysis is a useful process regarding the consideration of key organizational priorities, such as gender and cultural diversity and fundraising objectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_agency_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-change_agency-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SWOT_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some findings from Menon et al. (1999)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and Hill and Westbrook (1997)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> have suggested that SWOT may harm performance and that "no-one subsequently used the outputs within the later stages of the strategy".</p>
<p>Other critiques include the misuse of the SWOT analysis as a technique that can be quickly designed without critical thought leading to a misrepresentation of strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats within an organization's internal and external surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWOT_does_not_need_to_be_recalled_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-SWOT_does_not_need_to_be_recalled-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another limitation includes the development of a SWOT analysis simply to defend previously decided goals and objectives. This misuse leads to limitations on brainstorming possibilities and "real" identification of barriers. This misuse also places the organization’s interest above the well-being of the community. Further, a SWOT analysis should be developed as a collaborative with a variety of contributions made by participants including community members. The design of a SWOT analysis by one or two community workers is limiting to the realities of the forces, specifically external factors, and devalues the possible contributions of community members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-use_and_misuse_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-use_and_misuse-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
