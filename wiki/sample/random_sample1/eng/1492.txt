<p>"<b>Sk8er Boi</b>" (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔɪ/ 'oy' in 'boy'">ɔɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, "skater boy") is a song by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a>-<a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a> <a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne" title="Avril Lavigne">Avril Lavigne</a>, released as the second single from her debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Let_Go_(Avril_Lavigne_album)" title="Let Go (Avril Lavigne album)">Let Go</a></i> (2002). It was written by Avril Lavigne and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Matrix_(music_producer)" title="The Matrix (music producer)">The Matrix</a> (Scott Spock, <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Christy" title="Lauren Christy">Lauren Christy</a>, and Graham Edwards), and produced by The Matrix. The song is a <a href="/wiki/Power_pop" title="Power pop">power pop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a> track, which lyrically, tells a story told from the singer's viewpoint about her rocker boyfriend and a girl he knew in high school who rejected him because he was a skateboarder and she was a snob.</p>
<p>The song received critical appreciation, with most commending its hook, calling the song "funny" and "clever". It also received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy" title="Grammy">Grammy</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Female_Rock_Vocal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Female Rock Vocal Performance">Best Female Rock Vocal Performance</a> at the 2003 edition. Commercially, "Sk8er Boi" was a success, reaching the top-ten in more than ten countries, such as Australia, New Zealand, the United Kingdom and the United States (becoming Lavigne's second top ten single on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart).</p>
<p>The music video for the song, directed by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lawrence" title="Francis Lawrence">Francis Lawrence</a>, features a concert on a city street with Lavigne singing on the hood of a car with a crowd rocking out around her. It was a success on <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Request_Live" title="Total Request Live">TRL</a></i> and was voted one of the best music videos of the decade by <a href="/wiki/BT_TV" title="BT TV">BT TV</a>. The song was featured in all of the concerts and tours held by Lavigne, since her first, "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Avril_Lavigne_concert_tours#Try_to_Shut_Me_Up_Tour" title="List of Avril Lavigne concert tours">Try to Shut Me Up Tour</a>" (2002-2003) until her latest, "<a href="/wiki/The_Avril_Lavigne_Tour" title="The Avril Lavigne Tour">The Avril Lavigne Tour</a>" (2013-2014).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_release">Background and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After being signed to <a href="/wiki/Arista_Records" title="Arista Records">Arista Records</a> in November 2000 upon the authorization of the label's CEO, <a href="/wiki/L.A._Reid" title="L.A. Reid">Antonio "L.A." Reid</a>, Lavigne moved to New York with the assistance of Reid. There, she began working on her debut album, <i>Let Go</i>, collaborating with a host of prime songwriters and producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtvnews1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtvnews1-5">[5]</a></sup> While being on the verge of getting dropped off Arista, after an unsuccessful year of writing, Lavigne came to the attention of the three-piece production team <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Matrix_(record_production_team)" title="The Matrix (record production team)">The Matrix</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thematrix_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-thematrix-6">[6]</a></sup> who later discovered that she wanted songs with <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> inclinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ew1-7">[7]</a></sup> After writing "<a href="/wiki/Complicated_(Avril_Lavigne_song)" title="Complicated (Avril Lavigne song)">Complicated</a>" together, L.A. Reid agreed with the musical direction Lavigne and The Matrix were taking, and sent Lavigne back to The Matrix to work with them on more songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matrix_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matrix-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the sessions, they wrote 10 songs, with "Sk8er Boi" being one of the six songs on the album's final track list. After the huge success of "Complicated", "Sk8er Boi" was selected as the album's second single, being the more controversial choice, according to L.A. Reid: "Some people just really didn't get that. And with the first video, there was some concern that maybe because it's so young and so playful, it might alienate more serious music lovers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ewBoy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ewBoy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>"Sk8er Boi" was written by <a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne" title="Avril Lavigne">Avril Lavigne</a>, while the production team <a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_(production_team)" title="The Matrix (production team)">The Matrix</a> (consisting of <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Christy" title="Lauren Christy">Lauren Christy</a>, Graham Edwards and Scott Spock) co-wrote, arranged and produced the track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discogs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-discogs-10">[10]</a></sup> "Sk8er Boi" is a <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sput_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sput-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Power_pop" title="Power pop">power pop</a> song<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-13">[13]</a></sup> about a snobby girl who rejects a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skateboarder" title="Skateboarder">skateboarder</a> who has a crush on her (despite returning his feelings), letting her friends' prejudices sway her decision-making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pluggedin_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pluggedin-14">[14]</a></sup> Later on, he becomes a superstar musician (playing guitar on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>) while she ultimately ends up as a young single mom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song's lyrics, including the line "He was a punk/She did ballet/What more can I say," were criticized. Christina Saraceno of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a></i> wrote that on "Sk8er Boi", "she shows her lyrical shortcomings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicreview_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicreview-2">[2]</a></sup> A poll on the website <i>MagPieMusic.com</i> voted the song's lines "He was a boy, she was a girl. Can I make it any more obvious?" at fourth place, in its list of "Worst Lyrics of All Time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Sk8er Boi" received critical acclaim for its production. In a review for the album <i>Let Go</i>, Christina Saraceno of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> called "Sk8er Boi" a "terrific power pop bounce", highlighting the song as a "track pick".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicreview_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicreview-2">[2]</a></sup> Brendan Schroer of <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnikmusic" title="Sputnikmusic">Sputnikmusic</a></i> praised the track, writing that Lavigne "brings another injection of infectious vocal work, peppy but not overbearing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sput_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-sput-12">[12]</a></sup> Nick Reynolds of <i><a href="/wiki/BBC_Music" title="BBC Music">BBC Music</a></i> called it "brilliant", considering the song "a classic high energy pop song with crunchy guitars and a great hook." He also praised its tale, calling it "as slick and clever as an episode of <a href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer">Buffy</a>. It bowls you over with its energy and sticks in your mind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-15">[15]</a></sup> Pat Blashill of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> agreed, calling it "seventeen-year-old Lavigne's signature moment," further adding: "Over a rush of nouveau-punk guitar chords, she narrates a funny story line, but none of it would matter if Lavigne didn't have a voice, equal parts baby girl and husky siren, that seems capable of setting off car alarms several city blocks away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 2011 <i><a href="/wiki/AOL_Radio" title="AOL Radio">AOL Radio</a></i> listener's poll, "Sk8er Boi" was voted Lavigne's fourth best song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radioblog_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-radioblog-18">[18]</a></sup> Robert Copsey of <i><a href="/wiki/Digital_Spy" title="Digital Spy">Digital Spy</a></i> listed Lavigne's 7 best singles of all time, placing "Sk8er Boi" at number 4, saying: "Lavigne proved she was more than a one-hit wonder with her second single 'Sk8er Boi'. Despite the rebellious spelling of the title it turned out to be another solid effort, telling the tale of a girl who rejects a baggy-clothed boy who eventually goes on to be world-famous."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dslist_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-dslist-19">[19]</a></sup> Bill Lamb of <i><a href="/wiki/About.com" title="About.com">About.com</a></i> placed the song at number 2, writing: "It has been derided in the past as being too naive lyrically. However, with the benefit of hindsight, it seems perfectly pitched to her audience and the point in her career. The power pop hook of the song sticks strongly in your head. Later perky pop excursions still pale next to this one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-about.com-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song followed the success of Lavigne's debut single, "<a href="/wiki/Complicated_(Avril_Lavigne_song)" title="Complicated (Avril Lavigne song)">Complicated</a>", which topped many charts worldwide and reached the top-ten in others. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, "Sk8er Boi" debuted at its peak position, number three, and remained a further week there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aria_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-aria-21">[21]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, the song managed to spend 9 weeks until it reached the peak position, number two, on 8 December 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nz_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nz-22">[22]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, the song managed to reach the top position, becoming her second consecutive number-one single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, "Sk8er Boi" debuted at number 72 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> issue date 14 September 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> while on the issue date 2 November 2002, the song peaked at number 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It became Lavigne's second consecutive top-ten hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-13">[13]</a></sup> The song also topped <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a>'</i>s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_40_Mainstream" title="Top 40 Mainstream">Top 40 Mainstream</a> airplay chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb-27">[27]</a></sup> As of July 2013, <i>Sk8er Boi</i> had sold 755,000 digital copies in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the song was also a success, reaching number eight on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> on 28 December 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uk_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-uk-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Sk8er Boi" was nominated and won many awards. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_2003" title="Grammy Awards of 2003">2003 Grammy Awards edition</a>, Lavigne received 5 nominations, including <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Female_Rock_Vocal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Female Rock Vocal Performance">Best Female Rock Vocal Performance</a> for "Sk8er Boi", but lost it to <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Crow" title="Sheryl Crow">Sheryl Crow</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Steve_McQueen_(song)" title="Steve McQueen (song)">Steve McQueen</a>". The song won "Favorite Song" at the <a href="/wiki/2003_Kids%27_Choice_Awards" title="2003 Kids' Choice Awards">2003 Kids' Choice Awards</a>, "Choice Music Single" at the <a href="/wiki/Teen_Choice_Awards" title="Teen Choice Awards">2003 Teen Choice Awards</a> and "Best Pop Song" at "Socan Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radioblog_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-radioblog-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> was directed by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lawrence" title="Francis Lawrence">Francis Lawrence</a> and premiered on 22 August 2002 on <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Request_Live" title="Total Request Live">TRL</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The video shot to number-one on TRL on its second day of the countdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The video was considered the third 'Best music video of the decade' in the UK by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BT_Vision" title="BT Vision">BT Vision</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btvision_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-btvision-32">[32]</a></sup> while <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ET_Canada" title="ET Canada">ET Canada</a></i> ranked the video at number 2 on her "Top 10 Best Music Videos".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcanada_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-etcanada-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The video centers around an impromptu concert held by Lavigne in a Los Angeles street intersection. It begins by showing multiple people of Lavigne's band promoting the concert in advance via graffiti, flyers, email and posters. Lavigne and her band arrive at the location and begin performing on the roof of their cars; a crowd quickly gathers. Towards the end of the song, police cars and a helicopter arrive to disperse the crowd, while Lavigne uses her guitar to break the car windscreen. Lavigne then throws the guitar onto the road and looks up at the helicopter, where a shot was taken for the single's artwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions_and_appearances_in_other_media">Cover versions and appearances in other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk8er_Boi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cover versions and appearances in other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> optioned the song for adaptation into a feature film, hiring writer/producer <a href="/wiki/David_Zabel" title="David Zabel">David Zabel</a> to adapt its words. The film would focus on the two teens from different backgrounds and the social constraints in which they find themselves. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-bb-27">[27]</a></sup> However, as of April 2008, the film has apparently been abandoned or is in <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">development hell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurodance" title="Eurodance">Eurodance</a> group <a href="/wiki/Cascada" title="Cascada">Cascada</a> recorded a dance cover of the song for the European and Japanese editions of their 2007 album <i><a href="/wiki/Perfect_Day_(Cascada_album)" title="Perfect Day (Cascada album)">Perfect Day</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidz_Bop_Kids" title="Kidz Bop Kids">Kidz Bop Kids</a> also did a rendition of the track for their album <a href="/wiki/Kidz_Bop_4" title="Kidz Bop 4">Kidz Bop 4</a> (2003), with an accompanying music video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>A cover version of the song by Angela Michael appears in the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Elite_Beat_Agents" title="Elite Beat Agents">Elite Beat Agents</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The song is also included in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SingStar_Pop" title="SingStar Pop">SingStar Pop</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> game<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and another cover version is featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Revolution" title="Rock Revolution">Rock Revolution</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Konami" title="Konami">Konami</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The song was also used in a montage of funny rollerskates and skateboard clips in an edition of <i><a href="/wiki/America%27s_Funniest_Home_Videos" title="America's Funniest Home Videos">America's Funniest Home Videos</a></i>. "Sk8er Boi" was featured in the television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_Case_(TV_series)" title="Cold Case (TV series)">Cold Case</a></i> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Season_3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Season 3 (page does not exist)">Season 3</a>, "The Promise", October 2, 2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2008, <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> included the song in the episode "Get Some" of the miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_Kill_(TV_series)" title="Generation Kill (TV series)">Generation Kill</a></i>. The song is sung by Cpl. Josh Ray Person as he is urinating in the desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Filipinos" title="Filipinos">Filipino</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Kean_Cipriano" title="Kean Cipriano">Kean Cipriano</a> covered the song for his performance in the <a href="/wiki/Your_Face_Sounds_Familiar_(Philippines_season_2)" title="Your Face Sounds Familiar (Philippines season 2)">second season</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Your_Face_Sounds_Familiar_(Philippine_TV_series)" title="Your Face Sounds Familiar (Philippine TV series)">Your Face Sounds Familiar</a></i>, in which he impersonates Lavigne.</p>
