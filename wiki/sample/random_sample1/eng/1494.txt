<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Social justice warrior</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"SJW" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/SJW_(disambiguation)" title="SJW (disambiguation)">SJW (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>"<b>Social justice warrior</b>" (commonly abbreviated <b>SJW</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> term for an individual promoting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socially_progressive" title="Socially progressive">socially progressive</a> views,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_Johnson_2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Johnson_2016-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">multiculturalism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Identity_politics" title="Identity politics">identity politics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The accusation of being an SJW carries implications of pursuing personal validation rather than any deep-seated conviction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heronbelford_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-heronbelford-4">[4]</a></sup> and being engaged in disingenuous social justice arguments or <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activism</a> to raise personal reputation, also known as <a href="/wiki/Virtue_signalling" title="Virtue signalling">virtue signalling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ViceAllegra_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ViceAllegra-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phrase originated in the late 20th century as a neutral or positive term for people engaged in <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> activism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2011 when the term first appeared on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> it changed from a primarily positive term to an overwhelmingly negative one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Gamergate_controversy" title="Gamergate controversy">Gamergate controversy</a>, the negative connotation gained increased use, and was particularly aimed at those espousing views adhering to <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberalism</a>, cultural inclusiveness, or feminism, as well as views deemed to be <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">politically correct</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_Johnson_2016_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Johnson_2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term has entered <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>, including a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a> released in 2014 titled <i>Social Justice Warriors</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parreno_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-parreno-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technologytell_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-technologytell-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breiner_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-breiner-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">Social justice</a>.</div>
<p>Dating back to 1824, the term "social justice" refers to justice on a societal level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Abby Ohlheiser wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> that "social-justice warrior" or variations thereof had been used as a laudatory phrase in the past, and provided an example dating to 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> She quoted Katherine Martin, the head of U.S. dictionaries at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a>, who said, "All of the examples I've seen until quite recently are lionizing the person."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> According to <i>The Washington Post</i>, use of the phrase in a positive manner continued from the 1990s through the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> At the time of the article's publication in October 2015, Martin said "lexicographers there haven't done a full search for its earliest citation" of the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pejorative_use">Pejorative use</span></h2>
<p>Katherine Martin says that the term switched from primarily positive to overwhelmingly negative around 2011, when it was first used as an insult on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> The same year an <a href="/wiki/Urban_Dictionary" title="Urban Dictionary">Urban Dictionary</a> entry for the term also appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> The term's negative use became mainstream due to the <a href="/wiki/Gamergate_controversy" title="Gamergate controversy">Gamergate controversy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sarahjeong_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sarahjeong-10">[10]</a></sup> emerging as the favoured term of Gamergate proponents to describe their ideological opponents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_culture" title="Internet culture">Internet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_culture" title="Video game culture">video game culture</a> the phrase is broadly associated with the Gamergate controversy and wider <a href="/wiki/Culture_war" title="Culture war">culture war</a> fallout, including the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Sad_Puppies" title="Sad Puppies">Sad Puppies</a> campaign that affected the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_Johnson_2016_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Johnson_2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ViceAllegra_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ViceAllegra-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPoGamergate_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaPoGamergate-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SlateWaldman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SlateWaldman-13">[13]</a></sup> Usage of the term as a pejorative was popularized on websites <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">Reddit</a> and <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">4chan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxhill_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-maxhill-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Use of the term has been described as attempting to degrade the motivations of the person accused of being an SJW, implying that their motives are "for personal validation rather than out of any deep-seated conviction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heronbelford_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-heronbelford-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The negative connotation was particularly aimed at those espousing views adhering to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Progressivism" title="Social Progressivism">social progressivism</a>, cultural inclusiveness, or <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_Johnson_2016_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eric_Johnson_2016-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/Vice_(magazine)" title="Vice (magazine)">Vice</a></i>, the accusation of being an SJW implies that a person is engaging in disingenuous social justice arguments or activism to raise his or her personal reputation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ViceAllegra_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ViceAllegra-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Vice</i> observed: "It's awfully convenient to have a term at the ready to dismiss women who bring up sexism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ViceAllegra_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ViceAllegra-5">[5]</a></sup> The magazine assessed the use of the term: "The problem is, that's not a real category of people. It's simply a way to dismiss anyone who brings up social justice—and often those people are feminists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ViceAllegra_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ViceAllegra-5">[5]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/David_A._French" title="David A. French">David French</a>, the aims of social justice warriors are opposed to those of <a href="/wiki/Christian_right" title="Christian right">Christian conservatives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"the 'social justice warrior,' i.e., the stereotype of the feminist as unreasonable, sanctimonious, biased, and self-aggrandizing."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;"> —<i><a href="/wiki/New_Literary_History" title="New Literary History">New Literary History</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selisker_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-selisker-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The term is commonly used by participants in online discussion in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criticism_of_feminism" title="Criticism of feminism">criticism of feminism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selisker_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-selisker-16">[16]</a></sup> An article in <i><a href="/wiki/New_Literary_History" title="New Literary History">New Literary History</a></i> described their behavior patterns on the Internet: "they often make personal criticisms of what they see as a type: the 'social justice warrior,' i.e., the stereotype of the feminist as unreasonable, sanctimonious, biased, and self-aggrandizing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selisker_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-selisker-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2015, the derogatory term "Social Justice Warrior" was one of several new words and phrases added to Oxford Dictionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canyouusethat_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-canyouusethat-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeoxford_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeoxford-18">[18]</a></sup> In discussing the term's origin, Martin outlined the similarity with the pejorative use of "political correctness" to denigrate something, stating that "the perceived orthodoxy [of progressive politics] has prompted a backlash among people who feel their speech is being policed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some conservative outlets have described <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>'s actions and policies as social justice of the right. Stephen L. Miller, in an article for the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i>, wrote that Donald Trump is in fact the most politically correct candidate running for the presidency in 2016 and that his followers are "neo-nationalist culture warriors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-miller-19">[19]</a></sup> Daniel Payne writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Federalist_(website)" title="The Federalist (website)">The Federalist</a></i> website listed three general attributes of a social justice warrior and noted that they are attributes of Donald Trump, who has proven to be "the platonic social justice warrior candidate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payne_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-payne-20">[20]</a></sup> In an article for <i><a href="/wiki/Reason_(magazine)" title="Reason (magazine)">Reason</a></i> magazine, Elizabeth Nolan Brown compared social justice on the left and right and found many similarities such as victimhood, outrage and portraying the other side as bullying and evil and their side as the truly oppressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rod_Dreher" title="Rod Dreher">Rod Dreher</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Conservative" title="The American Conservative">The American Conservative</a></i> about Trump being the first social justice warrior presidential candidate: "Trump is also a man who constantly paints himself as a victim...So I wonder: Is Trump the first Social Justice Warrior presidential candidate, in the sense of weaponizing grievance in a way similar to that done by left-wing campus protesters?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dreher_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dreher-22">[22]</a></sup> Jay Caruso wrote an article for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redstate" title="Redstate">Redstate</a> on the "good little social justice warriors" who responded a vague discussion of how difficult it would be to remove Trump from office if elected with a Republican-controlled Congress with outraged accusations that Trump was being threatened with assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caruso_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-caruso-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span></h2>
<p>In May 2014, the concept was incorporated into a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a> titled <i>Social Justice Warriors</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parreno_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-parreno-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technologytell_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-technologytell-7">[7]</a></sup> Developed by Nonadecimal Creative, <i>Social Justice Warriors</i> involved the concept of debating online against <a href="/wiki/Internet_troll" title="Internet troll">Internet trolls</a> who make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racist" title="Racist">racist</a> and other provocative comments by choosing from different responses such as "'dismember their claims with your logic,' rebroadcast their message to be attacked by others, or go for the personal attack."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technologytell_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-technologytell-7">[7]</a></sup> Users were able to select a <a href="/wiki/Character_class" title="Character class">character class</a>; and gameplay involved changes to user meters of <i>Sanity</i> and <i>Reputation</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technologytell_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-technologytell-7">[7]</a></sup> The game became available on the computer platform <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a> in February 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breiner_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-breiner-8">[8]</a></sup> Game creator Eric Ford explained that the game was designed to foster <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">critical thinking</a> and was not "intended to suggest that racist, sexist, or other offensive comments shouldn't be confronted online. The goal is to encourage critical thinking on how it can be done more effectively, and at less cost to the real-world social justice warriors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breiner_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-breiner-8">[8]</a></sup> He commented: "Once you've embarked down the path of correcting every incorrect statement an anonymous stranger is making online, the only inevitable outcomes are that your patience is exhausted by frustration, your reputation is obliterated by the trolls' defamation or your own actions, or you give up in disgust."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breiner_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-breiner-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actress Caitlin Barlow described her character on the 2016 U.S. comedy television series <i><a href="/wiki/Teachers_(2016_U.S._TV_series)" title="Teachers (2016 U.S. TV series)">Teachers</a></i> as a social justice warrior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ninametz_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ninametz-24">[24]</a></sup> Barlow explained: "I play Cecilia Cannon, who is a super-crunchy hippie social justice warrior who is always trying to save the world, whether people care or not. And she's always pushing her left-wing agenda on her students."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ninametz_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ninametz-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> journalists Lesley Goldberg and Kate Stanhope noted in March 2016 that actress Isabella Gomez was cast in the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> remake of <i><a href="/wiki/One_Day_at_a_Time" title="One Day at a Time">One Day at a Time</a></i> and portrayed Elena, a character content to self-identify as a social justice warrior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hollywoodreporter_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-hollywoodreporter-25">[25]</a></sup> Goldberg and Stanhope wrote: "A proud nerd, idealist and social justice warrior, Elena is opinionated and not afraid to speak her mind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hollywoodreporter_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-hollywoodreporter-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>While promoting his film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Inferno_(film)" title="The Green Inferno (film)">The Green Inferno</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Eli_Roth" title="Eli Roth">Eli Roth</a> said "I wanted to write a movie that was about modern activism. I see that a lot of people want to care and want to help, but in general I feel like people don't really want to inconvenience their own lives. And I saw a lot of people just reacting to things on social media. These social justice warriors. 'This is wrong, this is wrong, this is wrong.' And they're just tweeting and retweeting. They're not actually doing anything. Or you see people get involved in a cause that they don't really know a lot about and they go crazy about it. I wanted to make a movie about kids like that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
