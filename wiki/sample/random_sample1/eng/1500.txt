<p><i><b>St. Anger</b></i> is the eighth studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> band <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>, released on June 5, 2003, by <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a>. It was the band's last album released through Elektra, thus marking the end of the second-longest timespan between studio albums from Metallica, with nearly six years between the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Reload_(Metallica_album)" title="Reload (Metallica album)">Reload</a></i> and this album. It is also the final collaboration between Metallica and producer <a href="/wiki/Bob_Rock" title="Bob Rock">Bob Rock</a>, whose relationship began with the band's fifth studio album, 1991's <i><a href="/wiki/Metallica_(album)" title="Metallica (album)">Metallica</a></i>. <i>St. Anger</i> was originally intended for release on June 10, 2003, but was released five days earlier due to concerns over unlicensed distribution through <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer file sharing</a> networks. The <i>St. Anger</i> sessions also mark the only time Rock played when the music was being recorded, filling in for the departed bassist <a href="/wiki/Jason_Newsted" title="Jason Newsted">Jason Newsted</a>.</p>
<p>Newsted left the band prior to the initial sessions for the album, and the band decided to wait until the album was recorded before finding a new permanent member. Recording of the album started initially on April 23, 2001, but was postponed indefinitely when rhythm guitarist and singer <a href="/wiki/James_Hetfield" title="James Hetfield">James Hetfield</a> entered rehab for "<a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a> and other addictions". <i>St. Anger</i> is often recognized for being a radical musical departure from Metallica's earlier work; it features a modern heavy metal style, raw production, and no <a href="/wiki/Guitar_solo" title="Guitar solo">guitar solos</a>. The artwork was created by <a href="/wiki/Pushead" title="Pushead">Pushead</a>, who had previously collaborated with the band.</p>
<p>Metallica spent the next couple of years touring to promote the album. <i>St. Anger</i> debuted at the top of sales charts in 14 countries, including the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>. Upon its release, it received mixed reviews. In 2004, the album's lead single, "<a href="/wiki/St._Anger_(song)" title="St. Anger (song)">St. Anger</a>", won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Metal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance">Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance</a>. The album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) for shipping two million copies in the US. <i>St. Anger</i> has sold nearly six million copies worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background.2C_writing_and_recording">Background, writing and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Anger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background, writing and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metallica rented an old <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Presidio_of_San_Francisco" title="Presidio of San Francisco">Presidio of San Francisco</a> and converted it into a makeshift studio in January 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Some_Kinda_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Some_Kinda-6">[6]</a></sup> As plans were being made to enter the studio to write and record its first album in nearly five years, the band postponed the recording because of the departure of bassist <a href="/wiki/Jason_Newsted" title="Jason Newsted">Jason Newsted</a>. He left Metallica on January 17, 2001, stating his departure was due to "private and personal reasons and the physical damage I have done to myself over the years while playing the music that I love."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jason_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jason-7">[7]</a></sup> Uncomfortable with immediately writing and recording with a new bassist, Metallica opted to include Bob Rock as bassist. The band stated they would find another bass player upon the album's completion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Some_Kinda_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Some_Kinda-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2001, recording came to a halt when <a href="/wiki/James_Hetfield" title="James Hetfield">James Hetfield</a> entered rehab for <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a> and other undisclosed addictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Hetfield returned to the band in April of the next year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but was only allowed to work on the album from 12:00 to 4:00 PM. Due to his personal issues, as well as Metallica's internal struggles, the band hired a personal enhancement coach, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Towle" title="Phil Towle">Phil Towle</a>, to help them. This, and the recording of the album, was documented by filmmakers <a href="/wiki/Joe_Berlinger" title="Joe Berlinger">Joe Berlinger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Sinofsky" title="Bruce Sinofsky">Bruce Sinofsky</a>. The band's recording process was filmed over the course of three years. Subsequent to the album's release, Berlinger and Sinofsky released the edited material as the film <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Kind_of_Monster_(film)" title="Some Kind of Monster (film)">Some Kind of Monster</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monster_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-monster-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hetfield stated that the album was written with "a lot of passion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-April03_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-April03-11">[11]</a></sup> He said, "There's two years of condensed emotion in this. We've gone through a lot of personal changes, struggles, epiphanies, it's deep. It's so deep lyrically and musically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-April03_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-April03-11">[11]</a></sup> [<i>St. Anger</i>] is just the best that it can be from us right now."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_it_can_be_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Best_it_can_be-12">[12]</a></sup> The band purposely wanted a raw sound on the album so that Rock did not polish the sound while mixing. The band desired the raw sound because of the depth of the emotion they felt and did not want to "mess with it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_it_can_be_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Best_it_can_be-12">[12]</a></sup> Rock commented, "I wanted to do something to shake up radio and the way everything else sounds. To me, this album sounds like four guys in a garage getting together and writing rock songs. There was really no time to get amazing performances out of James. We liked the raw performances. And we didn't do what everyone does and what I've been guilty of for a long time, which is tuning vocals. We just did it, boom, and that was it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rock_speaks_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rock_speaks-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Guitarist <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Hammett" title="Kirk Hammett">Kirk Hammett</a> commented on the lack of guitar solos on <i>St. Anger</i>, a departure from what Metallica had done in the past: "We wanted to preserve the sound of all four of us in a room just jamming. We tried to put guitar solos on, but we kept on running into this problem. It really sounded like an afterthought." Hammett said that he was happy with the final product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk_solos_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk_solos-14">[14]</a></sup> Rock stated, "We made a promise to ourselves that we'd only keep stuff that had integrity. We didn't want to make a theatrical statement by adding overdubs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rock_speaks_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rock_speaks-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Drummer <a href="/wiki/Lars_Ulrich" title="Lars Ulrich">Lars Ulrich</a> achieved a unique sound on <i>St. Anger</i> by turning off the snares on his <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drum</a> resulting in a drum tone with far more "ring" than is usual in rock and metal. This sound received much backlash from fans and critics alike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snare_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Snare-15">[15]</a></sup> Ulrich said, "One day I forgot to turn the snare on because I wasn't thinking about this stuff. At the playbacks, I decided I was really liking what I was hearing—it had a different ambience. It sang back to me in a beautiful way." Regarding the backlash about the sound, he stated, "It's crazy, that kind of closed-mindedness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snare_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Snare-15">[15]</a></sup> Rock said, "I would say I've only [done something] this brutal [sounding] when I've done demos. It probably sounds heavier because it's Metallica, but really this was a 15-minutes-on-the-drum-sound type of thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <i>St. Anger</i> was completed, Metallica hired a new, permanent bassist. In February 2003, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Trujillo" title="Robert Trujillo">Robert Trujillo</a> joined the band. He appears on the footage of studio rehearsals of <i>St. Anger</i> in its entirety, which was included on DVD in the album package.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Some_Kinda_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Some_Kinda-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork">Artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Anger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brian "<a href="/wiki/Pushead" title="Pushead">Pushead</a>" Schroeder designed the album cover and interior artwork for <i>St. Anger</i>. Pushead has designed a number of items for Metallica in the past, including liner artwork of <i><a href="/wiki/...And_Justice_for_All_(album)" title="...And Justice for All (album)">...And Justice for All</a></i>, several single covers, and many T-shirts; however, the album marks his first studio album cover art for the band. Originally, according to Metallica's official website, four different limited color variations of the cover were planned, but the idea was eventually scrapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Anger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>St. Anger</i> was released on June 5, 2003. It was originally scheduled for June 10, but due to Metallica's previous battle with <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a>, and fear that it would be released illegally onto <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer file sharing</a> networks, the band pushed the release date ahead by five days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A special edition of the album was released with a bonus <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, featuring live, in-the-studio rehearsals of all of the <i>St. Anger</i> tracks. First week sales of the album were 417,000 copies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VH1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-20">[20]</a></sup> and it debuted at number 1 on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, as well as in 30 other countries around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2004, Metallica won the award for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_Metal_Performance" title="Best Metal Performance">Best Metal Performance</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/St._Anger_(song)" title="St. Anger (song)">title track</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>After <i>St. Anger</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release, Metallica embarked on a tour that lasted nearly two years. The first leg was the U.S. 2003 <i>Summer Sanitarium</i> tour with support from: <a href="/wiki/Limp_Bizkit" title="Limp Bizkit">Limp Bizkit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deftones" title="Deftones">Deftones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linkin_Park" title="Linkin Park">Linkin Park</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mudvayne" title="Mudvayne">Mudvayne</a>. After <i>Summer Sanitarium</i>, the band began the <i><a href="/wiki/Madly_in_Anger_with_the_World_Tour" title="Madly in Anger with the World Tour">Madly in Anger with the World Tour</a></i> with support from <a href="/wiki/Godsmack" title="Godsmack">Godsmack</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Slipknot_(band)" title="Slipknot (band)">Slipknot</a> on certain European dates), which lasted until late 2004. The <i>St. Anger</i> songs "<a href="/wiki/Frantic_(song)" title="Frantic (song)">Frantic</a>", "St. Anger", "Dirty Window" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Unnamed_Feeling" title="The Unnamed Feeling">The Unnamed Feeling</a>" were performed frequently during the tour. "Sweet Amber" and "<a href="/wiki/Some_Kind_of_Monster_(song)" title="Some Kind of Monster (song)">Some Kind of Monster</a>" were also played live, but not as often as other songs on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tour_timeline_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tour_timeline-23">[23]</a></sup> The album tracks were altered when played live; sometimes they were shortened, or in some cases a guitar solo was added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Sometimes, only one song from the album was played live. By 2009, the songs from <i>St. Anger</i> were completely absent from Metallica's set lists. The last time any song was performed from the album on a major tour was "Frantic" on October 21, 2008, in Glendale, Arizona,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tour_timeline_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tour_timeline-23">[23]</a></sup> although "Dirty Window" and "Frantic" were performed again on December 10, 2011, during the last concert of Metallica's special and private 30th Anniversary Tour, in San Francisco, California. "St. Anger" was also played again during the "Metallica by Request" tour in 2014 when it was voted by the fans. In October 2007, "All Within My Hands" was performed live for the first time, <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustically</a>, at both nights of the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_School_Benefit" title="Bridge School Benefit">Bridge School Benefit</a> concerts.</p>
<p>Metallica also released four singles from <i>St. Anger</i>. The order of the releases was: "St. Anger", "Frantic", "The Unnamed Feeling" and "Some Kind of Monster". On the U.S. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Mainstream_Rock_Tracks" title="Hot Mainstream Rock Tracks">Mainstream Rock</a></i> chart, these singles charted at number 2, number 21, number 28 and number 18, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singles_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singles-25">[25]</a></sup> Promotional music videos were also made for all four of the songs. These videos can be found on Metallica's DVD video collection, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Videos_1989-2004" title="The Videos 1989-2004">The Videos 1989-2004</a></i>, and the video for "Some Kind of Monster" can also be found on the film <i>Some Kind of Monster</i>.</p>

<p><i>St. Anger</i> received mixed to positive reviews from critics; the album holds a score of 65 out of 100, based on 20 reviews, on review-aggregating website <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MC_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-MC-26">[26]</a></sup> Adrien Begrand of <i><a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a></i> took both sides, saying: "While it's an ungodly mess at times, what you hear on this album is a band playing with passion for the first time in years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Producer Bob Rock said that it was intended to sound like "a band jamming together in a garage for the first time, and the band just happened to be Metallica."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Some_Kinda_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Some_Kinda-6">[6]</a></sup> Talking about the album, Greg Kot of <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i> said, "It may be too late to rehabilitate Metallica's image, but once again, their music is all about bringing the carnage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blender_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blender-28">[28]</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i>, Ian Watson said that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...the songs are a stripped back, heroically brutal reflection of this fury. You get the sense that, as with their emotional selves, they've taken metal apart and started again from scratch. There's no space wasted here, no time for petty guitar solos or downtuned bass trickery, just a focused, relentless attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-NME-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Johnny Loftus of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> praised the album and described it as a "punishing, unflinching document of internal struggle—taking listeners inside the bruised yet vital body of Metallica, but ultimately revealing the alternately torturous and defiant demons that wrestle inside Hetfield's brain. <i>St. Anger</i> is an immediate record."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-27">[27]</a></sup> Barry Walter of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine also had a positive reaction to the direction taken in "St. Anger", stating: "No wonder there's an authenticity to <i>St. Anger's</i> fury that none of the band's rap-metal followers can touch." He also went further to note the lack of commercial influence and modern rock aspects of previous albums, continuing: "There's no radio-size, four-minute rock here, no pop-friendly choruses, no ballads, no solos, no wayward experimentation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although many reviews of <i>St. Anger</i> were positive, some reviewers had a strong distaste for the album. Brent DiCrescenzo of <i><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_(website)" title="Pitchfork (website)">Pitchfork</a></i> strongly disliked the album and criticized Ulrich and Hammett, saying that Ulrich was "playing a drum set consisting of steel drums, aluminum toms, programmed double kicks, and a broken church bell. The kit's high-end clamor ignored the basic principles of drumming: timekeeping," he added, "Hetfield and Hammett's guitars underwent more processing than cat food. When they both speedstrummed through 'St. Anger', and most other movements, [Hetfield and Hammett] seemed to overwhelm each other with different, terrible noise. Also the duration of most songs made it boring to hear them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Playlouder" title="Playlouder">Playlouder</a> reviewer William Luff cited the album's 75-minute length and sound ("a monolithic slab of noise") as reasoning that <i>St. Anger</i> was "just too dense and daunting to be truly enjoyable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playlouder_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-playlouder-38">[38]</a></sup> <i>PopMatters</i> reporter Michael Christopher said "<i>St. Anger</i> dispenses with the recent spate of radio friendly pleasantries in favor of pedal to the floor thrash, staggered and extended song structures, quick changes and a muddled production that tries to harken back to the <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_%27Em_All" title="Kill 'Em All">Kill 'Em All</a></i> days. All attempts fail miserably."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
