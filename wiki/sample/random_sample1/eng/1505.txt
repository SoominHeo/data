<p><b>SuicideGirls</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Online_community" title="Online community">online community</a> based website that revolves around <a href="/wiki/Pin-up_model" title="Pin-up model">pin-up</a> photography sets of models known as the Suicide Girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badger_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-badger-3">[3]</a></sup> The website was founded in 2001 by Selena Mooney ("Missy Suicide") and Sean Suhl ("Spooky").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-racked_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-racked-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of the site is only accessible to paying members. It offers members access to images provided by models and photographers worldwide, as well as personal profiles, blogging platforms and the option to join numerous groups based upon different interests. There is also an online merchandise store offering a range of clothing, books and DVDs.</p>
<p>Suicide Girls have appeared in a variety of media outlets including television shows and music videos as well as being portrayed by actresses in others, such as the character Dani California on the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Californication_(TV_series)" title="Californication (TV series)">Californication</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-rs-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001, Mooney returned to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> to study photography after working as director of technology at <a href="/wiki/Ticketmaster" title="Ticketmaster">Ticketmaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-racked_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-racked-4">[4]</a></sup> Inspired by <a href="/wiki/Bunny_Yeager" title="Bunny Yeager">Bunny Yeager</a>, Mooney began photographing her friends in the pinup style and wanted to create a website that featured her photographs as well as message boards and blog posts from the models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badger_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-badger-3">[3]</a></sup> Mooney's friend, Sean Suhl joined her and the two founded the website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-racked_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-racked-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> SuicideGirls was originally based in Portland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ObsceneButNotHeard_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ObsceneButNotHeard-1">[1]</a></sup> but relocated its operations to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> in 2003 to be closer to its distributor, label and publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oglive_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-oglive-8">[8]</a></sup> That same year, 70 models from the website appeared in a music video for the band <a href="/wiki/Probot" title="Probot">Probot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mooney has stated that the purpose of the site is to give women control over how their sexuality is depicted. The site is privately co-owned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trash_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-trash-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to Missy, the term "Suicide Girl" originally comes from <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Palahniuk" title="Chuck Palahniuk">Chuck Palahniuk</a>'s novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Survivor_(Chuck_Palahniuk_novel)" title="Survivor (Chuck Palahniuk novel)">Survivor</a></i> (1999): "It's the same with these suicide girls calling me up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oglive_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-oglive-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subu8_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-subu8-11">[11]</a></sup> Missy also states that the name describe girls who commit 'social suicide' by breaking away from the norm of society, and created the site 'as a place to celebrate beautiful women who choose not to fit into the norm and as a corner of the internet where outsiders could congregate and be appreciated for being themselves'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2005, SuicideGirls announced that it would remove a large number of images from its pages, in an effort to collaborate with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Justice_Department" title="U.S. Justice Department">U.S. Justice Department</a> standards at the time. The images involved depicted <a href="/wiki/Bondage_(BDSM)" title="Bondage (BDSM)">bondage</a>, weapons, or simulated blood. The Justice Department indicated that images of that type might be the subject of <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a> prosecutions. Although SuicideGirls was not mentioned as a target, they removed the images until the furor passed. In January 2007, the images were made visible again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-racked_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-racked-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2006, some of the Suicide Girls were featured in an episode of <a href="/wiki/CSI:_NY_(season_3)" title="CSI: NY (season 3)">CSI: NY</a> titled Oedipus Hex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csi_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-csi-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, it was reported that the website had 5 million monthly visitors, with 51 percent of them being female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-racked_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-racked-4">[4]</a></sup> SuicideGirls 15th anniversary was celebrated at its Peek-A-Boo burlesque show, which is a regular act at Pour Vous nightclub in Los Angeles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and was featured on the website of <a href="/wiki/Maxim_(magazine)" title="Maxim (magazine)"><i>Maxim</i></a> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>The website is an online community, formed around pin-up photosets of Suicide Girls.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photosets">Photosets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Photosets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Photosets are a collection of images ranging from fully clothed to fully nude that must share a theme or concept and take place in the same setting. Each photoset contains 40-60 images and is created by the model and photographer to portray images of 'alternative' beauty, showcasing each model's ideas regarding their own beauty.</p>
<p>As of May 2015, there were nearly 8 million images live on the site. Each day, a 'Set of the Day' is bought and featured, appearing on the front page, where official Suicide Girl status begins. The photographs are intended both as an <a href="/wiki/Homage_(arts)" title="Homage (arts)">homage</a> to classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pin-up" title="Pin-up">pin-up</a> art and a portrayal of images of alternative beauty.</p>
<p>The site hosts a collection of staff photographers, however anyone can submit photosets to the site. Actress <a href="/wiki/Paget_Brewster" title="Paget Brewster">Paget Brewster</a> has photographed models for the site,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> as have guitarist <a href="/wiki/Dave_Navarro" title="Dave Navarro">Dave Navarro</a> and singer <a href="/wiki/Mike_Doughty" title="Mike Doughty">Mike Doughty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suicidegirls.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-suicidegirls.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_features">Other features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The members and the models all have the option to create a personal profile, keep journals, upload their own photos and videos, and join public and private groups.</p>
<p>The site also features interviews conducted by members and a merchandise shop.</p>

<li><i>SuicideGirls: The First Tour</i> was self-produced and released on August 30, 2005 by <a href="/wiki/Epitaph_Records" title="Epitaph Records">Epitaph records</a>. It chronicles the lives of 10 performers on the first North American <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">Burlesque</a> Tour.</li>
<li><i>SuicideGirls: Italian Villa</i> was released on October 24, 2006. It features interviews and photo shoots of 15 European Suicide Girls.</li>
<li>The horror film <i>Suicide Girls Must Die!</i>, directed by Sawa Suicide, was released in certain theatres on March 12, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The film was released as <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">video on demand</a> on July 16, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>SuicideGirls: Guide to Living</i> was released on DVD and <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> on March 16, 2010 and features many Suicide Girls putting erotic twists on otherwise everyday activities.</li>
<li><i>SuicideGirls: UK Holiday</i> was released in September 2012 and is also available on both DVD and Blu-ray. It documents a week long stay in a converted mill within the UK countryside, featuring 30 Suicide Girls from across the globe.</li>
<li><i>SuicideGirls: Retrospective</i> was released on November 3, 2012 and was a collection of videos from the previous decade.</li>
<li><i>SuicideGirls: Relaunch</i> is the latest offering, released August 8, 2015. It chronicles the recent relaunch of the website, following co-owner Missy Suicide and her team to document the day-to-day operations of SuicideGirls.</li>
</ul>
<p><i>SuicideGirls: The First Tour</i>, <i>SuicideGirls:</i> <i>Italian Villa</i> and <i>SuicideGirls: Relaunch</i> all air on the US Cable network <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a> in regular rotations, since the years of their respective release dates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2013, Suicide Girls came to an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Akaneiro:_Demon_Hunters" title="Akaneiro: Demon Hunters">Akaneiro: Demon Hunters</a> game developer <a href="/wiki/Spicy_Horse" title="Spicy Horse">Spicy Horse</a> to use likenesses of their models in a <a href="/wiki/Freemium" title="Freemium">freemium</a> browser title called <i>BigHead BASH</i>. Players can purchase premium content for 220 in-game tokens each, to unlock 5 characters in total. The models featured are: Bob, Gogo, Milloux, Venom and Radeo.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blackheart_Burlesque">Blackheart Burlesque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Blackheart Burlesque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The SuicideGirls Blackheart <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">Burlesque</a> group first toured in 2003 and opened for <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N' Roses">Guns N' Roses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Courtney_Love" title="Courtney Love">Courtney Love</a>, before suspending the tour for nearly a decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badger_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-badger-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phx_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-phx-22">[22]</a></sup> The show returned in Fall 2013 and has toured across the US, Canada and Australia, selling out numerous shows in each country. The show has also visited the UK and Chile with its high-energy mix of classic and new burlesque dancing, choreographed by Manwe Sauls-Addison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phx_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-phx-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Blackheart Burlesque hosts a show of cult-classic numbers including performances based on <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Rocky_Horror_Picture_Show" title="The Rocky Horror Picture Show">The Rocky Horror Picture Show</a>, and pop culture references like <a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones" title="Game of Thrones">Game of Thrones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fifty_Shades_of_Grey" title="Fifty Shades of Grey">Fifty Shades of Grey</a>. As of 2016, the show is hosted by Sunny Suicide and Katherine Suicide and features a changing line-up of dancers. During the shows, there is an element of audience participation and guests are encouraged to take photos of the performers and share them on social media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballroom_Blitz">Ballroom Blitz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ballroom Blitz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The SuicideGirls Ballroom Blitz is a monthly rock and metal club night in the UK, taking place at Camden's <a href="/wiki/Electric_Ballroom" title="Electric Ballroom">Electric Ballroom</a>. It boasts various stage performances including fire performance, pole dancing and classic burlesque, alongside cage dancing and rock, metal and alternative music. Performances come from UK Suicide Girls and Suicide Girl Hopefuls, guest DJs and guest acts from dance and fire performance groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship">Censorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Censorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, a number of the paid models were reported to have resigned from the site or had their memberships revoked in connection with allegations of censorship and mistreatment of the models by the site's owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWOL_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AWOL-27">[27]</a></sup> Numerous members have reported that their journals and message board posts were removed because of bullying other members. This practice of deleting either objectionable content, disagreeable content, or membership altogether is referred to by Suicide Girls staffers as "zotting" and is implemented by the site's owners in the event that members are slanderous or abusive to other members.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusivity_agreement_and_lawsuits">Exclusivity agreement and lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Exclusivity agreement and lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A primary issue in the past has been SuicideGirls modeling contract, which prevented its models (including past models, for a time) from working for competing sites or agencies (specifically those dealing in nude photography or erotica).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defense_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Defense-28">[28]</a></sup> In response to this, the SuicideGirls website stated that only models "who have chosen to be involved in special projects" signed an exclusivity agreement in addition to their standard modeling contract barring them from working with direct competitors for a certain amount of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trash_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-trash-9">[9]</a></sup> SG replaced the contract with a <a class="external text" href="https://gmail123456.box.com/s/qbmj1f9pr3w8w8wzaj5e" rel="nofollow">model release</a> in 2006. Many models have received many mainstream modeling jobs from the exposure gained through SuicideGirls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-testimonial_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-testimonial-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the former models involved in the 2005 dispute are now involved with the competing sites <a href="/wiki/GodsGirls" title="GodsGirls">GodsGirls</a> and Deviant Nation. Gods Girls have been sued by SuicideGirls LLC for hiring models who were allegedly still under contract with SuicideGirls and for allegedly violating SuicideGirls trademarks. Several former models were also threatened with legal action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-x_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-x-31">[31]</a></sup> In November 2006, SuicideGirls fired one of their main photographers, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Warner" title="Philip Warner">Philip Warner</a>, (aka Lithium Picnic), for acting as the primary photographer for the website of former SuicideGirl Apnea. The termination was followed in February 2007 by a lawsuit by SuicideGirls against Warner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apneatic_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-apneatic-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> According to a press release by Warner and Apnea, as of February 2007, none of SuicideGirls LLC's lawsuits or threatened actions against former models or competing sites has resulted in a victory for the <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaintiff</a>. However, the legal expenses in the lawsuits have been costly and time-consuming for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defendants" title="Defendants">defendants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apneatic_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-apneatic-32">[32]</a></sup> In June 2008, Lithium Picnic and Apnea issued a press release stating "We all sat down together and worked out an agreement that is really fair to everyone... We want to make it clear that we 100% have no hostilities towards SuicideGirls in any way anymore, we all came to a really fair agreement over this dispute, and there were no bad people here, just mistakes and misunderstandings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_by_models">Criticism by models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Criticism by models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an article released in 2005, the alternative weekly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phoenix_(newspaper)" title="The Phoenix (newspaper)">The Boston Phoenix</a></i> reported on former models' dissatisfaction with company practices. Models interviewed referred to SuicideGirls president Sean Suhl as "verbally abusive" and an "active misogynist", and described the website as a "slap in the face to feminism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other allegations surrounding the SuicideGirls' administration have appeared in a number of established publications, including <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Press" title="New York Press">New York Press</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to statistics released by the website, in July 2005 one model left, followed by 11 in August, 25 in September, and 11 in October. According to former models interviewed in a feature piece by <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>'s magazine <i>Metro Active</i>, this was, in their opinion, due to the general homogenization of the site, "a process that alternative subcultures are unfortunately used to".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Job_loss">Job loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Job loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Olivia Black, a recent addition to the crew of Las Vegas' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_%26_Silver_Pawn_Shop" title="Gold &amp; Silver Pawn Shop">Gold &amp; Silver Pawn Shop</a>, and thus the <a href="/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="History (U.S. TV channel)">History Channel</a> TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Pawn_Stars" title="Pawn Stars">Pawn Stars</a></i>, was fired from the show on December 19, 2012 when her previous background as a SuicideGirl was revealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Richard_Prince">Richard Prince</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SuicideGirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Richard Prince">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, American artist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Prince" title="Richard Prince">Richard Prince</a> appropriated images from the Suicide Girls' <a href="/wiki/Instagram" title="Instagram">Instagram</a> printed them on canvas and added remarks into comment threads. The works were displayed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freize_Art_Fair" title="Freize Art Fair">Freize Art Fair</a> and one of the images was sold for $90,000. In response, the SuicideGirls sold prints of the images for $90 a piece with the proceeds going to charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
