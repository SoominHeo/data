<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_meaning">Origin and meaning</span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> estimates that the word "supercalifragilisticexpialidocious" was first attested in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The roots of the word have been defined<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> as follows: super- "above", cali- "beauty", fragilistic- "delicate", expiali- "to atone", and -docious "educable", with the sum of these parts signifying roughly "Atoning for educability through delicate beauty." According to the film, in which the word gained its popularity, it is defined as "something to say when you have nothing to say". However, it is commonly defined as "extraordinarily good" or "wonderful" as all references to the word in the film can be perceived as positive. <a href="/wiki/Dictionary.com" title="Dictionary.com">Dictionary.com</a> also notes that the word is "used as a nonsense word by children to express approval or to represent the longest word in English."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Sherman" title="Richard M. Sherman">Richard M. Sherman</a>, co-writer of the song with <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Sherman" title="Robert B. Sherman">his brother, Robert</a>, the word was one that the two knew in their youth. In an episode of the <i><a href="/wiki/Disney_Family_Album" title="Disney Family Album">Disney Family Album</a></i> featuring the story of the brother's careers, Richard Sherman stated, "we remembered this wonderful word from our childhood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2007 interview, Sherman indicated that the final version of the word was produced by the two brothers over the course of two weeks during the songwriting process, indicating only that the origins of the word were in their memories of creating <a href="/wiki/Double-talk" title="Double-talk">double-talk</a> words in their childhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supercalifragilisticexpialidocious was first added to the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed.com-2">[2]</a></sup> A well known <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">pun</a> plays on the word, by affirming that <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a> was a "super calloused fragile mystic hexed by halitosis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Supercalifragilistic" is an abbreviation of "supercalifragilisticexpialidocious,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> originating in 1966, two years after the word "supercalifragilisticexpialidocious" was popularized in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Story_context">Story context</span></h2>
<p>The song occurs in the chalk drawing outing animated sequence, just after Mary Poppins wins a <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse race</a>. Flush with her victory, she is immediately surrounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporter" title="Reporter">reporters</a> who pepper her with leading questions and they comment that she probably is at a loss for words. Mary disagrees, suggesting that at least one word is appropriate for the situation and begins the song.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Backwards_version">Backwards version</span></h2>
<p>During the song, Poppins says, "You know, you can say it backwards, which is 'dociousaliexpilisticfragicalirupes', but that's going a bit too far, don't you think?"</p>
<p>When the word is spelled backwards it actually becomes "suoicodilaipxecitsiligarfilacrepus", which is different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, her claim was not about <i>spelling</i> it backwards, but <i>saying</i> it backwards; if one breaks the word into several sections or <a href="/wiki/Foot_(prosody)" title="Foot (prosody)">prosodic feet</a> ("super-cali-fragi-listic-expi-ali-docious") and recites them in reverse sequence, and also modifies "super" to "rupes", one does come close to what Poppins said in the film.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins_(musical)" title="Mary Poppins (musical)">stage musical</a>, the word's actual spelling reversal<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is used, while Rapper <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a> said "dociousaliexpilisticfragicalisuper", which is the full prosody version, in his song "Buck 50".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_action">Legal action</span></h2>
<p>In 1965, the song was the subject of an unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> by songwriters <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Parker" title="Gloria Parker">Gloria Parker</a> and Barney Young against Wonderland Music, who published the version of the song from the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The plaintiffs alleged that it was a <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> of a 1951 song of their own called "Supercalafajalistickespeealadojus". Also known as "The Super Song", "Supercalafajalistickespeealadojus" was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Holmes" title="Alan Holmes">Alan Holmes</a> and his New Tones on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, vocal by Hal Marquess and the Holmes Men, music and lyrics by Patricia Smith (a <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Parker" title="Gloria Parker">Gloria Parker</a> pen name). In addition, "Supercalafajalistickespeealadojus" was recorded on Gloro Records (45) by The Arabian Knights. The Disney publishers won the lawsuit partially because affidavits were produced showing that "variants of the word were known ... many years prior to 1949".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first known appearance of the root or similar word (Supercaliflawjalisticexpialidoshus) is from an "A-Muse-ings" column by Helen Herman in <i>The Syracuse Daily Orange</i> (Syracuse University), March 10, 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In it, columnist Herman muses about her made-up word.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_musical">Stage musical</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins_(musical)" title="Mary Poppins (musical)">stage musical</a>, Mary Poppins takes Jane and Michael Banks to visit Mrs Corry's shop to buy "an ounce of conversation", only to find that Mrs Corry has run out of conversation. She does, however have some letters, and Jane and Michael each pick out seven, with Mary choosing one also. As Bert, Mary and the rest of the ensemble struggle to create words out of the fifteen letters, Mary reminds them that they can always use the same letter more than once, and creates the word (and song) Supercalifragilisticexpialidocious. In addition, the cast spells it out in a kind of gesture that was suggested by choreographer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Mear" title="Stephen Mear">Stephen Mear</a>, whose partner is deaf.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Football_headline">Football headline</span></h2>
<p>In February 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverness_Caledonian_Thistle" title="Inverness Caledonian Thistle">Inverness Caledonian Thistle</a> defeated Glasgow's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_FC" title="Celtic FC">Celtic FC</a> 3-1 in the third round of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Cup" title="Scottish Cup">Scottish Cup</a> football competition. The result, one of the biggest ever upsets in Scottish football, led to the famous newspaper headline "Super Caley go ballistic, Celtic are atrocious" by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> rated it as number 5 in six of the greatest football headlines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympic_history">Olympic history</span></h2>
<p>English yachtsman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodney_Pattison" title="Rodney Pattison">Rodney Pattison</a> won three Olympic medals in sailing during the Games of <a href="/wiki/Sailing_at_the_1968_Summer_Olympics" title="Sailing at the 1968 Summer Olympics">1968</a> (gold), <a href="/wiki/Sailing_at_the_1972_Summer_Olympics" title="Sailing at the 1972 Summer Olympics">1972</a> (gold) and <a href="/wiki/Sailing_at_the_1976_Summer_Olympics" title="Sailing at the 1976 Summer Olympics">1976</a> (silver) in a <a href="/wiki/Flying_Dutchman_(dinghy)" title="Flying Dutchman (dinghy)">Flying Dutchman</a> called <i>Supercalifragilisticexpialidocious</i> written in large colorful waves on the hull.</p>
