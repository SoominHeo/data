<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">T-X program</h1>

<p>The <b>T-X program</b> has been established to enable the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> to buy a new two-seat jet <a href="/wiki/Trainer_(aircraft)" title="Trainer (aircraft)">trainer</a> for fast-jet training to replace the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">Northrop T-38 Talon</a>; the average age of the T-38 fleet is over 43.5 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delay_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-delay-1">[1]</a></sup> About 350 aircraft are expected to be ordered to replace the T-38, but further purchases could push the overall purchase to over 1,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preview_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-preview-2">[2]</a></sup> The notional in-service date for the replacement trainer was 2017,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hawk-3">[3]</a></sup> but shrinking budgets have pushed initial operating capability to around 2023.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-budgslip_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-budgslip-4">[4]</a></sup> The program is expected to begin in FY 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The USAF's <a href="/wiki/Air_Education_and_Training_Command" title="Air Education and Training Command">Air Education and Training Command</a> (AETC) has been developing requirements for the T-38 replacement program since 2003. Originally, the replacement trainer was expected to enter service around 2020. A fatigue failure in 2008 killed the two-person crew of a T-38C, advancing the target service date to 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preview_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-preview-2">[2]</a></sup> In the Fiscal 2013 budget proposal the USAF suggested delaying the initial operating capability to FY2020 with the contract award not expected before FY2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Shrinking budgets and the need to fund higher priority modernization projects have pushed the IOC the T-X aircraft to "fiscal year 2023 or 2024." The delay was a direct result of budget constraints, so the T-X was pushed back to support higher Air Force priorities. Although the program was left out of the FY 2014 budget entirely, the service still views the trainer as a priority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-budgslip_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-budgslip-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2013, there was an expectation that the program might succumb to budget pressures in the USAF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In May 2013, the T-X industry day was postponed "until further notice" due to the fiscal climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In December 2013 the head of the program said there were no plans for 2014 or 2015, but that he would speak to the chief of staff about the program either in February 2014 or later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 20 March 2015, US Air Force officials released T-X program requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Requirements">Requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the driving requirements for the new trainer will be to help prepare pilots for the increased complexity in some areas, particularly information management, that are a part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_generation_jet_fighter" title="Fifth generation jet fighter">fifth generation jet fighters</a> like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-22_Raptor" title="F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-35_Lightning_II" title="F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a>. The Air Force first viewed this as unnecessary and costly, but industry analysis showed it to be cheaper in the long run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw2015-04-06_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw2015-04-06-11">[11]</a></sup> The aircraft and simulation system will have to fulfill several basic training roles; basic aircraft control, airmanship, formation, instrument and navigation, advanced air-to-air, advanced air-to-ground, and advanced crew/cockpit resource management. Furthermore, there are five advanced training roles that the system is expected to fulfill; sustained high-G operations at 6.5–7.5g,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw2015-04-06_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-aw2015-04-06-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a>, night vision imaging systems operations, air-to-air intercepts, and data-link operations. The 2009 Request For Information (RFI) mentions that some tasks, such as aerial refueling, may be performed in the simulator and not on the aircraft itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fbo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-fbo-12">[12]</a></sup> Aircraft <a href="/wiki/Availability" title="Availability">availability</a> is to be 80%, but not higher, as that would drive cost too high. Program requirements focus on <a href="/wiki/Whole-life_cost" title="Whole-life cost">life-cycle costs</a> rather than just purchase price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw2015-04-06_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-aw2015-04-06-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, while the RFI is specifically for a USAF trainer, it asked potential suppliers about the feasibility of a fighter/attack variant of the aircraft and a carrier-capable variant for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fbo_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-fbo-12">[12]</a></sup> However, the requirements manager for the program, Stephan Lyon, has stated that it is unlikely that potential combat performance will be considered. Similarly, while Navy officials will be participating in some stages of the program, carrier suitability will not be part of the evaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preview_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-preview-2">[2]</a></sup> Similarly, the Air Force considered the possibility of adapting the T-X aircraft to perform light attack and <a href="/wiki/Close_air_support" title="Close air support">close air support</a> as part of their effort to retire the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-10_Thunderbolt_II" title="A-10 Thunderbolt II">A-10 Thunderbolt II</a>. With the T-X already invested in, making it a multirole combat and training plane could defray costs of developing a new attack jet that can operate efficiently in a low-threat environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, by 2016 the Air Force decided to pursue other aircraft to meet CAS requirements and not "dilute" the trainer program to support other missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_competitors">Potential competitors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Potential competitors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the formal request for proposals has not been released, several competitors are expected to submit existing aircraft and others are considering all new designs. Those expected to propose existing aircraft are <a href="/wiki/Alenia_Aermacchi" title="Alenia Aermacchi">Alenia Aermacchi</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Alenia_Aermacchi_M-346_Master" title="Alenia Aermacchi M-346 Master">M-346 Master</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea_Aerospace_Industries" title="Korea Aerospace Industries">Korea Aerospace Industries</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> with the <a href="/wiki/KAI_T-50_Golden_Eagle" title="KAI T-50 Golden Eagle">T-50 Golden Eagle</a>. <a href="/wiki/Textron#Textron_Airland" title="Textron">Textron AirLand</a> is evaluating possible modifications to its <a href="/wiki/Textron_AirLand_Scorpion" title="Textron AirLand Scorpion">Scorpion</a>, currently a single flying prototype, from tactical jet to trainer aircraft to meet the expected T-X requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-textron1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-textron1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-farnborough2014_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-farnborough2014-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> initially planned to submit the <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Hawk" title="BAE Systems Hawk">BAE Systems Hawk</a>, but later switched to a new design. <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> was said to be considering several options, including a new aircraft. Boeing had an existing partnership with Alenia to market the M-346 internationally, and also with BAE on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-45_Goshawk" title="T-45 Goshawk">T-45 Goshawk</a>. Boeing instead partnered with <a href="/wiki/Saab_Group" title="Saab Group">Saab Group</a> to offer a new, purpose-built aircraft for offer to the U.S. Air Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boeing.2FSaab_T-X">Boeing/Saab T-X</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boeing/Saab T-X">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 6 December 2013, <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saab_Group" title="Saab Group">Saab Group</a> announced they would team up to offer a new aircraft (the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_T-X" title="Boeing T-X">Boeing T-X</a>) for the T-X program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 August 2016, Boeing publicly revealed first pictures of the T-X concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CombatAircraft_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CombatAircraft-19">[19]</a></sup> The single-engine, twin-tail aircraft was presented to the public on 13 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The aircraft was first flown on 20 December 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lockheed_Martin.2FKAI_T-50A_Golden_Eagle">Lockheed Martin/KAI T-50A Golden Eagle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lockheed Martin/KAI T-50A Golden Eagle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> with <a href="/wiki/Korea_Aerospace_Industries" title="Korea Aerospace Industries">Korea Aerospace Industries</a> (KAI) plan to propose their <a href="/wiki/KAI_T-50_Golden_Eagle" title="KAI T-50 Golden Eagle">KAI T-50 Golden Eagle</a>, which first flew in 2002, for the T-X program. While the T-50 was explicitly designed and built for the South Korean trainer requirement, Lockheed Martin officials have acknowledged that the aircraft was designed with replacing the T-38 in mind. The T-50 may also have an advantage as Lockheed also builds the F-22 and F-35, the aircraft the new trainer is supposed to prepare pilots for. Lockheed anticipates proposing the T-50 with few changes, mostly <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> related. They are considering building a new assembly line in the United States for manufacturing the T-50 for the United States Air Force instead of manufacturing them in South Korea, if they win the contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preview_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-preview-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 December 2015, Lockheed and KAI revealed their T-X prototype in South Korea based on the T-50, the first competitor in the program to do so publicly. Referred to as the "T-X demonstrator aircraft," it adds to the T-50 airframe the ability for <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a>, embedded ground training systems, and a large area display in place of the five smaller screens. South Korean president <a href="/wiki/Park_Geun-hye" title="Park Geun-hye">Park Geun-hye</a> attended the rollout ceremony. Lockheed Martin's upgraded T-50A jet trainer successfully completed its first flight test in Sacheon, South Korea, a key milestone for the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Flight tests in the U.S. should begin in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The aircraft also adds a large dorsal hump, which houses the aerial refueling receptacle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northrop_Grumman_.2F_BAE_Systems">Northrop Grumman / BAE Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Northrop Grumman / BAE Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> with partners <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-3_Communications" title="L-3 Communications">L-3 Communications</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Holdings" title="Rolls-Royce Holdings">Rolls-Royce Holdings</a>, initially had intentions of proposing an updated version of the <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Hawk" title="BAE Systems Hawk">Hawk T2/128</a> for the T-X program. Northrop Grumman assumed prime responsibilities for the team in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Northrop Grumman built the U.S. Air Force's current training aircraft, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-38_Talon" title="T-38 Talon">T-38 Talon</a>.</p>
<p>The Hawk T2 features an all glass cockpit, new wing, and fuselage components. Although the basic Hawk design dates back to the 1970s, the only parts shared between the T1 and T2 versions are the canopy and airbrake, making the T2 version essentially a new aircraft. The Northrop Grumman team was expecting this to be a low-risk, low-cost strategy for the competition, augmented by the fact that Hawk-based <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a>-<a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_T-45_Goshawk" title="McDonnell Douglas T-45 Goshawk">T-45C Goshawk</a> with glass cockpit is being used to train Student <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Aviator" title="Naval Aviator">Naval Aviators</a> and Student <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Flight_Officer" title="Naval Flight Officer">Naval Flight Officers</a> slated for tactical jet aircraft for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>. On 13 September 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Group_PLC" title="Rolls-Royce Group PLC">Rolls-Royce</a> announced it would join the team to offer the Hawk for the T-X program. Rolls-Royce will support the integration of the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Turbomeca_Adour" title="Rolls-Royce Turbomeca Adour">Adour Mk951</a> engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, in a significant reversal, it was later reported that Northrop Grumman were no longer submitting the Hawk for T-X consideration, primarily due to airframe performance shortcomings with maneuvers such as high angle-of-attack and sustained Gs, as well as concerns regarding affordability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avweek1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-avweek1-31">[31]</a></sup> Instead, Northrop had secretly begun a new clean-sheet design to closely match the evolving T-X requirements. <a href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites" title="Scaled Composites">Scaled Composites</a>, a wholly owned subsidiary of Northrop Grumman, will construct the prototype, with a first flight expected near the end of 2015.</p>
<p>On 19 August 2016 in Mojave, Northrop publicly revealed a model of the T-X concept powered by a single <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_F404" title="General Electric F404">GE F404-102D</a> engine with 17,200 lb thrust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flightglobal_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raytheon.2FAlenia_Aermacchi_T-100">Raytheon/Alenia Aermacchi T-100</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Raytheon/Alenia Aermacchi T-100">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Italian aerospace company <a href="/wiki/Alenia_Aermacchi" title="Alenia Aermacchi">Alenia Aermacchi</a> is part of a bid with its <a href="/wiki/Alenia_Aermacchi_M-346_Master" title="Alenia Aermacchi M-346 Master">M-346 Master</a>. The company had initially considered submitting the aircraft as the prime contractor, but changed its mind in 2010. The company is offering a variant of the aircraft as the <i>T-100 Integrated Training System</i> for the competition. Alenia anticipates moving the final assembly location from Italy to the United States if it wins the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preview_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-preview-2">[2]</a></sup> In January 2013, <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a> joined Alenia Aermacchi and signed a letter of intent (LOI) to compete jointly on the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In February 2014, <a href="/wiki/CAE_Inc." title="CAE Inc.">CAE Inc.</a>, which specializes in flight simulators and flight training, joined General Dynamics and Alenia Aermacchi to offer the T-100 for the T-X program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On 26 March 2015, General Dynamics withdrew as the prime contractor for the M-346 Master/T-100 offering. General Dynamics would no longer retain its role as the lead contractor and systems integrator for the bid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, in Italy, Alenia Aermacchi which had been a subsidiary to a holding company Finmeccanica, was taken over completely on 1 January 2016 and renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo-Finmeccanica" title="Leonardo-Finmeccanica">Leonardo-Finmeccanica</a>. Back in the USA, on 22 February 2016, <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a> announced it was replacing GD and going to head-up the bid in the US for the T-X program, as the primary contractor, teamed with Leonardo-Finmeccanica, <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_Aerospace" title="Honeywell Aerospace">Honeywell Aerospace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/CAE_Inc." title="CAE Inc.">CAE USA Inc.</a> The aircraft to be submitted would be a modified M-346 platform, named T-100, using two <a href="/wiki/Honeywell/ITEC_F124" title="Honeywell/ITEC F124">Honeywell/ITEC F124</a> low-bypass turbofan engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raytheon_T-100_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_T-100-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textron_AirLand_Scorpion">Textron AirLand Scorpion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Textron AirLand Scorpion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textron_AirLand" title="Textron AirLand">Textron AirLand</a>, a joint venture between <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=AirLand_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="AirLand Enterprises (page does not exist)">AirLand Enterprises</a>, developed the <a href="/wiki/Textron_AirLand_Scorpion" title="Textron AirLand Scorpion">Textron AirLand Scorpion</a> light attack jet which had potential as a candidate for the T-X program. Textron management stated that with some modifications, such as swapping the two engines with a single engine and changing the wings, the aircraft would be an ideal match for the T-X role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-textron1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-textron1-15">[15]</a></sup> Additionally, during the Farnborough 2014 Airshow, Textron representatives expressed significant interest in the T-X program, and indicated they were closely following the progression of the draft requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-farnborough2014_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-farnborough2014-16">[16]</a></sup> On 23 August 2014, Textron AirLand formally confirmed they would compete for the T-X trainer and modify the Scorpion to better fit the training requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> However, in September 2015 Textron AirLand revealed it would not offer a version of the Scorpion for the program due to changing Air Force requirements, moving from a low-cost advanced jet trainer requiring little development to a high-performance fly-by-wire trainer with top tier handling qualities. The company is still interested in the T-X, but would have to offer a clean-sheet design like most of the other competitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sierra_Nevada.2FTAI_Freedom">Sierra Nevada/TAI Freedom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T-X_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sierra Nevada/TAI Freedom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_Corporation" title="Sierra Nevada Corporation">Sierra Nevada Corporation</a> (SNC) and <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Aerospace_Industries" title="Turkish Aerospace Industries">Turkish Aerospace Industries</a> (TAI) are also considering offering a new aircraft for the T-X program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The companies have formed the Freedom Aircraft Ventures LLC in Centennial, Colorado to develop a lightweight, all-composite trainer for the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
