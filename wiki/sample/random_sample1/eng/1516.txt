<p><b>Compania Națională de Transporturi Aeriene Române TAROM S.A.</b>, doing business as <b>TAROM</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> and oldest currently operating <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Otopeni" title="Otopeni">Otopeni</a> near Bucharest. Its headquarters and its main hub are at <a href="/wiki/Henri_Coand%C4%83_International_Airport" title="Henri Coandă International Airport">Henri Coandă International Airport</a>. It is currently the largest airline operating in Romania based on international destinations, international flights and the second largest measured by fleet size and passengers carried.</p>
<p>The brand name is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>: <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><i><b>T</b>ransporturile <b>A</b>eriene <b>Rom</b>âne</i></span> (Romanian Air Transport). Over ninety-seven percent (97.05%) of TAROM is owned by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Romania" title="Government of Romania">Romanian Government</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Transport_and_Infrastructure_(Romania)" title="Ministry of Transport and Infrastructure (Romania)">Ministry of Transport</a>). The airline transported almost 2.4 million passengers in 2015, with an average load factor of 70%. The airline joined <a href="/wiki/SkyTeam" title="SkyTeam">SkyTeam</a> on 25 June 2010.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_beginnings">The beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Romanian National Air Transport Company can be traced back from 1920, when <a href="/wiki/CFRNA" title="CFRNA">CFRNA</a> - (<i>French-Romanian Company for Air Navigation</i>) was founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-2">[2]</a></sup> The airline used French-built <a href="/wiki/Potez_15" title="Potez 15">Potez 15</a> aircraft for its passenger/mail service between <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> via several cities in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. In 1925, the city of <a href="/wiki/Gala%C8%9Bi" title="Galați">Galați</a> became the first destination in Romania served by regular flights followed, from 24 June 1926, by an extended service to <a href="/wiki/Ia%C8%99i" title="Iași">Iași</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chi%C8%99in%C4%83u" title="Chișinău">Chișinău</a>. Ten <a href="/wiki/Airco_DH.9" title="Airco DH.9">de Havilland DH.9</a> and five <a href="/wiki/Ansaldo_A.300" title="Ansaldo A.300">Ansaldo A.300</a>, in addition to the Potez aircraft, operated the service.</p>
<p>In 1928 the airline changed its name to SNNA (<i>Serviciul Național de Navigație Aeriană</i>, The National Air Navigation Service). In 1930, the company adopted the name LARES (<i>Liniile Aeriene Române Exploatate de Stat</i>, Romanian State-Operated Air Lines) while 1937 saw the merger of LARES with its competitor, SARTA (<i>Societatea Anonimă Română de Transporturi Aeriene</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interbelic_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-interbelic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-World_War_II">Post-World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post-World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Immediately After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, in 1945, when the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> had extended its influence across Eastern Europe, a new reorganization replaced LARES with TARS (<i>Transporturi Aeriene Româno-Sovietice</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-2">[2]</a></sup> jointly owned by the governments of Romania and the Soviet Union. Domestic operations were started from Bucharest (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C4%83neasa_Airport" title="Băneasa Airport">Băneasa Airport</a>) on 1 February 1946, when TARS took over all air services and aircraft from LARES.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interbelic_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-interbelic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the following decade, the company's Soviet share was purchased by Romanian government and, on 18 September 1954, the airline adopted the name of TAROM (<i>Transporturi Aeriene Române</i>, Romanian Air Transport). By 1960, TAROM was flying to a dozen cities across Europe. 1966 saw the operation of its first transatlantic flight. On 14 May 1974, it launched a regular service to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> (<a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">John F. Kennedy International Airport</a>).</p>
<p>Being part of the regional group of airlines within <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> states meant that for much of its history TAROM has operated Soviet-designed aircraft. These included the <a href="/wiki/Lisunov_Li-2" title="Lisunov Li-2">Lisunov Li-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-14" title="Ilyushin Il-14">Ilyushin Il-14</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-18" title="Ilyushin Il-18">Ilyushin Il-18</a> long-range turboprop, <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-62" title="Ilyushin Il-62">Ilyushin Il-62</a> long-range jet airliner, <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">Antonov An-24</a> regional turboprop, and the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-154" title="Tupolev Tu-154">Tupolev Tu-154</a> medium-range tri-jet. As was the case with a number of other nations, the Il-62 was the first long-range jet airliner to be put into operation by Romania, in 1973. Five examples (three Il-62s and two later version Il-62Ms) were owned by TAROM, which also leased the aircraft to other operators.</p>
<p>An exception to Soviet-built aircraft was made in 1968, when TAROM bought <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAC_One_Eleven" title="BAC One Eleven">BAC One Elevens</a> for European and Middle East destinations, and in 1974 when it acquired <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a> aircraft to share its long-haul operations with the Il-62. Plans were made to acquire <a href="/wiki/Vickers_VC10" title="Vickers VC10">Vickers VC10</a> aircraft as well, but in the end the Soviets did not allow it, and made them buy the Il-62 instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With 59 aircraft in operation, in the late '70s, TAROM had the largest fleet in the Eastern Bloc, after <a href="/wiki/Aeroflot" title="Aeroflot">Aeroflot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978, a contract was signed with the UK enabling Rombac to manufacture the BAC One Eleven at <a href="/wiki/Romaero" title="Romaero">Romaero</a>, near Bucharest. Meanwhile, the 707 and Il-62 long-range aircraft were operating New York (via Amsterdam, later London and finally Vienna), Abu-Dhabi-Bangkok-Singapore, and Karachi-Beijing. TAROM was the only Eastern Bloc airline to operate flights to <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>, Israel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1990s">The 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_Revolution_of_1989" title="Romanian Revolution of 1989">collapse of the communist regime</a> in 1989, the airline, operating a fleet of 65 aircraft of six basic types, was able to acquire more Western-built jets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> By 1993, TAROM had introduced long-haul flights to <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> using Ilyushin Il-62 and <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A310" title="Airbus A310">Airbus A310</a> aircraft.</p>
<p>During the 1990s, TAROM replaced its long-haul fleet of Boeing 707s and IL-62s with <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A310" title="Airbus A310">Airbus A310s</a> (the last Il-62 being sold in 1999). In 2001, the airline cancelled its non-profitable long-haul services to Bangkok and Montreal and also terminated services to its remaining intercontinental destinations of <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> in 2003, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> in 2002, and New York City in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>TAROM terminated loss-making domestic services to <a href="/wiki/Craiova" title="Craiova">Craiova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tulcea" title="Tulcea">Tulcea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caransebe%C8%99" title="Caransebeș">Caransebeș</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Constan%C8%9Ba" title="Constanța">Constanța</a>, and focused its activity on service to key destinations in Europe and the Middle East. 2004 was the first profitable year of the last decade.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_onwards">2000 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>TAROM is recovering from a difficult period that began in the 1990s, when losses of up to $68 million per year were registered, caused by unprofitable routes. At the beginning of the new millennium, the airline initiated a programme that was aimed at restoring profitability. This was achieved by terminating loss-making intercontinental services. TAROM has decided to focus its operations on Bucharest (<a href="/wiki/Henri_Coand%C4%83_International_Airport" title="Henri Coandă International Airport">Henri Coandă International Airport</a>) (OTP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluj-Napoca_International_Airport" title="Cluj-Napoca International Airport">Cluj-Napoca International Airport</a> (CLJ), and initiated direct international flights from <a href="/wiki/Sibiu_International_Airport" title="Sibiu International Airport">Sibiu International Airport</a>.</p>
<p>A fleet upgrade programme started in 2006 with the acquisition of four <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A318" title="Airbus A318">Airbus A318s</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800s</a>, and two <a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">ATR 72-500s</a>, which resulted in a fleet increase to 26 by 2009.</p>
<p>The airline had a frequent-flyer programme "Smart Miles", which was turned into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_Blue" title="Flying Blue">Flying Blue</a> on 5 June 2010. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codeshare" title="Codeshare">Codeshare</a> agreements with foreign partner airlines are in place for several international routes. On 25 June 2010, TAROM joined <a href="/wiki/SkyTeam" title="SkyTeam">SkyTeam</a> as the alliance's thirteenth member.</p>
<p>Starting with November 2012, in accordance to the Romanian state-company legislation, TAROM was led by a private manager, the Belgian Christian Heinzmann occupying the positions of CEO and Accountable Manager until March 2016. During Heinzmann's leadership, the company reduced its financial losses by more than 75%, grew its yearly passenger number to a record 2.4 million and stabilised its load-factor around 70%. However, broad reforms like the fleet renewal and harmonisation, as well as the establishment of profit centers such as the TAROM Maintenance and TAROM Charter services, were not accomplished due to a constant lack of decision from the company's board of administrators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 October 2016, TAROM retired their remaining two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A310-300" title="Airbus A310-300">Airbus A310-300s</a> after a final flight from <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> to Bucharest. The A310s will be replaced with new smaller aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A310retirement_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-A310retirement-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo_and_livery">Logo and livery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Logo and livery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The TAROM logo, representing a <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> in flight, has been used on all TAROM aircraft since 1954. It is sometimes confused with the similar logo of <a href="/wiki/LOT_Polish_Airlines" title="LOT Polish Airlines">LOT Polish Airlines</a>, which features a crane in flight. In the 1970s livery the logo on the tail was painted in red, with a red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheatline" title="Cheatline">cheatline</a>. The livery introduced in the early 1990s (on the Airbus A310 aircraft) is an overall-white scheme with the titles and the tailfin painted in dark blue. The current color scheme (introduced in 2006 on the A318) is a slightly modified version of the previous one, with an oversized logo on the tailfin, and the engine pods also painted in dark blue.</p>
<p>All aircraft in the TAROM fleet receive a "name" which is a Romanian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponym" title="Toponym">toponym</a>. For instance, the names of the ATR aircraft in the fleet are related to the rivers of Romania, the Boeing aircraft bear names of Romanian cities, the Airbus long-haul aircraft bear Romanian historical province names, while the Airbus A318s bear names of Romanian aviation pioneers.</p>
<p>In 2009, marking the airline's 55th anniversary, a Boeing 737-700 (YR-BGG "Craiova") was painted in a retro jet color scheme, representing airline's first livery used in the 1950s on <a href="/wiki/Lisunov_Li-2" title="Lisunov Li-2">Lisunov Li-2</a> aircraft.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="TAROM_Technical_Division">TAROM Technical Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: TAROM Technical Division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The TAROM Technical Division provides aircraft maintenance services for the entire fleet of the company and for the fleet of other national and international companies. The objective of TAROM Technical Department is to be the best and the most efficient, in terms of costs, maintenance service provider for Boeing 737, ATR 42 and ATR 72 aircraft in Central and Eastern Europe. The services provided by the TAROM Technical Department include unscheduled maintenance works, scheduled maintenance works and repair works for spare parts.</p>
<p>The major maintenance activity is performed in the hangar of the technical department, built between 1969 and 1972, with an area of 6,000 m<sup>2</sup> and restored in 2000 to fully comply with EASA (European Safety Aviation Agency) and FAA (Federal Aviation Administration) standards. The hangar is equipped to perform all types of inspections for TAROM fleet, and the personnel is qualified and licensed for all types of aircraft in the fleet. Maintenance activities for 3 to 6 aircraft, depending on their size, may be carried out simultaneously in the hangar. The hangar is equipped with full MERO system for B737 docking.</p>
<p>The most important maintenance capacities of the TAROM Technical Department include full maintenance services for Boeing 737 and ATR42/72 aircraft, inspection capacity type C for Airbus A310 and A318 aircraft, total painting, interior cleaning, modifications.</p>
<p>Also, the technical department provides safe storage facilities for spare parts and materials necessary for maintenance activity, dedicated spaces for chemicals, dedicated spaces for special tools and testing equipments, quarantine spaces. The TAROM Technical Department also provides conveyance services (packaging, preparation of documents, customs) and acceptance services (customs, disassembly, and reception inspection) for various substances and equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Destinations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/TAROM_destinations" title="TAROM destinations">TAROM destinations</a></div>
<p>The airline directly operates 79 destinations including charter and seasonal services in 25 countries in Europe, the Middle East and Northern Africa including 6 domestic destinations. The airline's flights to the USA ceased in 2003 and are now operated under a codeshare agreement with Air France via Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliances">Alliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, TAROM was scheduled to join SkyTeam as an associate member (sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Alitalia" title="Alitalia">Alitalia</a>), but the entry into the alliance was postponed until 2008. On 7 May that year, SkyTeam signed a SkyTeam Alliance Associate Adherence Agreement (SAAAA) with TAROM. On 22 June 2010, SkyTeam announced that it had renewed its membership program, thereby making TAROM a future full member of the alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On 25 June 2010, TAROM became a full member of SkyTeam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TAROM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On 4 November 1957, a TAROM <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-14" title="Ilyushin Il-14">Ilyushin Il-14</a>, registration YR-PCC, operating an international administrative flight from <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> crashed short of the runway at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vnukovo_Airport" title="Vnukovo Airport">Vnukovo Airport</a>, killing four of 16 on board. The aircraft was on approach to Vnukovo Airport when the pilot noticed that the aircraft was too low, however the aircraft continued its descent until it struck tree tops and later crashed. The aircraft was carrying Romanian government members <a href="/wiki/Chivu_Stoica" title="Chivu Stoica">Chivu Stoica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grigore_Preoteasa" title="Grigore Preoteasa">Grigore Preoteasa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandru_Moghioro%C5%9F" title="Alexandru Moghioroş">Alexandru Moghioroş</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9Etefan_Voitec" title="Ştefan Voitec">Ştefan Voitec</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C5%9Fescu" title="Nicolae Ceauşescu">Nicolae Ceauşescu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonte_R%C4%83utu" title="Leonte Răutu">Leonte Răutu</a> and Marin Năstase to Moscow for the 40th anniversary of the October Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Preoteasa, who apparently was not wearing a safety belt, and three Soviet crew members lost their lives.</li>
<li>On 24 February 1962 a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-18V" title="Ilyushin Il-18V">Ilyushin Il-18V</a>, registration YR-IMB, operating on an international scheduled flight from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bucharest_Otopeni_Airport" title="Bucharest Otopeni Airport">Bucharest Otopeni Airport</a> (OTP) to <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a> via <a href="/wiki/Nicosia" title="Nicosia">Nicosia</a> lost power on all four engines and made a belly landing on a grassy field in Cyprus. While cruising at 23,000 feet over the Mediterranean Sea and 43 miles offshore, engine number 3 lost power, followed shortly by number 1 and 2. Then at 10,000 feet and 27 miles offshore engine 4 also quit. All 100 occupants survived. The aircraft was transported to Moscow for repairs, but it never re-entered service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 9 October 1964, an <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-14" title="Ilyushin Il-14">Ilyushin Il-14</a>, registration YR-ILB, operating a domestic scheduled flight from <a href="/wiki/Timi%C8%99oara" title="Timișoara">Timișoara</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> broke apart in mid-air and crashed 2 km south of <a href="/wiki/Cugir" title="Cugir">Cugir</a>, killing all 31 on board. The aircraft had flown into a strong downdraft; the pilot attempted to maintain altitude, but this caused the fuselage to overstress and break apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 4 February 1970, TAROM Flight 35, an <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">Antonov An-24</a>, registration YR-AMT, operating a domestic scheduled flight from Bucharest to <a href="/wiki/Oradea" title="Oradea">Oradea</a> struck the side of a mountain in the Vlădeasa mountain group, killing 20 of 21 on board. The aircraft began descending in poor visibility until it struck tree tops on a mountain side, after which it struck the slope of a second mountain. The aircraft was leased from the Romanian government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 29 December 1974, an <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">Antonov An-24</a>, registration YR-AMD, operating on a domestic scheduled flight from <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sibiu" title="Sibiu">Sibiu</a> crashed into the side of the Lotrului mountains (22 km south of Sibiu) at an altitude of 1,700 m, killing all 28 passengers and 5 crew members. The crew's incorrect approach procedure execution, which led to the aircraft drifting south off course by 20 km, while the wind was increasing turbulence was present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 7 August 1980, a <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-154" title="Tupolev Tu-154">Tupolev Tu-154</a>B-1 registered YR-TPH, operating on an international scheduled flight from Bucharest Otopeni Airport to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouadhibou_Airport" title="Nouadhibou Airport">Nouadhibou Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> ditched in the water 300 m short of the runway at Nouadhibou Airport. The crew could not see the runway while descending through the 90 m decision height. A missed approach procedure was initiated when the pilot felt contact with what he thought was ground, but was actually water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> All of the 152 passengers and 16 crew members survived the impact, but a passenger suffered a heart attack and died before he could be rescued. Most of the passengers were sailors who were going to replace the crew of two Romanian ships located on the Mauritanian coast. Many passengers swam to the land, while sharks were kept away by the vibrations of an engine which continued to function for a few hours after the crash.</li>
<li>On 5 September 1986, an <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">Antonov An-24</a> registered YR-AMF operating on a domestic scheduled flight from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bucharest_B%C4%83neasa_Airport" title="Bucharest Băneasa Airport">Bucharest Băneasa Airport</a> touched down nose wheel-first while landing at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluj_Airport" title="Cluj Airport">Cluj Airport</a>. A fire erupted, killing three crew members who were trapped in the cockpit. The other two crew members and all fifty passengers survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 28 December 1989, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_Revolution_of_1989" title="Romanian Revolution of 1989">Romanian revolution</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">Antonov An-24</a> flying from Bucharest to <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a>, carrying journalist Ian Henry Perry, was shot down by a missile at <a href="/wiki/Vi%C8%99ina,_D%C3%A2mbovi%C8%9Ba" title="Vișina, Dâmbovița">Vișina, Dâmbovița</a>. All the people on board (six crew members and the passenger) died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 24 September 1994, TAROM Flight 381, an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A310" title="Airbus A310">Airbus A310</a> registered YR-LCA flying from Bucharest to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Orly" title="Paris Orly">Paris Orly</a>, went into a sudden and uncommanded nose-up position and stalled. The crew attempted to countermand the aircraft's flight control system but were unable to get the nose down while remaining on course. Witnesses saw the aircraft climb with an extreme nose-up attitude, then bank sharply left, then right, then fall into a steep dive. Only when the dive produced additional speed was the crew able to recover steady flight. An investigation found that an overshoot of flap placard speed during approach, incorrectly commanded by the captain, caused a mode transition to flight level change. The auto-throttles increased power and trim went full nose-up as a result. The crew's attempt at commanding the nose-down elevator could not counteract effect of stabilizer nose-up trim, and the resulting dive brought the aircraft from a height of 4,100 ft at the time of the stall to 800 ft when the crew was able to recover command. The aircraft landed safely after a second approach. There were 186 people on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 31 March 1995, a TAROM Airbus A310 operating as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarom_Flight_371" title="Tarom Flight 371">Flight 371</a> crashed near <a href="/wiki/Balote%C8%99ti" title="Balotești">Balotești</a> due to a fault in the throttles and lack of recovery from the flight crew. All 50 passengers and 10 crew members were killed.</li>
<li>On 30 December 2007, a TAROM Boeing 737-300 (YR-BGC "Constanța"), flying <a href="/wiki/TAROM_Flight_3107" title="TAROM Flight 3107">Flight 3107</a> hit a car on the runway of Bucharest <a href="/wiki/Henri_Coand%C4%83_International_Airport" title="Henri Coandă International Airport">Henri Coandă International Airport</a> while taking off for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharm-el-Sheikh" title="Sharm-el-Sheikh">Sharm-el-Sheikh</a>. The aircraft stopped beside the runway and was severely damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> None of the passengers were injured. Because of <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, neither the tower nor the pilots saw the car belonging to staff who were repairing a runway beacon.</li>
</ul>
