<p><b>.nyc</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_level_domain" title="Top level domain">top level domain</a> (TLD) for <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was delegated to the root zone by ICANN on March 20, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icann.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-icann.org-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.nyc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="name.space.27s_proposal">name.space's proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.nyc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: name.space's proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The New York-based company name.space, founded by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Garrin" title="Paul Garrin">Paul Garrin</a>, began operating its own alternative <a href="/wiki/DNS_root_zone" title="DNS root zone">root zone</a> system in 1997 including a .nyc top level domain. name.space applied for inclusion of its .nyc, along with a number of other <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">strings</a>, as TLDs in the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">IANA</a> root during the 2000 ICANN application round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its application was refused. Another company Names@Work also put in an application in 2000 but withdrew for lack of funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008hearing_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008hearing-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first municipal support for the .nyc TLD was the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Empowerment_Resolution" title="Internet Empowerment Resolution">Internet Empowerment Resolution</a> passed by <a href="/wiki/Queens_Community_Board_3" title="Queens Community Board 3">Queens Community Board 3</a>, a local planning unit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_New_York" title="City of New York">City of New York</a>, on April 19, 2001. The Resolution called for the city's Commission on Public Information and Communication or a public interest organization to acquire and develop the TLD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the mid-2000s interest in gaining local TLDs had arisen in other cities, notably Paris and Berlin. Some of those proponents contacted Thomas Lowenhaupt, the former Community Board member who had introduced the Queens Resolution. In 2007, with the Bloomberg Administration having indicated that it did not intend to apply for the .nyc TLD, Lowenhaupt formed a non-profit 'Connecting .nyc' to acquire and develop the .nyc TLD for community use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On June 6, 2008 Council Member <a href="/wiki/Gale_Brewer" title="Gale Brewer">Gale Brewer</a> led the introduction of Resolution 1495-2008 supporting "the local efforts to acquire the .nyc Top Level Domain and urging The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers to approve the City’s application in order to meet the needs of city residents via the Internet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the ICANN meeting in Paris in July 2008 a green light was given for the development of a new TLD application round, including cities.</p>
<p>On October 17, 2008, Brewer held a public hearing in support of her bill. Witnesses included Lowenhaupt, Antony Van Couvering of Names@Work, and Paul Garrin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008hearing_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-2008hearing-5">[5]</a></sup> Van Couvering proposed that .nyc be run by his company as a purely commercial enterprise, with a portion of the revenue dedicated to benefiting the community. He testified that he was willing to work with Lowenhaupt on community interests. The bill itself would eventually be <a href="/wiki/Adjournment_sine_die" title="Adjournment sine die">shelved</a> at the end of 2009.</p>
<p>In her February 12, 2009 State of the City address City Council Speaker <a href="/wiki/Christine_Quinn" title="Christine Quinn">Christine Quinn</a> proposed the .nyc TLD as a public-private partnership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> names@work, now under the name DotNYC, opened a new dedicated website which reported on Quinn's speech. "The crowd, made up of elected politicians and dignitaries, literally chanted “Dot N – Y – C” at the end of her description of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Further press reports were confidently quoted by DotNYC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These included details that they expected to pay the city a third of all revenue, $3 million per year initially, rising to $10 million a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In June 2009 DotNYC released a testimonial video of former Mayor <a href="/wiki/Ed_Koch" title="Ed Koch">Ed Koch</a> saying "DotNYC is the best real estate opportunity since the Dutch bought Manhattan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wheels had been put in motion and, on April 15, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Information_Technology_and_Telecommunications" title="New York City Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications">New York City Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications</a> (DoITT) issued a <a href="/wiki/Request_for_information" title="Request for information">Request for Information</a> (RFI). Connecting.nyc published its response <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which called for many names (<a href="/wiki/Second-level_domain" title="Second-level domain">second level domains</a>) to be reserved for community use. On October 5, 2009 a Request for Proposals (RFP) was issued by the City of New York seeking "services to obtain, manage, administer, maintain and market the geographic Top Domain name .nyc."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Notably, conditions included that proposals include a system of ensuring nexus with the city, and also a preliminary list of reserved names including all city precincts, schools, districts, and neighborhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_.nyc">Creation of .nyc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.nyc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creation of .nyc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2012 name.space reported it had filed for trademark protection on a number of its TLDs, including .nyc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2012 the city announced that <a href="/wiki/Neustar" title="Neustar">NeuStar, Inc.</a>, a Virginia-based firm, had been selected from the RFP submissions and on June 12, 2012 the City of New York submitted an application to <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> for the .nyc TLD. Neustar paid the $185,000 application fee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In May 2012 Garrin wrote to local dignitaries protesting the Neustar contract, and asserting name.space's rights to the .nyc TLD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The application on behalf of the city passed initial evaluation at <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> on May 24, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>ICANN delegated .nyc to the <a href="/wiki/DNS_root_zone" title="DNS root zone">DNS root zone</a> on March 20, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icann.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-icann.org-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advisory_board">Advisory board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.nyc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advisory board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 22, 2013<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the .NYC Community Advisory Board was formed. Members include Thomas Lowenhaupt, former ICANN Chair <a href="/wiki/Esther_Dyson" title="Esther Dyson">Esther Dyson</a>, and representatives of the local tech and academic community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
