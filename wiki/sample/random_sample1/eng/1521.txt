<p><b>TD Place Arena</b> (originally the <b>Ottawa Civic Centre</b>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indoor_arena" title="Indoor arena">indoor arena</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada, seating 10,000. With temporary seating and standing room it can hold 10,585. Opened in December 1967, it is used primarily for sports, including <a href="/wiki/Curling" title="Curling">curling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse" title="Lacrosse">lacrosse</a>. The arena has hosted Canadian and world championships in figure skating and ice hockey, including the first women's world ice hockey championship in 1990. Canadian championships in curling have also been hosted at the arena. The arena is also used for concerts and conventions. It has also been used for <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_SuperEX" title="Ottawa SuperEX">Ottawa SuperEX</a> events and exhibits.</p>
<p>The arena is the home of the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_67%27s" title="Ottawa 67's">Ottawa 67's</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Hockey_League" title="Ontario Hockey League">OHL</a>. It was the former home arena of the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Senators" title="Ottawa Senators">Ottawa Senators</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a> (1992–1995), the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Nationals" title="Ottawa Nationals">Ottawa Nationals</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Hockey_Association" title="World Hockey Association">WHA</a> (1972–1973), the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Civics" title="Ottawa Civics">Ottawa Civics</a> of the WHA (1976), and the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Rebel" title="Ottawa Rebel">Ottawa Rebel</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Lacrosse_League" title="National Lacrosse League">NLL</a> (<a href="/wiki/2002_NLL_season" title="2002 NLL season">2002</a>–<a href="/wiki/2003_NLL_season" title="2003 NLL season">2003</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1960s, the City of Ottawa was preparing to rebuild the football stadium at Lansdowne Park, on Bank Street at the Rideau Canal. During the planning phase, the old <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Auditorium" title="Ottawa Auditorium">Ottawa Auditorium</a> arena was demolished and the City now needed two new sports venues. The City combined plans and the arena, named the Civic Centre, was built together under the north grandstand of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Clair_Stadium" title="Frank Clair Stadium">football stadium</a>. One side of the arena is located beneath the upper part of the stadium grandstand, with a much lower ceiling than the opposite side of the arena.</p>
<p>Dominion Bridge was the supplier of the huge steel girders for the arena and stadium's frame, some so large they had to be brought to the site by barge, up the Ottawa River and down the Rideau Canal. According to Dominion Bridge "the most striking feature of the unique design concept is a giant overhanging roof reaching out 170 degrees from atop eight massive steel A-frames."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citizen_121307_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-citizen_121307-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new Civic Centre opened on December 29, 1967, although seating was not complete, for an exhibition game between the Ottawa 67's, boosted by five players from the Montreal Junior Canadiens, and the NHL <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>. Seats were taken temporarily from the Coliseum building nearby. Then President Howard Darwin said about 500 fans had to be turned away at the door. Of the 9,000 who attended the opening game, only six ticket-holders requested and received refunds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The football stadium and arena complex was Ottawa's official "Centennial Project." Federal government grant money depended on the facility opening in 1967, and construction was rushed to meet the deadline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citizen_121307_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-citizen_121307-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Civic Centre was renovated and seating increased in 1992 in order to temporarily accommodate the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Senators" title="Ottawa Senators">Ottawa Senators</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a>. Luxury boxes were hung from the ceiling over ¾ of the bowl and all seats except for the club seats were narrowed slightly in order to increase capacity to over 10,000. The seats were replaced in 2005 and wider seats were installed, thus reducing capacity to under 10,000 again.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation">Renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/Lansdowne_Park_redevelopment" title="Lansdowne Park redevelopment">Lansdowne Park redevelopment</a>, the arena underwent renovations, which included new seats (up to 10,000 capacity), new scoreboard (replacing the video scoreboard installed in 2001), digital signage, and ceiling tiles to cover the steel support beams, in which the fire retardant was removed as well as sealing up constant leaks that have been a problem for the Civic Centre for years. During the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_OHL_season" title="2011–12 OHL season">2011–12 season</a>, a 67's game had to be rescheduled because of the leaking roof. Midway through the renovation process at the end of 2013, steel corrosion was discovered by workers and would cost an extra $17 million to repair.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While the arena was renovated, the 67's used the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Tire_Centre" title="Canadian Tire Centre">Canadian Tire Centre</a> for the 2012–13 and 2013-14 seasons.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curling">Curling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Curling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The arena played host to the <a href="/wiki/1979_Macdonald_Brier" title="1979 Macdonald Brier">1979</a>, <a href="/wiki/1993_Labatt_Brier" title="1993 Labatt Brier">1993</a>, <a href="/wiki/2001_Nokia_Brier" title="2001 Nokia Brier">2001</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2016_Tim_Hortons_Brier" title="2016 Tim Hortons Brier">2016</a> Brier Canadian men's championships. It also played host to the <a href="/wiki/1990_Scott_Tournament_of_Hearts" title="1990 Scott Tournament of Hearts">1990 Scott Tournament of Hearts</a> Canadian women's championships.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Figure_skating">Figure skating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Figure skating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The arena has hosted the 1978 and 1984 <a href="/wiki/World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Figure Skating Championships">World Figure Skating Championships</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skatecanada_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-skatecanada-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/ISU_World_Synchronized_Skating_Championships" title="ISU World Synchronized Skating Championships">2003 World Synchronized Skating Championships</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skatecanada_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-skatecanada-4">[4]</a></sup> The arena has hosted the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="Canadian Figure Skating Championships">Canadian Figure Skating Championships</a> in 1987, 1996, 1999, 2006 and 2017.</p>
<p>Both Worlds provided memorable moments in sports. In 1978 <a href="/wiki/Vern_Taylor" title="Vern Taylor">Vern Taylor</a> performed the first '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_axel" title="Triple axel">Triple axel</a>' jump in competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skatecanada_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-skatecanada-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1984, the Canadian pairs champions <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Underhill" title="Barbara Underhill">Barbara Underhill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Martini" title="Paul Martini">Paul Martini</a> won the Championship in dramatic fashion:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"After a disappointing Olympics in Sarajevo, Barbara and Paul seriously contemplated retiring from skating before World's. However, advised by their friend Brian Orser to put on her old skate boots, Barbara and Paul were propelled onto the ice, onto the podium and into history. Their flawless program is considered one of the great Canadian sporting moments and gave Canada a national thrill."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_hockey">Ice hockey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ice hockey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The primary tenant since the building's opening has been the Ottawa 67's junior men's team. The arena's <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> is large by junior standards. The team played before large crowds in the 1960s and 1970s but attendance started to drop in the late 80s and bottomed out after the arrival of the Ottawa Senators in the early 1990s. In 1998 the team was bought by local businessman <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Hunt" title="Jeff Hunt">Jeff Hunt</a> and he successfully improved attendance to take advantage of the arena's large capacity. Since then, the 67's have been one of the top-10 junior teams in Canada in terms of attendance, often finishing #1 on the list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The club has also been successful on the ice, winning the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Hockey_League" title="Ontario Hockey League">OHL</a> Championship in 1977, 1984, and 2001 and the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Cup" title="Memorial Cup">Memorial Cup</a> championship in 1984 and 1999. The <a href="/wiki/1972_Memorial_Cup" title="1972 Memorial Cup">1972</a> and <a href="/wiki/1999_Memorial_Cup" title="1999 Memorial Cup">1999</a> Memorial Cup tournaments were played at the arena, and the 1999 tournament was won by the host 67's.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, the arena was home to two WHA professional teams, the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Nationals" title="Ottawa Nationals">Ottawa Nationals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Civics" title="Ottawa Civics">Ottawa Civics</a>. Neither survived in Ottawa for more than one season. The Nationals played for one regular season, but moved their playoff games to Toronto, and subsequently moved there permanently to become the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Toros" title="Toronto Toros">Toronto Toros</a>. The Civics were the hastily transplanted <a href="/wiki/Denver_Spurs" title="Denver Spurs">Denver Spurs</a> franchise that played only two home games in Ottawa before disbanding.</p>
<p>The arena hosted the first-ever <a href="/wiki/1976_Canada_Cup" title="1976 Canada Cup">Canada Cup</a> hockey game on September 2, 1976, when <a href="/wiki/Canada_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Canada men's national ice hockey team">Canada</a> crushed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_national_men%27s_ice_hockey_team" title="Finnish national men's ice hockey team">Finland</a> 11-2. They also hosted games in the <a href="/wiki/1981_Canada_Cup" title="1981 Canada Cup">1981 Canada Cup</a>.</p>
<p>The arena was the site of the first <a href="/wiki/International_Ice_Hockey_Federation" title="International Ice Hockey Federation">IIHF</a> <a href="/wiki/1990_IIHF_Women%27s_World_Championship" title="1990 IIHF Women's World Championship">Women's World Ice Hockey Championships</a> in 1990. Canada defeated the United States 5–2 on March 25, 1990 to win the gold medal.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_Ottawa_Senators_season" title="1992–93 Ottawa Senators season">Starting in 1992</a>, the new National Hockey League Ottawa Senators called the arena home for three and a half seasons. In preparation for NHL hockey, the arena was refurbished for the Senators, adding additional seating and 32 private boxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1995 to 1997, <a href="/wiki/Roller_Hockey_International" title="Roller Hockey International">Roller Hockey International</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottawa_Loggers" title="Ottawa Loggers">Ottawa Loggers</a> (renamed the "Ottawa Wheels" for their final season) brought <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inline_hockey" title="Inline hockey">inline hockey</a> to the arena, though the inline version of the sport proved to be both unprofitable and unpopular in Ottawa.</p>
<p>In 2008 and 2009, the arena was used for games of the <a href="/wiki/2009_World_Junior_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2009 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships">2009 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lacrosse">Lacrosse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lacrosse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The arena was the home for the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Rebel" title="Ottawa Rebel">Ottawa Rebel</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Lacrosse_League" title="National Lacrosse League">National Lacrosse League</a> for one and a half seasons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pro_Wrestling">Pro Wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pro Wrestling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wrestling_Entertainment" title="World Wrestling Entertainment">World Wrestling Federation</a> has used the arena jointly with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Guertin_Centre" title="Robert Guertin Centre">Robert Guertin Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gatineau" title="Gatineau">Gatineau</a> for its shows in Ottawa until The Palladium (Canadian Tire Centre) was built in 1996. From 1992–1994, the arena would host the November event on the last Friday of the month, with the March and June events being held at Robert Guertin Arena. It still uses the arena from time to time for house shows. At a September 1991 Superstars of Wrestling taping, <a href="/wiki/Ric_Flair" title="Ric Flair">Ric Flair</a> made his WWF television debut.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-sports_usage">Non-sports usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TD_Place_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-sports usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1968, the arena hosted the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada_leadership_election,_1968" title="Liberal Party of Canada leadership election, 1968">Liberal Party of Canada leadership convention</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Trudeau" title="Pierre Trudeau">Pierre Trudeau</a> was named Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a>.</p>
<p>Until the mid-2000s, the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_SuperEX" title="Ottawa SuperEX">Central Canada Exhibition, aka 'Ottawa SuperEx'</a> used the arena for concerts and exhibits.</p>
