<p><b>MPEG-1 and/or MPEG-2 Audio Layer III</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfc5219_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rfc5219-4">[4]</a></sup> more commonly referred to as <b>MP3</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Audio_coding_format" title="Audio coding format">audio coding format</a> for <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">digital audio</a> which uses a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossy_data_compression" title="Lossy data compression">lossy data compression</a>. It is a common audio format for consumer audio <a href="/wiki/Streaming_media" title="Streaming media">streaming</a> or storage, as well as a <a href="/wiki/De_facto_standard" title="De facto standard">de facto standard</a> of digital audio compression for the transfer and playback of music on most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player">digital audio players</a> and computing devices.</p>
<p>The use of lossy compression is designed to reduce by a factor of 10 the amount of data required to represent digital audio recordings yet still sound like the original uncompressed audio to most listeners.</p>
<p>Compared to CD quality digital audio, MP3 compression commonly achieves 75 to 95% reduction in size. MP3 files are thus 1/4 to 1/20 the size of the original digital audio stream. This is important for both transmission and storage concerns. The basis for such comparison is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_CD_standard)" title="Red Book (audio CD standard)">CD-ROM digital audio</a> format which requires 1411200 bit/s. A commonly used MP3 encoding setting is CBR 128 kbit/s resulting in file size 1/11 (=9% or 91% compression) of the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_CD_standard)" title="Red Book (audio CD standard)">CD</a>-quality file.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MP3 lossy compression works by reducing (or approximating) the accuracy of certain parts of a continuous sound that are considered to be beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">auditory</a> resolution ability of most people. This method is commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">perceptual coding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jayant1993_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jayant1993-8">[8]</a></sup> It uses psychoacoustic models to discard or reduce precision of components less audible to human hearing, and then records the remaining information in an efficient manner.</p>
<p>MP3 was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Moving_Picture_Experts_Group" title="Moving Picture Experts Group">Moving Picture Experts Group</a> (MPEG) as part of its <a href="/wiki/MPEG-1" title="MPEG-1">MPEG-1</a> standard and later extended in the <a href="/wiki/MPEG-2" title="MPEG-2">MPEG-2</a> standard. The first subgroup for audio was formed by several teams of engineers at <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer IIS</a>, University of Hanover, <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">AT&amp;T-Bell Labs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomson_SA" title="Thomson SA">Thomson-Brandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centre_commun_d%27%C3%A9tudes_de_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9vision_et_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9communications" title="Centre commun d'études de télévision et télécommunications">CCETT</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiariglione_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiariglione-9">[9]</a></sup> MPEG-1 Audio (MPEG-1 Part 3), which included MPEG-1 Audio Layer I, II and III was approved as a committee draft of <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> standard in 1991,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd-1991_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cd-1991-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neuron2-cd-1991_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-neuron2-cd-1991-11">[11]</a></sup> finalised in 1992<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dis-1992_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-dis-1992-12">[12]</a></sup> and published in 1993 (ISO/IEC 11172-3:1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11172-3_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-11172-3-5">[5]</a></sup>). A backwards compatible MPEG-2 Audio (MPEG-2 Part 3) extension with lower sample and bit rates was published in 1995 (ISO/IEC 13818-3:1995).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13818-3_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-13818-3-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpeg-audio-faq-bc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpeg-audio-faq-bc-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>MP3 is a streaming or broadcast format (as opposed to a file format) meaning that individual frames can be lost without affecting the ability to decode successfully delivered frames. Storing an MP3 stream in a file enables time shifted playback.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MP3 lossy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_compression_(data)" title="Audio compression (data)">audio data compression</a> algorithm takes advantage of a perceptual limitation of human hearing called <a href="/wiki/Auditory_masking" title="Auditory masking">auditory masking</a>. In 1894, the American physicist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_M._Mayer" title="Alfred M. Mayer">Alfred M. Mayer</a> reported that a tone could be rendered inaudible by another tone of lower frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayer1894_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayer1894-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1959, Richard Ehmer described a complete set of auditory curves regarding this phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehmer1959_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehmer1959-15">[15]</a></sup> Ernst Terhardt <i>et al.</i> created an algorithm describing auditory masking with high accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Terhardt1982_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Terhardt1982-16">[16]</a></sup> This work added to a variety of reports from authors dating back to Fletcher, and to the work that initially determined critical ratios and critical bandwidths.</p>
<p>The psychoacoustic masking <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a> was first proposed in 1979, apparently independently, by <a href="/wiki/Manfred_R._Schroeder" title="Manfred R. Schroeder">Manfred R. Schroeder</a>, et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schroeder1979_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder1979-17">[17]</a></sup> from Bell Telephone Laboratories, Inc. in <a href="/wiki/Murray_Hill,_New_Jersey" title="Murray Hill, New Jersey">Murray Hill, New Jersey</a>, and M. A. Krasner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-18">[18]</a></sup> both in the United States. Krasner was the first to publish and to produce hardware for speech (not usable as music bit compression), but the publication of his results as a relatively obscure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Laboratory" title="Lincoln Laboratory">Lincoln Laboratory</a> Technical Report did not immediately influence the mainstream of psychoacoustic codec development. Manfred Schroeder was already a well-known and revered figure in the worldwide community of acoustical and electrical engineers, but his paper was not much noticed, since it described negative results due to the particular nature of speech and the <a href="/wiki/Linear_predictive_coding" title="Linear predictive coding">linear predictive coding</a> (LPC) gain present in speech. Both Krasner and Schroeder built upon the work performed by Eberhard F. Zwicker in the areas of tuning and masking of critical bands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zwicker_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zwicker-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eberhard_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eberhard-20">[20]</a></sup> that in turn built on the fundamental research in the area from <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> of Harvey Fletcher and his collaborators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher-21">[21]</a></sup> A wide variety of (mostly perceptual) audio compression algorithms were reported in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>'s refereed Journal on Selected Areas in Communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voice_Coding_for_Communications_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voice_Coding_for_Communications-22">[22]</a></sup> That journal reported in February 1988 on a wide range of established, working audio bit compression technologies, some of them using auditory masking as part of their fundamental design, and several showing real-time hardware implementations.</p>
<p>The immediate predecessors of MP3 were "Optimum Coding in the Frequency Domain" (OCF),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandenburg_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandenburg-23">[23]</a></sup> and Perceptual Transform Coding (PXFM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston1988_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston1988-24">[24]</a></sup> These two codecs, along with block-switching contributions from Thomson-Brandt, were merged into a codec called ASPEC, which was submitted to MPEG, and which won the quality competition, but that was mistakenly rejected as too complex to implement. The first practical implementation of an audio perceptual coder (OCF) in hardware (Krasner's hardware was too cumbersome and slow for practical use), was an implementation of a psychoacoustic transform coder based on <a href="/wiki/Motorola_56000" title="Motorola 56000">Motorola 56000</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Signal_Processor" title="Digital Signal Processor">DSP</a> chips.</p>
<p>As a doctoral student at Germany's <a href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen-Nuremberg" title="University of Erlangen-Nuremberg">University of Erlangen-Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Brandenburg" title="Karlheinz Brandenburg">Karlheinz Brandenburg</a> began working on digital music compression in the early 1980s, focusing on how people perceive music. He completed his doctoral work in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BusinessWeek_2007_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessWeek_2007-25">[25]</a></sup> MP3 is directly descended from OCF and PXFM, representing the outcome of the collaboration of Brandenburg—working as a postdoc at AT&amp;T-Bell Labs with James D. Johnston ("JJ") of AT&amp;T-Bell Labs—with the Fraunhofer Institute for Integrated Circuits, Erlangen (where he worked with <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Grill" title="Bernhard Grill">Bernhard Grill</a> and four other researchers - "The Original Six"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>), with relatively minor contributions from the MP2 branch of psychoacoustic sub-band coders. In 1990, Brandenburg became an assistant professor at Erlangen-Nuremberg. While there, he continued to work on music compression with scientists at the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> (in 1993 he joined the staff of the Fraunhofer Institute).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BusinessWeek_2007_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessWeek_2007-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song "<a href="/wiki/Tom%27s_Diner" title="Tom's Diner">Tom's Diner</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Vega" title="Suzanne Vega">Suzanne Vega</a> was the first song used by Karlheinz Brandenburg to develop the MP3. Brandenburg adopted the song for testing purposes, listening to it again and again each time refining the scheme, making sure it did not adversely affect the subtlety of Vega's voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterne_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sterne-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization">Standardization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standardization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, there were two available proposals that were assessed for an MPEG audio standard: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musicam" title="Musicam">Musicam</a> (<u>M</u>asking pattern adapted <u>U</u>niversal <u>S</u>ubband <u>I</u>ntegrated <u>C</u>oding <u>A</u>nd <u>M</u>ultiplexing) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adaptive_Spectral_Perceptual_Entropy_Coding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adaptive Spectral Perceptual Entropy Coding (page does not exist)">ASPEC</a> (<u>A</u>daptive <u>S</u>pectral <u>P</u>erceptual <u>E</u>ntropy <u>C</u>oding). As proposed by the Dutch corporation <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a>, the French research institute CCETT, and the German standards organization <a href="/wiki/Institut_f%C3%BCr_Rundfunktechnik" title="Institut für Rundfunktechnik">Institute for Broadcast Technology</a>, the Musicam technique was chosen due to its simplicity and error robustness, as well as for its high level of computational efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-santa-clara-1990_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-santa-clara-1990-28">[28]</a></sup> The Musicam format, based on <a href="/wiki/Sub-band_coding" title="Sub-band coding">sub-band coding</a>, became the basis for the MPEG Audio compression format, incorporating, for example, its frame structure, header format, sample rates, etc.</p>
<p>While much of Musicam's technology and ideas were incorporated into the definition of MPEG Audio Layer I and Layer II, only the filter bank alone would remain in the Layer III (MP3) format, as part of the computationally inefficient hybrid <a href="/wiki/Filter_(software)" title="Filter (software)">filter</a> bank. Under the chairmanship of Professor Musmann of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hanover" title="University of Hanover">University of Hanover</a>, the editing of the standard was delegated to Dutchman <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leon_van_de_Kerkhof&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leon van de Kerkhof (page does not exist)">Leon van de Kerkhof</a> and to German <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Stoll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gerhard Stoll (page does not exist)">Gerhard Stoll</a>, who worked on Layer I and Layer II respectively.</p>
<p>ASPEC was the joint proposal of AT&amp;T Bell Laboratories, Thomson Consumer Electronics, Fraunhofer Society and <a href="/wiki/Centre_national_d%27%C3%A9tudes_des_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9communications" title="Centre national d'études des télécommunications">CNET</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aspec_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aspec-29">[29]</a></sup> It provided the highest coding efficiency.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Working_group" title="Working group">working group</a> consisting of van de Kerkhof, Stoll, Italian <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Chiariglione" title="Leonardo Chiariglione">Leonardo Chiariglione</a> (<a href="/wiki/CSELT" title="CSELT">CSELT</a> VP for Media), Frenchman <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yves-Fran%C3%A7ois_Dehery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yves-François Dehery (page does not exist)">Yves-François Dehery</a>, German Karlheinz Brandenburg, and American James D. Johnston (United States) took ideas from ASPEC, integrated the filter bank from Layer II, added some of their own ideas and created the MP3 format, which was designed to achieve the same quality at 128 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kbit/s" title="Kbit/s">kbit/s</a> as <a href="/wiki/MPEG-1_Audio_Layer_II" title="MPEG-1 Audio Layer II">MP2</a> at 192 kbit/s.</p>
<p>The algorithms for MPEG-1 Audio Layer I, II and III were approved in 1991<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd-1991_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-cd-1991-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neuron2-cd-1991_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-neuron2-cd-1991-11">[11]</a></sup> and finalized in 1992<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dis-1992_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-dis-1992-12">[12]</a></sup> as part of <a href="/wiki/MPEG-1" title="MPEG-1">MPEG-1</a>, the first standard suite by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPEG" title="MPEG">MPEG</a>, which resulted in the international standard <b><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> 11172-3</b> (a.k.a. <i>MPEG-1 Audio</i> or <i>MPEG-1 Part 3</i>), published in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11172-3_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-11172-3-5">[5]</a></sup> Files or data streams conforming to this standard must handle sample rates of 48k, 44100 and 32k and continue to be supported by current MP3 players and decoders. Thus the first generation of MP3 defined 14*3=42 interpretations of MP3 frame data structures and size layouts.</p>
<p>Further work on MPEG audio<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sydney1993_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-sydney1993-30">[30]</a></sup> was finalized in 1994 as part of the second suite of MPEG standards, <a href="/wiki/MPEG-2" title="MPEG-2">MPEG-2</a>, more formally known as international standard <b>ISO/IEC 13818-3</b> (a.k.a. <i>MPEG-2 Part 3</i> or backwards compatible <i>MPEG-2 Audio</i> or <i>MPEG-2 Audio BC</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpeg-audio-faq-bc_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-mpeg-audio-faq-bc-13">[13]</a></sup>), originally published in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13818-3_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-13818-3-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandenburg1997_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandenburg1997-31">[31]</a></sup> MPEG-2 Part 3 (ISO/IEC 13818-3) defined 42 additional bit rates and sample rates for MPEG-1 Audio Layer I, II and III. The new sampling rates are exactly half that of those originally defined in MPEG-1 Audio. This reduction in sampling rate serves to cut the available frequency fidelity in half while likewise cutting the bitrate by 50%. MPEG-2 Part 3 also enhanced MPEG-1's audio by allowing the coding of audio programs with more than two channels, up to 5.1 multichannel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sydney1993_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-sydney1993-30">[30]</a></sup> An MP3 coded with MPEG-2 results in half of the bandwidth reproduction of MPEG-1 appropriate for piano and singing.</p>
<p>A third generation of "MP3" style data streams (files) extended the <i>MPEG-2</i> ideas and implementation but was named <i>MPEG-2.5</i> audio, since MPEG-3 already had a different meaning. This extension was developed at Fraunhofer IIS, the registered patent holders of MP3 by reducing the frame sync field in the MP3 header from 12 to 11 bits. As in the transition from MPEG-1 to MPEG-2, MPEG-2.5 adds additional sampling rates exactly half of those available using MPEG-2. It thus widens the scope of MP3 to include human speech and other applications yet requires only 25% of the bandwidth (frequency reproduction) possible using MPEG-1 sampling rates. While not an ISO recognized standard, MPEG-2.5 is widely supported by both inexpensive Chinese and brand name digital audio players as well as computer software based MP3 encoders (LAME), decoders (FFmpeg) and players (MPC) adding 3*8=24 additional MP3 frame types. Each generation of MP3 thus supports 3 sampling rates exactly half that of the previous generation for a total of 9 varieties of MP3 format files. The sample rate comparison table between MPEG-1, 2 and 2.5 is given later in the article.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5-2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-2-33">[33]</a></sup> MPEG 2.5 is supported by both LAME (since 2000), Media Player Classic (MPC), iTunes, and FFmpeg.</p>
<p>MPEG-2.5 was not developed by MPEG (see above) and was never approved as an international standard. MPEG-2.5 is thus an unofficial or proprietary extension to the MP3 format. It is nonetheless ubiquitous and especially advantageous for low-bit rate human speech applications.</p>

<p>Compression efficiency of encoders is typically defined by the bit rate, because compression ratio depends on the <a href="/wiki/Audio_bit_depth" title="Audio bit depth">bit depth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_rate" title="Sampling rate">sampling rate</a> of the input signal. Nevertheless, compression ratios are often published. They may use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Disc" title="Compact Disc">Compact Disc</a> (CD) parameters as references (44.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a>, 2 channels at 16 bits per channel or 2×16 bit), or sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Tape" title="Digital Audio Tape">Digital Audio Tape</a> (DAT) SP parameters (48 kHz, 2×16 bit). Compression ratios with this latter reference are higher, which demonstrates the problem with use of the term <i>compression ratio</i> for lossy encoders.</p>
<p>Karlheinz Brandenburg used a CD recording of <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Vega" title="Suzanne Vega">Suzanne Vega</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Tom%27s_Diner" title="Tom's Diner">Tom's Diner</a>" to assess and refine the MP3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_algorithm" title="Compression algorithm">compression algorithm</a>. This song was chosen because of its nearly <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">monophonic</a> nature and wide spectral content, making it easier to hear imperfections in the compression format during playbacks. Some refer to Suzanne Vega as "The mother of MP3".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motherofmp3_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-motherofmp3-35">[35]</a></sup> This particular track has an interesting property in that the two channels are almost, but not completely, the same, leading to a case where Binaural Masking Level Depression causes spatial unmasking of noise artifacts unless the encoder properly recognizes the situation and applies corrections similar to those detailed in the MPEG-2 AAC psychoacoustic model. Some more critical audio excerpts (<a href="/wiki/Glockenspiel" title="Glockenspiel">glockenspiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle_(musical_instrument)" title="Triangle (musical instrument)">triangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a>, etc.) were taken from the <a href="/wiki/European_Broadcasting_Union" title="European Broadcasting Union">EBU</a> V3/SQAM reference compact disc and have been used by professional sound engineers to assess the subjective quality of the MPEG Audio formats.</p>
<p>LAME is the most advanced MP3 encoder. LAME includes a VBR variable bit rate encoding which uses a quality parameter rather than a bit rate goal. Later versions 2008+) support an n.nnn quality goal which automatically selects MPEG -2 or MPEG-2.5 sampling rates as appropriate for human speech recordings which need only 5512 Hz bandwidth resolution.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Going_public">Going public</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Going public">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A reference simulation software implementation, written in the C language and later known as <i>ISO 11172-5</i>, was developed (in 1991–1996) by the members of the ISO MPEG Audio committee in order to produce bit compliant MPEG Audio files (Layer 1, Layer 2, Layer 3). It was approved as a committee draft of ISO/IEC technical report in March 1994 and printed as document CD 11172-5 in April 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paris_press_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-paris_press-36">[36]</a></sup> It was approved as a draft technical report (DTR/DIS) in November 1994,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singapore_press_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-singapore_press-37">[37]</a></sup> finalized in 1996 and published as international standard ISO/IEC TR 11172-5:1998 in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO.2FIEC_TR_11172-5:1998_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISO.2FIEC_TR_11172-5:1998-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_implementation_(computing)" title="Reference implementation (computing)">reference software</a> in C language was later published as a freely available ISO standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Software_Simulation.zip_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Software_Simulation.zip-39">[39]</a></sup> Working in non-real time on a number of operating systems, it was able to demonstrate the first real time hardware decoding (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Signal_Processor" title="Digital Signal Processor">DSP</a> based) of compressed audio. Some other real time implementation of MPEG Audio encoders were available for the purpose of digital broadcasting (radio DAB, television DVB) towards consumer receivers and set top boxes.</p>
<p>On 7 July 1994, the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> released the first software MP3 encoder called <a href="/wiki/L3enc" title="L3enc">l3enc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MP3_Todays_Technology_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-MP3_Todays_Technology-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Filename_extension" title="Filename extension">filename extension</a> <i>.mp3</i> was chosen by the Fraunhofer team on 14 July 1995 (previously, the files had been named <i>.bit</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mp3-name_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mp3-name-1">[1]</a></sup> With the first real-time software MP3 player <a href="/wiki/WinPlay3" title="WinPlay3">WinPlay3</a> (released 9 September 1995) many people were able to encode and play back MP3 files on their PCs. Because of the relatively small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">hard drives</a> back in that time (~ 500–1000 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a>) lossy compression was essential to store non-instrument based (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracker_(music_software)" title="Tracker (music software)">tracker</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_Instrument_Digital_Interface" title="Musical Instrument Digital Interface">MIDI</a>) music for playback on computer.</p>
<p>As sound scholar Jonathan Sterne notes, "An Australian hacker acquired <a href="/wiki/L3enc" title="L3enc">l3enc</a> using a stolen credit card. The hacker then reverse-engineered the software, wrote a new user interface, and redistributed it for free, naming it "thank you Fraunhofer"".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterne.2B_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sterne.2B-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_distribution">Internet distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Internet distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second half of '90s, MP3 files began to spread on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. The popularity of MP3s began to rise rapidly with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Nullsoft" title="Nullsoft">Nullsoft</a>'s audio player <a href="/wiki/Winamp" title="Winamp">Winamp</a>, released in 1997. In 1998, the first portable solid state digital audio player <a href="/wiki/MPMan" title="MPMan">MPMan</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/SaeHan_Information_Systems" title="SaeHan Information Systems">SaeHan Information Systems</a> which is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, was released and the <a href="/wiki/Rio_PMP300" title="Rio PMP300">Rio PMP300</a> was sold afterwards in 1998, despite legal suppression efforts by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattlepi_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattlepi-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1997, the website <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mp3.com" title="Mp3.com">mp3.com</a> was offering thousands of MP3s created by independent artists for free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattlepi_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-seattlepi-42">[42]</a></sup> The small size of MP3 files enabled widespread <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> of music <a href="/wiki/Ripping" title="Ripping">ripped</a> from CDs, which would have previously been nearly impossible. The first large peer-to-peer filesharing network, <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a>, was launched in 1999.</p>
<p>The ease of creating and sharing MP3s resulted in widespread <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> infringement. Major record companies argued that this free sharing of music reduced sales, and called it "<a href="/wiki/Music_piracy" title="Music piracy">music piracy</a>". They reacted by pursuing lawsuits against <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a> (which was eventually shut down and later sold) and against individual users who engaged in file sharing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giesler_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giesler-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unauthorized MP3 file sharing continues on next-generation <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer networks</a>. Some authorized services, such as <a href="/wiki/Beatport" title="Beatport">Beatport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bleep.com" title="Bleep.com">Bleep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juno_Records" title="Juno Records">Juno Records</a>, <a href="/wiki/EMusic" title="EMusic">eMusic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zune_Marketplace" title="Zune Marketplace">Zune Marketplace</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Walmart.com</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a>, the recording industry approved re-incarnation of <a href="/wiki/Napster_(pay_service)" title="Napster (pay service)">Napster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> sell unrestricted music in the MP3 format.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="File_structure">File structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: File structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An MP3 file is made up of MP3 frames, which consist of a header and a data block. This sequence of frames is called an <a href="/wiki/Elementary_stream" title="Elementary stream">elementary stream</a>. Due to the "byte reservoir", frames are not independent items and cannot usually be extracted on arbitrary frame boundaries. The MP3 Data blocks contain the (compressed) audio information in terms of frequencies and amplitudes. The diagram shows that the MP3 Header consists of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sync_word" title="Sync word">sync word</a>, which is used to identify the beginning of a valid frame. This is followed by a bit indicating that this is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPEG" title="MPEG">MPEG</a> standard and two bits that indicate that layer 3 is used; hence MPEG-1 Audio Layer 3 or MP3. After this, the values will differ, depending on the MP3 file. <i><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> 11172-3</i> defines the range of values for each section of the header along with the specification of the header. Most MP3 files today contain <a href="/wiki/ID3" title="ID3">ID3</a> <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>, which precedes or follows the MP3 frames, as noted in the diagram.</p>
<p>The data stream can contain an optional checksum, but the checksum only protects the header data, not the audio data.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_stereo" title="Joint stereo">Joint stereo</a> is done only on a frame-to-frame basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Limitations_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Limitations-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Encoding_and_decoding">Encoding and decoding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Encoding and decoding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/MPEG-1" title="MPEG-1">MPEG-1</a> standard does not include a precise specification for an MP3 encoder, but does provide example psychoacoustic models, rate loop, and the like in the non-normative part of the original standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpeg1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpeg1-45">[45]</a></sup> MPEG-2 doubles the number of sampling rates which are supported and MPEG-2.5 adds 3 more.</p>
<p>When this was written, the suggested implementations were quite dated. Implementers of the standard were supposed to devise their own algorithms suitable for removing parts of the information from the audio input. As a result, many different MP3 encoders became available, each producing files of differing quality. Comparisons were widely available, so it was easy for a prospective user of an encoder to research the best choice. Some encoders that were proficient at encoding at higher bit rates (such as <a href="/wiki/LAME" title="LAME">LAME</a>) were not necessarily as good at lower bit rates. Over time, LAME evolved on the SourceForge website until it became the de facto CBR MP3 encoder. Later an ABR mode was added. Work progressed on true variable bit rate using a quality goal between 0 and 10. Eventually numbers (such as -V 9.600) could generate excellent quality low bit rate voice encoding at only 41kbit/sec using the mpeg-2.5 extensions.</p>
<p>During encoding, 576 time-domain samples are taken and are transformed to 576 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_Transform" title="Fourier Transform">frequency-domain samples</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="576 samples per what? per mpeg-1 frame? This was probably written during the time MP3 encoding algorithms were being developed, criticized and possibly improved by aac proponents. U (December 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If there is a <a href="/wiki/Transient_(acoustics)" title="Transient (acoustics)">transient</a>, 192 samples are taken instead of 576. This is done to limit the temporal spread of quantization noise accompanying the transient. (See <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">psychoacoustics</a>.)</p>
<p>Frequency resolution is limited by the small long block window size, which decreases coding efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Limitations_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Limitations-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Time resolution can be too low for highly transient signals and may cause smearing of percussive sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Limitations_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Limitations-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the tree structure of the filter bank, pre-echo problems are made worse, as the combined impulse response of the two filter banks does not, and cannot, provide an optimum solution in time/frequency resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Limitations_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Limitations-44">[44]</a></sup> Additionally, the combining of the two filter banks' outputs creates aliasing problems that must be handled partially by the "aliasing compensation" stage; however, that creates excess energy to be coded in the frequency domain, thereby decreasing coding efficiency.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Decoding, on the other hand, is carefully defined in the standard. Most <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">decoders</a> are "<a href="/wiki/Elementary_stream" title="Elementary stream">bitstream</a> compliant", which means that the decompressed output that they produce from a given MP3 file will be the same, within a specified degree of <a href="/wiki/Rounding" title="Rounding">rounding</a> tolerance, as the output specified mathematically in the ISO/IEC high standard document (ISO/IEC 11172-3). Therefore, comparison of decoders is usually based on how computationally efficient they are (i.e., how much <a href="/wiki/Computer_memory" title="Computer memory">memory</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPU" title="CPU">CPU</a> time they use in the decoding process). Over time this concern has become less of an issue as CPU speeds transitioned from MHz to GHz.</p>
<p>Encoder / decoder overall delay is not defined, which means there is no official provision for <a href="/wiki/Gapless_playback" title="Gapless playback">gapless playback</a>. However, some encoders such as <a href="/wiki/LAME" title="LAME">LAME</a> can attach additional metadata that will allow players that can handle it to deliver seamless playback.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality">Quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When performing lossy audio encoding, such as creating an MP3 data stream, there is a trade-off between the amount of data generated and the sound quality of the results. The person generating an MP3 selects a <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rate</a>, which specifies how many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilobits" title="Kilobits">kilobits</a> per second of audio are desired. The higher the bit rate, the larger the MP3 data stream will be, and, generally, the closer it will sound to the original recording.</p>
<p>With too low a bit rate, <a href="/wiki/Compression_artifact" title="Compression artifact">compression artifacts</a> (i.e., sounds that were not present in the original recording) may be audible in the reproduction. Some audio is hard to compress because of its randomness and sharp attacks. When this type of audio is compressed, artifacts such as ringing or <a href="/wiki/Pre-echo" title="Pre-echo">pre-echo</a> are usually heard. A sample of applause compressed with a relatively low bit rate provides a good example of compression artifacts.</p>
<p>Besides the bit rate of an encoded piece of audio, the quality of MP3 encoded sound also depends on the quality of the encoder algorithm as well as the complexity of the signal being encoded. As the MP3 standard allows quite a bit of freedom with encoding algorithms, different encoders do feature quite different quality, even with identical bit rates. As an example, in a public listening test featuring two early MP3 encoders set at about 128 kbit/s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amorim_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amorim-46">[46]</a></sup> one scored 3.66 on a 1–5 scale, while the other scored only 2.22.</p>
<p>Quality is dependent on the choice of encoder and encoding parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-listening-test-128-2006_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-listening-test-128-2006-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>This observation caused a revolution in audio encoding. Early on bitrate was the prime and only consideration. At the time MP3 files were of the very simplest type: they used the same bit rate for the entire file: this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Constant_bitrate" title="Constant bitrate">Constant Bit Rate</a> (CBR) encoding. Using a constant bit rate makes encoding simpler and less CPU intensive. However, it is also possible to create files where the bit rate changes throughout the file. These are known as <a href="/wiki/Variable_bitrate" title="Variable bitrate">Variable Bit Rate</a> The bit reservoir and VBR encoding were actually part of the original MPEG-1 standard. The concept behind them is that, in any piece of audio, some sections are easier to compress, such as silence or music containing only a few tones, while others will be more difficult to compress. So, the overall quality of the file may be increased by using a lower bit rate for the less complex passages and a higher one for the more complex parts. With some advanced MP3 encoders, it is possible to specify a given quality, and the encoder will adjust the bit rate accordingly. Users that desire a particular "quality setting" that is <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(data_compression)" title="Transparency (data compression)">transparent</a> to their ears can use this value when encoding all of their music, and generally speaking not need to worry about performing personal listening tests on each piece of music to determine the correct bit rate.</p>
<p>Perceived quality can be influenced by listening environment (ambient noise), listener attention, and listener training and in most cases by listener audio equipment (such as sound cards, speakers and headphones). Furthermore, sufficient quality may be achieved by a lesser quality setting for lectures and human speech applications and reduces encoding time and complexity.</p>
<p>A test given to new students by <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> Music Professor Jonathan Berger showed that student preference for MP3-quality music has risen each year. Berger said the students seem to prefer the 'sizzle' sounds that MP3s bring to music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dougherty_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dougherty-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>An in-depth study of MP3 audio quality, sound artist and composer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ryan_Maguire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ryan Maguire (page does not exist)">Ryan Maguire</a>'s project "The Ghost in the MP3" isolates the sounds lost during MP3 compression. In 2015, he released the track "moDernisT" (an anagram of "Tom's Diner"), composed exclusively from the sounds deleted during MP3 compression of the song "Tom's Diner",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noisey_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-noisey-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schroeder2015_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-schroeder2015-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull2015_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-hull2015-51">[51]</a></sup> the track originally used in the formulation of the MP3 standard. A detailed account of the techniques used to isolate the sounds deleted during MP3 compression, along with the conceptual motivation for the project, was published in the 2014 Proceedings of the International Computer Music Conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maguire2014_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maguire2014-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bitrate is the product of the sample rate and number of bits per sample. CD audio is 44100 samples per second. The number of bits per sample also depends on the number of audio channels. CD is stereo and 16 bits per channel. So, multiplying 44100 by 32 gives 1411200—the bitrate of uncompressed CD digital audio. MP3 was designed to encode 1411 kbit/s at 320 kbit/s or less. As less complex passages are detected by MP3 algorithms then lower bitrates may be employed. MPEG-2 reduces bitrate further by cutting samples to only 8 bits in size and half the samples per second. MPEG-2 thus cuts half of the upper frequency spectrum of mpeg-1 off.</p>
<p>As shown in these two tables, 14 selected <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rates</a> are allowed in MPEG-1 Audio Layer III standard: 32, 40, 48, 56, 64, 80, 96, 112, 128, 160, 192, 224, 256 and 320 kbit/s, along with the 3 highest available <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_frequencies" title="Sampling frequencies">sampling frequencies</a> of 32, 44.1 and 48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5-2_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-2-33">[33]</a></sup> MPEG-2 Audio Layer III also allows 14 somewhat different (and mostly lower) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bit_rates" title="Bit rates">bit rates</a> of 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56, 64, 80, 96, 112, 128, 144, 160 kbit/s with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_frequencies" title="Sampling frequencies">sampling frequencies</a> of 16, 22.05 and 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a> which are exactly half that of MPEG-1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5-2_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-2-33">[33]</a></sup> MPEG-2.5 Audio Layer III frames are limited to only 8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bit_rates" title="Bit rates">bit rates</a> of 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56 and 64 kbit/s with 3 even lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_frequencies" title="Sampling frequencies">sampling frequencies</a> of 8, 11.025, and 12 kHz.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Based on results from the LAME encoder, these do seem to be the actual bit rates supported by MPEG-2.5, but official documents claim MPEG-2.5 has the same possible bit rates as MPEG-2. Answer: Bitrate switching implies VBR so, it is not CBR anymore. When MPEG-2 frames are needed instead of the smaller 2.5 frames, the former are generated. Can we find a source that mentions this limitation? (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>MPEG-1 frames contain the most detail in 320kbit/s mode with silence and simple tones still requiring 32 kbit/s. MPEG-2 frames can capture up to 12 kHz sound reproductions needed up to 160kbit/s. MP3 files made with MPEG-2 don't have 20 kHz bandwidth because of the <a href="/wiki/Nyquist%E2%80%93Shannon_sampling_theorem" title="Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem">Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem</a>. Frequency reproduction is always strictly less than half of the sampling frequency, and imperfect filters require a larger margin for error (noise level versus sharpness of filter), so an 8 kHz sampling rate limits the maximum frequency to 4 kHz, while a 48 kHz sampling rate limits an MP3 to a maximum 24 kHz sound reproduction. MPEG-2 uses half and MPEG-2.5 only a quarter of MPEG-1 sample rates.</p>
<p>For the general field of human speech reproduction, a bandwidth of 5512 Hz is sufficient to produce excellent results (for voice) using the sampling rate of 11025 and VBR encoding from 44100 (standard) wave files.. This is easily accomplished using LAME version 3.99.5 and the command line "lame -V 9.6 lecture.WAV" English speakers average 41-42kbit/s with -V 9.6 setting but this may vary with amount of silence recorded or the rate of delivery (wpm). Resampling to 12000 (6K bandwidth) is selected by the LAME parameter -V 9.4 Likewise -V 9.2 selects 16000 sample rate and a resultant 8K lowpass filtering. For more info see Nyquist - Shannon. Older versions of LAME and FFmpeg only support integer arguments for variable bit rate quality selection parameter. The n.nnn quality parameter (-V) is documented at lame.sourceforge.net but is only supported in LAME with the new style VBR variable bit rate quality selector—not average bit rate (ABR).</p>
<p>A sample rate of 44.1 kHz is commonly used for music reproduction, because this is also used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_CD_standard)" title="Red Book (audio CD standard)">CD audio</a>, the main source used for creating MP3 files. A great variety of bit rates are used on the Internet. A bit rate of 128 kbit/s is commonly used,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woon-Seng_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woon-Seng-54">[54]</a></sup> at a compression ratio of 11:1, offering adequate audio quality in a relatively small space. As Internet <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> availability and hard drive sizes have increased, higher bit rates up to 320 kbit/s are widespread.</p>
<p>Uncompressed audio as stored on an audio-CD has a bit rate of 1,411.2 kbit/s, (16 bit/sample × 44100 samples/second × 2 channels / 1000 bits/kilobit), so the bitrates 128, 160 and 192 kbit/s represent <a href="/wiki/Data_compression_ratio" title="Data compression ratio">compression ratios</a> of approximately 11:1, 9:1 and 7:1 respectively.</p>
<p>Non-standard bit rates up to 640 kbit/s can be achieved with the <a href="/wiki/LAME" title="LAME">LAME</a> encoder and the freeformat option, although few MP3 players can play those files. According to the ISO standard, decoders are only required to be able to decode streams up to 320 kbit/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouvigne_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouvigne-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early MPEG Layer III encoders used what is now called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constant_Bit_Rate" title="Constant Bit Rate">Constant Bit Rate</a> (CBR). The software was only able to use a uniform bitrate on all frames in an MP3 file. Later more sophisticated MP3 encoders were able to use the bit reservoir to target an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Average_bit_rate" title="Average bit rate">average bit rate</a> selecting the encoding rate for each frame based on the complexity of the sound in that portion of the recording.</p>
<p>A more sophisticated MP3 encoder can produce <a href="/wiki/Variable_bitrate" title="Variable bitrate">variable bitrate</a> audio. MPEG audio may use bitrate switching on a per-frame basis, but only layer III decoders must support it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5-2_33-6"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-2-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAME_GPSYCHO_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAME_GPSYCHO-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoLAME_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-TwoLAME-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-1_and_MPEG-2_BC_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-1_and_MPEG-2_BC-58">[58]</a></sup> VBR is used when the goal is to achieve a fixed level of quality. The final file size of a VBR encoding is less predictable than with <a href="/wiki/Constant_bitrate" title="Constant bitrate">constant bitrate</a>. <a href="/wiki/Average_bitrate" title="Average bitrate">Average bitrate</a> is a type of VBR implemented as a compromise between the two: the bitrate is allowed to vary for more consistent quality, but is controlled to remain near an average value chosen by the user, for predictable file sizes. Although an MP3 decoder must support VBR to be standards compliant, historically some decoders have bugs with VBR decoding, particularly before VBR encoders became widespread. The most evolved LAME MP3 encoder supports the generation of VBR, ABR, and even the ancient CBR MP3 formats.</p>
<p>Layer III audio can also use a "bit reservoir", a partially full frame's ability to hold part of the next frame's audio data, allowing temporary changes in effective bitrate, even in a constant bitrate stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEG-2.5-2_33-7"><a href="#cite_note-MPEG-2.5-2-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAME_GPSYCHO_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAME_GPSYCHO-56">[56]</a></sup> Internal handling of the bit reservoir increases encoding delay.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There is no scale factor band 21 (sfb21) for frequencies above approx 16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a>, forcing the encoder to choose between less accurate representation in band 21 or less efficient storage in all bands below band 21, the latter resulting in wasted bitrate in VBR encoding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAME_Y_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAME_Y-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancillary_Data">Ancillary Data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ancillary Data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ancillary data field can be used to store user defined data. The ancillary data is optional and the number of bits available is not explicitly given. The ancillary data is located after the Huffman code bits and ranges to where the next frame’s main_data_begin points to. <a href="/wiki/Mp3PRO" title="Mp3PRO">mp3PRO</a> uses ancillary data to encode their bits to improve audio quality.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metadata">Metadata</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Metadata">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/ID3" title="ID3">ID3</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APEv2_tag" title="APEv2 tag">APEv2 tag</a></div>
<p>A "tag" in an audio file is a section of the file that contains <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> such as the title, artist, album, track number or other information about the file's contents. The MP3 standards do not define tag formats for MP3 files, nor is there a standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_format_(digital)" title="Container format (digital)">container format</a> that would support metadata and obviate the need for tags.</p>
<p>However, several <i>de facto</i> standards for tag formats exist. As of 2010, the most widespread are <a href="/wiki/ID3" title="ID3">ID3v1 and ID3v2</a>, and the more recently introduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APEv2_tag" title="APEv2 tag">APEv2</a>. These tags are normally embedded at the beginning or end of MP3 files, separate from the actual MP3 frame data. MP3 decoders either extract information from the tags, or just treat them as ignorable, non-MP3 junk data.</p>
<p>Playing &amp; editing software often contains tag editing functionality, but there are also <a href="/wiki/Tag_editor" title="Tag editor">tag editor</a> applications dedicated to the purpose.</p>
<p>Aside from metadata pertaining to the audio content, tags may also be used for <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">DRM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rae_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rae-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/ReplayGain" title="ReplayGain">ReplayGain</a> is a standard for measuring and storing the loudness of an MP3 file (<a href="/wiki/Audio_normalization" title="Audio normalization">audio normalization</a>) in its metadata tag, enabling a ReplayGain-compliant player to automatically adjust the overall playback volume for each file. <a href="/wiki/MP3Gain" title="MP3Gain">MP3Gain</a> may be used to reversibly modify files based on ReplayGain measurements so that adjusted playback can be achieved on players without ReplayGain capability.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Licensing.2C_ownership_and_legislation"><span id="Licensing_and_patent_issues"></span>Licensing, ownership and legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MP3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Licensing, ownership and legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic MP3 decoding and encoding technology is patent-free in the European Union, all patents having expired there. In the United States, the technology will be substantially patent-free on 31 December 2017 (see below). The majority of MP3 patents expired in the US between 2007 and 2015.</p>
<p>In the past, many organizations have claimed ownership of <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> related to MP3 decoding or encoding. These claims led to a number of legal threats and actions from a variety of sources. As a result, uncertainty about which patents must be licensed in order to create MP3 products without committing patent infringement in countries that allow <a href="/wiki/Software_patent" title="Software patent">software patents</a> was a common feature of the early stages of adoption of the technology.</p>
<p>The initial near-complete MPEG-1 standard (parts 1, 2 and 3) was publicly available on 6 December 1991 as ISO CD 11172.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patel_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patel-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpegfa31.txt_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpegfa31.txt-62">[62]</a></sup> In most countries, patents cannot be filed after prior art has been made public, and patents expire 20 years after the initial filing date, which can be up to 12 months later for filings in other countries. As a result, patents required to implement MP3 expired in most countries by December 2012, 21 years after the publication of ISO CD 11172.</p>
<p>An exception is the United States, where patents filed prior to 8 June 1995 expire 17 years after the publication date of the patent, but application extensions make it possible for a patent to issue much later than normally expected (see <a href="/wiki/Submarine_patent" title="Submarine patent">submarine patents</a>). The various MP3-related patents expire on dates ranging from 2007 to 2017 in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-big-list_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-big-list-63">[63]</a></sup> Patents filed for anything disclosed in ISO CD 11172 a year or more after its publication are questionable. If only the known MP3 patents filed by December 1992 are considered, then MP3 decoding has been patent-free in the US since 22 September 2015 when <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5812672" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,812,672</a></span> expired which had a PCT filing in October 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cogliati_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cogliati-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US5812672_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-US5812672-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patent_expiration_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patent_expiration-66">[66]</a></sup> If the longest-running patent mentioned in the aforementioned references is taken as a measure, then the MP3 technology will be patent-free in the United States on 30 December 2017 when <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5703999" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,703,999</a></span>, held by the Fraunhofer-Gesellschaft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and administered by <a href="/wiki/Technicolor_SA" title="Technicolor SA">Technicolor</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> expires.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Technicolor_SA" title="Technicolor SA">Technicolor</a> (formerly called Thomson Consumer Electronics) claims to control MP3 licensing of the Layer 3 patents in many countries, including the United States, Japan, Canada and EU countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffii_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-ffii-69">[69]</a></sup> Technicolor has been actively enforcing these patents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Technicolor_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Technicolor-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>MP3 license revenues generated about €100 million for the Fraunhofer Society in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kistenfeger_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kistenfeger-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 1998, the Fraunhofer Institute sent a letter to several developers of MP3 software stating that a license was required to "distribute and/or sell decoders and/or encoders". The letter claimed that unlicensed products "infringe the patent rights of Fraunhofer and Thomson. To make, sell and/or distribute products using the [MPEG Layer-3] standard and thus our patents, you need to obtain a license under these patents from us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chillingeffects_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-chillingeffects-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sisvel S.p.A. and its United States subsidiary Audio MPEG, Inc. previously sued Thomson for patent infringement on MP3 technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZDNet_India_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZDNet_India-73">[73]</a></sup> but those disputes were resolved in November 2005 with Sisvel granting Thomson a license to their patents. Motorola followed soon after, and signed with Sisvel to license MP3-related patents in December 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SISVEL_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-SISVEL-74">[74]</a></sup> Except for three patents, the US patents administered by Sisvel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> had all expired in 2015, however (the exceptions are: <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5878080" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,878,080</a></span>, expires February 2017, <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5850456" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,850,456</a></span>, expires February 2017 and <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5960037" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,960,037</a></span>, expires 9. April 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2006, German officials seized MP3 players from <a href="/wiki/SanDisk" title="SanDisk">SanDisk</a>'s booth at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFA_show" title="IFA show">IFA show</a> in Berlin after an Italian patents firm won an injunction on behalf of Sisvel against SanDisk in a dispute over licensing rights. The injunction was later reversed by a Berlin judge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanDisk_MP3_seizure_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-SanDisk_MP3_seizure-77">[77]</a></sup> but that reversal was in turn blocked the same day by another judge from the same court, "bringing the Patent Wild West to Germany" in the words of one commentator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patent_Wild_West_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patent_Wild_West-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2007, Texas MP3 Technologies sued Apple, Samsung Electronics and Sandisk in <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Eastern_District_of_Texas" title="United States District Court for the Eastern District of Texas">eastern Texas federal court</a>, claiming infringement of a portable MP3 player patent that Texas MP3 said it had been assigned. Apple, Samsung, and Sandisk all settled the claims against them in January 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-law360_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-law360-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent" title="Alcatel-Lucent">Alcatel-Lucent</a> has asserted several MP3 coding and compression patents, allegedly inherited from AT&amp;T-Bell Labs, in litigation of its own. In November 2006, before the companies' merger, Alcatel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent_v._Microsoft" title="Alcatel-Lucent v. Microsoft">sued</a> <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> for allegedly infringing seven patents. On 23 February 2007, a San Diego jury awarded <a href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent" title="Alcatel-Lucent">Alcatel-Lucent</a> US $1.52 billion in damages for infringement of two of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MP3_payout_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-MP3_payout-81">[81]</a></sup> The court subsequently tossed the award, however, finding that one patent had not been infringed and that the other was not even owned by <a href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent" title="Alcatel-Lucent">Alcatel-Lucent</a>; it was co-owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT%26T_Inc." title="AT&amp;T Inc.">AT&amp;T</a> and Fraunhofer, who had licensed it to <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, the judge ruled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Microsoft_wins_reversal_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Microsoft_wins_reversal-82">[82]</a></sup> That defense judgment was upheld on appeal in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alcatel-Lucent_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alcatel-Lucent-83">[83]</a></sup> See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent_v._Microsoft" title="Alcatel-Lucent v. Microsoft">Alcatel-Lucent v. Microsoft</a> for more information.</p>

<p>Other lossy formats exist. Among these, <a href="/wiki/Mp3PRO" title="Mp3PRO">mp3PRO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Audio_Coding" title="Advanced Audio Coding">AAC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MPEG-1_Audio_Layer_II" title="MPEG-1 Audio Layer II">MP2</a> are all members of the same technological family as MP3 and depend on roughly similar <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">psychoacoustic models</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Gesellschaft</a> owns many of the basic <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> underlying these formats as well, with others held by Alcatel-Lucent, and <a href="/wiki/Technicolor_SA" title="Technicolor SA">Thomson Consumer Electronics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandenburg_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-brandenburg-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also open compression formats like <a href="/wiki/Opus_(audio_format)" title="Opus (audio format)">Opus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vorbis" title="Vorbis">Vorbis</a> that are available free of charge and without any known patent restrictions. Some of the newer audio compression formats, such as AAC, WMA Pro and Vorbis, are free of some limitations inherent to the MP3 format that cannot be overcome by any MP3 encoder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-big-list_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-big-list-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides lossy compression methods, <a href="/wiki/Lossless_compression" title="Lossless compression">lossless formats</a> are a significant alternative to MP3 because they provide unaltered audio content, though with an increased file size compared to lossy compression. Lossless formats include <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">FLAC</a> (Free Lossless Audio Codec), <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lossless" title="Apple Lossless">Apple Lossless</a> and many others.</p>
