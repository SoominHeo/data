<p><i><b>This Is Us</b></i> is the seventh studio album, sixth in the United States, from American <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Backstreet_Boys" title="Backstreet Boys">Backstreet Boys</a>. It is their second and last album as a quartet. It was released on September 30, 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-japan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-japan-2">[2]</a></sup> in Japan through <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment_Japan" title="Sony Music Entertainment Japan">Sony Music Japan</a>, October 5, 2009 in the UK through <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a>, and October 6 in the U.S. as the final studio album through <a href="/wiki/Jive_Records" title="Jive Records">Jive Records</a>.</p>
<p>On the album, the group has reunited with previous collaborator and producer <a href="/wiki/Max_Martin" title="Max Martin">Max Martin</a> (responsible for the previous hit "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_It_That_Way" title="I Want It That Way">I Want It That Way</a>") to try and create their best record since their 1999 worldwide hit album <i><a href="/wiki/Millennium_(Backstreet_Boys_album)" title="Millennium (Backstreet Boys album)">Millennium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They worked with <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Tedder" title="Ryan Tedder">Ryan Tedder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Kelly" title="Claude Kelly">Claude Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jonsin" title="Jim Jonsin">Jim Jonsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/RedOne" title="RedOne">RedOne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ne-Yo" title="Ne-Yo">Ne-Yo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brian_Kennedy_(producer)" title="Brian Kennedy (producer)">Brian Kennedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_James_(songwriter)" title="Alex James (songwriter)">Alex James</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitbull_(entertainer)" title="Pitbull (entertainer)">Pitbull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Galan" title="Eddie Galan">Eddie Galan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rami_Yacoub" title="Rami Yacoub">Rami Yacoub</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Lundin" title="Kristian Lundin">Kristian Lundin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a> amongst others for the album as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newswire_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-newswire-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album debuted at number 9 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> making it their seventh top ten album following <i><a href="/wiki/Unbreakable_(Backstreet_Boys_album)" title="Unbreakable (Backstreet Boys album)">Unbreakable</a></i> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard1-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/RedOne" title="RedOne">RedOne</a> produced the album's lead single "<a href="/wiki/Straight_Through_My_Heart" title="Straight Through My Heart">Straight Through My Heart</a>" which was released in August/September 2009 and reached number 1 in Taiwan, number 3 in Japan, number 5 in Spain, number 106 in Billboard Hot 100, 19 in Canada, 72 in the UK, and 18 on the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Songs" title="Hot Dance Club Songs">Hot Dance Club Songs</a> chart. It was their final album under Jive and their last album as a quartet before original member <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Richardson_(musician)" title="Kevin Richardson (musician)">Kevin Richardson</a> rejoined the band on April 29, 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_production">Recording and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Is_Us_(Backstreet_Boys_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_(TV_series)" title="Extra (TV series)">Extra TV</a></i>, the group confirmed the title of their seventh album to be <i>This Is Us</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the official press release RCA/Jive Records describes the album as "a finely crafted R&amp;B and pop album from four talented musicians who love what they do and who maintain the rare relevance in an industry that often disposes of pop acts. The 11 songs that make up album are the sounds of four skilled singers with a similar vision, who have dealt with the trials and tribulations that accompany fame at an early age and who came out as one of the most successful groups of all time. It shows remarkable growth as songwriters and continues to give us songs that has made millions smile."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newswire_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-newswire-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 1, 2009, the Backstreet Boys management team expressed discontent at the fact that four recorded songs had been leaked on the internet. In an interview, <a href="/wiki/A._J._McLean" title="A. J. McLean">A. J. McLean</a> said that the group were "P.O.ed that music had leaked especially since extra care had been taken to keep the record secret".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digispy1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-digispy1-9">[9]</a></sup> In the end, however, the group used the feedback from the leaks to help guide the direction of the album and even the song selection by comparing fan reviews to what producers thought about the songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digispy1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-digispy1-9">[9]</a></sup> It was later revealed in 2011, that songs recorded for <i>This Is Us</i> were targeted by the German hacker, Deniz A., also known as DJ Stolen. In July 2010, the Rasch law firm logged a criminal complaint against DJ Stolen for "constantly placing hacked songs on the internet". Amongst those songs listed in the complaint was one called "Masquerade", described at the time as a new recording by the Backstreet Boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However despite leaking, the song is still included on the album.</p>
<p>Regarding their collaboration with RedOne, McLean remarked that it had been largely a last minute affair. Due to timing, the group was not able to make any recordings with the in-demand producer; however, near to the album's turn-in date, RedOne revealed that he had worked on three songs for the group and was more than willing to collaborate. It was also revealed that there had previously been 5 or 6 songs in the running for the lead single, although a Kevin Borg production titled "PDA" was most likely to be released; however, the RedOne production felt like "it was meant to be".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digispy1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-digispy1-9">[9]</a></sup> Interestingly one Tedder song, "Shadows", which was co-written by McLean was written for this album, but failed to make it, so <a href="/wiki/Simon_Cowell" title="Simon Cowell">Simon Cowell</a> bought the song for <a href="/wiki/Leona_Lewis" title="Leona Lewis">Leona Lewis</a>'s second album <i><a href="/wiki/Echo_(Leona_Lewis_album)" title="Echo (Leona Lewis album)">Echo</a></i> but in the end felt it more suited for a boyband and it was featured on <a href="/wiki/Westlife" title="Westlife">Westlife</a>'s 10th album <i><a href="/wiki/Where_We_Are" title="Where We Are">Where We Are</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>One song that was not included on the album, "All in My Head", was later re-recorded as a duet with fellow <a href="/wiki/Boy_band" title="Boy band">boy band</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Kids_On_The_Block" title="New Kids On The Block">New Kids On The Block</a> and featured on the 2011 collaboration album <i><a href="/wiki/NKOTBSB_(album)" title="NKOTBSB (album)">NKOTBSB</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_and_release">Promotion and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Is_Us_(Backstreet_Boys_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Promotion and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tour">Tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Is_Us_(Backstreet_Boys_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/This_Is_Us_Tour" title="This Is Us Tour">This Is Us Tour</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Is_Us_(Backstreet_Boys_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The group simultaneously released the standard and deluxe editions of the album. The deluxe edition (limited first edition in Japan) featured a DVD with live performances of previous singles at <a href="/wiki/The_O2_Arena" title="The O2 Arena">The O2 Arena</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, along with the music video for the first single.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Is_Us_(Backstreet_Boys_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The first single "<a href="/wiki/Straight_Through_My_Heart" title="Straight Through My Heart">Straight Through My Heart</a>", was premiered on the group's website on August 17 and released to radio the following day. It peaked at number 3 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Hot_100" title="Japan Hot 100">Japan Hot 100</a> and 18 on the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Songs" title="Hot Dance Club Songs">Hot Dance Club Songs</a> chart. It was certified platinum in Japan.</li>
<li>The second single "<a href="/wiki/Bigger_(Backstreet_Boys_song)" title="Bigger (Backstreet Boys song)">Bigger</a>" was released in the UK on December 14, 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radio1.gr_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-radio1.gr-12">[12]</a></sup> and released in the US on February 1, 2010 in AC radios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p><i>This is Us</i> received generally favorable reviews from critics. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average score</a> of 67, based on 5 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metacritic_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> found the album's hooks more attention-grabbing than <i><a href="/wiki/Unbreakable_(Backstreet_Boys_album)" title="Unbreakable (Backstreet Boys album)">Unbreakable</a></i> and the production more modern than pandering compared to <a href="/wiki/New_Kids_on_the_Block" title="New Kids on the Block">New Kids on the Block</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Block_(album)" title="The Block (album)">The Block</a></i>, concluding that "the group sounds great for their age, and they sound like they're at their peak – which is no guarantee of a hit, but it sure makes for a better album than they've produced in quite a while."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-AM-15">[15]</a></sup> Mikael Wood of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> praised the album for bringing back the band's old teen-pop sound with new producers and delivering them with confident vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-EW-17">[17]</a></sup> Jason Lipshutz of <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> admired the band's "foray into throbbing electronica" mixed in with the typical pop fare, concluding that it "may be a stepping stone in ushering [Backstreet Boys] away from their days on pop radio and firmly through the club door."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>August Brown of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> gave credit to the band for continuing to deliver catchy tracks but ultimately called the album "a competent but very late-adopted pop-trance slurry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA_Times_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-LA_Times-18">[18]</a></sup> John Terauds of the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> noted that the tracks have an '80s influence to them but said that "nothing sounds truly original." He also added that it will appeal only to diehard fans of the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Star_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Star-20">[20]</a></sup> Jonathan Keefe of <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> criticized the album's producers for crafting songs that range from retreads of other songs ("Straight Through My Heart", "If I Knew Then" and "Undone") to "flat-out embarrassing" ("She's a Dream" and "P.D.A."), concluding that, "No matter how strong their voices might remain and no matter how sincere they may be about keeping their career going, Backstreet Boys' collaborators have failed them here."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slant_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slant-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
