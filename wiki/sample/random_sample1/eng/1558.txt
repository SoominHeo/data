<p><b>TrES-4b</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a>, and the one of largest exoplanets ever found, after <a href="/wiki/WASP-12b" title="WASP-12b">WASP-12b</a>, <a href="/wiki/WASP-17b" title="WASP-17b">WASP-17b</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CT_Chamaeleontis_b" title="CT Chamaeleontis b">CT Chamaeleontis b</a> (though the latter may be a brown dwarf), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HD_100546_b" title="HD 100546 b">HD 100546 b</a>. It was discovered in 2006, and announced in 2007, by the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Atlantic_Exoplanet_Survey" title="Trans-Atlantic Exoplanet Survey">Trans-Atlantic Exoplanet Survey</a>, using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_extrasolar_planets#Transit_method" title="Methods of detecting extrasolar planets">transit method</a>. It is 1,430 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (440 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">pc</a>) away at star <a href="/wiki/GSC_02620-00648" title="GSC 02620-00648">GSC 02620-00648</a>, in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Hercules_(constellation)" title="Hercules (constellation)">Hercules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandushev2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mandushev2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>TrES-4 orbits its primary star every 3.543 days and eclipses it when viewed from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. The planet is 0.919 times as massive as <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> but 1.799 times the diameter, the largest planet ever found at the time (next to <a href="/wiki/WASP-17b" title="WASP-17b">WASP-17b</a>, found on 1 May 2009), giving it an average density of only about 0.333 grams per cubic centimetre. This made TrES-4 both the largest known planet and the planet with the lowest known density at the time of its discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-binary_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-binary-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandushev2007_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mandushev2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>TrES-4's orbital radius is 0.05091 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>, giving it a predicted surface temperature of about 1782 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>. This by itself is not enough to explain the planet's low density, however. It is not currently known why TrES-4 is so large. The probable causes are the proximity to a parent star that is 3–4 times more luminous than the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_heat" title="Internal heat">internal heat</a> within the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-binary_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-binary-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandushev2007_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mandushev2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2008 study concluded that the GSC 06200-00648 system (among others) is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system allowing even more accurate determination of stellar and planetary parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-binary_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-binary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
