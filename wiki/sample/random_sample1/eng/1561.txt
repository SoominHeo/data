<p><b>U.S. Bank Stadium</b> is a fixed-roof <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Downtown_East,_Minneapolis" title="Downtown East, Minneapolis">Downtown East</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, on the former site of <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Metrodome" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome">Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome</a>. U.S. Bank Stadium serves as the home of the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Vikings" title="Minnesota Vikings">Minnesota Vikings</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) and the Minnesota Golden Gophers Baseball Team (NCAA). The Vikings played at the Metrodome from 1982 until its closure in 2013 and before that at <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Stadium" title="Metropolitan Stadium">Metropolitan Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Minnesota" title="Bloomington, Minnesota">Bloomington, Minnesota</a> from 1961 to 1981. During the 2014 and 2015 seasons, the Vikings played at <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a> on the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> while the new stadium was being built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On June 17, 2016, U.S. Bank Stadium was deemed substantially complete by contractor Mortenson Construction. The stadium was substantially completed six weeks before the ribbon-cutting ceremony and official grand opening, which was held on July 22, 2016. Authority to use and occupy the stadium was handed over to the Vikings and the Minnesota Sports Facilities Authority. The Vikings played the first regular-season game at U.S. Bank Stadium on September 18, 2016, against the <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_Packers" title="Green Bay Packers">Green Bay Packers</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/NBC_Sunday_Night_Football" title="NBC Sunday Night Football">NBC Sunday Night Football</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the first fixed-roof stadium built in the NFL since <a href="/wiki/Ford_Field" title="Ford Field">Ford Field</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Lions" title="Detroit Lions">Detroit Lions</a>, opened in 2002. As of March 2015, the overall budget was estimated to be $1.061 billion, of which $348 million is coming from the state of Minnesota, $150 million from the city of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, and $551 million from the team and private contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cost_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cost-3">[3]</a></sup> U.S. Bank Stadium is scheduled to host <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_LII" title="Super Bowl LII">Super Bowl LII</a> on February 4, 2018,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superbowllii_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-superbowllii-11">[11]</a></sup> the 2019 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Final Four</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> <a href="/wiki/X_Games" title="X Games">X Games</a> in 2017 and 2018. On June 15, 2015, the Vikings announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Bank" title="U.S. Bank">U.S. Bank</a> had acquired the <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> to the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The naming deal is worth $220 million over 25 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>While the Vikings' owners wanted an outdoor stadium, the state and local governments would only provide funding for an indoor stadium that is able to host major events like the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">Final Four</a>, and a retractable roof, the trend in 2010s football stadiums, would have been too expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Architecture firm <a href="/wiki/HKS,_Inc." title="HKS, Inc.">HKS, Inc.</a>, also responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Cowboys" title="Dallas Cowboys">Dallas Cowboys</a>' <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Stadium" title="AT&amp;T Stadium">AT&amp;T Stadium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Colts" title="Indianapolis Colts">Indianapolis Colts</a>' <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Stadium" title="Lucas Oil Stadium">Lucas Oil Stadium</a>, decided to go for a lightweight translucent roof and glazed entrances with giant pivoting doors, aiming to get as much natural light from the outside as possible. The roof is made up of 60% Ethylene tetrafluoroethylene (<a href="/wiki/ETFE" title="ETFE">ETFE</a>), a fluorine-based clear plastic, and is the largest in North America, spanning 240,000 square feet supplied and installed by the firm <a href="/wiki/Vector_Foiltec" title="Vector Foiltec">Vector Foiltec</a>. ETFE's low <a href="/wiki/R-factor_(crystallography)" title="R-factor (crystallography)">R-factor</a> and the roof's slanted design, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture_in_Norway" title="Vernacular architecture in Norway">Nordic vernacular architecture</a>, allows the stadium to endure heavy snow loads. Snow accumulates in areas that are more safely and easily accessible, and also moves down the slanted roof into a heated gutter, the water from which drains to the nearby the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. The translucent roof and large wall panels also give fans a view of downtown Minneapolis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arch_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-arch-15">[15]</a></sup> The glass operable wall panels will allow the stadium to experience some of the outdoor elements while providing protection from the snow, rain, and the cold winter weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_Cathedral_comparison">Crystal Cathedral comparison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crystal Cathedral comparison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The design for U.S. Bank Stadium has been compared to the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Cathedral" title="Crystal Cathedral">Crystal Cathedral</a> in California, which was created by architect <a href="/wiki/Philip_Johnson" title="Philip Johnson">Philip Johnson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Crystal Cathedral was previously considered America's largest glass dominated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> building. The U.S. Bank Stadium, which likewise sports transparent roofs, walls, and giant rotating doors, has the world's five largest pivoting doors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrodome_lease">Metrodome lease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metrodome lease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Vikings' lease with the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Sports_Facilities_Commission" title="Metropolitan Sports Facilities Commission">Metropolitan Sports Facilities Commission</a> (MSFC), as signed by both parties in August 1979, kept them in the Metrodome until 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPR112006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPR112006-22">[22]</a></sup> The lease was considered one of the least lucrative among NFL teams; it included provisions where the commission owned the stadium, and the Vikings were locked into paying rent until the end of the 2011 season. For several years prior to the Metrodome's demolition, however, the Metropolitan Sports Facilities Commission waived the team's nearly $4 million rent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinnPost11.2F17.2F2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-MinnPost11.2F17.2F2009-23">[23]</a></sup> The Vikings paid the MSFC 9.5% of their ticket sales; the commission "reserve[d] all rights to sell or lease advertising in any part of the Stadium," the team could not use the scoreboard for any ads, and the team did not control naming rights for the building. Additionally, the commission controlled the limited parking and its revenue and paid the team 10% of all concession sales while retaining roughly 35% of concessions sold during Vikings games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup> The Vikings were 30th out of 32 NFL teams in local revenues in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup> The Vikings, as well as the stadium's other tenants, continually turned down any proposals for renovating the Metrodome itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup> A plan for a joint Vikings/<a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_football" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers football">University of Minnesota</a> football stadium was proposed in 2002, but differences over how the stadium would be designed and run, as well as state budget constraints, led to the plan's failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The university would eventually open its own <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a> in 2009.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downtown_Minneapolis">Downtown Minneapolis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Downtown Minneapolis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the outset, <a href="/wiki/Zygi_Wilf" title="Zygi Wilf">Zygi Wilf</a>, a billionaire from New Jersey and principal owner of the Vikings since 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> had stated he was interested in redeveloping the downtown site of the Metrodome no matter where the new facility was built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup> Taking into consideration downtown Minneapolis' growing mass transit network, cultural institutions, and growing condo and office markets, Wilf considered underdeveloped areas on Downtown's east side, centered on the Metrodome, to be a key opportunity and began discussing the matter with neighboring landholders, primarily the City of Minneapolis and the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Tribune" title="Star Tribune">Star Tribune</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup> An unrelated 2008 study explains that the effect of the <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">media</a>, in this case an uncritical <i>Star Tribune</i>, matters a great deal in helping a stadium initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As a result, once negotiations for a different location had been put aside, the Vikings focused on proposing a stadium that would be the centerpiece of a larger urban redevelopment project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP010307_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-CP010307-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilf's Vikings began acquiring significant land holdings in the <a href="/wiki/Downtown_East,_Minneapolis" title="Downtown East, Minneapolis">Downtown East</a> neighborhood around the Metrodome. In June 2007, the Vikings acquired four blocks of mostly empty land surrounding the <i>Star Tribune</i> headquarters from <a href="/wiki/Avista_Capital_Partners" title="Avista Capital Partners">Avista Capital Partners</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> owner of the <i>Star Tribune</i>) for $45 million; it is also believed the Vikings have first right of refusal to later buy the paper's headquarters building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib062107a_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib062107a-28">[28]</a></sup> In May 2007, the Vikings also acquired three other downtown parking lots for a total of $5 million, and have made a bid for a city-owned, underground parking ramp next to the neighborhood's <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bank_Stadium_(Metro_Transit_station)" title="U.S. Bank Stadium (Metro Transit station)">light rail station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib062107a_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strib062107a-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposal_timeline">Proposal timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Proposal timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007">2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg"><img alt="Roots of US Bank Stadium" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1123" data-file-width="2818" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg/220px-Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg/330px-Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg/440px-Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stabbur_in_Figjan_Numedal_Norway_drawing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Example of <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture_in_Norway" title="Vernacular architecture in Norway">Nordic vernacular architecture</a>, the style used for the design of the stadium roof</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On April 19, 2007, the MSFC and the Vikings unveiled their initial plans for the stadium and surrounding urban area, with an estimated opening of 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-Levy-041907_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-Levy-041907-29">[29]</a></sup> The plan included substantial improvements to the surrounding area, including an improved light rail stop, 4,500 residential units, hotels with a combined 270 rooms, 1,700,000 square feet (160,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of office space and substantial retail space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-Levy-041907_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-Levy-041907-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2007, the stadium would have held approximately 73,600 people and was to have been complete by August 2011. The initial proposal did not have the final architectural design renderings, but did include key features that were to have been included in any final plan, including the plans for neighboring urban development. These included demands for a retractable roof, an open view of the surroundings (particularly the downtown skyline), a glass-enclosed Winter Garden alongside the already-existing adjacent Metrodome light-rail stop, leafy urban square with outdoor cafés and dense housing around its edges, aesthetic improvements to roads connecting the stadium to nearby cultural institutions, and adaptive reuse of neighboring historic buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-features-041907_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-features-041907-30">[30]</a></sup> The roof would have allowed Minneapolis to remain a potential venue for the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Final_Four" title="Final Four">Final Four</a>, both of which had been held at the Metrodome. The proposed urban plan itself was received with cautious welcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-Peterson-041907_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-Peterson-041907-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2007 proposed cost estimate for the downtown Minneapolis stadium was $953,916,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-cost-041907_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-cost-041907-32">[32]</a></sup> The total broke down to $616,564,000 for the stadium, $200,729,000 for a retractable roof, $58,130,000 for parking, $8,892,000 for adjacent land right-of-way, and $69,601,000 to take into account inflation by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-cost-041907_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-cost-041907-32">[32]</a></sup> The estimate compared to then-upcoming stadiums in <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Stadium" title="Lucas Oil Stadium">Indianapolis</a> at $675 million (retractable roof, completed 2008), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowboys_Stadium" title="Cowboys Stadium">Dallas</a> at $932 million (retractable roof, completed 2009), and New York at $1.7 billion (open-air, completed in 2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-cost-041907_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-cost-041907-32">[32]</a></sup> In addition, according to Wilf, taking into account the costs for the surrounding urban developments put forth in the proposal would have brought the estimated total to $2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib062107a_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strib062107a-28">[28]</a></sup> The estimated costs were based on projected 2008 construction and material costs, so it would have been possible that the stadium costs could have hovered near $1 billion if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Legislature" title="Minnesota State Legislature">Minnesota State Legislature</a> had not approved the project in the 2008 session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib062107b_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strib062107b-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated.">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>No proposals were made, at that time, for paying for the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strib-Levy-041907_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strib-Levy-041907-29">[29]</a></sup> The MSFC and Vikings made initial pitches to the Minnesota State Legislature during the end of the 2007 session, but expected to make serious efforts during the 2008 legislative session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paullevymaybe_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-paullevymaybe-34">[34]</a></sup> The Vikings proposed creating a Minnesota Football Stadium Task Force, which they expect would take 24 months to plan the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paullevymaybe_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-paullevymaybe-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008">2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the September 2008 MSFC vote to start feasibility studies for re-using the Metrodome, an unrelated study released for 38 U.S. cities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> found that "when a [NFL] team wins, people's moods improve,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stetz_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stetz-36">[36]</a></sup> and that personal income for residents of a city with an NFL team with 10 wins increases about <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>165 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stetz_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stetz-36">[36]</a></sup> While true for NFL football, for comparison, professional baseball and basketball gain no personal income for residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stetz_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stetz-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009">2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Feasibility studies for <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>-based design and local construction of a new stadium were expected in early 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vomhof_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vomhof-37">[37]</a></sup> Roy Terwilliger, a former Republican state senator from Edina, Ray Waldron, an AFL-CIO leader, and the Dome engineering expert and CEO, Bill Lester and Steve Maki of the MSFC selected architectural firm HKS of Dallas and construction manager Mortenson of Minnesota over the objections of Paul Thatcher and Timothy Rose of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis-St._Paul" title="Minneapolis-St. Paul">Minneapolis-St. Paul</a>, who preferred Ellerbe Beckett and Kraus-Anderson, both of Minnesota. Loanne Thrane of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a>, the sole female member of the commission, voiced opposition and later voted with the majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2009, commission chairman Terwilliger said, "We know what the art of the possible is at this particular location." A new proposal for 65,000 seats with a sliding roof was unveiled at $84 million less than the previous proposal, but with $50 million per year more scheduled for each year that construction is delayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakst_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bakst-39">[39]</a></sup> Vikings officials boycotted the presentation which estimated the total cost at $870 million, or $770 million if the sliding roof were omitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakst_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bakst-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2010 Vikings stadium proposal was dealt a setback on May 5, 2010, when a Minnesota House panel defeated the proposal by a 10–9 vote.</p>
<p>The stadium debate was revived in the aftermath of the Metrodome's roof deflation on December 12, 2010, which forced the relocation of the Vikings' final two home games of the 2010 season and led to more calls for a new stadium from various sources in the local and national media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT12132010_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAT12132010-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT12142010_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT12142010-41">[41]</a></sup> Minnesota Governor <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dayton" title="Mark Dayton">Mark Dayton</a> discussed the matter with NFL commissioner <a href="/wiki/Roger_Goodell" title="Roger Goodell">Roger Goodell</a>, but said "any new stadium must first benefit the people of Minnesota".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dayton_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dayton-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011">2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="City_of_Minneapolis_Proposal">City of Minneapolis Proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: City of Minneapolis Proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Hennepin_County,_Minnesota" title="Hennepin County, Minnesota">Hennepin County</a> stopped their pursuit of a Vikings stadium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the city of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> submitted a plan for a Vikings' stadium at the downtown Metrodome site. The Minneapolis plan was for a fixed-roof stadium costing an estimated $895 million. The proposal also included funding solutions for $95 million in renovations to the <a href="/wiki/Target_Center" title="Target Center">Target Center</a>. The team reacted with skepticism to the proposal and did not want to play at nearby 50,000-seat capacity <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a> during the three years of construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Because the Minneapolis dome site was a less expensive option, football fans were expected to return to the Minneapolis plan if the shortfall in the Ramsey County plan were not realized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ramsey_County_Proposal">Ramsey County Proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ramsey County Proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In May 2011, Ramsey County officials announced they had reached an agreement with the Minnesota Vikings to be the team’s local partner for a new stadium, subject to approval by the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Legislature" title="Minnesota Legislature">Minnesota Legislature</a> and to approval of a sales tax by the <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_County,_Minnesota" title="Ramsey County, Minnesota">Ramsey County</a> Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchschere_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duchschere-46">[46]</a></sup> The site of the stadium would be the former <a href="/wiki/Twin_Cities_Army_Ammunition_Plant" title="Twin Cities Army Ammunition Plant">Twin Cities Army Ammunitions Plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arden_Hills,_Minnesota" title="Arden Hills, Minnesota">Arden Hills</a>, which is about 10 miles from the <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Metrodome" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome">Metrodome</a> in Minneapolis and is a <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> clean up site. The agreement called for an $884 million stadium and an additional $173 million for on-site infrastructure, parking and environmental costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ramsey County said the Vikings would commit $407 million to the project, which would have been about 44% of the stadium cost and 39% of the overall cost. The county's cost would have been $350 million, to be financed by a half-cent sales tax increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP-47">[47]</a></sup> The state of Minnesota's cost would have been $300 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchschere_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Duchschere-46">[46]</a></sup> This totalled about $1.057 billion, leaving at least a $131 million shortfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012">2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 1, 2012, Governor Dayton announced an agreement for a new stadium to be built on the site of the Metrodome, pending approval by the state legislature and the Minneapolis city council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The $975 million project, half of which would be publicly funded, would be patterned after <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Stadium" title="Lucas Oil Stadium">Lucas Oil Stadium</a>. It would utilize part of the footprint of the Metrodome and would only require the Vikings to play at <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a> during the final year of construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The agreement met with mixed reaction, and some criticized the proposal as being unfair to taxpayers and a giveaway to team owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 10, 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Legislature" title="Minnesota Legislature">Minnesota Legislature</a> approved funding for a new Vikings stadium on that site. The project is projected to have a $975 million price tag, with the Vikings covering $477 million, the state covering $348 million, and $150 million covered by a hospitality tax in Minneapolis. The city of Minneapolis must pay a total of $678 million over the 30-year life of the deal, including interest, operations, and construction costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The bill was signed by Gov. Dayton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and received the approval of the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_City_Council" title="Minneapolis City Council">Minneapolis City Council</a> on May 25, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The Vikings played in the Metrodome through the 2013 season, as construction did not require the dome's immediate demolition. The Vikings then moved to <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a> on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> campus until the new stadium is complete.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 13, 2013, the Minnesota Sports Facilities Authority (MSFA), the Minnesota Vikings, and HKS Sports &amp; Entertainment Group together unveiled the new stadium's design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014">2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2014, a lawsuit was started by former <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_mayoral_election,_2013" title="Minneapolis mayoral election, 2013">Minneapolis mayoral candidate</a> Doug Mann and two others to block the construction of the new stadium. The suit questioned the constitutionality of the stadium's funding plan and delayed a $468 million bond sale. Officials warned the delay could stall the project's timeline and add costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The lawsuit was later dismissed by the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Supreme_Court" title="Minnesota Supreme Court">Minnesota Supreme Court</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charitable_gambling_funding_shortfall">Charitable gambling funding shortfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Charitable gambling funding shortfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The State of Minnesota's portion of the cost of the stadium was to be funded by revenue from a proposed new charitable gambling source, which was dubbed electronic <a href="/wiki/Pull-tab" title="Pull-tab">pulltabs</a>. When the stadium funding bill was passed in the legislature and signed by the governor on May 14, 2012, the new revenue from the games was estimated to be $34 million for 2013, and rising each year thereafter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="November_2012_revenue_forecast">November 2012 revenue forecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: November 2012 revenue forecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Six months later, the first budget estimate from the Minnesota Office of Management and Budget was released, revising the projected revenue from the electronic pulltab games. This first revision cut the estimated revenue from the game for 2013 by 51%, to $16 million (versus the legislation's estimate of $34 million).</p>
<p>From page 15 of the Minnesota Management and Budget Complete Forecast, November 2012: "For FY 2013, the projected reserve balance has been reduced from $34 to $16 million. Projected new gambling revenues from stadium legislation are expected to be $18 million (51%) below end of session estimates." "The forecast reduction reflects a slower than expected implementation of electronic gaming options and reduced estimates for daily revenue per gaming device."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="February_2013_revenue_forecast">February 2013 revenue forecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: February 2013 revenue forecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2013, the Minnesota Office of Management and Budget released another updated budget forecast for fiscal years 2013 to 2017. Included in this forecast was another revision in the projected revenue from charitable gambling sources, from the previous estimate of $16 million, down to $1.7 million, a further 90% reduction in the estimate for 2013 revenue. This total was a 95% reduction from what was estimated in the stadium bill passed in May 2012.</p>
<p>From page 12 of the Minnesota Management and Budget Complete Forecast, February 2013: "The forecast for lawful gambling revenue has been reduced $15 million in FY 2013 and $46 million in FY 2014–15. Slower than expected implementation of electronic gambling options and a reduction in estimates for daily revenue per gambling location were the reasons for the revenue reduction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_fallout_from_projected_shortfall">Political fallout from projected shortfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Political fallout from projected shortfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of the projected shortfall, members of the Minnesota Legislature and the Governor's office began discussing ideas to fix the shortfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The legislature decided to impose an inventory tax on cigarettes to make up for any shortfall over the next year of construction and closing of a corporate income-tax loophole for the following years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uptick_in_revenue">Uptick in revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Uptick in revenue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state reported in July 2016 that pulltab revenue is "soaring" and that there is optimism in Minneapolis about its continuing to rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>In August 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Sports_Facilities_Authority" title="Minnesota Sports Facilities Authority">Minnesota Sports Facilities Authority</a> (MSFA)—the stadium's newly created owner—received bids and plans from five architectural and engineering firms, all nationally-recognized stadium designers, including <a href="/wiki/Populous_(company)" title="Populous (company)">Populous</a>, <a href="/wiki/AECOM" title="AECOM">AECOM</a>, <a href="/wiki/EwingCole" title="EwingCole">EwingCole</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HNTB" title="HNTB">HNTB</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> On September 28, 2012, the MSFA selected the Dallas firm of <a href="/wiki/HKS,_Inc." title="HKS, Inc.">HKS, Inc.</a>, which had designed both <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Stadium" title="AT&amp;T Stadium">AT&amp;T Stadium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Stadium" title="Lucas Oil Stadium">Lucas Oil Stadium</a> within the previous decade, to serve as the project's architect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> HKS also designed <a href="/wiki/Globe_Life_Park_in_Arlington" title="Globe Life Park in Arlington">Globe Life Park in Arlington</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a>’ <a href="/wiki/Miller_Park_(Milwaukee)" title="Miller Park (Milwaukee)">Miller Park</a> in Milwaukee, Wisconsin; and renovations to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Guaranteed_Rate_Field" title="Guaranteed Rate Field">Guaranteed Rate Field</a>. Initial design plans were not immediately released to the public, but Viking officials said they hoped the budget would allow the new stadium to include a retractable roof, walls, or windows. The design team also planned to incorporate interactive technology into some elements to create a more engaging fan experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Construction of the facility was originally slated to begin in October 2013, but was delayed until December 3, 2013, as an ongoing investigation of the Wilfs' finances continued to take place after a 21-year lawsuit against them came to a conclusion in late August. On August 27, 2015, one worker died and another was injured after falling during construction on the U.S. Bank Stadium roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Minneapolis Fire received the call for a tactical rescue at the stadium at 7:48 AM, crews were on scene shortly afterwards by 7:54 AM <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Jeramie M. Gruber, 35, of Northfield and the other injured worker were employed by St. Paul-based Berwald Roofing Co. which had been cited 6 times since 2010 for OSHA violations regarding improper fall protection for workers. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) investigated the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> As a result of the investigation U.S. Bank Stadium contractor Mortenson Construction and subcontractor Berwald Roofing face fines of $173,400 for “serious” and “willful” safety violations. The reports don’t provide an explanation of the accidents, but the largest fine, $70,000, and most serious alleged violation faults Berwald for willfully failing to have workers use proper fall protection while working at heights above 6 feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_events">Major events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Major events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 20, 2014, the NFL awarded Minneapolis the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_LII" title="Super Bowl LII">2018 Super Bowl</a>. <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> had also made bids for the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superbowllii_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-superbowllii-11">[11]</a></sup> On November 14, 2014, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA">NCAA</a> announced the stadium will host the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">men's basketball Final Four</a> in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In May 2015, Governor Mark Dayton announced a bid to host the <a href="/wiki/College_Football_Playoff_National_Championship" title="College Football Playoff National Championship">College Football Playoff National Championship</a> in 2020. However, on November 4, 2015, it was announced that the game was awarded to New Orleans. This was the first losing bid for a major sporting event offered to be held at the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> On July 20, 2016, it was announced that U.S. Bank Stadium and <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> would host the 2017 and 2018 summer <a href="/wiki/X_Games" title="X Games">X Games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first sporting event at U.S. Bank Stadium was a soccer match between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC_Milan" title="AC Milan">AC Milan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelsea_FC" title="Chelsea FC">Chelsea FC</a> on August 3, 2016, as part of the <a href="/wiki/2016_International_Champions_Cup" title="2016 International Champions Cup">2016 International Champions Cup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first NFL game at the stadium was the Week 3 <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League_preseason" title="National Football League preseason">preseason game</a> against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Chargers" title="San Diego Chargers">San Diego Chargers</a> on August 28, 2016. Although the Vikings scored first with a field goal, the Chargers scored the first touchdown in the new stadium. The Vikings ultimately won, 23–10.</p>
<p>The first NFL regular season touchdown was scored by <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_Packers" title="Green Bay Packers">Green Bay Packers</a> wide receiver <a href="/wiki/Jordy_Nelson" title="Jordy Nelson">Jordy Nelson</a> on September 25, 2016. The Vikings would go on to win the game 17-14.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soccer">Soccer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Soccer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Vikings said the design includes a soccer field measuring 115 by 74 yards to accommodate a <a href="/wiki/Expansion_of_Major_League_Soccer#Minneapolis" title="Expansion of Major League Soccer">potential Major League Soccer expansion team</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In 2012, the Vikings received a five-year window to host a Major League Soccer team in the state's legislation to finance the stadium, and the Vikings ownership launched a bid to own an expansion franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minnesota_united_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-minnesota_united-76">[76]</a></sup> In December 2014, Vikings Vice President Lester Bagley presented rendering of the stadium configured for a potential Major League Soccer team, with tarps and curtains covering the upper deck to bring the capacity down to 20,000. He said the stadium was "being built specifically with soccer in mind" and drew a contrast with <a href="/wiki/Gillette_Stadium" title="Gillette Stadium">Gillette Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_England_Revolution" title="New England Revolution">New England Revolution</a>'s home field, which he called "a football stadium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 16, 2015, the Vikings announced they ended their expansion bid after MLS informed them that they preferred the bid by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minnesota_United_FC_(MLS)" title="Minnesota United FC (MLS)">Minnesota United</a> with its own plan for a smaller, outdoor stadium in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">Saint Paul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball">Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with the Metrodome, the new stadium will have the capability to host baseball games in the winter months. The <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_baseball" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers baseball">University of Minnesota</a> will play selected games, primarily during February and March, including hosting the Dairy Queen Classic, a non-conference series of games featuring top NCAA teams in Minnesota that was suspended during stadium construction. Dimensions are set to be 345 feet to left field, 365 to left center, 381 to center field, 400 slightly right of center field, 345 in right center field, and with retractable seating, 301 to right field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerts">Concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crowd_Noise">Crowd Noise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Bank_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Crowd Noise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>U.S. Bank Stadium was designed using an acoustically more reflective material than the old Metrodome, ETFE, which creates a very loud environment for opposing teams. It is already known as one of the loudest stadiums in the NFL.</p>
<p>Registered Decibel Levels:<br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b>114</b> - Vikings vs. Chargers, (Aug. 28, 2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>105</b> - International Soccer Match - Chelsea vs. A.C. Milan, (Aug. 3, 2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Record crowd noises for the top loudest NFL stadiums:<br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b>142.4</b>, <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead_Stadium" title="Arrowhead Stadium">Arrowhead Stadium</a> (Sep. 29, 2014) Kansas City Chiefs vs. New England Patriots</li>
<li><b>137.5</b>, <a href="/wiki/CenturyLink_Field" title="CenturyLink Field">CenturyLink Field</a> (Dec. 2, 2013) Seattle Seahawks vs. New Orleans Saints</li>
<li><b>122.6</b>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_Superdome" title="Mercedes-Benz Superdome">Mercedes-Benz Superdome</a> (Dec. 9, 2013) New Orleans Saints vs. Carolina Panthers</li>
<li><b>118.0</b>, <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Metrodome" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome">Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome</a> (Oct. 27, 1991) Minnesota Twins vs. Atlanta Braves</li>
</ul>
<p>The loudest ever indoor stadium noise level is 126.0 set in <a href="/wiki/Sleep_Train_Arena" title="Sleep Train Arena">Sleep Train Arena</a> on Nov. 15, 2013 during a Sacramento Kings basketball game.</p>
