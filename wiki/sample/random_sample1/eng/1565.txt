<p><b>UB40</b> are an English <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> band, formed in December 1978 in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. The band has had more than 50 singles in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>, and has also achieved considerable international success. They have been nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Reggae_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Reggae Album">Grammy Award for Best Reggae Album</a> four times, and in 1984 were nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brit_Award" title="Brit Award">Brit Award</a> for Best British Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> One of the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_music_artists" title="List of best-selling music artists">best-selling music artists</a>, UB40 have sold over 70 million records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TFE_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TFE-2">[2]</a></sup> The ethnic make-up of the band's original line-up was diverse, with musicians of English, Irish, Jamaican, Scottish and Yemeni parentage.</p>
<p>Their <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">hit singles</a> include their debut "<a href="/wiki/Food_for_Thought_(song)" title="Food for Thought (song)">Food for Thought</a>" and two <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> number ones with "<a href="/wiki/Red_Red_Wine" title="Red Red Wine">Red Red Wine</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Help_Falling_in_Love#UB40_version" title="Can't Help Falling in Love">Can't Help Falling in Love</a>". Both of these also topped the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>, as did the band's version of "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_You_Babe" title="I Got You Babe">I Got You Babe</a>". Their two most successful albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Labour_of_Love" title="Labour of Love">Labour of Love</a></i> (1983) and <i><a href="/wiki/Promises_and_Lies" title="Promises and Lies">Promises and Lies</a></i> (1993), reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>.</p>
<p>The band's line-up was stable for nearly 29 years, from March 1979 until January 2008, when frontman <a href="/wiki/Ali_Campbell" title="Ali Campbell">Ali Campbell</a> left the band; followed shortly thereafter by keyboardist Mickey Virtue. Another member, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astro_(UB40)" title="Astro (UB40)">Astro</a>, remained with the band until November 2013; when he departed the original band to team up with Campbell and Virtue in a new version of UB40. In 2014, legal advice was sought by the original band (now consisting of remaining co-founding members drummer Jimmy Brown, guitarist Robin Campbell, bassist <a href="/wiki/Earl_Falconer" title="Earl Falconer">Earl Falconer</a>, percussionist <a href="/wiki/Norman_Hassan" title="Norman Hassan">Norman Hassan</a>, and saxophonist Brian Travers, along with new vocalist <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Campbell_(UB40)" title="Duncan Campbell (UB40)">Duncan Campbell</a>) against the group containing Campbell, Virtue, and Astro over usage of the band name, due to it being used by both parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UB40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1978.E2.80.932008:_Formation_and_rise_to_stardom">1978–2008: Formation and rise to stardom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UB40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1978–2008: Formation and rise to stardom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band members began as friends who knew each other from various schools across <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. The name "UB40" was selected in reference to the signing-on document issued to people claiming <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_benefits" title="Unemployment benefits">unemployment benefit</a> from the UK government's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Employment" title="Department of Employment">Department of Employment</a> at the time of the band's formation. The designation UB40 stood for <b>U</b>nemployment <b>B</b>enefit, Form <b>40</b>. Following a serious assault on Ali Campbell, he received a hefty compensation package which he put towards funding musical instruments for the fledgling band members.</p>
<p>The origins of what would become UB40 began when in mid-1978 guitarist Ali Campbell, together with the rhythm section of drummer Jimmy Brown and bassist Earl Falconer, began rehearsing charting reggae songs in addition to some of their own original compositions. They were soon joined by several of their friends, firstly percussionists Yomi Babayemi and Norman Hassan, and then saxophonist Brian Travers and keyboardist Jimmy Lynn. Robin Campbell, although initially reluctant to commit to forming a band with the others, was invited to join once again by his brother and bought a guitar with which to do so in December of that year. Once Robin had joined the others in their jamming sessions, the eight musicians formed a band, deciding on the name 'UB40' after a friend suggested it was an appropriate name given the unemployed status of all of the band members. Prior to this, Travers had work as an electrical apprentice for NG Bailey; whilst Robin Campbell had been an apprentice toolmaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLC1985_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LLC1985-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CampbellCampbell200660_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CampbellCampbell200660-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This lineup of the band lasted long enough to play their first show at the Hare &amp; Hounds pub in Kings Heath in February 1979 and one other, before the band underwent its first lineup change in the form of Babyemi and Lynn leaving the band and Mickey Virtue joining in place of Lynn. A month later UB40's classic lineup was rounded out with the inclusion of percussionist and vocalist Astro. Astro had previously been working for Duke Alloy's <a href="/wiki/Sound_system_(Jamaican)" title="Sound system (Jamaican)">sound system</a> attending reggae dances in Birmingham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLC1985_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-LLC1985-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CampbellCampbell200660_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CampbellCampbell200660-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before some of them could play their <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">instruments</a>, Ali Campbell and Brian Travers travelled around Birmingham promoting the band, putting up UB40 <a href="/wiki/Poster" title="Poster">posters</a>. Their sound was created and honed through many long jam sessions at various locations in Birmingham.</p>

<p>Their first gig took place on 9 February 1979 at The Hare &amp; Hounds Pub in <a href="/wiki/Kings_Heath" title="Kings Heath">Kings Heath</a>, Birmingham for a friend's birthday party. This was commemorated in October 2011 by the unveiling of a plaque at the venue, indicating the band receiving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performing_Rights_Society" title="Performing Rights Society">Performing Rights Society</a>'s Music Heritage Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>UB40 caught their first break when <a href="/wiki/Chrissie_Hynde" title="Chrissie Hynde">Chrissie Hynde</a> saw them at a pub and gave them an opportunity as a support act to her band, <a href="/wiki/The_Pretenders" title="The Pretenders">The Pretenders</a>. UB40's first single, "King"/"<a href="/wiki/Food_for_Thought_(song)" title="Food for Thought (song)">Food for Thought</a>" was released on Graduate Records, a local independent label run by David Virr. It reached No. 4 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.</p>
<p>The title of their first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Signing_Off" title="Signing Off">Signing Off</a></i>, indicates the band was signing off from, or ending, their claim for unemployment benefits. It was recorded in a <a href="/wiki/Bedsit" title="Bedsit">bedsit</a> in Birmingham and was produced by Bob Lamb. Norman Hassan said of the recording: "if you stripped my track down, you could hear the birds in the background." This is because his tracks were recorded outside in the garden. <i>Signing Off</i> was released on 29 August 1980. It entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> on 2 October 1980. It reached as high as No. 2 in the UK and spent 71 weeks in total on the chart. <i>Signing Off</i> is now a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_album" title="Platinum album">Platinum album</a>. As UB40 grew in popularity, they encouraged and supported local musicians and bands from Birmingham, such as <a href="/wiki/Beshara_(band)" title="Beshara (band)">Beshara</a>, often bringing them on tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After great success in the UK, UB40's popularity in the United States was established when they released <i><a href="/wiki/Labour_of_Love" title="Labour of Love">Labour of Love</a></i>, an album of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_song" title="Cover song">cover songs</a>, in 1983. The album reached No. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> and No. 8 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> in the US. The album featured the song "<a href="/wiki/Red_Red_Wine" title="Red Red Wine">Red Red Wine</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover version</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Neil_Diamond" title="Neil Diamond">Neil Diamond</a> song (in an arrangement similar to that of Tony Tribe's version); it stayed on the charts for over 100 weeks. Three years later UB40 performed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birmingham_Heart_Beat_Charity_Concert_1986" title="Birmingham Heart Beat Charity Concert 1986">Birmingham Heart Beat Charity Concert 1986</a>.</p>
<p>In 1987 Ray "Pablo" Falconer, producer of UB40 music, died in a car crash. His brother, Earl Falconer, the band's bassist, was driving with nearly twice the legal limit of alcohol in his blood. Earl was sentenced to six months imprisonment in June 1988 and banned from driving for three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their most successful worldwide single release is their reggae/pop version of "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Help_Falling_in_Love" title="Can't Help Falling in Love">(I Can't Help) Falling in Love With You</a>", which was the main title to the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Stone" title="Sharon Stone">Sharon Stone</a> movie <i><a href="/wiki/Sliver_(film)" title="Sliver (film)">Sliver</a></i>; it was a number one hit across Europe and in the US.In 1995 they covered the <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Superstition_(song)" title="Superstition (song)">Superstition</a>" for the <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy" title="Eddie Murphy">Eddie Murphy</a> movie <i><a href="/wiki/Vampire_in_Brooklyn" title="Vampire in Brooklyn">Vampire in Brooklyn</a></i> and it appears on their album <i>The Best of UB40 – Volume Two</i> which they released that year. The group also made a guest appearance in the 1997 feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Speed_2:_Cruise_Control" title="Speed 2: Cruise Control">Speed 2: Cruise Control</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inc.1997_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inc.1997-9">[9]</a></sup> UB40 is featured in the 1988 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yob" title="The Yob">The Yob</a></i>.</p>

<p>UB40 toured South Africa in July 2007 and headlined the <a href="/wiki/Live_Earth_concert,_Johannesburg" title="Live Earth concert, Johannesburg">Live Earth</a> concert at the <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_Humankind" title="Cradle of Humankind">Cradle of Humankind</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008-present:_Line-up_changes_and_the_current_era">2008-present: Line-up changes and the current era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UB40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2008-present: Line-up changes and the current era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 24 January 2008 it was announced that Ali Campbell would be leaving the group after 30 years. It was originally stated that this was in order for Campbell to concentrate on solo projects, but Campbell later said he was leaving due to management and business disputes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The remaining seven members released a statement saying: "Ali made a very simple decision; he chose to pursue and put his solo career over and above continuing to work with UB40 after February 2008. It’s as simple as that".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Mickey Virtue departed shortly afterwards citing the same issues that Campbell had as reasons for his departure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was reported by some Birmingham newspapers on 13 March 2008, that <a href="/wiki/Maxi_Priest" title="Maxi Priest">Maxi Priest</a> would be the new lead singer of UB40 and had recorded a cover of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_the_Sheriff" title="I Shot the Sheriff">I Shot the Sheriff</a>" with the band, based on information from an unnamed "source close to the band." Priest had joined UB40 on their arena tour in 2007, culminating in sell-out shows at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_Birmingham" title="NEC Birmingham">NEC Birmingham</a> in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Another local newspaper reporting that Maxi Priest would be the new UB40 frontman, also included a statement from band spokesman Gerard Franklyn which contradicted this claim: "Maxi is collaborating with the band to record material but no decision has been made to replace Ali Campbell with one definitive singer. The reports are half correct he will be appearing with them for this new recording."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In April 2008, the BBC reported that Campbell was to be replaced in the band by his brother Duncan, with reggae singer Maxi Priest also bolstering the line-up on tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band released their next album, <i><a href="/wiki/TwentyFourSeven_(UB40_album)" title="TwentyFourSeven (UB40 album)">TwentyFourSeven</a></i>, UB40's last with their classic lineup, by way of a free insert in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mail_on_Sunday" title="The Mail on Sunday">The Mail on Sunday</a>'</i>s 4 May 2008 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The newspaper sold nearly three million copies. This led to a backlash when the full 17 track version was released 21 June 2008, and most of the big retailers refused to stock it. It failed to reach the Top 75 in the UK, which was a first, as all their official albums had previously made the Top 50 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ukcharts_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ukcharts-18">[18]</a></sup> Their next release, on EMI, was a collection called <i>Love Songs</i>, which was a compilation of hits mainly from the Labour of Love series and all featuring Ali Campbell on vocals – it reached number 3 in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ukcharts_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ukcharts-18">[18]</a></sup> The band toured the US, which included their first show at the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Bowl" title="Hollywood Bowl">Hollywood Bowl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hb2008_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-hb2008-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 the band released the first new album with their new lead singer <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Campbell_(UB40)" title="Duncan Campbell (UB40)">Duncan Campbell</a>; another in the <i>Labour of Love</i> cover series entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Labour_of_Love_IV" title="Labour of Love IV">Labour of Love IV</a></i>. The album was in the charts for only two weeks reaching number 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ukcharts_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-ukcharts-18">[18]</a></sup> During the 2009 US Tour UB40 offered fans live concert recordings on USB wristbands. The wristbands also included the Dub Sessions remix album and photos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>UB40 announced that after completing a coast-to-coast 2010 American tour they would be playing a nationwide UK tour of theatres in October/November 2010 performing their seminal album <i>Signing Off</i>, in full, along with a second set of popular UB40 songs. To coincide, on 1 November 2010 a remastered 2CD+DVD of <i>Signing Off</i> was released as a '30th Anniversary Special Edition'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2011 five founder members of the group and directors of their <a href="/wiki/DEP_International" title="DEP International">DEP International</a> label, had bankruptcy proceedings started against them relating to debts of the record label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bankruptcy1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bankruptcy1-22">[22]</a></sup> In October 2011 Travers, Wilson, Hassan and Brown were declared bankrupt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brupt_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-brupt-23">[23]</a></sup> Former member Ali Campbell was also declared bankrupt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2013 a new album, <i><a href="/wiki/Getting_Over_the_Storm" title="Getting Over the Storm">Getting Over the Storm</a></i> was announced, their first since 2010 and <i>Labour of Love IV</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CM_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-CM-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2013, UB40 revealed dates in anticipation of their UK Tour in early 2014. The announcement of the tour follows the success of the band's latest Top 30 album, <i>Getting Over The Storm</i>, which was awarded <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a>'s 'Album of the Week' accolade and received 5-star reviews from the UK's music press on its release in September 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On 22 November 2013 Astro announced in a statement that he had left the band, describing it as a "rudderless ship" and criticising the "serious lack of communication between the band and management" and the country-orientated direction of their latest album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Astro joined former UB40 members Ali Campbell and Mickey Virtue on stage at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IndigO2" title="IndigO2">indigO2</a> Arena in London on 6 December 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and on 17 January 2014 the trio announced on their website that they were in the studio recording new music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Ali Campbell was highly critical of his replacement in UB40, stating "I sat back for five years and watched my brother Duncan murdering my songs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YP_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-YP-30">[30]</a></sup> Ali Campbell toured as UB40 with Astro and Virtue over the summer, including an appearance at the <a href="/wiki/Brentwood_Festival" title="Brentwood Festival">Brentwood Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The new album Silhouette, featuring the trio is now set for release on 6 October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2014 Ali Campbell claimed that he would be prepared to go to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court" title="High Court">High Court</a> over the matter rather than <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settle out of court</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UB40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UB40 were influenced by the many <a href="/wiki/Sound_system_(Jamaican)" title="Sound system (Jamaican)">blues parties</a> they attended as teenagers in the multicultural <a href="/wiki/Balsall_Heath" title="Balsall Heath">Balsall Heath</a> area of Birmingham. Their love of <a href="/wiki/Ska" title="Ska">ska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Lovers_rock" title="Lovers rock">lovers rock</a> inspired such original tracks as "King", "Madam Medusa", "Food for Thought", "Signing Off" and "One in Ten".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their early musical style was unique, with a heavy influence of <a href="/wiki/Analog_synthesizer" title="Analog synthesizer">analogue synthesisers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock</a> guitar, saxophone and <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music">dub</a> producer techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Campbell brothers are the sons of the late <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk musician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Campbell_(folk_musician)" title="Ian Campbell (folk musician)">Ian Campbell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Their father regularly took them to folk festivals and gigs and introduced them to music and to touring. It was at his father's folk club "Jug O' Punch" that Ali Campbell made his singing debut with <a href="/wiki/Dave_Swarbrick" title="Dave Swarbrick">Dave Swarbrick</a>'s daughter, Suss, singing "Why Does It have To Be Me?".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CampbellCampbell200610_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-CampbellCampbell200610-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements">Achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UB40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UB40 are one of the most commercially successful reggae acts of all time in terms of record sales (over 70 million), chart positions and touring schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TFE_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TFE-2">[2]</a></sup> During their three-decade long career, they have been performing sell-out shows worldwide and headlining the <a href="/wiki/Reggae_Sunsplash" title="Reggae Sunsplash">Reggae Sunsplash</a> music festival in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, as well as spreading reggae to Russia, South America, etc. They have performed twice at the <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Proms" title="Night of the Proms">Night of the Proms</a>, in 2000 and in 2006. In 2006 UB40 was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> (Reggae category) for their album <i>Who You Fighting For</i>.</p>
<p>All three of their UK number one hits and four of their five US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">top ten</a> hits were <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover versions</a>.</p>
<p>UB40 collaborators include: <a href="/wiki/Pato_Banton" title="Pato Banton">Pato Banton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madness_(band)" title="Madness (band)">Madness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bitty_McLean" title="Bitty McLean">Bitty McLean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrissie_Hynde" title="Chrissie Hynde">Chrissie Hynde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maxi_Priest" title="Maxi Priest">Maxi Priest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Palmer_(singer)" title="Robert Palmer (singer)">Robert Palmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunterz" title="Hunterz">Hunterz</a>, Japanese artist Mikidozan, French artist Nuttea, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Saw" title="Lady Saw">Lady Saw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Afrika Bambaataa</a>, <a href="/wiki/808_State" title="808 State">808 State</a>.</p>
