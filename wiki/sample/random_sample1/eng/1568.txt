<p><b>UDFy-38135539</b> (also known as "HUDF.YD3") is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Ultra_Deep_Field" title="Hubble Ultra Deep Field">Hubble Ultra Deep Field</a> (UDF) identifier for a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> which was calculated as of October 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=UDFy-38135539&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> to have a light travel time of 13.1 <a href="/wiki/Giga-" title="Giga-">billion</a> years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup> with a present <a href="/wiki/Comoving_distance#Uses_of_the_proper_distance" title="Comoving distance">proper distance</a> of around 30 billion <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>.</p>
<p>It was discovered by three teams in September 2009 in sensitive infrared <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> images and identified by these as source UDF-38135539 (<a href="/wiki/Rychard_Bouwens" title="Rychard Bouwens">R Bouwens</a> <i>et al</i>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouwens_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwens-1">[1]</a></sup>) source HUDF.YD3 (A Bunker <i>et al</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunker_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunker-5">[5]</a></sup>) and source 1721 (R McLure <i>et al</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLure_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-McLure-6">[6]</a></sup>), and additionally reported in the <i>Astrophysical Journal</i>, and the <i>Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society</i>. All teams independently identified the source likely an extremely distant galaxy because there were no measurable light at <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible wavelengths</a> (caused by absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen gas</a> along the line of sight). Following the discovery of this candidate distant galaxy, another team targeted this object with ground-based <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> to confirm the distance, reporting a <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> z=8.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehnert2010_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lehnert2010-4">[4]</a></sup> However, attempts to replicate this observation strongly suggest the original claim was in error, meaning that at the present time the galaxy only has a photometric redshift estimate. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection">Detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UDFy-38135539&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its first known imaging was in <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble telescope</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Ultra_Deep_Field" title="Hubble Ultra Deep Field">Hubble Ultra Deep Field</a>, the most detailed deep space picture at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup> The galaxy was observed in August and September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciAm_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciAm-8">[8]</a></sup> The image data was released to the scientific community, which led to the galaxy's detection by the teams of Bouwens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouwens_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwens-1">[1]</a></sup> Bunker<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunker_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bunker-5">[5]</a></sup> and McLure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLure_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-McLure-6">[6]</a></sup> and subsequent spectroscopic campaign by the team of Lehnert and colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMUK_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMUK-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just on Hubble data, the galaxy could be an object intrinsically red and relatively close to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup> therefore, confirmation using suitably sensitive <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> equipment was needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMUK_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-DMUK-9">[9]</a></sup> This was attempted using the <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">European Southern Observatory</a>'s SINFONI-equipped <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a> unit Yepun, located atop <a href="/wiki/Cerro_Paranal" title="Cerro Paranal">Cerro Paranal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciAm_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-SciAm-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-11">[11]</a></sup> Lehnert's team observed the galaxy for 16 hours, and then analysed their results over 2 months,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMUK_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-DMUK-9">[9]</a></sup> and published their findings in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>, in October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehnert2010_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lehnert2010-4">[4]</a></sup> Since then, more sensitive measurements have failed to replicate the result, suggesting the spectroscopic claim was in error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunker2013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunker2013-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UDFy-38135539&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The galaxy is located in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Fornax" title="Fornax">Fornax</a>, and is estimated to have contained roughly a billion <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-13">[13]</a></sup> although it was only at most one tenth of the diameter of our own galaxy, the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, and had less than 1% of the mass of the Milky Way's stars. According to Lehnert (of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_de_Paris" title="Observatoire de Paris">Observatoire de Paris</a>), it was forming the same number of stars per year as our galaxy, but they were much smaller and less massive, making it "intensely star forming".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The light travel distance of the light that we observe from UDFy-38135539 (HUF.YD3) is more than 4 billion <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-13">[13]</a></sup> (13.1 billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a>), and it has a <a href="/wiki/Luminosity_distance" title="Luminosity distance">luminosity distance</a> of 86.9 billion parsecs (about 283 billion light years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> There are a number of different <a href="/wiki/Distance_measures_(cosmology)" title="Distance measures (cosmology)">distance measures in cosmology</a>, and both "light travel distance" and "luminosity distance" are different from the <a href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">comoving distance</a> or "proper distance" generally used in defining the size of the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (comoving distance and proper distance are defined to be equal at the present cosmological time, so they can be used interchangeably when talking about the distance to an object at present, but proper distance increases with time due to the expansion of the universe, and is the distance used in <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>; <i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/Comoving_distance#Uses_of_the_proper_distance" title="Comoving distance">Uses of the proper distance</a> for more on the physical meaning of this notion of 'distance'). The luminosity distance <i>D<sub>L</sub></i> is related to a factor called the "comoving transverse distance" <i>D<sub>M</sub></i> by the equation <span class="nowrap"><i>D<sub>L</sub></i> = (1 + <i>z</i>) <i>D<sub>M</sub></i></span>, where <i>z</i> is the redshift, and the comoving transverse distance is itself equal to the radial comoving distance (i.e., comoving distance between an object and ourselves) in a spatially flat universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> So with <span class="nowrap"><i>D<sub>L</sub></i> = 86.9 billion parsecs</span> and <span class="nowrap"><i>z</i> = 8.55</span>, the comoving distance would be about 9.1 billion parsecs (about 30 billion light years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calculator_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Calculator-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The infrared light that we now observe from the galaxy was emitted as ultraviolet radiation toward the end of an era when the universe was filled with atomic hydrogen, which absorbed at ultraviolet wavelengths. Because the galaxy's own light alone would not have been intense enough to ionize a large region and render it transparent, scientists suspect that a population of smaller, undetected galaxies, contributed to the reionization making UDFy-38135539 visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMUK_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-DMUK-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UDFy-38135539&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The period of universal star birth was the <a href="/wiki/Reionization" title="Reionization">reionization</a> epoch. The universe's first stars were massive, ionizing hydrogen in the surrounding environment (<i>Trenti</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>UDFy-38135539 (HUDF.YD3) is thought to be one of the first galaxies observed in the reionization epoch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> astronomer Brant Robertson, commenting on the study, stated that the "galaxy happens to reside at a very special time in cosmic history when the properties of gas in the universe were changing rapidly, and therefore this galaxy and others like it may teach us a lot about the early history of the universe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-14">[14]</a></sup> Michele Trenti, an astronomer who was not involved in the study but provided commentary published with the report, says that the discovery of the distant galaxy represents a</p>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">... fundamental leap forward in observational cosmology ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-14">[14]</a></sup></td>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_discoveries">Subsequent discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UDFy-38135539&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subsequent discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scientists hope to find older galaxies; however, closer to the Big Bang, fewer exist and they are dimmer on average. They will therefore be increasingly difficult to find, since they would be very faint with fewer observable stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery-20">[20]</a></sup> Trenti says that new "most distant" record holders will soon be announced, but only incremental distance gains will be realized until NASA's <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a> becomes operational in 2018.</p>
<p>The James Webb telescope should be able to detect galaxies more than 13.4 billion light years away, less than 300 million years after the Big Bang. Bremer states that it, and eventually the <a href="/wiki/European_Extremely_Large_Telescope" title="European Extremely Large Telescope">European Extremely Large Telescope</a>, which will have a mirror five times the diameter of Yepun's,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-11">[11]</a></sup> and is tentatively scheduled for completion in 2018, will enable more detailed study of galaxies at such great distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-23">[23]</a></sup> Lehnert states that this discovery is not "the limit, perhaps not even that close to it".</p>
<p>At that time, Trenti said redshift 8.6 would likely to be as high as we can reach with the current generation of Earth-bound telescopes, but that with Hubble, "it might be possible to find some galaxies up to redshift 10".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery-20">[20]</a></sup> Candidates with higher redshifts than UDFy-38135539's have been reported subsequently, but not yet confirmed with light spectrum instruments.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-NS-2">[2]</a></sup> for example <a href="/wiki/UDFj-39546284" title="UDFj-39546284">UDFj-39546284</a> and <a href="/wiki/MACS0647-JD" title="MACS0647-JD">MACS0647-JD</a>.</p>
