<p><b>.co</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_code" title="Country code">country code</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (<a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">ccTLD</a>) assigned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Colombia" title="Republic of Colombia">Colombia</a>. It is administered by .CO Internet S.A.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iana_root_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-iana_root-3">[3]</a></sup> As of July 10, 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there are no registration restrictions on second-level .co domains; any individual or entity in the world can register a .co domain.</p>
<p>.co has become increasingly popular among tech startups. The .co domain is also used by many established brands for social and mobile media, such as <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> (t.co), <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google Inc.</a> (<a href="/wiki/G.co" title="G.co">g.co</a>), <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> (a.co), <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> (amex.co) and <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a> (sbux.co).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GO.CO_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GO.CO-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>.co domain names are available for registration globally through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accredited_registrars" title="Accredited registrars">accredited registrars</a>.</p>
<p>.CO Internet S.A.S from <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bogotá</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, was appointed as the manager for the .co TLD through a public procurement process that took place in early 2009. .CO Internet received the re-delegation approval as the manager of the .co TLD by ICANN on December 9, 2009, and received formal confirmation of the request by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a> on December 23, 2009.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-level_domain_names">Second-level domain names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Second-level domain names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When they took over administration of the .CO domain, .CO Internet S.A.S. implemented new domain policies that were more flexible than the historic ones that had been administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_the_Andes,_Colombia" title="University of the Andes, Colombia">University of the Andes</a>. The new policies were adjusted to international best practices and defined in consultation with local and international communities. With the new policies, Colombia would be able to sell second-level domain names to the world, such as widgets.co, where previously only third-level domain names were available, such as widgets.com.co.</p>
<p>To celebrate the launch of second-level domains, the registry auctioned the first single letter .CO domain name <a class="external text" href="http://e.CO/" rel="nofollow">"e.CO"</a> during Internet Week on June 10, 2010. A video of the auction can be seen here:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For a purchase price of $81,000, the winner of the auction was internet entrepreneur Lonnie Borck of B52 Media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Proceeds were donated to a charitable cause of the winner's choice.</p>

<p>As of June 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, more than 1 million .CO domains had been registered by people in over 200 countries and territories worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GO.CO_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-GO.CO-4">[4]</a></sup> As of January 2014, that number has grown to over 1.6 million .CO domains registered.</p>
<p>With respect to <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">search engine optimization</a>, Google confirmed that "it will rank .co domains appropriately if the content is globally targeted".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Google has also confirmed that it will treat .co as a <a href="/wiki/GccTLD" title="GccTLD">gccTLD</a> for purposes of indexing and seo.</p>
<p>More information about .CO and SEO can be found on the Registry's consumer facing website, including videos from Google's SEO expert Matt Cutts confirming Google's positive treatment of .CO for SEO purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_policies_since_2010">Summary of policies since 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary of policies since 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Any person or entity in the world can register .co domain names</li>
<li>There are no domicile or burdensome documentation requirements</li>
<li>Registration period is between 1 and 5 years, subject to renewal</li>
<li>Registrants can easily transfer domain names</li>
</ul>
<p>.CO domains became available via the following timeline:</p>
<ul>
<li>April 1, 2010 – April 20, 2010: Sunrise A allowed registered local trademarks to apply for exact match domains.</li>
<li>April 26, 2010 – June 10, 2010: Sunrise B allowed trademarks of national effect to apply for exact match domains.</li>
<li>June 21, 2010 – July 13, 2010: Landrush allowed anyone to apply for domain names of high commercial value.</li>
<li>July 20, 2010: .co domains became generally available.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Third-level_domain_registrations">Third-level domain registrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Third-level domain registrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The third level domain registrations closely mirror the "traditional" IANA .com / .net / .org / .gov / .edu / .mil hierarchy, with the addition of a national equivalent of .name. Different from registrations directly under .co which are used to signal globally relevant interests, third level domains are used to signal locally relevant business, organizations, academic institutions, and government.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>com.co</b> – commercial</li>
<li><b>org.co</b> – organizations</li>
<li><b>edu.co</b> – educational</li>
<li><b>gov.co</b> – government</li>
<li><b>net.co</b> – network infrastructure</li>
<li><b>mil.co</b> – military</li>
<li><b>nom.co</b> – private person</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">IANA</a> delegates <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166-1_alpha-2" title="ISO 3166-1 alpha-2">ISO 3166-1 alpha-2</a> codes as <a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">country code top-level domains</a>, and on December 24, 1991, the .co top-level domain was assigned to Colombia and delegated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_the_Andes,_Colombia" title="University of the Andes, Colombia">Universidad de los Andes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iana_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-iana-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the university began to consider the possibility of marketing the domain as an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Generic_top-level_domain" title="Generic top-level domain">generic top-level domains</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Colombia" title="Government of Colombia">government of Colombia</a> objected on the basis that the university, a private entity, did not have regulatory oversight of the TLD and the Minister of Communications, Angela Montoya Holguín, wrote to them requesting that they not continue. In turn the university wrote to <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>, rejecting the government's objections and stating their intention to appoint a <a href="/wiki/Subcontractor" title="Subcontractor">subcontractor</a> to handle the commercialisation of the domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iana_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-iana-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a meeting on December 11, 2001, Holguín asked the Consultative Chamber and Civil Service of the Council of State to consider three issues:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radication1376_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-radication1376-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>whether the .co domain is a public resource</li>
<li>if the domain is public resource, whether it is intrinsically linked with <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a></li>
<li>if the domain is linked with telecommunications, who should profit from its commercialisation</li>
</ol>
<p>In relation to these three issues, the meeting concluded that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radication1376_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-radication1376-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>the .co domain, having been assigned to Colombia, is of public interest</li>
<li>the administration of the domain is intrinsically related to telecommunications, and hence falls under the purview of the Ministry of Communications, with the exception of those functions assigned to the <a href="/wiki/ICFES" title="ICFES">ICFES</a> by the Ministry of National Education</li>
<li>unless the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Colombia" title="Congress of Colombia">Congress of Colombia</a> adopts an act allowing tax to be collected in relation to the registration of domain names, no amount can be charged for such a service</li>
</ol>
<p>In response to the Council of State meeting, the university wrote to ICANN on 12 February 2002 stating that it had abandoned plans to commercialise the domain, and that as it could "no longer bear the administrative and operational responsibilities" it wished to discontinue its responsibility for operating the domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iana_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-iana-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, with the enactment of Law 1065 of 2006, the Ministry of Communications of Colombia initiated a public consultation process involving local and international participants, including members of the ICANN community, with the objective of defining the future of the .CO TLD. As a result of that process, through Resolution 001652 of 2008, the Ministry approved new policies that would govern the administration of the .CO TLD. A public procurement process began which resulted in the award of the administration contract to .CO Internet SAS. Finally, on February 7, 2010, the administration of the TLD was transitioned from the University of Andes to .CO Internet SAS, under the regulatory and policy supervision of the Ministry of Communications of Colombia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MINTIC_Public_Consultation_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MINTIC_Public_Consultation-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 20, 2010, second level .co domains became available to the rest of the world on a <a href="/wiki/First-come,_first-served" title="First-come, first-served">first-come, first-served</a> basis.</p>
<p>In a historic moment for Colombia and the .CO domain extension, ICANN celebrated its 39th International public meeting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">Cartagena de Indias</a> from December 5, 2010 through December 10, 2010. The meeting's host was .CO Internet S.A.S., the registry operator of the .CO domain.</p>
<p>In addition to more than 1000 guests from 100 countries, the meeting was introduced by Colombia's President <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Santos" title="Juan Manuel Santos">Juan Manuel Santos</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-President_Santos.2C_ICANN_39.2C_Opening_Ceremony_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-President_Santos.2C_ICANN_39.2C_Opening_Ceremony-12">[12]</a></sup> Communications Minister Diego Molano, ICANN Chairman Peter Dengate Thrush, and ICANN CEO Rod Beckstrom. During his opening remarks, .CO Internet CEO <a href="/wiki/Juan_Diego_Calle" title="Juan Diego Calle">Juan Diego Calle</a> hailed the meeting as one of historic proportion, marking Colombia's entry into the world stage as a new and significant player in the development of the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Juan_Diego_Calle.2C_ICANN_39.2C_Opening_Ceremony_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Juan_Diego_Calle.2C_ICANN_39.2C_Opening_Ceremony-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, .CO Internet S.A.S was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Neustar" title="Neustar">Neustar</a> for US$109 Million, and became a wholly owned subsidiary of Neustar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is responsible for the promotion, administration, and technical operation of the .co TLD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accredited_registrars">Accredited registrars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accredited registrars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only accredited registrars are able to sell .co domain names directly; other registrars selling .co domain names are acting as <a href="/wiki/Reseller" title="Reseller">resellers</a>. The list of accredited registrars is available on the .CO Internet website, and as of October 2011 there are 20 accredited registrars. Some of the 20 registrars operate under multiple brands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fake_news">Fake news</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.co&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fake news">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
