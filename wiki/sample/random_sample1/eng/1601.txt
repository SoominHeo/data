<p><b>USS <i>Nimitz</i> (CVN-68)</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Supercarrier" title="Supercarrier">supercarrier</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lead_ship" title="Lead ship">lead ship</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nimitz_class_aircraft_carrier" title="Nimitz class aircraft carrier">her class</a>. One of the largest <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warships</a> in the world, she was laid down, launched and commissioned as CVAN-68 but was redesignated CVN 68 (nuclear-powered multimission aircraft carrier) on 30 June 1975 as part of the fleet realignment.</p>
<p>The ship was named for <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Pacific fleet commander <a href="/wiki/Chester_W._Nimitz" title="Chester W. Nimitz">Chester W. Nimitz</a>, who was the Navy’s third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_Admiral_(United_States)" title="Fleet Admiral (United States)">fleet admiral</a>. <i>Nimitz</i> had her homeport at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a> until 1987, when she was relocated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puget_Sound_Naval_Shipyard" title="Puget Sound Naval Shipyard">Puget Sound Naval Shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bremerton,_Washington" title="Bremerton, Washington">Bremerton, Washington</a>. Following her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refueling_and_Complex_Overhaul" title="Refueling and Complex Overhaul">Refueling and Complex Overhaul</a> in 2001, her homeport was changed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAS_North_Island" title="NAS North Island">NAS North Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The home port of <i>Nimitz</i> was again moved to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Everett" title="Naval Station Everett">Naval Station Everett</a> in 2012.</p>
<p>In January 2015 <i>Nimitz</i> changed homeport from <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Everett" title="Naval Station Everett">Naval Station Everett</a> to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Base_Kitsap" title="Naval Base Kitsap">Naval Base Kitsap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nimitz</i> was authorized by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 1967 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_Newport_News" title="Northrop Grumman Newport News">Newport News Shipbuilding and Dry Dock Co.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News, Virginia</a> was awarded the $106.5 million (equivalent to $764.95 million today). The <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keel</a> was laid down 22 June 1968. It was christened on 13 May 1972 by Catherine Nimitz Lay, the daughter of the late Admiral Nimitz. <i>Nimitz</i> was delivered to the Navy in 1975, and was commissioned at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a> on 3 May 1975 by President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_history">Service history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Service history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>USS <i>Nimitz</i> first deployed to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> on 7 July 1976 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_8" title="Carrier Air Wing 8">Carrier Air Wing 8</a> embarked in company with the nuclear-powered <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_South_Carolina_(CGN-37)" title="USS South Carolina (CGN-37)">USS <i>South Carolina</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_California_(CGN-36)" title="USS California (CGN-36)">USS <i>California</i></a>. In November 1976, <i>Nimitz</i> was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_%22E%22" title='Battle "E"'>Battle "E"</a> from <a href="/wiki/Commander,_Naval_Air_Force_U.S._Atlantic_Fleet" title="Commander, Naval Air Force U.S. Atlantic Fleet">Commander, Naval Air Force U.S. Atlantic Fleet</a>, for being the most efficient and foremost aircraft carrier in the Atlantic Fleet. The cruise was uneventful, and the carrier returned to <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a> on 7 February 1977.</p>
<p>A second uneventful Mediterranean cruise was conducted from 1 December 1977 to 20 July 1978. The third deployment began on 10 September 1979 to the Mediterranean. The ship moved to the Indian Ocean in response to the <a href="/wiki/Iran_hostage_crisis" title="Iran hostage crisis">Iran hostage crisis</a> in which the U.S. Embassy in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, was overtaken and 52 hostages were held. Prior to this trip, the ship took part in the shooting of the 1980 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Final_Countdown_(film)" title="The Final Countdown (film)">The Final Countdown</a></i>, whose story was specifically set aboard the <i>Nimitz</i>. After four months on station, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Evening_Light" title="Operation Evening Light">Operation Evening Light</a> was launched from <i>Nimitz</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s decks in an attempt to rescue the U.S. Embassy staff. The mission was aborted after a helicopter crashed at a refueling point in the Iranian desert. The ship returned home 26 May 1980, having spent 144 days at sea.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 26 May 1981, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_EA-6B_Prowler" title="Northrop Grumman EA-6B Prowler">EA-6B Prowler</a> assigned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_8" title="Carrier Air Wing 8">Carrier Air Wing 8</a> (CVW-8) crashed on the flight deck, killing 14 crewmen and injuring 45 others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-7">[7]</a></sup> The Prowler was fuel-critical after a "bolter" (missed approach), and its crash and the subsequent fire and explosions destroyed or damaged nineteen other aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USS_Nimitz_crash_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-USS_Nimitz_crash-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Despite having no connection to the accident, the media focused on the autopsy results of several members of the <i>Nimitz</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s enlisted flight deck crew who were killed, who <a href="/wiki/Drug_test" title="Drug test">tested positive</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">marijuana</a>. As a result, President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> instituted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_Tolerance_drug_policy" title="Zero Tolerance drug policy">"Zero Tolerance" drug policy</a> across all of the U.S. armed services, which started the mandatory drug testing of all U.S. military personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i> deployed again to the Mediterranean on 3 August 1981. The ship, in company with <a href="/wiki/USS_Forrestal_(CV-59)" title="USS Forrestal (CV-59)">USS <i>Forrestal</i></a>, conducted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Navigation" title="Freedom of Navigation">Freedom of Navigation</a> exercise in international waters in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra" title="Gulf of Sidra">Gulf of Sidra</a> near <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> on 18 and 19 August 1981. On the morning of 19 August 1981, two <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F-14_Tomcat" title="Grumman F-14 Tomcat">Grumman F-14 Tomcats</a> of <a href="/wiki/VF-41" title="VF-41">VF-41</a> were engaged by two Libyan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-23" title="MiG-23">MiG-23s</a>, resulting in the two Libyan aircraft being shot down in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra_incident_(1981)" title="Gulf of Sidra incident (1981)">Gulf of Sidra incident</a>.</p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s fourth deployment, from 10 November 1982 to 20 May 1983, was to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the Mediterranean Sea. <i>Nimitz</i> deployed for a fifth time on 8 March 1985. On 14 June 1985, two Lebanese gunmen hijacked <a href="/wiki/TWA_Flight_847" title="TWA Flight 847">TWA Flight 847</a>, which carried 153 passengers and crew and included Americans. In response, <i>Nimitz</i> was deployed to the coast of <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, where it remained until August 1985. The embarked Airwing 8 flew continuous sorties for 67 days, bombing several sites in <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a> including the runways of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beirut_Rafic_Hariri_International_Airport" title="Beirut Rafic Hariri International Airport">Beirut Rafic Hariri International Airport</a>. The ship returned to Norfolk on 4 October 1985.</p>

<p><i>Nimitz</i>, again with CVW-8 embarked, departed Norfolk for its sixth and final Mediterranean deployment on 30 December 1986. After four months and numerous Mediterranean port visits, the carrier crossed the equator en route to <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>. From Rio de Janeiro, it proceeded south around <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. After a brief stop in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, to offload its East Coast air wing, it arrived at its new home port of <a href="/wiki/Bremerton,_Washington" title="Bremerton, Washington">Bremerton, Washington</a>, on 2 July 1987.</p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i> deployed to the Western Pacific with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_9" title="Carrier Air Wing 9">Carrier Air Wing 9</a> embarked on 2 September 1988. During the <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Olympics" title="1988 Summer Olympics">1988 Olympic Games</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <i>Nimitz</i> provided security off the coast of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. In October, it operated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Arabian_Sea" title="North Arabian Sea">North Arabian Sea</a> participating in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Earnest_Will" title="Operation Earnest Will">Operation Earnest Will</a>, the protection of reflagged <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwaiti</a> tankers. On 30 November 1988, while in the Arabian Sea, a 20 mm cannon accidentally fired during maintenance, striking a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KA-6_Intruder" title="KA-6 Intruder">KA-6 Intruder</a>. The ensuing fire spread to six other aircraft, and two sailors were killed. <i>Nimitz</i> returned to Bremerton on 2 March 1989.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 25 February 1991, <i>Nimitz</i> departed Bremerton for the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> in relief of <a href="/wiki/USS_Ranger_(CV-61)" title="USS Ranger (CV-61)">USS <i>Ranger</i></a> in the aftermath of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Storm" title="Operation Desert Storm">Operation Desert Storm</a>, returning to Bremerton on 24 August 1991. <i>Nimitz</i> again deployed to the Persian Gulf on 1 February 1993, in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Southern_Watch" title="Operation Southern Watch">Operation Southern Watch</a>, returning on 1 August 1993.</p>

<p>On 27 November 1995, <i>Nimitz</i> deployed to the Western Pacific, Indian Ocean and Persian Gulf with Carrier Air Wing Nine (CVW-9). In March 1996, it patrolled the waters off <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> amid <a href="/wiki/Third_Taiwan_Strait_Crisis" title="Third Taiwan Strait Crisis">missile tests conducted by the Chinese</a> in the area, becoming the first American warship to pass through the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Strait" title="Taiwan Strait">Taiwan Strait</a> since 1976. It also cruised the Persian Gulf in support of Southern Watch prior to returning from deployment on 20 May 1996.</p>
<p>Between 14–24 July 1997, <i>Nimitz</i> participated in Joint Task Force Exercise 97-2 (JTFEX 97-2) off the coast of southern California, which also served as a "Revolution in Strike Warfare" demonstration. The latter event was designed to demonstrate the capability of an aircraft carrier and its embarked air wing to project carrier-based airpower into littoral warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS-5">[5]</a></sup> On 20 July 1997, <i>Nimitz</i> and Carrier Air Wing Nine began a high-intensity strike campaign. When flight operations were completed four days later, <i>Nimitz</i> and Carrier Air Wing Nine had carried out 771 strike sorties while dropping 1,337 bombs on target. Carrier Air Wing Nine flew 975 fixed-wing sorties during this four-day surge operation. Almost 80 percent of the sorties flown were strike sorties, with strike support accounting for another 10 percent. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F/A-18" title="F/A-18">F/A-18 Hornet strike fighters</a> flew nearly 80 percent of the strike sorties. Of the 771 strike sorties, 727 were loaded with ordnance, while 44 were electronic support by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EA-6B_Prowler" title="EA-6B Prowler">EA-6B Prowlers</a>. During this four-day period, only a portion of the medium-range interdiction strikes required tanking support. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-135" title="KC-135">KC-135</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-130" title="KC-130">KC-130</a> tanker aircraft provided most of this support. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-3_Viking" title="S-3 Viking">S-3 Vikings</a> conducted recovery tanking and supplied more than one-third of the fuel passed to Carrier Air Wing Nine aircraft during this surge operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNIblog08272009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNIblog08272009-12">[12]</a></sup> This surge had been preceded by a 16-hour preparation after undergoing four days that had generated about 700 fixed-winged sorties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNIblog08272009_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-USNIblog08272009-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A following study by the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Naval_Analyses" title="Center for Naval Analyses">Center for Naval Analyses</a> determined that <i>Nimitz</i> and Carrier Air Wing Nine could have maintained this high-sortie operational tempo for another twelve to twenty-four hours before requiring equipment maintenance, rest for its crews and its ordnance and aviation fuel stocks to be replenished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 September 1997, <i>Nimitz</i> began an around the world cruise, again supporting Southern Watch, which ended in <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News, Virginia</a> on 2 March 1998. It next spent the next three years undergoing a nuclear Refueling and Complex Overhaul that ended on 25 June 2001.</p>

<p>On 21 September 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UScarriers_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UScarriers-15">[15]</a></sup> after sea trials in the Virginia Capes, <i>Nimitz</i> began its transit around South America to its new home port of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAS_North_Island" title="NAS North Island">NAS North Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, arriving there on 13 November 2001. Aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Tactical_Support_Wing" title="Tactical Support Wing">Carrier Air Reserve Wing 20</a> were embarked for the transit. From January to May 2002, it underwent a four-month post-shakedown maintenance availability at North Island; during this time <a href="/wiki/Advanced_combat_direction_system" title="Advanced combat direction system">Advanced combat direction system</a> was installed.</p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s eleventh operational deployment began on 3 March 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It relieved <a href="/wiki/USS_Abraham_Lincoln_(CVN-72)" title="USS Abraham Lincoln (CVN-72)">USS <i>Abraham Lincoln</i></a> in the Persian Gulf in mid-April 2003, launching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_11" title="Carrier Air Wing 11">Carrier Air Wing 11</a> aircraft sorties over <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in support of <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> (OIF) and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Enduring_Freedom" title="Operation Enduring Freedom">Operation Enduring Freedom</a> (OEF). It returned to San Diego on 5 November 2003. <i>Nimitz</i> and CVW-11 were awarded the 2003 Battle "E"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatley_Award" title="Flatley Award">Flatley Award</a> in early 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i>, again with CVW-11 embarked, deployed to the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> on 7 May 2005, returning on 8 November 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This deployment marked three decades of service, and was depicted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy" title="Emmy">Emmy</a> award-winning 2008 <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/Carrier_(documentary)" title="Carrier (documentary)">Carrier</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In June 2006, <i>Nimitz</i> was awarded the 2005 Battle "E".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The carrier departed North Island for its thirteenth deployment on 2 April 2007 to the Arabian Sea, relieving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Dwight_D._Eisenhower_(CVN-69)" title="USS Dwight D. Eisenhower (CVN-69)">USS <i>Dwight D. Eisenhower</i></a> in support of OIF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It anchored off <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, India on 2 July 2007 as part of efforts to expand bilateral defense cooperation between India and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Sailors participated in community work in Chennai prior to departing, on 5 July 2007, along with the destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Pinckney_(DDG-91)" title="USS Pinckney (DDG-91)">USS <i>Pinckney</i></a> towards the Persian Gulf. It returned to North Island on 30 September 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 January 2008, <i>Nimitz</i> deployed to the Pacific for a "surge"-deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On 9 February 2008, two Russian <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-95" title="Tupolev Tu-95">Tu-95 'Bear'</a> bombers overflew the carrier in the Western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-26">[26]</a></sup> Four F/A-18C Hornets were launched when the bombers were 500 miles (800 km) away from the US ships, and intercepted the bombers 50 miles (80 km) south of <i>Nimitz</i>. Two F/A-18s trailed one of the bombers, which twice flew over the deck of the carrier at an altitude of 2,000 feet (610 m), while the other two F/A-18s trailed another Tu-95 circling about 50 miles (80 km) away from the carrier. Reportedly, there was no radio communication between the American and Russian aircraft. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>, one of the two aircraft was said to have flown above <i>Nimitz</i> at an altitude of 2,000 feet (610 m). On the same day, Russian aircraft entered Japanese airspace, which caused the Japanese to raise protest to the Russian ambassador in Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Again, on 5 March 2008, a Russian bomber came within 3 to 5 nautical miles (6 to 9 km) and flew 2,000 feet (610 m) above <i>Nimitz</i> and its battle group. Two F/A-18 fighters intercepted the Russian aircraft and escorted it out of the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i> was awarded the Navy Battle "E" for battle efficiency for 2007 along with the Ney award for food service excellence. It returned to its home port of San Diego on 3 June 2008.</p>
<p>The <i>Nimitz</i> Strike Group, including CVW-11, departed the States for a scheduled Western Pacific deployment on 31 July 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and began to fly combat missions in support of Operation Enduring Freedom 21 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>In January 2010, while in the Persian Gulf, the ship was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Meritorious_Unit_Commendation" title="Meritorious Unit Commendation">Meritorious Unit Commendation</a> for its back-to-back deployments in support of the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan in 2007 and 2008. The award was presented by Admiral <a href="/wiki/Gary_Roughead" title="Gary Roughead">Gary Roughead</a> in a ceremony on the ship 6 January 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Nimitz</i> visited <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> for five days in February 2010 to allow its crew to rest and visit the city. The visit occurred despite China previously preventing a visit by the carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_Kitty_Hawk_(CV-63)" title="USS Kitty Hawk (CV-63)">USS <i>Kitty Hawk</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 December 2010, the Navy formally announced that <a href="/wiki/Everett,_Washington" title="Everett, Washington">Everett, Washington</a> was to be the new home port for <i>Nimitz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NNS101209-21_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-NNS101209-21-33">[33]</a></sup> This move was expected to save the Navy $100 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On 9 March 2012, <i>Nimitz</i> arrived at its new homeport of Naval Station Everett after spending nearly a week at sea conducting post overhaul sea trials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2012, <i>Nimitz</i> arrived at its new home port of <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Everett" title="Naval Station Everett">Naval Station Everett</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington state</a> after more than a year of maintenance work in Bremerton. It replaced the <i>Nimitz</i>-class carrier, <i>Abraham Lincoln</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> On 3 August 2012, <i>Nimitz</i> departed from Pearl Harbor after a two-day port call, arriving at NAS North Island on 9 August 2012 to begin <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Replacement_Squadron" title="Fleet Replacement Squadron">Fleet Replacement Squadron</a> carrier qualifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UScarriers_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-UScarriers-15">[15]</a></sup> On 6 October 2012, a <a href="/wiki/Bell_Boeing_V-22_Osprey" title="Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey">Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey</a> tilt-rotor aircraft from squadron <a href="/wiki/VMM-165" title="VMM-165">VMM-165</a> landed and refuelled on board <i>Nimitz</i>. This operation was part of an evaluation of the feasibility of the MV-22 as a potential replacement for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-2_Greyhound" title="C-2 Greyhound">C-2 Greyhound</a> <a href="/wiki/Carrier_onboard_delivery" title="Carrier onboard delivery">carrier onboard delivery</a> (COD) cargo transport aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>The BBC reported that <i>Nimitz</i> was located in the Persian Gulf, ready to contribute to an operation against Syria when President Obama ordered a military strike. Two days later it was reported said that the carrier and its task group had been re-routed westwards across the Arabian Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was reported that the ship—in its eighth month out to sea—transited the Suez Canal on 20 October 2013 into the U.S. 6th Fleet area of responsibility, where the Navy intended to keep it for a few weeks conducting joint training with allied nations before returning it home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <i>Nimitz</i> returned to Everett on 16 December 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2014, following the completion of work up qualifications, <i>Nimitz</i> participated in her first deployment, a two-week multi-national fleet exercise involving the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Third_Fleet" title="United States Third Fleet">Third Fleet</a>, as well as ships from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Navy" title="Royal Canadian Navy">Royal Canadian Navy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Maritime_Self_Defence_Force" title="Japanese Maritime Self Defence Force">JMSDF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Following the conclusion of the exercise, on November 3 the first <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35C Lightning II</a> to land on an aircraft carrier recovered aboard <i>Nimitz</i> to begin a two-week Development Testing I deployment. This saw a pair of aircraft from <a href="/wiki/VX-23" title="VX-23">VX-23</a> undertaking carrier operations of launch, recovery and handling aboard ship in both day and night conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The initial deployment was completed on 14 November 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In 2015, <i>Nimitz</i> transferred to Bremerton to undergo a 16-month maintenance cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eventual_replacement">Eventual replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Nimitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Eventual replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Nimitz</i>-class carriers have a lifespan of approximately 50 years. <i>Nimitz</i> itself is projected to be replaced around 2025-2027 by the <a href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Ford-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carrier"><i>Gerald R. Ford</i>-class</a> aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_John_F._Kennedy_(CVN-79)" title="USS John F. Kennedy (CVN-79)">USS <i>John F. Kennedy</i> (CVN-79)</a>. The exact date of the ship's inactivation and decommissioning will likely depend on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Defense Department</a> funding considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
