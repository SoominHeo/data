<p><b>USS <i>John C. Stennis</i> (CVN-74)</b> is the seventh <a href="/wiki/Nimitz-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Nimitz-class aircraft carrier"><i>Nimitz</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear-powered</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercarrier" title="Supercarrier">supercarrier</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, named for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_C._Stennis" title="John C. Stennis">John C. Stennis</a> of Mississippi. She was commissioned on 9 December 1995. Her <a href="/wiki/Home_port" title="Home port">home port</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bremerton,_Washington" title="Bremerton, Washington">Bremerton, Washington</a>.</p>

<p>The mission of <i>John C. Stennis</i> and her air wing (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CVW-9" title="CVW-9">CVW-9</a>) is to conduct sustained combat air operations while forward-deployed. The embarked air wing consists of eight to nine squadrons. Attached aircraft are Navy and Marine Corps <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F/A-18_Hornet" title="F/A-18 Hornet">F/A-18 Hornet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EA-6_Prowler" title="EA-6 Prowler">EA-6B Prowler</a>, MH-60R, MH-60S, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-2_Hawkeye" title="E-2 Hawkeye">E-2C Hawkeye</a>.</p>
<p>The air wing can engage enemy aircraft, submarines, and land targets, or lay mines hundreds of miles from the ship. <i>John C. Stennis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s aircraft are used to conduct strikes, support land battles, protect the battle group or other friendly shipping, and implement a sea or air blockade. The air wing provides a visible presence to demonstrate American power and resolve in a crisis. The ship normally operates as the centerpiece of a <a href="/wiki/Carrier_battle_group" title="Carrier battle group">carrier battle group</a> commanded by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_Officer" title="Flag Officer">flag officer</a> embarked upon <i>John C. Stennis</i> and consisting of four to six other ships.</p>
<p><i>John C. Stennis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s two <a href="/wiki/A4W_reactor" title="A4W reactor">nuclear reactors</a> give her virtually unlimited range and endurance and a top speed in excess of 30 knots (56 km/h, 34.5 mph). The ship's four catapults and four <a href="/wiki/Arresting_gear" title="Arresting gear">arresting gear</a> engines enable her to launch and recover aircraft rapidly and simultaneously. The ship carries approximately 3 million US gallons (11,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of fuel for her aircraft and escorts, and enough weapons and stores for extended operations without replenishment. <i>John C. Stennis</i> also has extensive repair capabilities, including a fully equipped Aircraft Intermediate Maintenance Department, a micro-miniature electronics repair shop, and numerous ship repair shops.</p>
<p>For defense, in addition to her air wing and accompanying vessels, <i>John C. Stennis</i> has <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">RIM-7 Sea Sparrow</a> and <a href="/wiki/RIM-116_Rolling_Airframe_Missile#Sea_RAM" title="RIM-116 Rolling Airframe Missile">Rolling Airframe Missile</a> (RAM) <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> systems, the <a href="/wiki/Phalanx_CIWS" title="Phalanx CIWS">Phalanx</a> <a href="/wiki/Close-in_weapon_system" title="Close-in weapon system">Close-in Weapons System</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missile</a> defense, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AN/SLQ-32" title="AN/SLQ-32">AN/SLQ-32</a> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">Electronic Warfare</a> System.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nuclear-powered USS <i>John C. Stennis</i> (CVN 74) was contracted on 29 March 1988, and the keel was laid on 13 March 1991 at <a href="/wiki/Newport_News_Shipbuilding" title="Newport News Shipbuilding">Newport News Shipbuilding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newport_News" title="Newport News">Newport News</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.</p>
<p>The ship was christened on 11 November 1993, in honor of Senator John Cornelius Stennis (D-Mississippi) who served in the Senate from 1947 to 1989. The daughter of the ship’s namesake, Mrs. Margaret Stennis-Womble, was the ship’s sponsor. <i>John C. Stennis</i> was commissioned on 9 December 1995 at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a>, Va, and she conducted <a href="/wiki/Flight_deck" title="Flight deck">flight deck</a> certification in January 1996. The first arrested landing was by a <a href="/wiki/VX-23" title="VX-23">VX-23</a> <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F-14_Tomcat" title="Grumman F-14 Tomcat">F-14</a>B. The ship conducted numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_US_Navy_carrier_air_operations#Carrier_qualifications" title="Modern US Navy carrier air operations">carrier qualifications</a> and independent steaming exercises off the East Coast throughout the next two years. Included among these events was the first carrier landing of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F/A-18E/F_Super_Hornet" title="F/A-18E/F Super Hornet">F/A-18E/F Super Hornet</a> on 18 January 1997.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1998_.E2.80.93_World_Cruise">1998 – World Cruise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1998 – World Cruise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 26 February 1998 with <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_Seven" title="Carrier Air Wing Seven">Carrier Air Wing Seven</a> embarked, <i>John C. Stennis</i> left Norfolk for her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden deployment</a>, transiting the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a> on 7 March and arriving in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> on 11 March 1998. The ship traveled 8020 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nm</a> in 274 hours, an average speed of 29.4 knots (54.4 km/h; 33.8 mph) to relieve <a href="/wiki/USS_George_Washington_(CVN-73)" title="USS George Washington (CVN-73)">USS <i>George Washington</i></a> in conducting <a href="/wiki/Operation_Southern_Watch" title="Operation Southern Watch">Operation Southern Watch</a> missions. <i>John C. Stennis</i> departed the Persian Gulf on 19 July 1998 for her new home port of <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_North_Island" title="Naval Air Station North Island">Naval Air Station North Island</a> in San Diego, California, arriving on 26 August 1998.</p>
<p>In October 1998, she entered a six-month maintenance and upgrade period at North Island, returning to sea in April 1999. During the maintenance period, a <a href="/wiki/Jet_blast_deflector" title="Jet blast deflector">jet blast deflector</a> collapsed, severely injuring two sailors.</p>
<p>On 30 November 1999, the ship ran aground in a shallow area adjacent to the turning basin near North Island. Silt clogged the intake pipes to the steam condensing systems for the nuclear reactor plants, causing the carrier's two nuclear reactors to be shut down (one reactor by crew, the other automatically) for a period of 45 minutes. She was towed back to her pier for maintenance and observation for the next two days. The cleanup cost was about $2 million.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_.E2.80.93_Persian_Gulf.2FPacific_Ocean">2000 – Persian Gulf/Pacific Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000 – Persian Gulf/Pacific Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 7 January 2000, <i>John C. Stennis</i> deployed to the Persian Gulf to relieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_John_F._Kennedy_(CV_67)" title="USS John F. Kennedy (CV 67)">USS <i>John F. Kennedy</i></a> in Operation Southern Watch. During the deployment, the ship made port visits to South Korea, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Bahrain, the United Arab Emirates and Australia, and Pearl Harbor, before returning to San Diego on 3 July 2000.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, <i>John C. Stennis</i> conducted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_Eagle" title="Noble Eagle">Noble Eagle</a> missions off the U.S. West Coast. In 2000 and 2001, <i>John C. Stennis</i> was part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Group_7" title="Carrier Group 7">Carrier Group 7</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001_.E2.80.93_Persian_Gulf">2001 – Persian Gulf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2001 – Persian Gulf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 12 November 2001, two months earlier than scheduled, the ship left on her third deployment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Fifth_Fleet" title="U.S. Fifth Fleet">U.S. Fifth Fleet</a> area of responsibility in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Enduring_Freedom" title="Operation Enduring Freedom">Operation Enduring Freedom</a>, returning to San Diego on 28 May 2002. From June 2002 to January 2003, <i>JCS</i> underwent a seven-month Planned Incremental Availability (PIA).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_.E2.80.93_Pacific_Ocean">2004 – Pacific Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2004 – Pacific Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 24 May to 1 November 2004, <i>John C. Stennis</i> conducted her fourth major overseas deployment, participating in Exercise Northern Edge 2004 in the Gulf of Alaska, Rim of the Pacific (RimPac) Exercise off Hawaii, exercises with <i>Kitty Hawk</i> off Japan and goodwill visits to Japan, Malaysia and Western Australia. Shortly after returning from deployment to San Diego, <i>JCS</i> changed her home port to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Bremerton" title="Naval Station Bremerton">Naval Station Bremerton</a>, Washington on 19 January 2005. Once at Bremerton, <i>John C. Stennis</i> underwent an 11-month docking planned incremental availability (DPIA), the first time she had been dry-docked since commissioning. Upgrades included a new mast. The new mast’s structure is the first of its kind. A new type of steel alloy was used, making it stiffer and thicker than before. The new mast is also heavier and taller, allowing it to support new antennae the old mast would not have been able to support. Other upgrades included the installation of a new integrated bridge system in the pilothouse that will save manpower and provide state-of-the-art displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the maintenance cycle and pre-deployment training exercises, the carrier returned to Bremerton, Washington, and the carrier was certified surge ready, meaning the ship maintained a high state of readiness in case of an unscheduled deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007_.E2.80.93_Persian_Gulf">2007 – Persian Gulf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2007 – Persian Gulf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On 20 January 2007, the carrier and her group set sail for the Persian Gulf as part of an increase in US military presence. <i>John C. Stennis</i> arrived in the area on 19 February 2007, joining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Dwight_D._Eisenhower_(CVN_69)" title="USS Dwight D. Eisenhower (CVN 69)">USS <i>Dwight D. Eisenhower</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fifth_Fleet" title="United States Fifth Fleet">United States Fifth Fleet</a> area of operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007-02-20_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-2007-02-20-9">[9]</a></sup> This marked the first time since 2003 that there were two <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> battle groups in the region simultaneously.</p>
<p>On 23 May 2007, <i>John C. Stennis</i>, along with eight other warships including the aircraft carrier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Nimitz_(CVN_68)" title="USS Nimitz (CVN 68)"><i>Nimitz</i></a> and amphibious assault ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Bonhomme_Richard_(LHD-6)" title="USS Bonhomme Richard (LHD-6)"><i>Bonhomme Richard</i></a>, passed through the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" title="Strait of Hormuz">Strait of Hormuz</a>. US Navy officials said it was the largest such move since 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005-05-28_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005-05-28-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 31 August 2007 <i>John C. Stennis</i> returned to Bremerton.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_.E2.80.93_Western_Pacific">2009 – Western Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2009 – Western Pacific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>John C. Stennis</i> departed Bremerton for a 6-month deployment to the western Pacific on 13 January 2009. On 24 April, the ship arrived in Singapore. That same day, one of the ship's sailors was crushed and killed while working from a small harbor boat to secure a drain that discharges oily water from the aircraft catapults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 April, the ship's executive officer, Commander David L. Burnham, was relieved by Rear Admiral Mark A. Vance over unspecified personal conduct. Burnham was reassigned to a base in San Diego, pending an investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>After participating in exercises with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Maritime_Self_Defense_Force" title="Japan Maritime Self Defense Force">Japan Maritime Self Defense Force</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Korea" title="Republic of Korea">Republic of Korea</a>, as well as joint exercise Northern Edge 2009, <i>John C. Stennis</i> returned from deployment in early July 2009. Carrier Air Wing 9 debarked on 6 July at NAS North Island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVW9_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CVW9-13">[13]</a></sup> prior to the ship's arrival at her homeport of Bremerton on 10 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 30 March 2011, a VMFAT-101 F/A-18C Hornet suffered an uncontained catastrophic engine failure, exploded and caught fire just before launch from <i>John C. Stennis</i> about 100 miles (160 km) off the coast of San Diego during launch and recovery training operations. The aircraft was at full power, in tension on the catapult when the accident occurred. Eleven flight deck crewmen were injured while the pilot was unhurt. There was no major damage to the carrier but the aircraft was a total loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 December 2011, the final command-and-control mission for U.S. forces over Iraq was flown by an E-2C Hawkeye (<i>pictured</i>) from <a href="/wiki/VAW-112" title="VAW-112">Carrier Airborne Early Warning Squadron 112</a> (VAW-112), catapulting off the carrier <i>John C. Stennis</i> at 7:32 am and returning at 11:04 a.m, both local time. This mission effectively ended U.S. naval support for <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Operation New Dawn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012">2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 3 January 2012, Iranian General Ataollah Salehi warned <i>John C. Stennis</i> "not to return to the Persian Gulf."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The United States dismissed the warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 January, <i>John C. Stennis</i> led the rescue of an Iranian-flagged fishing vessel, <i>Al Mulahi,</i> following its seizure by pirates. The pirates ambushed the ship and Iranian flag to search for other ships to hijack, while holding the original crew hostage. When some of the pirates attempted to board a Bahamian-flagged cargo ship, <i>Sunshine</i>, it radioed for assistance. <i>John C. Stennis</i> dispatched a helicopter and cruiser to assist. A boarding party captured the pirates who attacked <i>Sunshine</i>, fed them, then released them temporarily. A helicopter then secretly followed the pirates back to their mother ship, <i>Al Mulahi</i>. Crew from the destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Kidd_(DDG-100)" title="USS Kidd (DDG-100)">USS <i>Kidd</i></a> then boarded the fishing vessel (upon permission in Urdu from the captain), and arrested all of the pirates with no casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2 March 2012, <i>John C Stennis</i> returned home from its 7-month deployment to homeport Bremerton, Washington.</p>
<p>On 7 July 2012, crew members were informed that <i>John C. Stennis</i> would be returning to the Middle East in August, much sooner than expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 August 2012, <i>John C. Stennis</i> departed to the Middle East originally for six months, but was extended to eight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 1 April 2013, the ship arrived at <a href="/wiki/Changi_Naval_Base" title="Changi Naval Base">Changi Naval Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>. Local <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Technical_Education" title="Institute of Technical Education">ITE</a> students were invited for a guided tour inside the aircraft carrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following that the ship sailed to Pearl Harbor, where it performed a week long tiger cruise to San Diego <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 12:45 on 3 May 2013, <i>John C. Stennis</i> arrived at its home port of <a href="/wiki/Naval_Base_Kitsap" title="Naval Base Kitsap">Naval Base Kitsap</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bremerton,_Washington" title="Bremerton, Washington">Bremerton, Washington</a>, the completion of an eight-month, 66,000 miles (106,000 km) deployment to the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. During this deployment, squadron aircraft flew more than 1,300 sorties from the carrier's deck in the war in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015">2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In mid-January 2015, <i>John C. Stennis</i> departed its home port of <a href="/wiki/Naval_Base_Kitsap" title="Naval Base Kitsap">Naval Base Kitsap</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bremerton,_Washington" title="Bremerton, Washington">Bremerton, Washington</a>, and arrived at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Magazine_Indian_Island" title="Naval Magazine Indian Island">Naval Magazine Indian Island</a> to load munitions prior to departing for <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> to receive aircraft and another 2,000 sailors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On 1 September, the carrier arrived back at Bremerton, Washington.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_John_C._Stennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 January 2016, <i>John C. Stennis</i> left <a href="/wiki/Naval_Base_Kitsap" title="Naval Base Kitsap">Naval Base Kitsap</a> for a scheduled deployment to the Western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On 19 April she arrived to Singapore for a regularly scheduled port visit after completing an annual bilateral training exercise in the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On 26 April 2016, China denied <i>John C. Stennis</i> permission to make a port visit to <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> as well as its escort ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 10 August, the carrier arrived back at San Diego, California.</p>

<p>The <i>John C. Stennis</i> strike group (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Strike_Group_Three" title="Carrier Strike Group Three">Carrier Strike Group Three</a>) is equipped and trained to work as a forward deployed force providing a deterrent force as well as serving to protect U.S. interests abroad.</p>
<p>USS <i>John C. Stennis</i> is the flagship of the strike group, and hosts the group's air wing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_Air_Wing_9" title="Carrier Air Wing 9">Carrier Air Wing 9</a>. <i>John C. Stennis</i> is also home to the commander of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destroyer_Squadron_21" title="Destroyer Squadron 21">Destroyer Squadron 21</a> (DESRON 21).</p>

<p><i>John C. Stennis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s seal was produced from the combined efforts of several crew members with historical help from Stennis Center for Public Service, <a href="/wiki/John_C._Stennis_Space_Center" title="John C. Stennis Space Center">John C. Stennis Space Center</a> and United States Senate Historian. The seal implies peace through strength, just as Senator <a href="/wiki/John_C._Stennis" title="John C. Stennis">John C. Stennis</a> was referred to as an "unwavering advocate of peace through strength" by President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, when the ship's name was announced in June 1988.</p>
<p>The circular shape signifies the <i>Nimitz</i>-class aircraft carrier's unique capability to circle the world without refueling while providing a forward presence from the sea. The predominant colors are red, white, blue and gold, the same as those of the United States and the Navy. The outer border, taken from one version of a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senate</a> crest, represents the strength through unity of the ship's crew. The four gold bands and eight ties denote John C. Stennis' four decades (41 years) in the Senate and the eight presidents he served with, from President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Truman</a> to President Reagan. The seven stars in the blue border represent his seven terms in the Senate and characterize <i>John C. Stennis</i> as the seventh <i>Nimitz</i>-class aircraft carrier. The red and white stripes inside the blue border represent the American flag and the American people <i>John C. Stennis</i> serves. They also honor the courage and sacrifice of the United States' armed forces.</p>
<p>The eagle and shield is a representation of the gilt eagle and shield overlooking the Old Senate Chamber. The shield represents the United States of America. The twenty stars represent the US's twentieth state, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, the home of John C. Stennis. The three arrows in the eagle's talons symbolize the ship's and air wing's ability to project power. The burst of light emanating from the shield, representative of the emergence of a new nation in the United States Senate Seal, portrays the birth of over 25 major <a href="/wiki/Naval_aviation" title="Naval aviation">Naval Aviation</a> programs under Senator Stennis' leadership, including all aircraft carriers from <a href="/wiki/USS_Forrestal_(CV-59)" title="USS Forrestal (CV-59)">USS <i>Forrestal</i></a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Harry_S._Truman_(CVN-75)" title="USS Harry S. Truman (CVN-75)">USS <i>Harry S. Truman</i></a>, and aircraft from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-4_Phantom" title="F-4 Phantom">F-4 Phantom</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F/A-18_Hornet" title="F/A-18 Hornet">F/A-18 Hornet</a>. The eagle is representative of John C. Stennis' stature in the Senate, where he was respected and admired as a "soaring eagle" by some of his colleagues.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The ship herself is pictured in the seal. On the edges of the flight deck are the words "Honor, Courage, Commitment" which are the United States Navy's Core Values.</p>
<p>The seal, after selection by the ship's crew, was submitted to Mrs. Margaret Stennis Womble, the ship's sponsor and daughter of Senator Stennis, and to Mrs. John Hampton Stennis, the matron of honor and wife of Senator Stennis' son, for their approval. In February 1995 they approved the design.</p>
