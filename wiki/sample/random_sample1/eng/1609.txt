<p><b>USS <i>Holland</i> (SS-1)</b> was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s first modern commissioned <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a>, although not the first military submarine of the United States, which was the 1775 <a href="/wiki/Turtle_(submersible)" title="Turtle (submersible)">submersible <i>Turtle</i></a>. The boat was originally laid down as <i><b>Holland VI</b></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Shipyard" title="Crescent Shipyard">Crescent Shipyard</a> of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth,_New_Jersey" title="Elizabeth, New Jersey">Elizabeth, New Jersey</a> for John Holland's Holland Torpedo Boat Company, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">launched</a> on 17 May 1897. She was acquired by the USN on 11 April 1900 and commissioned on 12 October 1900, Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/H._H._Caldwell" title="H. H. Caldwell">H. H. Caldwell</a> commanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-dates_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-dates-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Holland_(SS-1)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS-1_Holland_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="291" data-file-width="567" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/SS-1_Holland_diagram.png/220px-SS-1_Holland_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/SS-1_Holland_diagram.png/330px-SS-1_Holland_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/SS-1_Holland_diagram.png/440px-SS-1_Holland_diagram.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS-1_Holland_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rough sketch of <i>Holland</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Holland</i> was built at former Navy Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Nixon_(naval_architect)" title="Lewis Nixon (naval architect)">Lewis Nixon's</a> Crescent Shipyard of Elizabeth, New Jersey for John Holland's Holland Torpedo Boat Company, which became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_Boat" title="Electric Boat">Electric Boat</a> company in 1899.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The vessel was built under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Holland" title="John Philip Holland">John Philip Holland</a>, who designed the vessel and her details. <i>Holland</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keel</a> was laid at Nixon's Crescent Shipyard with both men present. The two men worked together using many of John Holland's proven concepts and patents to make the submarine a reality, each man complementing the other's contributions to the development of the modern submarine. Important contributions were also made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_L._Busch" title="Arthur L. Busch">Arthur L. Busch</a> (or Du Busc), Crescent's superintendent.</p>
<p><i>Holland VI</i> included many features that submarines of the early 20th century would exhibit, albeit in later, more advanced forms. There was a <a href="/wiki/Conning_tower" title="Conning tower">conning tower</a> from which the boat and her weapons could be directed. Also, she had all the necessary <a href="/wiki/Ballast_tank" title="Ballast tank">ballast</a> and trim tanks to make precise changes in depth and attitude underwater. Her crew was six men and maximum diving depth was 75 feet (23 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-specs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-specs-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>For armament, she had a reloadable 18 inch (457 mm) <a href="/wiki/Torpedo_tube" title="Torpedo tube">torpedo tube</a> with three <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedoes</a> and an 8.425-inch (214.0 mm) pneumatic <a href="/wiki/Dynamite_gun" title="Dynamite gun">dynamite gun</a> in the bow (the dynamite gun's projectiles were called "aerial torpedoes").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Register-5">[5]</a></sup> A second dynamite gun in the stern was removed in 1900 to make room for an improved engine exhaust, prior to delivery to the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>She had both an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> (specifically, a 4-stroke <a href="/wiki/Otto_engine" title="Otto engine">Otto gasoline engine</a> of 45 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower#Brake_horsepower" title="Horsepower">bhp</a> (34 kW)) for running on the surface and an <a href="/wiki/Electro-Dynamic_Company" title="Electro-Dynamic Company">Electro Dynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> of 50 shp (37 kW) for submerged operation, with one shaft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conways1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conways1-7">[7]</a></sup> A 66-<a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">cell</a> <a href="/wiki/Exide" title="Exide">Exide</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> powered the electric motor when submerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-specs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-specs-4">[4]</a></sup> This allowed speeds of 6 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (11 km/h; 6.9 mph) surfaced and 5.5 knots (10.2 km/h; 6.3 mph) submerged. Surfaced range was 200 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nmi</a> (370 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>; 230 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) at 6 kn (11 km/h; 6.9 mph), while submerged range was 30 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nmi</a> (56 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>; 35 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) at 5.5 kn (10.2 km/h; 6.3 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-specs_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-specs-4">[4]</a></sup> There is significant variation in references as to the vessel's horsepower and speed, for example the <i>Register of Ships of the U. S. Navy</i> gives horsepower figures of 45 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower#Brake_horsepower" title="Horsepower">bhp</a> (34 kW) surfaced and 75 shp (56 kW) submerged, with 8 knots (15 km/h; 9.2 mph) surfaced and 5 knots (9.3 km/h; 5.8 mph) submerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Register-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service">Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Holland_(SS-1)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Holland VI</i> eventually proved her validity and worthiness as a warship and was ultimately purchased by the U.S. government for the sum of $150,000 on 11 April 1900. She was considered to be the first truly successful craft of her type.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> The United States Government soon ordered more submarines from Holland's company, which were to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Plunger-class_submarine" title="Plunger-class submarine"><i>Plunger</i> class</a>. These became America's first fleet of underwater naval vessels.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Holland_(SS-1)_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="544" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg/220px-USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg/330px-USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg/440px-USS_Holland_%28SS-1%29_-_Scientific_American_1898.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>.</p>
<p><i>Holland VI</i> was modified after her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">christening</a>, and was renamed USS <i>Holland</i> (SS-1) when she was <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> by the US Navy on 12 October 1900, at <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, with Lieutenant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_H._Caldwell" title="Harry H. Caldwell">Harry H. Caldwell</a> in command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Holland</i> was the first commissioned submarine in the US Navy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-8">[8]</a></sup> and is the first of the unbroken line of submarines in the Navy. She was the third submarine to be owned by the Navy, however. (The first submarine was <i>Propeller</i> (also known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_submarine_Alligator_(1862)" title="American submarine Alligator (1862)">Alligator</a></i>) and the second was <i><a href="/wiki/Intelligent_Whale" title="Intelligent Whale">Intelligent Whale</a></i>.)</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holland_(SSl)._Starboard_bow,_on_ways,_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2337" data-file-width="3000" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Holland_%28SSl%29._Starboard_bow%2C_on_ways%2C_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Holland_(SSl)._Starboard_bow,_on_ways,_1900_-_NARA_-_512954.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Holland</i> under construction, 1900</div>
</div>

<p>On 16 October 1900, in order to be kept serviceable throughout the winter, <i>Holland</i> left Newport under tow of the tug <i>Leyden</i> for <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis, Maryland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-8">[8]</a></sup> where she was used to train <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midshipmen" title="Midshipmen">midshipmen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy</a>, as well as officers and enlisted men ordered there to receive training vital in preparing for the operation of other submarines being built for the Fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Holland</i> proved valuable for experimental purposes in collecting data for submarines under construction or contemplation. Her 166 mi (267 km) surface run, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a> from 8–10 January 1901, provided useful data on her performance underway over an extended period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Holland</i> (briefly in 1899, on trials)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and five <a href="/wiki/Plunger-class_submarine" title="Plunger-class submarine"><i>Plunger</i> class</a> Holland-type submarines were based in <a href="/wiki/New_Suffolk,_New_York" title="New Suffolk, New York">New Suffolk, New York</a> on the North Fork of <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> from 1899–1905, prompting the hamlet to claim to be the first submarine base in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Except for the period from 15 June to 1 October 1901, which was passed training cadets at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Torpedo_Station" title="Naval Torpedo Station">Naval Torpedo Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, <i>Holland</i> remained at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a> as a training submarine until 17 July 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Holland</i> finished her career at <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>. Her name was struck from the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Vessel_Register" title="Naval Vessel Register">Naval Vessel Register</a> on 21 November 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-dates_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-dates-1">[1]</a></sup> This revolutionary submarine was sold as scrap to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_G._Hitner" title="Joseph G. Hitner">Henry A. Hitner &amp; Sons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> on 18 June 1913 for $100. Her purchaser was required to put up $5,000 bond as assurance that the submarine would be broken up and not used as a ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-DANFS1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 1915 the hulk of the <i>Holland</i>, stripped of her external fittings, was sold to Peter J. Gibbons. As of October 1916 she was on display in Philadelphia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In May 1917 she was moved to the Bronx, New York as a featured attraction at the <a href="/wiki/Bronx_International_Exposition_of_Science,_Arts_and_Industries" title="Bronx International Exposition of Science, Arts and Industries">Bronx International Exposition of Science, Arts and Industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Holland</i> was on display for several years in <a href="/wiki/Paterson,_New_Jersey" title="Paterson, New Jersey">Paterson, New Jersey</a> until she was finally scrapped in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedmanSubs1-dates_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FriedmanSubs1-dates-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Holland_(SS-1)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The success of the submarine was instrumental in the founding of the Electric Boat Company, now known as the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">Electric Boat Division</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Corporation" title="General Dynamics Corporation">General Dynamics Corporation</a>. This company, therefore, can trace its origins to the formation of John Philip Holland's original company and the revolutionary submarines that were developed at this shipyard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
