<p>The <b>V-3</b> (<i><b>Vergeltungswaffe 3</b></i>) was a German <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/Supergun" title="Supergun">supergun</a> working on the multi-charge principle whereby secondary propellant charges are fired to add velocity to a projectile.</p>
<p>The weapon was planned to be used to bombard London from two large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunkers" title="Bunkers">bunkers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pas-de-Calais" title="Pas-de-Calais">Pas-de-Calais</a> region of northern France, but they were rendered unusable by Allied bombing raids before completion. Two similar guns were used to bombard <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> from December 1944 to February 1945.</p>
<p>The V-3 was also known as the <i>Hochdruckpumpe</i> ("High Pressure Pump," HDP for short), which was a <a href="/wiki/Code_name" title="Code name">code name</a> intended to hide the real purpose of the project. It was also known as <i>Fleißiges Lieschen</i> ("Busy Lizzie").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nat-military-museum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nat-military-museum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The gun used multiple propellant charges placed along the barrel's length and timed to fire as soon as the projectile passed them in order to provide an additional boost. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">Solid-fuel rocket</a> boosters were used instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a> charges because of their greater suitability and ease of use. These were arranged in symmetrical pairs along the length of the barrel, angled to project their thrust against the base of the projectile as it passed. This layout spawned the German codename <i>Tausendfüßler</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipede</a>"). The barrel and side chambers were designed as identical sections to simplify production and allow damaged sections to be replaced. The entire gun would use multiple such sections bolted together. The <a href="/wiki/Smoothbore" title="Smoothbore">smoothbore</a> gun fired a fin-stabilized shell that depended upon aerodynamic forces rather than <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopic</a> forces to prevent tumbling (distinct from conventional <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifled</a> weapons which cause the projectile to spin); this resulted in a lower <a href="/wiki/Drag_coefficient" title="Drag coefficient">drag coefficient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the multi-chamber gun dates back to the 19th century. In 1857, U.S. arms expert <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Azel_Storrs_Lyman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Azel Storrs Lyman (page does not exist)">Azel Storrs Lyman</a> (1815–1885) was granted a patent on <i>"Improvement in accelerating fire-arms"</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and he built a prototype in 1860 which proved to be unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pal03_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pal03-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lyman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lyman-8">[8]</a></sup> Lyman then modified the design in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/James_Richard_Haskell" title="James Richard Haskell">James Richard Haskell</a>, who had been working for years on the same principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harkel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-harkel-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haskell and Lyman reasoned that subsidiary propellant charges could increase the muzzle velocity of a projectile if the charges were spaced at intervals along the barrel of a gun in side chambers and ignited an instant after a shell had passed them. The "Lyman-Haskell multi-charge gun" was constructed on the instructions of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_of_Ordnance" title="Chief of Ordnance">Chief of Ordnance</a>, but it did not resemble a conventional artillery piece. The barrel was so long that it had to be placed on an inclined ramp, and it had pairs of chambers angled back at 45 degrees discharging into it. It was test fired at the Frankford Arsenal at <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> in 1880 and was unsuccessful. The flash from the original propellant charge bypassed the projectile due to faulty <a href="/wiki/Obturation" title="Obturation">obturation</a> and prematurely ignited the subsidiary charges before the shell passed them, slowing the shell down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The best velocity that could be obtained from it was 335 metres per second (1,100 ft/s), inferior to the performance of a conventional <a href="/wiki/RBL_7_inch_Armstrong_gun" title="RBL 7 inch Armstrong gun">RBL 7 inch Armstrong gun</a> of the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> New prototypes of multi-charge guns were built and tested,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> but Lyman and Haskell abandoned the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lyman_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-lyman-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the same period, French engineer <a href="/wiki/Louis-Guillaume_Perreaux" title="Louis-Guillaume Perreaux">Louis-Guillaume Perreaux</a>, one of the pioneers of the <a href="/wiki/Michaux-Perreaux_steam_velocipede" title="Michaux-Perreaux steam velocipede">motorcycle</a>, had been working on a similar project since before 1860.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Perreaux was granted a patent in 1864 for a multi-chamber gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1878, Perreaux presented his invention at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1878)" title="Exposition Universelle (1878)">World Exhibition of Paris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1918, the French Army made plans for a very long range multi-chamber gun in response to the German <a href="/wiki/Paris_Gun" title="Paris Gun">Paris Gun</a>. The Paris Gun was built by <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Friedrich Krupp AG</a> and could bombard <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> from German lines over a distance of no less than 125 kilometres (78 mi). However, the French initiative did not reach the prototype stage, as it was discontinued when the retreat of the German armies and the armistice put an end to the bombardment. The plans for the multi-chamber gun were archived, as they had been envisioned to counter the German fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>France collapsed in June 1940 at the beginning of World War II, when the German troops acquired the plans of this long-range gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pal03_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pal03-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1942, this patent attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/August_Coenders" title="August Coenders">August Cönders</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[notes 1]</a></sup> developer of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6chling_shell" title="Röchling shell">Röchling shell</a> and chief engineer of the plants "Röchling Stahlwerk AG" in <a href="/wiki/Wetzlar" title="Wetzlar">Wetzlar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Cönders thought that the gradual acceleration of the shell by a series of small charges spread over the length of the barrel might be the solution to the problem of designing very long range guns. The very strong explosive charge needed to project shells at a high speed were causing very rapid degradation of the gun tubes of conventional guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[notes 2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cönders proposed the use of electrically activated charges to eliminate the problem of the premature ignition of the subsidiary charges, the problem experienced by the Lyman-Haskell gun. Cönders built a prototype of a 20 mm multi-chamber gun using machinery readily available at the Wetzlar plant, machinery that was producing tubes of this caliber for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_cm_FlaK_30/38/Flakvierling" title="2 cm FlaK 30/38/Flakvierling">Flak 38 anti-aircraft guns of 20 mm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pal03_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pal03-7">[7]</a></sup> The first tests were encouraging, but to get the support of the Ministry of arms, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_R%C3%B6chling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hermann Röchling (page does not exist)">Hermann Röchling</a> had to present to <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a> Cönders' project of a cannon capable of firing on London from the coast of the Pas-de-Calais. The project intended to use two batteries to crush London under a barrage of hundreds of shells per hour, shells of 140 kilograms (310 lb) with an explosive charge of 25 kilograms (55 lb).</p>
<p>Cönders constructed a full-calibre gun at the <a href="/wiki/Hillersleben" title="Hillersleben">Hillersleben</a> proving ground near <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> but, by the end of 1943, he had encountered severe problems both in putting the gun's basic principle into operation and in producing a feasible design for the shells that it was to fire. Even when everything worked, the muzzle velocity was just over 1,000 metres per second (3,300 ft/s), which was nowhere near what had been promised. Nonetheless, a proposal was made to build a single full-sized gun with a 150-metre (490 ft) barrel at <a href="/wiki/Mi%C4%99dzyzdroje" title="Międzyzdroje">Misdroy</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Wolin" title="Wolin">Wolin</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenemünde">Peenemünde</a>, while construction went ahead at the Mimoyecques site in France (which had already been attacked by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAAF" title="USAAF">USAAF</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAF" title="RAF">RAF</a>). The <i><a href="/wiki/Waffenamt" title="Waffenamt">Heereswaffenamt</a></i> (Weapon Procurement Office) took control of the project by March 1944, with no good news from Misdroy, and Cönders became one of the engineers working on the three chief problems: projectile design, obturation, and ignition of the secondary charges.</p>
<p>Six different companies produced satisfactory designs for projectiles, including <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Skoda</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obturate" title="Obturate">Obturation</a> problems were solved by placing a sealing <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a> between the projectile and the initial propellant charge, which in turn prevented the flash from the charge from getting ahead of the projectile and solved the problem of controlling the initiation of the secondary charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-wood-25">[23]</a></sup> By the end of May 1944, there were four designs for the 150-mm finned projectile, one manufactured by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fasterstoff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fasterstoff (page does not exist)">Fasterstoff</a> (designed by Füstenberg) and three others by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R%C3%B6chling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Röchling (page does not exist)">Röchling</a> (Cönders), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bochumer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bochumer (page does not exist)">Bochumer</a> (Verein-Haack), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Witkowitz_Ironworks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Witkowitz Ironworks (page does not exist)">Witkowitz</a> (Athem).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trials were held at Misdroy from 20–24 May 1944 with ranges of up to 88 km (55 mi) being attained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> On 4 July 1944, the Misdroy gun was test-fired with 8 rounds (one of the 1.8 m (5.9 ft) long shells travelled 93 km (58 mi)). The gun burst during the testing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> putting an end to the tests.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimoyecques_site">Mimoyecques site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mimoyecques site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fortress_of_Mimoyecques" title="Fortress of Mimoyecques">Fortress of Mimoyecques</a></div>
<p>Major Bock of Festung Pioneer-Stab 27 (the fortification regiment of LVII Corps, <a href="/wiki/15th_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="15th Army (Wehrmacht)">Fifteenth Army</a>, at the time based in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dieppe,_Seine-Maritime" title="Dieppe, Seine-Maritime">Dieppe area</a>) was given the task of finding a suitable site for the HDP batteries following Hitler's decision that HDP guns should be sited in northern France to bombard London. A study in early 1943 concluded that a hill with a rock core would be most suitable, as the gun tubes could be placed in <a href="/wiki/Drift_mining" title="Drift mining">drifts</a> (inclined tunnels) and support equipment and supplies located in adjacent tunnels. The guns would not be movable and would be permanently aimed at London.</p>
<p>A suitable site was selected at a <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> hill about 5 kilometres (3.1 mi) north of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hidrequent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hidrequent (page does not exist)">Hidrequent</a> quarries, near Mimoyecques in the <a href="/wiki/Pas-de-Calais" title="Pas-de-Calais">Pas-de-Calais</a> region of northern France behind <a href="/wiki/Cap_Gris_Nez" title="Cap Gris Nez">Cap Gris Nez</a>, very close to the French end of the present day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Channel_tunnel" title="Channel tunnel">Channel tunnel</a>, where <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a> launch sites were already under construction. The site was 8 kilometres (5.0 mi) from the sea and 165 kilometres (103 mi) from London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcdl_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-lcdl-29">[27]</a></sup> It was code-named <i>Wiese</i> (meadow) and <i>Bauvorhaben 711</i> (Construction Project 711), and <a href="/wiki/Organisation_Todt" title="Organisation Todt">Organisation Todt</a> began construction in September 1943 with the building of railway lines to support the work, and began to excavate the gun shafts in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zaloga_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-zaloga-30">[28]</a></sup> The initial layout comprised two parallel facilities approximately 1,000 metres (3,300 ft) apart, each with five drifts which were to hold a stacked cluster of five HDP gun tubes, for a total of 50 guns. Both facilities were served by an underground railway tunnel and underground ammunition storage galleries.</p>
<p>The eastern complex consisted of five drifts angled at 50 degrees reaching 105 metres (344 ft) below the hilltop. The five drifts exited the hilltop through a concrete slab 30 metres (98 ft) wide and 5.5 metres (18 ft) thick. Large steel plates protected the five openings and each drift had a special armoured door. Extensive tunnels and elevator shafts supported the guns and, if the site had become operational, about 1,000 troops from Artillerie Abteilung 705 and supporting units would have been deployed at Mimoyecques. Artillerie Abteilung 705 had been organised in January 1944 under Oberstleutnant Georg Borttscheller to operate the Wiese gun complex.</p>
<p>The plans were to have the first battery of five gun tubes ready for March 1944, and the full complex of 25 gun tubes by 1 October 1944. A failure occurred, however, at the Misdroy proving ground in April 1944 after only 25 rounds had been fired and, as a result, the project was further cut back from five drifts to three, even though work had begun on some of the other drifts. The site was finally put out of commission on 6 July 1944, when bombers of <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a>'s <a href="/wiki/No._617_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 617 Squadron RAF">617 Squadron</a> (the famous "Dambusters") attacked using 5,400-kilogram (11,900 lb) "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tallboy_bomb" title="Tallboy bomb">Tallboy</a>" deep-penetration bombs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Luxembourg_bombardment">Luxembourg bombardment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Luxembourg bombardment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The project eventually came under the control of the SS, and SS General <a href="/wiki/Hans_Kammler" title="Hans Kammler">Hans Kammler</a> ordered it to be ready for action in late 1944, assisted by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Dornberger" title="Walter Dornberger">Walter Dornberger</a>. A battery was constructed of two shorter guns approximately 50 metres (160 ft) long with 12 side-chambers, and it was placed in the hands of the army artillery unit Artillerie Abteilung 705 under the command of Hauptmann (Captain) Patzig. These were sited in a wooded ravine of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruwer_River" title="Ruwer River">Ruwer River</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lampaden" title="Lampaden">Lampaden</a> about 13 kilometres (8.1 mi) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luxem_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-luxem-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two guns were aimed west, resting on 13 steel support structures on solid wooden bases on a 34 degree slope. The city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(city)" title="Luxembourg (city)">Luxembourg</a> (which had been liberated in September 1944) was at a range of about 43 kilometres (27 mi) and was designated Target No. 305. Concrete <a href="/wiki/Blockhouse" title="Blockhouse">blockhouses</a> were constructed between the two gun tubes, as well as ten smaller bunkers to hold projectiles and propellant charges.</p>
<p>The assembly and mounting of the Lampaden guns coincided with the final preparations for the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a>. However, the supply of ammunition became problematic due to the state of the German railway network. Time had become critical, and it was decided to use a 150-millimetre (5.9 in) finned projectile with a discarding <a href="/wiki/Sabot" title="Sabot">sabot</a>, weighing 95 kilograms (209 lb) and carrying a 7–9 kg (15–20 lb) explosive charge. The propellant comprised a 5 kg (11 lb) main charge and 24 subsidiary charges for a total of 73 kg (161 lb).</p>
<p>By the time that the Ardennes offensive began on 16 December 1944, Kammler received orders from <a href="/wiki/OB_West" title="OB West">OB West</a> (German Army Command in the West) to begin firing at the end of the month, and the first gun tube was ready for action on 30 December 1944. Two warm-up rounds were initially fired, followed by five high-explosive shells which were fired in sequence, attended by Kammler. The muzzle velocity was approximately 935 metres per second (3,070 ft/s).</p>
<p>The second gun tube was brought into operation on 11 January 1945 and some 183 rounds in total were fired until 22 February 1945, with 44 confirmed hits in the urban area. The guns were not particularly effective; from the 142 rounds that struck Luxembourg, total casualties were 10 dead and 35 wounded.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate">Fate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the two Lampaden guns was dismantled on 15 February,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> and firing ceased on 22 February, when <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">US Army</a> units had advanced to within 3 kilometres (1.9 mi) of the Lampaden site.</p>
<p>A second battery of guns began to be deployed in January 1945 at <a href="/wiki/Buhl,_Haut-Rhin" title="Buhl, Haut-Rhin">Buhl</a>, aimed at <a href="/wiki/Belfort" title="Belfort">Belfort</a> in support of the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Nordwind" title="Operation Nordwind">Operation Nordwind</a> offensive. One gun was erected before the failure of the Nordwind offensive put the site at risk, and the equipment was removed before firing could begin.</p>
<p>There were other proposals to deploy batteries to bombard <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and other cities but these were not implemented due to the poor state of the German railway network and a lack of ammunition. All four HDP guns were eventually abandoned at the Röchling works in <a href="/wiki/Wetzlar" title="Wetzlar">Wetzlar</a> and Artillerie Abteilung 705 was re-equipped with conventional artillery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zaloga_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-zaloga-30">[28]</a></sup> The disassembled gun tubes, spare parts, and remaining ammunition were later captured by the US Army and shipped to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States</a> where they were tested and evaluated at the <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Proving_Ground" title="Aberdeen Proving Ground">Aberdeen Proving Ground</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, and finally scrapped there in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luxem_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-luxem-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="V-3_museum">V-3 museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: V-3 museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mimoyecques museum allows visitors to view the galleries (in various stages of construction and bombing damage), remains of the guns, a small scale V-3 replica, and examples of machinery, rail systems and tools employed. The site also contains memorials to the slave laborers who were employed by the Nazis to construct it<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basev3_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-basev3-33">[31]</a></sup> and to the airmen killed in action during the destruction of the base.</p>
<p>The Międzyzdroje site also has a museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bunkierv3_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-bunkierv3-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_model_of_V-3_gun">Scale model of V-3 gun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-3_cannon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scale model of V-3 gun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dr. Hugh Hunt of the Cambridge University, together with explosives engineer Charlie Adcock created a working scale model of the V-3 gun and was able to prove the ignition of the propellants was done by the advancing gas behind the projectile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
