<p><b>VIA</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <i>ВИА</i>) is an abbreviation for Vocal-[Music] Instrumental-Ensemble (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Вокально-инструментальный ансамбль</span>, <i>Vokalno-instrumentalny ansambl</i>). It is the general name used for <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> bands that were formally recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> government from the 1960s to the 1980s.</p>
<p>In Soviet times, the term <i>VIA</i> generally meant <i>band</i>, but it is now used in Russia to refer specifically to pop, rock, and folk groups active during the Soviet period.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VIA_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term VIA appeared in the Soviet Union in the 1960s and represented a model under which the Soviet government was willing to permit domestic rock and pop music acts to develop. To break through to the state-owned Soviet media, a band needed to become an officially-recognized VIA. Each VIA had an artistic director (художественный руководитель) who served as manager, producer, and state-appointed censor. In some bands (such as Pesniary) the artistic director was the band's leading member and songwriter, while in others he played the role of impresario.</p>
<p>Soviet VIAs played a specific style of pop music. They performed youth-oriented (but officially approved) radio-friendly music, which combined contemporary Western and Soviet trends. Folk instruments were often used, and occasionally a <a href="/wiki/Keytar" title="Keytar">keytar</a> (a keyboard held like a guitar). Songs varied from pop ballads, dance-beat disco and new wave to mainstream rock (although bands avoided the "rock music" label until the late 1970s, because rock was considered "bourgeois" and formally banned). Many VIAs had up to ten members (including a number of vocalists and multi-instrumentalists), who were in frequent rotation.</p>
<p>Due to state censorship, the lyrics of VIAs were family-friendly; typical topics were universal emotions like love, joy, and nostalgia, or idealized vignettes from daily life. Many bands also encouraged national culture and patriotism, (especially those of national minorities from the smaller Soviet republics) such as <a href="/wiki/Yalla_(band)" title="Yalla (band)">Yalla</a> from <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, Labyrinth from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chervona_Ruta_(ensemble)" title="Chervona Ruta (ensemble)">Chervona Ruta</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Folk-based VIAs such as <a href="/wiki/Pesniary" title="Pesniary">Pesniary</a>, Siabry and <a href="/wiki/Verasy" title="Verasy">Verasy</a> were especially popular in Belarus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Russian bands from Moscow and Leningrad (such as <a href="/wiki/Zemlyane" title="Zemlyane">Zemlyane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsvety" title="Tsvety">Tsvety</a>) were more oriented towards Western pop and rock music.</p>
<p>Many VIAs were created by musicians that played together in local choruses or musical theatrical productions. The earliest VIAs included <a href="/wiki/Avangard" title="Avangard">Avangard</a> (Avantgarde) in 1964, <a href="/wiki/Poyushchiye_Gitary" title="Poyushchiye Gitary">Poyushchiye Gitary</a> (Singing Guitars) in 1966, <a href="/wiki/Vesyolye_Rebyata" title="Vesyolye Rebyata">Vesyolye Rebyata</a> (Jolly Fellows) in 1966, and Dobry Molodtsy (Good People) in 1969.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unique_Characteristics">Unique Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VIA_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unique Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The typical VIA consisted of 6 to 10 band members, with several singers and musicians capable of playing multiple instruments. Lead vocalists in VIAs usually did not play an instrument, but only sang. Virtually every member of a VIA was a professional musician with formal musical education and many years of performance experience.</p>
<p>The Soviet government had strict rules governing how members of a VIA were to behave on stage and conduct themselves in public. Performers were only allowed to wear suits, folks costumes, or military uniforms. Movement around the stage was discouraged. Musicians typically remained in one place as they played their instruments or sang. Anything outside of the conservative "norm", such as long hair, tattoos, leather jackets, or metallic accessories were forbidden.</p>
<p>VIA song recordings were done by the State owned record company <a href="/wiki/Melodiya" title="Melodiya">Melodiya</a> (Melody). Concerts and performances were organized by professional associations such as Soyuzkontsert (Union Concerts), Moskontsert (Moscow Concerts), Lenkonsert (Leningrad Concerts), Roskontsert (Russian Concerts), and Goskontsert (Government Concerts), along with regional orchestra groups.</p>
<p>At times, a VIA would collaborate with a well-known solo singer to provide musical backing. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Antonov_(musician)" title="Yuri Antonov (musician)">Yury Antonov</a> and VIAs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Araks" title="Araks">Araks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerobus" title="Aerobus">Aerobus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alla_Pugacheva" title="Alla Pugacheva">Alla Pugacheva</a> and VIA <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Retsital&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Retsital (page does not exist)">Retsital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Rotaru" title="Sofia Rotaru">Sofia Rotaru</a> and VIA <a href="/wiki/Chervona_Ruta" title="Chervona Ruta">Chervona Ruta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valeriy_Obodzinskiy" title="Valeriy Obodzinskiy">Valeriy Obodzinskiy</a> and VIA <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Verniye_Druziya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Verniye Druziya (page does not exist)">Verniye Druziya</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lev_Leschenko" title="Lev Leschenko">Lev Leschenko</a> and VIA <a href="/wiki/Spektr" title="Spektr">Spektr</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Repertoire">Repertoire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VIA_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Repertoire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>VIAs typically performed songs written by professional composers and lyricists who were members in good standing of the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Composers" title="Union of Soviet Composers">Composers' Union</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writers%27_Union_of_the_U.S.S.R." title="Writers' Union of the U.S.S.R.">Writers' Union</a> in the former Soviet Union. Some songs were created by VIA members or the VIA's artistic director. In the early 1980s, there was an unspoken rule that at least 80% of a VIA's performance repertoire had to be songs written by union members.</p>
<p>VIAs performed songs in a range of musical styles, including folk music, disco, rock, and synth-pop. Although tightly controlled by the government, VIAs had an enormous influence on the Russian public and created an audience for the rock music wave that followed. On the other hand, VIAs tended to deliver songs that were mainstream, simple in expression, and conservative in performance. Lyrics tended to focus on uncontroversial topics such as patriotic motifs, love stories, idealizations of work, light humor, ballads, current events, and folk themes. Any social criticism or protest was heavily censored and largely forbidden, except when directed against the West.</p>
<p>A number of VIAs did cover versions of foreign hits, typically changing the lyrics to entirely different meanings, but matching the music and vocalization. At times, VIAs went so far as to claim that the music was their own original work.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_and_Fall">Rise and Fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VIA_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rise and Fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>VIAs as a movement in Soviet music existed for approximately two decades, from the mid-1960s until the mid-1980s. They were very popular, particularly among Soviet youth. The best known VIAs would perform year round, giving several concerts a day. Many of these concerts received television and radio air time. Their albums sold tens of millions of copies.</p>
<p>Most VIAs dissolved in the late 1980s. This was in part due to rock music becoming more popular and the censorship apparatus largely falling apart. The advent of synthesizers, samplers, and other technologies that enabled the creation of music without the use of a large number of musicians was also a contributing factor. Finally, the lyrical content of VIA songs became far less relevant in the new, more open society.</p>
<p>Although most VIAs disappeared, a number of VIA members enjoyed successful subsequent careers as solo performers or members of new bands. Many Russian pop and rock stars of the 1990s were VIA members in the 1970s and 1980s. A few of the most popular VIAs endured and continued to exist into the 1990s and thereafter, usually with a significantly different membership.</p>
