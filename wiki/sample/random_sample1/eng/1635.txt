<p><b>WASP-18b</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> that is notable for having an orbital period of less than one day. It has a mass equal to 10 <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass">Jupiter masses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hellier2009_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hellier2009-4">[4]</a></sup> just below the boundary line between planets and <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarfs</a>, about 13 Jupiter masses. Due to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">tidal deceleration</a>, it is expected to spiral towards and eventually merge with its host star, <a href="/wiki/WASP-18" title="WASP-18">WASP-18</a>, in less than a million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hellier2009_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hellier2009-4">[4]</a></sup> The planet is approximately 3.1 million kilometres (1.9 million miles) from its star, which is about 325 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from Earth. It was discovered by a team led by Coel Hellier, a professor of astrophysics at <a href="/wiki/Keele_University" title="Keele University">Keele University</a> in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hellier2009_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hellier2009-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists at Keele and at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a> are working to understand whether the discovery of this planet so shortly before its expected demise (with less than 0.1% of its lifetime remaining) was fortuitous, or whether tidal dissipation by WASP-18 is actually much less efficient than astrophysicists typically assume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hellier2009_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hellier2009-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamilton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton-5">[5]</a></sup> Observations made over the next decade should yield a measurement of the rate at which WASP-18b's orbit is decaying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The closest example of a similar situation in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>' moon <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a>. Phobos orbits Mars at a distance of only about 9,000 km (5,600 mi), 40 times closer than the Moon is to the Earth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and is expected to be destroyed in about eleven million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
