<p><b>WNJU</b>, <a href="/wiki/Virtual_channel" title="Virtual channel">channel</a> 47, is an <a href="/wiki/Owned-and-operated_station" title="Owned-and-operated station">owned-and-operated</a> station of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish language</a> <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a> television network, <a href="/wiki/City_of_license" title="City of license">licensed</a> to <a href="/wiki/Linden,_New_Jersey" title="Linden, New Jersey">Linden, New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a> and serving the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> television market. WNJU is owned by the <a href="/wiki/NBC_Owned_Television_Stations" title="NBC Owned Television Stations">NBCUniversal Owned Television Stations</a> subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a>, as part of a <a href="/wiki/Duopoly_(broadcasting)" title="Duopoly (broadcasting)">duopoly</a> with <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s flagship station <a href="/wiki/WNBC" title="WNBC">WNBC</a> (channel 4). WNJU maintains studios and offices in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lee,_New_Jersey" title="Fort Lee, New Jersey">Fort Lee, New Jersey</a>, and transmitter located atop the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WNJU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WNJU-TV signed on the air on May 16, 1965 as the first commercial <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> station in the New York City/New Jersey television market. The station originally broadcast from the Mosque Theater (now Symphony Hall), located at 1020 Broad Street in Newark, in the former studios of WATV (channel 13; later WNTA, now <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>). The station was owned by Henry Becton (son of Maxwell Becton, co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Becton_Dickinson" title="Becton Dickinson">Becton Dickinson</a>) and Fairleigh S. Dickinson Jr. (son of Fairleigh S. Dickinson Sr. the founder of <a href="/wiki/Fairleigh_Dickinson_University" title="Fairleigh Dickinson University">Fairleigh Dickinson University</a> and also the co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Becton_Dickinson" title="Becton Dickinson">Becton Dickinson</a>. The general manager during WNJU's early years was pioneering UHF broadcaster Edwin Cooperstein. The station's initial schedule featured a mix of English, Asian, Spanish and Italian shows. During the mid-1960s, the station broadcast a live and locally produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teenager" title="Teenager">teenage</a> dance show called <a class="external text" href="http://tv.groups.yahoo.com/group/Discoteen/" rel="nofollow"><i>Disc-O-Teen</i></a>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/John_Zacherle" title="John Zacherle">John Zacherle</a>; and a <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> program, <i><a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Quest" title="Rainbow Quest">Rainbow Quest</a></i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>. WNJU was involved in some controversy when it aired bullfights, which some critics believed were too violent. The station was not profitable due to the lack of awareness of UHF stations in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_metropolitan_area" title="New York metropolitan area">New York metropolitan area</a>. The market had seven VHF stations, six of which were commercial, at a time when most cities had an average of three commercial stations. WNJU already had two strikes against it, and served minority audiences with mostly brokered programming.</p>
<p>WNJU was sold in the fall of 1970 for $8 million (a fairly high price for a UHF station back in 1970) to <a href="/wiki/Screen_Gems" title="Screen Gems">Screen Gems Broadcasting</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>. It was thought that WNJU would now become competitive because Screen Gems had deep pockets, but the brokered ethnic format would ultimately continue. It maintained an English-speaking audience a few hours a week during the 1970s when it was the only New York broadcast outlet for the <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">World Wide Wrestling Federation</a>. The station used a logo with <a href="/wiki/WAPA-TV" title="WAPA-TV">WAPA-TV</a>'s "Open 4", as it was WAPA-TV's sister station at the time.</p>
<p>By the late 1970s, WNJU had evolved into mostly Spanish programming, along with some ethnic brokered programs that aired on weekends. During the week, WNJU ran English-speaking religious programming until Noon. From 12:00 p.m. onward, the station ran Spanish programming. On Sundays, the station also aired English-language religious programs in the morning. WNJU was sold in 1979 to a consortium led by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Perenchio" title="Jerry Perenchio">Jerry Perenchio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bud_Yorkin" title="Bud Yorkin">Bud Yorkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Lear" title="Norman Lear">Norman Lear</a>. By the early 1980s, much of the other brokered foreign language programming disappeared, with WNJU airing English language religious programming in the morning and Spanish programming the rest of the day. Some brokered programs, including Greek (and Italian in the early-mid 1970s), aired on Sunday afternoons into the early 1990s.</p>
<p>In 1984, WNJU joined with two Spanish language television stations that were not affiliated with the Spanish International Network (now <a href="/wiki/Univision" title="Univision">Univision</a>) and formed NetSpan, the United States' second Spanish-language television network. NetSpan's original group of affiliates included WNJU, <a href="/wiki/KSTS" title="KSTS">KSTS</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/WXFT-DT" title="WXFT-DT">WBBS</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> (which aired its programming in the evenings and late nights only); the latter two stations were locally owned. In 1985, <a href="/wiki/KVEA" title="KVEA">KVEA</a> in <a href="/wiki/Corona,_California" title="Corona, California">Corona, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/WSCV" title="WSCV">WSCV</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale, Florida</a> (both of which were owned by Blair Broadcasting), and locally owned <a href="/wiki/WCIU-TV" title="WCIU-TV">WCIU-TV</a> in Chicago (which aired NetSpan programming after 5 p.m. only) joined NetSpan. The network acquired WNJU and the other stations, except for WCIU, outright in 1986.</p>

<p>In 1987, NetSpan added more affiliates, and changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a>. In Chicago, <a href="/wiki/WSNS-TV" title="WSNS-TV">WSNS-TV</a> dropped its <a href="/wiki/Univision" title="Univision">Univision</a> affiliation and joined Telemundo, with WCIU carrying Univision programming after 5 p.m. In the early 1990s, WNJU dropped its English-language religious shows and became a full-time Telemundo station.</p>
<p>In 1989, the station moved its operations to 39 Industrial Avenue in <a href="/wiki/Teterboro,_New_Jersey" title="Teterboro, New Jersey">Teterboro</a>. In 2001, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> (then-owner of <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>) purchased Telemundo. WNJU witnessed major overhauls, adopting similar opening graphics to those used at New York City's <a href="/wiki/WNBC" title="WNBC">WNBC</a>, and adopting a tweaked version of its opening music sequence. In 2003, WNJU relocated to the Sixth Floor at 2200 Fletcher Avenue in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lee,_New_Jersey" title="Fort Lee, New Jersey">Fort Lee</a>, occupying the former studios and offices of the NBC-owned <a href="/wiki/CNBC" title="CNBC">CNBC</a> cable network, which around the same time moved to a state-of-the-art new studio complex at 900 Sylvan Avenue (Route 9W) in <a href="/wiki/Englewood_Cliffs,_New_Jersey" title="Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey">Englewood Cliffs</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009, WNJU debuted the morning magazine program <i>Las Comadres con Gloria B</i>, which became the #1 program in the market in its timeslot, garnering 1.5 million viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analog-to-digital_conversion">Analog-to-digital conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WNJU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analog-to-digital conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>WNJU discontinued regular programming on its analog signal, over <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> channel 47, on June 12, 2009, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Digital_television_transition_in_the_United_States" title="Digital television transition in the United States">federally mandated transition from analog to digital television</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The station's digital signal remained on its pre-transition UHF channel 36,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCC_Form_387_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FCC_Form_387-8">[8]</a></sup> using <a href="/wiki/Program_and_System_Information_Protocol" title="Program and System Information Protocol">PSIP</a> to display WNJU's <a href="/wiki/Virtual_channel" title="Virtual channel">virtual channel</a> as 47 on digital television receivers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="News_operation">News operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WNJU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: News operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WNJU presently broadcasts 17 hours of locally produced newscasts each week (with three hours on weekdays and one hour each on Saturdays and Sundays); in addition, the station produces a newsmagazine program titled <i>Acceso Total</i>, which airs weekday mornings at 10 a.m. and the <a href="/wiki/Public_affairs_(broadcasting)" title="Public affairs (broadcasting)">public affairs</a> program <i>Enfoque New York</i> (a local version of the Telemundo <a href="/wiki/Sunday_morning_talk_show" title="Sunday morning talk show">discussion program</a> <i>Enfoque</i>), which airs Sundays at 12 p.m.</p>
<p>WNJU launched its news operation in the late 1980s, with 6 p.m. and 11 p.m. newscasts anchored by <a href="/wiki/Jorge_L._Ramos" title="Jorge L. Ramos">Jorge L. Ramos</a> In 1997, it launched a weekend edition of <i>Noticiero 47</i>, anchored by Ivan Taylor. In 2001, it launched a morning newscast called <i>Noticiero 47 Primera Edicion</i>, as well as a midday newscast, and the weekend newscast was taken over by Ramon Zayas. However, due to cutbacks, WNJU pulled the plug on its morning, midday, and weekend newscasts in 2009.</p>
<p>Three years later, in 2012, the weekend newscast was relaunched, and in November 2012, a new morning newscast was introduced, called <i>Buenos días, Nueva York</i>. <i>Buenos Días</i> expanded to 5 a.m., adding El Pacha from rival <a href="/wiki/WXTV-DT" title="WXTV-DT">WXTV-DT</a>, though it later reverted to its former title of <i>Noticiero 47 Primera Edicion</i>. Gloria Echeverry was also added to the weeknight newscasts as co-anchor, while Yaima Crespo joined the weekend newscasts in that same capacity. Entertainment reporter Odalys Molina was moved to the morning newscast. During that time, the station also received a new helicopter, <i>HelicopteroT47</i>, along with sister station <a href="/wiki/WNBC" title="WNBC">WNBC</a> and rival <a href="/wiki/WXTV-DT" title="WXTV-DT">WXTV-DT</a>. Morning weather anchor Victoria Sosa and <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Bello" title="Rafael Bello">Rafael Bello</a> now anchor <i>Acceso Total</i>.</p>
<p>On September 18, 2014 <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a> announced a new 5:30 p.m./4:30 p.m. newscast for all 14 of its owned-and-operated stations, including WNJU. Additionally, a new daytime newscast debuted November 3, 2014.</p>
