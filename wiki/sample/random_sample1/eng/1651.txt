<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_Wish_You_a_Merry_Christmas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1935, Oxford University Press published an elaborate four-part choral arrangement by Arthur Warrell under the title "A Merry Christmas", describing the piece as a "West Country Traditional Song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warrell_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-warrell-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Warrell's arrangement is notable for using "I" instead of "we" in the lyrics; the first line is "<b>I</b> wish you a Merry Christmas". It was subsequently republished in the collection <i><a href="/wiki/Carols_for_Choirs" title="Carols for Choirs">Carols for Choirs</a></i> (1961), and remains widely performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earlier history of the carol is unclear. It is absent from the collections of West-countrymen <a href="/wiki/Davies_Gilbert" title="Davies Gilbert">Davies Gilbert</a> (1822 and 1823)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/William_Sandys_(antiquarian)" title="William Sandys (antiquarian)">William Sandys</a> (1833),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as from the great anthologies of Sylvester (1861)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sylvester_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sylvester-6">[6]</a></sup> and Husk (1864).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is also missing from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Oxford_Book_of_Carols" title="The Oxford Book of Carols">The Oxford Book of Carols</a></i> (1928). In the comprehensive <i><a href="/wiki/New_Oxford_Book_of_Carols" title="New Oxford Book of Carols">New Oxford Book of Carols</a></i> (1992), editors Hugh Keyte and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Parrott" title="Andrew Parrott">Andrew Parrott</a> describe it as "English traditional" and "[t]he remnant of an <i>envoie</i> much used by wassailers and other luck visitors"; no source or date is given.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_Wish_You_a_Merry_Christmas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The greeting "a merry Christmas and a happy New Year" is recorded from 1740.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The English custom of performing inside or outside homes in return for food and drink is illustrated in the short story <i>The Christmas Mummers</i> (1858) by <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Mary_Yonge" title="Charlotte Mary Yonge">Charlotte Yonge</a>, in which a group of boys run to a farmer's door and sing:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I wish you a merry Christmas<br/>
And a happy New Year,<br/>
A pantryful of good roast-beef,<br/>
And barrels full of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>After they are allowed in and perform a <a href="/wiki/Mummers_play" title="Mummers play">Mummers play</a>, the boys are served beer by the farmer's maid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origin of this Christmas carol lies in the English tradition wherein wealthy people of the community gave Christmas treats to the carolers on Christmas Eve, such as "<a href="/wiki/Figgy_pudding" title="Figgy pudding">figgy pudding</a>" that was very much like modern-day <a href="/wiki/Christmas_pudding" title="Christmas pudding">Christmas puddings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brech_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brech-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meera_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-meera-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-world-14">[14]</a></sup> A variety of nineteenth-century sources state that, in the West Country of England, "figgy pudding" referred to a raisin or plum pudding, not necessarily one containing figs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
