<p><i><b>Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not</b></i> is the debut studio album by English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Monkeys" title="Arctic Monkeys">Arctic Monkeys</a>, released on 23 January 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Domino_Recording_Company" title="Domino Recording Company">Domino</a>. The album surpassed <a href="/wiki/Elastica" title="Elastica">Elastica</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Elastica_(album)" title="Elastica (album)">self-titled album</a> to become the fastest selling debut album in British music history, shifting over 360,000 copies in its first week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rolling_Stone_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling_Stone-2">[2]</a></sup> and remains the fastest selling debut album by a band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme_susan_boyle_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nme_susan_boyle-4">[4]</a></sup> It has since gone quintuple <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> in the UK.</p>
<p>The album includes both tracks from the band's original <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Five_Minutes_with_Arctic_Monkeys" title="Five Minutes with Arctic Monkeys">Five Minutes with Arctic Monkeys</a></i>, as well as their first two singles and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_UK_Singles_Chart_number_ones_of_2005" title="List of UK Singles Chart number ones of 2005">UK number ones</a>, "<a href="/wiki/I_Bet_You_Look_Good_on_the_Dancefloor" title="I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor">I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/When_the_Sun_Goes_Down_(Arctic_Monkeys_song)" title="When the Sun Goes Down (Arctic Monkeys song)">When the Sun Goes Down</a>". It has since appeared in several critics' publications, and is often cited as one of the best rock albums of the decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rolling_stone_100_of_2000s_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rolling_stone_100_of_2000s-5">[5]</a></sup> It received the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Prize" title="Mercury Prize">Mercury Prize</a> for Best Album. It was ranked number 371 on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time"><i>Rolling Stone</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>. In 2013 <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> ranked it the 30th greatest debut album of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rolling_Stone_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling_Stone-2">[2]</a></sup> In October 2013, music magazine <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> also ranked the album at number 19 in their poll of the 500 greatest albums of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_content">Composition and content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whatever_People_Say_I_Am,_That%27s_What_I%27m_Not&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition and content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musically, <i>Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not</i> features <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-absolutepunk_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-absolutepunk-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guestlist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guestlist-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garage_rock_revival" title="Garage rock revival">garage rock revival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guestlist_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guestlist-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Post-punk_revival" title="Post-punk revival">post-punk revival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guestlist_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guestlist-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The common thematic content of the album has led to it being considered by some a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> concerning "the lives of young <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a> clubbers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rolling_Stone_review_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling_Stone_review-11">[11]</a></sup> All tracks record first-person narratives of observations made within this context. "I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor", "Still Take You Home", "You Probably Couldn't See for the Lights But You Were Staring Straight at Me" and "Dancing Shoes" all examine human behaviour in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nightclubs" title="Nightclubs">nightclubs</a>. Frontman <a href="/wiki/Alex_Turner_(musician)" title="Alex Turner (musician)">Alex Turner</a> describes "Dancing Shoes" as being about "people always looking to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pull" title="wikt:pull">pull</a> when they go out however much they mask it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Own_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Own-12">[12]</a></sup> Other songs examine other aspects of <a href="/wiki/Nightlife" title="Nightlife">nightlife</a>; "From the Ritz to the Rubble" is an account of nightclub bouncers, "Red Light Indicates Doors Are Secured" tells the typical experiences and troubles of getting a <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxicab</a> after a night out, and "When the Sun Goes Down" was inspired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitutes" title="Prostitutes">prostitutes</a> in the locality of their practice room in the <a href="/wiki/Neepsend" title="Neepsend">Neepsend</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_review_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_review-13">[13]</a></sup> Other songs are themed on romantic relationships, such as "Mardy Bum", or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Youth_subcultures" title="Youth subcultures">youth subcultures</a>, such as "Fake Tales of San Francisco" and "A Certain Romance". In <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s list of the top 100 tracks of the decade, "A Certain Romance" was described as "a strangely even-handed song which starts out scorning local townies then appears to absolve them at the end of the song."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Album_title_and_artwork">Album title and artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whatever_People_Say_I_Am,_That%27s_What_I%27m_Not&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Album title and artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album's title was taken from a line from the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_and_Sunday_Morning" title="Saturday Night and Sunday Morning">Saturday Night and Sunday Morning</a></i> written by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sillitoe" title="Alan Sillitoe">Alan Sillitoe</a>. The name was chosen after Turner recognised similarities between the two works and the appropriateness of the title. He said that "it's good because the book is called <i>Saturday Night and Sunday Morning</i> and that's kind of what the album is, so there's a link there. And also, there's a lot of people saying a lot of things about us and you don't have control over it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AG_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AG-15">[15]</a></sup> He also said that "songs including 'The View from the Afternoon', 'Dancing Shoes', 'Still Take You Home' and 'From the Ritz to the Rubble' all cover that bit of the weekend and feature the same character."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cover_art" title="Cover art">Cover artwork</a> of the album is a photo of Chris McClure—a close friend of the band, frontman of <a href="/wiki/The_Violet_May" title="The Violet May">The Violet May</a> and brother of <a href="/wiki/Jon_McClure" title="Jon McClure">Jon McClure</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reverend_and_the_Makers" title="Reverend and the Makers">Reverend and the Makers</a>—taken in the early hours of the morning in <a href="/wiki/Korova_(Liverpool)" title="Korova (Liverpool)">Korova</a> bar, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AG_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-AG-15">[15]</a></sup> after the band had given him, his cousin and his best friend "seventy <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">quid</a> to spend on a night out".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The image caused some controversy when the head of Scotland's <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> criticised the cover for "reinforcing the idea that smoking is okay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The band's product manager denied the accusation, and in fact suggested the opposite: "You can see from the image smoking is not doing him the world of good." <a href="/wiki/Billboard" title="Billboard">Billboard</a> advertisements for the album used a similar image to the cover picture, but without the cigarette.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whatever_People_Say_I_Am,_That%27s_What_I%27m_Not&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the release of the album, the tracks "Mardy Bum", "I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor", "Fake Tales of San Francisco", "Dancing Shoes", "Still Take You Home", "Riot Van", "When the Sun Goes Down" (then known as "Scummy" or "Scummy Man") and "A Certain Romance" had been released for free via the internet in late 2004 and consolidated on the unofficial <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_the_Boardwalk" title="Beneath the Boardwalk">Beneath the Boardwalk</a></i> compilation.</p>
<p>The original release date was 30 January 2006, but was brought forward to 23 January 2006 due to "high demand". Although the same was done with <i><a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(album)" title="Franz Ferdinand (album)">Franz Ferdinand</a></i>, it was speculated that the move was an attempt to counter the effects of the album's leak onto online <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">file-sharing</a> sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NME1-19">[19]</a></sup> The re-recorded album versions had been leaked onto the internet by December 2005.</p>
<p>On the first day of its release, the album became the fastest-selling debut album in British history, selling just under 120,000 copies. By the end of the week, the album had sold 363,735 copies—more than the rest of the Top 20 combined and making it the overall fastest selling debut album in British history. Its release in the United States on 21 February 2006 saw it become the second fastest selling debut indie album in history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20">[20]</a></sup> turning over around 34,000 copies in its first week and achieving number 24 in the album charts. The album also went to number 1 in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. UK sales as of September 2013 stood at 1,475,982 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In February 2014, the album was certified 5× platinum, indicating sales of 1,500,000 copies.</p>
<p>The track "Mardy Bum", while not released as a single, appeared on radio playlists throughout the UK in mid-2006, and is still played infrequently on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a> and some alternative rock stations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sirius_XM" title="Sirius XM">Sirius XM</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sirius_XMU" title="Sirius XMU">Sirius XMU</a>. The track "A Certain Romance" was ranked number 90 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>'s Top 100 Tracks of 2006 and cited as the standout track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>NME</i> also placed "A Certain Romance" at 10 in their list of 100 Tracks of the Decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In October 2011, <i>NME</i> placed it at number 140 on its list "150 Best Tracks of the Past 15 Years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whatever_People_Say_I_Am,_That%27s_What_I%27m_Not&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/I_Bet_You_Look_Good_on_the_Dancefloor" title="I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor">I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor</a>" was released as the first single from the album, the song debuted at number one on the UK Singles Chart in October 2005 for one week, knocking <a href="/wiki/Sugababes" title="Sugababes">Sugababes</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Push_the_Button_(Sugababes_song)" title="Push the Button (Sugababes song)">Push the Button</a>" off the top.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/When_the_Sun_Goes_Down_(Arctic_Monkeys_song)" title="When the Sun Goes Down (Arctic Monkeys song)">When the Sun Goes Down</a>" was released as the second single from the album in January 2006, also debuting at number one on the UK Singles Chart, becoming their second consecutive UK number-one single. In the US, "Fake Tales of San Francisco" was released as the album's second single and peaked at number 30 on the <i>Billboard</i> charts.</p>

<p>Since its release the album has received universal acclaim with a <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> rating of 82.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MC_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-MC-25">[25]</a></sup> It featured highly in many year-end lists and has been hailed as a modern classic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanneh_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-35">[35]</a></sup> Many critics and figures in the British media hyped the Arctic Monkeys and their rapid rise to acclaim through unconventional means<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_review_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_review-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicomh.com_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-musicomh.com-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and some even cited the Arctic Monkeys as revolutionising the way people find music as they built a fanbase on the basis of a few demos shared by fans through the internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>NME</i> declared the Arctic Monkeys "Our Generation's Most Important Band", and Alex Turner's lyrics and depiction of Sheffield, and the night lives of teenagers in particular, were praised, with him being labelled as a "master of observation"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nme.com_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-nme.com-29">[29]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> writing "you probably won't hear a better CD all year long", calling it "utterly infectious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanneh_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-35">[35]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/MusicOMH" title="MusicOMH">MusicOMH</a></i> wrote that it was the sort of guitar rock that "makes you fall in love with music all over again" and along with many other critics cited "A Certain Romance" as the standout track and as being "a wonderfully articulate dissection of youth culture that belies Turner's tender years". It was, however, noted that some of the tracks which had previously been released on the internet as demos had lost some of their quality and "don't sound as good".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicomh.com_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-musicomh.com-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
