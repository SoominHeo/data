<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">XScale</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For the model railroad scale, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X_scale" title="X scale">X scale</a>.</div>
<p><b>XScale</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Microarchitecture" title="Microarchitecture">microarchitecture</a> for <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing units</a> initially designed by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> implementing the <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM architecture</a> (version 5) <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a>. XScale comprises several distinct families: IXP, IXC, IOP, PXA and CE (see more below), with some later models designed as <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">SoCs</a>. Intel sold the PXA family to <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell Technology Group</a> in June 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Marvell then extended the brand to include processors with other <a href="/wiki/Microarchitecture" title="Microarchitecture">microarchitectures</a>, like <a href="/wiki/ARM_Holdings" title="ARM Holdings">ARM</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cortex-A" title="Cortex-A">Cortex</a>.</p>
<p>The XScale architecture is based on the ARMv5TE <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instruction_Set" title="Instruction Set">ISA</a> without the <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a> instructions. XScale uses a seven-stage integer and an eight-stage memory super-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instruction_pipeline" title="Instruction pipeline">pipelined</a> <a href="/wiki/Microarchitecture" title="Microarchitecture">microarchitecture</a>. It is the successor to the Intel <a href="/wiki/StrongARM" title="StrongARM">StrongARM</a> line of <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a>, which Intel acquired from <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a>'s Digital Semiconductor division as part of a settlement of a lawsuit between the two companies. Intel used the StrongARM to replace its ailing line of outdated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RISC" title="RISC">RISC</a> processors, the <a href="/wiki/Intel_i860" title="Intel i860">i860</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intel_i960" title="Intel i960">i960</a>.</p>
<p>All the generations of XScale are 32-bit ARMv5TE processors manufactured with a 0.18 µm or 0.13 µm (as in IXP43x parts) process and have a 32 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">KB</a> data <a href="/wiki/CPU_cache" title="CPU cache">cache</a> and a 32 KB instruction cache. First and second generation XScale <a href="/wiki/Multi-core_processor" title="Multi-core processor">multi-core processors</a> also have a 2 KB mini data cache (claimed it "avoids “thrashing” of the D-Cache for frequently changing data streams"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>). Products based on the 3rd generation XScale have up to 512 KB unified L2 cache.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processor_families">Processor families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processor families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The XScale core is used in a number of <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontroller</a> families manufactured by Intel and Marvell:</p>
<ul>
<li>Application processors (with the prefix PXA). There are four generations of XScale application processors, described below: PXA210/PXA25x, PXA26x, PXA27x, and PXA3xx.</li>
<li>I/O processors (with the prefix IOP).</li>
<li>Network processors (with the prefix IXP).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Control_plane" title="Control plane">Control plane</a> processors (with the prefix IXC).</li>
<li>Consumer electronics processors (with the prefix CE).</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also standalone processors: the 80200 and 80219 (targeted primarily at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripheral_Component_Interconnect" title="Peripheral Component Interconnect">PCI</a> applications).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA">PXA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: PXA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA210.2FPXA25x">PXA210/PXA25x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: PXA210/PXA25x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PXA210 was Intel's entry-level XScale targeted at mobile phone applications. It was released with the PXA250 in February 2002 and comes clocked at 133 MHz and 200 MHz.</p>
<p>The PXA25x family (code-named <b>Cotulla</b>) consists of the PXA250 and PXA255. The PXA250 was Intel's first generation of XScale processors. There was a choice of three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clock_speed" title="Clock speed">clock speeds</a>: 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHz" title="MHz">MHz</a>, 300 MHz and 400 MHz. It came out in February 2002. In March 2003, the revision C0 of the PXA250 was renamed to PXA255. The main differences were a doubled internal bus speed (100 MHz to 200 MHz) for faster data transfer, lower core voltage (only 1.3 V at 400 MHz) for lower power consumption and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cache_misses" title="Cache misses">writeback</a> functionality for the data cache, the lack of which had severely impaired performance on the PXA250.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA26x">PXA26x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: PXA26x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PXA26x family (code-named <b>Dalhart</b>) consists of the PXA260 and PXA261-PXA263. The PXA260 is a stand-alone processor clocked at the same frequency as the PXA25x, but features a TPBGA package which is about 53% smaller than the PXA25x's PBGA package. The PXA261-PXA263 are the same as the PXA260 but have Intel <a href="/wiki/StrataFlash" title="StrataFlash">StrataFlash</a> memory stacked on top of the processor in the same package; 16 MB of 16-bit memory in the PXA261, 32 MB of 16-bit memory in the PXA262 and 32 MB of 32-bit memory in the PXA263. The PXA26x family was released in March 2003.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA27x">PXA27x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: PXA27x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Intel_PXA270,_624MHz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1473" data-file-width="1573" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg/280px-Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg/420px-Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg/560px-Intel_PXA270%2C_624MHz.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Intel_PXA270,_624MHz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Intel PXA270 with 624 MHz</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The PXA27x family (code-named <b>Bulverde</b>) consists of the PXA270 and PXA271-PXA272 processors. This revision is a huge update to the XScale family of processors. The PXA270 is clocked in four different speeds: 312 MHz, 416 MHz, 520 MHz and 624 MHz and is a stand-alone processor with no packaged memory. The PXA271 can be clocked to 13, 104, 208 MHz or 416 MHz and has 32 MB of 16-bit stacked StrataFlash memory and 32 MB of 16-bit SDRAM in the same package. The PXA272 can be clocked to 312 MHz, 416 MHz or 520 MHz and has 64 MB of 32-bit stacked StrataFlash memory.</p>
<p>Intel also added many new technologies to the PXA27x family such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/SpeedStep" title="SpeedStep">SpeedStep</a>: the operating system can clock the processor down based on load to save power.</li>
<li>Wireless <a href="/wiki/MMX_(instruction_set)" title="MMX (instruction set)">MMX</a>: 43 new <a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">SIMD</a> instructions containing the full MMX <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> and the integer instructions from Intel's <a href="/wiki/Streaming_SIMD_Extensions" title="Streaming SIMD Extensions">SSE</a> instruction set along with some instructions unique to the XScale. Wireless MMX provides 16 extra 64-bit <a href="/wiki/Processor_register" title="Processor register">registers</a> that can be treated as an array of two 32-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_(data_type)" title="Word (data type)">words</a>, four 16-bit halfwords or eight 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">bytes</a>. The XScale core can then perform up to eight adds or four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiply%E2%80%93accumulate" title="Multiply–accumulate">MACs</a> in parallel in a single cycle. This capability is used to boost speed in <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">decoding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Encoder" title="Encoder">encoding</a> of multimedia and in playing games.</li>
<li>Additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripherals" title="Peripherals">peripherals</a>, such as a USB-Host interface and a camera interface.</li>
<li>Internal 256 KB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Static_random_access_memory" title="Static random access memory">SRAM</a> to reduce power consumption and latency.</li>
</ul>
<p>The PXA27x family was released in April 2004. Along with the PXA27x family Intel released the <a href="/wiki/Intel_2700G" title="Intel 2700G">2700G</a> embedded graphics <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-processor" title="Co-processor">co-processor</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA3xx">PXA3xx</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: PXA3xx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="940" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg/280px-Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg/420px-Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg/560px-Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Toradex_Colibri_XScale_Monahans_PXA290.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Toradex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Toradex (page does not exist)">Toradex</a> Colibri XScale <b>Monahans</b> PXA290 SODIMM-module (Prototype Of Marvell PXA320 SODIMM-module)</div>

</div>
<p>In August 2005 Intel announced the successor to <b>Bulverde</b>, codenamed <b>Monahans</b>.</p>
<p>They demonstrated it showing its capability to play back high definition encoded video on a PDA screen.</p>
<p>The new processor was shown clocked at 1.25 GHz but Intel said it only offered a 25% increase in performance (800 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million_instructions_per_second" title="Million instructions per second">MIPS</a> for the 624 MHz PXA270 processor vs. 1000 MIPS for 1.25 GHz <b>Monahans</b>). An announced successor to the 2700G graphics processor, code named Stanwood, has since been canceled. sd features of Stanwood are integrated into <b>Monahans</b>. For extra graphics capabilities, Intel recommends third-party chips like the <a href="/wiki/Nvidia" title="Nvidia">Nvidia</a> <a href="/wiki/GoForce" title="GoForce">GoForce</a> chip family.</p>
<p>In November 2006, <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell Semiconductor</a> officially introduced the <b>Monahans</b> family as Marvell PXA320, PXA300, and PXA310.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> PXA320 is currently shipping in high volume, and is scalable up to 806 MHz. PXA300 and PXA310 deliver performance "scalable to 624 MHz", and are software-compatible with PXA320.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA90x">PXA90x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: PXA90x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PXA90x was released by Marvell and combines an XScale Core with a GSM/CDMA communication module.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The PXA90x is build using a 130 nm process<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA16x">PXA16x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: PXA16x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SOM168.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/SOM168.jpg/220px-SOM168.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/SOM168.jpg/330px-SOM168.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/SOM168.jpg/440px-SOM168.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SOM168.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
PXA168 System On Module by tianyeit.com</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>PXA16x is a processor designed by Marvell, combining the earlier Intel designed PXA <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">SoC</a> components with a new ARMv5TE CPU core named <b>Mohawk</b> or <b>PJ1</b> from Marvell's <b>Sheeva</b> family instead of using wdc Xscale or ARM design. The CPU core is derived from the <b>Feroceon</b> core used in Marvell's embedded <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Kirkwood</a> product line, but extended for instruction level compatibility with the XScale IWMMX.</p>
<p>The PXA16x delivers strong performance at a mass market price point for cost sensitive consumer and embedded markets such as digital picture frames, E Readers, multifunction printer user interface (UI) displays, interactive VoIP phones, IP surveillance cameras, and home control gadgets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA930.2F935">PXA930/935</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: PXA930/935">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PXA930 and PXA935 processor series were again built using the Sheeva microarchitecture developed by Marvell but upgraded to ARMv7 instruction set compatibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This core is a so-called Tri-core architecture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvell.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-marvell.com-9">[9]</a></sup> codenamed Tavor; Tri-core means it supports the ARMv5TE, ARMv6 and ARMv7 instruction sets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvell.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-marvell.com-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdadb.net_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdadb.net-10">[10]</a></sup> This new architecture was a significant leap from the old Xscale architecture. The PXA930 uses 65 nm technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> while the PXA935 is build using the 45 nm process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdadb.net_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdadb.net-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The PXA930 is used in the BlackBerry Bold 9700.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA940">PXA940</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: PXA940">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Little is known about the PXA940, although it is known to be <a href="/wiki/ARM_Cortex-A8" title="ARM Cortex-A8">ARM Cortex-A8</a> compliant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is utilized in the BlackBerry Torch 9800<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and is built using 45 nm technology.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA986.2FPXA988">PXA986/PXA988</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: PXA986/PXA988">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After XScale and Sheeva, the PXA98x uses the third CPU core design, this time licensed directly from ARM, in form of dual core <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cortex_A9" title="Cortex A9">Cortex A9</a> application processors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> utilized by devices like <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Galaxy_Tab_3_7.0" title="Samsung Galaxy Tab 3 7.0">Samsung Galaxy Tab 3 7.0</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PXA1088">PXA1088</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: PXA1088">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>It is a quad core <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cortex_A7" title="Cortex A7">Cortex A7</a> application processor with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivante" title="Vivante">Vivante</a> GPU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IXC">IXC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: IXC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="IXC1100">IXC1100</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: IXC1100">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The IXC1100 processor features clock speeds at 266, 400, and 533 MHz, a 133 MHz bus, 32 KB of instruction cache, 32 KB of data cache, and 2 KB of mini-data cache. It is also designed for low power consumption, using 2.4 W at 533 MHz. The chip comes in the 35 mm PBGA package.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IOP">IOP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: IOP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The IOP line of processors is designed to allow computers and storage devices to transfer data and increase performance by offloading I/O functionality from the main CPU of the device. The IOP3XX processors are based on the XScale architecture and designed to replace the older 80219 sd and i960 family of chips. There are ten different IOP processors currently available: IOP303, IOP310, IOP315, IOP321, IOP331, IOP332, IOP333, IOP341, IOP342 and IOP348. Clock speeds range from 100 MHz to 1.2 GHz. The processors also differ in PCI bus type, PCI bus speed, memory type, maximum memory allowable, and the number of processor cores.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IXP_network_processor">IXP network processor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: IXP network processor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The XScale core is utilized in the second generation of Intel's IXP network processor line, while the first generation used StrongARM cores. The IXP network processor family ranges from solutions aimed at small/medium office network applications, IXP4XX, to high performance network processors such as the IXP2850, capable of sustaining up to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OC-192" title="OC-192">OC-192</a> line rates. In IXP4XX devices the XScale core is used as both a control and data plane processor, providing both system control and data processing. The task of the XScale in the IXP2XXX devices is typically to provide control plane functionality only, with data processing performed by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Microengines&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Microengines (page does not exist)">microengines</a>, examples of such control plane tasks include routing table updates, microengine control, memory management.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CE">CE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: CE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2007, Intel announced an XScale-based processor targeting <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> markets, the Intel CE 2110 (codenamed Olo River).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>XScale microprocessors can be found in products such as the popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_In_Motion" title="Research In Motion">RIM</a> <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry" title="BlackBerry">BlackBerry</a> handheld, the <a href="/wiki/Dell_Axim" title="Dell Axim">Dell Axim</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Pocket_PC" title="Pocket PC">Pocket PCs</a>, most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zire" title="Zire">Zire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Treo" title="Palm Treo">Treo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten_Handheld" title="Tungsten Handheld">Tungsten Handheld</a> lines by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PalmOne,_Inc." title="PalmOne, Inc.">Palm</a>, later versions of the <a href="/wiki/Sharp_Zaurus" title="Sharp Zaurus">Sharp Zaurus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Motorola_A780" title="Motorola A780">Motorola A780</a>, the Acer n50, the Compaq <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPaq" title="IPaq">iPaq</a> 3900 series and many other <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">PDAs</a>. It is used as the main <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Iyonix_PC" title="Iyonix PC">Iyonix PC</a> desktop computer running <a href="/wiki/RISC_OS" title="RISC OS">RISC OS</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/NSLU2" title="NSLU2">NSLU2</a> (Slug) running a form of <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. The XScale is also used in devices such as PVPs (Portable Video Players), PMCs (Portable Media Centres), including the <a href="/wiki/Creative_Zen" title="Creative Zen">Creative Zen</a> Portable Media Player and <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Kindle" title="Amazon Kindle">Amazon Kindle</a> E-Book reader, and industrial embedded systems. At the other end of the market, the XScale IOP33x Storage I/O processors are used in some Intel <a href="/wiki/Xeon" title="Xeon">Xeon</a>-based server platforms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_of_PXA_processor_line">Sale of PXA processor line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=XScale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sale of PXA processor line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 27, 2006, the sale of Intel's XScale PXA mobile processor assets was announced. Intel agreed to sell the XScale PXA business to <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell Technology Group</a> for an estimated $600 million in cash and the assumption of unspecified liabilities. The move was intended to permit Intel to focus its resources on its core x86 and server businesses. Marvell holds a full architecture license for ARM, allowing it to design chips to implement the ARM instruction set, not just license a processor core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The acquisition was completed on November 9, 2006. Intel was expected to continue manufacturing XScale processors until Marvell secures other manufacturing facilities, and would continue manufacturing and selling the IXP and IOP processors, as they were not part of the deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The XScale effort at Intel was initiated by the purchase of the <a href="/wiki/StrongARM" title="StrongARM">StrongARM</a> division from <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reg20060627_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-reg20060627-21">[21]</a></sup> Intel still holds an ARM license even after the sale of XScale;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reg20060627_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-reg20060627-21">[21]</a></sup> this license is at the architectural level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
