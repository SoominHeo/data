<p><b>Triple X syndrome</b>, also known as <b>trisomy X</b> and <b>47 XXX</b>, is characterized by the presence of an extra <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a> in each <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">female</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Those affected are often taller than average. Usually there is no other physical differences and normal <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a>. Occasionally there are <a href="/wiki/Learning_disability" title="Learning disability">learning difficulties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypotonia" title="Hypotonia">decreased muscle tone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seizure" title="Seizure">seizures</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_problems" title="Kidney problems">kidney problems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Triple X is due to a random event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Triple X can result either during the division of the mother's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_cells" title="Reproductive cells">reproductive cells</a> or during division of cells during early development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tar2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tar2010-2">[2]</a></sup> It is not typically <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">inherited</a> from one generation to the next. A form where only a percentage of the body cells contain XXX can also occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Diagnosis is by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomal_analysis" title="Chromosomal analysis">chromosomal analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GARD2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GARD2016-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Treatment may include speech therapy, physical therapy, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counseling" title="Counseling">counseling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GARD2016_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GARD2016-3">[3]</a></sup> It occurs in about one in every 1,000 female births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tar2010_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tar2010-2">[2]</a></sup> It is estimated that 90% of those affected are not diagnosed as they either have no or only few symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tar2010_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tar2010-2">[2]</a></sup> It was first identified in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span></h2>
<p>Because the vast majority of triple X females are never diagnosed, it may be very difficult to make generalizations about the effects of this syndrome. The samples that were studied were small and may be nonrepresentative. Because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyonization" title="Lyonization">lyonization</a>, inactivation, and formation of <a href="/wiki/Barr_body" title="Barr body">Barr bodies</a> in all female cells, only one <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a> is active at any time. Thus, triple X syndrome most often has only mild effects or has no effects. The symptoms vary from person to person, with some women being more affected than others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span></h3>
<p>Symptoms may include tall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stature" title="Stature">stature</a>, vertical skin folds that may cover the inner corners of the eyes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicanthal_fold" title="Epicanthal fold">epicanthal folds</a>), poor muscle tone, and a curve in the 5th finger towards the 4th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tar2010_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tar2010-2">[2]</a></sup> There may also be a small head (<a href="/wiki/Microcephaly" title="Microcephaly">microcephaly</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> There are seldom any observable physical anomalies in triple X females, other than being taller than average.</p>
<p>Poor <a href="/wiki/Motor_coordination" title="Motor coordination">coordination</a> may be present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup> Those affected appear to have higher rates of <a href="/wiki/Scoliosis" title="Scoliosis">scoliosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological">Psychological</span></h3>
<p>Females with triple X syndrome often have delayed language development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup> On average those affected have IQs that are 20 points lower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup> Poor self-esteem, <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a> are also common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tar2010_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tar2010-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span></h2>

<p>Triple X syndrome is not inherited, but usually, occurs as an event during the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gametes" title="Gametes">reproductive cells</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ovum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">sperm</a>). An error in cell division called <a href="/wiki/Nondisjunction" title="Nondisjunction">nondisjunction</a> can result in reproductive cells with additional chromosomes. For example, an <a href="/wiki/Oocyte" title="Oocyte">oocyte</a> or sperm cell may gain an extra copy of the X chromosome as a result of the non-disjunction. If one of these cells contributes to the genetic makeup of a child, the child will have an extra X chromosome in each of her cells. In some cases, trisomy X occurs during cell division in early <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryonic</a> development.</p>
<p>Some females with triple X syndrome have an extra X chromosome in only some of their cells. These cases are called 46,XX/47,XXX <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(genetics)" title="Mosaic (genetics)">mosaics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span></h2>
<p>The vast majority of triple X women are never diagnosed, unless they undergo tests for other medical reasons later in life. Triple X can be diagnosed by a blood test which is able to look at a person’s chromosomes (karyotype). Abnormalities on the <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalography</a> may be present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Triple X syndrome can be diagnosed prenatally through <a href="/wiki/Amniocentesis" title="Amniocentesis">amniocentesis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chorionic_villus_sampling" title="Chorionic villus sampling">chorionic villus sampling</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, between 1970 and 1984, 76% of the prenatally diagnosed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetuses" title="Fetuses">fetuses</a> with triple-X were aborted. Between 1985-1987, this figure dropped to 56%. With improved information, the number of <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortions</a> diminished. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, between 1991 and 2000, 33% (18/54) of the couples that were confronted with a prenatal diagnosis of 47, XXX elected to abort. If balanced information is provided to prospective parents, prenatally, the incidence of voluntary termination (abortion) is reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span></h2>
<p>A stable home environment can improve some of the symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ot2010_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ot2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span></h2>
<p>Triple X syndrome occurs in around 1 in 1,000 girls. On average, five to ten girls with triple X syndrome are born in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ghr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ghr-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The first published report of a woman with a 47,XXX karyotype was by <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Jacobs" title="Patricia Jacobs">Patricia A. Jacobs</a>, et al. at <a href="/wiki/Western_General_Hospital" title="Western General Hospital">Western General Hospital</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edinburgh,_Scotland" title="Edinburgh, Scotland">Edinburgh, Scotland</a>, in 1959. It was found in a 35‑year-old, 5 ft. 9 in. (176 cm) tall, 128 lb. (58.2 kg) woman who had <a href="/wiki/Premature_ovarian_failure" title="Premature ovarian failure">premature ovarian failure</a> at age 19; her mother was age 41 and her father was 40 at the time of her conception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs_1959_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs_1959-9">[9]</a></sup> Jacobs, et al. called the 47,XXX woman a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfemale" title="Superfemale">superfemale</a>", a term which was immediately criticized, did not gain acceptance, and was based on the incorrect assumption that the <a href="/wiki/Sex-determination_system" title="Sex-determination system">sex-determination system</a> in mammals was the same as in the fruit fly <i><a href="/wiki/Drosophila" title="Drosophila">Drosophila</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superfemale_1959_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-superfemale_1959-10">[10]</a></sup> British pathologist and geneticist Bernard Lennox, the principal consultant on medical terms for the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, suggested the term "XXX syndrome".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lennox_1960_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lennox_1960-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
