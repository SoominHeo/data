<p><b>Generation X</b>, or <b>Gen X</b>, is the demographic cohort following the <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">Baby boomers</a>. There are no precise dates for when this cohort starts or ends; demographers and researchers typically use the starting birth years ranging from the early-to-mid 1960s and ending birth years ranging from the late 1970s to early 1980s. Generation X is a relatively smaller demographic cohort “sandwiched” between two larger demographic cohorts, the Baby boomers and the <a href="/wiki/Millennials" title="Millennials">Millennials</a>.</p>
<p>Members of Generation X were children during a time of shifting societal values and as children were sometimes called the "<a href="/wiki/Latchkey_kid" title="Latchkey kid">latchkey</a> generation”, due to reduced adult supervision compared to previous generations, a result of increasing <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> rates and increased maternal participation in the workforce, prior to widespread availability of childcare options outside of the home. As adolescents and young adults, they were dubbed the “<a href="/wiki/MTV_Generation" title="MTV Generation">MTV Generation</a>” and characterized as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slackers" title="Slackers">slackers</a> and as <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(contemporary)" title="Cynicism (contemporary)">cynical</a> and disaffected. In midlife, research describes Gen X adults as active, happy, and as achieving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work-life_balance" title="Work-life balance">work-life balance</a>. The cohort has been credited with <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurship" title="Entrepreneurship">entrepreneurial</a> tendencies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_term">Origin of term</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="1479" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg/200px-Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg/300px-Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg/400px-Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_Coupland_Photo_of_Author.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Douglas_Coupland" title="Douglas Coupland">Douglas Coupland</a> popularized the term "Generation X" in his 1991 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Generation_X:_Tales_for_an_Accelerated_Culture" title="Generation X: Tales for an Accelerated Culture">Generation X: Tales for an Accelerated Culture</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The term "Generation X" has been used at various times throughout history to describe alienated youth. In the 1950s, Hungarian photographer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Capa" title="Robert Capa">Robert Capa</a> used Generation X as the title for a photo-essay about young men and women growing up immediately following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1976, English musician <a href="/wiki/Billy_Idol" title="Billy Idol">Billy Idol</a> used the moniker as the name for <a href="/wiki/Generation_X_(band)" title="Generation X (band)">a punk rock band</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adweek_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adweek-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term acquired its contemporary definition after the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Generation_X:_Tales_for_an_Accelerated_Culture" title="Generation X: Tales for an Accelerated Culture">Generation X: Tales for an Accelerated Culture</a></i>, a 1991 novel written by Canadian author <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Coupland" title="Douglas Coupland">Douglas Coupland</a>. Demographer <a href="/wiki/Neil_Howe" title="Neil Howe">Neil Howe</a> noted the delay in naming this demographic cohort saying, "Over 30 years after their birthday, they didn't have a name. I think that's germane." Previously, the cohort had been referred to as Post-Boomers, Baby Busters, New <a href="/wiki/Lost_Generation" title="Lost Generation">Lost Generation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latch-key_kids" title="Latch-key kids">Latch-key kids</a>, <a href="/wiki/MTV_Generation" title="MTV Generation">MTV Generation</a>, and the 13th Generation (they were described as the 13th generation since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_independence" title="American independence">American independence</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adweek_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Adweek-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demographer <a href="/wiki/Strauss%E2%80%93Howe_generational_theory" title="Strauss–Howe generational theory">William Strauss</a> observed that Coupland applied the term to older members of the cohort born between 1961–1964, who were sometimes told by demographers that they were <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">baby boomers</a>, but who did not feel like boomers. Strauss also noted that around the time Coupland's 1991 novel was published the symbol "X" was prominent in popular culture, as the film <i><a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X_(1992_film)" title="Malcolm X (1992 film)">Malcolm X</a></i> was released in 1992, and that the name "Generation X" ended up sticking. The "X" refers to an unknown variable or to a desire not to be defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date_and_age_range_defining">Date and age range defining</span></h2>
<p>Generation X is the demographic cohort following the <a href="/wiki/Post%E2%80%93World_War_II_baby_boom" title="Post–World War II baby boom">post–World War II baby boom</a>, representing a generational change from the <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">baby boomers</a>, but there is debate over what this means because the end date of the baby boomer generation is disputed. Many demographers use dates which correspond to the strict fertility patterns in the population, which results in a Generation X starting date of 1965, such as <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> which uses a range of 1965–1980,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> MetLife which uses 1965–1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife2-3">[3]</a></sup> Australia’s McCrindle Research Center which uses 1965–1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrindle_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCrindle-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gallup_(company)" title="Gallup (company)">Gallup</a> which also uses 1965–1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research from <a href="/wiki/MetLife" title="MetLife">MetLife</a> examining the boomers split their cohort into "older boomers", which they defined as born between 1946 and 1955, and “younger boomers”, which they defined as born between 1956 and 1964. They found much of the cultural identity of the baby boomer generation is associated with the "older boomers", while half of the "younger boomers" were averse to being associated with the baby boomer cohort and a third of those born between 1956 and 1964 actively identified as members of Generation X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demographers <a href="/wiki/Strauss%E2%80%93Howe_generational_theory" title="Strauss–Howe generational theory">William Straus and Neil Howe</a> rejected the 1964 end date of the baby boomer cohort (which results in a 1965 start year for Generation X), saying that a majority of those born between 1961–1964 do not self-identify as boomers, and that they are culturally distinct from boomers in terms of shared historical experiences. Howe says that while many demographers use 1965 as a start date for Generation X, this is a statement about fertility in the population (birth rates which began declining in 1957, declined more sharply following 1964) and fails to take into consideration the shared history and cultural identity of the individuals. Strauss and Howe define Generation X as those born between 1961–1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13th_Gen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-13th_Gen-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Generation X Report, a quarterly research report from <i>The Longitudinal Study of American Youth</i>, a <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> study conducted at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> also defines Generation X as those born between 1961–1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-LSA-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a>, a multinational professional services network headquartered in London, describes Generation X employees as those born from the early 1960s to the early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Gordinier" title="Jeff Gordinier">Jeff Gordinier</a>, in his 2008 book <i>X Saves the World</i>, defines Generation X as those born roughly between 1961–1977 but possibly as late as 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordinier_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordinier-18">[18]</a></sup> In his book <i>Boom Bust &amp; Echo: How to Profit from the Coming Demographic Shift</i>, Canadian author and professor <a href="/wiki/David_Foot" title="David Foot">David Foot</a> divides the post-boomer generation into two groups:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Generation X, born between 1960 and 1966; and the "Bust Generation", born between 1967 and 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Generation X</i>, a six-part 2016 documentary series produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Channel" title="National Geographic Channel">National Geographic</a> uses a 1961–1981 birth year range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>On the American television program <i><a href="/wiki/Survivor_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Survivor (U.S. TV series)">Survivor</a></i>, for their 33rd season, subtitled <i><a href="/wiki/Survivor:_Millennials_vs._Gen_X" title="Survivor: Millennials vs. Gen X">Millennials vs. Gen X</a></i>, the "Gen X tribe" consisted of individuals born between 1963 and 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>Other demographers and researchers use a wide range of dates to describe Generation X, with the beginning birth year ranging from as early as 1960<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> to as late as 1965,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrindle_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCrindle-9">[9]</a></sup> and with the final birth year ranging from as early as 1976<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> to as late as 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masnick_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Masnick-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohort_confusion_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohort_confusion-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due in part to the frequent birth-year overlap and resulting incongruence existing between attempts to define Generation X and <a href="/wiki/Millennials" title="Millennials">Millennials</a>, a number of individuals born in the late 1970s or early 1980s see themselves as being "between" the two generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huffington1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huffington1-32">[32]</a></sup> Names given to those born on the Generation X/Millennial cusp years include Xennials, The Lucky Ones, Generation Catalano, and the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Trail_Generation" title="Oregon Trail Generation">Oregon Trail Generation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huffington1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huffington1-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span></h2>
<p>Generation X is a relatively smaller demographic cohort "sandwiched" between two larger demographic cohorts, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Boomers" title="Baby Boomers">Baby Boomers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Millennials" title="Millennials">Millennials</a>, although debate regarding exact date range defining makes it difficult to precisely define this cohort’s relative size. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_control_pill" title="Birth control pill">birth control pill</a>, which was introduced in the early 1960s, was a contributing factor to the declining birth rates seen in this generation. In the United States, increased immigration partially offset declining birth rates and contributed to making Generation X an ethnically and culturally diverse demographic cohort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adweek_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Adweek-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohort_confusion_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohort_confusion-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2012 article for the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Center_for_Housing_Studies" title="Joint Center for Housing Studies">Joint Center for Housing Studies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, George Masnick wrote that the "Census counted 82.1 million" Gen Xers in the U.S. The Harvard Center uses 1965 to 1984 to define Gen X so that Boomers, Xers, and <a href="/wiki/Millennials" title="Millennials">Millennials</a> "cover equal 20-year age spans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masnick_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Masnick-27">[27]</a></sup> Masnick concluded that immigration filled in any birth year deficits during low fertility years of the late 1960s and early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masnick_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Masnick-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U.S._Census_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Census-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_children_and_adolescents">As children and adolescents</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strauss%E2%80%93Howe_generational_theory" title="Strauss–Howe generational theory">Demographers William Strauss and Neil Howe</a>, who authored several books on generations including the 1993 book, <i>13th Gen: Abort, Retry, Ignore, Fail?</i>, specifically on Generation X reported that Gen Xers were children at a time when society was less focused on children and more focused on adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13th_Gen_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-13th_Gen-14">[14]</a></sup> Gen Xers were children during a time of increasing <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> rates, with divorce rates doubling in the mid-1960s, before peaking in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adweek_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Adweek-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Strauss and Howe described a cultural shift where the long held societal value of staying together for the sake of the children was replaced with a societal value of parental and individual <a href="/wiki/Self-actualization" title="Self-actualization">self-actualization</a>. Strauss wrote that society “moved from what Leslie Fiedler called a 1950s-era ‘cult of the child’ to what Landon Jones called a 1970s-era ‘cult of the adult’.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13th_Gen_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-13th_Gen-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stauss_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stauss-40">[40]</a></sup> <i>The Generation Map</i>, a report from Australia's McCrindle Research Center writes of Gen X children: "their <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">Boomer</a> parents were the most divorced generation in Australian history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GenMap_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-GenMap-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gen X childhood coincided with the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a>, which Susan Gregory Thomas described in her book <i>In Spite of Everything</i> as confusing and frightening for children in cases where a parent would bring new sexual partners into their home. Thomas also discussed how divorce was different during the Gen X childhood, with the child having a limited or severed relationship with one parent following divorce, often the father, due to differing societal and legal expectations. In the 1970s, only 9 U.S states allowed for <a href="/wiki/Joint_custody" title="Joint custody">joint custody</a> of children, which has since been adopted by all 50 states following a push for joint custody during the mid-1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The time period of the Gen X childhood saw an increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latchkey_child" title="Latchkey child">latchkey children</a>, leading to the terminology of the “latchkey generation” for Generation X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highbeam.com_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-highbeam.com-45">[45]</a></sup> These latchkey children lacked adult supervision in the hours between the end of the school day and when a parent returned home from work in the evening, and for longer periods of time during the summer. Latchkey children became common among all socioeconomic demographics, but were particularly common among middle and upper class children. The higher the educational attainment of the parents, the higher the odds the children of this time would be latchkey children, due to increased maternal participation in the workforce at a time before childcare options outside the home were widely available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highbeam.com_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-highbeam.com-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> McCrindle Research Center described the cohort as "the first to grow up without a large adult presence, with both parents working", stating this led to Gen Xers being more peer-oriented than previous generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GenMap_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-GenMap-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Social_Research_Council" title="Economic and Social Research Council">Economic and Social Research Council</a> described Generation X as "Thatcher's children" because the cohort grew up while <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> was Prime Minister from 1979 to 1990, "a time of social flux and transformation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, Gen Xers spent their formative years of the 1980s during the "hyper-politicized environment of the final years of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US, Generation X was the first cohort to grow up post-integration. They were described in a marketing report by <i>Specialty Retail</i> as the kids who "lived the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movement</a>." They were among the first children to be <a href="/wiki/Desegregation_busing" title="Desegregation busing">bused</a> to attain <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">integration</a> in the public school system. In the 1990s, demographer William Strauss reported Gen Xers were “by any measure the least racist of today's generations”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stauss_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stauss-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retail_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retail-53">[53]</a></sup> In the US, <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a>, which passed in 1972 provided increased athletic opportunities to Gen X girls in the public school setting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, Generation Xers are referred to as "the last Soviet children", as the last children to come of age prior to the downfall of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> in their nation and prior to the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrindle_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-McCrindle-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Politically, in the United States, the Gen X childhood coincided with a time when government funding tended to be diverted away from programs for children and often instead directed toward the elderly population, with cuts to <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> and programs for children and young families, and protection and expansion of <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a> for the elderly population. One in five American children grew up in poverty during this time. These programs for the elderly were not tied to economic need. Congressman <a href="/wiki/David_Durenberger" title="David Durenberger">David Durenberger</a> criticized this political situation, stating that while programs for poor children and for young families were cut, the government provided “free health care to elderly millionaires”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stauss_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stauss-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gen Xers came of age or were children during the <a href="/wiki/Crack_epidemic" title="Crack epidemic">crack epidemic</a>, which disproportionately impacted urban areas and also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> community in the US. Drug turf battles increased violent crime, and <a href="/wiki/Crack_cocaine" title="Crack cocaine">crack addiction</a> impacted communities and families. Between 1984 and 1989, the homicide rate for black males aged 14 to 17 doubled in the US, and the homicide rate for black males aged 18 to 24 increased almost as much. The crack epidemic had a destabilizing impact on families with an increase in the number of children in <a href="/wiki/Foster_care" title="Foster care">foster care</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Generation X was the first cohort to come of age with <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> and are sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/MTV_Generation" title="MTV Generation">MTV Generation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isaksen_2002_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isaksen_2002-59">[59]</a></sup> They experienced the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music videos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>The emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> coincided with Gen X's adolescence, with the disease first clinically observed in the United States in 1981. By 1985, an estimated one to two million Americans were HIV positive. As the virus spread, at a time before effective treatments were available, a public panic ensued. <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">Sex education</a> programs in schools were adapted to address the AIDS epidemic which taught Gen X students that sex could kill you.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Gen Xers were the first children to have access to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_computers" title="Personal computers">computers</a> in their homes and schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GenMap_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-GenMap-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Generally speaking, Gen Xers are the children of the <a href="/wiki/Silent_Generation" title="Silent Generation">Silent Generation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Boomers" title="Baby Boomers">Baby Boomers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordinier_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordinier-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GenMap_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-GenMap-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_young_adults">As young adults</span></h3>
<p>In the 1990s, media pundits and advertisers struggled to define the cohort, typically portraying them as “unfocused twentysomethings”. A <a href="/wiki/MetLife" title="MetLife">MetLife</a> report noted: “media would portray them as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friends_Series" title="Friends Series">Friends</a></i> generation: rather self-involved and perhaps aimless...but fun.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time1_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time1-63">[63]</a></sup> In France, Gen Xers were sometimes referred to as ‘Génération Bof’ because of their tendency to use the word ‘bof ’, which translated into English means ‘whatever”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrindle_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-McCrindle-9">[9]</a></sup> Gen Xers were often portrayed as <a href="/wiki/Apathy" title="Apathy">apathetic</a> or as “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slackers" title="Slackers">slackers</a>”, a stereotype which was initially tied to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Linklater" title="Richard Linklater">Richard Linklater</a>’s comedic and essentially plotless 1991 film <i><a href="/wiki/Slacker_(film)" title="Slacker (film)">Slacker</a></i>. After the film was released, “journalists and critics thought they put a finger on what was different about these young adults in that ‘they were reluctant to grow up’ and ‘disdainful of earnest action’.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time1_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time1-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stereotypes of Gen X young adults also included that they were “bleak, cynical, and disaffected”. Such stereotypes prompted sociological research at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> to study the accuracy of the characterization of Gen X young adults as cynical and disaffected. Using the national <a href="/wiki/General_Social_Survey" title="General Social Survey">General Social Survey</a>, the researchers compared answers to identical survey questions asked of 18-29 year-olds in three different time periods. Additionally, they compared how older adults answered the same survey questions over time. The surveys showed 18-29 year-old Gen Xers did exhibit higher levels of cynicism and disaffection than previous cohorts of 18-29 year-olds surveyed; however, they also found that cynicism and disaffection had increased among all age groups surveyed over time, not just young adults, making this a period effect, not a <a href="/wiki/Cohort_effect" title="Cohort effect">cohort effect</a>. In other words, adults of all ages were more cynical and disaffected in the 1990s, not just Generation X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine published an article titled <i>Living:Proceeding With Caution</i>, which described those in their 20s as aimless and unfocused; however, in 1997, they published an article titled <i>Generation X Reconsidered</i>, which retracted the previously reported negative stereotypes and reported positive accomplishments, citing Gen Xers' tendency to found technology start ups and small businesses as well as Gen Xers' ambition, which research showed was higher among Gen X young adults than older generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time1_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time1-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jury_Expert_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jury_Expert-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time2_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time2-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the 1990s and 2000s progressed, Gen X gained a reputation for <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurship" title="Entrepreneurship">entrepreneurship</a>. In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> dubbed them "Generation 1099", describing them as the "once pitied but now envied group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-employed" title="Self-employed">self-employed</a> workers whose income is reported to the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> not on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W-2_form" title="W-2 form">W-2 form</a>, but on <a href="/wiki/Form_1099" title="Form 1099">Form 1099</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> In 2002, <i>Time</i> magazine published an article titled <i>Gen Xers Aren't Slackers After All</i>, reporting four out of five new businesses were the work of Gen Xers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retail_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Retail-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In 2001, sociologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Males" title="Mike Males">Mike Males</a> reported confidence and optimism common among the cohort saying “surveys consistently find 80% to 90% of Gen Xers self-confident and optimistic.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Males_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Males-71">[71]</a></sup> In August 2001, Males wrote “these young Americans should finally get the recognition they deserve”, praising the cohort and stating that "the permissively raised, universally deplored Generation X is the true 'great generation,' for it has braved a hostile social climate to reverse abysmal trends", describing them as the hardest-working group since the World War II generation, which was dubbed by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Brokaw" title="Tom Brokaw">Tom Brokaw</a> as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Generation" title="The Greatest Generation">The Greatest Generation</a>". He reported Gen Xers' entrepreneurial tendencies helped create the high-tech industry that fueled the 1990s economic recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Males_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Males-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US, Gen Xers were described as the major heroes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11_terrorist_attacks" title="September 11 terrorist attacks">September 11 terrorist attacks</a> by demographer <a href="/wiki/Strauss%E2%80%93Howe_generational_theory" title="Strauss–Howe generational theory">William Strauss</a>. The firefighters and police responding to the attacks were predominantly Generation Xers. Additionally, the leaders of the passenger revolt on <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_93" title="United Airlines Flight 93">United Airlines Flight 93</a> were predominantly Gen Xers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jury_Expert_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jury_Expert-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Demographer <a href="/wiki/Neil_Howe" title="Neil Howe">Neil Howe</a> reported survey data showed Gen Xers were <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">moving in with one another</a> and getting married in increasing numbers following the terrorists attacks, with Gen X survey respondents reporting they no longer wanted to live alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In October 2001, <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Post-Intelligencer" title="Seattle Post-Intelligencer">Seattle Post-Intelligencer</a></i> wrote of Generation Xers: “now they could be facing the most formative events of their lives and their generation”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greensboro_News_%26_Record" title="Greensboro News &amp; Record">Greensboro News &amp; Record</a></i> reported Gen Xers "felt a surge of patriotism since terrorists struck" reporting many were responding to the crisis of the terrorist attacks by giving blood, working for charities, donating to charities, and by joining the military to fight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_War_on_Terror" title="The War on Terror">The War on Terror</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <i>The Jury Expert</i>, a puplication of The American Society of Trial Consultants, reported: “Gen X members responded to the terrorist attacks with bursts of patriotism and national fervor that surprised even themselves”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jury_Expert_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jury_Expert-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In entertainment, Gen Xers were responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> movement of the 1990s and 2000s, and Gen Xers were largely responsible for the “<a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">Indie Film</a>” movement of the 1990s, both as young directors and in large part as the movie audiences fueling demand for such films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time2_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time2-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In cinema, directors <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">Kevin Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Coppola" title="Sofia Coppola">Sofia Coppola</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Singleton" title="John Singleton">John Singleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spike_Jonze" title="Spike Jonze">Spike Jonze</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Fincher" title="David Fincher">David Fincher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soderbergh" title="Steven Soderbergh">Steven Soderbergh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Linklater" title="Richard Linklater">Richard Linklater</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> have been called Generation X filmmakers. Smith is most known for his <a href="/wiki/View_Askewniverse" title="View Askewniverse">View Askewniverse</a> films, the flagship film being <i><a href="/wiki/Clerks" title="Clerks">Clerks</a></i>, which is set in New Jersey circa 1994, and focuses on two convenience-store clerks in their twenties. Linklater's <i><a href="/wiki/Slacker_(film)" title="Slacker (film)">Slacker</a></i> similarly explores young adult characters who were interested in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophizing" title="Philosophizing">philosophizing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> While not a member of Gen X himself, director <a href="/wiki/John_Hughes_(filmmaker)" title="John Hughes (filmmaker)">John Hughes</a> has been recognized as having created a series of classic films with Gen X characters which "an entire generation took ownership of" with films such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Breakfast_Club" title="The Breakfast Club">The Breakfast Club</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Candles" title="Sixteen Candles">Sixteen Candles</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Weird_Science_(film)" title="Weird Science (film)">Weird Science</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ferris_Bueller%27s_Day_Off" title="Ferris Bueller's Day Off">Ferris Bueller's Day Off</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_midlife">In midlife</span></h3>
<p>Guides regarding managing multiple generations in the workforce describe Gen Xers as: independent, resourceful, self-managing, adaptable, cynical, pragmatic, skeptical of authority, and as seeking a work life balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetLife_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-MetLife-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> In a 2007 article published in the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a>, demographers Strauss &amp; Howe wrote of Generation X; “They are already the greatest entrepreneurial generation in U.S. history; their high-tech savvy and marketplace resilience have helped America prosper in the era of globalization.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2008 book, <i>X Saves the World: How Generation X Got the Shaft but Can Still Keep Everything from Sucking</i>, author <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Gordinier" title="Jeff Gordinier">Jeff Gordinier</a> describes Generation X as a "<a href="/wiki/Dark_horse" title="Dark horse">dark horse</a> demographic" which “doesn’t seek the limelight”. Gordiner cited examples of Gen Xers' contributions to society such as: <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>, arguing if Boomers had created them, “we’d never hear the end of it”. In the book, Gordinier contrasts Gen Xers to Baby Boomers, saying Boomers tend to trumpet their accomplishments more than Gen Xers do, creating what he describes as “elaborate mythologies” around their achievements. Gordiner cites <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> as an example, while Gen Xers, he argues, are more likely to “just quietly do their thing”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordinier_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gordinier-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, survey analysis from the <i>Longitudinal Study of American Youth</i> found Gen Xers to be “balanced active and happy” in midlife (between ages of 30 and 50). The Longitudinal Study of Youth is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIH" title="NIH">NIH</a>-<a href="/wiki/National_Institute_on_Aging" title="National Institute on Aging">NIA</a> funded study by the University of Michigan which has been studying Generation X since 1987, Asking survey questions such as “Thinking about all aspects of your life, how happy are you? If zero means that you are very unhappy and 10 means that you are very happy, please rate your happiness.” LSA reported that “mean level of happiness was 7.5 and the median (middle score) was 8. Only four percent of Generation X adults indicated a great deal of unhappiness (a score of three or lower). Twenty-nine percent of Generation X adults were very happy with a score of 9 or 10 on the scale.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSA_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-LSA-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>In terms of advocating for their children in the educational setting, demographer <a href="/wiki/Neil_Howe" title="Neil Howe">Neil Howe</a> describes Gen X parents as distinct from Baby Boomer parents. Howe argues that Gen Xers are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helicopter_parents" title="Helicopter parents">helicopter parents</a>, which Howe describes as a parenting style of Boomer parents of Millennials. Howe described Gen Xers instead as “stealth fighter parents”, due to the tendency of Gen X parents to let minor issues go and to not hover over their children in the educational setting, but to intervene forcefully and swiftly in the event of more serious issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_National_and_Community_Service" title="Corporation for National and Community Service">Corporation for National and Community Service</a> ranked Gen X volunteer rates in the U.S. at "29.4% per year", the highest compared with other generations. The rankings were based on a three-year moving average between 2009 and 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, a 2016 study of over 2,500 office workers conducted by <a href="/wiki/Workfront" title="Workfront">Workfront</a> found that survey respondents of all ages selected those from Generation X as the hardest-working employees in today’s workforce (chosen by 60%). Gen X was also ranked highest among fellow workers for having the strongest work ethic (chosen by 59.5%), being the most helpful (55.4%), the most skilled (54.5%), and the best troubleshooters/problem solvers (41.6%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, a global consumer insights project from <a href="/wiki/Viacom_International_Media_Networks" title="Viacom International Media Networks">Viacom International Media Networks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viacom" title="Viacom">Viacom</a>, based on over 12,000 respondents across 21 countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> reported on Gen X’s unconventional approach to sex, friendship and family,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> their desire for flexibility and fulfillment at work<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and the absence of <a href="/wiki/Midlife_crisis" title="Midlife crisis">midlife crisis</a> for Gen Xers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> The project also included a 20min documentary titled <i>Gen X Today</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pew_Research" title="Pew Research">Pew Research</a>, a nonpartisan American think tank, describes Generation X as intermediary between Baby Boomers and Millennials on multiple factors such as attitudes on political or social issues, educational attainment, and social media use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h2>
<p>Studies done by <a href="/wiki/The_Pew_Charitable_Trusts" title="The Pew Charitable Trusts">the Pew Charitable Trusts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">Brookings Institution</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Heritage_Foundation" title="The Heritage Foundation">the Heritage Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Urban_Institute" title="Urban Institute">Urban Institute</a> challenged the notion that each generation will be better off than the one that preceded it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isabel_Sawhill.2C_Ph.D_2007_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isabel_Sawhill.2C_Ph.D_2007-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>A report titled <i>Economic Mobility: Is the American Dream Alive and Well?</i> focused on the income of males 30–39 in 2004 (those born April 1964 – March 1974). The study was released on May 25, 2007 and emphasized that this generation's men made less (by 12%) than their fathers had at that same age in 1974, thus reversing a historical trend. It concluded that per year increases in household income generated by fathers/sons have slowed (from an average of 0.9% to 0.3%), barely keeping pace with inflation. "Family incomes have risen though (over the period 1947 to 2005) because more women have gone to work, supporting the incomes of men, by adding a second earner to the family. And as with male income, the trend is downward".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isabel_Sawhill.2C_Ph.D_2007_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-Isabel_Sawhill.2C_Ph.D_2007-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Generation Flux</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychographic" title="Psychographic">psychographic</a> designation coined by <i><a href="/wiki/Fast_Company_(magazine)" title="Fast Company (magazine)">Fast Company</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_States" title="Economy of the United States">American employees</a> who need to make several changes in <a href="/wiki/Career" title="Career">career</a> throughout their working lives because of the chaotic nature of the job market following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">Financial crisis of 2007–08</a>. Those in "Generation Flux" have birth years in the ranges of Gen X and Millennials.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entrepreneurship">Entrepreneurship</span></h3>
<p>According to authors Michael Hais and Morley Winograd:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Small businesses and the entrepreneurial spirit that Gen Xers embody have become one of the most popular institutions in America. There's been a recent shift in consumer behavior and Gen Xers will join the “idealist generation” in encouraging the celebration of individual effort and business risk-taking. As a result, Xers will spark a renaissance of <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurship" title="Entrepreneurship">entrepreneurship</a> in economic life, even as overall confidence in economic institutions declines. Customers, and their needs and wants (including Millennials) will become the North Star for an entire new generation of entrepreneurs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A 2015 study by <a href="/wiki/Sage_Group" title="Sage Group">Sage Group</a> reports Gen Xers "dominate the playing field" with respect to founding <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startups</a> in the United States and Canada, with Gen Xers launching the majority (55%) of all new businesses in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
