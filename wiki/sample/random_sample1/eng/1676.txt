<p><b>YBP 1194</b> is a <a href="/wiki/G-type_main-sequence_star" title="G-type main-sequence star">G-type main-sequence star</a>, class G5V, in the <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">open cluster</a> <a href="/wiki/Messier_67" title="Messier 67">M67</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Cancer_(constellation)" title="Cancer (constellation)">Cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMBAD_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIMBAD-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-canada-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the best <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_twin" title="Solar twin">solar twin</a> found to date, having the near temperature and mass as our <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. YBP 1194 has a higher <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a> than the Sun. YBP 1194 is .5 billion years younger than the Sun at an age of 4.2 billion years old, but due to the distance, the error bar is high at ±1.6 billion years. On December 19, 2013, it was announced to have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> with a period of 6.9 days, and a high eccentricity of 0.24 with a mass of <span class="nowrap">0.34</span> <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass"><i>M</i><sub>J</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-canada-3">[3]</a></sup>YBP 1194 is about 2,772 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-years" title="Light-years">light-years</a> (850 parsecs) from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brucalassi2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brucalassi2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> YBP 1194 is packed in a small cluster, M67-NGC 2682, with a radius 10 ly, with over 500 other stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For comparison the Sun has 17 stars at a distance of 10 ly and about 134 stars at a distance of 20 ly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exoplanets">Exoplanets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=YBP_1194&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Exoplanets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exoplanet YBP 1194 b was discovered in January 2014 by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">European Southern Observatory</a> (ESO) when three new planets were discovered in the M67 cluster, one of them orbiting YPB 1194, showing that open star clusters are more likely to have planets orbiting them than originally thought. The exoplanet is prominently about 100 times the mass of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Exoplanet YBP 1194 b when compared to the Sun-Earth, orbits the star about 87 million miles closer than planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> of YPB 1194 b is 0.24, close to <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> of 0.24905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-canada-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-9">[9]</a></sup> The composition of these planets is currently, unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-canada-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>To date no solar twin with an exact match as that of the Sun has been found, however, there are some stars that come very close to being identical to that of the Sun, and are such considered solar twins by the majority of the public. An exact solar twin would be a G2V star with a 5,778K temperature, be 4.6 billion years old, with the correct metallicity and a 0.1% <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a> variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Stars with an age of 4.6 billion years are at the most stable state. Proper metallicity and size are also very important to low luminosity variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
