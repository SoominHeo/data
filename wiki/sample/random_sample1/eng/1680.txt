<p>"<b>Yesterday</b>" is a song by the English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> (credited to <a href="/wiki/Lennon%E2%80%93McCartney" title="Lennon–McCartney">Lennon–McCartney</a>), and first released on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Help!_(album)" title="Help! (album)">Help!</a></i> in the United Kingdom in August 1965.</p>
<p>"Yesterday", with the B-side "<a href="/wiki/Act_Naturally" title="Act Naturally">Act Naturally</a>", was released as a single in the United States in September 1965. While it topped the American chart in October the song also hit the British top 10 in a cover version by <a href="/wiki/Matt_Monro" title="Matt Monro">Matt Monro</a>. The song also appeared on the UK EP "Yesterday" in March 1966 and the Beatles' US album <i><a href="/wiki/Yesterday_and_Today" title="Yesterday and Today">Yesterday and Today</a></i>, released in June 1966.</p>
<p>McCartney's vocal and <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a>, together with a <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a>, essentially made for the first solo performance of the band. It remains popular today with more than 2,200 <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover versions</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2009-2">[2]</a></sup> and is one of the most covered songs in the history of recorded music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[note 1]</a></sup> "Yesterday" was voted the best song of the 20th century in a 1999 <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> poll of music experts and listeners and was also voted the No. 1 pop song of all time by MTV and <i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine the following year. In 1997, the song was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Hall_of_Fame" title="Grammy Hall of Fame">Grammy Hall of Fame</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music_Incorporated" title="Broadcast Music Incorporated">Broadcast Music Incorporated</a> (BMI) asserts that it was performed over seven million times in the 20th century alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Yesterday" is a melancholy <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> about the break-up of a relationship. The singer laments for yesterday when he and his love were together, before she left because of something he said. McCartney is the only member of the Beatles to appear on the recording. The final recording was so different from other works by the Beatles that the band members vetoed the release of the song as a single in the United Kingdom, although other artists were quick to do so. It was issued as a single in the US in September 1965 and later released as a single in the UK in 1976.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to biographers of McCartney and the Beatles, McCartney composed the entire <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">melody</a> in a dream one night in his room at the Wimpole Street home of his then girlfriend <a href="/wiki/Jane_Asher" title="Jane Asher">Jane Asher</a> and her family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurner200583_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurner200583-6">[5]</a></sup> Upon waking, he hurried to a piano and played the tune to avoid forgetting it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCartney's initial concern was that he had subconsciously <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarised</a> someone else's work (known as <a href="/wiki/Cryptomnesia" title="Cryptomnesia">cryptomnesia</a>). As he put it, "For about a month I went round to people in the music business and asked them whether they had ever heard it before. Eventually it became like handing something in to the police. I thought if no one claimed it after a few weeks then I could have it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon being convinced that he had not robbed anyone of their melody, McCartney began writing <a href="/wiki/Lyrics" title="Lyrics">lyrics</a> to suit it. As Lennon and McCartney were known to do at the time, a substitute working lyric, titled "Scrambled Eggs" (the working opening verse was "Scrambled eggs/Oh my baby how I love your legs/Not as much as I love scrambled eggs"), was used for the song until something more suitable was written. In his biography, <i><a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney:_Many_Years_from_Now" title="Paul McCartney: Many Years from Now">Paul McCartney: Many Years from Now</a></i>, McCartney recalled: "So first of all I checked this melody out, and people said to me, 'No, it's lovely, and I'm sure it's all yours.' It took me a little while to allow myself to claim it, but then like a prospector I finally staked my claim; stuck a little sign on it and said, 'Okay, it's mine!' It had no words. I used to call it 'Scrambled Eggs'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1997201.E2.80.93202_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1997201.E2.80.93202-8">[7]</a></sup> The original song, Scrambled Eggs, was written to hold the music and phrasing in place. It was performed by Paul McCartney and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a> on <a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Jimmy_Fallon" title="The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon">The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon</a>, and has been recorded and released by the Brittles, a Beatles-pastiche band.</p>
<p>During the shooting of <i><a href="/wiki/Help!_(film)" title="Help! (film)">Help!</a></i>, a piano was placed on one of the stages where filming was being conducted and McCartney took advantage of this opportunity to tinker with the song. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lester" title="Richard Lester">Richard Lester</a>, the director, was eventually greatly annoyed by this and lost his temper, telling McCartney to finish writing the song or he would have the piano removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1997203_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1997203-9">[8]</a></sup> The patience of the other Beatles was also tested by McCartney's work in progress, <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> summed this up when he said: "Blimey, he's always talking about that song. You'd think he was Beethoven or somebody!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColeman199511_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColeman199511-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCartney originally claimed he had written "Yesterday" during the Beatles' tour of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1964; however, the song was not released until the summer of 1965. During the intervening time, the Beatles released two albums, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hard_Day%27s_Night_(album)" title="A Hard Day's Night (album)">A Hard Day's Night</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Beatles_for_Sale" title="Beatles for Sale">Beatles for Sale</a></i>, both of which could have included "Yesterday". Although McCartney has never elaborated on his claims, a delay may have been due to a disagreement between McCartney and <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a> regarding the song's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrangement_(music)" title="Arrangement (music)">arrangement</a>, or the opinion of the other Beatles who felt it did not suit their image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2005464.E2.80.93465-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lennon later indicated that the song had been around for a while before:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"The song was around for months and months before we finally completed it. Every time we got together to write songs for a recording session, this one would come up. We almost had it finished. Paul wrote nearly all of it, but we just couldn't find the right title. We called it 'Scrambled Eggs' and it became a joke between us. We made up our minds that only a one-word title would suit, we just couldn't find the right one. Then one morning Paul woke up and the song and the title were both there, completed. I was sorry in a way, we'd had so many laughs about it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHammond2001_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHammond2001-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>McCartney said the breakthrough with the lyrics came during a trip to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> in May 1965:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"I remember mulling over the tune 'Yesterday', and suddenly getting these little one-word openings to the verse. I started to develop the idea ... da-da da, yes-ter-day, sud-den-ly, fun-il-ly, mer-il-ly and Yes-ter-day, that's good. All my troubles seemed so far away. It's easy to rhyme those a's: say, nay, today, away, play, stay, there's a lot of rhymes and those fall in quite easily, so I gradually pieced it together from that journey. Sud-den-ly, and 'b' again, another easy rhyme: e, me, tree, flea, we, and I had the basis of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1997204_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1997204-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 27 May 1965, McCartney and Asher flew to <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> for a holiday in <a href="/wiki/Albufeira" title="Albufeira">Albufeira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, and he borrowed an acoustic guitar from <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Welch" title="Bruce Welch">Bruce Welch</a>, in whose house they were staying, and completed the work on "Yesterday".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1997204.E2.80.93205_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1997204.E2.80.93205-13">[12]</a></sup> The song was offered as a demo to <a href="/wiki/Chris_Farlowe" title="Chris Farlowe">Chris Farlowe</a> before the Beatles recorded it, but he turned it down as he considered it "too soft".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENapier-Bell2001100_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENapier-Bell2001100-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a March 1967 interview with <a href="/wiki/Brian_Matthew" title="Brian Matthew">Brian Matthew</a>, McCartney claimed that Lennon came up with the song's title:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Brian: "Give us the inside story on the song 'Yesterday.'"</p>
<p>John: "Ah well, this is John saying I don't know anything about that one. I'll hand you over to Paul."</p>
<p>Paul: "[laughs] This is Paul, taking up the story in a holiday villa in <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>. Strumming away on a medieval guitar, I thought [sings] 'Scrambled Egg.' But I never could finish it, and eventually I took it back in. With the ancient wisdom of the east, John came out with [sings] 'Yesterday'."</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resemblance_to_other_songs">Resemblance to other songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Resemblance to other songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Ian Hammond speculated that McCartney subconsciously based "Yesterday" on <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>' version of Hoagy Carmichael's "<a href="/wiki/Georgia_on_My_Mind" title="Georgia on My Mind">Georgia on My Mind</a>", but closed his article by saying that despite the similarities "Yesterday" is a "completely original and individual [work]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHammond2001_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHammond2001-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2003, British <a href="/wiki/Musicology" title="Musicology">musicologists</a> stumbled upon superficial similarities between the lyric and rhyming schemes of "Yesterday" and <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Laine" title="Frankie Laine">Frankie Laine</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Answer_Me,_My_Love" title="Answer Me, My Love">Answer Me, My Love</a>" (originally a German song by Gerhard Winkler and Fred Rauch called <i>Mütterlein</i>, it was a No.1 hit for Laine on the UK charts in 1953 as "Answer Me, O Lord"), leading to speculation that McCartney had been influenced by the song. McCartney's publicists denied any resemblance between "Answer Me, My Love" and "Yesterday".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News2003_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News2003-16">[15]</a></sup> "Yesterday" begins with the lines: "Yesterday, all my troubles seemed so far away. Now it looks as though they're here to stay." In its second stanza, "Answer Me, My Love" has the lines: "You were mine yesterday. I believed that love was here to stay. Won't you tell me where I've gone astray".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_structure">Composition and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ostensibly simple, featuring only McCartney playing an <a href="/wiki/Epiphone_Texan" title="Epiphone Texan">Epiphone Texan</a> <a href="/wiki/Steel-string_acoustic_guitar" title="Steel-string acoustic guitar">steel-string acoustic guitar</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEverett199912_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEverett199912-17">[16]</a></sup> backed by a <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a> in one of the Beatles' first use of session musicians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEverett199913_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEverett199913-18">[17]</a></sup> "Yesterday" has two contrasting sections, differing in melody and rhythm, producing a sense of disjunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEverett199915_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEverett199915-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first section ("Yesterday, all my troubles seemed so far away ...") opens with an F <a href="/wiki/Chord_(music)" title="Chord (music)">chord</a> (the 3rd of the chord is omitted), then moving to Em<sub>7</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack-20">[19]</a></sup> before proceeding to A<sub>7</sub> and then to D-minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup> In this sense, the opening chord is a decoy; as musicologist <a href="/wiki/Alan_W._Pollack" title="Alan W. Pollack">Alan Pollack</a> points out, the home key (F-major) has little time to establish itself before "heading towards the relative D-minor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup> He points out that this diversion is a compositional device commonly used by Lennon and McCartney, which he describes as "delayed gratification".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second section ("Why she had to go I don't know ...") is, according to Pollack, less musically surprising on paper than it sounds. Starting with Em<sub>7</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack-20">[19]</a></sup> the harmonic progression quickly moves through the A-major, D-minor, and (closer to F-major) B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>, before resolving back to F-major, and at the end of this, McCartney holds F while the strings descend to resolve to the home key to introduce the restatement of the first section, before a brief hummed closing phrase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pollack described the scoring as "truly inspired", citing it as an example of "[Lennon &amp; McCartney's] flair for creating stylistic hybrids";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup> in particular, he praises the "ironic tension drawn between the schmaltzy content of what is played by the quartet and the restrained, spare nature of the medium in which it is played."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tonic_(music)" title="Tonic (music)">tonic key</a> of the song is F major (although, since McCartney tuned his guitar down a whole step, he was playing the chords as if it were in G), where the song begins before veering off into the key of D minor. It is this frequent use of the minor, and the ii-V7 <a href="/wiki/Chord_progression" title="Chord progression">chord progression</a> (Em and A<sub>7</sub> chords in this case) leading into it, that gives the song its melancholy aura. The A<sub>7</sub> chord is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_dominant" title="Secondary dominant">secondary dominant</a>, specifically a V/vi chord. The G<sub>7</sub> chord in the bridge is another secondary dominant, in this case a V/V chord, but rather than <a href="/wiki/Resolution_(music)" title="Resolution (music)">resolve</a> it to the expected chord, as with the A<sub>7</sub> to Dm in the verse, McCartney instead follows it with the IV chord, a B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>. This motion creates a descending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromatic" title="Chromatic">chromatic</a> line of C–B–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>–A to accompany the title lyric.</p>
<p>The string arrangement reinforces the song's air of sadness, in the groaning cello line that connects the two halves of the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(music)" title="Bridge (music)">bridge</a>, notably the "<a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blue</a>" seventh in the second bridge pass (the E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> played after the vocal line, "I don't know / she wouldn't say") and in the descending run by the <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a> that segues the bridge back into the verses, mimicked by McCartney's vocal on the second pass of the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECahill2005162_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECahill2005162-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-21">[20]</a></sup> This viola line, the "blue" cello phrase, the high A sustained by the violin over the final verse and the minimal use of vibrato are elements of the string arrangement attributable to McCartney rather than George Martin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the song was performed on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i>, it was done in the above-mentioned key of F, with McCartney as the only Beatle to perform, and the studio orchestra providing the string accompaniment. However, all of the Beatles played in a G-major version which was used in the Tokyo concerts during their 1966 tours.</p>
<p>When McCartney appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Howard_Stern_Show" title="The Howard Stern Show">The Howard Stern Show</a></i>, he stated that he owns the original lyrics to "Yesterday" written on the back of an <a href="/wiki/Envelope" title="Envelope">envelope</a>. McCartney later performed the original "Scrambled Eggs" version of the song, plus additional new lyrics, with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Jimmy_Fallon" title="Late Night with Jimmy Fallon">Late Night with Jimmy Fallon</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The track was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> on 14 June 1965, immediately following the taping of "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Down" title="I'm Down">I'm Down</a>", and four days before McCartney's 23rd birthday. There are conflicting accounts of how the song was recorded, the most quoted one being that McCartney recorded the song by himself, without bothering to involve the other band members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrtiz2005_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrtiz2005-25">[24]</a></sup> Alternative sources, however, state that McCartney and the other Beatles tried a variety of instruments, including drums and an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a>, and that <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a> later persuaded them to allow McCartney to play his <a href="/wiki/Epiphone_Texan" title="Epiphone Texan">Epiphone Texan</a> <a href="/wiki/Steel-string_acoustic_guitar" title="Steel-string acoustic guitar">steel-string acoustic guitar</a>, later on editing-in a string quartet for backup. Regardless, none of the other band members were included in the final recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallick2000_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallick2000-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006-27">[26]</a></sup> However, the song was played with the other members of the band in concert during 1966, in <a href="/wiki/G_major" title="G major">G major</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/F_major" title="F major">F major</a>.</p>
<p>McCartney performed two <a href="/wiki/Take" title="Take">takes</a> of "Yesterday" on 14 June 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn199410_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn199410-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198859_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198859-29">[28]</a></sup> Take 2 was deemed better and used as the <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" title="Audio mastering">master</a> take. On 17 June, an additional vocal track by McCartney and a string quartet were <a href="/wiki/Overdubbing" title="Overdubbing">overdubbed</a> on take 2 and that version was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198859_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198859-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Take 1, without the string overdub, was later released on the <i><a href="/wiki/Anthology_2" title="Anthology 2">Anthology 2</a></i> compilation. On take 1, McCartney can be heard giving chord changes to Harrison before starting, but while Harrison does not appear to actually play, he was most certainly present because his voice is captured on the session tapes. Take 2 had two lines transposed from the first take: "There's a shadow hanging over me"/"I'm not half the man I used to be",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles20002.E2.80.9310_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles20002.E2.80.9310-30">[29]</a></sup> though it seems clear that their order in take 2 was the correct one, because McCartney can be heard, in take 1, suppressing a laugh at his mistake.</p>
<p>In 2006, just before the album <i><a href="/wiki/Love_(Beatles_album)" title="Love (Beatles album)">Love</a></i> was released, George Martin elaborated on the recording set-up of the song:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERees2006_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERees2006-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Paul played his guitar and sang it live, a mic on the guitar and mic on the voice. But, of course, the voice comes on to the guitar mic and the guitar comes on to the voice mic. So there's leakage there. Then I said I'd do a string quartet. The musicians objected to playing with headphones, so I gave them Paul's voice and guitar on two speakers either side of their microphones. So there's leakage of Paul's guitar and voice on the string tracks."</p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beatles-singles-yesterday-uk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="170" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e4/Beatles-singles-yesterday-uk.jpg" width="170"/></a>

<p>Concerning the debate on how the song should be released, Martin later said:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">[Yesterday] wasn't really a Beatles record and I discussed this with <a href="/wiki/Brian_Epstein" title="Brian Epstein">Brian Epstein</a>: 'You know this is Paul's song ... shall we call it Paul McCartney?' He said 'No, whatever we do we are not splitting up the Beatles.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000175_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000175-34">[33]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Since "Yesterday" was unlike the Beatles' previous work and did not fit in with their image, and was essentially a solo recording, the Beatles refused to permit the release of a single in the United Kingdom. This did not prevent <a href="/wiki/Matt_Monro" title="Matt Monro">Matt Monro</a> from recording the first of many cover versions of "Yesterday". His version made it into the top ten in the UK charts soon after its release in the autumn of 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Beatles' influence over their US record label, <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol</a>, was not as strong as it was over <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> in Britain. A single was released in the US, pairing "Yesterday" with "<a href="/wiki/Act_Naturally" title="Act Naturally">Act Naturally</a>", a track which featured vocals by Starr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallgren198243_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallgren198243-35">[34]</a></sup> The single was released on 13 September 1965 and topped the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart for four weeks, beginning on 9 October. The song spent a total of 11 weeks on the chart, selling a million copies within five weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2004_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2004-36">[35]</a></sup> The single was also number one for three weeks on the US <i><a href="/wiki/Cashbox_(magazine)" title="Cashbox (magazine)">Cash Box</a></i> pop singles chart the same year.</p>
<p>"Yesterday" was the fifth of six number one singles in a row on the American charts, a record at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> The other singles were "<a href="/wiki/I_Feel_Fine" title="I Feel Fine">I Feel Fine</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eight_Days_a_Week_(song)" title="Eight Days a Week (song)">Eight Days a Week</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Ticket_to_Ride" title="Ticket to Ride">Ticket to Ride</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Help!_(song)" title="Help! (song)">Help!</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/We_Can_Work_It_Out" title="We Can Work It Out">We Can Work It Out</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallgren198238.E2.80.9345_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallgren198238.E2.80.9345-38">[37]</a></sup> "Yesterday" also marked a turning point in who wrote number one singles for the group. Lennon wrote five through "Help!", whereas afterwards McCartney wrote eight starting with "Yesterday". On 4 March 1966, "Yesterday" was released as an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> in the UK, joined by "<a href="/wiki/Act_Naturally" title="Act Naturally">Act Naturally</a>" on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-side" title="A-side">A-side</a> with "<a href="/wiki/You_Like_Me_Too_Much" title="You Like Me Too Much">You Like Me Too Much</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Only_Love" title="It's Only Love">It's Only Love</a>" on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-side" title="B-side">B-side</a>. By 12 March, it had begun its run on the charts. On 26 March 1966, the EP went to number one, a position it held for two months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2004_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2004-36">[35]</a></sup> Later that same year, "Yesterday" was included as the title track for the US-only <i><a href="/wiki/Yesterday_and_Today" title="Yesterday and Today">Yesterday and Today</a></i> album, which was originally packaged in the "butcher sleeve".</p>
<p>The "Yesterday"/"Act Naturally" single was reissued on Apple Records in 1971, with "Act Naturally" having the "full apple" side and "Yesterday" on the "sliced apple" side. At the time of its original US release in September 1965, "Act Naturally" was the intended A-side and has always been listed as such in Capitol's files.</p>
<p>Ten years later on 8 March 1976, "Yesterday" was released by <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> as a single in the UK, featuring "<a href="/wiki/I_Should_Have_Known_Better" title="I Should Have Known Better">I Should Have Known Better</a>" on the B-side. Entering the charts on 13 March, the single stayed there for seven weeks, peaking at No. 8 (however, by this time the song had been featured on no less than three top 5 albums and an EP which topped the charts). The release came about due to the expiration of the Beatles' contract with <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>, Parlophone's parent, which allowed them to repackage the Beatles' recordings as they wished. EMI re-issued all 22 of the Beatles' UK singles, plus "Yesterday," on the same day, leading to six of them hitting the UK charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECross2004_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECross2004-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, a version of the song was included on the album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_(The_Beatles_album)" title="Love (The Beatles album)">Love</a></i>. The version begins with the <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a> intro from the song "<a href="/wiki/Blackbird_(Beatles_song)" title="Blackbird (Beatles song)">Blackbird</a>" only with "Blackbird" transposed down a whole step to F major from its original key G to transition smoothly into "Yesterday".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yesterday_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Yesterday" is one of the most recorded songs in the history of popular music; its entry in <i>Guinness World Records</i> states that, by January 1986, 1,600 cover versions had been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2009-2">[2]</a></sup> After <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muzak_Holdings" title="Muzak Holdings">Muzak</a> switched in the 1990s to programs based on commercial recordings, Muzak's inventory grew to include about 500 "Yesterday" covers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOwen2006_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOwen2006-39">[38]</a></sup> At the 2006 Grammy Awards, McCartney performed the song live as a mash-up with <a href="/wiki/Linkin_Park" title="Linkin Park">Linkin Park</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Numb/Encore" title="Numb/Encore">Numb/Encore</a>".</p>
<p>"Yesterday" won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello_Award" title="Ivor Novello Award">Ivor Novello Award</a> for 'Outstanding Song of 1965', and came second for 'Most Performed Work of the Year', losing out to the Lennon/McCartney composition, "<a href="/wiki/Michelle_(song)" title="Michelle (song)">Michelle</a>". The song has received its fair share of acclaim in recent times as well, ranking 13th on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>'</i>s 2004 list "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Songs_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Songs of All Time">The 500 Greatest Songs of All Time</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2007_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2007-40">[39]</a></sup> and fourth on the magazine's list "The Beatles 100 Greatest Songs" (compiled in 2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2010_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2010-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone100_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone100-42">[41]</a></sup> In 1999, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music_Incorporated" title="Broadcast Music Incorporated">Broadcast Music Incorporated</a> (BMI) placed "Yesterday" third on their list of songs of the 20th century most performed on American radio and television, with approximately seven million performances. "Yesterday" was surpassed only by <a href="/wiki/The_Association" title="The Association">The Association</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Never_My_Love" title="Never My Love">Never My Love</a>" and <a href="/wiki/The_Righteous_Brothers" title="The Righteous Brothers">The Righteous Brothers</a>' "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/You%27ve_Lost_That_Loving_Feeling" title="You've Lost That Loving Feeling">You've Lost That Loving Feeling</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBMI2007_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBMI2007-43">[42]</a></sup> "Yesterday" was voted Best Song of the 20th century in a 1999 BBC Radio 2 poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News1999_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News1999-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Hall_of_Fame_Award" title="Grammy Hall of Fame Award">Grammy Hall of Fame</a> in 1997. Although the song was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Song of the Year</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1966_Grammy_Awards" title="1966 Grammy Awards">1966 Grammy Awards</a>, it ultimately lost to <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Shadow_of_Your_Smile" title="The Shadow of Your Smile">The Shadow of Your Smile</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with one of McCartney's influences, <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Berry" title="Chuck Berry">Chuck Berry</a> said that "Yesterday" was the song that he wished that he had written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Yesterday", however, has also been criticised for being mundane and mawkish; <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> had a marked dislike for the song, stating that "If you go into the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>, you can find a lot better than that. There are millions of songs like 'Michelle' and 'Yesterday' written in <a href="/wiki/Tin_Pan_Alley" title="Tin Pan Alley">Tin Pan Alley</a>". Ironically, Dylan ultimately recorded his own version of "Yesterday" four years later, but it was never released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallick2000_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallick2000-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly before his death in 1980, Lennon commented that "Although the lyrics don't resolve into any sense, they're good lines. They certainly work, you know what I mean? They're good—but if you read the whole song, it doesn't say anything" and added the song was "beautiful — and I never wished I'd written it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Lennon made reference to "Yesterday" in his song "<a href="/wiki/How_Do_You_Sleep%3F_(John_Lennon_song)" title="How Do You Sleep? (John Lennon song)">How Do You Sleep?</a>" on his 1971 album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imagine_(album)" title="Imagine (album)">Imagine</a></i>. The song appears to attack McCartney with the line "The only thing you done was yesterday, but since you've gone you're just another day," a reference to McCartney's recent hit "<a href="/wiki/Another_Day_(Paul_McCartney_song)" title="Another Day (Paul McCartney song)">Another Day</a>."</p>
<p>In 2012, it was reported by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> that "Yesterday" remained the 4th most successful song of all-time in terms of royalties paid, having amassed a total of £19.5 million in payments <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.didyouwatchit.com/bbc4/bbc4-the-worlds-richest-songs/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>.</p>
