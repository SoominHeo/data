<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Y-chromosomal Adam</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human_genetics" title="Human genetics">human genetics</a>, the <b>Y-chromosomal most recent common ancestor</b> (<b>Y-MRCA</b>, informally known as <b>Y-chromosomal Adam</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> (MRCA) from whom all <a href="/wiki/Present_day" title="Present day">currently living</a> people are descended <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineally</a>. The term Y-MRCA reflects the fact that the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosomes</a> of all currently living males are directly derived from the Y chromosome of this remote ancestor. The analogous concept of the matrilineal most recent common ancestor is known as "<a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_Eve" title="Mitochondrial Eve">Mitochondrial Eve</a>" (mt-MRCA, named for the matrilineal transmission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a>), the most recent woman from whom all living humans are descended <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineally</a>. As with "Mitochondrial Eve", the title of "Y-chromosomal Adam" is not permanently fixed to a single individual, but can advance over the course of human history as paternal lineages become extinct.</p>
<p>Estimates of the time when Y-MRCA lived have also shifted as modern knowledge of human ancestry changes. In 2013, the discovery of a previously unknown Y-chromosomal haplogroup was announced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendez13_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez13-1">[1]</a></sup> which resulted in a slight adjustment of the estimated age of the human Y-MRCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature.com-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>By definition, it is not necessary that the Y-MRCA and the mt-MRCA should have lived at the same time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ancestors_tale_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ancestors_tale-3">[3]</a></sup> While estimates as of 2014 suggested the possibility that the two individuals may well have been roughly contemporaneous (albeit with uncertainties ranging in the tens of thousands of years),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cann_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cann-4">[4]</a></sup> the discovery of archaic Y-haplogroup has pushed back the estimated age of the Y-MRCA beyond the most likely age of the mt-MRCA. As of 2015, estimates of the age of the Y-MRCA range around 200,000 to 300,000 years ago, roughly consistent with the emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">anatomically modern humans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bottleneck_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bottleneck-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Y-chromosomal data taken from a <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">neanderthal</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sidr%C3%B3n_Cave" title="Sidrón Cave">El Sidrón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> produced a Y-T-MRCA of 588,000 years ago for neanderthal and <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> patrilineages, dubbed <i>ante</i> Adam and 275,000 years ago for Y-MRCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendez2016_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez2016-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">MRCA</a></div>
<p>The Y-chromosomal most recent common ancestor is the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y-chromosome" title="Y-chromosome">Y-chromosomes</a> found in currently living human males.</p>
<p>Due to the definition via the "currently living" population, the identity of a MRCA, and by extension of the human Y-MRCA, is time-dependent (it depends on the moment in time intended by the term "currently"). The MRCA of a population may move forward in time as archaic lineages within the population go extinct: once a lineage has died out, it is irretrievably lost. This mechanism can thus only shift the title of Y-MRCA forward in time. Such an event could be due to the total extinction of several basal haplogroups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ancestors_tale_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ancestors_tale-3">[3]</a></sup> The same holds for the concepts of matrilineal and patrilineal MRCAs: it follows from the definition of Y-MRCA that he had at least two sons who both have unbroken lineages that have survived to the present day. If the lineages of all but one of those sons die out, then the title of Y-MRCA shifts forward from the remaining son through his patrilineal descendants, until the first descendant is reached who had at least two sons who both have living, patrilineal descendants. The title of Y-MRCA is not permanently fixed to a single individual, and the Y-MRCA for any given population would himself have been part of a population which had its own, more remote, Y-MRCA.</p>
<p>Although the informal name "Y-chromosomal Adam" is a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">biblical Adam</a>, this should not be misconstrued as implying that the bearer of the chromosome was the only human male alive during his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Takahata93_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Takahata93-7">[7]</a></sup> His other male contemporaries also have descendants alive today, but not, by definition, through solely patrilineal descent.</p>
<p>By the nature of the concept of most recent common ancestors, these estimates can only represent a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminus_ante_quem" title="Terminus ante quem">terminus ante quem</a></i> ("limit before which"), until the genome of the entire population has been examined (in this case, the genome of all living humans).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_estimate">Age estimate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Age estimate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the tendency of the title of Y-MRCA to shift forward in time, the estimate of the Y-MRCA's DNA sequence, his position in the family tree, the time when he lived, and his place of origin, are all subject to future revisions.</p>
<p>The following events would change the estimate of who the individual designated as Y-MRCA was:</p>
<ul>
<li>Further sampling of Y chromosomes could uncover previously unknown divergent lineages. If this happens, Y-chromosome lineages would converge on an individual who lived further back in time.</li>
<li>The discovery of additional deep rooting mutations in known lineages could lead to a rearrangement of the family tree.</li>
<li>Revision of the Y-chromosome mutation rate (see below) can change the estimate of the time when he lived.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Method">Method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The time when Y-MRCA lived is determined by applying a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock" title="Molecular clock">molecular clock</a> to human Y-chromosomes. In contrast to mitochondrial DNA, which has a short sequence of 16,000 <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairs</a>, and mutates frequently, the Y chromosome is significantly longer at 60 million base pairs, and has a lower mutation rate. These features of the Y chromosome have slowed down the identification of its polymorphisms; as a consequence, they have reduced the accuracy of Y-chromosome mutation rate estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thomson-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Methods of estimating the age of the Y-MRCA for a population of human males whose Y-chromosomes have been sequenced are based on applying the theories of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">molecular evolution</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a>. Unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomes" title="Autosomes">autosomes</a>, the human Y-chromosome does not <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombine</a> often with the <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a> during <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>, but is usually transferred intact from father to son; however, it can recombine with the X chromosome in the pseudoautosomal regions at the ends of the Y chromosome. <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">Mutations</a> occur periodically within the Y chromosome, and these mutations are passed on to males in subsequent generations.</p>
<p>These mutations can be used as markers to identify shared patrilineal relationships. Y chromosomes that share a specific mutation are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup" title="Human Y-chromosome DNA haplogroup">haplogroups</a>. Y chromosomes within a specific haplogroup are assumed to share a common patrilineal ancestor who was the first to carry the defining mutation. (This assumption could be mistaken, as it is possible for the same mutation to occur more than once.) A family tree of Y chromosomes can be constructed, with the mutations serving as branching points along lineages. The Y-MRCA is positioned at the root of the family tree, as the Y chromosomes of all living males are descended from his Y chromosome.</p>
<p>Researchers can reconstruct ancestral Y chromosome DNA sequences by reversing mutated DNA segments to their original condition. The most likely original or ancestral state of a DNA sequence is determined by comparing human DNA sequences with those of a closely related species, usually non-human primates such as chimpanzees and gorillas. By reversing known mutations in a Y-chromosome lineage, a hypothetical ancestral sequence for the MRCA, Y-chromosomal Adam, can be inferred.</p>
<p>Determining the Y-MRCA's DNA sequence, and the time when he lived, involves identifying the human Y-chromosome lineages that are most divergent from each other—the lineages that share the fewest mutations with each other when compared to a non-human primate sequence in a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a>. The common ancestor of the most divergent lineages is therefore the common ancestor of all lineages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimates">Estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current (as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) estimates for the age for the Y-MRCA are roughly compatible with the estimate for the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_human" title="Anatomically modern human">anatomically modern humans</a> some 200,000 years ago (200 kya), although there are substantial uncertainties.</p>
<p>Early estimates published during the 1990s ranged between roughly 200 and 300 kya,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Such estimates were later substantially revised downward, as in Thomson <i>et al.</i> 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomson_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-thomson-8">[8]</a></sup> which proposed an age of about 59,000. This date suggested that the Y-MRCA lived about 84,000 years after his female counterpart <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt-MRCA" title="Mt-MRCA">mt-MRCA</a> (the matrilineal most recent common ancestor), who lived 150,000–200,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This date also meant that Y-chromosomal Adam lived at a time very close to, and possibly after, the <a href="/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans" title="Recent African origin of modern humans">migration from Africa</a> which is believed to have taken place 50,000–80,000 years ago. One explanation given for this discrepancy in the time depths of patrilineal vs. matrilineal lineages was that females have a better chance of reproducing than males due to the practice of <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">polygyny</a>. When a male individual has several wives, he has effectively prevented other males in the community from reproducing and passing on their Y chromosomes to subsequent generations. On the other hand, polygyny does not prevent most females in a community from passing on their mitochondrial DNA to subsequent generations. This differential reproductive success of males and females can lead to fewer male lineages relative to female lineages persisting into the future. These fewer male lineages are more sensitive to drift and would most likely <a href="/wiki/Coalescent_theory" title="Coalescent theory">coalesce</a> on a more recent common ancestor. This would potentially explain the more recent dates associated with the Y-MRCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "hyper-recent" estimate of significantly below 100 kya was again corrected upward in studies of the early 2010s, which ranged at about 120 kya to 160 kya. This revision was due to the discovery of additional mutations and the rearrangement of the backbone of the Y-chromosome phylogeny following the resequencing of <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup A</a> lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cell.com-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2013, Francalacci <i>et al.</i> reported the sequencing of male-specific single-nucleotide Y-chromosome polymorphisms (MSY-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphisms" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphisms">SNPs</a>) from 1204 <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_people" title="Sardinian people">Sardinian men</a>, which indicated an estimate of 180,000 to 200,000 years for the common origin of all humans through paternal lineage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fran_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fran-14">[14]</a></sup> or again as high as 180 to 200 kya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poz_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-poz-15">[15]</a></sup> Also in 2013, Poznik <i>et al.</i> reported the Y-MRCA to have lived between 120,000 and 156,000 years ago, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genome_sequencing" title="Genome sequencing">genome sequencing</a> of 69 men from 9 different populations. In addition, the same study estimated the age of Mitochondrial Eve to about 99,000 and 148,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unive_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-unive-16">[16]</a></sup> As these ranges overlap for a time-range of 28,000 years (148 to 120 kya), the results of this study have been cast in terms of the possibility that "Genetic Adam and Eve may have walked on Earth at the same time" in the popular press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cann_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cann-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstechnica_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstechnica-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The announcement of yet another discovery of a previously unknown lineage, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A00" title="Haplogroup A00">haplogroup A00</a>, in 2013, resulted in another shift in the estimate for the age of Y-chromosomal. Elhaik et al. (2014) dated it to between 163,900 and 260,200 years ago (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/95%25_confidence_interval" title="95% confidence interval">95% CI</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elhaik_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-elhaik-18">[18]</a></sup> Karmin et al. (2015) dated it to between 192,000 and 307,000 years ago (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/95%25_confidence_interval" title="95% confidence interval">95% CI</a>). The same study reports that non-African populations converge to a cluster of Y-MRCAs in a window close to 50kya (<a href="/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans" title="Recent African origin of modern humans">out-of-Africa migration</a>), and an additional <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">bottleneck</a> for non-African populations at about 10kya, interpreted as reflecting cultural changes increasing the variance in male reproductive success (i.e. increased <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">social stratification</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bottleneck_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bottleneck-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_tree">Family tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Family tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Initial sequencing (Karafet et al., 2008) of the human Y chromosome suggested that two most basal Y-chromosome lineages were <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup A</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_BT_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup BT (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup BT</a>. Haplogroup A is found at low frequencies in parts of Africa, but is common among certain hunter-gatherer groups. Haplogroup BT lineages represent the majority of African Y-chromosome lineages and virtually all non-African lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karafet_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet-19">[19]</a></sup> Y-chromosomal Adam was represented as the root of these two lineages. Haplogroup A and Haplogroup BT represented the lineages of the two male descendants of Y-chromosomal Adam.</p>
<p>Cruciani et al. 2011, determined that the deepest split in the Y-chromosome tree was found between two previously reported subclades of Haplogroup A, rather than between Haplogroup A and Haplogroup BT. Subclades A1b and A1a-T are now believed to descend directly from the root of the tree and now represent the lineages of Y-chromosomal Adam's two sons. The rearrangement of the Y-chromosome family tree implies that lineages classified as Haplogroup A do not necessarily form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> clade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cruciani_2011_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cruciani_2011-20">[20]</a></sup> Haplogroup A therefore refers to a collection of lineages that do not possess the markers that define Haplogroup BT, though Haplogroup A includes the most distantly related Y chromosomes.</p>
<p>The M91 and P97 mutations distinguish Haplogroup A from Haplogroup BT. Within Haplogroup A chromosomes, the M91 marker consists of a stretch of 8 <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">T nucleobase</a> units. In Haplogroup BT and <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzee</a> chromosomes, this marker consists of 9 T nucleobase units. This pattern suggested that the 9T stretch of Haplogroup BT was the ancestral version and that Haplogroup A was formed by the <a href="/wiki/Deletion_(genetics)" title="Deletion (genetics)">deletion</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobase</a>. Haplogroups A1b and A1a were considered subclades of Haplogroup A as they both possessed the M91 with 8Ts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karafet_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cruciani_2011_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cruciani_2011-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>But according to Cruciani et al. 2011, the region surrounding the M91 marker is a mutational hotspot prone to recurrent mutations. It is therefore possible that the 8T stretch of Haplogroup A may be the ancestral state of M91 and the 9T of Haplogroup BT may be the derived state that arose by an <a href="/wiki/Insertion_(genetics)" title="Insertion (genetics)">insertion</a> of 1T. This would explain why subclades A1b and A1a-T, the deepest branches of Haplogroup A, both possess the same version of M91 with 8Ts. Furthermore, Cruciani et al. 2011 determined that the P97 marker, which is also used to identify Haplogroup A, possessed the ancestral state in Haplogroup A but the derived state in Haplogroup BT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cruciani_2011_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cruciani_2011-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Likely_geographic_origin">Likely geographic origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y-chromosomal_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Likely geographic origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>As current estimates on TMRCA converge with estimates for the age of <a href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_human" title="Anatomically modern human">anatomically modern humans</a> and well predate the <a href="/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans" title="Recent African origin of modern humans">Out of Africa</a> migration, geographical origin hypotheses continue to be limited to the <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African continent</a>.</p>
<p>According to Cruciani <i>et al.</i> 2011, the most basal lineages have been detected in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Africa" title="Northwest Africa">Northwest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>, suggesting plausibility for the Y-MRCA living in the general region of "Central-Northwest Africa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scozzari <i>et al.</i> (2012) agreed with a plausible placement in "the north-western quadrant of the African continent" for the emergence of the A1b haplogroup. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The 2013 report of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A00" title="Haplogroup A00">haplogroup A00</a> found among the <a href="/wiki/Mbo_people_(Cameroon)" title="Mbo people (Cameroon)">Mbo people</a> of western present-day <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> is also compatible with this picture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendez13_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez13-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The revision of Y-chromosomal phylogeny since 2011 has affected estimates for the likely geographical origin of Y-MRCA as well as estimates on time depth. By the same reasoning, future discovery of presently-unknown archaic haplogroups in living people would again lead to such revisions. In particular, the possible presence of between 1% and 4% <a href="/wiki/Archaic_human_admixture_with_modern_humans" title="Archaic human admixture with modern humans">Neanderthal-derived DNA</a> in Eurasian genomes implies that the (unlikely) event of a discovery of a single living Eurasian male exhibiting a Neanderthal patrilineal line would immediately push back T-MRCA ("time to MRCA") to at least twice its current estimate. However, the discovery of a neanderthal Y-chromosome by Mendez <i>et al</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendez2016_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez2016-6">[6]</a></sup> suggests the extinction of neanderthal patrilineages, as the lineage inferred from the neanderthal sequence is outside of the range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">contemporary human</a> genetic variation. Questions of geographical origin would become part of the debate on Neanderthal evolution from <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>.</p>
