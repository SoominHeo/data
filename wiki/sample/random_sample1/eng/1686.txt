<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ë</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Yo_(Cyrillic)" title="Yo (Cyrillic)">Cyrillic Yo (Ё)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Ë</b>, <b>ë</b> (<a href="/wiki/E" title="E">e</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trema_(diacritic)" title="Trema (diacritic)">diaeresis</a>) is a letter in the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_language" title="Kashubian language">Kashubian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">Emilian-Romagnol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a> alphabets. This letter also appears in <a href="/wiki/Acehnese_language" title="Acehnese language">Acehnese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Italian" title="Southern Italian">Abruzzese</a> dialect, the Ascolano dialect and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_Language" title="Luxembourgish Language">Luxembourgish</a> as a variant of letter <i>e</i>. The letter is used in <a href="/wiki/Seneca_language" title="Seneca language">Seneca</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese Hokkien</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Turoyo_language" title="Turoyo language">Turoyo</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_language" title="Uyghur language">Uyghur</a> when written in <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin script</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_various_languages">Usage in various languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage in various languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Afrikaans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">trema</a> (Afrikaans: <i>deelteken</i>) is mostly used to indicate that the vowel should not be <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongised</a>, for example <i>geër</i> ("giver") is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Afrikaans" title="Help:IPA for Afrikaans">[χeər]</a></span>, whilst <i>geer</i> (a wedge-shaped piece of fabric) is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Afrikaans" title="Help:IPA for Afrikaans">[χiːr]</a></span>. There are some cases where the deelteken does nothing to the pronunciation, like in <i>reën</i> ("rain"), which is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Afrikaans" title="Help:IPA for Afrikaans">[reən]</a></span>. The non-existent word <i>reen</i> would be pronounced the same. The deelteken in this case has only a historical background; the archaic form of <i>reën</i> is <i>regen</i> and the deelteken indicates that the g was removed. Some older people still pronounce <i>reën</i> in two syllables (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈreː.ən]</a></span>).</p>
<p>The <i>deelteken</i> does exactly what it says (<i>deelteken</i> being Afrikaans for "separation mark"). It separates syllables, as it indicates the start of a new one. An example of this is the word <i>voël</i> ("bird"), which is pronounced in two syllables. Without it, the word would become <i>voel</i> ("feel"), which is pronounced in one syllable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albanian">Albanian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Albanian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ë</i> is the 8th letter of the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_alphabet" title="Albanian alphabet">Albanian alphabet</a> and represents the vowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel#Mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">ə</a>/</span>. It is the most commonly used letter of the language comprising 10 percent of all writings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascolano">Ascolano</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ascolano">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ë</i> is a phonetic symbol also used in the transcription of Abruzzese dialects and in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Ascoli_Piceno" title="Province of Ascoli Piceno">Province of Ascoli Piceno</a> (the ascolano dialect). It is called "mute E" and sounds like a hummed <i>é</i>. It is important for the prosody of the dialect itself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emilian-Romagnol">Emilian-Romagnol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emilian-Romagnol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ë is used in <a href="/wiki/Romagnol_dialect" title="Romagnol dialect">Romagnol</a> to represent [ɛː~ɛə], e.g. <i>fradël</i> [fraˈdɛəl~fraˈdɛːl] "brother". In some peripheral <a href="/wiki/Emilian_dialect" title="Emilian dialect">Emilian</a> dialects, ë is used to represent [ə], e.g. <i>strëtt</i> [strətː] "narrow".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Use of the character <i>Ë</i> in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> is relatively rare. Some publications, such as the American magazine <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>, use it more often than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is used to indicate that the <i>e</i> is to be pronounced separately from the preceding vowel (e.g. in the word "reëntry", the girl's name "Chloë" or in the boy's name "Raphaël"), or at all - like in the name of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB_sisters" title="Brontë sisters">Brontë sisters</a>, where without diaeresis the final <i>e</i> would be mute.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_and_Dutch">French and Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: French and Dutch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ë</i> appears in words like French <i>Noël</i> and Dutch <i>koloniën</i>. This so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">trema</a> is used to indicate that the vowel should not be <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthonged</a>. For example, <i>Noël</i> is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[nɔɛl]</a></span>, whilst <i>Noel</i> would be pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[nœl]</a></span>. Likewise, "koloniën" is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[koːˈloːniən]</a></span>, whilst "kolonien" would be pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[koːˈloːnin]</a></span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kashubian">Kashubian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kashubian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ë</i> is the 9th letter of the <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_alphabet" title="Kashubian alphabet">Kashubian alphabet</a> and represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel#Mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">ə</a>/</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ladin">Ladin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ladin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although not used in standard <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a>, <i>Ë</i> is used in some local dialects. It represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid central unrounded vowel">ɜ</a>/</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin">Latin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Latin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many editions of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> texts, the diaeresis is used to indicate that <i>ae</i> and <i>oe</i> form a <a href="/wiki/Hiatus_(linguistics)" title="Hiatus (linguistics)">hiatus</a>, not a diphthong (in the Classical pronunciation) or a monophthong (in traditional English pronunciations). Examples: <i>aër</i> "air", <i>poëta</i> "poet", <i>coërcere</i> "to coerce".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luxembourgish">Luxembourgish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Luxembourgish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">Luxembourgish</a>, ⟨ë⟩ is used for stressed <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> like in the word <i>ëmmer</i> ("always"). It is also used to indicate a morphological plural ending after two ⟨ee⟩ such as in <i>eeër</i> ("eggs") or <i>leeën</i> ("lay").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayan_languages">Mayan languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mayan languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the modern orthography of <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>, the letter <i>Ë</i> represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel#Mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">ə</a>/</span>, as in Albanian.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some Latin transliterations of <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <i>Ë</i> is used to represent its <a href="/wiki/Homoglyph" title="Homoglyph">homoglyph</a>, representing a stressed <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Yo_(Cyrillic)" title="Yo (Cyrillic)">jo</a>/</span>, as in <i>Potëmkin</i> to render the Cyrillic <i>Потёмкин</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syriac">Syriac</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Syriac">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Latin-script <a href="/wiki/Turoyo_language" title="Turoyo language">Turoyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Neo-Aramaic" title="Assyrian Neo-Aramaic">Assyrian</a>, the letter <i>Ë</i> gives a <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>. In grammar, sometimes it is a replacement for the other, original vowels (a, o, e, i, u). Example words that have <i>Ë</i>: <i>knoţër</i> ("he is waiting"), <i>krëhţi</i> ("they are running"), <i>krëqdo</i> ("she is dancing"), <i>sxërla</i> ("she has closed"), <i>gfolëḥ</i> ("he will work"), <i>madënḥo</i> ("east"), <i>mën</i> ("what"), <i>ašër</i> ("believe").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seneca">Seneca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Seneca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Seneca_language" title="Seneca language">Seneca</a> the letter <i>Ë</i> is used to represent <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ẽ/</span>, a <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">close-mid front unrounded</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasalized_vowel" title="Nasalized vowel">nasalized vowel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tagalog">Tagalog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tagalog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>, <i>Ë</i> is used to represent the <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>, particularly in words originating from other <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Philippines" title="Languages of the Philippines">Philippine languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uyghur">Uyghur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8B&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Uyghur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ë</i> is the 6th letter of the <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_Latin_alphabet" title="Uyghur Latin alphabet">Uyghur Latin alphabet</a> and represents close-mid front unrounded vowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">e</a>/</span>.</p>
