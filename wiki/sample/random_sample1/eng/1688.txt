<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yogh</h1>

<p>The letter <b>yogh</b> (<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">Ȝ</span> <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span>; <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>: <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">yoȝ</span>) was used in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Older_Scots" title="Older Scots">Older Scots</a>, representing <i>y</i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span>) and various <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velar</a> phonemes. It was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_Latin_alphabet" title="Old English Latin alphabet">Old English</a> form of the letter <i>g</i>.</p>
<p>In Middle English writing, <a href="/wiki/Z#Variant_and_derived_forms" title="Z">tailed z</a> came to be indistinguishable from yogh.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Middle Scots</a>, the character yogh became confused with a cursive z and the early Scots printers often used z when yogh was not available in their fonts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DSL_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DSL-1">[1]</a></sup> Consequently, some <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Lowland Scots</a> words have a <i>z</i> in place of a yogh.</p>
<p>Yogh is shaped similarly to the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_numerals" title="Arabic numerals">Arabic numeral</a> three (3), which is sometimes substituted for the character in online reference works. There is some confusion about the letter in the literature, as the English language was far from standardised at the time. The upper and lower case letters (<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">Ȝ</span>, <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span>) are represented in Unicode by code points <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+021C</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">Ȝ</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LATIN CAPITAL LETTER YOGH</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#540;</code>) and <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+021D</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">ȝ</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LATIN SMALL LETTER YOGH</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#541;</code>) respectively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Modern English, <i>Yogh</i> is pronounced either <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/x/ 'ch' in 'loch'">x</span></span>/</a></span></span>, with a short <i>o</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-2">[2]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/x/ 'ch' in 'loch'">x</span></span>/</a></span></span>, with a long <i>o</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It stood for <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span> and its various fricative">voiced velar fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣ]</span>—as well as the phoneme <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span></span>/</a></span></span> (⟨y⟩ in modern <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English orthography</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, it also stood for the phoneme <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> and its allophone [ç] as in ⟨<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">niȝt</span>⟩ ("night", then pronounced as spelled: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[niçt]</span>). Sometimes, yogh stood for <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span>, as in the word ⟨<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝoȝelinge</span>⟩ <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈjowəlɪŋɡə]</span>, "yowling".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Middle Scots</a>, it represented the sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> in the clusters <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lj/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋj/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nj/</span> written <i>l<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span></i> and <i>n<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOST_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOST-4">[4]</a></sup> Yogh was also used for <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> rather than <i>y</i>.</p>
<p>In medieval <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> manuscripts, yogh was used to represent the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">voiced dental fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ð]</span>, as in ⟨<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝoȝo</span>⟩, now written ⟨dhodho⟩, pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ðoðo]</span>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_English">Old English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Insular_G" title="Insular G">Insular G</a></div>
<p>The original Germanic <i>g</i> sound was expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Gyfu" title="Gyfu">gyfu</a> rune in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_runes" title="Anglo-Saxon runes">Anglo-Saxon futhorc</a> (which is itself rendered as <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span></i> in modern <a href="/wiki/Runic_transliteration_and_transcription" title="Runic transliteration and transcription">transliteration</a>). Following palatalization, both <i>gyfu</i> and Latin <i>g</i> in Old English expressed the /j/ sound before front vowels. For example, "year" was written as either <i>ȝear</i> or <i>gear</i>, even though the word had never had a <i>g</i> sound (deriving from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>*jērą</i>).</p>
<p>With the re-introduced possibility of a /g/ sound before front vowels, notably in the form of loanwords from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> (such as <i>gere</i> from Norse <i>gervi</i>, Modern English <i>gear</i>), this orthographical state of affairs became a source for confusion, and a distinction of "real <i>g</i>" (/g/) from "palatalized <i>g</i>" (/j/) became desirable.</p>
<p>In the Old English period, the <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span> glyph was simply the way Latin <i><a href="/wiki/G" title="G">g</a></i> was written in the <a href="/wiki/Uncial_script" title="Uncial script">Uncial script</a> introduced at the <a href="/wiki/Christianisation_of_Anglo-Saxon_England" title="Christianisation of Anglo-Saxon England">Christianisation of Anglo-Saxon England</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">Hiberno-Scottish mission</a>. It only came to be used as a letter <i>distinct</i> from <i>g</i> in the Middle English period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_English">Middle English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Scribe" title="Scribe">scribes</a> despised non-<a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin characters</a> and certain spellings in English and therefore replaced the yogh with the <a href="/wiki/Gh_(digraph)" title="Gh (digraph)">digraph <i>gh</i></a>; still, the variety of pronunciations persisted, as evidenced by <i>cough</i>, <i>taught</i>, and <i>though</i>. The process of replacing the yogh with <i>gh</i> was slow, and was not completed until the end of the fifteenth century. Not every English word that contains a <i>gh</i> was originally spelled with a yogh: for example, <i>spaghetti</i> is <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, where the <i>h</i> makes the <i>g</i> hard (i.e., <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡ]</span> instead of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʒ]</span>); <i>ghoul</i> is <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, in which the <i>gh</i> was <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣ/</span>.</p>
<p>The medieval author <a href="/wiki/Ormulum" title="Ormulum">Orm</a> used this letter in three ways when writing Early Middle English. By itself, it represented <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span>, so he used this letter for the <i>y</i> in "yet". Doubled, it represented <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>, so he ended his spelling of "may" with two yoghs. Finally, the digraph of yogh followed by an <i>h</i> represented <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣ/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Melibee" title="The Tale of Melibee">The Tale of Melibee</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> wrote, "Ȝe haue cast alle here wordes in an <a href="/wiki/Hodge-Podge_(soup)" title="Hodge-Podge (soup)">hochepoche</a>."</p>
<p>In the late Middle English period, yogh was no longer used: <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">niȝt</span> came to be spelled <i>night</i>. Middle English re-imported <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_G" title="Carolingian G">G in its French form</a> for <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scots">Scots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In words of <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> origin, the <a href="/wiki/Early_Scots" title="Early Scots">Early Scots</a> <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">palatal consonant</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Palatal_nasal" title="Palatal nasal">ɲ</a>/</span> had become <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nj/</span> or in some cases <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋj/</span>, and the palatal consonant <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">ʎ</a>/</span> had become <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lj/</span> by the Middle Scots period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOST_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DOST-4">[4]</a></sup> Those were variously written <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">nȝ(h)e</span></i>, <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ngȝe</span></i>, <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ny(h)e</span></i> or <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ny(i)e</span></i>, and <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">lȝ(h)e</span></i>, <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ly(i)e</span></i> or <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">lyhe</span></i>. By the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Scots" title="Modern Scots">Modern Scots</a> period the yogh had been replaced by the character <i>z</i>, in particular for <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋj/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nj/</span> (<i>n<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span></i>) and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lj/</span> (<i>l<span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝ</span></i>), written <i>nz</i> and <i>lz</i>. The original <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hj/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/çj/</span> developed into <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ(j)/</span> in some words such as <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">Ȝ</span>etland</i> or <i>Zetland</i> for <i>Shetland</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DSL_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DSL-1">[1]</a></sup> Yogh was also used to represent <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> in words such as <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝe</span></i>, <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝhistirday</span> <i>(yesterday) and <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ȝoung</span></i> but by the Modern Scots period <i>y</i> had replaced yogh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The pronunciation of <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_(surname)" title="Mackenzie (surname)">MacKenzie</a> (and its variant spellings) (from Scottish Gaelic <i>MacCoinnich</i>), originally pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[məˈkɛŋjiː]</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> shows where yogh became zed. <a href="/wiki/Menzies_Campbell" title="Menzies Campbell">Menzies Campbell</a> is another example.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_development_of_printing">After the development of printing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After the development of printing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Middle Scots orthography, the use of yogh became confused with a cursive <i>z</i> and the early Scots printers often used <i>z</i> when yogh was not available in their fonts.</p>
<p>The yogh glyph can be found in surnames that start with a <i>Y</i> in Scotland and Ireland; for example the surname Yeoman, which would have been spelled <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">Ȝeman</span></i>. Sometimes, the yogh would be replaced by the letter <i>z</i>, because the shape of the yogh was identical to some forms of handwritten <i>z</i>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> 1.0, the character yogh was mistakenly unified with the quite different character <a href="/wiki/Ezh" title="Ezh">ezh</a> (Ʒ ʒ), and yogh itself was not added to Unicode until version 3.0.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Egyptology">In Egyptology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yogh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Egyptology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
