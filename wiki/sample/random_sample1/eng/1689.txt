<p>The <b>ΛCDM</b> (<b>Lambda cold dark matter</b>) or <b>Lambda-CDM</b> model is a <a href="/wiki/Parametrization" title="Parametrization">parametrization</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> model in which the universe contains a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, denoted by <a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">Lambda</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> <b>Λ</b>), associated with <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cold_dark_matter" title="Cold dark matter">cold dark matter</a> (abbreviated <b>CDM</b>). It is frequently referred to as the <b>standard model</b> of Big Bang cosmology because it is the simplest model that provides a reasonably good account of the following properties of the cosmos:</p>
<ul>
<li>the existence and structure of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe#Large-scale_structure" title="Observable universe">large-scale structure</a> in the distribution of galaxies</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">abundances</a> of <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">hydrogen (including deuterium), helium, and lithium</a></li>
<li>the accelerating <a href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expansion of the universe</a> observed in the light from distant galaxies and <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The model assumes that <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> is the correct theory of gravity on cosmological scales. It emerged in the late 1990s as a <b>concordance cosmology</b>, after a period of time when disparate observed properties of the universe appeared mutually inconsistent, and there was no consensus on the makeup of the energy density of the universe.</p>
<p>The ΛCDM model can be extended by adding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_inflation" title="Cosmological inflation">cosmological inflation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">quintessence</a> and other elements that are current areas of speculation and research in cosmology.</p>
<p>Some alternative models challenge the assumptions of the ΛCDM model. Examples of these are <a href="/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics" title="Modified Newtonian dynamics">modified Newtonian dynamics</a>, modified gravity and theories of large-scale variations in the matter density of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Most modern cosmological models are based on the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_principle" title="Cosmological principle">cosmological principle</a>, which states that our observational location in the universe is not unusual or special; on a large-enough scale, the universe looks the same in all directions (<a href="/wiki/Isotropy" title="Isotropy">isotropy</a>) and from every location (<a href="/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics)" title="Homogeneity (physics)">homogeneity</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The model includes an expansion of metric space that is well documented both as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift" title="Red shift">red shift</a> of prominent spectral absorption or emission lines in the light from distant galaxies and as the time dilation in the light decay of supernova luminosity curves. Both effects are attributed to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">Doppler shift</a> in electromagnetic radiation as it travels across expanding space. Although this expansion increases the distance between objects that are not under shared gravitational influence, it does not increase the size of the objects (e.g. galaxies) in space. It also allows for distant galaxies to recede from each other at speeds greater than the speed of light; local expansion is less than the speed of light, but expansion summed across great distances can collectively exceed the speed of light.</p>
<p>The letter Λ (lambda) represents the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, which is currently associated with a vacuum energy or <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> in empty space that is used to explain the contemporary accelerating expansion of space against the attractive effects of gravity. <p><a href="/wiki/Cold_dark_matter" title="Cold dark matter">Cold dark matter</a> is a form of matter introduced in order to account for gravitational effects observed in very large-scale structures (the "flat" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotation_curve" title="Rotation curve">rotation curves</a> of galaxies; the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">gravitational lensing</a> of light by galaxy clusters; and enhanced clustering of galaxies) that cannot be accounted for by the quantity of observed matter. <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">Dark matter</a> is described as being cold (i.e. its velocity is far less than the speed of light at the epoch of radiation-matter equality); <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Non-baryonic_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Non-baryonic matter (page does not exist)">non-baryonic</a> (i.e. consisting of matter other than protons and neutrons); dissipationless (i.e. cannot cool by radiating photons); and collisionless (i.e. the dark matter particles interact with each other and other particles only through gravity and possibly the weak force). The dark matter component is currently [2013] estimated to constitute about 26.8% of the mass-energy density of the universe.</p>
<p>The remaining 4.9% [2013] comprises all ordinary matter observed as atoms, chemical elements, gas and plasma, the stuff of which visible planets, stars and galaxies are made. As a matter of fact, the great majority of ordinary matter in the universe is unseen, since visible stars and gas inside galaxies and clusters account for less than 10 per cent of the ordinary matter contribution to the mass-energy density of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, the energy density includes a very small fraction (~ 0.01%) in cosmic microwave background radiation, and not more than 0.5% in relic neutrinos. Although very small today, these were much more important in the distant past, dominating the matter at redshift &gt; 3200.</p>
<p>The model includes a single originating event, the "Big Bang" or initial singularity, which was not an explosion but the abrupt appearance of expanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space-time" title="Space-time">space-time</a> containing radiation at temperatures of around 10<sup>15</sup> K. This was immediately (within 10<sup>−29</sup> seconds) followed by an exponential expansion of space by a scale multiplier of 10<sup>27</sup> or more, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a>. The early universe remained hot (above 10,000 K) for several hundred thousand years, a state that is detectable as a residual <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a>, or CMB, a very low energy radiation emanating from all parts of the sky. The "Big Bang" scenario, with cosmic inflation and standard particle physics, is the only current cosmological model consistent with the observed continuing expansion of space, the observed distribution of <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">lighter elements in the universe</a> (hydrogen, helium, and lithium), and the spatial texture of minute irregularities (<a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropies</a>) in the CMB radiation. Cosmic inflation also addresses the "<a href="/wiki/Horizon_problem" title="Horizon problem">horizon problem</a>" in the CMB; indeed, it seems likely that the universe is larger than the observable <a href="/wiki/Particle_horizon" title="Particle horizon">particle horizon</a>.</p>
<p>The model uses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FLRW_metric" title="FLRW metric">FLRW metric</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann_equations" title="Friedmann equations">Friedmann equations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology)" title="Equation of state (cosmology)">cosmological equations of state</a> to describe the observable universe from right after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Big_Bang#Inflationary_epoch" title="Timeline of the Big Bang">inflationary epoch</a> to present and future.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Microwave_Background" title="Cosmic Microwave Background">Cosmic Microwave Background</a> (CMB) in 1965 confirmed a key prediction of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> cosmology. From that point on, it was generally accepted that the universe started in a hot, dense state and has been expanding over time. The rate of expansion depends on the types of matter and energy present in the universe, and in particular, whether the total density is above or below the so-called critical density. During the 1970s, most attention focused on pure-baryonic models, but there were serious challenges explaining the formation of galaxies, given the small anisotropies in the CMB (upper limits at that time). In the early 1980s, it was realized that this could be resolved if cold dark matter dominated over the baryons, and the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a> motivated models with critical density. During the 1980s, most research focused on cold dark matter with critical density in matter, around 95% CDM and 5% baryons: these showed success at forming galaxies and clusters of galaxies, but problems remained; notably, the model required a Hubble constant lower than preferred by observations, and observations around 1988-1990 showed more large-scale galaxy clustering than predicted. These difficulties sharpened with the discovery of CMB anisotropy by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer" title="Cosmic Background Explorer">COBE</a> in 1992, and several modified CDM models, including ΛCDM and mixed cold+hot dark matter, came under active consideration through the mid-1990s. The ΛCDM model then became the leading model following the observations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerating_universe" title="Accelerating universe">accelerating expansion</a> in 1998, and was quickly supported by other observations: in 2000, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BOOMERanG" title="BOOMERanG">BOOMERanG</a> microwave background experiment measured the total (matter+energy) density to be close to 100% of critical, whereas in 2001 the <a href="/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey" title="2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey">2dFGRS</a> galaxy redshift survey measured the matter density to be near 25%; the large difference between these values supports a positive Λ or <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>. Much more precise measurements of the microwave background from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMAP" title="WMAP">WMAP</a> in 2003 – 2010 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck" title="Planck">Planck</a> in 2013 - 2015 have continued to support the model and pin down the parameters, now mostly constrained below 1 percent uncertainty.</p>
<p>There is currently active research into many aspects of the ΛCDM model, both to refine the parameters and possibly detect deviations. In addition, ΛCDM has no explicit physical theory for the origin or physical nature of dark matter or dark energy; the nearly scale-invariant spectrum of the CMB perturbations, and their image across the celestial sphere, are believed to result from very small thermal and acoustic irregularities at the point of recombination. A large majority of astronomers and astrophysicists support the ΛCDM model or close relatives of it, but <a href="/wiki/Mordehai_Milgrom" title="Mordehai Milgrom">Milgrom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stacy_McGaugh" title="Stacy McGaugh">McGaugh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Kroupa" title="Pavel Kroupa">Kroupa</a> are leading critics, attacking the dark matter portions of the theory from the perspective of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation" title="Galaxy formation">galaxy formation</a> models and supporting the alternative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOND" title="MOND">MOND</a> theory, which requires a modification of the <a href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equations" title="Einstein field equations">Einstein field equations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann_equations" title="Friedmann equations">Friedmann equations</a> as seen in proposals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOG_theory" title="MOG theory">MOG theory</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tensor%E2%80%93vector%E2%80%93scalar_gravity" title="Tensor–vector–scalar gravity">TeVeS theory</a>. Other proposals by theoretical astrophysicists of cosmological alternatives to Einstein's general relativity that attempt to account for dark energy or dark matter include <a href="/wiki/F(R)_gravity" title="F(R) gravity">f(R) gravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scalar%E2%80%93tensor_theory" title="Scalar–tensor theory">scalar–tensor theories</a> such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Galileon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Galileon (page does not exist)">galileon</a> theories, <a href="/wiki/Brane_cosmology" title="Brane cosmology">brane cosmologies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/DGP_model" title="DGP model">DGP model</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Massive_gravity" title="Massive gravity">massive gravity</a> and its extensions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bimetric_theory" title="Bimetric theory">bimetric gravity</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Successes">Successes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Successes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to explaining pre-2000 observations, the model has made a number of successful predictions: notably the existence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation" title="Baryon acoustic oscillation">baryon acoustic oscillation</a> feature, discovered in 2005 in the predicted location; and the statistics of weak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_lensing" title="Gravitational lensing">gravitational lensing</a>, first observed in 2000 by several teams. The <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background#Polarization" title="Cosmic microwave background">polarization</a> of the CMB, discovered in 2002 by DASI <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is now a dramatic success: in the 2015 Planck data release,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> there are seven observed peaks in the temperature (TT) power spectrum, six peaks in the temperature-polarization (TE) cross spectrum, and five peaks in the polarization (EE) spectrum, and all well fit by Lambda-CDM.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Extensive searches for dark matter particles have so far shown no well-agreed detection; the dark energy may be almost impossible to detect in a laboratory, and its value is unnaturally small compared to naive theoretical predictions.</p>
<p>Comparison of the model with observations is very successful on large scales (larger than galaxies, up to the observable horizon), but may have some problems on sub-galaxy scales, possibly predicting <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy_problem" title="Dwarf galaxy problem">too many dwarf galaxies</a> and too much dark matter in the innermost regions of galaxies. These small scales are harder to resolve in computer simulations, so it is not yet clear whether the problem is the simulations, non-standard properties of dark matter, or a more radical error in the model.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parameters">Parameters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Parameters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The simple ΛCDM model is based on six <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameters</a>: physical baryon density parameter; physical dark matter density parameter; the age of the universe; scalar spectral index; curvature fluctuation amplitude; and reionization optical depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In accordance with <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam's razor">Occam's razor</a>, six is the smallest number of parameters needed to give an acceptable fit to current observations; other possible parameters are fixed at "natural" values, e.g. total density parameter = 1.00, dark energy equation of state = −1. (See below for extended models that allow these to vary.)</p>
<p>The values of these six parameters are mostly not predicted by current theory (though, ideally, they may be related by a future "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Everything" title="Theory of Everything">Theory of Everything</a>"), except that most versions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a> predict the scalar spectral index should be slightly smaller than 1, consistent with the estimated value 0.96. The parameter values, and uncertainties, are estimated using large computer searches to locate the region of parameter space providing an acceptable match to cosmological observations. From these six parameters, the other model values, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble constant</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> density, can be readily calculated.</p>
<p>Commonly, the set of observations fitted includes the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> anisotropy, the brightness/redshift relation for supernovae, and large-scale galaxy clustering including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation" title="Baryon acoustic oscillation">baryon acoustic oscillation</a> feature. Other observations, such as the Hubble constant, the abundance of galaxy clusters, <a href="/wiki/Weak_gravitational_lensing" title="Weak gravitational lensing">weak gravitational lensing</a> and globular cluster ages, are generally consistent with these, providing a check of the model, but are less precisely measured at present.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extended_models">Extended models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda-CDM_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Extended models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Extended models allow one or more of the "fixed" parameters above to vary, in addition to the basic six; so these models join smoothly to the basic six-parameter model in the limit that the additional parameter(s) approach the default values. For example, possible extensions of the simplest ΛCDM model allow for spatial curvature (Ω<sub>tot</sub> may be different from 1); or <a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">quintessence</a> rather than a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology)" title="Equation of state (cosmology)">equation of state</a> of dark energy is allowed to differ from −1. Cosmic inflation predicts tensor fluctuations (<a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational waves</a>). Their amplitude is parameterized by the tensor-to-scalar ratio (denoted <var style="padding-right: 1px;">r</var>), which is determined by the unknown energy scale of inflation. Other modifications allow <a href="/wiki/Hot_dark_matter" title="Hot dark matter">hot dark matter</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> more massive than the minimal value, or a running spectral index; the latter is generally not favoured by simple cosmic inflation models.</p>
<p>Allowing additional variable parameter(s) will generally <i>increase</i> the uncertainties in the standard six parameters quoted above, and may also shift the central values slightly. The Table below shows results for each of the possible "6+1" scenarios with one additional variable parameter; this indicates that, as of 2015, there is no convincing evidence that any additional parameter is different from its default value.</p>
