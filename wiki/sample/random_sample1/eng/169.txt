<p>An <b>asterisk</b> (<b>*</b>; from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <span lang="lat" xml:lang="lat">asteriscus</span>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">á¼€ÏƒÏ„ÎµÏÎ¯ÏƒÎºÎ¿Ï‚</span>, <i>asteriskos</i>, "little star")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typographical" title="Typographical">typographical</a> symbol or <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a>. It is so called because it resembles a conventional image of a <a href="/wiki/Star_(heraldry)" title="Star (heraldry)">star</a>.</p>
<p>Computer scientists and mathematicians often vocalize it as <b>star</b> (as, for example, in <i>the <a href="/wiki/A*_search_algorithm" title="A* search algorithm">A* search algorithm</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/C*-algebra" title="C*-algebra">C*-algebra</a></i>). In English, an asterisk is usually five-pointed in <a href="/wiki/Sans-serif" title="Sans-serif">sans-serif</a> <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>, six-pointed in <a href="/wiki/Serif" title="Serif">serif</a> typefaces,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="I don't see relation between # of points and serif-ness for some 20 installed fonts. Even if references are shown, it may not apply to today's norm. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and six- or eight-pointed when handwritten. It can be used as censorship. It is also used on the Internet to correct one's spelling.</p>
<p>The asterisk is derived from the need of the printers of family trees in feudal times for a symbol to indicate date of birth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Particularly as feudal times had almost ended before the invention of printing. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The original shape was seven-armed,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> each arm like a teardrop shooting from the center.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, the asterisk is commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Wildcard_character" title="Wildcard character">wildcard character</a>, or to denote <a href="/wiki/Pointer_(computer_programming)" title="Pointer (computer programming)">pointers</a>, repetition, or <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a>.</p>

<p>The asterisk derives from the two thousand year old character used by <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samothrace" title="Aristarchus of Samothrace">Aristarchus of Samothrace</a> called the asteriskos, â€», which he used when proofreading Homeric poetry to mark lines that were duplicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origin Adamantius</a> is known to have also used the asteriskos to mark missing Hebrew lines from his <a href="/wiki/Hexapla" title="Hexapla">Hexapla</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The asterisk evolved in shape over time, but its meaning as a symbol used to correct defects remained.</p>
<p>In the Middle Ages, the asterisk was used to emphasize a particular part of text, often linking those parts of the text to a marginal comment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, an asterisk was not always used.</p>
<p>One hypothesis to the origin of the asterisk is that it stems from the five thousand year old Sumerian character <a href="/wiki/Dingir" title="Dingir">dingir</a>, ğ’€­.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> though this hypothesis seems to only be based on visual appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship">Censorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Censorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wordfilter" title="Wordfilter">Wordfilter</a></div>
<p>When toning down <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">expletives</a>, asterisks are often used to replace letters. For example, the word 'fuck' might become 'f**k', 'f*ck' or even '****'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitive_sports_and_games">Competitive sports and games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Competitive sports and games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In colloquial usage, an asterisk is used to indicate that a <a href="/wiki/World_record" title="World record">record</a> is somehow tainted by circumstances, which are putatively explained in a footnote referenced by the asterisk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This usage arose after the 1961 baseball season in which <a href="/wiki/Roger_Maris" title="Roger Maris">Roger Maris</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> broke <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>'s 34-year-old single-season <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> record. Because Ruth had amassed 60 home runs in a season with only 154 games, compared to Maris's 61 over 162 games, baseball commissioner <a href="/wiki/Ford_Frick" title="Ford Frick">Ford Frick</a> announced that Maris' accomplishment would be recorded in the record books with an explanation (often referred to as "an asterisk" in the retelling). In fact, <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> had no official record book at the time, but the stigma remained with Maris for many years, and the concept of a real or figurative asterisk denoting less-than-official records has become widely used in sports and other competitive endeavors. A 2001 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_movie" title="TV movie">TV movie</a> about Maris' record-breaking season was called <i><a href="/wiki/61*" title="61*">61*</a></i> (pronounced <i>sixty-one asterisk</i>) in reference to the controversy.</li>
<li>In recent years, the asterisk has come into use on baseball scorecards to denote a "great defensive play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>In February 2011 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Committee" title="United States Olympic Committee">United States Olympic Committee</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ad_Council" title="Ad Council">Ad Council</a> launched an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Use_of_performance-enhancing_drugs_in_sport#Anti-Doping_organizations_and_legislation" title="Use of performance-enhancing drugs in sport">anti-steroid</a> campaign called "Play Asterisk Free"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> aimed at teens. The campaign, whose logo uses a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heavy asterisk (page does not exist)">heavy asterisk</a>, first launched in 2008 under the name Don't Be An Asterisk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Barry_Bonds">Barry Bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Barry Bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fans critical of <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>, who has been accused of using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performance-enhancing_drugs" title="Performance-enhancing drugs">performance-enhancing drugs</a> during his baseball career, invoked the asterisk notion during the 2007 season, as he approached and later broke <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a>'s career home run record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Opposing fans would often hold up signs bearing asterisks whenever Bonds came up to bat. After Bonds hit his record-breaking 756th home run on August 7, 2007, fashion designer and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Marc_Ecko" title="Marc Ecko">Marc Ecko</a> purchased the home run ball from the fan who caught it, and ran a poll on his Web site to determine its fate. On September 26, Ecko revealed on NBC's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" title="Today (NBC program)"><i>Today</i> show</a> that the ball will be branded with an asterisk and donated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a>. The ball, marked with a die-cut asterisk, was finally delivered to the hall on July 2, 2008 after Marc Ecko unconditionally donated the artifact rather than loaning it to the hall as originally intended.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cricket">Cricket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cricket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>, it signifies a total number of runs scored by a batsman without losing his wicket; e.g. 107* means '107 <a href="/wiki/Not_out" title="Not out">not out</a>'.</li>
<li>Where only the scores of the two batsmen that are currently in are being shown, an asterisk following a batsman's score indicates that he is due to face the next ball to be delivered.</li>
<li>When written before a player's name on a scorecard, it indicates the <a href="/wiki/Captain_(cricket)" title="Captain (cricket)">captain</a> of the team.</li>
<li>It is also used on television when giving a career statistic during a match. For example, 47* in a number of matches column means that the current game is the player's 47th.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_science">Computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, the asterisk is used in <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expressions</a> to denote zero or more repetitions of a pattern; this use is also known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Kleene_star" title="Kleene star">Kleene star</a></i> or <i>Kleene closure</i> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_Kleene" title="Stephen Kleene">Stephen Kleene</a>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language" title="Unified Modeling Language">Unified Modeling Language</a>, the asterisk is used to denote zero to many classes.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_interfaces">Computer interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Computer interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_line_interface" title="Command line interface">command line interfaces</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Unix_shell" title="Unix shell">Unix shell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMD_(Windows)" title="CMD (Windows)">CMD</a>, the asterisk is the <i><a href="/wiki/Wildcard_character" title="Wildcard character">wildcard character</a></i> and stands for any <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">string</a> of <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">characters</a>. This is also known as a wildcard symbol. A common use of the wildcard is in searching for files on a computer. For instance, if a user wished to find a document called <tt>DocumentÂ 1</tt>, search terms such as <tt>Doc*</tt> and <tt>D*ment*</tt> would return this file. <tt>Document*</tt> would also return any file that begins with <tt>Document</tt>.</li>
<li>In some <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interfaces</a> an asterisk is pre- or appended to the current working document name shown in a window's title bar to indicate that unsaved changes exist. In many computing and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> applications an asterisk, or another character, is displayed to indicate that a character of a password or other confidential information has been entered, without the risk of displaying the actual character.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> (and related) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filesystem" title="Filesystem">filesystems</a>, an asterisk appearing next to a filename in a directory listing denotes an improperly closed file, commonly called a "splat file."</li>
<li>In travel industry <a href="/wiki/Computer_reservations_system" title="Computer reservations system">Global Distribution Systems</a>, the asterisk is the display command to retrieve all or part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passenger_Name_Record" title="Passenger Name Record">Passenger Name Record</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_form" title="Web form">web forms</a>, an asterisk can be used to denote required fields.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chat_Room" title="Chat Room">Chat Room</a> etiquette calls on one asterisk to correct a misspelled word that has already been submitted. For example, one could post <tt>lck</tt>, then follow it with <tt>luck*</tt> to correct the word's spelling, or if it's someone else that notices the mistake, they would use <tt>*luck</tt>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
<ul>
<li>Enclosing a phrase between two asterisks is used to denote an action the user is "performing", e.g. <tt>*pulls out a paper*</tt>, although this usage is also common on forums, and less so on most chat rooms due to /me or similar commands. <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">Hyphens</a> (<tt style="white-space:nowrap;">-action-</tt>) and double <a href="/wiki/Colon_(punctuation)" title="Colon (punctuation)">colons</a> (<tt>::action::</tt>) as well as the operator <tt>/me</tt> are also used for similar purposes.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Adding_machines_and_printing_calculators">Adding machines and printing calculators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Adding machines and printing calculators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Some international models of <a href="/wiki/Adding_machine" title="Adding machine">adding machines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">printing calculators</a> use the asterisk to denote the <i>total,</i> or the terminal sum or difference of an addition or subtraction sequence, respectively, sometimes on the keyboard where the total key is marked with an asterisk and sometimes a capital T, and on the printout.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculators</a> use the asterisk as a symbol for <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a>. It also has a number of special meanings in specific languages, for instance:</p>
<ul>
<li>In some programming languages such as the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Go_(programming_language)" title="Go (programming language)">Go</a> programming languages, the asterisk is used to dereference or to declare a pointer variable.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Common_Lisp" title="Common Lisp">Common Lisp</a> programming language, the names of <a href="/wiki/Global_variable" title="Global variable">global variables</a> are conventionally set off with asterisks, <tt>*LIKE-THIS*</tt>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Ada_(programming_language)" title="Ada (programming language)">Ada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language)" title="Ruby (programming language)">Ruby</a> programming languages, in some dialects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pascal_programming_language" title="Pascal programming language">Pascal programming language</a>, and many others, a double asterisk is used to signify <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponentiation</a>: 5**3 is 5*5*5 or 125.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl programming language</a>, the asterisk is used to refer to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typeglob" title="Typeglob">typeglob</a></i> of all variables with a given name.</li>
<li>In the programming languages <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruby_programming_language" title="Ruby programming language">Ruby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>, * has two specific uses. First, the unary * operator applied to a list object inside a function call will expand that list into the arguments of the function call. Second, a parameter preceded by * in the parameter list for a function will result in any extra positional parameters being aggregated into a <a href="/wiki/Tuple" title="Tuple">tuple</a> (Python) or <a href="/wiki/Array_data_type" title="Array data type">array</a> (Ruby), and likewise in Python a parameter preceded by ** will result in any extra <a href="/wiki/Named_parameter" title="Named parameter">keyword parameters</a> being aggregated into a <a href="/wiki/Associative_array" title="Associative array">dictionary</a>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/APL_(programming_language)" title="APL (programming language)">APL</a> language, the asterisk represents the <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">exponential</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponentiation</a> functions.</li>
<li>In IBM <a href="/wiki/Job_Control_Language" title="Job Control Language">Job Control Language</a>, the asterisk has various functions, including in-stream data in the DD statement, the default print stream as SYSOUT=*, and as a self-reference in place of a procedure step name to refer to the same procedure step where it appears.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Haskell_(programming_language)" title="Haskell (programming language)">Haskell</a>, the asterisk represents the set of well-formed, fully applied types - that is a 0-ary <i>kind</i> of types.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_in_programming_languages">Comments in programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Comments in programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_comments" title="Block comments">block comments</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/B_(programming_language)" title="B (programming language)">B programming language</a> and languages that borrow syntax from it, such as <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>, <a href="/wiki/PHP" title="PHP">PHP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java_(software_platform)" title="Java (software platform)">Java</a>, or <a href="/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language)" title="C Sharp (programming language)">C#</a>, comments in the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> (for information to people, ignored by the <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a>) are marked by an asterisk combined with the slash:</p>
<pre>
/* This section displays message if user input was not valid
   (comment ignored by compiler) */
</pre>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>-like programming languages, for example, <a href="/wiki/Object_Pascal" title="Object Pascal">Object Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modula-2" title="Modula-2">Modula-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modula-3" title="Modula-3">Modula-3</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(programming_language)" title="Oberon (programming language)">Oberon</a>, as well as several other languages including <a href="/wiki/ML_(programming_language)" title="ML (programming language)">ML</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Language" title="Wolfram Language">Wolfram Language</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematica" title="Mathematica">Mathematica</a>), <a href="/wiki/AppleScript" title="AppleScript">AppleScript</a>, <a href="/wiki/OCaml" title="OCaml">OCaml</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standard_ML" title="Standard ML">Standard ML</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maple_(software)" title="Maple (software)">Maple</a>, use an asterisk combined with a parenthesis:</p>
<pre>
(* Do not change this variable - it is used later
   (comment ignored by compiler) *)
</pre>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSS" title="CSS">CSS</a>, while not a programming language, also uses the slash-star comment format.</p>
<div class="mw-highlight mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr">
<pre>
<span class="nt">body</span> <span class="p">{</span>
  <span class="c">/* This ought to make the text more readable for far-sighted people */</span>
  <span class="nb">font-size</span><span class="o">:</span> <span class="m">24pt</span><span class="p">;</span>
<span class="p">}</span>
</pre></div>
<p>Each computing language has its own way of handling comments; "/* ... */" and similar notations are not universal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, the use of an asterisk after a letter indicating a <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variable</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a>, <a href="/wiki/Output_(economics)" title="Output (economics)">output</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a> indicates that the variable is at its optimal level (that which is achieved in a perfect market situation). For instance, <i>p</i>* is the price level <i>p</i> when output <i>y</i> is at its corresponding optimal level of <i>y</i>*.</li>
<li>Also in international economics asterisks are commonly used to denote economic variables in a foreign country. So, for example, "p" is the price of the home good and "p*" is the price of the foreign good, etc.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="General Certificate of Secondary Education">GCSE</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_General_Certificate_of_Education" title="Advanced Level General Certificate of Education">A-Level</a> examinations in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Primary_School_Leaving_Examination" title="Primary School Leaving Examination">PSLE</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, A* ("<i>A</i>-star") is a special top grade that is distinguished from grade A. (This will phase out starting from 2021)</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Diploma_of_Secondary_Education" title="Hong Kong Diploma of Secondary Education">Hong Kong Diploma of Secondary Education</a> (HKDSE) examination in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, 5** (5-star-star) and 5* (5-star) are two special top grades that are distinguished from Level 5. Level 5** is the highest level a candidate can attain in HKDSE.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluid_mechanics">Fluid mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fluid mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">fluid mechanics</a> an asterisk in superscript is sometimes used to mean a property at sonic speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Certain categories of character types in <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a> are called <b>splats</b>, and the game supplements describing them are called <b><a href="/wiki/Splatbook" title="Splatbook">splatbooks</a></b>. This usage originated with the shorthand "*book" for this type of supplement to various <a href="/wiki/World_of_Darkness" title="World of Darkness">World of Darkness</a> games, such as <i>Clanbook: Ventrue</i> (for <i><a href="/wiki/Vampire:_The_Masquerade" title="Vampire: The Masquerade">Vampire: The Masquerade</a></i>) or <i>Tribebook: Black Furies</i> (for <i><a href="/wiki/Werewolf:_The_Apocalypse" title="Werewolf: The Apocalypse">Werewolf: The Apocalypse</a></i>), and this usage has spread to other games with similar character-type supplements. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> Third Edition has had several lines of splatbooks: the "X &amp; Y" series including <i>Sword &amp; Fist</i> and <i>Tome &amp; Blood</i> prior to the "3.5" revision, the "Complete X" series including <i>Complete Warrior</i> and <i>Complete Divine</i>, and the "Races of X" series including <i>Races of Stone</i> and <i>Races of the Wild</i>.</li>
<li>In many <a href="/wiki/MUD" title="MUD">MUDs</a> and <a href="/wiki/MOO" title="MOO">MOOs</a>, as well as "male", "female", and other more esoteric genders, there is a gender called "splat", which uses an asterisk to replace the letters that differ in standard English gender pronouns. For example, <i>h*</i> is used rather than <i>him</i> or <i>her</i>. Also, asterisks are used to signify doing an action, for example, "*<i>action</i>*".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Game_show" title="Game show">Game show</a> producer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Goodson" title="Mark Goodson">Mark Goodson</a> used a six-pointed asterisk as his trademark. It is featured prominently on many set pieces from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Price_Is_Right_(U.S._game_show)" title="The Price Is Right (U.S. game show)">The Price Is Right</a>.</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a> players put an asterisk after a word to indicate that an illegal play was made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_genetics">Human genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Human genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In human genetics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/*_(haplogroup)" title="* (haplogroup)">*</a> is used to denote that someone is a member of a <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup" title="Haplogroup">haplogroup</a> and not any of its subclades (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/*_(haplogroup)" title="* (haplogroup)">* (haplogroup)</a>).</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, an asterisk is placed before a word or phrase to indicate that it is not used, or there are no records of it being in use. This is used in several ways depending on what is being discussed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_linguistics">Historical linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Historical linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">historical linguistics</a>, the asterisk marks words or phrases that are not directly recorded in texts or other media, and that are therefore <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_reconstruction" title="Linguistic reconstruction">reconstructed</a> on the basis of other linguistic material (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparative_method" title="Comparative method">comparative method</a>).</p>
<p>In the following example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> word <i>ainlif</i> is a reconstructed form.</p>
<ul>
<li>*<i>ainlif</i> â†’ <i>endleofan</i> â†’ <i>eleven</i></li>
</ul>
<p>A double asterisk indicates a form that would be expected according to a rule, but is not actually found. That is, it indicates a <i>reconstructed</i> form that is not found or used, and in place of which <i>another</i> form is found in actual usage:</p>
<ul>
<li>For the plural, **<i>kubar</i> would be expected, but separate masculine plural <i>akÄbir</i> Ø£ÙƒØ§Ø¨Ø± and feminine plural <i>kubrayÄt</i> ÙƒØ¨Ø±ÙŠØ§Øª are found as irregular forms.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Generative_linguistics">Generative linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Generative linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" title="Generative linguistics">generative linguistics</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, an asterisk in front of a word or phrase indicates that the word or phrase is not used because it is <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">ungrammatical</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>wake her up / *wake up her (in Standard American English)</li>
</ul>
<p>An asterisk before a parenthesis indicates that the lack of the word or phrase inside is ungrammatical, while an asterisk after the opening bracket of the parenthesis indicates that the existence of the word or phrase inside is ungrammatical.</p>
<ul>
<li>go *(to) the station - Here, "go the station" would be ungrammatical.</li>
<li>go (*to) home - Here, "go to home" would be ungrammatical.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguity">Ambiguity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ambiguity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Since a word marked with an asterisk could mean either "unattested" or "impossible", it is important in some contexts to distinguish these meanings. In general, authors retain asterisks for "unattested", and prefix Ë£, **, or a superscript "?" for the latter meaning.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_texts">Religious texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Religious texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_results">Statistical results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Statistical results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many scientific publications, the asterisk is employed as a shorthand to denote the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">statistical significance</a> of results when testing <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypotheses</a>. When the likelihood that a result occurred by chance alone is below a certain level, one or more asterisks are displayed. Popular significance levels are &lt;0.05 (*), &lt;0.01 (**), and &lt;0.001 (***).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telephony">Telephony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Telephony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch-Tone" title="Touch-Tone">Touch-Tone</a> <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> keypad, the asterisk (called <i>star</i>, or less commonly, <i>palm</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Sextile" title="Sextile">sextile</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-p-17">[17]</a></sup> is one of the two special keys (the other is the <a href="/wiki/Number_sign" title="Number sign">number sign</a> (<i>pound sign</i> or <i>hash</i>, <i>hex</i> or, less commonly, <i>octothorp</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-p-17">[17]</a></sup> or <i>square</i>)), and is found to the left of the zero. They are used to navigate menus in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch-Tone" title="Touch-Tone">Touch-Tone</a> systems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_mail" title="Voice mail">Voice mail</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Vertical_service_code" title="Vertical service code">Vertical service codes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typography">Typography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asterisk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Typography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The asterisk is used to call out a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Footnote" title="Footnote">footnote</a>, especially when there is only one on the page. Less commonly, multiple asterisks are used to denote different footnotes on a page (i.e., *, **, ***). Typically, an asterisk is positioned after a word or phrase and preceding its accompanying footnote. Other characters are used for this purpose, including â€ , â€¡, superscript numbers (as in Wikipedia), etc. In marketing and advertising, asterisks or other symbols are used to refer readers discreetly to terms or conditions for a certain statement, the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_print" title="Small print">small print</a>".</li>
<li>The asterisk is placed after all other punctuation marks (for example, commas, colons, or periods) except for the dash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>

<ul>
<li>A group of three asterisks arranged in a triangular formation is called an <a href="/wiki/Asterism_(typography)" title="Asterism (typography)">asterism</a>.</li>
<li>One or more asterisks may be used as censorship over all or part of a word.</li>
<li>Asterisks are sometimes used as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Bullet_(typography)" title="Bullet (typography)">typographical bullets</a> to indicate items of a list.</li>
<li>Asterisks can be used in textual media to represent *<a href="/wiki/Emphasis_(typography)" title="Emphasis (typography)">emphasis</a>* when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bold_text" title="Bold text">bold</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italic_text" title="Italic text">italic text</a> is not available (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">text messaging</a>).</li>
<li>Asterisks may denote corrections to misspelling or misstatements in previous electronic messages, particularly when replacement or retraction of a previous writing is not possible, such as with "immediate delivery" messages or "instant messages". Usually this takes the form of a message consisting solely of the corrected text, with an asterisk placed prior to the correction. For example, one might send a message reading "*morning" to correct the misspelling in the message "I had a good mroning".</li>
<li>Bounding asterisks as "a kind of self-describing stage direction", as linguist <a href="/wiki/Ben_Zimmer" title="Ben Zimmer">Ben Zimmer</a> has put it. For example, in "<i>Another gas station robbery *sigh*</i>," the writer uses *sigh* to express disappointment (but does not necessarily literally sigh).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
