<p><b>I</b> (И и; italics: <span style="font-family: times, 'Times New Roman', serif; font-size: larger"><i>И</i> <i>и</i></span>) is a letter used in almost all ancient and modern <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_alphabets" title="Cyrillic alphabets">Cyrillic alphabets</a>.</p>
<p>It commonly represents the <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">close front unrounded vowel</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>, like the pronunciation of ⟨i⟩ in "mach<b>i</b>ne", or the <a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front unrounded vowel">near-close near-front unrounded vowel</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span>, like the pronunciation of ⟨i⟩ in "b<b>i</b>n".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_(Cyrillic)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cyrillic letter <b>I</b> was derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eta_(letter)" title="Eta (letter)">Greek letter Eta</a> (Η η). This is why the earliest (up to the 13th century) shape of Cyrillic ⟨И⟩ was ⟨H⟩.</p>
<p>The name of the Cyrillic letter I in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Early Cyrillic alphabet">Early Cyrillic alphabet</a> was <span class="script-slavonic" style="font-family: 'Fedorovsk Unicode', 'Fedorovsk Unicode TT', 'Menaion Unicode', 'Menaion Unicode TT', 'Monomakh Unicode', 'Monomakh Unicode TT', 'Ponomar Unicode', 'Ponomar Unicode TT', BukyVede, 'Kliment Std', 'RomanCyrillic Std', 'Monomachus', 'Old Standard', 'Old Standard TT', Dilyana, Menaion, 'Menaion Medieval', Lazov, Code2000, 'DejaVu Sans', 'DejaVu Serif', Code2001, 'FreeSerif', 'TITUS Cyberbit Basic', 'Charis SIL', 'Doulos SIL', 'Chrysanthi Unicode', 'Bitstream Cyberbit', 'Bitstream CyberBase', Thryomanes, 'Lucida Grande', 'FreeSans', 'Arial Unicode MS', 'Microsoft Sans Serif', 'Lucida Sans Unicode';" title="Slavonic">ижє</span> (<i>iže</i>), meaning "which".</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_numerals" title="Cyrillic numerals">Cyrillic numeral system</a>, the Cyrillic letter I had a value of 8.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Early Cyrillic alphabet">Early Cyrillic alphabet</a> there was little or no distinction between the letter ⟨И⟩ and the letter ⟨І⟩ which was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Iota" title="Iota">Greek letter Iota</a> (Ι ι). They both remained in the alphabetical repertoire because they represented different numbers in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrillic_numeral_system" title="Cyrillic numeral system">Cyrillic numeral system</a>, eight and ten. They are, therefore, sometimes referred to as <i>octal I</i> and <i>decimal I</i>.</p>
<p>Today they co-exist in Church Slavonic, with no pronunciation difference; and in Ukrainian, representing actual pronunciation differences. Other modern orthographies for Slavic languages eliminated one of the two letters in alphabet reforms of the 19th or 20th centuries: Russian, Macedonian, Serbian and Bulgarian languages use only ⟨И⟩, and <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">Belarusian</a> uses only ⟨І⟩.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Form">Form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_(Cyrillic)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally, Cyrillic ⟨И⟩ had the shape identical to the capital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eta_(letter)" title="Eta (letter)">Greek letter Eta</a> ⟨Η⟩. Later, the middle stroke was turned counterclockwise resulting in the modern form looking like a mirrored capital <a href="/wiki/N" title="N">Latin letter N</a> ⟨N⟩ (this is why ⟨И⟩ is used in <a href="/wiki/Faux_Cyrillic" title="Faux Cyrillic">faux Cyrillic</a> typography). But the style of the two letters is not fully identical: in roman fonts, <span style="font-family: times, 'Times New Roman', serif; font-size: larger">⟨И⟩</span> has heavier vertical strokes and serifs on all four corners, whereas <span style="font-family: times, 'Times New Roman', serif; font-size: larger">⟨N⟩</span> has a heavier diagonal stroke and lacks a serif on the bottom-right corner.</p>
<p>In roman and oblique fonts, the lowercase letter ⟨и⟩ has the same shape as the uppercase letter ⟨И⟩. In italic fonts, the lowercase letter <span style="font-family: times, 'Times New Roman', serif; font-size: larger">⟨<i>и</i>⟩</span> looks like the italic form of the lowercase Latin U <span style="font-family: times, 'Times New Roman', serif; font-size: larger">⟨<i>u</i>⟩</span>. Both capital and small hand-written forms of the Cyrillic letter I look like hand-written forms of the Latin letter U.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_(Cyrillic)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the 1930s, ⟨и⟩ has been the tenth letter of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">Russian alphabet</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, it represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>, like the <i>i</i> in mach<b>i</b>ne except after some consonants (see below). In Russian, it typically denotes a preceding <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">soft consonant</a> and, therefore, is considered the soft counterpart to ⟨<a href="/wiki/Yery" title="Yery">ы</a>⟩ (which represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɨ]</span>) but, unlike other "soft" vowels (⟨<a href="/wiki/E_(Cyrillic)" title="E (Cyrillic)">е</a>⟩, ⟨<a href="/wiki/Yo_(Cyrillic)" title="Yo (Cyrillic)">ё</a>⟩, ⟨<a href="/wiki/Yu_(Cyrillic)" title="Yu (Cyrillic)">ю</a>⟩ and ⟨<a href="/wiki/Ya_(Cyrillic)" title="Ya (Cyrillic)">я</a>⟩), ⟨и⟩ in isolation is not preceded by the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> semivowel.</p>
<p>⟨И⟩ pronounced as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɨ]</span> in ⟨жи⟩ (sounds like ⟨жы⟩ <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʐɨ]</span>), ⟨ши⟩ (sounds like ⟨шы⟩ <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʂɨ]</span>) and ⟨ци⟩ (sounds like ⟨цы⟩ <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t͡sɨ]</span>), because in Russian, the sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i]</span> is inarticulable after "zh" ⟨ж⟩, "sh" ⟨ш⟩ and "ts" ⟨ц⟩.</p>
<p>In Kazakh is romanized in an loanwords with the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8F" title="Ï">I with diaeresis</a>, for the sounds /iy/, derived from Latin <a href="/wiki/%C3%8F" title="Ï">Ï</a> with <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">diaeresis</a>, <a href="/wiki/I" title="I">I</a> letter is the <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh</a>, it uses the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> Ï instead, appeared Latin Ï used in the И.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">Belarusian</a>, the sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> is represented by another letter ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_I" title="Ukrainian I">і</a>⟩, sometimes called <i>Ukrainian I</i>, removed from the modern Russian alphabet.</p>
<p>Ukrainian and Belarusian ⟨і⟩ sounds like Russian ⟨и⟩ <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i]</span>, but a clearly distinct sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front unrounded vowel">ɪ</a>]</span> is represented by ⟨и⟩ in Ukrainian, which differs only slightly from Russian ⟨ы⟩ and is perceived as ⟨ы⟩ by a Russian speaker.</p>
<p>The letter ⟨и⟩ is the eleventh letter of the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_alphabet" title="Ukrainian alphabet">Ukrainian alphabet</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Serbian Cyrillic alphabet">Serbian Cyrillic alphabet</a>, ⟨и⟩ is the tenth letter of the alphabet. In <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, it represents <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>, like the <i>i</i> in mach<b>i</b>ne or <i>i</i> in b<i>i</i>ll. In the <a href="/wiki/Gaj%27s_Latin_alphabet" title="Gaj's Latin alphabet">Latin Serbian alphabet</a>, the same vowel is represented by "I/i".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>, it is the eleventh letter of the alphabet and represents the sound /i/.</p>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterated</a> from Russian as ⟨i⟩ or from Ukrainian as ⟨y⟩ or ⟨i⟩, depending on the <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanization</a> system. See <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">romanization of Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ukrainian" title="Romanization of Ukrainian">romanization of Ukrainian</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tuvan_language" title="Tuvan language">Tuvan</a> the Cyrillic letter can be written as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_vowel" title="Double vowel">double vowel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accented_forms_and_derived_letters">Accented forms and derived letters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_(Cyrillic)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accented forms and derived letters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vowel represented by ⟨и⟩, as well as almost any other Slavonic vowel, can be stressed or unstressed. Stressed variants are sometimes (in special texts, like dictionaries, or to prevent ambiguity) graphically marked by <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">grave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Double_grave_accent" title="Double grave accent">double grave</a> or <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">circumflex</a> accent marks.</p>
<p>Special Serbian texts also use ⟨и⟩ with a <a href="/wiki/Macron" title="Macron">macron</a> to represent long unstressed variant of the sound. Serbian ⟨и⟩ with a circumflex can be unstressed as well; then, it represents the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a> of plural forms to distinguish them from other similar forms.</p>
<p>Modern Church Slavonic orthography uses the smooth breathing sign (Greek and Church Slavonic: <i>psili</i>, Latin: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiritus_lenis" title="Spiritus lenis">spiritus lenis</a></i>) above the initial vowels (just for tradition, as there is no difference in pronunciation). It can be combined with acute or grave accents, if necessary.</p>
<p>None of those combinations is considered as a separate letter of respective alphabet, but one of them (⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%8D" title="Ѝ">Ѝ</a>⟩) has an individual code position in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>.</p>
<p>⟨И⟩ with a <a href="/wiki/Breve" title="Breve">breve</a> forms the letter ⟨<a href="/wiki/Short_I" title="Short I">й</a>⟩ for the consonant <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> or a similar semivowel, like the <b>y</b> in English "<b>y</b>es." The form has been used regularly in Church Slavonic since the 16th century, but it officially became a separate letter of alphabet much later (in Russian, only in 1918). The original name of ⟨й⟩ was <i>I s kratkoy</i> ('I with the short [line]'), later <i>I kratkoye</i> ('short I') in <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>. It is known similarly as <i>I kratko</i> in <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a> but as <i>Yot</i> in Ukrainian.</p>
<p>Cyrillic alphabets of non-Slavic languages have additional ⟨и⟩-based letters, like ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D3%A4" title="Ӥ">Ӥ</a>⟩ or ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D3%A2" title="Ӣ">Ӣ</a>⟩.</p>
