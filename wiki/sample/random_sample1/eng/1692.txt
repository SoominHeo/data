<div class="thumbcaption">Early Cyrillic numeral character Koppa (=90)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Koppa</b> (Ҁ ҁ; italics: <span style="font-family: times, Times New Roman, serif; font-size: larger"><i>Ҁ ҁ</i></span>) is an archaic letter-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrillic_numeral" title="Cyrillic numeral">numeral character</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic script</a>. Its form (and modern name) are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Koppa_(letter)" title="Koppa (letter)">Greek letter Koppa</a> (Ϙ ϙ).</p>
<p>Koppa was used as a numeral character in the oldest Cyrillic manuscripts, representing the value 90 (exactly as its Greek ancestor did). It was replaced relatively early by the <a href="/wiki/Che_(Cyrillic)" title="Che (Cyrillic)">Cyrillic letter Che</a> (Ч ч), which is similar in appearance and originally had no numeric value. Isolated examples of Ч used as a numeral are found in the East and South Slavonic areas as early as the eleventh century, though Koppa continued in regular use into the fourteenth century. In some varieties of Western Cyrillic, however, Koppa was retained, and Ч used with the value 60, replacing the Cyrillic letter <a href="/wiki/Ksi_(Cyrillic)" title="Ksi (Cyrillic)">Ksi</a> (Ѯ ѯ).</p>
<p>Cyrillic Koppa never had a phonetic value and was never used as a letter by any national language using Cyrillic. However, certain modern textbooks and dictionaries of Old Church Slavonic language insert this character among other letters of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Early Cyrillic alphabet">early Cyrillic alphabet</a>, either between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%9F" title="П">П</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%A0" title="Р">Р</a> (to reproduce the Greek alphabetical order) or at the very end of the list.</p>
