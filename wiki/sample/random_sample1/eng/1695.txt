<p><b>Yodh</b> (also spelled <b>Yud</b>, <b>Yod</b>, <b>Jod</b>, or <b>Jodh</b>) is the tenth <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letter</a> of the Semitic <a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">abjads</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician</a> Yōd <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoenician_yodh.svg"><img alt="Phoenician yodh.svg" data-file-height="85" data-file-width="85" height="10" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Phoenician_yodh.svg/10px-Phoenician_yodh.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Phoenician_yodh.svg/15px-Phoenician_yodh.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Phoenician_yodh.svg/20px-Phoenician_yodh.svg.png 2x" width="10"/></a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">Hebrew</a> Yōd <span lang="he">י</span>, <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet" title="Aramaic alphabet">Aramaic</a> Yodh <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yod.svg"><img alt="Yod.svg" data-file-height="33" data-file-width="31" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yod.svg/10px-Yod.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yod.svg/15px-Yod.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yod.svg/20px-Yod.svg.png 2x" width="10"/></a>, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_alphabet" title="Syriac alphabet">Syriac</a> Yōḏ ܚ, and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_alphabet" title="Arabic alphabet">Arabic</a> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">Yāʾ</span> <span lang="ar">ي</span> (in <a href="/wiki/Abjad_numerals" title="Abjad numerals">abjadi order</a>, 28th in modern order). Its sound value is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">j</a>/</span> in all languages for which it is used; in many languages, it also serves as a <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">long vowel</a>, representing <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">iː</a>/</span>.</p>
<p>The Phoenician letter gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Iota" title="Iota">Iota</a> (Ι),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/I" title="I">I</a>, <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">J</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dotted_I_(Cyrillic)" title="Dotted I (Cyrillic)">І</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_I" title="Short I">Й</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_alphabet" title="Coptic alphabet">Coptic</a> iauda (Ⲓ) and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_alphabet" title="Gothic alphabet">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Isaz" title="Isaz">eis</a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gothic_letter_eis.svg"><img alt="Gothic letter eis.svg" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gothic_letter_eis.svg/4px-Gothic_letter_eis.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gothic_letter_eis.svg/6px-Gothic_letter_eis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gothic_letter_eis.svg/8px-Gothic_letter_eis.svg.png 2x" width="4"/></a>.</p>

It may be related to the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphic</a> of an arm:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic_y.C4.81.CA.BC">Arabic yāʼ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arabic yāʼ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It is pronounced in four ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>As a consonant, it is pronounced as a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal approximant</a> <b><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span></b>, typically at the beginnings of words in front of short or long vowels.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">long</a> <b><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span></b> usually in the middle or end of words. In this case it has no diacritic, but could be marked with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasrah" title="Kasrah">kasra</a> in the preceding letter in some traditions.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">long</a> <b><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span></b> In many <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Arabic" title="Varieties of Arabic">dialects</a>, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">monophthongization</a> that underwent the diphthong <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aj/</span> in most of the words.</li>
<li>A part of a <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a>, <b><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aj/</span></b>. Then, it has no diacritic but could be marked with a sukun in some traditions. The preceding consonant could have no diacritic or have <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatha" title="Fatha">fatḥa</a></span> sign, hinting to the first vowel in the diphthong, i.e. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>As a vowel, yāʾ can serve as the "seat" of the <a href="/wiki/Hamza" title="Hamza">hamza</a>: <span lang="ar">ئ</span></p>
<p>Yāʾ serves several functions in the Arabic language. Yāʾ as a prefix is the marker for a singular <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">imperfective</a> verb, as in <span lang="ar">يَكْتُب</span> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">yaktub</span></i> "he writes" from the root <span lang="ar">ك-ت-ب</span> K-T-B ("write, writing"). Yāʾ with a <a href="/wiki/Shadda" title="Shadda">shadda</a> is particularly used to turn a noun into an adjective, called a <i>nisbah</i> (<span lang="ar">نِسْبَة</span>). For instance, <span lang="ar">مِصْر</span> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">Miṣr</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>) → <span lang="ar">مِصْرِيّ</span> <i>Miṣriyy</i> (Egyptian). The transformation can be more abstract; for instance, <span lang="ar">مَوْضَوع</span> <i>mawḍūʿ</i> (matter, object) → <span lang="ar">مَوْضُوعِيّ</span> <i>mawḍūʿiyy</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/objectivity" title="wikt:objectivity">objective</a>). Still other uses of this function can be a bit further from the root: <span lang="ar">إِشْتِرَاك</span> <i>ishtirāk</i> (cooperation) → <span lang="ar">إِشْتِرَاكِيّ</span> <i>ishtirākiyy</i> (<a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a>). The common pronunciation of the final <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-ijj/</span> is most often pronounced as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Arabic" title="Help:IPA for Arabic">[i]</a></span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Arabic" title="Help:IPA for Arabic">[iː]</a></span>.</p>
<p>A form similar to but distinguished from yāʾ is the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">ʾalif maqṣūrah</span></i> (<span lang="ar">أَلِف مَقْصُورَة</span>) "broken <a href="/wiki/Aleph" title="Aleph">alif</a>", with the form <span lang="ar">ى</span>. It indicates a final long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span>.</p>
<p>In Egypt, Sudan and sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, the final form is always <span lang="ar">ى</span> (without dots), both in handwriting and in print, representing both final <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-iː/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-aː/</span>. <span lang="ar">ى</span> representing final <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-aː/</span> (<a href="/wiki/DIN_31635" title="DIN 31635">DIN 31635</a> transliteration: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">ā</span>) is less likely to occur in Modern Standard Arabic. In this case, it is commonly known as, especially in Egypt, <span lang="ar">أَلِف لَيِّنَة</span> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">ʾalif layyinah</span></i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Egyptian_Arabic" title="Help:IPA for Egyptian Arabic">[ˈʔælef læjˈjenæ]</a></span>. In Egypt, it is always short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[-æ, -ɑ]</span> if used in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Arabic" title="Egyptian Arabic">Egyptian Arabic</a> and most commonly short in Modern Standard Arabic, as well.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Persian_alphabet" title="Persian alphabet">Persian alphabet</a>, the letter is generally called <i>ye</i> following <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian-language</a> custom. In its final form, the letter does not have dots (<span lang="fa">ی</span>), much like the Arabic <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alif_maqsura" title="Alif maqsura">ʾalif maqṣūrah</a></span></i> or, more to the point, much like the custom in Egypt, Sudan and sometimes Maghreb. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Returned_y.C4.81.CA.BE">Returned yāʾ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Returned yāʾ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In different calligraphic styles like the <a href="/wiki/Hijazi_script" title="Hijazi script">Hijazi script</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kufic" title="Kufic">Kufic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nasta%CA%BFl%C4%ABq_script" title="Nastaʿlīq script">Nastaʿlīq script</a>, a final <i>yāʾ</i> might have a particular shape with the descender turned to the right (<span class="Nastaliq" style="font-size: 125%; font-family: 'Jameel Noori Nastaleeq', 'Urdu Typesetting', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu Draft', 'Hussaini Nastaleeq', 'AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq', IranNastaliq, 'Awami Nastaliq', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta4', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta3', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta2', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta1', 'Nafees Nastaleeq', 'Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01', 'Pak Nastaleeq', 'PDMS_Jauhar', 'Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq';" title="Nastaliq">ـے</span>), called <i>al-yāʾ al-mardūdah/al-rājiʿah</i> ("returned, recurred <i>yāʾ</i>"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> either with two dots or without them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Urdu_alphabet" title="Urdu alphabet">Urdu</a> this is called <i><a href="/wiki/Ba%E1%B9%9B%C4%AB_ye" title="Baṛī ye">baṛī ye</a></i> ("big ye"), but is an independent letter used for /ɛː, eː/ and differs from the basic ye (<i>choṭī ye</i>, "little ye"). For this reason the letter has its own <a href="/wiki/Code_point" title="Code point">code point</a> in Unicode. Nevertheless, its initial and medial forms are not different from the other <i>ye</i> (practically <i>baṛī ye</i> is not used in these positions).</p>

<p>Hebrew spelling: <big><span lang="he">יוֹד</span></big> <span style="unicode-bidi:bidi-override">;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></span> colloquial <big><span lang="he">יוּד</span></big></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In both Biblical and modern <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, Yud represents a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal approximant</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">j</a>]</span>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yud is a <a href="/wiki/Mater_lectionis" title="Mater lectionis">mater lectionis</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Aleph" title="Aleph">Aleph</a>, <a href="/wiki/He_(letter)" title="He (letter)">He</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waw_(letter)" title="Waw (letter)">Vav</a>. At the end of words with a vowel or when marked with a <a href="/wiki/Shva#Shva_Na.E1.B8.A5" title="Shva">sh'va nach</a>, it represents the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a>, such as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ei/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ai/</span>, or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oi/</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Gematria" title="Gematria">gematria</a>, Yud represents the number ten.</p>
<p>As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefix_(linguistics)" title="Prefix (linguistics)">prefix</a>, it designates the third person singular (or plural, with a <a href="/wiki/Waw_(letter)" title="Waw (letter)">Vav</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a>) in the future tense.</p>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a>, it indicates first person singular possessive; <i>av</i> (father) becomes <i>avi</i> (my father).</p>
<p>"Yod" in the Hebrew language signifies <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_religion">In religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yodh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Two Yuds in a row designate the name of God <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_Judaism#Adonai" title="Names of God in Judaism">Adonai</a> and in pointed texts are written with the vowels of Adonai; this is done as well with the <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Tetragrammaton</a>.</p>
<p>As Yud is the smallest letter, much <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">kabbalistic</a> and mystical significance is attached to it. According to the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> mentioned it during the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5#Antitheses" title="Matthew 5">Antithesis of the Law</a>, when he says: "One jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled." Jot, or iota, refers to the letter Yud; it was often overlooked by scribes because of its size and position as a <a href="/wiki/Mater_lectionis" title="Mater lectionis">mater lectionis</a>. In modern Hebrew, the phrase "tip of the Yud" refers to a small and insignificant thing, and someone who "worries about the tip of a Yud" is someone who is picky and meticulous about small details.</p>
<p>Much <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">kabbalistic</a> and mystical significance is also attached to it because of its gematria value as ten, which is an important number in Judaism, and its place in the name of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
