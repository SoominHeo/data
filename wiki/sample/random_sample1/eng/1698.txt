<p><b>く</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a>, or <b>ク</b> in <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a>, is one of the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a>, which each represent one <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">mora</a>. Both represent <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ku͍]</span>, and their shapes come from the <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> 久.</p>
<p>This kana may have a <a href="/wiki/Dakuten" title="Dakuten">dakuten</a> added, transforming it into <b>ぐ</b> in hiragana, <b>グ</b> in katakana, and <i>gu</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn romanization</a>. The dakuten's addition also changes the sound of the syllable represented, to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡu͍]</span> in initial positions, and varying between <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋu͍]</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣu͍]</span> in the middle of words. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handakuten" title="Handakuten">handakuten</a> (゜) does not occur with <i>ku</i> in normal Japanese text, but it may be used by linguists to indicate a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal</a> pronunciation <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋu͍]</span>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_language#Writing" title="Ainu language">Ainu language</a>, the katakana ク can be written as small ㇰ, representing a final k sound as in アイヌイタㇰ <i>Ainu itak</i> (Ainu language).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This was developed along with other extended katakana to represent sounds in Ainu that are not found in standard Japanese katakana.</p>
