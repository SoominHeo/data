<p><b>し</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a>, or <b>シ</b> in <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a>, is one of the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a>, which each represent one <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">mora</a>. Both represent the phoneme <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/si/</span> although for <a href="/wiki/Japanese_phonology" title="Japanese phonology">phonological reasons</a>, the actual pronunciation is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Japanese" title="Help:IPA for Japanese">[ɕi]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Ja-Shi.oga" title="File:Ja-Shi.oga"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/Ja-Shi.oga" title="Ja-Shi.oga">listen</a>)</small>. The shapes of these kana have origins in the character 之. The katakana form has become increasingly popular as an <a href="/wiki/Emoticon" title="Emoticon">emoticon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> due to its resemblance to a smiling face, thus this variant form ㋛ was created.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This character may be combined with a <a href="/wiki/Dakuten" title="Dakuten">dakuten</a>, forming じ in hiragana, ジ in katakana, and <i>ji</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn romanization</a>; the pronunciation becomes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/zi/</span> (phonetically <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d͡ʑi]</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʑi]</span> in the middle of words).</p>
<p>The dakuten form of this character is used when transliterating "di" occasionally, as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E3%83%81" title="チ">チ</a>'s dakuten form; for example, <i>Aladdin</i> is written as アラジン <i>Arajin</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> is written as ラジオ.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_language#Writing" title="Ainu language">Ainu language</a>, シ is used to represent the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ʃi</span> sound. It can also be written as a small ㇱ to represent a final s sound, pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɕ</span>.</p>
