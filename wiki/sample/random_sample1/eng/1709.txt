<p><b>The Venerable</b> is used as a style or epithet in several <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian churches</a>. It is also the common <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> translation of a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> titles, and is used as a word of praise in some cases.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, after a deceased Catholic has been declared a <a href="/wiki/Servant_of_God" title="Servant of God">Servant of God</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Bishop_(Catholic_Church)" title="Bishop (Catholic Church)">bishop</a> and proposed for beatification by the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>, such a servant of God may next be declared <i>venerable</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Heroic_virtue" title="Heroic virtue">heroic in virtue</a>") during the investigation and process leading to possible <a href="/wiki/Canonization" title="Canonization">canonization</a> as a saint. Before a person is considered to be <i>venerable</i>, that person must be declared as such by a proclamation, approved by the Pope, of having lived a life that was "heroic in virtue", the virtues being the <a href="/wiki/Theological_virtues" title="Theological virtues">theological virtues</a> of faith, hope, and charity and the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_virtues" title="Cardinal virtues">cardinal virtues</a> of prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance. The next steps are <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">beatification</a>, from which point the person is referred to as <i>The Blessed</i>, and finally canonization, from which point he is referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">Saint</a></i>. For example, Popes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_XII" title="Pius XII">Pius XII</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> were both declared venerable by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> in December 2009 and John Paul II was declared saint in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2013-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other examples are <a href="/wiki/Princess_Louise_of_France_(1737%E2%80%931787)" title="Princess Louise of France (1737–1787)">Princess Louise of France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Libermann" title="Francis Libermann">Francis Libermann</a>, and Don <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza" title="Juan de Palafox y Mendoza">Juan de Palafox y Mendoza</a>, seventeenth-century bishop of <a href="/wiki/Puebla" title="Puebla">Puebla</a>, Mexico.</p>
<p>The 7th/8th century English monk <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Bede" title="St. Bede">St. Bede</a> was referred to as <i>venerable</i> soon after his death and is still very often called "the Venerable Bede" despite having been canonized in 1899.</p>
<p>This is also the <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> used for hermits of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthusian" title="Carthusian">Carthusian</a> Order, in place of the usual term of "Reverend".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglican">Anglican</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Venerable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anglican">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, "The Venerable" (abbreviated as "the Ven") is the style usually given to an <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon" title="Archdeacon">archdeacon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Venerable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodoxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_venerable_people_(Eastern_Orthodox)" title="List of venerable people (Eastern Orthodox)">List of venerable people (Eastern Orthodox)</a></div>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a> the term "Venerable" is commonly used as the English-language translation of the title given to <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monastic</a> saints (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <i>Hosios</i>, <a href="/wiki/Church_Slavonic_language" title="Church Slavonic language">Church Slavonic</a>: <i>Prepodobni</i>; both Greek and Church Slavonic forms are masculinum).</p>
<p>A monastic saint who was <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyred</a> for the Orthodox faith is referred to as "Venerable Martyr".</p>
<p>In the 20th century, some English-language Orthodox sources began to use the term "Venerable" to refer to a righteous person who was a candidate for <a href="/wiki/Glorification" title="Glorification">glorification</a> (canonization), most famously in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_John_of_Shanghai_and_San_Francisco" title="Saint John of Shanghai and San Francisco">Saint John of Shanghai and San Francisco</a>; however, this has not altered the original usage of this term in reference to monastic saints.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Venerable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4801" data-file-width="3281" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg/100px-Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg/150px-Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg/200px-Venerable_Galboda_Gnanissara_Thera.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>

<p>In Buddhism, the Western style of <i>Venerable</i> (also abbreviated as Ven.) is given to ordained <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">Buddhist monks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhuni" title="Bhikkhuni">nuns</a> and also to novices (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%9Ar%C4%81ma%E1%B9%87era" title="Śrāmaṇera">Śrāmaṇeras</a>). The title of <i>Master</i> may be followed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for senior members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sangha_(Buddhism)" title="Sangha (Buddhism)">Sangha</a>. "Venerable", along with "Reverend" (Rev.) is used as a western alternative to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahathera" title="Mahathera">Mahathera</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> branch and <i>Făshī</i> (法師) in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
