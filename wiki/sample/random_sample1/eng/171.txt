<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dot-com bubble</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>dot-com bubble</b> (also known as the <b>dot-com boom</b>, the <b>tech bubble,</b> the <b>Internet bubble</b>, the <b>dot-com collapse</b>, and the <b>information technology bubble</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) was a historic <a href="/wiki/Economic_bubble" title="Economic bubble">speculative bubble</a> covering roughly 1995–2001 during which <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock markets</a> in industrialized nations saw their equity value rise rapidly from growth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_industry" title="Quaternary sector of industry">Internet sector</a> and related fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> While the latter part was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boom_and_bust" title="Boom and bust">boom and bust</a> cycle, the <b>Internet boom</b> is sometimes meant to refer to the steady commercial growth of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> with the advent of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, as exemplified by the first release of the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">Mosaic web browser</a> in 1993, and continuing through the 1990s.</p>
<p>The period was marked by the founding (and, in many cases, spectacular failure) of several new <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>-based companies commonly referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-coms</a></i>. Companies could cause their stock prices to increase by simply adding an <a href="/wiki/Internet-related_prefixes" title="Internet-related prefixes">"e-" prefix</a> to their name or a "<a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a>" <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a>, which one author called "<a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefix</a> investing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A combination of rapidly increasing stock prices, market confidence that the companies would turn future profits, individual <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculation</a> in stocks, and widely available <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> created an environment in which many investors were willing to overlook traditional metrics, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P/E_ratio" title="P/E ratio">P/E ratio</a>, in favor of basing confidence on technological advancements. By the end of the 1990s, the NASDAQ hit a price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio of 200, a truly astonishing plateau that dwarfed Japan's peak P/E ratio of 80 a decade earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The collapse of the bubble took place during 1999–2001. Some companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Pets.com" title="Pets.com">pets.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/Webvan" title="Webvan">Webvan</a>, failed completely. Others – such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cisco" title="Cisco">Cisco</a>, whose stock declined by 86% – lost a large portion of their market capitalization but remained stable and profitable. Some, such as <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay.com</a>, later recovered and even surpassed their dot-com-bubble peaks. The stock of <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> came to exceed $700 per share, for example, after having gone from $107 to $7 in the crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bubble_growth">Bubble growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bubble growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:269px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="500" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/267px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/401px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/534px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 2x" width="267"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ Composite</a> index spiked in the late 1990s and then fell sharply as a result of the dot-com bubble.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the rise in the commercial growth of the Internet, venture capitalists saw record-setting growth as "dot-com" companies experienced meteoric rises in their stock prices and therefore moved faster and with less caution than usual, choosing to mitigate the risk by investing in many contenders and letting the market decide which would succeed. The low interest rates of 1998–99 helped increase the start-up capital amounts. A canonical "dot-com" company's <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business model</a> relied on harnessing <a href="/wiki/Network_effect" title="Network effect">network effects</a> by operating at a sustained net loss and building <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Mind_share" title="Mind share">mind share</a>). These companies offered their services or end product for free with the expectation that they could build enough brand awareness to charge profitable rates for their services later. The motto "get big fast" reflected this strategy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This occurred in industrialized nations due to the reducing "digital divide" in the late 1990s, and early 2000s. Previously, individuals were less capable of accessing the Internet, many stopped by lack of local access/connectivity to the infrastructure, and/or the failure to understand use for Internet technologies. The absence of infrastructure and a lack of understanding were two major obstacles that previously obstructed mass connectivity. For these reasons, individuals had limited capabilities in what they could do and what they could achieve in accessing technology. Increased means of connectivity to the Internet than previously available allowed the use of ICT (<a href="/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology" title="Information and communications technology">information and communications technology</a>) to progress from a luxury good to a necessity good. As connectivity grew, so did the potential for venture capitalists to take advantage of the growing field. The functionalism, or impacts of technologies driven from the cost effectiveness of new Internet websites ultimately influenced the demand growth during this time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soaring_stocks">Soaring stocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soaring stocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In financial markets, a <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_bubble" title="Stock market bubble">stock market bubble</a> is a self-perpetuating rise or boom in the share prices of stocks of a particular industry; the term may be used with certainty only in retrospect after share prices have crashed. A bubble occurs when speculators note the fast increase in value and decide to buy in anticipation of further rises, rather than because the shares are undervalued. Typically, during a bubble, many companies thus become grossly overvalued. When the bubble "bursts", the share prices fall dramatically. The prices of many non-technology stocks increased in tandem and were also pushed up to valuations discorrelated relative to fundamentals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>American news media, including respected business publications such as <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i>, encouraged the public to invest in risky companies, despite many of the companies' disregard for basic financial and even legal principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Smith_(author)" title="Andrew Smith (author)">Andrew Smith</a> argued that the financial industry's handling of <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offerings</a> tended to benefit the banks and initial investors rather than the companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-7">[7]</a></sup> This is because company staff were typically barred from reselling their shares for a <a href="/wiki/Lock-up_period" title="Lock-up period">lock-in period</a> of 12 to 18 months, and so did not benefit from the common pattern of a huge short-lived share price spike on the day of the launch. In contrast, the financiers and other initial investors were typically entitled to sell at the peak price, and so could immediately profit from short-term price rises. Smith argues that the high profitability of the IPOs to <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> was a significant factor in the course of events of the bubble. He writes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"But did the kids [the often young dotcom entrepreneurs] dupe the establishment by drawing them into fake companies, or did the establishment dupe the kids by introducing them to <a href="/wiki/Mammon" title="Mammon">Mammon</a> and charging a commission on it?"</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In spite of this, however, a few company founders made vast fortunes when their companies were bought out at an early stage in the dot-com stock market bubble. These early successes made the bubble even more buoyant. An unprecedented amount of personal investing occurred during the boom, and the press reported the phenomenon of people quitting their jobs to become full-time <a href="/wiki/Day_trading" title="Day trading">day traders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Academics Preston Teeter and Jorgen Sandberg have criticized Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan for his role in the promotion and rise in tech stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Their research cites numerous examples of Greenspan putting a positive spin on historic stock valuations despite a wealth of evidence suggesting that stocks were overvalued.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_spending">Free spending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Free spending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>According to dot-com theory, an Internet company's survival depended on expanding its customer base as rapidly as possible, even if it produced large annual losses. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> did not see any profit in their first years. Amazon was spending to alert people to its existence and expand its customer base, and Google was busy spending to create more powerful machine capacity to serve its expanding <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The phrase "Get large or get lost" was the wisdom of the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At the height of the boom, it was possible for a promising dot-com to make an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> (IPO) of its stock and raise a substantial amount of money even though it had never made a profit—or, in some cases, earned any revenue whatsoever. In such a situation, a company's lifespan was measured by its <a href="/wiki/Burn_rate" title="Burn rate">burn rate</a>: that is, the rate at which a non-profitable company lacking a viable <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business model</a> ran through its capital.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Public awareness campaigns were one of the ways in which dot-coms sought to expand their customer bases. These included television ads, print ads, and targeting of professional sporting events. Many dot-coms named themselves with <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> nonsense words that they hoped would be memorable and not easily confused with a competitor. <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXXIV" title="Super Bowl XXXIV">Super Bowl XXXIV</a> in January 2000 featured <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_commercials_during_Super_Bowl_XXXIV" title="Dot-com commercials during Super Bowl XXXIV">16 dot-com companies</a> that each paid over $2 million for a 30-second spot. By contrast, in January 2001, just three dot-coms bought advertising spots during <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXXV" title="Super Bowl XXXV">Super Bowl XXXV</a>. In a similar vein, CBS-backed <a href="/wiki/IWon" title="IWon">iWon</a>.com gave away $10 million to a lucky contestant on an April 15, 2000 half-hour primetime special that was broadcast on CBS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not surprisingly, the "growth over profits" mentality and the aura of "<a href="/wiki/New_economy" title="New economy">new economy</a>" invincibility led some companies to engage in lavish internal spending, such as elaborate business facilities and luxury vacations for employees. Executives and employees who were paid with <a href="/wiki/Employee_stock_option" title="Employee stock option">stock options</a> instead of cash became instant millionaires when the company made its initial public offering; many invested their new wealth into yet more dot-coms.</p>
<p>Cities all over the United States sought to become the "next <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>" by building network-enabled office space to attract Internet entrepreneurs. Communication providers, convinced that the future economy would require ubiquitous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadband_access" title="Broadband access">broadband access</a>, went deeply into debt to improve their networks with high-speed equipment and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">fiber optic</a> cables. Companies that produced network equipment like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nortel_Networks" title="Nortel Networks">Nortel Networks</a> were irrevocably damaged by such over-extension; Nortel declared <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> in early 2009. Companies like Cisco, which did not have any production facilities, but bought from other manufacturers, were able to leave quickly and actually do well from the situation as the bubble burst and products were sold cheaply.</p>
<p>In the struggle to become a technology hub, many cities and states used tax money to fund technology conference centers, advanced infrastructure, and created favorable business and tax law to encourage development of the dotcom industry in their locale. Virginia's <a href="/wiki/Dulles_Technology_Corridor" title="Dulles Technology Corridor">Dulles Technology Corridor</a> is a prime example of this activity. Large quantities of high-speed fiber links were laid, and the State and local governments gave tax exemptions to technology firms. Many of these buildings could be viewed along <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Capital_Beltway)" title="Interstate 495 (Capital Beltway)">I-495</a>, after the burst, as vacant office buildings.</p>
<p>Similarly, in Europe the vast amounts of cash the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile</a> operators spent on <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a> licences in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, for example, <a href="/wiki/Telecoms_crash" title="Telecoms crash">led them into deep debt</a>. The investments were far out of proportion to both their current and projected <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow" title="Cash flow">cash flow</a>, but this was not publicly acknowledged until as late as 2001 and 2002. Due to the highly networked nature of the <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> industry, this quickly led to problems for small companies dependent on contracts from operators. One example is of a then Finnish mobile network company Sonera, which paid huge sums in German broadband auction then dubbed as 3G licenses. Third-generation networks however took years to catch on and Sonera ended up as a part of TeliaSonera, then simply Telia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Early_2000s_recession" title="Early 2000s recession">Early 2000s recession</a></div>
<p>On January 10, 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America_Online" title="America Online">America Online</a> (now Aol.), a favorite of dot-com investors and pioneer of dial-up Internet access, announced plans to merge with <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>, the world's largest media company, in the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">M&amp;A</a> transaction worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The transaction has been described as "the worst in history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Within two years, boardroom disagreements drove out both of the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEOs</a> who made the deal, and in October 2003 AOL Time Warner dropped "AOL" from its name.</p>
<p>On March 10, 2000 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ</a> peaked at 5,132.52<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Day_trading" title="Day trading">intraday</a> before closing at 5,048.62. Afterwards, the NASDAQ fell as much as 78%.</p>
<p>Several communication companies could not weather the financial burden and were forced to file for bankruptcy. One of the more significant players, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WorldCom" title="WorldCom">WorldCom</a>, was found engaging in <a href="/wiki/Accounting_scandals" title="Accounting scandals">illegal accounting practices</a> to exaggerate its profits on a yearly basis. WorldCom was one of the last standing combined competitive local exchange and inter-exchange companies and struggled to survive after the implementation of the Telecommunications Act of 1996. This Act favored incumbent players formerly known as Regional Bell Operating Companies (RBOCS) and led to the demise of competition and the rise of consolidation and a current day oligopoly ruled by lobbyist saturated powerhouses AT&amp;T and Verizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>WorldCom's stock price fell drastically when this information went public, and it eventually filed the third-largest corporate bankruptcy in U.S. history. Other examples include <a href="/wiki/NorthPoint_Communications" title="NorthPoint Communications">NorthPoint Communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_Crossing" title="Global Crossing">Global Crossing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JDS_Uniphase" title="JDS Uniphase">JDS Uniphase</a>, <a href="/wiki/XO_Communications" title="XO Communications">XO Communications</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covad_Communications" title="Covad Communications">Covad Communications</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Dot-com_bubble#This_is_related.2C_but_is_a_separate_crash.2C_occurring_at_a_later_time." title="Talk:Dot-com bubble">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> Companies such as <a href="/wiki/Nortel" title="Nortel">Nortel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cisco" title="Cisco">Cisco</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corning_Incorporated" title="Corning Incorporated">Corning</a> were at a disadvantage because they relied on infrastructure that was never developed which caused the stock of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corning_Incorporated" title="Corning Incorporated">Corning</a> to drop significantly.</p>
<p>Many dot-coms ran out of capital and were acquired or <a href="/wiki/Liquidation" title="Liquidation">liquidated</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain names</a> were picked up by old-economy competitors, speculators or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cybersquatter" title="Cybersquatter">cybersquatters</a>. Several companies and their executives were accused or convicted of <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a> for misusing shareholders' money, and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> fined top investment firms like <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a> millions of dollars for misleading investors. Various supporting industries, such as advertising and shipping, scaled back their operations as demand for their services fell. A few large dot-com companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> have become industry-dominating mega-firms.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_downturn_of_2002" title="Stock market downturn of 2002">stock market crash of 2000–2002</a> caused the loss of $5 trillion in the <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market value</a> of companies from March 2000 to October 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001,_attacks" title="September 11, 2001, attacks">September 11, 2001, attacks</a> accelerated the stock market drop; the NYSE suspended trading for four sessions. When trading resumed, some of it was transacted in temporary new locations.</p>
<p>More in-depth analysis shows that 48% of the dot-com companies survived through 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ssrn_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ssrn-19">[19]</a></sup> With this, it is safe to assume that the assets lost from the stock market do not directly link to the closing of firms. More importantly, however, it can be concluded that even companies who were categorized as the "small players" were adequate enough to endure the destruction of the financial market during 2000–2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ssrn_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ssrn-19">[19]</a></sup> Additionally, retail investors who felt burned by the burst transitioned their investment portfolios to more cautious positions.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, laid-off technology experts, such as computer programmers, found a glutted job market. University degree programs for computer-related careers saw a noticeable drop in new students.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Anecdotes of unemployed programmers going back to school to become accountants or lawyers were common.</p>
<p>Turning to the long-term legacy of the bubble, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Wilson_(financier)" title="Fred Wilson (financier)">Fred Wilson</a>, who was a venture capitalist during it, said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"A friend of mine has a great line. He says 'Nothing important has ever been built without <a href="/wiki/Irrational_exuberance" title="Irrational exuberance">irrational exuberance</a>'. Meaning that you need some of this mania to cause investors to open up their pocketbooks and finance the building of the railroads or the automobile or aerospace industry or whatever. And in this case, much of the capital invested was lost, but also much of it was invested in a very high <a href="/wiki/Throughput" title="Throughput">throughput</a> backbone for the Internet, and lots of software that works, and databases and server structure. All that stuff has allowed what we have today, which has changed all our lives... that's what all this speculative mania built".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As the technology boom receded, consolidation and growth by market leaders caused the tech industry to come to more closely resemble other traditional U.S. sectors. As of 2014, ten information technology firms are among the 100 largest U.S. corporations by revenues: <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingram_Micro" title="Ingram Micro">Ingram Micro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_companies_significant_to_the_bubble">List of companies significant to the bubble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: List of companies significant to the bubble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>For discussion and a list of dot-com companies outside the scope of the dot-com bubble, see <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-com company</a></i>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boo.com" title="Boo.com">Boo.com</a> – spent $188 million in just six months<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in an attempt to create a global online fashion store that went bankrupt in May 2000.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Books-a-Million" title="Books-a-Million">Books-a-Million</a> - Saw its stock price soar by over 1,000% in one week simply by announcing an updated website on November 25, 1998. The company's share price rose from around $3 previously to an all-time closing high of $38.94 on November 27 and an intra-day high of $47.00 on November 30, before quickly pulling back to around $10 two weeks later. By 2000, the share price had returned to $3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Broadcast.com" title="Broadcast.com">Broadcast.com</a> – Acquired by Yahoo! for $5.9 billion in stock, making <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cuban" title="Mark Cuban">Mark Cuban</a> a multi-billionaire. The site is now defunct and redirects to Yahoo!'s home page.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/E.Digital_Corporation" title="E.Digital Corporation">e.Digital Corporation</a> (EDIG) – Long-term, unprofitable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OTCBB" title="OTCBB">OTCBB</a>-traded company founded in 1988 previously named Norris Communications. Changed its name to e.Digital in January 1999 when stock was at $0.06 level. The stock rose rapidly in 1999 and went from closing price of $2.91 on December 31, 1999, to intraday high of $24.50 on January 24, 2000. It quickly retraced and has traded between $0.07 and $0.165 in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> As of 2013, the stock continues to trade low, ranging between $0.12 and $0.19 a share<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/EToys.com" title="EToys.com">eToys.com</a> - Online toy retailer founded in 1997, hit a high of $84.35 in October 1999. Only two years later in February 2001, it filed for bankruptcy with $247 million in debt. It was bought out by KB Toys, which also had to file for bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freei" title="Freei">FreeInternet.com</a> – Filed for bankruptcy in October 2000, soon after canceling its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>. At the time Freeinternet.com was the fifth-largest <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">ISP</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with 3.2 million users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Famous for its mascot <a href="/wiki/Baby_Bob" title="Baby Bob">Baby Bob</a>, the company lost $19 million in 1999 on revenues of less than $1 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GeoCities" title="GeoCities">GeoCities</a> – Purchased by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> for $3.57 billion in January 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Yahoo! closed GeoCities on October 26, 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TheGlobe.com" title="TheGlobe.com">theGlobe.com</a> – Social networking service, that went live in April 1995 and made headlines by going public on November 1998 and posting the largest first day gain of any IPO in history up to that date. The CEO became in 1999 a visible symbol of the excesses of dot-com millionaires</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GovWorks.com" title="GovWorks.com">GovWorks.com</a> – Doomed dot-com featured in the <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Startup.com" title="Startup.com">Startup.com</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inktomi" title="Inktomi">inktomi</a> – Valuation of $25 billion in March 2000</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InfoSpace" title="InfoSpace">InfoSpace</a> – In March 2000 this stock reached a price $1,305 per share, but by April 2001 the price had crashed down to $22 a share<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattletimes-timeline_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattletimes-timeline-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kozmo.com" title="Kozmo.com">Kozmo.com</a> – offered one-hour local delivery of a number of retail items, from March 1998 to April 2001</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lastminute.com" title="Lastminute.com">Lastminute.com</a> – IPO in the UK coincided with the bursting of the bubble</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a>, bought by <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> in 1999 for $3.5 billion, sold for $27.3 million in 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lycos" title="Lycos">Lycos</a> – Purchased by Spanish telecommunications provider <a href="/wiki/Telef%C3%B3nica" title="Telefónica">Telefónica</a> for $12.5 billion in 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> to expand its <a href="/wiki/Terra_Networks" title="Terra Networks">Terra Networks</a> online platform. It was sold in 2004 to <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daum_Communications_Corporation" title="Daum Communications Corporation">Daum Communications Corporation</a> for $95.4 million in cash, less than 2% of Terra's initial multibillion-dollar investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MicroStrategy" title="MicroStrategy">MicroStrategy</a> – Shares lost more than half their value on March 20, 2000, following their announcement of re-stated financials for the previous two years. A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BusinessWeek" title="BusinessWeek">BusinessWeek</a></i> editorial said at the time, "The company's misfortune is a wake-up call to all dot-com investors. The message: It's time, at last, to pay attention to the numbers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Open.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Open.com (page does not exist)">Open.com</a> – Was a big software security producer, reseller, and distributor; declared bankruptcy in 2001</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pets.com" title="Pets.com">Pets.com</a> – Former dot-com enterprise that sold pet supplies to retail customers before entering bankruptcy in 2000</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pixelon" title="Pixelon">Pixelon</a> – Internet streaming video company that hosted a $16 million launch party in 1999 hosting celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Chicks" title="Dixie Chicks">Dixie Chicks</a>. Failed less than a year later when it became apparent that its technologies were fraudulent or misrepresented<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Startups.com" title="Startups.com">Startups.com</a> – "Ultimate dot-com startup" that went out of business in 2002</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Think_Tools_AG" title="Think Tools AG">Think Tools AG</a> – One of the most extreme symptoms of the bubble in Europe: market valuation of CHF 2.5 billion in March 2000, no prospects of having a substantial product (investor deception), followed by a collapse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tiscali" title="Tiscali">Tiscali</a> – Important Italian telecommunications company whose share price grew from €46 (IPO in November 1999) to €1,197 in four months. They fell to €40 afterwards in less than two months and have continued plummeting to well under 0.20 euro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VA_Linux" title="VA Linux">VA Linux</a> - A provider of built-to-order Intel systems based on Linux and other open source projects. They set the record for largest first-day IPO price gain; after the price was set at $30/share, it ended the first day of trading at $239.25/share, a 698% gain (9 December 1999). After that, its stock declined consistently. After several business transitions it became <a href="/wiki/Geeknet" title="Geeknet">Geeknet</a>. It provides the backdrop of the documentary <a href="/wiki/Revolution_OS" title="Revolution OS">Revolution OS</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Webvan" title="Webvan">Webvan</a>, Online grocer that operated on a "credit and delivery" system; the original company went bankrupt in 2001. It was later resurrected by <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MCI_Inc." title="MCI Inc.">WorldCom</a>, a long-distance telephone and internet-services provider that became notorious for using fraudulent accounting practices to increase their stock price. The company filed for bankruptcy in 2002 and former CEO <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Ebbers" title="Bernard Ebbers">Bernard Ebbers</a> was convicted of fraud and conspiracy.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Xcelera.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Xcelera.com (page does not exist)">Xcelera.com</a> (XLA) – <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> investor in start-up technology firms that was one of the<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> "greatest one-year rise of any exchange-listed stock in the history of Wall Street"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
