<p><i><b>The Tale of the Princess Kaguya</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">かぐや姫の物語</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Kaguya-hime no Monogatari</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a 2013 <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">Japanese animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Studio_Ghibli" title="Studio Ghibli">Studio Ghibli</a>. Co-written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Isao_Takahata" title="Isao Takahata">Isao Takahata</a>, the film features an ensemble cast that includes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aki_Asakura&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aki Asakura (page does not exist)">Aki Asakura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kengo_Kora" title="Kengo Kora">Kengo Kora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Chii" title="Takeo Chii">Takeo Chii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobuko_Miyamoto" title="Nobuko Miyamoto">Nobuko Miyamoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atsuko_Takahata" title="Atsuko Takahata">Atsuko Takahata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomoko_Tabata" title="Tomoko Tabata">Tomoko Tabata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tatekawa_Shinosuke" title="Tatekawa Shinosuke">Tatekawa Shinosuke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takaya_Kamikawa" title="Takaya Kamikawa">Takaya Kamikawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hikaru_Ij%C5%ABin" title="Hikaru Ijūin">Hikaru Ijūin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryudo_Uzaki" title="Ryudo Uzaki">Ryudo Uzaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakamura_Shichinosuke_II" title="Nakamura Shichinosuke II">Nakamura Shichinosuke II</a>, Isao Hashizume and Tamaki Kojo, and is based on the folktale <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Bamboo_Cutter" title="The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter">The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is Takahata's fifth film for Studio Ghibli, and his first since 1999's <i><a href="/wiki/My_Neighbors_the_Yamadas" title="My Neighbors the Yamadas">My Neighbors the Yamadas</a></i>. It was released on November 23, 2013. The film received critical acclaim and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Animated_Feature" title="Academy Award for Best Animated Feature">Academy Award for Best Animated Feature</a> at the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">87th Academy Awards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A bamboo cutter named Sanuki no Miyatsuko discovers a miniature girl inside a glowing bamboo shoot. Believing her to be a divine presence, he and his wife decide to raise her as their own, calling her "Princess". The girl grows rapidly and conspicuously, marveling her parents and earning her the nickname "Takenoko" (Little Bamboo) from the other village children. Sutemaru, the oldest among Kaguya's friends, develops a close relationship with her.</p>
<p>Miyatsuko comes upon gold and fine cloth in the bamboo grove in the same way he found his daughter. He takes these as proof of her divine royalty and begins planning to make her a proper princess. He relocates the family to the capital, forcing her to leave her friends behind. She finds herself in a mansion, replete with servants. She is also saddled with a governess who is tasked with taming her into a noblewoman. She struggles with the restraints of nobility, arguing that life should be full of laughter and struggle.</p>
<p>When the girl comes of age, she is granted the formal name of "Princess Kaguya" for the light and life that radiates from her. Miyatsuko holds a celebration in commemoration of her naming. At the celebration, Kaguya overhears partygoers ridiculing her father's attempts to turn a peasant girl into a noble through money. Kaguya flees the capital in despair and runs back to the mountains, seeking Sutemaru and her other friends, but discovers that they have all moved away. Kaguya passes out in the snow and awakens back at the party.</p>
<p>Kaguya grows in beauty, attracting scores of suitors. Five men of noble standing court her, comparing her to mythical treasures. Kaguya tells them she will only marry whoever can bring her the mythical treasure mentioned. Two suitors attempt to persuade her with counterfeits. The third abandons his conquest out of cowardice, and the fourth attempts to woo her with flattering lies. When one of the men is killed in his quest, Kaguya falls into <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>. Eventually, the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor</a> takes notice of her. Taken with her beauty, he makes advances toward her, revolting her. Kaguya then demonstrates the ability to disappear at will, surprising the Emperor. Understanding that he has been too forward, the Emperor takes his leave.</p>
<p>Kaguya cries to the Moon, praying for protection. She reveals to her parents that she originally came from the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> after it spoke to her. Once a resident of the Moon, she broke its laws, hoping to be exiled to Earth, so that she could experience mortal life. When the Emperor made his advances, she silently begged the Moon to help her. Having heard her prayer, the Moon will reclaim her during the next <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>. Kaguya confesses her attachment to Earth and her reluctance to leave.</p>
<p>Miyatsuko swears to protect Kaguya and begins assembling defensive forces. Kaguya returns to her hometown in the mountains once more. She finds Sutemaru and tells him she would have been happiest with him; Sutemaru vows to protect her. The two run around the grass field and Kaguya demonstrates the ability to fly. However, she loses it and the two drop into the water. Sutemaru wakes up on the grass field, thinking it was a dream, while Kaguya is seen in a carriage going back to the palace.</p>
<p>On the night of the full moon, a procession of celestial beings descends from the Moon, and Miyatsuko is unable to stop it. An attendant offers Kaguya a robe that will erase her memories of Earth. Kaguya begs the attendant to grant her a last moment with her parents.</p>
<p>The attendant assures her that upon returning to the Moon, she will be free of Earth's impurities. Kaguya rebuffs her, saying that Earth is full of wonder and life. The attendant then drapes the robe around Kaguya, and she appears to forget about her life on Earth. The procession ascends to the Moon, leaving Miyatsuko and his wife distraught, as Kaguya looks back one last time with tears in her eyes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studio Ghibli revealed that Isao Takahata was working on a feature-length film in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ann20080101_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ann20080101-11">[10]</a></sup> Takahata announced at the 62nd <a href="/wiki/Locarno_International_Film_Festival" title="Locarno International Film Festival">Locarno International Film Festival</a> in 2009 that he intended to direct a film based on the Japanese folktale <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Bamboo_Cutter" title="The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter">The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ann20090812_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ann20090812-12">[11]</a></sup> The release of <i>The Tale of the Princess Kaguya</i> was finally confirmed by Studio Ghibli and distributor Toho on 13 December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cinematoday20121213_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cinematoday20121213-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, Shinichiro Ikebe was announced to write the film's score. However, in 2013, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Hisaishi" title="Joe Hisaishi">Joe Hisaishi</a> replaced Ikebe as the composer. This is the first time that Hisaishi has scored a film directed by Isao Takahata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The theme song "When I Remember This Life" was performed by Kazumi Nikaidō.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cinematoday20130523_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cinematoday20130523-15">[14]</a></sup> The film's soundtrack was released on 20 November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>All tracks written by Joe Hisaishi.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Tale of The Princess Kaguya</i> was initially announced to be released simultaneously with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_Rises" title="The Wind Rises">The Wind Rises</a></i>, another Ghibli film by <a href="/wiki/Hayao_Miyazaki" title="Hayao Miyazaki">Hayao Miyazaki</a> in Japan in the summer of 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ann20121213_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ann20121213-17">[16]</a></sup> which would have marked the first time that the works of the two directors were released together since the release of the films <i><a href="/wiki/My_Neighbor_Totoro" title="My Neighbor Totoro">My Neighbor Totoro</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Grave_of_the_Fireflies" title="Grave of the Fireflies">Grave of the Fireflies</a></i> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ann20121213_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ann20121213-17">[16]</a></sup> However, in February 2013, distributor Toho announced that the release of <i>Kaguya-Hime no Monogatari</i> would be delayed to Fall 2013, citing concerns that the storyboards were not yet complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> On March 12, 2014, independent distributor <a href="/wiki/GKIDS" title="GKIDS">GKIDS</a> announced that it had acquired the US rights for the film and that it would release an English dub version produced by Studio Ghibli and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Marshall_(film_producer)" title="Frank Marshall (film producer)">Frank Marshall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chlo%C3%AB_Grace_Moretz" title="Chloë Grace Moretz">Chloë Grace Moretz</a> is the voice of the title character in the English dub. It was released in select theatres in North America on October 17, 2014 and was also released on DVD and Blu-ray in Japan on December 3, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The film was selected to be screened as part of the <a href="/wiki/Directors%27_Fortnight" title="Directors' Fortnight">Directors' Fortnight</a> section of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2014 Cannes Film Festival">2014 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cannes2014_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cannes2014-23">[22]</a></sup> Its North American premiere took place at the <a href="/wiki/2014_Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="2014 Toronto International Film Festival">2014 Toronto International Film Festival</a> as part of the festival's "Masters" program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIFF_2014_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIFF_2014-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film debuted at first place during its opening weekend in Japan, grossing <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">¥</a>284 million (<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>2.8 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> By February 2, 2014, the film had grossed <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">¥</a>2,313,602,733 (US$22,613,153) at the Japanese box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Total gross of the film worldwide is $24,186,232.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoxOfficeMojo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoxOfficeMojo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tale_of_the_Princess_Kaguya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> assigned the film <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_with_a_100%25_rating_on_Rotten_Tomatoes" title="List of films with a 100% rating on Rotten Tomatoes">a score of 100%</a> "Certified Fresh" with an average rating of 8.3/10 based on 80 reviews. The critics' consensus says, "Boasting narrative depth, frank honesty, and exquisite visual beauty, <i>The Tale of the Princess Kaguya</i> is a modern animated treasure with timeless appeal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2014, <i>The Tale of the Princess Kaguya</i> placed 4th in both <a href="/wiki/Kinema_Junpo" title="Kinema Junpo">Kinema Junpo</a>'s Best Ten and their Reader's Choice Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> David Ehrlich of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> gave the film an A, deeming it "the best animated movie of the year," adding that it is "destined to be remembered as one of the revered Studio Ghibli’s finest achievements."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Nicolas Rapold of <i>The New York Times</i> praised the artwork calling it "exquisitely drawn with both watercolor delicacy and a brisk sense of line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
