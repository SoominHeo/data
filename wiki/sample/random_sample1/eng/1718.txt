<p><b>Kanazōshi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">仮名草子</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> describes a type of printed <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> book that was produced primarily in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> between 1600 and 1680. The term literally means “books written in <i><a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a></i>” (<i>kana</i> being the phonetic Japanese syllabary that is simpler to read and write than <i><a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a></i>, or Chinese ideographs). The designation thus derives from the fact that the text of these books was written either entirely in <i>kana</i> or in a mixture of <i>kana</i> and <i>kanji</i>. <i>Kanazōshi</i> are considered to be a transitional genre, bridging the gap between medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" title="Romance (heroic literature)">romances</a> and the first high point of <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1600-1868) literature, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukiyoz%C5%8Dshi" title="Ukiyozōshi">ukiyozōshi</a></i> composed by <a href="/wiki/Ihara_Saikaku" title="Ihara Saikaku">Ihara Saikaku</a> (1642–93). The genre comprises an unlikely assortment of <a href="/wiki/Essay" title="Essay">essays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">stories</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Travel_guide" title="Travel guide">travel guides</a> for famous places, military <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">chronicles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious" title="Religious">religious</a> writings, and <a href="/wiki/Criticism" title="Criticism">critical</a> pieces. Despite the lack of uniformity in content, <i>kanazōshi</i> are classified as a distinct genre primarily based on the fact that they were the first literary works to be printed and widely circulated in Japan. Scholars also maintain that <i>kanazōshi</i> are generally of higher literary quality and more realistic than medieval forms, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Otogiz%C5%8Dshi" title="Otogizōshi">otogizōshi</a></i>, that preceded them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_Kanaz.C5.8Dshi">Characteristics of <i>Kanazōshi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kanaz%C5%8Dshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics of Kanazōshi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the 1620s, the only books available in Japan were handwritten manuscripts. The printed <i>kanazōshi</i> were less expensive and more widely available than these earlier manuscripts. They are thus considered the first example of commercial literature produced in Japan. One should keep in mind, however, the comparatively limited nature of their popularity. The cost of a single volume was still prohibitive, costing roughly the equivalent of what a laborer could earn for two or three days of work (4000 of Japanese currency). Moreover, the books, because of their small print runs (often only a few hundred copies), rarely circulated beyond Kyoto, Osaka, and Edo, the publishing centers in premodern Japan.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kanazoshi4.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="200" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/9f/Kanazoshi4.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Illustration from <i>Ukiyo Monogatari</i>, showing patrons visiting the licensed prostitution quarter</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite these limitations, the appearance of these books amounted to an important new trend in literary production. Closely tied to the rise of Japan's urban centers, the growing economic power of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dnin" title="Chōnin">chōnin</a></i> (urban commoner) class, the improvement of literacy rates, and the advent of woodblock print technology, <i>kanazōshi</i> emerged as a new, distinctly plebeian form of literature. Its authors arose from the educated portion of the population, including scholars, Buddhist priests, courtiers, samurai and rōnin. But its readership consisted mostly of non-aristocratic residents of Japan's growing cities.</p>
<p>In contrast to <i>otogizōshi</i> and other forms of medieval Japanese tales, <i>kanazōshi</i> tended to be more realistic, with fewer supernatural or fantastic elements. Whether meant to entertain or inform, <i>kanazōshi</i> narratives conveyed more details about the characters and their setting, contained more natural dialogue, and showcased a more representative slice of life.</p>
<p>Although more skillfully written than <i>otogizōshi</i>, <i>kanazōshi</i> are considered less advanced in terms of structure and wordplay than the subsequent <i>ukiyozōshi</i> composed by Saikaku. Reflecting the tastes of their comparatively less sophisticated audience, <i>kanazōshi</i> often relied upon simple puns to generate humor. For instance, the term “hanatare”, which can mean both a runny nose or a drooping flower, is used to describe a young child with the family name of Fujiwara (wisteria field). This type of pun typifies the level of humor found in <i>kanazōshi</i>.</p>
<p>Each <i>kanazōshi</i> book consisted of between one and twelve slim volumes of twenty to thirty leaves each, with roughly one-fifth of the space devoted to illustrations. Book prices were principally determined by the number of volumes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_Kanaz.C5.8Dshi">Development of <i>Kanazōshi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kanaz%C5%8Dshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of Kanazōshi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholars generally divide <i>kanazōshi</i> into two groups:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_kanaz.C5.8Dshi">Early <i>kanazōshi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kanaz%C5%8Dshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early kanazōshi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early <i>kanazōshi</i> were written mainly by the educated classes, including lesser <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>, courtiers, Buddhist priests, and scholars. Because these works were written by highly educated authors, they were often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Didactic" title="Didactic">didactic</a>, promoting moral behavior based on the previous generation’s sense of morality. The early <i>kanazōshi</i> are broken down into three categories: works meant to entertain, works meant to intellectually enlighten, and works written to educate people about practical matters. <i>Kanazōshi</i> which were written to entertain include war tales, romances and parodies of earlier classics such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ise_monogatari" title="Ise monogatari">Ise monogatari</a>. Those written to promote intellectual growth mainly deal with reconciling the ideas of Buddhism, Shintoism and Confucianism. The more practical <i>kanazōshi</i> include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Travel_guide" title="Travel guide">travel guides</a>, samples of well written love letters, and critiques of famous courtesans and <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> actors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_kanaz.C5.8Dshi">Late <i>kanazōshi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kanaz%C5%8Dshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late kanazōshi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The late <i>kanazōshi</i> are those works which were written in the latter half of the 17th century. Unlike the early <i>kanazōshi</i>, the late <i>kanazōshi</i> were written mostly by commoners for a commoner readership. This shift in the social class of the authors is reflected in the fact that the protagonists in the later works are usually commoners. In addition, the language used in the later <i>kanazōshi</i> is more realistic, and male and female characters speak using structures that are specific to their gender. Many scholars believe that this shift towards realism paved the way for <i>ukiyozōshi</i>, a later genre which is partially defined by its intense realism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representative_Authors">Representative Authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kanaz%C5%8Dshi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Representative Authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Famous <i>kanazōshi</i> writers include <a href="/wiki/Asai_Ry%C5%8Di" title="Asai Ryōi">Asai Ryōi</a> (d. 1691) and Suzuki Shōsan (1579-1655). Main works include: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nise_monogatari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nise monogatari (page does not exist)">Nise monogatari</a> (Tale of Falsehoods: A Parody of Tales of Ise), <i>Shimizu monogatari</i> (Tale of Shimizu), and <i>Tōkaidō meishoki</i> (Famous Sites on the <a href="/wiki/Japan_National_Route_1" title="Japan National Route 1">Tokaido Highway</a>). The most celebrated example of the genre is Ryōi’s <i>Ukiyo monogatari</i> (Tales of the Floating World, 1661), a comedic tale about a young man named Hyōtarō who gets himself into all kinds of trouble with gambling, prostitutes, and the like, and then learns valuable lessons about the proper way to live one’s life from town elders. One translation from Europe was distributed as kanazōshi – a three-volume edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_Fables" title="Aesop's Fables">Aesop's Fables</a>,</i> from 1593, entitled <i>Isopo Monogatari</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">伊曾保</a><a href="/wiki/Monogatari" title="Monogatari">物語</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This was the sole Western work to survive in later publication after the expulsion of Westerners from Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
