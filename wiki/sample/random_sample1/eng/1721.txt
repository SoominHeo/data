<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marmosops_as_a_genus"><i>Marmosops</i> as a genus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Marmosops as a genus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus known as <i>Marmosops</i> was originally treated as a subgenus from the genus <i>Marmosa</i> rather than having their own classification. This was changed in 1989 by Gardner and Crieghton, who officially separated the group and made them their own genus. The mix-up between to genera <i><a href="/wiki/Marmosa" title="Marmosa">Marmosa</a></i> and <i>Marmosops</i> was common due to the similar appearances including size and other external features. However, the two groups differ significantly in their <a href="/wiki/Integument" title="Integument">integument</a> and in the arrangement of their skull and <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The dentition is similar in morphology between the two groups, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> lower third premolar varying from one genus to the next. The similarity between the two continues to cause the <i>Marmosops</i> genus to be frequently misidentified due to the lack of knowledge regarding the species along with the overlooked traits that help separate them from other opossums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>Marmosops</i> are also commonly confused with the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Gracilinanus" title="Gracilinanus">Gracilinanus</a></i>, but this is quickly ruled out by a large number of differing characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> These differences include the arrangement of their digits, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_(anatomical_term)" title="Caudal (anatomical term)">caudal (anatomical term)</a> scales, and the central hair on the scales changing from a three hairs per follicle to many more. This causes the hair of the <i>Gracilinanus</i> to be thicker and has also found to be heavily pigmented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The last group commonly confused with <i>Marmosops</i> is known as the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Thylamys" title="Thylamys">Thylamys</a></i>. These animals have a contrasting dorsal body <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelage" title="Pelage">pelage</a> and the taxa are actually quite different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_traits">General traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General traits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<pre>
The <i>Marmosops</i> tend to be nocturnal, small marsupials and have been found to live on the ground. Many females of the <i>M. paulensis</i> species are known to be extremely territorial with a strong defense of their food, especially while in the presence of their young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiner_25_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leiner_25-3">[3]</a></sup> The <i>Marmosops</i> genus is frequently misidentified and lumped into groups containing other species. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> As of today, <i>Marmosops</i> are currently recognized as a distinct taxon with 36 taxa: 14 valid species and 22 subspecies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The species within the genus differ slightly, but not enough to be classified as separate genera. Some of the species are also nicknamed by appearance, with the <i>M. paulensis</i> known as the slender opossum, the <i>M. noctivagus</i> recognized as the largest species and the <i>M. bishopi</i> the smallest of the genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hice_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hice-5">[5]</a></sup>
</pre>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main habitat for the <i>Marmosops</i> is found between the Neotropical humid forests ranging from <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> to the South of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and sometimes in Bolivia as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i>Marmosops</i> prefer to live on the grounds of forests, staying away from high branches of trees. They often hide in the dense understory with high plant coverage surrounding them. They are highly influenced by rain and predation, with migration occurring more often during certain seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The genus is never found to live up in a <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopy (biology)</a> or in the trees, but instead typically prefer more accessible habitats where it is easier to hide. There is not much known regarding the <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> of the genus due to their migration patterns and unique preferences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The diet of <i>Marmosops</i>includes, but is not limited to fruit, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthropods" title="Arthropods">arthropods</a>, flowers and small vertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiner_52_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leiner_52-7">[7]</a></sup> They also will eat insects, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnids" title="Arachnids">arachnids</a>, and gastropods depending on the species and time of the year. Their diet seems to change with the seasons, with more diversity in food preference shown in the drier seasons. Each species has a slightly different diet depending on their habitat and what is available in that area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the <i>Marmosops</i> carry their offspring <a href="/wiki/In_utero" title="In utero">in utero</a> and feed them through the process of lactation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hice_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hice-5">[5]</a></sup> There is a high reproduction rate in the months of September through March for <i>M. paulensis</i>, with more mating occurring when there is a larger food supply. For this species, death occurred after every mating event was completed. This ensured that there was no more than one mating event for each animal that reproduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NO_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NO-9">[9]</a></sup> Successful breeding depends on the presence of females, and many may have multiple mating partners in order to produce more offspring if they do not die after one event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiner_25_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leiner_25-3">[3]</a></sup> The <i>M. paulensis</i> species have higher breeding rates when the length of day is longer and there is more fruit available to eat. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semelparity" title="Semelparity">Semelparity</a> also was found to occur in this species, but is hypothesized to occur only due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenic" title="Phylogenic">phylogenic</a> constraints. It seems as though there is synchrony of the females for the species due to swollen nipples around the time of breeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NO_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NO-9">[9]</a></sup> The <i>M. incanus</i> have also been found to exhibit semelparity with a distinct breeding season between the months of October through December. For the breeding season, it is more beneficial for the females to be larger in order to produce larger litters, yielding higher breeding success over the smaller sized females. When looking at milk production and food supply for the genus, there was a strong correlation found between the two as well. In times where there was more food, there was more milk produced by the females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NO_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-NO-9">[9]</a></sup> As far as the males, they seem to generally disappear after the mating event while the females remain in the area until their young are done <a href="/wiki/Weaning" title="Weaning">weaning</a>. This pattern leads to the generations being replaced every breeding season due to mortality of the adults. Overall, there is not much known about the reproductive patterns of the other species due to the little knowledge collected on the genus as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiner_52_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leiner_52-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species-specific_information">Species-specific information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmosops&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Species-specific information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Marmosops noctivagus</i> species have been involved in a big debate on which Genus they belong to. One study found them located in Columbia, which was previously an unknown location as far as <i>Marmosops</i> are concerned. They were identified by the chestnut brown hair on their back and white hair on their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventral</a> side along with black facial features. They also have long tails with two different colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i>M. noctivagus</i> also has a skull with a long nose and cranial openings between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squamosal" title="Squamosal">squamosal</a> and parietal bones. This is present in some of the animals and absent in others. The cranial openings under the squamosal bones, also known as <a href="/wiki/Fenestra_(anatomy)" title="Fenestra (anatomy)">fenestra (anatomy)</a>, are small and rounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> The dental arrangement for the species consists of large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canine_teeth" title="Canine teeth">canine teeth</a> but lack accessory cusps, correlating with the age and sex of the animal. These teeth are significantly smaller in the females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i>Marmosops bishopi</i> are another species of the <i>Marmosops</i> genus found in the western Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> and other areas of Peru and Bolivia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diaz_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-diaz-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Marmosops incanus</i> are another species in the genus that have a mask made of black fur around their eyes. Some other distinguishing characteristics include the third digit being longer than the second and the fourth along with an extremely long tail. They do not have a fenestra on the <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">maxilla</a>, but the fenestra <a href="/wiki/Cochlea" title="Cochlea">cochlea</a> is exposed <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>Marmosops creightoni</i> is a species that is medium in size with a coat that is almost completely dark with no clear distinction between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsum_(anatomy)" title="Dorsum (anatomy)">dorsal</a> and ventral sides. This is the only species of the genus without the white markings on the underside. The chin fur is white and continues down to the throat along with a black mask around the eyes. The skull consists of small orbits with long nasal bones and the <a href="/wiki/Infraorbital_foramen" title="Infraorbital foramen">infraorbital foramen</a> is very large. This species has a fenestra between the maxilla and <a href="/wiki/Palatine_bone" title="Palatine bone">palatine bone</a>, which are both long and narrow on each side. The palatine fenestra are rather small in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_2_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Voss_2-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>M. handleyi</i> is one last species with very little known about it. However, rather recently there has been new information uncovered. The species is characterized by its woody dorsal fur, gray ventral fur and a rounded palatine fenestra. They are intermediately sized compared to the rest of the genus and have dark black facial masks. They were found to lack throat glands as well. They have white or light gray ears and the females are unique due to the odd rusty-red color of their mammary area with seven nipples located on the abdominal region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
