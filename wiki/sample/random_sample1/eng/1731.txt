<p>The <b>Gardermoen Line</b> (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i>Gardermobanen</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed</a> <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eidsvoll" title="Eidsvoll">Eidsvoll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, running past <a href="/wiki/Lillestr%C3%B8m" title="Lillestrøm">Lillestrøm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Gardermoen" title="Oslo Airport, Gardermoen">Oslo Airport, Gardermoen</a>. The line is 64 kilometres (40 mi) long and replaced the older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoved_Line" title="Hoved Line">Hoved Line</a> as the main line north-east of Oslo. The older Hoved Line now handles commuter and freight traffic, while the Gardermoen Line handles high-speed passenger trains and freight trains laden with jet fuel for the airport. Both lines are owned by <a href="/wiki/Bane_NOR" title="Bane NOR">Bane NOR</a>.</p>
<p>The line was opened in 1998, at the same time as the airport that gave the line its name. It is used by the <a href="/wiki/Flytoget" title="Flytoget">Flytoget</a> airport express train service as well as express trains by <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_State_Railways" title="Norwegian State Railways">Norges Statsbaner</a>. It is the only high-speed railway in the kingdom, with a maximum permitted speed of 210 km/h (130 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbvstat_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jbvstat-1">[1]</a></sup> Most of the line between Oslo and Lillestrøm is through the 14,580-metre (47,830 ft) long <a href="/wiki/Romerike_Tunnel" title="Romerike Tunnel">Romeriksporten</a> tunnel—the longest railway tunnel in Norway. The decision to build the line was made in 1992; construction started two years later. The line was subjected to severe criticism during construction when the <a href="/wiki/Romerike_Tunnel" title="Romerike Tunnel">Romerike Tunnel</a> sprung severe leaks due to hurried construction. As a result, the tunnel was opened a year after the rest of the line.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Norway" title="Parliament of Norway">Parliament of Norway</a> decided on 8 October 1992 to build a new central airport for <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Norway" title="Eastern Norway">Eastern Norway</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen-30_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-owen-30-2">[2]</a></sup> they also decided that the main mode of ground transport to the airport should be by railway. While the previous airport, <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Fornebu" title="Oslo Airport, Fornebu">Oslo Airport, Fornebu</a>, was located just outside the city limits; the new Oslo Airport, Gardermoen, would be located some 50 kilometers north of the city, outside the reach of the existing public transport systems. The political agreement to build the airport stated that the costs of construction should not be borne by the tax payers. As a result, the entire airport was financed with money borrowed through <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Gardermoen" title="Oslo Airport, Gardermoen">Oslo Lufthavn AS</a>, a subsidiary of the <a href="/wiki/Avinor" title="Avinor">Norwegian Airport Administration</a>. The same principle was chosen for the <a href="/wiki/Airport_rail_link" title="Airport rail link">airport rail link</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_State_Railways_(1883%E2%80%931996)" title="Norwegian State Railways (1883–1996)">Norwegian State Railways</a> (NSB) created the subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Flytoget" title="Flytoget">NSB Gardermobanen AS</a>, on 24 November 1992 to perform the construction of the line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen-30_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-owen-30-2">[2]</a></sup> This company would be able to charge train operators using the railway line; and could use this income to cover its down payments and <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> on the debt created to pay for the infrastructure investment. It was planned to give a <a href="/wiki/Profit_margin" title="Profit margin">profit margin</a> of 7.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996 NSB Gardermoen stated that they expected to charge commuter and intercity trains 100 million <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_krone" title="Norwegian krone">Norwegian krone</a> per year to use the new line. County politicians in Akershus stated that it was not reasonable for commuters to have to pay for the new railway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Early estimates showed that this would almost double to price from Oslo to Eidsvoll, and that passengers would have different prices, depending on which route they followed. <a href="/wiki/Stor-Oslo_Lokaltrafikk" title="Stor-Oslo Lokaltrafikk">Stor-Oslo Lokaltrafikk</a>, which was responsible for buses in Akershus, stated that they had no way to influence NSB's prices, but that they would consider starting a coach service along the route, which would not charge the surcharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Construction of the railway started on 1 August 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen-30_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-owen-30-2">[2]</a></sup> Gardermoen is located about 50 kilometres (31 mi) north of Oslo, but is not located on the Hoved Line. A different right-of-way had to be chosen north of <a href="/wiki/Kl%C3%B8fta_Station" title="Kløfta Station">Kløfta</a>. The Hoved Line has heavy traffic, with many small stops until <a href="/wiki/Lillestr%C3%B8m_Station" title="Lillestrøm Station">Lillestrøm</a>, and continues northwards from Lillestrøm as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_track_(rail)" title="Single track (rail)">single track</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banedata_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-banedata-6">[6]</a></sup> Therefore, an all-new railway would have to be built; it would bypass the intermediate railway until Lillestrøm, running through a tunnel, and continue northwards to the airport and onwards to <a href="/wiki/Eidsvoll_Station" title="Eidsvoll Station">Eidsvoll</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen-30_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-owen-30-2">[2]</a></sup> This northernmost part is 16 kilometres (10 mi) used to allow trains operating on the <a href="/wiki/Dovre_Line" title="Dovre Line">Dovre Line</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lillehammer_Station" title="Lillehammer Station">Lillehammer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trondheim_Central_Station" title="Trondheim Central Station">Trondheim</a> to use the Gardermoen Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elektrolok_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-elektrolok-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gardermoen Line was the second attempt to build a high-speed railway in Norway. The first was the 35 km line from <a href="/wiki/Ski_Station" title="Ski Station">Ski</a> to <a href="/wiki/Moss_Station" title="Moss Station">Moss</a> on the <a href="/wiki/%C3%98stfold_Line" title="Østfold Line">Østfold Line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen-11_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-owen-11-8">[8]</a></sup> However, operational speeds in excess of 160 kilometres per hour (99 mph) could not be achieved due to the short distances and limitations on rolling stock. The Gardermoen Line, therefore, became the first real high-speed railway line in Norway. Due to the domination of single track in Norway, the 64 kilometres (40 mi) Gardermoen Line increased the total length of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_track" title="Double track">double track</a> in the kingdom by more than 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbvstat_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jbvstat-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The projected costs of the railway line were <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_krone" title="Norwegian krone">NOK</a> 4.3 billion ±20%. Total costs ended at NOK 7.7 billion, including 1.3 billion extra used on extraordinary expenditures on the <a href="/wiki/Romerike_Tunnel" title="Romerike Tunnel">Romeriksporten</a>. On 1 October 1996 it was decided that the construction company, NSB Gardermobanen, would also become the operator of the airport express train. However, due to limited profitability with the company, from 1 January 2001 the ownership of Gardermobanen was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_National_Rail_Administration" title="Norwegian National Rail Administration">Norwegian National Rail Administration</a>, who also owns the rest of railway lines in Norway. The operating company changed its name to Flytoget AS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_challenges">Construction challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lutvann.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Lutvann.jpg/220px-Lutvann.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Lutvann.jpg/330px-Lutvann.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Lutvann.jpg/440px-Lutvann.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lutvann.jpg" title="Forstørr"></a></div>
The lake <a href="/wiki/Lutvann" title="Lutvann">Lutvann</a> was severely depleted due to complications during the construction of <a href="/wiki/Romerike_Tunnel" title="Romerike Tunnel">Romeriksporten</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Romerike_Tunnel" title="Romerike Tunnel">Romerike Tunnel</a></div>
<p>The most challenging part of the construction was the 14,580 metres (47,830 ft) railway tunnel from <a href="/wiki/Etterstad" title="Etterstad">Etterstad</a>, just east of Oslo S, to Lillestrøm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbvstat_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-jbvstat-1">[1]</a></sup> Romeriksporten is the longest railway tunnel in Norway, and is underneath the recreational area <a href="/wiki/%C3%98stmarka" title="Østmarka">Østmarka</a>, in geologically highly unstable ground. During the construction in 1997, the water level in some of the lakes above the tunnel, including <a href="/wiki/Lutvann" title="Lutvann">Lutvann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nordre_Puttjern" title="Nordre Puttjern">Nordre Puttjern</a>, fell dramatically. After they were discovered on 3 February 1997 sanctions were initiated by the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Water_Resources_and_Energy_Directorate" title="Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate">Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate</a> requiring leakage prevention measures in the tunnel. At the worst, 3,000 liters of water per minute leaked into the tunnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romeriksporten_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-romeriksporten-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The substance <a href="/wiki/Rhoca-Gil" title="Rhoca-Gil">Rhoca-Gil</a> was used to fix the leakages, but the substance failed to work properly. Not only did it fail to <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerize</a> and stop the leaks, it also contaminated the surroundings with <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">acrylamide</a>. The entire process of fixing the leak and cleaning up the toxicity delayed the process of building the tunnel by one year; and it was first opened on 22 August 1999. Further complications arose due to conflicts between NSB Gardermobanen and the construction company. Retrospective surveys showed a lack of control and reporting procedures during incidents that should have been addressed in 1995, and were never taken seriously. About sixty houses received damage due to the construction of the tunnel. An evaluation performed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Ministry_of_Transport_and_Communications" title="Norwegian Ministry of Transport and Communications">Ministry of Transport and Communications</a> showed that NOK 500 million was used on fixing the leaks; however, the report claimed this was, to a large extent, a waste of money due to inefficient engineering procedures. The same report criticized the planning and organization of the entire construction of the railway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romeriksporten_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-romeriksporten-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening">Opening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the new airport opened on 8 October 1998, and the Flytoget airport express train service started operations with sixteen <a href="/wiki/GMB_Class_71" title="GMB Class 71">Type 71</a> multiple units. Trains had to use the old Hoved Line from Oslo S to Lillestrøm, but could use the new high-speed line from Lillestrøm to Gardermoen. Regular operations using Romeriksporten started on 22 August 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> To be able to use the new Gardermoen Line infrastructure, Norges Statsbaner had to buy new trains capable of higher speeds. Twenty-two locomotive designated <a href="/wiki/NSB_El_18" title="NSB El 18">El 18</a>, based on the Swiss <a href="/wiki/SBB-CFF-FFS_Re_460" title="SBB-CFF-FFS Re 460">lok 2000</a>, where delivered in 1996. They are capable of speeds of up to 200 kilometres per hour (120 mph). However, the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_railway_carriages" title="Norwegian railway carriages">B7</a> passenger carriages used are not permitted to operate at more than 150 kilometres per hour (93 mph). Full speed utilization of long-distance trains is only possible with the <a href="/wiki/NSB_Class_73" title="NSB Class 73">Type 73</a> units that started operation on the Dovre Line in 2000. These trains are nearly identical to those used by the Flytoget airport express train company, and capable of 210 kilometres per hour (130 mph) on the Gardermoen Line. They are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Tilting_train" title="Tilting train">tilting technology</a> allowing quicker operation on the rest of the line to Trondheim. Other trains used on the line by NSB are only capable of 160 kilometres per hour (99 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elektrolok_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-elektrolok-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_expansion">Future expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plans for an expansion of the high-speed line to <a href="/wiki/Hamar" title="Hamar">Hamar</a> have been launched, but have not been incorporated into any specific plans. This suggestion would increase the length of the high-speed rail north of Oslo with 62 kilometres (39 mi)—almost a doubling. The project may or may not incorporate a further high-speed line to Trondheim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The railway is used by both Norges Statsbaner, Flytoget and SJ for their passenger services, as well as freight trains with jet fuel for the airport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport_express_train">Airport express train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Airport express train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1278" data-file-width="1917" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg/220px-GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg/330px-GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg/440px-GMB_BFM_71104_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flytoget" title="Flytoget">Flytoget</a></div>
<p>The Flytoget airport express train service is the largest user of the Gardermoen Line, with six departures from Oslo Central to Oslo Airport per hour. The trains only use the line as far as Gardermoen, and not the northernmost sixteen kilometers. Service is provided using sixteen Type 71 three-car units, which together transported 5.4 million passengers in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar07_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar07-12">[12]</a></sup> Direct services to Oslo S take 19 minutes. Half of the services make an intermediate stop at Lillestrøm, and continue on past Oslo Central to <a href="/wiki/Asker_Station" title="Asker Station">Asker Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-13">[13]</a></sup> Flytoget has a 34% market share on ground transport to Oslo Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utbytte_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-utbytte-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norges_Statsbaner">Norges Statsbaner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Norges Statsbaner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norges_Statsbaner" title="Norges Statsbaner">Norges Statsbaner</a></div>
<p>NSB uses Gardermobanen for express and some regional trains north of Oslo. This includes five daily departures to Trondheim (including one <a href="/wiki/NSB_Night_Train" title="NSB Night Train">NSB Night Train</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The line is operated by two regional services, the R10 service<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> between <a href="/wiki/Drammen_Station" title="Drammen Station">Drammen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lillehammer_Station" title="Lillehammer Station">Lillehammer</a>, the R11 service<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> between <a href="/wiki/Skien_Station" title="Skien Station">Skien</a>/<a href="/wiki/Larvik_Station" title="Larvik Station">Larvik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eidsvoll_Station" title="Eidsvoll Station">Eidsvoll</a>, as well as the local service, L12<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> between <a href="/wiki/Kongsberg_Station" title="Kongsberg Station">Kongsberg</a> and Eidsvoll. These services is carried out with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSB_Class_74" title="NSB Class 74">Type 74</a> for R10 and R11, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSB_Class_75" title="NSB Class 75">Type 75</a> for line L12. Both trains are capable of 200 km/t operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The southernmost part of the line is used by commuter trains heading to the <a href="/wiki/Kongsvinger_Line" title="Kongsvinger Line">Kongsvinger Line</a>. These trains divert from the Gardermo Line at Lillestrøm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norges_Statsbaner_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norges_Statsbaner-20">[20]</a></sup> Also the commuter trains operating to <a href="/wiki/Dal_Station" title="Dal Station">Dal</a> along the Hoved Line use the Gardermoen Line until Lillestrøm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_services">Other services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gardermoen_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because there is a parallel line, the Hoved Line running all the way from Oslo S to Eidsvoll, some trains can choose to use this line instead. This is primarily done by some <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter trains</a>, as well as all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight_rail_transport" title="Freight rail transport">freight trains</a>. The only exception to this are trains hauling <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> to the airport, who have to use the Gardermoen Line from Kløfta. This service is provided by <a href="/wiki/CargoNet" title="CargoNet">CargoNet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Swedish state railways, <a href="/wiki/SJ_AB" title="SJ AB">SJ</a> also operate on the southern part of Gardermoen on their intercity services <a href="/wiki/X_2000" title="X 2000">X 2000</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This was for some years provided by <a href="/wiki/Linx_(railway_company)" title="Linx (railway company)">Linx</a>, a joint venture between SJ and NSB, using <a href="/wiki/SJ_X2" title="SJ X2">X2</a> stock, but the company was later dissolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
