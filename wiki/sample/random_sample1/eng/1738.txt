<p><b>Kamaishi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">釜石市</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kamaishi-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Japan" title="Cities of Japan">city</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Sanriku" title="Sanriku">Sanriku</a> <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">rias</a> coast in <a href="/wiki/Iwate_Prefecture" title="Iwate Prefecture">Iwate Prefecture</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tohoku_region" title="Tohoku region">Tohoku region</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. As of September 2015, the city had an estimated <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of 35,308 and a <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> of 80.2 persons per km<sup>2</sup>. The total area was 441.43 square kilometres (170.44 sq mi).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spectacular, rugged coast of Kamaishi is entirely within the <a href="/wiki/Sanriku_Fukk%C5%8D_National_Park" title="Sanriku Fukkō National Park">Sanriku Fukkō National Park</a>. There are four large bays, Ōtsuchi Bay in the north, Ryōishi Bay, Kamaishi Bay and Tōni Bay in the south. Each is separated by large, rocky, pine-covered peninsulas which jut out into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Immediately the rocky cliffs develop into hills rising to 400 or 500 metres (1,300 or 1,600 ft) along the coast and 1,200 or 1,300 metres (3,900 or 4,300 ft) farther inland.</p>
<p>The highest point in Kamaishi is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Goy%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Goyō (page does not exist)">Goyō-zan</a> in the southwest at 1,341.3 meters in elevation. Most of the land is mountainous, allowing for little agriculture. The main rivers are the Kasshi-gawa River which empties into Kamaishi Bay and the Unosumai-gawa River which empties into Ōtsuchi Bay. Both have small floodplains that allow for development and agriculture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area of present-day Kamaishi was part of ancient <a href="/wiki/Mutsu_Province" title="Mutsu Province">Mutsu Province</a>, and has been settled since at least the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jomon_period" title="Jomon period">Jomon period</a>. The area was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Emishi" title="Emishi">Emishi</a> people, and came under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamato_dynasty" title="Yamato dynasty">Yamato dynasty</a> during the early <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a>, the area was dominated by various <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> clans before coming under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nambu_clan" title="Nambu clan">Nambu clan</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, who ruled <a href="/wiki/Morioka_Domain" title="Morioka Domain">Morioka Domain</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>.</p>
<p>The town of Kamaishi was established within Minamihei District on April 1, 1889. Minamihei and Nishihei Districts merged to form <a href="/wiki/Kamihei_District,_Iwate" title="Kamihei District, Iwate">Kamihei District</a> in 1896. Kamaishi attained city status on May 5, 1937, and expanded in 1955 with the absorption of the four neighboring villages of Kasshi, Unosumai, Kurihashi and Tōni.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-industrial_Kamaishi">Pre-industrial Kamaishi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pre-industrial Kamaishi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> in 1727, Kamaishi was little different from any of the other small fishing communities along the coast. However, it was not until 1857 and the construction of the first small <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> that any real changes could be seen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-WWII_Kamaishi">Pre-WWII Kamaishi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pre-WWII Kamaishi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1850s the feudal domains of Japan were engaged in an arms race to develop the first Western-style armaments, particularly large guns. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanbu_Domain" title="Nanbu Domain">Nanbu Domain</a> constructed <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> of a foreign design in Kamaishi under the direction of their military engineer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takat%C5%8D_%C5%8Cshima" title="Takatō Ōshima">Takatō Ōshima</a>. Ten furnaces were built in all but some were owned by private corporations. The first of these furnaces was lit on December 1, 1857; a day honored as the start of modern iron production in Japan. <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">Pig iron</a> from this furnace was sent to <a href="/wiki/Mito,_Ibaraki" title="Mito, Ibaraki">Mito</a> where Ōshima supervised the making of the first cannons in Japan.</p>
<p>In 1875 the newly established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiji_government" title="Meiji government">Meiji government</a> bought all of the furnaces and created the Kamaishi Iron Works. They also put Ōshima and a German engineer in charge of its modernization. When the two directors could not agree on a plan the Meiji government chose the plan of the German engineer and Oshima left. The German director imported two large steam-driven blast furnaces of the latest design from Britain and set up a railway with 15 miles of track and a locomotive from <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> to deliver the ore. Production began in 1880 but had to be stopped soon after due to a lack of charcoal. An attempt to resume operations in 1882 by replacing charcoal with coke failed and the plant was closed.</p>
<p>There were <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> outbreaks in Kamaishi in July 1882 and April 1884. The first left 302 people dead and warnings about the drinking water were posted throughout the prefecture.</p>
<p>In 1885, a new foundry was established which used coal from <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> and iron from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1896_Sanriku_earthquake" title="1896 Sanriku earthquake">1896 Sanriku earthquake</a> struck on June 15 at 7:32 pm while families were celebrating Boy's Festival on the beach. The earthquake measured magnitude 8.5 while the tsunami on the Iwate coast reached as high as 24 meters in places – the highest ever recorded in Japan. The city of Kamaishi was completely destroyed. The French Catholic missionary Henri Lispard was also swept out to sea and died when the wave struck.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kamaishi_in_WWII">Kamaishi in WWII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kamaishi in WWII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an important foundry town, Kamaishi played a significant role in the Japanese war effort and was targeted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. On 14 July 1945, under the command of Rear Admiral John F. Shafroth, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battleships" title="Battleships">battleships</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_South_Dakota_(BB-57)" title="USS South Dakota (BB-57)"><i>South Dakota</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Indiana_(BB-58)" title="USS Indiana (BB-58)"><i>Indiana</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/USS_Massachusetts_(BB-59)" title="USS Massachusetts (BB-59)"><i>Massachusetts</i></a>, the heavy cruisers <a href="/wiki/USS_Chicago_(CA-136)" title="USS Chicago (CA-136)"><i>Chicago</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Quincy_(CA-71)" title="USS Quincy (CA-71)"><i>Quincy</i></a>, and nine destroyers bombarded the Japan Ironworks and warehouses, along with nearby oil tanks and vessels, to great effect. This was the <a href="/wiki/Allied_naval_bombardments_of_Japan_during_World_War_II" title="Allied naval bombardments of Japan during World War II">first naval bombardment of the Japanese mainland</a>. Rear Admiral John F. Shafroth's battleships and cruisers, joined by two <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> light cruisers, attacked again on 8 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kamaishi_after_WWII">Kamaishi after WWII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kamaishi after WWII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kamaishi played its part in Japan's post-war boom, continuing its reputation as a steel town, a reputation reflected in the name of its rugby team - the Kaminashi Nippon Steel Rugby Club. In 1960, the town was crippled by a Tsunami generated by the <a href="/wiki/1960_Valdivia_earthquake" title="1960 Valdivia earthquake">1960 Valdivia earthquake</a>. In 1988 though, the steel mills closed and Kamaishi is now known more for <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a> than heavy industry. On September 30, 2010, <i>Foreign Policy</i> magazine <a class="external text" href="http://www.foreignpolicy.com/articles/2010/09/30/the_japan_syndrome" rel="nofollow">used Kamaishi as an example</a> of Japan's relative decline in the <a href="/wiki/Lost_Decade_(Japan)" title="Lost Decade (Japan)">'Lost Decade'</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_T.C5.8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kamaishi was heavily damaged by the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in which 1,250 city residents were killed or are missing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> at least 4 of the town's 69 designated evacuation sites <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and three of the town's 14 schools were inundated. Of the 2,900 students who attended the town's schools, five elementary or junior high school students were killed or are missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noted fatalities, victims of the earthquake and tsunami in Kamaishi, included 104-year-old <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Shimokawara" title="Takashi Shimokawara">Takashi Shimokawara</a>, holder of the world athletics records in the men's shot put, discus throw and javelin throw for the over-100s age category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tsunami waves as tall as 14 ft (4.3 m) surmounted the 1,950 m (6,400 ft) long and 63 m (207 ft) deep Kamaishi Tsunami Protection Breakwater,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which had been completed in March 2009 after three decades of construction, at a cost of $1.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was once recognized by the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> as the world's deepest <a href="/wiki/Breakwater_(structure)" title="Breakwater (structure)">breakwater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The subsequent decision to rebuild the breakwater at a cost of over 650 million dollars was criticised as 'a waste of money that aims to protect an area of rapidly declining population with technology that is a proven failure.' <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many news videos were broadcast of the city, which can be recognized by a large green crane in the background and water rushing against tall buildings at the edge of the city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kamaishi was famous in modern times for its steel production and most recently for its promotion of eco-tourism. <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a> and shellfish production are also important economic activities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamaishi,_Iwate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kamaishi has nine elementary schools, five middle schools and three high schools, and one special education school.</p>
