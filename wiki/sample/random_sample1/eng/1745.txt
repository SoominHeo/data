<p><b>Barn-owls</b> (family <b>Tytonidae</b>) are one of the two <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a> of <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, the other being the <a href="/wiki/True_owl" title="True owl">true owls</a> or typical owls, <a href="/wiki/True_owl" title="True owl">Strigidae</a>. They are medium to large owls with large heads and characteristic heart-shaped faces. They have long, strong legs with powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talon_(anatomy)" title="Talon (anatomy)">talons</a>. They also differ from the Strigidae in structural details relating in particular to the sternum and feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The barn owls comprise two extant subfamilies: the Tytoninae or <i>Tyto</i> owls (including the common <a href="/wiki/Barn_owl" title="Barn owl">barn owl</a>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phodilinae" title="Phodilinae">Phodilinae</a> or bay-owls. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibley-Ahlquist_taxonomy" title="Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy">Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy</a> unites the <a href="/wiki/Caprimulgiformes" title="Caprimulgiformes">Caprimulgiformes</a> with the owl <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a>; here, the barn-owls are a <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">subfamily</a> Tytoninae. This is unsupported by more recent research (see <a href="/wiki/Cypselomorphae" title="Cypselomorphae">Cypselomorphae</a>), but the relationships of the owls in general are still unresolved.</p>
<p>The barn-owls are a wide-ranging family, although they are absent from northern North America, <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> Africa, and large areas of Asia. They live in a wide range of habitats from <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> to <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, and from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> latitudes to the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. The majority of the 16 living species of barn-owls are poorly known. Some, like the <a href="/wiki/Red_owl" title="Red owl">red owl</a>, have barely been seen or studied since their discovery, in contrast to the common barn-owl, which is one of the best known owl species in the world. However, some subspecies of the common barn-owl possibly deserve to be separate species, but are very poorly known.</p>
<p>Five species of barn-owl are threatened, and some island species have gone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> or earlier (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Tyto_pollens" title="Tyto pollens">Tyto pollens</a></i>, known from the fossil record of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andros_Island" title="Andros Island">Andros Island</a> in the Bahamas, and possibly the basis for the mythical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chickcharnie" title="Chickcharnie">Chickcharnie</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>). The barn-owls are mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>, and generally non<a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, living in pairs or singly.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barn-owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The barn-owls' main characteristic is the heart-shaped <a href="/wiki/Facial_disc" title="Facial disc">facial disc</a>, formed by stiff <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> which serve to amplify and locate the source of sounds when hunting. Further adaptations in the wing feathers eliminate sound caused by flying, aiding both the hearing of the owl listening for hidden prey and keeping the prey unaware of the owl. Barn-owls overall are darker on the back than the front, usually an orange-brown colour, the front being a paler version of the back or mottled, although considerable variation is seen even amongst species. The bay-owls closely resemble the <i>Tyto</i> owls, but have a divided facial disc, ear tufts, and tend to be smaller.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barn-owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> of the barn-owls goes back to the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>, with the family eventually losing ground to the true owls after the radiation of <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and owls during the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> epoch. Two subfamilies are only known from the fossil record, the Necrobyinae and the Selenornithinae. Numerous extinct species of <i>Tyto</i> have been described.</p>
