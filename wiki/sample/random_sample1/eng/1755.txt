<p><b>Hideki Kamiya</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">神谷 英樹</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kamiya Hideki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born December 19, 1970)</span> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_designer" title="Video game designer">video game designer</a> and director working for <a href="/wiki/PlatinumGames" title="PlatinumGames">PlatinumGames</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-platinum_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-platinum-1">[1]</a></sup> Kamiya was formerly employed by <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">Capcom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clover_Studio" title="Clover Studio">Clover Studio</a>, and founded PlatinumGames in 2006, along with other former Capcom staff.</p>
<p>As a game designer, Kamiya states that he has been most inspired by the games <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Gradius" title="Gradius">Gradius</a></i>. His favorite action game is the original <i><a href="/wiki/Castlevania_(1986_video_game)" title="Castlevania (1986 video game)">Castlevania</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-1up-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early">Early</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kamiya was born in 1970 in Matsumoto in <a href="/wiki/Nagano_Prefecture" title="Nagano Prefecture">Nagano Prefecture</a>. At a young age he was already a fan of video games thanks to a neighbour who often invited him to play with his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoch_Cassette_Vision" title="Epoch Cassette Vision">Epoch Cassette Vision</a> console. Gaming appealed to Kamiya mainly due to the sounds it produced. During his early years of junior school he got his own first console, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Famicom" title="Famicom">Famicom</a>. The first game he bought was <i><a href="/wiki/Nuts_%26_Milk" title="Nuts &amp; Milk">Nuts &amp; Milk</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When reading an interview from the <i>Family Computer Magazine</i> that featured game creators <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masanobu_Endo" title="Masanobu Endo">Masanobu Endo</a>, Kamiya decided he would become a video game developer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ptwo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ptwo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In high school, Kamiya bought a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_PC-8801" title="NEC PC-8801">NEC PC-8801</a> to study programming but ended up playing video games every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ptwo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ptwo-4">[4]</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/List_of_video_games_considered_the_best" title="List of video games considered the best">favorite games</a> that he credits as an influence on his work include <i><a href="/wiki/Gradius" title="Gradius">Gradius</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Castlevania" title="Castlevania">Castlevania</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Harrier" title="Space Harrier">Space Harrier</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cybernator" title="Cybernator">Cybernator</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Punch-Out!!" title="Punch-Out!!">Punch-Out</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wonder_Boy_in_Monster_Land" title="Wonder Boy in Monster Land">Wonder Boy in Monster Land</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Snatcher_(video_game)" title="Snatcher (video game)">Snatcher</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sorcerian" title="Sorcerian">Sorcerian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Cruiser" title="Star Cruiser">Star Cruiser</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After graduating from college, Kamiya applied for jobs at various game developers. He was turned down by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> and had an application accepted by <a href="/wiki/Namco" title="Namco">Namco</a>. However, Namco wanted him to be an artist rather than a game designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resident_Evil"><i>Resident Evil</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resident Evil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kamiya joined Capcom as a designer in 1994. His early works included planner of the original <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_(1996_video_game)" title="Resident Evil (1996 video game)">Resident Evil</a></i>.</p>
<p>The development of <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_2" title="Resident Evil 2">Resident Evil 2</a></i> was carried out by a 40- to 50-person group that would later be part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capcom_Production_Studio_4" title="Capcom Production Studio 4">Capcom Production Studio 4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famitsu_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-famitsu-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The game was directed by Kamiya, who led the team, which was composed of newer Capcom employees and over half of the staff from the original <i>Resident Evil</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famitsu_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-famitsu-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamefan_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamefan-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-research-10">[10]</a></sup> In the initial stages of development, producer <a href="/wiki/Shinji_Mikami" title="Shinji Mikami">Shinji Mikami</a> often had creative disagreements with Kamiya, and tried to influence the team with his own direction. He eventually stepped back to an overseeing role as producer, and only demanded to be shown the current build once a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-research-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>To fulfill Capcom's sales plan of two million copies, director Kamiya tried to attract new customers with a more ostentatious and <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood-like</a> story presentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As Okamoto did not want to simply enforce the new direction, he had Sugimura discuss the plot revisions with Mikami and the development staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-research-10">[10]</a></sup> The planners redesigned the game from the ground up to fit the changes, and the programmers and other remaining members of the team were sent to work on <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_(1996_video_game)#Director.27s_Cut" title="Resident Evil (1996 video game)">Resident Evil Director's Cut</a></i>, which was shipped with a playable preview disc of the new <i>Resident Evil 2</i> version in order to promote the sequel and to apologize to the players for its belated release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famitsu_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-famitsu-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iobh_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-iobh-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Devil_May_Cry"><i>Devil May Cry</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Devil May Cry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was later the director of <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry_(video_game)" title="Devil May Cry (video game)">Devil May Cry</a></i>. <i>Devil May Cry</i> started out as the earliest incarnation of <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_4" title="Resident Evil 4">Resident Evil 4</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Initially developed for the PlayStation 2, the game was <a href="/wiki/Game_design#Game_designer" title="Game design">directed</a> by Hideki Kamiya after producer <a href="/wiki/Shinji_Mikami" title="Shinji Mikami">Shinji Mikami</a> requested him to create a new entry in the <i>Resident Evil</i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcbegin_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dmcbegin-14">[14]</a></sup> Around the turn of the millennium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcscenario_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-dmcscenario-15">[15]</a></sup> regular series writer <a href="/wiki/Noboru_Sugimura" title="Noboru Sugimura">Noboru Sugimura</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcscenario2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dmcscenario2-16">[16]</a></sup> created a scenario for the title, based on Kamiya's idea to make a very cool and stylized <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The story was based on unraveling the mystery surrounding the body of protagonist Tony,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> an invincible man with skills and an intellect exceeding that of normal people, his superhuman abilities explained with biotechnology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcscenario_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-dmcscenario-15">[15]</a></sup> As Kamiya felt the playable character did not look brave and heroic enough in battles from a fixed angle, he decided to drop the prerendered backgrounds from previous <i>Resident Evil</i> installments and instead opted for a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_camera_system#Interactive" title="Virtual camera system">dynamic camera system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcbegin_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-dmcbegin-14">[14]</a></sup> This new direction required the team to make a trip to Europe where they spent eleven days in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> photographing things like <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> statues, bricks, and stone pavements for use in <a href="/wiki/Texture_mapping" title="Texture mapping">textures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Though the developers tried to make the "coolness" theme fit into the world of <i>Resident Evil</i>, Mikami felt it strayed too far from the series' survival horror roots and gradually convinced all of the staff members to make the game independent from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Kamiya eventually rewrote the story to be set in a world full of demons and changed the hero's name to "Dante".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmcscenario_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-dmcscenario-15">[15]</a></sup> The cast of characters remained largely identical to that in Sugimura's scenario,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> although appearances of the hero's mother and father were written out of the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The game's new title was revealed as <i>Devil May Cry</i> in November 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game was developed by Team Little Devils, a group of staff members within <a href="/wiki/Capcom#Development_studios" title="Capcom">Capcom Production Studio 4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Some of the major gameplay elements were partially inspired by a bug found in <i><a href="/wiki/Onimusha:_Warlords" title="Onimusha: Warlords">Onimusha: Warlords</a></i>. During a test-play, Kamiya discovered that enemies could be kept in the air by slashing them repeatedly, which led to the inclusion of juggles by gunfire and sword strikes in <i>Devil May Cry</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-egm-28">[28]</a></sup> According to the director, <i>Devil May Cry</i> was designed from the ground up around Dante's acrobatics and combat abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamiya_Touch_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamiya_Touch-29">[29]</a></sup> The decision was made late in the development process to change the game to a more mission-based advancement, instead of the more open-ended structure of the <i>Resident Evil</i> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preview1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Preview1-30">[30]</a></sup> <i>Devil May Cry'</i>s difficulty was intentional, according to Kamiya, who called it his "challenge to those who played light, casual games."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top200_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-top200-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the success of the original <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry_(video_game)" title="Devil May Cry (video game)">Devil May Cry</a></i>, the sequel was not created by Hideki Kamiya or Team Little Devils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The first notice Kamiya's team was given about any sort of sequel occurred during localization of <i>Devil May Cry</i> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, a move which greatly surprised Kamiya. Instead, <a href="/wiki/Hideaki_Itsuno" title="Hideaki Itsuno">Hideaki Itsuno</a> was appointed as the director of the sequel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Since the game's release, Kamiya has expressed disappointment that he was not called on by his superiors at Capcom to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry_2" title="Devil May Cry 2">Devil May Cry 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamiya1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamiya1-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clover_Studio">Clover Studio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clover Studio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clover_Studio" title="Clover Studio">Clover Studio</a></div>
<p>He directed the original <i><a href="/wiki/Viewtiful_Joe" title="Viewtiful Joe">Viewtiful Joe</a></i>. The game was conceived as a "staff-focused project" aimed at increasing the skill of its creators, specifically director Kamiya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postcard_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-postcard-36">[36]</a></sup> Kamiya provides the voice for Six Machine in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staff_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-staff-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Kamiya worked as the director for <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckami" title="Ōkami">Ōkami</a></i>. <i>Ōkami</i> resulted from the combined ideas of Clover Studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy-inaba_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-inaba-38">[38]</a></sup> The game was originally built around "depict[ing] a lot of nature", but had no central concept or theme, according to lead designer Hideki Kamiya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okami_prototype_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-okami_prototype-39">[39]</a></sup> Kamiya eventually created a minute-long demonstration movie showing a wolf running about a forest, with flowers blossoming in its wake, but still lacked any gameplay. Kamiya and other members of the team introduced ideas around the nature aspect and eventually led to the game's initial prototype, which Kamiya admitted was "incredibly boring to play".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okami_prototype_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-okami_prototype-39">[39]</a></sup> Eventually, they settled onto the gameplay found in the final product, With the core feature of allowing the player to pause the gameplay at any time to draw on the landscape to affect the world around them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okami_prototype_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-okami_prototype-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gameplay style is a mix of action, platform, and puzzle gaming genres, and has been noted by many reviewers to have numerous similarities in overall gameplay style to <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda" title="The Legend of Zelda"><i>The Legend of Zelda</i> series</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> an inspiration that director Hideki Kamiya, a self-proclaimed Zelda-fan, has admitted has influenced his general game design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clover Studio was closed by Capcom in late 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-platinum_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-platinum-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PlatinumGames">PlatinumGames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hideki_Kamiya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: PlatinumGames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PlatinumGames" title="PlatinumGames">PlatinumGames</a></div>
<p>PlatinumGames was founded under the name Seeds, Inc. on August 1, 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Shinji_Mikami" title="Shinji Mikami">Shinji Mikami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atsushi_Inaba" title="Atsushi Inaba">Atsushi Inaba</a> and Hideki Kamiya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-platinum_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-platinum-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clover_Reborn_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clover_Reborn-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2008, the company, now renamed to PlatinumGames, announced a four-game deal with publisher <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref005_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref005-43">[43]</a></sup> The games involved in the development and publishing deal included <i><a href="/wiki/Bayonetta" title="Bayonetta">Bayonetta</a></i>, a "stylish action game" for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a> directed by Kamiya. The game was considered as the successor of <i>Devil May Cry</i> with Kamiya having used its latest sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Devil_May_Cry_4" title="Devil May Cry 4">Devil May Cry 4</a></i> as part of his research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot-qanda_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-qanda-44">[44]</a></sup> First announced at E3 2012, Kamiya directed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_101" title="The Wonderful 101">The Wonderful 101</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U" title="Wii U">Wii U</a>, which was released in September 2013. He also supervised production on the sequel to <i>Bayonetta</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bayonetta_2" title="Bayonetta 2">Bayonetta 2</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> released on the Wii U in October 2014.</p>
<p>Kamiya has stated he is interested in making a new <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Fox_(series)" title="Star Fox (series)">Star Fox</a></i> game, and due to a lot of fans asking him on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> over and over again, he was encouraged to submit ideas to Nintendo without success. Eventually, Platinum Games did end up working with Nintendo on the next installment of the <i>Star Fox</i> series, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Fox_Zero" title="Star Fox Zero">Star Fox Zero</a></i>, and its companion game <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Fox_Guard" title="Star Fox Guard">Star Fox Guard</a></i>, both of which were released in April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Kamiya was working on <i><a href="/wiki/Scalebound" title="Scalebound">Scalebound</a></i>, a new game for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Studios" title="Microsoft Studios">Microsoft Studios</a> until its cancellation in January 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
