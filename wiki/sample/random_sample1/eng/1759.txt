<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gavotte</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>gavotte</b> (also <b>gavot</b> or <b>gavote</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> dance, taking its name from a <a href="/wiki/Folk_dance" title="Folk dance">folk dance</a> of the Gavot, the people of the <a href="/wiki/Gap,_Hautes-Alpes" title="Gap, Hautes-Alpes">Pays de Gap</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Dauphin%C3%A9" title="Dauphiné">Dauphiné</a> in the southeast of France, where the dance originated according to one source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to another reference, however, the word "gavotte" is a generic term for a variety of French folk dances, and most likely originated in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Brittany" title="Lower Brittany">Lower Brittany</a> in the west, or possibly <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> in the southeast or the <a href="/wiki/French_Basque_Country" title="French Basque Country">French Basque Country</a> in the southwest of France. It is notated in <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>4</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span> or <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>2</b><br/>
<b>2</b></span></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">time</a> and is usually of moderate <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, though the folk dances also use meters such as <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>9</b><br/>
<b>8</b></span></span></span> and <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>5</b><br/>
<b>8</b></span></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_dance" title="Renaissance dance">renaissance dance</a> the gavotte is first mentioned as the last of a suite of <a href="/wiki/Branle" title="Branle">branles</a>. Popular at the court of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, it became one of many optional dances in the classical <a href="/wiki/Suite_(music)" title="Suite (music)">suite of dances</a>. Many were composed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lully" title="Jean-Baptiste Lully">Lully</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rameau" title="Rameau">Rameau</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gluck" title="Gluck">Gluck</a>, and the 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Cibell" title="Cibell">cibell</a> is a variety. The dance was popular in France throughout the 18th century and spread widely. In early courtly use the gavotte involved kissing, but this was replaced by the presentation of flowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gavotte of the 16th, 17th, and 18th centuries has nothing in common with the 19th-century column-dance called the "gavotte"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but may be compared with the <a href="/wiki/Rigaudon" title="Rigaudon">rigaudon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Bourr%C3%A9e" title="Bourrée">bourrée</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_characteristics">Musical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gavotte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Musical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gavotte_rhythm.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="115" data-file-width="501" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gavotte_rhythm.png/220px-Gavotte_rhythm.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gavotte_rhythm.png/330px-Gavotte_rhythm.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gavotte_rhythm.png/440px-Gavotte_rhythm.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gavotte_rhythm.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gavotte rhythm</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The phrases of the 18th-century French court gavotte begin in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(music)" title="Bar (music)">bar</a>, creating a half-measure (half-bar) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upbeat" title="Upbeat">upbeat</a>. However the music for the earlier court gavotte, first described by <a href="/wiki/Thoinot_Arbeau" title="Thoinot Arbeau">Thoinot Arbeau</a> in 1589, invariably began on the downbeat of a duple measure. Later composers also wrote gavottes that began on the <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)#Downbeat" title="Beat (music)">downbeat</a> rather than on the half-measure: an example is <a href="/wiki/Jean-Philippe_Rameau" title="Jean-Philippe Rameau">Jean-Philippe Rameau</a>'s <i>Gavotte Variée</i> in A minor for keyboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Various folk gavottes found in mid-20th-century Brittany are danced to music in <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>4</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span>, <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>2</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span>, <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>9</b><br/>
<b>8</b></span></span></span>, and <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>5</b><br/>
<b>8</b></span></span></span> time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gavotte_dance_pattern.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="217" data-file-width="1697" height="32" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gavotte_dance_pattern.png/250px-Gavotte_dance_pattern.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gavotte_dance_pattern.png/375px-Gavotte_dance_pattern.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gavotte_dance_pattern.png/500px-Gavotte_dance_pattern.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

Another typical gavotte <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatter_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blatter-8">[8]</a></sup></div>

<p>In the ball-room the gavotte was often paired with a preceding triple-time <a href="/wiki/Minuet" title="Minuet">minuet</a>: both dances are stately, and the gavotte's lifted step contrasted with the shuffling <a href="/wiki/Minuet_step" title="Minuet step">minuet step</a>. It had a steady rhythm, not broken up into faster notes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gavotte.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="935" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Gavotte.gif/220px-Gavotte.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Gavotte.gif/330px-Gavotte.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Gavotte.gif/440px-Gavotte.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gavotte.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>A Tempo di Gavotti</i> by <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the Baroque suite the gavotte is played after (or sometimes before) the <a href="/wiki/Sarabande" title="Sarabande">sarabande</a>. Like most dance movements of the Baroque period it is typically in <a href="/wiki/Binary_form" title="Binary form">binary form</a> but this may be extended by a second melody in the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" title="Meter (music)">metre</a>, often one called the <i>musette</i>, having a <a href="/wiki/Pedal_point" title="Pedal point">pedal</a> <a href="/wiki/Drone_(music)" title="Drone (music)">drone</a> to imitate the <a href="/wiki/Musette_de_cour" title="Musette de cour">French bagpipes</a>, played after the first to create a grand <a href="/wiki/Ternary_form" title="Ternary form">ternary form</a>; A–(A)–B–A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There is a <i>Gavotte en Rondeau</i> ("Gavotte in <a href="/wiki/Rondo" title="Rondo">rondo</a> form") in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.S._Bach" title="J.S. Bach">J.S. Bach</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Partita" title="Partita">Partita</a> No. 3 in E Major for solo violin, BWV 1006.</p>
<p>The gavotte could be played at a variety of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tempo_(music)" title="Tempo (music)">tempi</a></i>: Johann Gottfried Walther wrote that the gavotte is "often quick but occasionally slow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gavotte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The gavotte is first described in the late 16th century as a suite or miscellany of <a href="/wiki/Branle" title="Branle">double branles</a> danced in a line or circle to music in duple time, "with little springs in the manner of the Haut Barrois" branle and with some of the steps "divided" with figures borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Galliard" title="Galliard">galliard</a>.</p>
<p>The basic gavotte step, as described by Arbeau, is that of the common or double branle, a line of dancers moving alternately to the left and right with a <i>double à gauche</i> and <i>double à droite</i>, each requiring a count of four. In the double branle these composite steps consist of; a <i>pied largi</i> (firm outward step), a <i>pied approche</i> (the other foot drawn near to the first), another <i>pied largi</i> and a <i>pied joint</i> (the other foot drawn against the first).</p>
<p>In the gavotte's <i>double à gauche</i> a skip (<i>petit saut</i>) is inserted after each of the four components; the second <i>pied largi</i> is replaced by a <i>marque pied croisé</i> (the following foot crosses over the left with toe contacting the floor); the final <i>pied approche</i> is replaced by a <i>grève croisée</i> (the right foot crosses over the left, raised).</p>
<p>The <i>double à droite</i> begins with a <i>pieds joints</i> and <i>petit saut</i>, followed by two quick steps, a <i>marque pied gauche croisé</i> and <i>marque pied droit croisé</i>, during beat two, a <i>grève droit croisée</i> and <i>petit saut</i> on beat three and on the last beat <i>pieds joints</i> and a <i>capriole</i> (leap into the air with <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ballet#E" title="Glossary of ballet">entrechat</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The gavotte became popular in the court of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> where <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lully" title="Jean-Baptiste Lully">Jean-Baptiste Lully</a> was the leading court composer. <a href="/wiki/Ga%C3%A9tan_Vestris" title="Gaétan Vestris">Gaétan Vestris</a> did much to define the dance. Subsequently many composers of the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> period incorporated the dance as one of many optional additions to the standard instrumental <a href="/wiki/Suite_(music)" title="Suite (music)">suite</a> of the era. The examples in suites and partitas by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> are well known.</p>
<p>Movements of early 18th-century musical works entitled <i>Tempo di gavotta</i> sometimes indicated the sense of a gavotte rhythm or movement, without fitting the number of measures or strains typical of the actual dance. Examples of these can be found in the works of <a href="/wiki/Arcangelo_Corelli" title="Arcangelo Corelli">Arcangelo Corelli</a> or <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_examples">Later examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gavotte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Composers in the 19th century wrote gavottes that began, like the 16th-century gavotte, on the <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)#Downbeat" title="Beat (music)">downbeat</a> rather than on the half-measure upbeat. The famous Gavotte in D by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Gossec" title="François Joseph Gossec">Gossec</a> is such an example, as is the Gavotte in <a href="/wiki/Jules_Massenet" title="Jules Massenet">Massenet's</a> <i>Manon</i> but not the one in <a href="/wiki/Ambroise_Thomas" title="Ambroise Thomas">Ambroise Thomas's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mignon" title="Mignon">Mignon</a></i>. A gavotte also occurs in the second act of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gondoliers" title="The Gondoliers">The Gondoliers</a></i> and the Act I Finale of <i><a href="/wiki/Ruddigore" title="Ruddigore">Ruddigore</a></i>, both by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a> employs a gavotte instead of a minuet in his "Classical" Symphony.</p>

<ul>
<li>Early 20th-Century recording artist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Siegel" title="Samuel Siegel">Samuel Siegel</a> recorded a <a href="/wiki/Ragtime" title="Ragtime">ragtime</a> mandolin tune called Gavotte.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carly_Simon" title="Carly Simon">Carly Simon</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_So_Vain" title="You're So Vain">You're So Vain</a>" includes the lyric "You had one eye in the mirror as you watched yourself gavotte". In this context it can be taken to mean a pretentious or egotistical style of dancing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The Stephen Sondheim musical <i>Sunday in the Park With George</i> uses the word <i>gavotte</i> as a satirical device in the otherwise irregular, non-steadily rhythmical, song "It's Hot Up Here" to start the second Act, "We're stuck up here in this gavotte."</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mercer" title="Johnny Mercer">Johnny Mercer</a> song "Strip Polka" includes the lyric "Oh, she hates corny waltzes and she hates the gavotte".</li>
<li>Geneticist <a href="/wiki/W._D._Hamilton" title="W. D. Hamilton">W. D. Hamilton</a> in his paper entitled "Gamblers since life began: barnacles, aphids, elms." in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quarterly_Review_of_Biology" title="Quarterly Review of Biology">Quarterly Review of Biology</a></i> (1975) referred to the drilled formality of the mechanisms of individual reproduction as "the gavotte of chromosomes".</li>
<li>Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Stephen_David_Ross" title="Stephen David Ross">Stephen David Ross</a> characterises metaphysical <a href="/wiki/Aporia" title="Aporia">aporia</a> as "the disruptive side of a tradition that needs both repetition and its annihilation for intelligibility. It is a site at which same and other dance their unending gavotte of life and death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agustin_Barrios" title="Agustin Barrios">Agustin Barrios</a> wrote a solo guitar piece called <i>Madrigal Gavotte</i>, which is a combination of the two styles.</li>
<li>In the anime <i>Kiniro no Corda (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Corda_D%27Oro" title="La Corda D'Oro">La Corda D'Oro</a>)</i>, the "Gavotte in D by Gossec" is heard many times, though referred to only as "Gavotte".</li>
<li>In the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Omens" title="Good Omens">Good Omens</a></i>, it is noted that one cannot determine how many angels can dance on the head of a pin, because angels do not dance—the exception being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principality_(angel)" title="Principality (angel)">Principality</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aziraphale" title="Aziraphale">Aziraphale</a>, who once learned to do the gavotte.</li>
<li>The "Cutting Gavotte" is an attack in the Japanese version of the RPG <i>Infinite Undiscovery</i>.</li>
<li>In the Broadway musical <i>1776</i> during the song "Cool, Considerate Men", reference is made to "Mr. Adams' new gavotte"—a reference regarding John Adams' ideas for a declaration of independence from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.</li>
<li>In the 1967 movie, <i>How To Succeed In Business Without Really Trying</i>, the song "A Secretary Is Not A Toy" refers to a gavotte. The song discourages personal indiscretions with secretaries at the firm. The reference to a gavotte is meant to be ironic, as the original dance accompanying the song from the Broadway show was a modified gavotte.</li>
<li>In the manga and anime <i><a href="/wiki/One_Piece" title="One Piece">One Piece</a></i>, the skeleton musician character Brooke (and his "zombie," Ryuuma, which was given life by Brooke's shadow) has a signature technique: <i>Gavotte Bond en Avant</i>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pinsky" title="Robert Pinsky">Robert Pinsky</a> poem <i>Impossible To Tell</i> the gavotte is mentioned in the first line.</li>
<li>"The Ascot Gavotte" is a song in the 1956 musical <a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady" title="My Fair Lady">My Fair Lady</a> by Alan Jay Lerner and Frederick Loewe.</li>
<li>In the "<a href="/wiki/Young_Dracula" title="Young Dracula">Young Dracula</a>" episode "Son and Heir", Count Dracula says, "Everyone loves the gavotte."</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>'s novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bech_at_Bay" title="Bech at Bay">Bech at Bay</a></i>, for the protagonist, "It embarrassed him that for these young Czechs American writing, its square dance of lame old names, should appear such a lively gavotte, prancing carefree into the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the science-fiction novel <i>Blood and Fire</i>, David Gerrold offers a description, "A blue-white dwarf and a red giant circled each other off-center, a stately gavotte."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the mid-nineteenth-century novel <i>The Scout</i>, William Gilmore Simms describes a lonely sentry: "He sang, and whistled, and soliloquized; and, not unfrequently, relieved the dull measured step of the sentinel by the indulgence of such a gavotte as a beef-eating British soldier of the ‘prince’s own’ might be supposed capable of displaying in that period of buckram movement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>James Hawkins, in his novel <i>Crazy Lady</i>, has one character say, "'’But I am neither a dueller nor jouster, not of any repute,' protested the love-sick man. 'Though I am esteemed for my grace in the minuet and gavotte.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the novel <i>Nimisha’s Ship</i>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_McCaffrey" title="Anne McCaffrey">Anne McCaffrey</a> describes a ship christening: "Lady Cuiva duly activated the traditional bottle of champagne—an excellent vintage had been chosen from the Vegan vineyards—which was sent at speed against the prow of Five B and satisfactorily broke, leaving globules of wine and shards of glass to do a gavotte in space until a patrol could surreptitiously corral them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>In his popular spiritual novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hammer_of_God_(Bo_Giertz_novel)" title="The Hammer of God (Bo Giertz novel)">The Hammer of God</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Bo_Giertz" title="Bo Giertz">Bo Giertz</a> describes, "He saw it with almost supernatural clarity, as from outside: he saw himself, slightly intoxicated, red garter rosettes at the knees, stepping into the carriage in the summer night with his head full of gavotte melodies and his heart full of selfish concern for his own honor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Describing American foreign policy in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, author <a href="/wiki/Norman_Podhoretz" title="Norman Podhoretz">Norman Podhoretz</a> says, "Far from 'rushing into war,' we were spending months dancing a diplomatic gavotte in the vain hope of enlisting the help of France, Germany, and Russia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the play <i>Kate and Isabel</i>, author Anne Ludlum has her character, Kate, say "Before you children born, I dance. I do the sarabanda; I do the gavotte; I do this very dance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the novel <i>A Cost of Magic</i>, South African author Desmond Meiring writes, "The oil companies knew these hectic negotiations as the annual gavotte."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>Polish resistance fighter Jan Kamieński describes his personal experience of the chaos of the first German air strike on Poland in these terms: "Paintings were falling off the walls, the Biedermeier sofa and its complement of chairs bounced around as if dancing some crazy gavotte, the Bechnstein grand piano slid past me on two of its casters …".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>The poem "Wakefulness" by John Ashbery includes the sentence: "A gavotte of dust-motes / came to replace my seeing." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
