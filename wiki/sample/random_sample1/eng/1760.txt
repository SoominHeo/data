<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lotus position</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>Padmasana</b> or <b>Lotus Position</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a>: <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">पद्मासन</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Sanskrit" title="Help:IPA for Sanskrit">[pɐd̪mɑːs̪ɐn̪ɐ]</a></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAST" title="IAST">IAST</a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">padmāsana</span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a cross-legged sitting <a href="/wiki/Asana" title="Asana">asana</a> originating in meditative practices of <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a>, in which the feet are placed on the opposing thighs. It is an established asana, commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">Yoga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist contemplative</a> traditions. The <i>asana</i> is said to resemble a <a href="/wiki/Nelumbo_nucifera" title="Nelumbo nucifera">lotus</a>, to encourage breathing properly through associated meditative practice, and to foster physical stability.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>, the meditating ascetic God of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siddhartha_Gautama" title="Siddhartha Gautama">Siddhartha Gautama</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">Tirthankaras</a> (Teaching Gods) in <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a> have been depicted in the lotus position.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lotus_position&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="476" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg/170px-Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg/255px-Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg/340px-Hsuan_Hua_Hong_Kong_1.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p><i>Padmāsana</i> means "Lotus throne" and is also a term for actual thrones, often decorated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotus_Flower" title="Lotus Flower">lotus</a> foliage motifs, on which figures in art sit.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a>, the lotus position is also called the "<a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">vajra</a> position" (Skt. <i>vajrāsana</i>, Ch. 金剛座 <i>jīngāngzuò</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The traditions of <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a> also refer to the lotus position as the "vajra position."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Position">Position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lotus_position&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Babaji.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1342" data-file-width="1047" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Babaji.jpg/170px-Babaji.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Babaji.jpg/255px-Babaji.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Babaji.jpg/340px-Babaji.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>From the common <a href="/wiki/Sitting" title="Sitting">sitting</a> down on the floor (Indian Style, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-legged" title="Cross-legged">Cross-legged</a>) position (asana), one foot is placed on top of the opposite thigh with its sole facing upward and heel close to the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>. The other foot is then lifted up slowly and placed on the opposite thigh in a symmetrical way.</p>
<p>The knees are in contact with the ground. The <a href="/wiki/Torso" title="Torso">torso</a> is placed in balance and alignment such that the spinal column supports it with minimal muscular effort. The torso is centered above the hips. To relax the head and neck, the jaw is allowed to fall towards the neck and the back of the neck to lengthen. The shoulders move backwards and the ribcage lifts. The tongue rests on the roof of the mouth. The hands may rest on the knees in chin or jnana <a href="/wiki/Mudra" title="Mudra">mudra</a>. The arms are relaxed with the elbows slightly bent.</p>
<p>The eyes may be closed, the body relaxed, with awareness of the overall asana. Adjustments are made until balance and alignment are experienced. Alignment that creates relaxation is indicative of a suitable position for the asana. The asana should be natural and comfortable, without any sharp pains.</p>
<p>In most cases, a cushion (<a href="/wiki/Zafu" title="Zafu">zafu</a>) or mat (<a href="/wiki/Zabuton" title="Zabuton">zabuton</a>) is necessary in order to achieve this balance. One sits on the forward edge of the cushion or mat in order to incline one's pelvis forward, making it possible to center the spine and provide the necessary support. Only the most flexible people can achieve this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asana_(yoga)" title="Asana (yoga)">asana</a> without a support under their pelvis (and likewise does the <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a> explicitly advise).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Iconography">In Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lotus_position&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">Tirthankara</a> is represented either seated in Lotus posture or standing in the <i><a href="/wiki/Kayotsarga" title="Kayotsarga">Kayotsarga</a></i> posture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Balinese</a> Hinduism, a prominent feature of temples is a special form of <i>padmasana</i> shrine, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empty_throne" title="Empty throne">empty thrones</a> mounted on a column, for deities, especially <a href="/wiki/Acintya" title="Acintya">Acintya</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contra-indications">Contra-indications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lotus_position&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contra-indications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG/220px-Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG/330px-Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG/440px-Tanum%C3%A2nas%C3%AE_en_Meditacion_Loto_Padmasana.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Other meditation asanas are indicated until sufficient flexibility has been developed to sit comfortably in the Lotus. <a href="/wiki/Sciatica" title="Sciatica">Sciatica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacrum" title="Sacrum">sacral</a> infections and weak or injured knees are contra-indications to attempting the asana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since January 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<p>If your knees point in when your feet point straight ahead, the lotus position may be impossible to achieve. The joints on the opposite ends of the femurs and tibiae are rotated relative to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>== Benefits पद्मासन (padmasana)]] The Lotus position is adopted to allow the body to be held completely steady for long periods of time. This allows the mind to calm—the first step towards meditation. The asana applies pressure to the lower spine which may facilitate relaxation. The breath can slow down, muscular tension can decrease and blood pressure may subside. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccygeal" title="Coccygeal">coccygeal</a> and sacral nerves are toned as the normally large blood flow to the legs is redirected to the abdominal region, which may help to improve digestion. The practice of this asana leads to a straight spinal cord. It is believed that sitting in this posture causes good thoughts to come to the mind and thoughts of anger and lust to subside. The practice of this asana with repetition of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aum" title="Aum">Aum</a></i> leads to reduction in stress.</p>
