<p><b>Gabriel Naudé</b> (2 February 1600 – 10 July 1653) was a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Librarian" title="Librarian">librarian</a> and scholar. He was a prolific writer who produced works on many subjects including politics, religion, history and the supernatural. An influential work on <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a> was the 1627 book <i>Advice on Establishing a Library</i>. Naudé was later able to put into practice all the ideas he put forth in <i>Advice</i>, when he was given the opportunity to build and maintain the library of Cardinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jules_Mazarin" title="Jules Mazarin">Jules Mazarin</a>.</p>
<p>Naudé is a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bayle" title="Pierre Bayle">Pierre Bayle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_le_Bovier_de_Fontenelle" title="Bernard le Bovier de Fontenelle">Fontenelle</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Naud%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Naudé was born in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in early 1600 to a family of modest means. His father was a lowly official and his mother was a young illiterate woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was described by his teachers as tenacious and passionate about his education. Naudé entered college at a young age where he studied philosophy and grammar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Later he studied medicine at Paris and <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a> (where he attended <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Cremonini_(philosopher)" title="Cesare Cremonini (philosopher)">Cesare Cremonini</a>'s lessons), and became physician to <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII</a>.</p>
<p>At the age of twenty, Naudé published his first book <i>Le Marfore ou Discours Contre les Lisbelles</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The work would bring him to the attention of Henri de Mesme, President of the Paris Parlement. Mesme offered Naudé the job of librarian to his personal collection. Mesmes had a large library for the period (about 8,000 volumes) and it was open to scholars who had the appropriate references.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Naudé's service in Mesme's library would give him experience which he would use later to write the book <i>Advice on Establishing a Library</i>. Naudé wrote <i>Advice</i> for Mesme as a guide for building and maintaining his library. In 1629 he became librarian to Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Francesco_Guidi_di_Bagno" title="Giovanni Francesco Guidi di Bagno">Guidi di Bagno</a> at Rome, and on Bagni's death in 1641 librarian to Cardinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Barberini_(seniore)" title="Francesco Barberini (seniore)">Francesco Barberini</a>.</p>
<p>At the desire of <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Cardinal Richelieu">Cardinal Richelieu</a> he began a controversy with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictines" title="Benedictines">Benedictines</a>, denying <a href="/wiki/Jean_Gerson" title="Jean Gerson">Jean Gerson</a>'s authorship of <i>De Imitatione Christi</i>. Richelieu intended to make Naudé his librarian, and on his death Naudé accepted a similar offer from <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Mazarin" title="Cardinal Mazarin">Cardinal Mazarin</a>. For the next ten years he devoted himself to bringing together from all parts of Europe the noble assemblage of books known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Mazarine" title="Bibliothèque Mazarine">Bibliothèque Mazarine</a></i>. Mazarin had brought with him to Paris a collection numbering over 5,000 volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Like Naudé, he believed in an open library to be used by the public for the public good. In 1642 he purchased a building to house his library and he instructed Naudé to build up the finest collection possible.</p>
<p>The fastest way was to absorb entire libraries into the collection, advice that Naudé included in his book. Naudé plundered second hand book sellers, and Mazarin instructed his ambassadors, government officials and generals to collect books for him. Naudé was able to travel Europe, and during one trip that lasted several months he collected over 14,000 volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> By 1648 the library had built up to an estimated at 40,000 volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> It was open on a regular basis and had built up a sizable number (almost 100) of regular patrons, and several staff members to keep it functioning properly. It became the first in France to be open for all, without references.</p>
<p>Mazarin's library was sold by the <i><a href="/wiki/Parlement" title="Parlement">parlement</a></i> of Paris during the troubles of the <a href="/wiki/Fronde" title="Fronde">Fronde</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christina_of_Sweden" title="Christina of Sweden">Queen Christina</a> invited Naudé to <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>. He was not happy in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and on Mazarin's appeal that he should re-form his scattered library Naudé returned at once. But his health was broken, and he died on the journey in <a href="/wiki/Abbeville" title="Abbeville">Abbeville</a> on 10 July 1653.</p>
<p>The friend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gui_Patin" title="Gui Patin">Gui Patin</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">Pierre Gassendi</a> and all the liberal thinkers of his time, Naudé was no mere bookworm; his books show traces of the critical spirit which made him a worthy colleague of the humorists and scholars who prepared the way for the better known writers of the <i>siècle de <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_as_a_Librarian">Career as a Librarian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Naud%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career as a Librarian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Naudé, in his career as a librarian, “opposed censorship, and encouraged library owners to allow others to use their books, a practice he considered a great honor for the owner – an honor equal to that of having the opportunity to build a fine library."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was adamant about collecting in all languages, about all religions, subject matters, and literature.</p>
<p>During his career in librarianship, Naudé helped instruct collectors and libraries in the selection and acquisition of their titles and how to create catalogs for their libraries. He was a major proponent of scouring secondhand bookshops and print shops for valuable and hard to find literary works. “When Naudé has been in town, the booksellers' shops seem devastated as by a whirlwind. Having bought up in every last one of them all the books, whether in manuscript or in print, dealing in any language whatever with any subject or division of learning no matter what, he has left the stores stripped and bare.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Naudé also had interesting ideas on the locale where a library should be located. “While centrally located within the community it serves, a library should be at some distance from the noisiest streets. It should, if possible, be situated between some spacious court and a pleasant garden, from which it may enjoy good light, a wide and agreeable prospect, and pure air, unpolluted by marshes, sinks, or dunghills; the whole arrangement so well planned and ordered that it is compelled to share nothing unpleasant or obviously inconvenient.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Probably the most famous library that Naudé helped shape, and in which he served as librarian, was the <i><a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Mazarine" title="Bibliothèque Mazarine">Bibliothéque Mazarine</a></i> in Paris, the library of <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Mazarin" title="Cardinal Mazarin">Cardinal Jules Mazarin</a>. Naudé spent ten years of his life improving and shaping the <i>Bibliothéque</i>. It became the first public library in France because of Naudé’s insistence and was open to the public as early as 1644.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Naud%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Including works edited by him, a list of ninety-two pieces is given in the <i>Naudaeana</i>. The principal ones are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Le Marfore, ou discours contre les libelles</i> (Paris, 1620), very rare, reprinted 1868;</li>
<li><i>Instruction à la France sur la vérité de l'histoire des Frères de la Roze-Croix</i> (1623, 1624), displaying their impostures;</li>
<li><i>Apologie pour tous les grands personages faussement soupçonnez de magie</i> (1625, 1653, 1669, 1712), <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> Jerome Cardan and <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a> are among those defended;</li>
<li><i>Advis pour dresser une bibliothèque</i> (1627, 1644, 1676; translated by <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a>, 1661), full of sound and liberal views on librarianship and considered as a founding stone of <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a>;</li>
<li><i>Addition à l'histoire de <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louys XI</a></i> (1630), this includes an account of the origin of <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>;</li>
<li><i>Bibliographia politica</i> (Venice, 1633, etc.; in French, 1642);</li>
<li><i>De studio liberali syntagma</i> (1632, 1654), a practical treatise found in most collections of directions for studies;</li>
<li><i>De studio militari syntagma</i> (1637), esteemed in its day;</li>
<li><i>Considérations politiques sur les coups d'êtat</i>. A disciple of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machiavelli" title="Machiavelli">Machiavelli</a>, he considered that politics must be rendered "autonomous from morality, sovereign in relation to religion"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A <i>Bibliotheca Pontificia</i> was completed and seen into print by <a href="/wiki/Ludovicus_a_S._Carolo" title="Ludovicus a S. Carolo">Louis Jacob</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advice_on_establishing_a_library"><i>Advice on establishing a library</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Naud%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advice on establishing a library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Advice</i>, written as a set of instructions for a private collector and was based on Naudé's own experience and research. In the introduction of his book, Naudé wrote that he is not an expert in the field of librarianship but he presented what he believed to be the most important ideas. He based some of the opinions in <i>Advice</i> on his own experience in Mesme's library, and wrote out for Mesme the accepted practices and principles of librarians of the time. Chapters covered topics such as number of books, selecting the books, procuring the books, etc.</p>
<p>Naudé's first chapter poses the question, "Why establish a library?" He answers the question with a simple message; there is no greater honor than building a great library and sharing it with the public. Naudé believes libraries should model themselves after the best libraries of the world. The first task is to create a plan. Before a person can erect a library, he must educate himself on the subject of collecting and organizing books. A person must also seek the guidance of those who have already built their own libraries or are in the process. He suggests studying and copying the catalogues of other libraries.</p>
<p>Naudé devotes an entire chapter to book selection, remarked upon throughout. The first authors who need to be purchased are those considered experts in their respective fields. No matter whether they are ancient or modern works, if a book is held in high regard by practitioners of a particular field then it should be present in any collection. In addition, any well known interpretations or commentaries that exist are a necessity. Naudé suggested purchasing books in the original languages because meaning can often be lost in translation. He is strongly against censorship of any kind. Naudé believes that every book has a reader regardless of the subject; and that information should be free and available. Readers could always find use of a book, even if it is to refute the ideas presented on its pages. Certain books are popular at times but later forgotten; he argued that it would be beneficial to a library if there were multiple copies of these books to accommodate the popular tastes of the times.</p>
<p>In his chapter on book acquisition, Naudé gave tips. The easiest way is to purchase another library in its entirety. Naudé went on to praise second-hand book sellers who often provided good books at cheap prices. Naudé himself browsed book-binding and printing shops for used paper, and had once discovered a rare manuscript that a book binder was using as scrap paper.</p>
<p>Naudé included a chapter in <i>Advice</i> for arranging the books. In discussing arrangement he quoted <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, "It is order that gives light to memory." He gave instructions that he considered logical. His subject headings included: theology, philosophy, jurisprudence, medicine, history, mathematics and humanities. Naudé would add other subject heading in later years but these categories best represented the known body of knowledge in the world. Each section should be divided into subheadings and begin with the principal authors followed by the commentaries.</p>
