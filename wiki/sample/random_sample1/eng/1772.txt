<p>A <b>virtual private network</b> (<b>VPN</b>) extends a <a href="/wiki/Private_network" title="Private network">private network</a> across a public network, such as the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. It enables users to send and receive data across shared or public networks as if their computing devices were directly connected to the private network. Applications running across the VPN may therefore benefit from the functionality, security, and management of the private network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Virtual Private Networks may allow employees to securely access a corporate <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranet</a> while located outside the office. They are used to securely connect geographically separated offices of an organization, creating one cohesive network. Individual Internet users may secure their <a href="/wiki/Wireless" title="Wireless">wireless</a> transactions with a VPN, to circumvent geo-restrictions and censorship, or to connect to <a href="/wiki/Proxy_server" title="Proxy server">proxy servers</a> for the purpose of protecting personal identity and location. However, some Internet sites block access to known VPN technology to prevent the circumvention of their geo-restrictions.</p>
<p>A VPN is created by establishing a virtual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(network_topology)" title="Point-to-point (network topology)">point-to-point</a> connection through the use of dedicated connections, virtual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocols" title="Tunneling protocols">tunneling protocols</a>, or traffic <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a>. A VPN available from the public Internet can provide some of the benefits of a <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">wide area network</a> (WAN). From a user perspective, the resources available within the private network can be accessed remotely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditional VPNs are characterized by a point-to-point topology, and they do not tend to support or connect <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_domain" title="Broadcast domain">broadcast domains</a>, so services such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS" title="NetBIOS">NetBIOS</a> may not be fully supported or work as they would on a <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a> (LAN). Designers have developed VPN variants, such as <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Private_LAN_Service" title="Virtual Private LAN Service">Virtual Private LAN Service</a> (VPLS), and <a href="/wiki/Layer_2_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol">layer-2 tunneling protocols</a>, to overcome this limitation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early data networks allowed VPN-style remote connectivity through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dial-up_modem" title="Dial-up modem">dial-up modem</a> or through <a href="/wiki/Leased_line" title="Leased line">leased line</a> connections utilizing <a href="/wiki/Frame_Relay" title="Frame Relay">Frame Relay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asynchronous_Transfer_Mode" title="Asynchronous Transfer Mode">Asynchronous Transfer Mode</a> (ATM) virtual circuits, provisioned through a network owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Telephone_company" title="Telephone company">telecommunication carriers</a>. These networks are not considered true VPNs because they passively secure the data being transmitted by the creation of logical data streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They have been replaced by VPNs based on IP and IP/<a href="/wiki/Multiprotocol_Label_Switching" title="Multiprotocol Label Switching">Multi-protocol Label Switching</a> (MPLS) Networks, due to significant cost-reductions and increased bandwidth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> provided by new technologies such as Digital Subscriber Line (DSL)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and fiber-optic networks.</p>
<p>VPNs can be either remote-access (connecting a computer to a network) or site-to-site (connecting two networks). In a corporate setting, remote-access VPNs allow employees to access their company's <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranet</a> from home or while travelling outside the office, and site-to-site VPNs allow employees in geographically disparate offices to share one cohesive virtual network. A VPN can also be used to interconnect two similar networks over a dissimilar middle network; for example, two <a href="/wiki/IPv6" title="IPv6">IPv6</a> networks over an <a href="/wiki/IPv4" title="IPv4">IPv4</a> network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>VPN systems may be classified by:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">protocols</a> used to <a href="/wiki/IP_tunnel" title="IP tunnel">tunnel</a> the traffic</li>
<li>The tunnel's termination point location, e.g., on the customer <a href="/wiki/Edge_device" title="Edge device">edge</a> or network-provider edge</li>
<li>The type of topology of connections, such as site-to-site or network-to-network</li>
<li>The levels of security provided</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/OSI_model" title="OSI model">OSI layer</a> they present to the connecting network, such as Layer 2 circuits or Layer 3 network connectivity</li>
<li>The number of simultaneous connections</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_mechanisms">Security mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Security mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>VPNs cannot make online connections completely anonymous, but they can usually increase privacy and security. To prevent disclosure of private information, VPNs typically allow only authenticated remote access using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocols" title="Tunneling protocols">tunneling protocols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> techniques.</p>
<p>The VPN security model provides:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_security#Confidentiality" title="Information security">Confidentiality</a> such that even if the network traffic is sniffed at the packet level (see <a href="/wiki/Packet_analyzer" title="Packet analyzer">network sniffer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_packet_inspection" title="Deep packet inspection">Deep packet inspection</a>), an attacker would only see <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encrypted data</a></li>
<li>Sender <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authentication</a> to prevent unauthorized users from accessing the VPN</li>
<li>Message <a href="/wiki/Data_integrity" title="Data integrity">integrity</a> to detect any instances of tampering with transmitted messages</li>
</ul>
<p>Secure VPN protocols include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol_Security" title="Internet Protocol Security">Internet Protocol Security</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol_Security" title="Internet Protocol Security">IPsec</a>) as initially developed by the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a> (IETF) for <a href="/wiki/IPv6" title="IPv6">IPv6</a>, which was required in all standards-compliant implementations of <a href="/wiki/IPv6" title="IPv6">IPv6</a> before <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6434" rel="nofollow">RFC 6434</a> made it only a recommendation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfc6434_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rfc6434-7">[7]</a></sup> This standards-based security protocol is also widely used with <a href="/wiki/IPv4" title="IPv4">IPv4</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Layer_2_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol">Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol</a>. Its design meets most security goals: authentication, integrity, and confidentiality. IPsec uses encryption, encapsulating an IP packet inside an IPsec packet. De-encapsulation happens at the end of the tunnel, where the original IP packet is decrypted and forwarded to its intended destination.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">Transport Layer Security</a> (<a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">SSL/TLS</a>) can <a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">tunnel</a> an entire network's traffic (as it does in the <a href="/wiki/OpenVPN" title="OpenVPN">OpenVPN</a> project and <a href="/wiki/SoftEther_VPN" title="SoftEther VPN">SoftEther VPN</a> project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>) or secure an individual connection. A number of vendors provide remote-access VPN capabilities through SSL. An SSL VPN can connect from locations where IPsec runs into trouble with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_Address_Translation" title="Network Address Translation">Network Address Translation</a> and firewall rules.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Datagram_Transport_Layer_Security" title="Datagram Transport Layer Security">Datagram Transport Layer Security</a> (<a href="/wiki/Datagram_Transport_Layer_Security" title="Datagram Transport Layer Security">DTLS</a>) – used in <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems#Cisco_AnyConnect_VPN_Client" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco AnyConnect VPN</a> and in <a href="/wiki/OpenConnect" title="OpenConnect">OpenConnect</a> VPN<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to solve the issues <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">SSL/TLS</a> has with tunneling over <a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">UDP</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Point-to-Point_Encryption" title="Microsoft Point-to-Point Encryption">Microsoft Point-to-Point Encryption</a> (<a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Point-to-Point_Encryption" title="Microsoft Point-to-Point Encryption">MPPE</a>) works with the <a href="/wiki/Point-to-Point_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol">Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol</a> and in several compatible implementations on other platforms.</li>
<li>Microsoft <a href="/wiki/Secure_Socket_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol">Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol</a> (<a href="/wiki/Secure_Socket_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol">SSTP</a>) tunnels <a href="/wiki/Point-to-Point_Protocol" title="Point-to-Point Protocol">Point-to-Point Protocol</a> (PPP) or <a href="/wiki/Layer_2_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol">Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol</a> traffic through an <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">SSL</a> 3.0 channel. (SSTP was introduced in <a href="/wiki/Windows_Server_2008" title="Windows Server 2008">Windows Server 2008</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Windows_Vista" title="Windows Vista">Windows Vista</a> Service Pack 1.)</li>
<li>Multi Path Virtual Private Network (MPVPN). Ragula Systems Development Company owns the registered <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> "MPVPN".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Secure Shell (SSH) VPN – <a href="/wiki/OpenSSH" title="OpenSSH">OpenSSH</a> offers VPN tunneling (distinct from <a href="/wiki/Port_forwarding" title="Port forwarding">port forwarding</a>) to secure remote connections to a network or to inter-network links. OpenSSH server provides a limited number of concurrent tunnels. The VPN feature itself does not support personal authentication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Authentication">Authentication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Authentication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tunnel endpoints must be authenticated before secure VPN tunnels can be established. User-created remote-access VPNs may use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passwords" title="Passwords">passwords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biometrics" title="Biometrics">biometrics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-factor_authentication" title="Two-factor authentication">two-factor authentication</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptographic" title="Cryptographic">cryptographic</a> methods. Network-to-network tunnels often use passwords or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_certificates" title="Digital certificates">digital certificates</a>. They permanently store the key to allow the tunnel to establish automatically, without intervention from the administrator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Routing">Routing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Routing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">Tunneling protocols</a> can operate in a <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> <a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">network topology</a> that would theoretically not be considered as a VPN, because a VPN by definition is expected to support arbitrary and changing sets of network nodes. But since most <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a> implementations support a software-defined tunnel interface, customer-provisioned VPNs often are simply defined tunnels running conventional routing protocols.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provider-provisioned_VPN_building-blocks">Provider-provisioned VPN building-blocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Provider-provisioned VPN building-blocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Depending on whether a provider-provisioned VPN (PPVPN)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> operates in layer 2 or layer 3, the building blocks described below may be L2 only, L3 only, or combine them both. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiprotocol_label_switching" title="Multiprotocol label switching">Multi-protocol label switching</a> (MPLS) functionality blurs the L2-L3 identity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (June 2013)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc4026" rel="nofollow">RFC 4026</a> generalized the following terms to cover L2 and L3 VPNs, but they were introduced in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2547" rel="nofollow">RFC 2547</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> More information on the devices below can also be found in Lewis, Cisco Press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Customer (C) devices</dt>
</dl>
<p>A device that is within a customer's network and not directly connected to the service provider's network. C devices are not aware of the VPN.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Customer Edge device (CE)</dt>
</dl>
<p>A device at the edge of the customer's network which provides access to the PPVPN. Sometimes it's just a demarcation point between provider and customer responsibility. Other providers allow customers to configure it.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Provider edge device (PE)</dt>
</dl>
<p>A PE is a device, or set of devices, at the edge of the provider network which connects to customer networks through CE devices and presents the provider's view of the customer site. PEs are aware of the VPNs that connect through them, and maintain VPN state.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Provider device (P)</dt>
</dl>
<p>A P device operates inside the provider's core network and does not directly interface to any customer endpoint. It might, for example, provide routing for many provider-operated tunnels that belong to different customers' PPVPNs. While the P device is a key part of implementing PPVPNs, it is not itself VPN-aware and does not maintain VPN state. Its principal role is allowing the service provider to scale its PPVPN offerings, for example, by acting as an aggregation point for multiple PEs. P-to-P connections, in such a role, often are high-capacity optical links between major locations of providers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="User-visible_PPVPN_services">User-visible PPVPN services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: User-visible PPVPN services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OSI_Layer_2_services">OSI Layer 2 services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: OSI Layer 2 services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. </tr>
</table>
<dl>
<dt>Virtual LAN</dt>
</dl>
<p>A Layer 2 technique that allow for the coexistence of multiple LAN broadcast domains, interconnected via trunks using the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.1Q" title="IEEE 802.1Q">IEEE 802.1Q</a> trunking protocol. Other trunking protocols have been used but have become obsolete, including Inter-Switch Link (ISL), IEEE 802.10 (originally a security protocol but a subset was introduced for trunking), and ATM LAN Emulation (LANE).</p>
<dl>
<dt>Virtual private LAN service (VPLS)</dt>
</dl>
<p>Developed by <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a>, VLANs allow multiple tagged LANs to share common trunking. VLANs frequently comprise only customer-owned facilities. Whereas VPLS as described in the above section (OSI Layer 1 services) supports emulation of both point-to-point and point-to-multipoint topologies, the method discussed here extends Layer 2 technologies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.1d" title="802.1d">802.1d</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.1q" title="802.1q">802.1q</a> LAN trunking to run over transports such as <a href="/wiki/Metro_Ethernet" title="Metro Ethernet">Metro Ethernet</a>.</p>
<p>As used in this context, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VPLS" title="VPLS">VPLS</a> is a Layer 2 PPVPN, rather than a private line, emulating the full functionality of a traditional <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a> (LAN). From a user standpoint, a VPLS makes it possible to interconnect several LAN segments over a packet-switched, or optical, provider core; a core transparent to the user, making the remote LAN segments behave as one single LAN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a VPLS, the provider network emulates a learning bridge, which optionally may include VLAN service.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Pseudo wire (PW)</dt>
</dl>
<p>PW is similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VPLS" title="VPLS">VPLS</a>, but it can provide different L2 protocols at both ends. Typically, its interface is a WAN protocol such as <a href="/wiki/Asynchronous_Transfer_Mode" title="Asynchronous Transfer Mode">Asynchronous Transfer Mode</a> or <a href="/wiki/Frame_Relay" title="Frame Relay">Frame Relay</a>. In contrast, when aiming to provide the appearance of a LAN contiguous between two or more locations, the Virtual Private LAN service or IPLS would be appropriate.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Ethernet over IP tunneling</dt>
</dl>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://tools.ietf.org/search/rfc3378" rel="nofollow">EtherIP (RFC 3378)</a> is an Ethernet over IP tunneling protocol specification. EtherIP has only packet encapsulation mechanism. It has no confidentiality nor message integrity protection. EtherIP was introduced in the <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a> network stack<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/SoftEther_VPN" title="SoftEther VPN">SoftEther VPN</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-net-security.org_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-net-security.org-18">[18]</a></sup> server program.</p>
<dl>
<dt>IP-only LAN-like service (IPLS)</dt>
</dl>
<p>A subset of VPLS, the CE devices must have Layer 3 capabilities; the IPLS presents packets rather than frames. It may support IPv4 or IPv6.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OSI_Layer_3_PPVPN_architectures">OSI Layer 3 PPVPN architectures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: OSI Layer 3 PPVPN architectures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This section discusses the main architectures for PPVPNs, one where the PE disambiguates duplicate addresses in a single routing instance, and the other, virtual router, in which the PE contains a virtual router instance per VPN. The former approach, and its variants, have gained the most attention.</p>
<p>One of the challenges of PPVPNs involves different customers using the same address space, especially the IPv4 private address space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The provider must be able to disambiguate overlapping addresses in the multiple customers' PPVPNs.</p>
<dl>
<dt>BGP/MPLS PPVPN</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the method defined by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2547" rel="nofollow">RFC 2547</a>, BGP extensions advertise routes in the IPv4 VPN address family, which are of the form of 12-byte strings, beginning with an 8-byte <a href="/wiki/Route_distinguisher" title="Route distinguisher">Route Distinguisher</a> (RD) and ending with a 4-byte IPv4 address. RDs disambiguate otherwise duplicate addresses in the same PE.</p>
<p>PEs understand the topology of each VPN, which are interconnected with MPLS tunnels, either directly or via P routers. In MPLS terminology, the P routers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Label_Switch_Router" title="Label Switch Router">Label Switch Routers</a> without awareness of VPNs.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Virtual router PPVPN</dt>
</dl>
<p>The virtual router architecture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> as opposed to BGP/MPLS techniques, requires no modification to existing routing protocols such as BGP. By the provisioning of logically independent routing domains, the customer operating a VPN is completely responsible for the address space. In the various MPLS tunnels, the different PPVPNs are disambiguated by their label, but do not need routing distinguishers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unencrypted_tunnels">Unencrypted tunnels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Unencrypted tunnels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some virtual networks use <a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">tunneling protocols</a> without encryption for protecting the privacy of data. While VPNs often do provide security, an unencrypted <a href="/wiki/Overlay_network" title="Overlay network">overlay network</a> does not neatly fit within the secure or trusted categorization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, a tunnel set up between two hosts with <a href="/wiki/Generic_Routing_Encapsulation" title="Generic Routing Encapsulation">Generic Routing Encapsulation</a> (GRE) is a virtual private network, but neither secure nor trusted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Native <a href="/wiki/Plaintext" title="Plaintext">plaintext</a> tunneling protocols include <a href="/wiki/Layer_2_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol">Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol</a> (L2TP) when it is set up without <a href="/wiki/IPsec" title="IPsec">IPsec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Point-to-Point_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol">Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol</a> (PPTP) or <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Point-to-Point_Encryption" title="Microsoft Point-to-Point Encryption">Microsoft Point-to-Point Encryption</a> (MPPE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trusted_delivery_networks">Trusted delivery networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trusted delivery networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trusted VPNs do not use cryptographic <a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">tunneling</a>, and instead rely on the security of a single provider's network to protect the traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-Protocol_Label_Switching" title="Multi-Protocol Label Switching">Multi-Protocol Label Switching</a> (MPLS) often overlays VPNs, often with quality-of-service control over a trusted delivery network.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Layer_2_Tunneling_Protocol" title="Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol">Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol</a> (L2TP)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which is a standards-based replacement, and a compromise taking the good features from each, for two proprietary VPN protocols: Cisco's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L2F" title="L2F">Layer 2 Forwarding (L2F)</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> (obsolete as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) and Microsoft's Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PPTP" title="PPTP">PPTP</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>From the security standpoint, VPNs either trust the underlying delivery network, or must enforce security with mechanisms in the VPN itself. Unless the trusted delivery network runs among physically secure sites only, both trusted and secure models need an authentication mechanism for users to gain access to the VPN.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="VPNs_in_mobile_environments">VPNs in mobile environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: VPNs in mobile environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mobile_virtual_private_network" title="Mobile virtual private network">Mobile virtual private networks</a> are used in settings where an endpoint of the VPN is not fixed to a single <a href="/wiki/IP_address_spoofing" title="IP address spoofing">IP address</a>, but instead roams across various networks such as data networks from cellular carriers or between multiple <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> access points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phifer_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phifer-29">[29]</a></sup> Mobile VPNs have been widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_safety" title="Public safety">public safety</a>, where they give law enforcement officers access to mission-critical applications, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer-assisted_dispatch" title="Computer-assisted dispatch">computer-assisted dispatch</a> and criminal databases, while they travel between different subnets of a mobile network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> They are also used in <a href="/wiki/Field_service_management" title="Field service management">field service management</a> and by healthcare organizations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng-31">[31]</a></sup> among other industries.</p>
<p>Increasingly, mobile VPNs are being adopted by mobile professionals who need reliable connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng-31">[31]</a></sup> They are used for roaming seamlessly across networks and in and out of wireless coverage areas without losing application sessions or dropping the secure VPN session. A conventional VPN can not withstand such events because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_tunnel" title="Network tunnel">network tunnel</a> is disrupted, causing applications to disconnect, time out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phifer_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Phifer-29">[29]</a></sup> or fail, or even cause the computing device itself to <a href="/wiki/Crash_(computing)" title="Crash (computing)">crash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead of logically tying the endpoint of the network tunnel to the physical IP address, each tunnel is bound to a permanently associated IP address at the device. The mobile VPN software handles the necessary network authentication and maintains the network sessions in a manner transparent to the application and the user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phifer_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Phifer-29">[29]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Host_Identity_Protocol" title="Host Identity Protocol">Host Identity Protocol</a> (HIP), under study by the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a>, is designed to support mobility of hosts by separating the role of <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> for host identification from their locator functionality in an IP network. With HIP a mobile host maintains its logical connections established via the host identity identifier while associating with different IP addresses when roaming between access networks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="VPN_on_routers">VPN on routers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: VPN on routers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the increasing use of VPNs, many have started deploying VPN connectivity on routers for additional security and encryption of data transmission by using various cryptographic techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Setting up VPN support on a router and establishing a VPN allows any networked device to have access to the entire network—all devices look like local devices with local addresses. Supported devices are not restricted to those capable of running a VPN client.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draytek_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-draytek-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many router manufacturers, including Asus, Cisco, Draytek,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draytek_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-draytek-33">[33]</a></sup> Linksys, Netgear, and Yamaha, supply routers with built-in VPN clients. Some use open-source firmware such as <a href="/wiki/DD-WRT" title="DD-WRT">DD-WRT</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OpenWRT" title="OpenWRT">OpenWRT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tomato_(firmware)" title="Tomato (firmware)">Tomato</a>, in order to support additional protocols such as <a href="/wiki/OpenVPN" title="OpenVPN">OpenVPN</a>.</p>
<p>Setting up VPN services on a router requires a deep knowledge of network security and careful installation. Minor misconfiguration of VPN connections can leave the network vulnerable. Performance will vary depending on the ISP.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Networking_limitations">Networking limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_private_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Networking limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One major limitation of traditional VPNs is that they are point-to-point, and do not tend to support or connect <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_domain" title="Broadcast domain">broadcast domains</a>. Therefore, communication, software, and networking, which are based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OSI_layer" title="OSI layer">layer 2</a> and broadcast <a href="/wiki/Network_packet" title="Network packet">packets</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS" title="NetBIOS">NetBIOS</a> used in <a href="/wiki/My_Network_Places" title="My Network Places">Windows networking</a>, may not be fully supported or work exactly as they would on a real <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">LAN</a>. Variants on VPN, such as <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Private_LAN_Service" title="Virtual Private LAN Service">Virtual Private LAN Service</a> (VPLS), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Layer_2" title="Layer 2">layer 2</a> <a href="/wiki/Tunneling_protocol" title="Tunneling protocol">tunneling protocols</a>, are designed to overcome this limitation.</p>
