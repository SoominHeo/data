<p><b>Virtual work</b> arises in the application of the <i><a href="/wiki/Principle_of_least_action" title="Principle of least action">principle of least action</a></i> to the study of forces and movement of a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_system" title="Mechanical system">mechanical system</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> of a force acting on a particle as it moves along a displacement will be different for different displacements. Among all the possible displacements that a particle may follow, called <a href="/wiki/Virtual_displacement" title="Virtual displacement">virtual displacements</a>, one will minimize the action. This displacement is therefore the displacement followed by the particle according to the principle of least action. The work of a force on a particle along a virtual displacement is known as the virtual work.</p>
<p>Historically, virtual work and the associated <a href="/wiki/Calculus_of_variations" title="Calculus of variations">calculus of variations</a> were formulated to analyze systems of rigid bodies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lanczos_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lanczos-1">[1]</a></sup> but they have also been developed for the study of the mechanics of deformable bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principle of virtual work had always been used in some form since antiquity in the study of statics. It was used by the Greeks, medieval Arabs and Latins, and Renaissance Italians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capecchi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-capecchi-3">[3]</a></sup> The idea of virtual work was invoked by many notable physicists of the 17th century, such as Galileo, Descartes, Torricelli, Wallis, and Huygens, in varying degrees of generality, when solving problems in statics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capecchi_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-capecchi-3">[3]</a></sup> Working with Leibnizian concepts, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli" title="Johann Bernoulli">Johann Bernoulli</a> systematized the virtual work principle and made explicit the concept of infinitesimal displacement. He was able to solve problems for both rigid bodies as well as fluids. Bernoulli's version of virtual work law appeared in his letter to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Varignon" title="Pierre Varignon">Pierre Varignon</a> in 1715, which was later published in Varignon's second volume of <i>Nouvelle mécanique ou Statique</i> in 1725. This formulation of the principle is today known as the principle of virtual velocities and is commonly considered as the prototype of the contemporary virtual work principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capecchi_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-capecchi-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1743 D'Alembert published his <i>Traite de Dynamique</i> where he applied the principle of virtual work, based on the Bernoulli's work, to solve various problems in dynamics. His idea was to convert a dynamical problem into static problem by introducing <i>inertial force</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dugas_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dugas-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1768, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange" title="Lagrange">Lagrange</a> presented the virtual work principle in a more efficient form by introducing generalized coordinates and presented it as an alternative principle of mechanics by which all problems of equilibrium could be solved. A systematic exposition of Lagrange's program of applying this approach to all of mechanics, both static and dynamic, essentially the <a href="/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_principle" title="D'Alembert's principle">D'Alembert's principle</a>, was given in his <i>Méchanique Analitique</i> of 1788.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capecchi_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-capecchi-3">[3]</a></sup> Although Lagrange had presented his version of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_action_principle" title="Least action principle">least action principle</a> prior to this work, he recognized the virtual work principle to be more fundamental mainly because it could be assumed alone as the foundation for all mechanics, unlike the modern understanding that least action does not account for non-conservative forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-capecchi_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-capecchi-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If a force acts on a particle as it moves from point <i>A</i> to point <i>B</i>, then, for each possible trajectory that the particle may take, it is possible to compute the total work done by the force along the path. The <i>principle of virtual work</i>, which is the form of the principle of least action applied to these systems, states that the path actually followed by the particle is the one for which the difference between the work along this path and other nearby paths is zero (to first order). The formal procedure for computing the difference of functions evaluated on nearby paths is a generalization of the derivative known from differential calculus, and is termed <i>the calculus of variations</i>.</p>
<p>Consider a point particle that moves along a path which is described by a function <b>r</b>(<i>t</i>) from point <i>A</i>, where <b>r</b>(<i>t</i> = <i>t</i><sub>0</sub>), to point <i>B</i>, where <b>r</b>(<i>t</i> = <i>t</i><sub>1</sub>). It is possible that the particle moves from <i>A</i> to <i>B</i> along a nearby path described by <b>r</b>(<i>t</i>) + δ<b>r</b>(<i>t</i>), where δ<b>r</b>(<i>t</i>) is called the variation of <b>r</b>(<i>t</i>). The variation δ<b>r</b>(t) satisfies the requirement δ<b>r</b>(<i>t</i><sub>0</sub>) = δ<b>r</b>(<i>t</i><sub>1</sub>) = 0. The components of the variation, δ<i>r</i><sub>1</sub>(t), δ<i>r</i><sub>2</sub>(t) and δ<i>r</i><sub>3</sub>(t), are called virtual displacements. This can be generalized to an arbitrary mechanical system defined by the <a href="/wiki/Generalized_coordinates" title="Generalized coordinates">generalized coordinates</a> <i>q</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> , <i>i</i> = 1, ..., <i>n</i>. In which case, the variation of the trajectory <i>q</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> (<i>t</i>) is defined by the virtual displacements <i>δq</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> , <i>i</i> = 1, ..., <i>n</i>.</p>
<p>Virtual work is the total work done by the applied forces and the inertial forces of a mechanical system as it moves through a set of virtual displacements. When considering forces applied to a body in static equilibrium, the principle of least action requires the virtual work of these forces to be zero.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_equilibrium">Static equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Static equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">Static equilibrium</a> is a state in which the net force and net torque acted upon the system is zero. In other words, both <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">linear momentum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> of the system are conserved. The principle of virtual work states that <i>the virtual work of the applied forces is zero for all <a href="/wiki/Virtual_displacement" title="Virtual displacement">virtual movements</a> of the system from <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">static equilibrium</a></i>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constraint_forces">Constraint forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Constraint forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important benefit of the principle of virtual work is that only forces that do work as the system moves through a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_displacement" title="Virtual displacement">virtual displacement</a> are needed to determine the mechanics of the system. There are many forces in a mechanical system that do no work during a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_displacement" title="Virtual displacement">virtual displacement</a>, which means that they need not be considered in this analysis. The two important examples are (i) the internal forces in a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">rigid body</a>, and (ii) the constraint forces at an ideal <a href="/wiki/Kinematic_pair" title="Kinematic pair">joint</a>.</p>
<p>Lanczos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lanczos_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lanczos-1">[1]</a></sup> presents this as the postulate: "The virtual work of the forces of reaction is always zero for any <a href="/wiki/Virtual_displacement" title="Virtual displacement">virtual displacement</a> which is in harmony with the given kinematic constraints." The argument is as follows. The principle of virtual work states that in <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> the virtual work of the forces applied to a system is zero. <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws</a> state that at <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> the applied forces are equal and opposite to the reaction, or constraint forces. This means the virtual work of the constraint forces must be zero as well.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_of_the_Lever">Law of the Lever</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Law of the Lever">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">lever</a> is modeled as a rigid bar connected to a ground frame by a hinged joint called a fulcrum. The lever is operated by applying an input force <b>F</b><sub><i>A</i></sub> at a point <i>A</i> located by the coordinate vector <b>r</b><sub><i>A</i></sub> on the bar. The lever then exerts an output force <b>F</b><sub><i>B</i></sub> at the point <i>B</i> located by <b>r</b><sub><i>B</i></sub>. The rotation of the lever about the fulcrum <i>P</i> is defined by the rotation angle <i>θ</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gear_train">Gear train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gear train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A gear train is formed by mounting gears on a frame so that the teeth of the gears engage. Gear teeth are designed to ensure the pitch circles of engaging gears roll on each other without slipping, this provides a smooth transmission of rotation from one gear to the next. For this analysis, we consider a gear train that has one degree-of-freedom, which means the angular rotation of all the gears in the gear train are defined by the angle of the input gear.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_equilibrium_for_rigid_bodies">Dynamic equilibrium for rigid bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamic equilibrium for rigid bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the principle of virtual work for applied forces is used on individual particles of a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">rigid body</a>, the principle can be generalized for a rigid body: <i>When a rigid body that is in equilibrium is subject to virtual compatible displacements, the total virtual work of all external forces is zero; and conversely, if the total virtual work of all external forces acting on a rigid body is zero then the body is in equilibrium</i>.</p>
<p>If a system is not in static equilibrium, D'Alembert showed that by introducing the acceleration terms of Newton's laws as inertia forces, this approach is generalized to define dynamic equilibrium. The result is D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work, which is used to derive the equations of motion for a mechanical system of rigid bodies.</p>
<p>The expression <i>compatible displacements</i> means that the particles remain in contact and displace together so that the work done by pairs of action/reaction inter-particle forces cancel out. Various forms of this principle have been credited to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli" title="Johann Bernoulli">Johann (Jean) Bernoulli</a> (1667–1748) and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a> (1700–1782).</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>The physical interpretation of the above equation is, <i>the External virtual work is equal to internal virtual work when equilibrated forces and stresses undergo unrelated but consistent displacements and strains</i>.</p>
<p>For practical applications:</p>
<ul>
<li>In order to impose equilibrium on real stresses and forces, we use consistent virtual displacements and strains in the virtual work equation.</li>
<li>In order to impose consistent displacements and strains, we use equilibriated virtual stresses and forces in the virtual work equation.</li>
</ul>
<p>These two general scenarios give rise to two often stated variational principles. They are valid irrespective of material behaviour.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_forms">Alternative forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_work&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Alternative forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A specialization of the principle of virtual forces is the <a href="/wiki/Unit_dummy_force_method" title="Unit dummy force method">unit dummy force method</a>, which is very useful for computing displacements in structural systems. According to <a href="/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_principle" title="D'Alembert's principle">D'Alembert's principle</a>, inclusion of inertial forces as additional body forces will give the virtual work equation applicable to dynamical systems. More generalized principles can be derived by:</p>
<ul>
<li>allowing variations of all quantities.</li>
<li>using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_multipliers" title="Lagrange multipliers">Lagrange multipliers</a> to impose boundary conditions and/or to relax the conditions specified in the two states.</li>
</ul>
<p>These are described in some of the references.</p>
<p>Among the many <a href="/wiki/Energy_principles_in_structural_mechanics" title="Energy principles in structural mechanics">energy principles in structural mechanics</a>, the virtual work principle deserves a special place due to its generality that leads to powerful applications in <a href="/wiki/Structural_analysis" title="Structural analysis">structural analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solid_mechanics" title="Solid mechanics">solid mechanics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_method_in_structural_mechanics" title="Finite element method in structural mechanics">finite element method in structural mechanics</a>.</p>
