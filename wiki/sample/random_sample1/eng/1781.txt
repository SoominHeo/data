<p><b>Echidnas</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, sometimes known as <b>Spiny Bois</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwstSBE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pwstSBE-1">[1]</a></sup> belong to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Tachyglossidae</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">monotreme</a> order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a>-laying <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. The four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> species, together with the <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a>, are the only surviving members of the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> Monotremata and are the only living mammals that lay eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwf03_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwf03-2">[2]</a></sup> The diet of some species consists of ants and termites, but they are not closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Anteater" title="Anteater">true anteaters</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. Echidnas live in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>.</p>
<p>Echidnas evidently evolved between 20 and 50 million years ago, descending from a platypus-like monotreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-3">[3]</a></sup> This ancestor was aquatic, but echidnas adapted to life on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The echidnas are named after <a href="/wiki/Echidna_(mythology)" title="Echidna (mythology)">Echidna</a>, a creature from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> who was half-woman, half-snake, as the animal was perceived to have qualities of both mammals and reptiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Echidnas are medium-sized, <a href="/wiki/Solitary_animal" title="Solitary animal">solitary</a> mammals covered with coarse <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spine_(zoology)" title="Spine (zoology)">spines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO06_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO06-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg/220px-Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg/330px-Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg/440px-Onkapringa_River_NP_echidna_spines_P1000601.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Superficially, they resemble the anteaters of South America and other spiny mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porcupine" title="Porcupine">porcupines</a>. They are usually black or brown in color. There have been several reports of <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albino</a> echidnas, their eyes pink and their spines white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO06_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO06-5">[5]</a></sup> They have elongated and slender snouts that function as both mouth and nose. Like the platypus, they are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Electroreception" title="Electroreception">electrosensors</a>, but while the platypus has 40,000 electroreceptors on its bill, the long-billed echidna has only 2,000 electroreceptors, and the short-billed echidna, which lives in a drier environment, has no more than 400 located at the tip of its snout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOL100707_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOL100707-6">[6]</a></sup> They have very short, strong <a href="/wiki/Limb_(anatomy)" title="Limb (anatomy)">limbs</a> with large <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a>, and are powerful diggers. Echidnas have tiny <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouths</a> and toothless <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">jaws</a>. The echidna feeds by tearing open soft logs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthill" title="Anthill">anthills</a> and the like, and using its long, sticky <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, which protrudes from its snout, to collect prey. The echidnas' ears are slits on the sides of their heads that are usually unseen due to the fact that they are blanketed by their spines. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinna_(anatomy)" title="Pinna (anatomy)">external ear</a> is created by a large cartilaginous funnel, deep in the muscle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO06_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO06-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Short-beaked_echidna" title="Short-beaked echidna">short-beaked echidna</a>'s diet consists largely of ants and termites, while the <i>Zaglossus</i> species typically eats worms and insect larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-info_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-info-7">[7]</a></sup> They have no teeth, and break down their food by grinding it between the bottoms of their mouths and their tongues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Echidnas do not tolerate extreme temperatures; they use caves and rock crevices to shelter from harsh weather conditions. Echidnas are found in forests and woodlands, hiding under vegetation, roots or piles of debris. They sometimes use the burrows of animals such as rabbits and wombats. Individual echidnas have large, mutually overlapping territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Echidnas are capable swimmers. When swimming, they expose their snout and some of their spines. They are known to journey to water in order to groom and bathe themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short-beak_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Short-beak-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tongues of <a href="/wiki/Long-beaked_echidna" title="Long-beaked echidna">long-beaked echidnas</a> have sharp, tiny spines that help them capture their prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-info_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-info-7">[7]</a></sup> Echidnas faeces are 7 centimetres long and are cylindrical in shape; they are usually broken and unrounded, and composed largely of dirt and ant-hill material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Echidnas and the platypus are the only egg-laying mammals, known as monotremes. The average lifespan of an echidna in the wild is estimated around 16 years. When fully grown a female can weigh up to 4.5 kilograms (9.9 lbs) and a male can weigh up to 6 kilograms (13.2 lbs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup> The echidnas' sex can be inferred from their size, as males are 25% larger than females on average. The reproductive organs also differ, but both sexes have a single opening called a cloaca, which they use to urinate, release their faeces and to mate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO06_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO06-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Male echidnas have non-venomous <a href="/wiki/Spur_(zoology)" title="Spur (zoology)">spurs</a> on the hind feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" title="Neocortex">neocortex</a> makes up half of the echidna's brain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc121119_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc121119-11">[11]</a></sup> compared to 80% of a human brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar1993_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar1993-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar1998_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar1998-13">[13]</a></sup> Due to their low <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and accompanying stress resistance, echidnas are long-lived for their size; the longest recorded lifespan for a captive echidna is 50 years, with anecdotal accounts of wild individuals reaching 45 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andiv09_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-andiv09-14">[14]</a></sup> Contrary to previous research, the echidna does enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_eye_movement_(sleep)" title="Rapid eye movement (sleep)">REM</a> sleep, but only when the ambient temperature is around 25 °C (77 °F). At temperatures of 15 °C (59 °F) and 28 °C (82 °F), REM sleep is suppressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsl0003_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsl0003-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joeys_and_Puggles.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="521" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Joeys_and_Puggles.png/220px-Joeys_and_Puggles.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Joeys_and_Puggles.png/330px-Joeys_and_Puggles.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Joeys_and_Puggles.png/440px-Joeys_and_Puggles.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

Echidna "puggle" (a) compared to various "joeys": <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Opossum" title="Virginia Opossum">Virginia Opossum</a> (b), <a href="/wiki/Gray_short-tailed_opossum" title="Gray short-tailed opossum">Gray short-tailed opossum</a> (c), <a href="/wiki/Eastern_quoll" title="Eastern quoll">Eastern quoll</a> (d), <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">Koala</a> (e), <a href="/wiki/Brushtail_possum" title="Brushtail possum">Brushtail possum</a> (f) and <a href="/wiki/Southern_brown_bandicoot" title="Southern brown bandicoot">Southern brown bandicoot</a> (g).</div>

<p>The female lays a single soft-shelled, leathery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a> 22 days after mating, and deposits it directly into her pouch. While hatching, the baby echidna opens the leather shell with a reptile-like egg tooth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Hatching takes place after 10 days; the young echidna, born larval and fetus-like, then sucks <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Lactiferous_duct" title="Lactiferous duct">pores</a> of the two <a href="/wiki/Mammary_gland" title="Mammary gland">milk patches</a> (monotremes have no <a href="/wiki/Nipple" title="Nipple">nipples</a>) and remains in the pouch for 45 to 55 days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arkiveTGA_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-arkiveTGA-17">[17]</a></sup> at which time it starts to develop spines. The mother digs a nursery burrow and deposits the young, returning every five days to suckle it until it is weaned at seven months.</p>
<p><span id="Mating"></span>Male echidnas have a four-headed <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>. During mating, the heads on one side "shut down" and do not grow in size; the other two are used to release semen into the female's two-branched reproductive tract. Each time it copulates, it alternates heads in sets of two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsc071026_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsc071026-19">[19]</a></sup> When not in use, the penis is retracted inside a <a href="/wiki/Preputial_gland" title="Preputial gland">preputial</a> sac in the cloaca. The male echidna's penis is 7 centimetres (2.8 in) long when erect, and its shaft is covered with <a href="/wiki/Penile_spines" title="Penile spines">penile spines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VogelnestWoods2008_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-VogelnestWoods2008-20">[20]</a></sup> These may be used to induce ovulation in the female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a challenge to study the echidna in their natural habitat and they show no interest in mating while in captivity. Therefore, no one has ever seen an echidna ejaculate. There have been previous attempts, trying to force the echidna to ejaculate through the use of <a href="/wiki/Electroejaculation" title="Electroejaculation">electrically stimulated ejaculation</a> in order to <a href="/wiki/Semen_collection" title="Semen collection">obtain semen samples</a> but has only resulted in the penis swelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsc071026_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-nsc071026-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Breeding season begins in late June and extends through September. Males will form lines up to ten individuals long, the youngest echidna trailing last, that follow the female and attempt to mate. During a mating season an echidna may switch between lines. This is known as the "train" system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup> Two weeks after mating, a single fertilized egg, weighing 380 milligrams<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (some sources say 1.5 – 2 grams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>) and being about 1.4 centimetres long, is implanted in a rear-facing pouch that has developed on the female, where it is held for ten days before hatching. The young echidna, called a puggle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taronga_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taronga-24">[24]</a></sup> is then held in the pouch for two to three months before being expelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short-beak_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Short-beak-9">[9]</a></sup> Puggles will stay within their mother's den for up to a year before leaving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Echidnas are very timid animals. When they feel endangered they attempt to bury themselves or if exposed they will curl into a ball, both methods using their spines to shield them. Strong front arms allow echidnas to continue to dig themselves in whilst holding fast against a predator attempting to remove them from the hole. Although they have a way to protect themselves, the echidnas still face many dangers. Some predators include wild cats, foxes, domestic dogs and <a href="/wiki/Goanna" title="Goanna">goannas</a>. Snakes pose as a large threat to the echidna species because they slither into their burrows and prey on the young spineless puggles. Some precautions that can be taken include keeping the environment clean by picking up litter and causing less pollution, planting vegetation for echidnas to use as shelter, supervising pets, reporting hurt echidnas or just leaving them undisturbed. Merely grabbing them may cause stress, and picking them up improperly may even result in injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Echidna_skeleton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="883" data-file-width="1200" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Echidna_skeleton.jpg/220px-Echidna_skeleton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Echidna_skeleton.jpg/330px-Echidna_skeleton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Echidna_skeleton.jpg/440px-Echidna_skeleton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Echidna_skeleton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Short-beaked echidna skeleton</div>

<p>The first divergence between oviparous (egg-laying) and viviparous (offspring develop internally) mammals is believed to have occurred during the Triassic period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> However, there is still some disagreement on this estimated time of divergence. Though most findings from genetics studies (especially those concerning nuclear genes) are in agreement with the paleontological findings, some results from other techniques and sources, like mitochondrial DNA, are in slight disagreement with findings from fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock" title="Molecular clock">Molecular clock</a> data suggest echidnas split from <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypuses</a> between 19 and 48 million years ago, and that platypus-like fossils dating back to over 112.5 million years ago therefore represent <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> forms, rather than close relatives of the modern platypus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-3">[3]</a></sup> This would imply that echidnas evolved from water-foraging ancestors that returned to living completely on the land, even though this put them in competition with marsupials. Further evidence of possible water-foraging ancestors can be found in some of the echidna's phenotypic traits as well. Traits such as: as aqua dynamic streamlining, dorsally projecting hind limbs acting as rudders, and locomotion founded on hypertrophied humeral long-axis rotation, which provides a very efficient swimming stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-3">[3]</a></sup> Consequently, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a> reproduction in monotremes may have given them an advantage over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupials" title="Marsupials">marsupials</a>, a view consistent with present ecological partitioning between the two groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-3">[3]</a></sup> This advantage could as well be in part responsible for the observed associated adaptive radiation of echidnas and expansion of the niche space, which together contradict the fairly common assumption of halted morphological and molecular evolution that continues to be associated with monotremes. Furthermore, studies of mitochondrial DNA in platypuses have also found that monotremes and marsupials are most likely sister taxa. It also implies that any shared derived morphological traits between marsupials and placental mammals either occurred independently from one another or were lost in the lineage to monotremes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Echidnas are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_classification" title="Biological classification">classified</a> into three <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flannery_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flannery-28">[28]</a></sup> The genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaglossus" title="Zaglossus">Zaglossus</a></i> includes three extant <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and two species known only from <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>, while only one extant species from the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tachyglossus" title="Tachyglossus">Tachyglossus</a></i> is known. The third genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Megalibgwilia" title="Megalibgwilia">Megalibgwilia</a></i>, is known only from fossils.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zaglossus"><i>Zaglossus</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Zaglossus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The three living <i>Zaglossus</i> species are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flannery_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flannery-28">[28]</a></sup> They are rare and are hunted for food. They forage in leaf litter on the forest floor, eating <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. The species are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Western_long-beaked_echidna" title="Western long-beaked echidna">Western long-beaked echidna</a> (<i>Z. bruijni</i>), of the highland <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sir_David%27s_long-beaked_echidna" title="Sir David's long-beaked echidna">Sir David's long-beaked echidna</a> (<i>Z. attenboroughi</i>), described in 1961 and preferring a still higher habitat;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eastern_long-beaked_echidna" title="Eastern long-beaked echidna">Eastern long-beaked echidna</a> (<i>Z. bartoni</i>), of which four distinct subspecies have been identified.</li>
</ul>

<li>†<i><a href="/wiki/Zaglossus_hacketti" title="Zaglossus hacketti">Zaglossus hacketti</a></i>.</li>

In Australia, the short-beaked echidna may be found in many environments, including urban parkland, such as the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Burley_Griffin" title="Lake Burley Griffin">Lake Burley Griffin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>, as depicted here.</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Short-beaked_echidna" title="Short-beaked echidna">short-beaked echidna</a> (<i>Tachyglossus aculeatus</i>) is found in southern, southeast and northeast <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, and also occurs in almost all Australian environments, from the snow-clad <a href="/wiki/Australian_Alps" title="Australian Alps">Australian Alps</a> to the deep <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">Outback</a>, essentially anywhere ants and termites are available. It is smaller than the <i>Zaglossus</i> species, and it has longer hair.</p>

<p>Despite the similar dietary habits and methods of consumption to those of an anteater, there is no evidence supporting the idea that echidna-like monotremes have been myrmecophagic (ant or termite-eating) since the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>. The fossil evidence of invertebrate-feeding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandicoots" title="Bandicoots">bandicoots</a> and rat-kangaroos, from around the time of the platypus–echidna <a href="/wiki/Genetic_divergence" title="Genetic divergence">divergence</a> and pre-dating <i>Tachyglossus</i>, show evidence that echidnas expanded into new ecospace despite competition from marsupials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megalibgwilia"><i>Megalibgwilia</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Megalibgwilia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Megalibgwilia" title="Megalibgwilia">Megalibgwilia</a></i> is known only from fossils:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>M. ramsayi</i> from <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> sites in Australia;</li>
<li><i>M. robusta</i> from <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> sites in Australia.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Echidnas_in_popular_culture">Echidnas in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echidna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Echidnas in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li>The echidna appears on the reverse of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_five-cent_coin" title="Australian five-cent coin">Australian five-cent coin</a>.</li>

<li>Millie, an echidna, was a mascot for the <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics" title="2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Summer Olympics</a>.</li>
