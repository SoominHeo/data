<p><b>Katsuobushi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%B0%B9%E7%AF%80" title="wikt:鰹節">鰹節</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is dried, <a href="/wiki/Fermented_fish" title="Fermented fish">fermented</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smoked_fish" title="Smoked fish">smoked</a> <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a> (<i>Katsuwonus pelamis</i>). Sometimes known as <b>Bonito flakes</b>, young <a href="/wiki/Bonito" title="Bonito">bonito</a> is sometimes used as a cheaper substitute for <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a>, particularly in canned products, and has a similar color and texture, but not all countries allow marketing of bonito as skipjack tuna. Katuobushi or similarly prepared fish is also known as <i><b>okaka</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">おかか</span>)</span>.</p>
<p>Shaved <i>Katsuobushi</i> and dried <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> - <i><a href="/wiki/Kombu" title="Kombu">kombu</a></i> - are the main ingredients of <i><a href="/wiki/Dashi" title="Dashi">dashi</a></i>, a broth that forms the basis of many soups (such as <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">miso</a>) and sauces (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Soba" title="Soba">soba</a> no tsukejiru</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>.</p>
<p>Katsuobushi's distinct <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">umami</a> taste comes from its high <a href="/wiki/Inosinic_acid" title="Inosinic acid">inosinic acid</a> content. Traditionally made <i>katsuobushi</i>, known as <i>karebushi</i>, is deliberately planted with <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_glaucus" title="Aspergillus glaucus">Aspergillus glaucus</a></i> fungus in order to reduce moisture. Katsuobushi has also been shown to impart a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kokumi" title="Kokumi">kokumi</a> flavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon being placed on hot food, the heat waves cause the thin and light katsuobushi to move about, giving it a special aesthetic look. It is placed on pizzas to make the top of the pizza seem alive with the "dancing" katsuobushi.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although katsuobushi is common in Japanese cuisine, other fish may be used like mackarel (<i>sababushi</i>) or <i>urumebushi</i> (sardine) for example.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_production_process">Traditional production process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditional production process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Katsuobushi_block.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="944" data-file-width="2592" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Katsuobushi_block.JPG/220px-Katsuobushi_block.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Katsuobushi_block.JPG/330px-Katsuobushi_block.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Katsuobushi_block.JPG/440px-Katsuobushi_block.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Katsuobushi_block.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Katsuobushi is sold in wood-like blocks</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The fish is beheaded, gutted and filleted. The fatty belly area does not lend well to being preserved, so it is also trimmed off. The fillets are then arranged in a basket and simmered just below boiling for an hour to an hour and a half, depending on the size of the fillets. The rib bones are removed after the fillets have been boiled.</p>
<p>The fillets are then smoked using oak, pasania, or castanopsis wood; this process can take up to a month. They are smoked for 5–6 hours in one session, left to rest for one day for the condensation to rise to the surface, then fired and smoked again the next day, repeating this smoking and resting cycle 12–15 times. The built up tar from the smoke is cleaned from the surface using a grinder. At this stage the fillets are called <i>aragatsuo</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">荒節</span>)</span> and most commonly found in stores shaved and packaged for sale under the name <i>katsuo-kezuri-bushi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鰹削り節</span>)</span> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanakatsuo" title="Hanakatsuo">hanakatsuo</a> — they are not true katsuobushi without the last fermentation stage, but still valued as a good substitute.</p>
<p>The last stage of creating a katsuobushi is to allow the fish to sun-dry using the assistance of mold. The fillets are sprayed with <i>Aspergillus glaucus</i> culture and left for 2 weeks in a closed cultivation room. The mold ferments the fillets and also siphons out any residual moisture.</p>
<p>The mold is continually scraped off, with further sun-drying of the fish increasing hardness and dryness until it resembles a piece of wood, with less than 20% of its original weight. By definition, only fillets that have been treated in this manner may be referred to as a katsuobushi. However, after repeating this process of mold growth and sun-drying at least twice, the katsuobushi can also be called <i>karebushi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">枯節</span>, "dried fillet")</span>, and fillets repeating this process more than three times can be called <i>honkarebushi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">本枯節</span>, "true dried fillet")</span>. When tapped together lightly, they sound almost metallic, and unlike their dull beige outer appearance, when broken open they are a translucent deep ruby color inside. Rarely, very high-end <i>honkarebushi</i> repeat this drying process for over two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaving">Shaving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shaving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="626" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg/220px-Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg/330px-Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg/440px-Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Katsuobushi_kezuriki.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Katsuobushi kezuriki used to prepare Katsuobushi shavings</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditionally, large chunks of <i>katsuobushi</i> were kept at hand and shaved when needed with an instrument called a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katsuobushi_kezuriki" title="Katsuobushi kezuriki">katsuobushi kezuriki</a></i>, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Plane_(tool)" title="Plane (tool)">wood plane</a>.</p>
<p>Katsuobushi is today typically found in bags of small pink-brown shavings. Smaller, thinner shavings, called hanakatsuo (花鰹; はなかつお), are used as a flavoring and topping for many Japanese dishes, such as <a href="/wiki/Okonomiyaki" title="Okonomiyaki">okonomiyaki</a>. Larger, thicker shavings, called kezurikatsuo (削り鰹; けずりかつお), are used to make the ubiquitous dashi stock.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to making dashi, other popular uses of katsuobushi include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Okaka (おかか), finely chopped katsuobushi dressed with soy sauce.
<ul>
<li>As a stuffing for rice balls (<a href="/wiki/Onigiri" title="Onigiri">onigiri</a>).</li>
<li>As a topping for rice. Popular for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bent%C5%8D" title="Bentō">bentō</a>, often covered with <a href="/wiki/Nori" title="Nori">strips of laver</a>.</li>
<li>Dried okaka is used as an ingredient of <i><a href="/wiki/Furikake" title="Furikake">furikake</a></i> rice topping (called "okaka furikake").</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>As a seasoning for cold <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">tofu</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Hiyayakko" title="Hiyayakko">hiyayakko</a></i>, 冷奴) along with grated <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_onion" title="Welsh onion">Welsh onion</a> (a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_onion" title="Spring onion">spring onion</a>).</li>
<li>Sprinkled with <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> seeds and chopped <a href="/wiki/Nori" title="Nori">laver</a> atop cold soba noodles (<a href="/wiki/Soba" title="Soba">zarusoba</a>).</li>
<li>As a topping on <a href="/wiki/Takoyaki" title="Takoyaki">takoyaki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Okonomiyaki" title="Okonomiyaki">okonomiyaki</a>.</li>
<li>As a seasoning on <a href="/wiki/Century_egg" title="Century egg">century egg</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Sesame_oil" title="Sesame oil">sesame oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a>.</li>
<li>As a high-protein treat for <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> sold at pet stores.</li>
<li>As a topping for ramen mixed with salt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mycotoxin" title="Mycotoxin">mycotoxin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-nitropropionic_acid" title="Beta-nitropropionic acid">beta-nitropropionic acid</a> has been found on katsuobushi as well as <a href="/wiki/Miso" title="Miso">miso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a>, two other Japanese fungal fermented products. Certain strains of <i>A. glaucus</i> are reported to produce mycotoxins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the smoking process which involves tar and charcoal, amounts of <a href="/wiki/Benzopyrene" title="Benzopyrene">Benzopyrene</a> exceeding EU standards, as much as 37μg per kilogram, have been detected in commercially sold katsuobushi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As a result, they have been once banned for sales in the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katsuobushi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
