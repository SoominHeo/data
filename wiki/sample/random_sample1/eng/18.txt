<p><i><b>Pokémon Gold Version</b></i> and <i><b>Silver Version</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> are the second installments of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(video_game_series)" title="Pokémon (video game series)"><i>Pokémon</i> series</a> of <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/Game_Freak" title="Game Freak">Game Freak</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a>. They were released in Japan in 1999, Australia and North America in 2000, and Europe in 2001. <i><a href="#Pok.C3.A9mon_Crystal">Pokémon Crystal</a></i>, a special edition, was released roughly a year later in each region. In 2009, Nintendo <a href="/wiki/Video_game_remake" title="Video game remake">remade</a> <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> as <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_HeartGold_and_SoulSilver" title="Pokémon HeartGold and SoulSilver"><i>Pokémon HeartGold</i> and <i>SoulSilver</i></a>.</p>
<p>The games introduce 100 new species of Pokémon, and follow the progress of the central character, whose name the player may choose, in his quest to master <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_game_mechanics#Pok.C3.A9mon_battles" title="Pokémon game mechanics">Pokémon battling</a>. Both games are independent of each other but feature largely the same plot and, while both can be played separately, it is necessary to trade between them and their <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward compatible</a> predecessors in order to fully complete the games' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9dex" title="Pokédex">Pokédexes</a>. The Johto Saga of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(anime)" title="Pokémon (anime)">Pokémon anime</a> is based on the new region introduced in the games.</p>
<p><i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were critically acclaimed upon release and they are considered by many to be the best games in the entire series, as well as some of the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_generation_of_video_game_consoles" title="Fifth generation of video game consoles">fifth generation console gaming</a>'s most significant titles. They continued the enormous success of <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue">their predecessors</a> as Pokémon began to form into a multi-billion dollar franchise. The games almost matched the sales of <i>Pokémon Red</i> and <i>Blue</i> and went on to jointly sell millions of copies worldwide. By 2010, the recorded sales of <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were at 23 million units.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gameplay_of_Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Gameplay of Pokémon">Gameplay of Pokémon</a></div>
<p>Like <i>Pokémon Red</i>, <i>Blue</i> and <i>Yellow</i>, <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> are played from a top-down perspective, or third-person, with players directly navigating the <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">fictional universe</a>, interacting with objects and people. As the player explores this world, he or she will encounter different <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrains</a>, such as grassy fields, forests, caves, and seas in which different Pokémon species reside. As the player randomly encounters one of these creatures, the field switches to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn-based" title="Turn-based">turn-based</a> "battle scene", where the Pokémon will fight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two main goals within the games: following through the main storyline and defeating the Elite Four and Lance to become the new Champion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> and completing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9dex" title="Pokédex">Pokédex</a> by capturing, evolving, and trading to obtain all 251 creatures. A major aspect of this is developing and raising the player's Pokémon by battling other Pokémon, which can be found in the wild or owned by other Trainers. This system of accumulating <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience points</a> (EXP) and leveling up, characteristic and integral to all <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(video_game_series)" title="Pokémon (video game series)"><i>Pokémon</i> video games</a>, controls the physical properties of the Pokémon, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pokemon_statistics" title="Pokemon statistics">battle statistics</a> acquired, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_moves" title="Pokémon moves">moves</a> learned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNBasics_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNBasics-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_features">New features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> retain the basic mechanics of capturing, battling, and evolving introduced in <i>Pokémon Red</i> and <i>Blue</i>, new features were added. A time system was introduced using a real-time internal clock that keeps track of the current time and day of the week. Certain events, including Pokémon appearances, are influenced by this feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[3]</a></sup> New items were added, with some designed to exploit a new mechanic: Pokémon being able to <a href="/wiki/Gameplay_of_Pok%C3%A9mon#Holding_items" title="Gameplay of Pokémon">hold items</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> A new type of item able to be held was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Pokemon_items" title="List of Pokemon items">berry</a>, which comes in varieties and can restore health or cure status effects. Other held items can give boosts to the Pokémon during battle. More specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9_Ball" title="Poké Ball">Poké Balls</a> were introduced, which make Pokémon catching easier in certain situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN3_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN3-8">[7]</a></sup> A new item called the Pokégear <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ポケギア</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Pokegia</i>)</span> was introduced, functioning as a watch, map, radio, and phone, allowing the player to call other characters who offer their phone number. Trainers will call for a rematch and others will call about rare Pokémon that can be caught in a certain area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign1-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The games introduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raikou_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Raikou (Pokémon)">Raikou</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entei_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Entei (Pokémon)">Entei</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicune" title="Suicune">Suicune</a>, some new types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Legendary Pokémon">legendary Pokémon</a> that wander around Johto, changing locations frequently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> They can be tracked by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9dex" title="Pokédex">Pokédex</a> once encountered, and will always attempt to flee, but will retain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pokemon_statistics" title="Pokemon statistics">HP</a> loss. In addition there is the possibility of encountering a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiny_Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Shiny Pokémon">shiny Pokémon</a>, which have a different coloration than normal Pokémon of their species, and appear very rarely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Two new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_types" title="Pokémon types">Pokémon types</a> were added, the Steel-type and the Dark-type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot1-12">[11]</a></sup> Steel-type Pokémon have very high defense and resistance to other types, while Dark-type Pokémon are immune to Psychic-type moves and are strong against Psychic-type Pokémon, as well as having few weaknesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNBasics_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGNBasics-6">[5]</a></sup> In <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>, new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_moves" title="Pokémon moves">moves</a> were added, but Pokémon knowing these moves are not allowed to be traded to the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue">first generation games</a>. To solve this, a move deleter was introduced, capable of erasing moves known by the Pokémon. Another major change was the splitting of the <i>Special</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pokemon_statistics" title="Pokemon statistics">stat</a> into <i>Special Attack</i> and <i>Special Defense</i>, which increased aspects of strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNBasics_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-IGNBasics-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_breeding" title="Pokémon breeding">Pokémon breeding</a>, Pokémon are assigned to one or two breeding groups. When a male and female Pokémon that share at least one breeding group are left at a Pokémon Daycare, they may produce an egg, which will hatch into a young Pokémon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot2-13">[12]</a></sup> The young Pokémon will inherit the species of its mother, and moves from its father. However, legendary Pokémon, among certain other species, cannot breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> are set in the region of <b>Johto</b>, situated to the west of the Kanto region from the previous <i>Red</i> and <i>Blue</i> games, and three years after the conclusion of the previous games. The design of Johto was inspired by Japan's <a href="/wiki/Kansai_region" title="Kansai region">Kansai</a> and <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkai_region" title="Tōkai region">Tōkai regions</a>, with many of the region's temples and more traditional Japanese aesthetics finding their way into Johto.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Story">Story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with the previous games, the player character (only a boy in <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>, the option to play as a girl player character was added in <i>Crystal</i>) receives their first Pokémon, a choice between <a href="/wiki/Chikorita" title="Chikorita">Chikorita</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyndaquil" title="Cyndaquil">Cyndaquil</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totodile" title="Totodile">Totodile</a>, from the region's local Pokémon scientist Professor Elm and then begins his journey to win the eight Gym Badges of the Johto region and then challenge the Elite Four and Champion to become the region's new Pokémon Master.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot1-12">[11]</a></sup> Opposing them is their mysterious rival, a boy who stole one of the other Pokémon from Professor Elm and regularly challenges the player to test his strengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ign1-9">[8]</a></sup> The player also encounters the villainous <a href="/wiki/Team_Rocket" title="Team Rocket">Team Rocket</a>, having reunited to seek out their previous leader Giovanni to return the group to their former glory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN3_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN3-8">[7]</a></sup> After the player defeats Team Rocket once and for all and defeats the Elite Four and Champion of the Pokémon League on Indigo Plateau, the player can then travel to the Kanto region from the previous games and challenge the Gym Leaders there, discovering how much has changed in the three years following the events of <i>Red</i> and <i>Blue</i>. After defeating the Kanto region's Gym Leaders, the player is allowed to enter the treacherous Mt. Silver area, home to very powerful Pokémon, and deep within Mt. Silver's caves is hidden Red, the protagonist of <i>Red</i> and <i>Blue</i>, who the player can challenge for the most difficult battle in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were first publicly showcased at the 1997 Nintendo SpaceWorld Expo in Japan, becoming the most popular exhibit at the program. Unlike the previous game in the series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_High_Voltage" title="Pokémon High Voltage">Pokémon Yellow</a></i>, the new titles were announced to be more than a small upgrade to <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue"><i>Pokémon Red</i> and <i>Blue</i></a>. Instead, they would feature a new storyline, a new world, and new species of Pokémon. <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were designed for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a>, allowing them full color support and more detailed <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(computer_graphics)" title="Sprite (computer graphics)">sprites</a>. Other additions that were shown included Pokémon breeding, <a href="/wiki/Gameplay_of_Pok%C3%A9mon#Holding_items" title="Gameplay of Pokémon">held items</a>, an in-game gadget known as the PokéGear, a real-time internal clock, and <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward compatibility</a> with the previous games in the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During an <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a></i> interview, president of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatures_Inc." title="Creatures Inc.">Creatures Inc.</a> Tsunekazu Ishihara gave insight into the brainstorming process for developing new Pokémon species. He explained, "The ideas for each of these monsters came from the imagination of the software developers at Game Freak who get these ideas from their childhood experiences, including from reading Manga, the name for Japanese comic books. Ideas come from scary experiences they had as kids, catching insects, and so forth. So from these experiences in childhood, these ideas for Pokemon came out".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> In the same vein as the Pokémon <a href="/wiki/Mew_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Mew (Pokémon)">Mew</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue"><i>Red</i> and <i>Blue</i></a> versions, the exclusive Pokémon <a href="/wiki/Celebi_(Pok%C3%A9mon)" title="Celebi (Pokémon)">Celebi</a> was implemented in the <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> games but is only accessible after attending a Nintendo promotional event. The first official event offering Celebi was <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Space_World" title="Nintendo Space World">Nintendo Space World</a> 2000 in Japan, in which 100,000 attendees would be awarded the rare Pokémon. In order to be selected, players had to send in a postcard to enter a lottery for one of 100,000 certificates of Celebi, allowing them to enter the event and obtain it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with Nintendo president <a href="/wiki/Satoru_Iwata" title="Satoru Iwata">Satoru Iwata</a>, Ishihara stated that <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> started development right after <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue#Development" title="Pokémon Red and Blue"><i>Pokémon Red</i> and <i>Green</i></a></i> were released in Japan. The original intention was to release the game in 1998, even synchronizing with the supposed end of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(anime)" title="Pokémon (anime)">anime's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon:_Indigo_League" title="Pokémon: Indigo League">first season</a>. Development issues, worsened by Game Freak being sidetracked with <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Stadium" title="Pokémon Stadium">Pokémon Stadium</a></i> and the localization of the first generation, led the game to be postponed, and the original release slate was taken over by <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Yellow" title="Pokémon Yellow">Pokémon Yellow</a></i>. Programmer Shigeki Morimoto stated that part of why development took three and a half years was due to being a small team of only four programmers, "We were very greedy in terms of all the features we wanted to include in the games". Ishihara declared that <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were supposed to be the last in the series – "I didn’t intend to make any more Pokémon titles. I even thought that once we entered the twenty-first century, it would be time for me to do something else entirely" – and most of the licensed merchandising, including the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Trading_Card_Game" title="Pokémon Trading Card Game">Pokémon Trading Card Game</a>, were to "ensure <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were successful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio">Audio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Audio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Junichi_Masuda" title="Junichi Masuda">Junichi Masuda</a> composed his music on an <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> computer, presumably in a <a href="/wiki/Music_tracker" title="Music tracker">music tracker</a> format, converted to MIDI data and converted again to the Game Boy Color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The games were announced for release in Japan in November 1999 and at the same time a U.S. release date was estimated for September 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Nintendo announced the release of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Pikachu" title="Pokémon Pikachu">Pocket Pikachu Color</a>, a full-color portable <a href="/wiki/Digital_pet" title="Digital pet">digital pet</a> similar to the one released the year before. The unit was made compatible with <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>, allowing the transfer of in-game currency known as "watt points". Pocket Pikachu Color was slated for release in Japan on November 21, 1999, the same day as the release of <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> In addition, an officially-licensed <a href="/wiki/Pikachu" title="Pikachu">Pikachu</a>-themed <a href="/wiki/Game_Link_Cable" title="Game Link Cable">Game Link Cable</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Kemco" title="Kemco">Kemco</a> was set for release in Japan on November 18, 1999. The product functions like a normal Game Link Cable and consists of a yellow cable with a figure of Pikachu on one end, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9_Ball" title="Poké Ball">Poké Ball</a> on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anticipating high sales, Nintendo set its first production shipment for the games in Japan at three million, predicting that eventually more than eight million copies would be sold in the country alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> However, they were soon forced to cut the first shipment number in half following an earthquake in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, which Nintendo claimed had damaged their cartridge manufacturing facilities. Regardless, speculation arose that Nintendo was instead using the event as an excuse to limit shipment and keep the demand high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a precursor to the North American release, <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were displayed for audiences to interact with at the 2000 <a href="/wiki/American_International_Toy_Fair" title="American International Toy Fair">American International Toy Fair</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> To further promote the games, Nintendo modified five <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_PT_Cruiser" title="Chrysler PT Cruiser">Chrysler PT Cruisers</a> to resemble the new Pokémon <a href="/wiki/Lugia" title="Lugia">Lugia</a> and had them driven around the U.S.A. The vehicles had fins and tails attached to them and were painted with logos and images of the Pokémon franchise. In addition, they were equipped with a television set hooked up to game consoles which allowed spectators to play <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Puzzle_League" title="Pokémon Puzzle League">Pokémon Puzzle League</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hey_You,_Pikachu!" title="Hey You, Pikachu!">Hey You, Pikachu!</a></i>, and <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> The television series <i>Pokémon GS</i>, based on the games, was announced to be a part of the fall lineup on <a href="/wiki/Kids%27_WB" title="Kids' WB">Kids' WB</a>. The show would feature the same protagonist <a href="/wiki/Ash_Ketchum" title="Ash Ketchum">Ash Ketchum</a> in a new region with different Pokémon species from the games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> The Americanized names of the 100 new Pokémon were kept confidential by Nintendo, with the company releasing names periodically. The domain names 'pokemongold.com' and 'pokemonsilver.com' were registered for this very purpose,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> and such names released included <a href="/wiki/Chikorita" title="Chikorita">Chikorita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lugia" title="Lugia">Lugia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ho-Oh" title="Ho-Oh">Ho-Oh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Togepi" title="Togepi">Togepi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoothoot" title="Hoothoot">Hoothoot</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marill" title="Marill">Marill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2000 Nintendo announced the official North American release date of <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> to be on October 16 of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Nintendo started accepting <a href="/wiki/Pre-order" title="Pre-order">pre-orders</a> for the games in August,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preorder_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-preorder-32">[31]</a></sup> and announced that consumers who pre-ordered one of the games would receive a free <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> with a Pokémon-themed <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/MediaBrowser" title="MediaBrowser">MediaBrowser</a> which featured floating Pokémon species and <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">links</a> to Pokémon sites. The application was available for download on the official Pokémon website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> The games broke record sales as approximately 600,000 copies of them were pre-ordered in just two months, compared to <i>Pokémon Yellow'</i>s number of 150,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preorder_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-preorder-32">[31]</a></sup> As the release date neared, retailers such as <a href="/wiki/EB_Games" title="EB Games">Electronics Boutique</a> reported receiving shipments of games earlier than October 16, and opted to sell them immediately; first giving them to pre-orderers and then selling the leftover copies. The games were obtainable as early as October 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> continued the enormous success of <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Red_and_Blue" title="Pokémon Red and Blue">its predecessors</a>, beginning the formation of Pokémon into a multi-billion dollar franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> As of April 2000 roughly 6.5 million copies of the games had been sold in Japan. <i>Silver</i> proved to be the slightly more popular version, edging out <i>Gold</i> by approximately 100,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> By the first week of their release in the U.S.A. the games had eclipsed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_High_Voltage" title="Pokémon High Voltage"><i>Pokémon Yellow</i></a>'s previous record sales of a little over 600,000 copies; selling a combined total of 1.4 million copies to become the fastest selling games ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bNET_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bNET-40">[39]</a></sup> The commercial success was expected, as Peter Main, the executive vice president of sales and marketing, stated "There's no question about it; kids love to play Pokemon. [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] So far in 2000 the best-selling game in America for any home console is Pokemon Stadium for Nintendo 64, and the best-selling game for any handheld video game system is Pokemon  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Yellow for Game Boy Color, but Pokemon  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Gold and Silver will eclipse even those impressive sales totals. We project sales of 10 million units total of these two games in less than six months time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bNET_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-bNET-40">[39]</a></sup> By 2010, the recorded sales of <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were at 23 million units sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reviews from critics were mostly strong, with many saying that the extended length of gameplay and the new features were valued additions that kept the sequels as interesting as the original games. Craig Harris of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave the games a "masterful" 10 out of 10 rating, stating that: "As awesome as the original Pokémon edition was, <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> blow it away in gameplay elements, features, and goodies. There are so many little additions to the design it's impossible to list them all".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[3]</a></sup> There was particular praise given to the innovative internal clock feature, with Frank Povo of <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a>, noting: "The first major addition to Pokémon GS is the presence of a time element... Although it may sound like a gimmick, the addition of a clock adds quite a bit of variety to the game". Povo went on to give the games a "great" 8.8 rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot2-13">[12]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Power" title="Nintendo Power">Nintendo Power</a></i> listed the <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> versions of it combined as the sixth best <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a>/<a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> video game, praising it for its new Pokémon, features, and full-color graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overall, <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> were stated to be solid gaming additions that would please a large audience. "After playing the game dozens of hours, I really can't think of a bad point to make about <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>. Nintendo and Game Freak have tweaked the original and built a sequel that's long, challenging and tremendous fun to play. There's a reason why Pokémon is so popular, and <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i> is going to help the series move further into the 21st century", said Harris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequels_and_remakes">Sequels and remakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sequels and remakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pok.C3.A9mon_Crystal"><i>Pokémon Crystal</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pokémon Crystal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><b>Pokémon Crystal Version</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ポケットモンスター クリスタルバージョン</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Poketto Monsutā Kurisutaru Bājon</i>, "Pocket Monsters: Crystal Version")</span>, for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a>, is the seventh game in the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(video_game_series)" title="Pokémon (video game series)"><i>Pokémon</i> video game series</a> in Japan, and the sixth in North America and Europe. The game is an updated version of the previous two versions, <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>, and was released in Japan on December 14, 2000. It was released in North America on July 29, 2001 and in Europe on November 2, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNCrystal_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNCrystal-44">[43]</a></sup> Unlike its predecessors, it was developed specifically for the Game Boy Color, and is thus not compatible with the original Game Boy, its variants, and the <a href="/wiki/Super_Game_Boy" title="Super Game Boy">Super Game Boy</a>.</p>
<p>The plot and gameplay of <i>Pokémon Crystal</i> is largely the same as in <i>Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>, although it holds new features. It is the first game to allow players to choose the sex of their character, while previously the character was always male. Pokémon have animated sprites; for example, when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyndaquil" title="Cyndaquil">Cyndaquil</a> enters battle, the flames on its back flicker. This feature was absent in <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Ruby_and_Sapphire" title="Pokémon Ruby and Sapphire"><i>Pokémon Ruby</i> and <i>Sapphire</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_FireRed_and_LeafGreen" title="Pokémon FireRed and LeafGreen"><i>Pokémon FireRed</i> and <i>LeafGreen</i></a>, but has appeared in all subsequent games. In addition, a couple of subplots were added, one involving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Legendary Pokémon">legendary Pokémon</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicune" title="Suicune">Suicune</a>, featured on the front cover of the game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_Crystal2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot_Crystal2-45">[44]</a></sup> and the other involving the <a href="/wiki/Unown" title="Unown">Unown</a>. The game's biggest addition was the Battle Tower, a new building which allows players to participate in <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Stadium" title="Pokémon Stadium">Pokémon Stadium</a></i>-like fights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNCrystal_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGNCrystal-44">[43]</a></sup> The Japanese edition of the game was exclusively bundled with the Mobile Adapter GB <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">モバイルアダプタGB</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Mobairu Adaputa Jī Bī</i>)</span>, a device that allowed for connecting with other players via <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Pokémon Crystal</i> was received well by critics, although many commented that there were just not enough new additions and features to significantly set it apart from <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>. Craig Harris of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave the game an "outstanding" 9 out of 10 stating, "The final (hopefully) Game Boy Color edition is definitely the version to get if you aren't already one of the upteenth [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] billion owners of the previous games, with <i>Crystal'</i>s slight updates to the design and graphics. But there's not much in this edition that makes it a "must buy" for folks who already own a copy or two of the previous editions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNCrystal_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-IGNCrystal-44">[43]</a></sup> In Japan, <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> magazine scored the game a 34 out of 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pok.C3.A9mon_Prism"><i>Pokémon Prism</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pokémon Prism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><b>Pokémon Prism</b></i> is an unauthorized fan-made modification of <i>Pokémon Crystal</i> that adds features such as character customization, playing as a Pokémon, new monsters and a new region, among other changes. Intended for a release by Christmas 2016, the modification was cancelled by its pseudonymous creator "Koolboyman" in December 2016 following a <a href="/wiki/Cease_and_desist" title="Cease and desist">cease and desist</a> letter by Nintendo, but a pre-release version of the ROM hack continued to be distributed in Internet forums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pok.C3.A9mon_HeartGold_and_SoulSilver"><i>Pokémon HeartGold</i> and <i>SoulSilver</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pok%C3%A9mon_Gold_and_Silver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pokémon HeartGold and SoulSilver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_HeartGold_and_SoulSilver" title="Pokémon HeartGold and SoulSilver">Pokémon HeartGold and SoulSilver</a></div>
<p><i><b>Pokémon HeartGold Version</b></i> and <i><b>SoulSilver Version</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ポケットモンスター ハートゴールド・ソウルシルバー</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Poketto Monsutā Hātogōrudo Sōrushirubā</i>, "Pocket Monsters: HeartGold &amp; SoulSilver)</span> are <a href="/wiki/Video_game_remake" title="Video game remake">enhanced remakes</a> of <i>Pokémon Gold</i> and <i>Silver</i>. The games are part of the <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_(video_game_series)" title="Pokémon (video game series)"><i>Pokémon</i> series</a> of <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a>, and were developed by <a href="/wiki/Game_Freak" title="Game Freak">Game Freak</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>. First released in Japan on September 12, 2009, the games were later released to North America, Australia, and Europe during March 2010.</p>
<p>Game director Shigeki Morimoto aimed to respect the feelings of those who played the previous games, while also ensuring that it felt like a new game to those that were introduced to the series in more recent years. Reception to the games was highly positive, the two being amongst the highest rated DS games of all time on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>. Commercially, the two are among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_Nintendo_DS_video_games" title="List of best-selling Nintendo DS video games">best-selling Nintendo DS games</a> of all time, with combined sales of 10 million units as of July 29, 2010.</p>
