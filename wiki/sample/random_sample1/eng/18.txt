<p><b>.yu</b> was the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_code" title="Country code">country code</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a>) that was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> and was mainly used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Federal Republic of Yugoslavia</a> and its successor <a href="/wiki/Serbia_and_Montenegro" title="Serbia and Montenegro">Serbia and Montenegro</a> between 1994 and 2010. After <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a> acquired separate <a href="/wiki/.rs" title=".rs">.rs</a> and <a href="/wiki/.me" title=".me">.me</a> domains in 2007, a transition period started, and the .yu domain finally expired on 30 March 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rnids-home_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-rnids-home-1">[1]</a></sup> It was the most heavily used top-level domain ever to be deleted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.yu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .yu ccTLD was assigned originally to the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia</a>, during the government project for the development of scientific-technological information (SNTIJ). The official registrants were the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maribor" title="University of Maribor">University of Maribor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jo%C5%BEef_Stefan_Institute" title="Jožef Stefan Institute">Jožef Stefan Institute</a>, which were located in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>.</p>
<p>When SFR Yugoslavia dissolved, Slovenia and Croatia registered their own ccTLDs (<a href="/wiki/.si" title=".si">.si</a> and <a href="/wiki/.hr" title=".hr">.hr</a>). Serbia and Montenegro formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Federal Republic of Yugoslavia</a>, but was under international sanctions at the time because of ongoing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_wars" title="Yugoslav wars">Yugoslav wars</a>. The old .yu domain registry had been left in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and the domain became a <a href="/wiki/Succession_of_states" title="Succession of states">succession matter</a> when the Slovenians refused to relinquish the domain name to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Belgrade" title="University of Belgrade">University of Belgrade</a> in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, which had requested they do so.</p>
<p>In 1994, the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">Internet Assigned Numbers Authority</a> finally decreed that the domain should pass to FR Yugoslavia. After that, the domain was managed by the YUNET Association, an organization based in the Faculty of Electrical Engineering at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Belgrade" title="University of Belgrade">University of Belgrade</a>.</p>
<p>FR Yugoslavia renamed itself <a href="/wiki/Serbia_and_Montenegro" title="Serbia and Montenegro">Serbia and Montenegro</a> in February 2003. The code YU was replaced by CS in July 2003 following the official name change, and the ccTLD <a href="/wiki/.cs" title=".cs">.cs</a> was reserved for Serbia and Montenegro after the name change. However, .cs was never actually used, and .yu remained one of the few ccTLDs that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD#ccTLDs_not_in_ISO_3166-1" title="CcTLD">did not correspond</a> to a current <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166-1" title="ISO 3166-1">ISO 3166-1</a> two-letter code.</p>
<p>The state union of Serbia and Montenegro was dissolved in July 2006, and in September 2006, ISO accordingly proposed the replacement the codes RS for Serbia and ME for Montenegro. On 26 September 2006 the ISO 3166 Maintenance Agency agreed on the change of ISO 3166-1 Alpha-2 code CS to RS. The new domains <a href="/wiki/.rs" title=".rs">.rs</a> for Serbia and <a href="/wiki/.me" title=".me">.me</a> for Montenegro became active shortly thereafter.</p>
<p>In September 2007 <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> resolved<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> that the .yu domain would be operated temporarily under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_National_Register_of_Internet_Domain_Names" title="Serbian National Register of Internet Domain Names">Serbian National Register of Internet Domain Names</a>, operators of the new .rs domain registry. A two-year transition period started, and the .yu domain was scheduled to expire on 30 September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the Serbian registrar requested an extension<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and ICANN decided to extend the transition deadline another six months away. Finally, the Serbian registrar declared the end of the .yu domain at 12:00 CEST on 30 March 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rnids-home_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-rnids-home-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_use_of_.yu_domains">Former use of .yu domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.yu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Former use of .yu domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All of the domains directly under .yu were reserved for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juristic_person" title="Juristic person">legal entities</a> only. Top level domain was reserved for federal institutions and official governmental institutions, as well as <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service providers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a> was also allowed to use .yu domain.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Second-level_domain" title="Second-level domain">second-level domains</a> under .yu included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Academic organizations, such as universities, were registered under the academic domain, <b>.ac.yu</b>. For example, the School of Electrical Engineering (.etf) at the University of Belgrade (.bg) had the etf.bg.ac.yu domain.</li>
<li>Educational institutions, such as primary and high schools, used the <b>.edu.yu</b> domain.</li>
<li>Independent organizations used the <b>.org.yu</b> domain.</li>
<li>Corporations used the <b>.co.yu</b> domain.</li>
<li>Government used the <b>.gov.yu</b> domain.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegrin</a> websites often used subdomain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/.cg.yu" title=".cg.yu">.cg.yu</a> which was given for free to customers of a Montenegrin ISP which registered the domain, making it a popular option for those who opt not to register a domain for their website.</p>
