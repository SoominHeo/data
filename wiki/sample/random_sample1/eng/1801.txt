<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Kawasaki's theorem</h1>

<p><b>Kawasaki's theorem</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Theorem" title="Theorem">theorem</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_of_paper_folding" title="Mathematics of paper folding">mathematics of paper folding</a>, named after <a href="/wiki/Toshikazu_Kawasaki" title="Toshikazu Kawasaki">Toshikazu Kawasaki</a>, that gives a criterion for determining whether a <a href="/wiki/Crease_pattern" title="Crease pattern">crease pattern</a> with a single <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertex</a> may be folded to form a flat figure.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statement_of_the_theorem">Statement of the theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kawasaki%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statement of the theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maekawa%27s_theorem" title="Maekawa's theorem">Maekawa's theorem</a> states that the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_fold" title="Mountain fold">mountain folds</a> in a flat-folded vertex figure differs from the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valley_fold" title="Valley fold">valley folds</a> by exactly two folds. From this it follows that the total number of folds must be even.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hull-1">[1]</a></sup> Therefore, suppose that a crease pattern consists of an even number <span class="texhtml">2<i>n</i></span> of creases radiating from a single vertex <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">v</span>, without specification of which creases should be mountain folds and which should be valley folds. In this crease pattern, let <span class="texhtml">α<sub>1</sub>, α<sub>2</sub>, ⋯, α<sub>2<i>n</i></sub></span> be the consecutive angles between the creases around <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">v</span>, in clockwise order, starting at any one of the angles. Then Kawasaki's theorem is the statement that the crease pattern may be folded flat if and only if the <a href="/wiki/Alternating_series" title="Alternating series">alternating sum and difference</a> of the angles adds to zero:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">α<sub>1</sub> − α<sub>2</sub> + α<sub>3</sub> − ⋯ + α<sub>2<i>n</i> − 1</sub> − α<sub>2<i>n</i></sub> = 0</span></dd>
</dl>
<p>An equivalent way of stating the same condition is that, if the angles are partitioned into two alternating subsets, then the sum of the angles in either of the two subsets is exactly 180 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-charm-2">[2]</a></sup> However, this equivalent form applies only to a crease pattern on a flat piece of paper, whereas the alternating sum form of the condition remains valid for crease patterns on conical sheets of paper with nonzero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defect_(geometry)" title="Defect (geometry)">defect</a> at the vertex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_and_global_flat-foldability">Local and global flat-foldability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kawasaki%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local and global flat-foldability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kawasaki's theorem, applied to each of the vertices of an arbitrary crease pattern, determines whether the crease pattern is locally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat-foldability" title="Flat-foldability">flat-foldable</a>, meaning that the part of the crease pattern near the vertex can be flat-folded. However, there exist crease patterns that are locally flat-foldable but that have no global flat folding that works for the whole crease pattern at once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-hull-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hull" title="Tom Hull">Tom Hull</a> (<a href="#CITEREFHull1994">1994</a>) conjectured that global flat-foldability could be tested by checking Kawasaki's theorem at each vertex of a crease pattern, and then also testing <a href="/wiki/Bipartite_graph" title="Bipartite graph">bipartiteness</a> of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undirected_graph" title="Undirected graph">undirected graph</a> associated with the crease pattern,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but this conjecture was disproven by <a href="#CITEREFBernHayes1996">Bern &amp; Hayes (1996)</a>, who showed that the problem of testing global flat-foldability is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NP-complete" title="NP-complete">NP-complete</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bh96_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bh96-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof">Proof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kawasaki%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To show that Kawasaki's condition necessarily holds for any flat-folded figure, it suffices to observe that, at each fold, the orientation of the paper is reversed. Thus, if the first crease in the flat-folded figure is placed in the plane parallel to the <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span>-axis, the next crease must be rotated from it by an angle of <span class="texhtml">α<sub>1</sub></span>, the crease after that by an angle of <span class="texhtml">α<sub>1</sub> − α<sub>2</sub></span> (because the second angle has the reverse orientation from the first), etc. In order for the paper to meet back up with itself at the final angle, Kawasaki's condition must be met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-hull-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-charm-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roses_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-roses-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6.849_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-6.849-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Showing that the condition is also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufficient_condition" title="Sufficient condition">sufficient condition</a> is a matter of describing how to fold a given crease pattern (that is, how to choose whether to make mountain or valley folds, and in what order the flaps of paper should be arranged on top of each other) so that it folds flat. One way to do this is to choose a number <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span> such that the partial alternating sum</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">α<sub>1</sub> − α<sub>2</sub> + α<sub>3</sub> − ⋯ + α<sub>2<i>i</i> − 1</sub> − α<sub>2<i>i</i></sub></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>is as small as possible: either <span class="texhtml"><i>i</i> = 0</span> and the partial sum is an <a href="/wiki/Empty_sum" title="Empty sum">empty sum</a> that is also zero, or for some nonzero choice of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span> the partial sum is negative. Then, accordion fold the pattern, starting with angle <span class="texhtml">α<sub>2<i>i</i> + 1</sub></span> and alternating between mountain and valley folds, placing each angular wedge of the paper below the previous folds. At each step until the final fold, an accordion fold of this type will never self-intersect, and the choice of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span> ensures that the first wedge sticks out to the left of all the other folded pieces of paper, allowing the final wedge to connect back up to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6.849_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-6.849-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternative proof of sufficiency is to consider the smallest angle <span class="texhtml">α<sub><i>i</i></sub></span> and the two creases on either side of it. If one of these two creases is mountain-folded and the other valley-folded, and then the resulting flap of paper is glued down onto the remaining part of the crease pattern, the result will be a crease pattern with two fewer creases, on a conical sheet of paper, that still satisfies Kawasaki's condition. Therefore, by <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_induction" title="Mathematical induction">mathematical induction</a>, repeating this process will eventually lead to a flat folding. The base case of the induction is a cone with only two creases and two equal-angle wedges, which can obviously be flat-folded by using a mountain fold for both creases. Using this method, it can be shown that any crease pattern that satisfies Kawasaki's condition has at least <span class="texhtml">2<sup><i>n</i></sup></span> different choices of mountain and valley folds that all lead to valid flat foldings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kawasaki%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 1970s, Yasuji Husimi and <a href="/wiki/David_A._Huffman" title="David A. Huffman">David A. Huffman</a> independently discovered the special case of Kawasaki's theorem for crease patterns with four creases; Huffman called it the "critical <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi"><span class="texhtml">π</span></a> condition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull10_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hull10-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The theorem for crease patterns with arbitrarily many creases was discovered by Kawasaki, by Stuart Robertson, and by Jacques Justin (again, independently of each other) in the late 1970s and early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bh96_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bh96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull10_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hull10-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmto_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-tmto-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because of Justin's contribution to the problem, it has also been called the Kawasaki–Justin theorem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kawasaki himself has called the result <b>Husimi's theorem</b>, after Yasuji Husimi, and some other authors have followed this terminology as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roses_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-roses-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The name "Kawasaki's theorem" was first given to this result in <i>Origami for the Connoisseur</i> by <a href="/wiki/Kunihiko_Kasahara" title="Kunihiko Kasahara">Kunihiko Kasahara</a> and Toshie Takahama (Japan Publications, 1987).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hull_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-hull-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="#CITEREFHull2003">Hull (2003)</a> credits the lower bound of <span class="texhtml">2<sup><i>n</i></sup></span> on the number of different flat-foldings of a crease pattern meeting the conditions of the theorem to independent work in the early 1990s by Azuma,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Justin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmto_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-tmto-13">[13]</a></sup> and Ewins and Hull.</p>
