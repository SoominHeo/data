<p><b>Tetsuharu Kawakami</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">川上 哲治</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kawakami Tetsuharu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, March 23, 1920 – October 28, 2013)</span> was a Japanese baseball player and manager, known for his red bat, and his nickname 打撃の神様 <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">dageki no kamisama,</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"the God of Batting/Hitting"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. He was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame">Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in 1965.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetsuharu_Kawakami&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Player">Player</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetsuharu_Kawakami&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Player">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Hitoyoshi,_Kumamoto" title="Hitoyoshi, Kumamoto">Hitoyoshi, Kumamoto</a>, he played for Kumamoto Tech (Kumamoto) in the 1937 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summer_K%C5%8Dshien" title="Summer Kōshien">Summer Kōshien</a>. Kumamoto advanced to the championship game, but lost. After the game, Kawakami grabbed a handful of dirt from the playing field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dshien_Stadium" title="Kōshien Stadium">Kōshien Stadium</a> and put it in his uniform pocket as a memento. This became known as the original scooping of "the dirt of Kōshien" (甲子園の土 Kōshien no tsuchi?). Since then, as a memento of their fleeting time on the hallowed grounds of Kōshien, players from the losing teams take home a pouch of the precious soil.</p>
<p>Kawakami played for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_Kyojin" title="Tokyo Kyojin">Tokyo Kyojin</a>/<a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Giants" title="Yomiuri Giants">Yomiuri Giants</a> between 1938 and 1958 (though he missed the years 1943–1945 when he served in the Japanese military). Kawakami signed with the Giants as a pitcher/first baseman, and actually pitched in 39 games between 1938 and 1941, compiling 11 wins against 9 losses, with an excellent 2.61 ERA. He converted full-time to first base in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hit2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hit2000-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1951, he struck out only 6 times, which is the Japanese single-season tie record. In 1954, Kawakami hit the first cycle in Yomiuri Giants' franchise history.</p>
<p>He was a professional player for 18 years, winning the batting title five times, two home run crowns, three RBI titles, and had six titles for the most hits in a season. He was the MVP of the 1953 <a href="/wiki/Japan_Series" title="Japan Series">Japan Series</a>. He was the first player in Japanese pro baseball to achieve 2,000 hits and was named the league's MVP three times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manager">Manager</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetsuharu_Kawakami&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manager">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As manager of the Giants from 1961–1974, he was known for his ruthless, tough-love style, but he led the Yomiuri Giants to nine consecutive championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmography">Filmography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetsuharu_Kawakami&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filmography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tetsuharu Kawakami appeared in three films:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>人生選手 (1949)</li>
<li>川上哲治物語　背番号１６ <i><a href="/wiki/Kawakami_Tetsuharu_monogatari_sebang%C5%8D_16" title="Kawakami Tetsuharu monogatari sebangō 16">Kawakami Tetsuharu monogatari sebangō 16</a></i> (1957) — a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-pic" title="Bio-pic">bio-pic</a> where he played himself</li>
<li>ＢＩＧ−１物語　王貞治 (1977)</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, Kawakami is referred to by name in the baseball game scene from film director <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Stray_Dog_(film)" title="Stray Dog (film)">Stray Dog</a></i> (1949); a.k.a. <i>Nora Inu</i>.</p>
