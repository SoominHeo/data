<p>The <b>Megachilidae</b> are a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_family" title="Cosmopolitan family">cosmopolitan family</a> of (mostly) solitary <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> whose pollen-carrying structure (called a <i><a href="/wiki/Scopa_(biology)" title="Scopa (biology)">scopa</a></i>) is restricted to the ventral surface of the abdomen (rather than mostly or exclusively on the hind legs as in other bee families).</p>

<p>Megachilid genera are most commonly known as <b><a href="/wiki/Mason_bee" title="Mason bee">mason bees</a></b> and <b>leafcutter bees</b>, reflecting the materials from which they build their nest cells (soil or leaves, respectively); a few collect plant or animal hairs and fibers, and are called <b>carder bees</b>. All species feed on nectar and pollen, but a few are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleptoparasite" title="Cleptoparasite">cleptoparasites</a> (informally called "<a href="/wiki/Cuckoo_bee" title="Cuckoo bee">cuckoo bees</a>"), feeding on pollen collected by other megachilid bees. Parasitic species do not possess scopae. Megachilid bees are among the world's most efficient <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a>. The motion of Megachilidae in the reproductive structures of flowers is highly energetic and swimming-like. This vigorous agitation releases large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>. While they are efficient as pollinators, they are inefficient as foragers; megachilids require an average of ten times as many trips to flowers to gather sufficient resources to provision a single brood cell as other types of bees.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle">Lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megachilidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonparasitic_species">Nonparasitic species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megachilidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nonparasitic species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LeafCutterLyd.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="790" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/LeafCutterLyd.png/220px-LeafCutterLyd.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/LeafCutterLyd.png/330px-LeafCutterLyd.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/LeafCutterLyd.png/440px-LeafCutterLyd.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LeafCutterLyd.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lifecycle</div>
</div>

<p>The lifecycle of nonparasitic Megachilidae is typically that nests are built, divided into cells. Each cell receives a supply of food (pollen or a pollen/nectar mix) and an egg; after finding a suitable spot (often near where she emerged), a female starts building a first cell, stocks it, and oviposits. She builds a wall that separates the completed cell from the next one. The larva hatches from the egg and consumes the food supply. After moulting a few times, it spins a cocoon and pupates. It emerges from the nest as an adult. Males die shortly after mating, but females survive for another few weeks, during which they build new nests.</p>
<p>Nests are often built in natural or artificial cavities. Some embed individual cells in a mass of clay or resin attached to a wall, rock surface, or plant stem. Nest cavities are often linear, for example in hollow plant stems, but not always (snail shells are used by some <i>Osmia</i>, and some species readily use irregular cavities).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasitic_species">Parasitic species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megachilidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parasitic species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some genera of megachilids are <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasites</a>, so have no ventral scopa (e.g. <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stelis_(insect)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stelis (insect) (page does not exist)">Stelis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Coelioxys" title="Coelioxys">Coelioxys</a></i>). They often parasitize related taxa. They typically enter the nest before it is sealed and lay their eggs in a cell. After hatching, the parasite larva kills the host larva, unless the female parasite has already done so, and then consumes the provisions. Parasitic species are of equal size or smaller than their victims. In 1921, the journal <i><a href="/wiki/American_Museum_Novitates" title="American Museum Novitates">American Museum Novitates</a></i> published a preliminary report on parasitic megachilid bees of the western United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMNH_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMNH-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity">Diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megachilidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>North America has many native megachilid species, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfalfa_leafcutter_bee" title="Alfalfa leafcutter bee">alfalfa leafcutter bees</a> (<i>Megachile rotundata</i>) are an imported <a href="/wiki/Pollination_management" title="Pollination management">species used for pollination</a>. The most significant native species is <i><a href="/wiki/Osmia_lignaria" title="Osmia lignaria">Osmia lignaria</a></i> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchard_mason_bee" title="Orchard mason bee">orchard mason bee</a> or blue orchard bee), which is sold commercially for use in orchard crop pollination, and which can be attracted to nest in wooden blocks with holes drilled in them (which are also sold commercially for this purpose). <i><a href="/wiki/Anthidium_manicatum" title="Anthidium manicatum">Anthidium manicatum</a></i>, also known as the European wool carder bee, is another species within the Megachilidae family. Rather than cutting leaves or petals, however, <i>A. manicatum</i> scrapes the hairs from leaves to use for nesting material, a behavior that is atypical in Megachilidae bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The fossil record for megachilid bees is poor, but a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Eocene" title="Middle Eocene">Middle Eocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledonous</a> leaf shows definite semicircular cutouts along its margin, implying that leaf-cutting bees existed at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Multiply-cut leaves and rare body fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> of Germany and the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> of France suggest that Megachilinae began cutting leaves early in their evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Phylogenetic analysis yields an age consistent with this Eocene origin for the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
