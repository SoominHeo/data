<p><a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(Canada)" title="Lieutenant-general (Canada)">Lieutenant-General</a> <b>Guy Granville Simonds</b> <span style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">CC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Bath" title="Order of the Bath">CB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Service_Order" title="Distinguished Service Order">DSO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces_Decoration" title="Canadian Forces Decoration">CD</a></span> (April 23, 1903 – May 15, 1974) was a senior <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">officer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a> who commanded the <a href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Division" title="1st Canadian Division">1st Canadian Infantry Division</a> and <a href="/wiki/II_Canadian_Corps" title="II Canadian Corps">II Canadian Corps</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. He served as <a href="/wiki/Acting_(rank)" title="Acting (rank)">acting commander</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Army" title="First Canadian Army">First Canadian Army</a>, leading the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> to victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Scheldt" title="Battle of the Scheldt">Battle of the Scheldt</a> in late 1944. In 1951 he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Canadian_Army" title="Commander of the Canadian Army">Chief of the General Staff</a>, the most senior member of the Canadian Army.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_Background">Family Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guy was born in <a href="/wiki/Ixworth" title="Ixworth">Ixworth</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Bury_St_Edmunds" title="Bury St Edmunds">Bury St Edmunds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> on April 24, 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corps_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-corps-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Simonds came from a military family: his great-grandfather had been in the army of the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">Honourable East India Company</a>, his grandfather had been a <a href="/wiki/Major-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Major-general (United Kingdom)">major-general</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">British Indian Army</a> and his father an <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">officer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Artillery" title="Royal Artillery">Royal Regiment of Artillery</a>. The Simonds family was related to <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Maxse" title="Ivor Maxse">Ivor Maxse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Milner,_1st_Viscount_Milner" title="Alfred Milner, 1st Viscount Milner">Lord Milner</a>. On his maternal side, his grandfather William Easton was a wealthy Virginian horse breeder, who had moved to England, renting <a href="/wiki/Ixworth" title="Ixworth">Ixworth Abbey</a>. Eleanor "Nellie" Easton, his mother, was one of five daughters, four of whom married army officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham9-15_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham9-15-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>His father Cecil, a <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_Kingdom)" title="Major (United Kingdom)">major</a>, resigned from the British Army in fall 1911 (when Guy was 9) and moved his family to <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, working as a surveyor for a railway. Cecil's expectations of having his own survey company were frustrated by the requirement to pass local professional examinations. Re-joining the army at the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Cecil was wounded in 1918, and demobilized in 1919 with the rank of <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Colonel (United Kingdom)">colonel</a>. The family spent the war in a rented house in <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">Victoria</a>. Guy's mother sold family possessions to make ends meet. Guy had to quit school for two years at age fourteen to help support the family. Graham speculates that the period of fatherlessness made him a "loner" and self-reliant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham15_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham15-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Simonds had three siblings, Cicely, Peter and Eric. Eric (anecdotally an excellent rifle shot, having won prizes at <a href="/wiki/Bisley,_Surrey" title="Bisley, Surrey">Bisley</a>) became a <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>, but died in an air accident off <a href="/wiki/Felixstowe" title="Felixstowe">Felixstowe</a> in July 1937 in a <a href="/wiki/Miles_Magister" title="Miles Magister">Miles Magister</a> while serving with the <a href="/wiki/Aeroplane_and_Armament_Experimental_Establishment" title="Aeroplane and Armament Experimental Establishment">A&amp;AEE</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in England. Cicely worked as a secretary in the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> during the war. She and her daughter were killed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-1_(flying_bomb)" title="V-1 (flying bomb)">V-1 (flying bomb)</a> attack in June 1944, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham4244_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham4244-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guy attended Collegiate School in Victoria and then <a href="/wiki/Ashbury_College" title="Ashbury College">Ashbury College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> beginning in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham15_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graham15-3">[3]</a></sup> The College's dining hall is named after him.</p>
<p>He studied at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_of_Canada" title="Royal Military College of Canada">Royal Military College of Canada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">Kingston, Ontario</a> between 1921 and 1925,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> cadet number 1596. Simonds' class was the last to be selected from nationwide exams (Simonds having placed second) and the first after the war to enter a four-year course.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham17_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham17-7">[7]</a></sup> At graduation he was awarded the Sword of Honour, judged the best "all rounder", placed second academically, and was generally considered the best horseman in the class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham24_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham24-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He joined the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">commissioned</a> in 1926 as a <a href="/wiki/Second_lieutenant" title="Second lieutenant">second lieutenant</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Horse_Artillery" title="Royal Canadian Horse Artillery">Royal Canadian Horse Artillery</a>, serving first with B Battery in Kingston, then C Battery in Winnipeg. In September 1932 (just weeks after his wedding) with the rank of <a href="/wiki/Brevet_(military)" title="Brevet (military)">brevet</a> <a href="/wiki/Captain_(Canadian_army_and_air_force)" title="Captain (Canadian army and air force)">captain</a>, he attended the Long Gunnery Staff Course in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> He was accompanied to England by his wife, and his first child was born there. He returned to Kingston in 1934. In 1936 and 1937 he attended the <a href="/wiki/Staff_College,_Camberley" title="Staff College, Camberley">Staff College, Camberley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> Promoted to <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">major</a>, he returned to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_of_Canada" title="Royal Military College of Canada">Royal Military College of Canada</a> as Associate Professor of Artillery and later as Instructor in Tactics.</p>
<p>During the pre-war years, Simonds and <a href="/wiki/E._L._M._Burns" title="E. L. M. Burns">Eedson Louis Millard Burns</a> debated concepts in the pages of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army_Journal" title="Canadian Army Journal">Canadian Defence Quarterly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_220_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_220-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_career">Wartime career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wartime career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1548" data-file-width="1000" height="341" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg/220px-Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg/330px-Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg/440px-Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guy_Simonds_e010778918-v8.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Simonds in Italy, 1943.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1939 he became a <a href="/wiki/Staff_(military)#Division_level" title="Staff (military)">GSO II (operations)</a> with the <a href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Division" title="1st Canadian Division">1st Canadian Infantry Division</a> and was posted to the United Kingdom in December, three months after the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In July 1940, he went on to be <a href="/wiki/Commanding_officer" title="Commanding officer">commanding officer</a> of the 1st Field Regiment, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Regiment_of_Canadian_Artillery" title="Royal Regiment of Canadian Artillery">Royal Canadian Artillery</a>, his first command since leaving C Battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham47.2C48_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham47.2C48-10">[10]</a></sup> In November 1940 he was appointed commandant of the Canadian Junior War Staff Course (an intensive 14 week program that compressed one year of the Camberly course), intended to fill the shortage of trained staff officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham55-57_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham55-57-11">[11]</a></sup> He then became GSO I with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Canadian_Infantry_Division" title="2nd Canadian Infantry Division">2nd Canadian Infantry Division</a> under <a href="/wiki/Victor_Odlum" title="Victor Odlum">Victor Odlum</a>.</p>
<p>Shortly thereafter, in August 1941 he was made <a href="/wiki/Acting_(rank)" title="Acting (rank)">acting</a> <a href="/wiki/Staff_(military)#Division_level" title="Staff (military)">Brigadier General Staff</a> of <a href="/wiki/I_Canadian_Corps" title="I Canadian Corps">I Canadian Corps</a> under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_McNaughton" title="Andrew McNaughton">Andrew McNaughton</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Pearkes" title="George Pearkes">George Pearkes</a>. Later he was confirmed as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brigadier_(Canada)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brigadier (Canada) (page does not exist)">brigadier</a> and stayed in the BGS role under <a href="/wiki/Harry_Crerar" title="Harry Crerar">Harry Crerar</a> until mid-July 1942. Crerar, however, had opposed Simonds' appointment and considered his removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_155_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_155-12">[12]</a></sup> During his time as BGS, numerous exercises, including Bumper in September 1941 and <a href="/wiki/Exercise_Tiger_(1942)" title="Exercise Tiger (1942)">Tiger</a> in May 1942 were conducted, with Simonds catching British <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-general (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery" title="Bernard Montgomery">Bernard Montgomery</a>'s eye on both occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham66_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham66-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July and August 1942 Simonds was involved in planning for an abortive <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Churchill</a>-inspired attack on Norway, codenamed "Jupiter", thereby avoiding the <a href="/wiki/Dieppe_Raid" title="Dieppe Raid">Dieppe Raid</a> debacle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_155_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_155-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 1942, he was made commander of the 1st Infantry Brigade in 1st Canadian Infantry Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_155_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_155-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1943 Simonds became <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_staff" title="Chief of staff">chief of staff</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Army" title="First Canadian Army">First Canadian Army</a>, again serving under McNaughton. The Army performed poorly in Exercise Spartan (March 1943). Simonds suggested that McNaughton separate his "political" functions (CMHQ) from "fighting" headquarters (First Canadian Army). McNaughton grew angry, and within 48 hours Simonds was on attachment to the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Eighth Army (United Kingdom)">British Eighth Army</a>, under Montgomery, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunisia_Campaign" title="Tunisia Campaign">fighting in Tunisia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham59_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham59-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham67_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham67-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisional_Command_and_Italy">Divisional Command and Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Divisional Command and Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 20, 1943 (three days before his fortieth birthday) Simonds was promoted to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Major-general_(Canada)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Major-general (Canada) (page does not exist)">major-general</a> and appointed <a href="/wiki/General_officer_commanding" title="General officer commanding">General Officer Commanding</a> (GOC) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Canadian_Infantry_Division" title="2nd Canadian Infantry Division">2nd Canadian Infantry Division</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Z_Husky_47_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Z_Husky_47-16">[16]</a></sup> having risen from <a href="/wiki/Major_(Canada)" title="Major (Canada)">major</a> to major-general in three-and-a-half years – faster than any other officer in the Canadian Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_158_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_158-17">[17]</a></sup> Just nine days later he was transferred to command the <a href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Division" title="1st Canadian Division">1st Canadian Infantry Division</a>, replacing Major-General <a href="/wiki/H._L._N._Salmon" title="H. L. N. Salmon">Harry Salmon</a> who had died in a plane crash while planning for <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Operation Husky</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Allied invasion of Sicily</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Z_Husky_47_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Z_Husky_47-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_156_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_156-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In this last post he led the 1st Canadian Division through the invasion of Sicily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> The division was placed under the command of <a href="/wiki/XXX_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XXX Corps (United Kingdom)">British XXX Corps</a>, serving alongside the veteran <a href="/wiki/51st_(Highland)_Division" title="51st (Highland) Division">51st (Highland) Division</a>, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-general (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Leese" title="Oliver Leese">Oliver Leese</a>. At this point, he was the youngest Canadian to lead a <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> in action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_158_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_158-17">[17]</a></sup> He came under fire for the first time July 16, 1943<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_157_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_157-19">[19]</a></sup> after nearly 17 years service in the Canadian Army.</p>
<p>On September 3, 1943 Simonds and the 1st Canadian Division, now serving alongside the <a href="/wiki/5th_Infantry_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="5th Infantry Division (United Kingdom)">British 5th Division</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/XIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XIII Corps (United Kingdom)">British XIII Corps</a>, commanded by Lieutenant-General <a href="/wiki/Miles_Dempsey" title="Miles Dempsey">Miles C. Dempsey</a> (whom Simonds was to form a high opinion of), landed on the mainland of Italy in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Baytown" title="Operation Baytown">Operation Baytown</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy" title="Allied invasion of Italy">Allied invasion of Italy</a>. Meeting light resistance, they suffered only 9 casualties on the first day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_118_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_118-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Falling ill on September 22, he was replaced as commander of the 1st Canadian Division by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Vokes" title="Christopher Vokes">Christopher Vokes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_124_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_124-21">[21]</a></sup> Subsequently he was appointed the GOC of the recently arrived <a href="/wiki/5th_Canadian_Division" title="5th Canadian Division">5th Canadian Armoured Division</a> when it arrived on the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Front</a> in November 1943. Simonds viewed this, along with the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Crerar" title="Harry Crerar">Crerar</a> and <a href="/wiki/I_Canadian_Corps" title="I Canadian Corps">I Canadian Corps</a>, as something of a comedown, although this was not the intent of CMHQ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_158_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_158-17">[17]</a></sup> His initial meeting with Crerar went poorly (possibly strained by Simonds' illness), and relations further deteriorated when Simonds ejected an officer sent by Crerar to measure his headquarters caravan. Crerar took the matter to <a href="/wiki/General_(United_Kingdom)" title="General (United Kingdom)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery" title="Bernard Montgomery">Montgomery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Eighth Army (United Kingdom)">British Eighth Army</a> commander, but found little support. However, Crerar discussed the event with army psychiatrists, the <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Army" title="First Canadian Army">First Canadian Army</a> commander <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Stuart" title="Kenneth Stuart">Kenneth Stuart</a>, and post-war pushed for <a href="/wiki/Charles_Foulkes_(Canadian_Army_general)" title="Charles Foulkes (Canadian Army general)">Charles Foulkes</a> to be selected as <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Canadian_Army" title="Commander of the Canadian Army">Chief of the General Staff</a> (CGS) over Simonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_205-207_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_205-207-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Simonds was furious when he learned that, to save shipping, his new division would have to take old equipment from the <a href="/wiki/7th_Armoured_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="7th Armoured Division (United Kingdom)">British 7th Armoured Division</a> (famous in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_Campaign" title="Western Desert Campaign">Western Desert</a> as the <i>Desert Rats</i>), then in Italy but soon to return to the United Kingdom to participate in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation Overlord</a>. Crerar nixed an idea to use 3,350 brand-new I Corps headquarters vehicles to equip the 5th Armoured Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_208_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_208-23">[23]</a></sup> The division would not be fully equipped until the end of January 1944.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corps_Commander">Corps Commander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corps Commander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="470" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg/250px-General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg/375px-General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:General_Guy_G_Simonds.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Simonds inspecting <a href="/wiki/II_Canadian_Corps" title="II Canadian Corps">II Canadian Corps</a> in Meppen, Germany, May 31, 1945.</div>
</div>

<p>In January 1944 he was recalled to the United Kingdom and made <a href="/wiki/General_officer_commanding" title="General officer commanding">General Officer Commanding</a> <a href="/wiki/II_Canadian_Corps" title="II Canadian Corps">II Canadian Corps</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> which went on to take part in the <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">Normandy landings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">advance through France</a>. At age forty-one, Simonds was purported to be the youngest corps commander in the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_163_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_163-24">[24]</a></sup> Simonds made numerous personnel changes: The Chief Engineer, Chief Medical Officer and the Commander Corps Royal Artillery (CCRA) were sacked, and <a href="/wiki/F._F._Worthington" title="F. F. Worthington">F. F. Worthington</a> was replaced as commander of <a href="/wiki/4th_Canadian_Division" title="4th Canadian Division">4th Canadian Armoured Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_163_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_163-24">[24]</a></sup> Talented officers such as <a href="/wiki/George_Kitching" title="George Kitching">George Kitching</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_Matthews_(Canadian_Army_officer)" title="Bruce Matthews (Canadian Army officer)">Bruce Matthews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Walsh" title="Geoffrey Walsh">Geoffrey Walsh</a> were brought by Simonds from Italy to II Corps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_221_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_221-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once II Corps was activated, Simonds would direct four major attacks during the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Battle of Normandy</a> in five weeks: Operations <a href="/wiki/Operation_Atlantic" title="Operation Atlantic">Atlantic</a> (the Canadian part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Goodwood" title="Operation Goodwood">Operation Goodwood</a>), <a href="/wiki/Operation_Spring" title="Operation Spring">Spring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Totalize" title="Operation Totalize">Totalize</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Tractable" title="Operation Tractable">Tractable</a>.</p>
<p>For Totalize (beginning August 7, 1944), which involved a night attack, numerous navigation aids were devised, along with heavy bomber support. Having learned from Operation Spring, Simonds devised the "<a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_(armoured_personnel_carrier)" title="Kangaroo (armoured personnel carrier)">Kangaroo</a>", an early <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">armoured personnel carrier</a> converted from non-operational armoured vehicles "defrocked <a href="/wiki/M7_Priest" title="M7 Priest">Priests</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keegan_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keegan-26">[26]</a></sup> Granatstein characterizes the plan as "brilliant if too complicated",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granat2005_169_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granat2005_169-27">[27]</a></sup> in that it did not account for the inexperience of the troops.</p>
<p>Tractable (August 14) used a smoke screen in an attempt to shield armour from German anti-tank weaponry.</p>
<p>In September 1944, Simonds temporarily took charge of the <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Army" title="First Canadian Army">First Canadian Army</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(Canada)" title="Lieutenant-general (Canada)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Harry_Crerar" title="Harry Crerar">Harry Crerar</a>, who was recovering from a bout of dysentery, and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Scheldt" title="Battle of the Scheldt">led the liberation</a> of the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> "By most accounts, Simonds’ assumption of command reinvigorated the army HQ; where Crerar managed, Simonds commanded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_easy_thing_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-No_easy_thing-28">[28]</a></sup> When Crerar resumed command with the First Army, Simonds resumed his command of II Canadian Corps for the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Western Front (World War II)">liberation of North-Western Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_war_Army">Post war Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post war Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Simonds was "undoubtedly deeply hurt" when he was overlooked and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Foulkes_(Canadian_Army_general)" title="Charles Foulkes (Canadian Army general)">Charles Foulkes</a> was chosen instead as <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Canadian_Army" title="Commander of the Canadian Army">CGS</a> in August 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDD_207_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDD_207-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1946–1949 he was Chief Instructor at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Defence_Studies" title="Royal College of Defence Studies">Imperial Defence College</a>, "a signal honour for a Canadian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris2000p2167_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2000p2167-30">[30]</a></sup> He returned to Canada in 1949 to take a role as <a href="/wiki/Commandant" title="Commandant">Commandant</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_of_Canada" title="Royal Military College of Canada">Royal Military College of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1951 he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Canadian_Army" title="Commander of the Canadian Army">Chief of the General Staff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juno_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-juno-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_later_years">Retirement and later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Retirement and later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After retiring from the Canadian Army, he worked for Halifax Insurance Company, and <a href="/wiki/Don_Valley_Brick_Works" title="Don Valley Brick Works">Toronto Brick and Associates</a>. He was active with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Life_Saving_Society_of_Canada" title="Royal Life Saving Society of Canada">Royal Life Saving Society of Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gurkha" title="Gurkha">Gurkha</a> Appeal, the <a href="/wiki/Commissionaire" title="Commissionaire">Canadian Corps of Commissionaires</a> and was chairman of the <a href="/wiki/National_Ballet_of_Canada" title="National Ballet of Canada">National Ballet of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham275-276_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham275-276-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>He criticized the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">government</a> for seeking closer ties with the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris2000p2167_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2000p2167-30">[30]</a></sup> and opposed the reliance on nuclear weapons, advocating strong conventional forces. Simonds proposed the use of aerial supply to reduce the vulnerability of army divisional supply chains. Dubious of air-power advocates, he foresaw the increasing use of missiles. He believed in the "integration" of defence headquarters, but opposed the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hellyer" title="Paul Hellyer">Hellyer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces#Unification_and_beyond" title="Canadian Forces">"unification"</a> of the armed forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham260-267_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham260-267-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A street is named after him in Antwerp ("Generaal Simondslaan").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham277_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham277-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Simonds was honorary colonel of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Regiment_of_Canada" title="Royal Regiment of Canada">Royal Regiment of Canada</a> at the time of the regiment's 100th anniversary in October 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham278_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham278-36">[36]</a></sup> He was offered an honorary degree from <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_of_Canada" title="Royal Military College of Canada">RMC</a> which he declined, as he had opposed the degree program, fearing the long tenure of civilian instructors would unduly influence the <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curriculum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham275.2C278_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham275.2C278-37">[37]</a></sup> On October 29, 1971 he was made a Companion of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham279_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham279-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diagnosed with lung cancer, he died in Toronto on May 15, 1974. He was buried in Toronto's <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pleasant_Cemetery,_Toronto" title="Mount Pleasant Cemetery, Toronto">Mount Pleasant Cemetery</a> after a service at Grace Church-on-the-Hill.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Married_life">Married life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Married life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 17, 1932 he married Katherine "K" Lockhart Taylor, the daughter of a Winnipeg businessman. K was a spirited young woman, having taken flying lessons, a motor mechanics course, and eventually teaching Guy to how to drive. As a <a href="/wiki/Subaltern" title="Subaltern">subaltern</a>, Simonds had to ask special permission to marry. They had a daughter, Ruth, born in England in June 1933, and a son Charles born in Kingston in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham38-41_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham38-41-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>While overseas in World War II, Simonds had an affair, which K subsequently discovered while in England in 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham227_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham227-40">[40]</a></sup> They separated shortly thereafter.</p>
<p>Shortly before retirement, Simonds met Dorothy "Do" Sinclair (the widow of George Graham "Gus" Sinclair) whom he married on January 16, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham274_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham274-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessment">Assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Simonds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randall Wakelam says "Canadian biographies and memoirs carry two themes: innovative and hard driving commander; cold and uninspiring leader."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_easy_thing_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-No_easy_thing-28">[28]</a></sup> Terry Copp suggests "overwhelming self-confidence and a degree of arrogance which did not encourage expressions of dissent. Simonds did not attempt to lead; he sought only to command." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_easy_thing_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-No_easy_thing-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book "The Normandy Campaign" Victor Brooks lists Simonds as the most effective corps-level commander of the Allied Forces in Normandy. He wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The corps commander among the units that comprised the 21st Army Group who most likely had the largest personal impact on the Normandy campaign was Lieutenant General Guy Simonds. This senior officer of the II Canadian Corps created one of the most effective tank-infantry teams in the Allied forces through a high degree of improvisation during the drive from Caen to Falaise. This general was versatile and imaginative but was not able to generate the momentum that would have more fully closed off the <a href="/wiki/Falaise_Pocket" title="Falaise Pocket">Falaise gap</a> at an earlier date. Despite this drawback, Simonds deserves credit for his effective command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Max_Hastings" title="Max Hastings">Max Hastings</a> states: "one of the outstanding Allied corps commanders in Europe, a dour, direct officer who brought unusual imagination to bear on every operational plan for which he was responsible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
