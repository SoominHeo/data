<p>The <b>Government Employees Insurance Company</b> (<b>GEICO</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_insurance" title="Vehicle insurance">auto insurance</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Chevy_Chase,_Maryland" title="Chevy Chase, Maryland">Chevy Chase, Maryland</a>. It is the second largest auto insurer in the United States, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Farm" title="State Farm">State Farm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a> that as of 2015 provided coverage for more than 22 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_vehicles" title="Motor vehicles">motor vehicles</a> owned by more than 14 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insurance_contract" title="Insurance contract">policy</a> holders. GEICO writes private passenger automobile insurance in all 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._states" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>. GEICO sells its policies through local agents, called GEICO Field Representatives, and over the phone directly to the consumer, and through their website. Its mascot is a <a href="/wiki/Gold_dust_day_gecko" title="Gold dust day gecko">gold dust day gecko</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">Cockney</a> accent, voiced by English actor <a href="/wiki/Jake_Wood" title="Jake Wood">Jake Wood</a>. GEICO is well known in popular culture for its advertising, having made a large number of commercials intended to entertain viewers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GEICO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GEICO was founded in 1936 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_Goodwin,_Sr." title="Leo Goodwin, Sr.">Leo Goodwin Sr.</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Goodwin" title="Lillian Goodwin">Lillian Goodwin</a> to provide auto insurance directly to federal government employees and their families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> Since 1925, Goodwin had worked for <a href="/wiki/USAA" title="USAA">USAA</a>, an insurer which specialized in insuring only military personnel; he decided to start his own company after rising as far as a civilian could go in USAA's military-dominated hierarchy. Based on Goodwin's experience at USAA, GEICO's original business model was predicated on the assumption that federal employees as a group would constitute a less risky and more financially stable pool of insureds, as opposed to the general public. Despite the presence of the word "government" in its name, GEICO has always been a private <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> not affiliated with any government organization.</p>
<p>In 1937, the Goodwins relocated GEICO from <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and reincorporated the company as a D.C. corporation after realizing that their business model would work best in the place with the highest concentration of federal employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>An important figure in GEICO's history is <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_Kreeger" title="David Lloyd Kreeger">David Lloyd Kreeger</a>, who became president of the company in 1964 and helped steer it into a major insurance enterprise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorktimes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-newyorktimes-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1948, he formed a group of investors who bought into GEICO right before it went public that year. He became senior vice president and general counsel of the company. Six years after becoming president of GEICO in 1964, he was named chairman and chief executive officer. He retained those titles until he retired in 1975. He continued as chairman of the executive committee until 1979, when he was named honorary chairman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorktimes_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-newyorktimes-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, under Kreeger's leadership, GEICO began to insure the general public, after real-time access to computerized driving records became available throughout the United States, and it was briefly the fifth-largest U.S. auto insurer. By 1975, it was clear that GEICO had expanded far too rapidly (during the <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9375_recession" title="1973–75 recession">1973–75 recession</a>) when it reported a $126.5 million loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> To prevent GEICO from collapsing, a consortium of 45 insurance companies agreed to take over a quarter of its policies, and it was forced to issue a stock offering (thus diluting existing stockholders) to raise money to pay claims. It took five years (during which the company shrank significantly) and a massive reorganization to set GEICO on the path to recovery.</p>
<p>GEICO has also offered other types of insurance besides auto, including homeowner's insurance from 1962 to 1996. A sister company, the Government Employees Life Insurance Company (GELICO), offered life insurance from 1975 to 1985. Although GEICO has since focused on its core auto insurance competency (selling GELICO to <a href="/wiki/Legal_%26_General" title="Legal &amp; General">Legal &amp; General</a>), it uses its established direct sales infrastructure to market homeowner's and other types of insurance underwritten by other companies.</p>
<p>In 1996, after many years as a publicly traded firm, GEICO became a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>GEICO generally deals directly with consumers via <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>; however, the local agent program has more than 150 offices countrywide. GEICO is now the second largest writer of private auto insurance in the United States.</p>
<p>After several years of denying claims and even canceling policies for policyholders that used their personal car for <a href="/wiki/Real-time_ridesharing" title="Real-time ridesharing">ridesharing</a> companies such as <a href="/wiki/Uber_(company)" title="Uber (company)">Uber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyft" title="Lyft">Lyft</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> GEICO began offering rideshare coverage in select states in 2015, including in high-population states such as <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>. The policy, which is issued through GEICO's commercial department, has received praise from insurance experts and quickly launched GEICO as the largest insurance provider for <a href="/wiki/Transportation_network_company" title="Transportation network company">TNC</a> drivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising_campaigns">Advertising campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GEICO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advertising campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/GEICO_advertising_campaigns" title="GEICO advertising campaigns">GEICO advertising campaigns</a></div>
<p>GEICO has many well-known ad campaigns. In 2012 GEICO spent over $1.1 billion in advertising, or 6.8% of its revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> All are made and produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Martin_Agency" title="The Martin Agency">The Martin Agency</a>, which is based in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>. GEICO ads have featured several well-known mascots, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The GEICO Gecko is the most prevalent spokesperson mascot and speaks with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockney_accent" title="Cockney accent">Cockney accent</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/GEICO_Cavemen" title="GEICO Cavemen">GEICO Cavemen</a> (from ads claiming using their website is "so easy, a caveman could do it").</li>
<li>Maxwell, the GEICO "Piggy" who shouts a long "Whee" and appears in more radio and TV commercials.</li>
<li>Actor <a href="/wiki/Mike_McGlone" title="Mike McGlone">Mike McGlone</a>, who uses <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a>-style narration to compare the ease of GEICO to things, famous people, or idioms. ("Could switching to GEICO really save you 15% or more on car insurance?...Is having a snowball fight with pitching great <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a> a bad idea?") The scene is then acted out, with typically humorous results. In addition to Johnson, other ads have included <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels" title="Charlie Daniels">Charlie Daniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Cantor" title="Andrés Cantor">Andrés Cantor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foghorn_Leghorn" title="Foghorn Leghorn">Foghorn Leghorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elmer_Fudd" title="Elmer Fudd">Elmer Fudd</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Lee_Ermey" title="R. Lee Ermey">R. Lee Ermey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ed_%22Too_Tall%22_Jones" title='Ed "Too Tall" Jones'>Ed "Too Tall" Jones</a> among others, including Maxwell the Piggy.</li>
<li>The "money savers" campaign enlisted actors to portray average consumers who have resorted to various humorous extremes in order to save money, such as teaching a dog to sing or teaching a group of Guinea pigs to row a boat and perform some mundane task for the consumer, and then presented switching to GEICO as an easy alternative to such endeavors with the common line ".... there's an easier way to save money."</li>
<li>The "Happier Than...." duo features Jimmy (actor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_ryan_cole&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Timothy ryan cole (page does not exist)">Timothy Ryan Cole</a>) and Ronnie (musician <a href="/wiki/Alex_Harvey_(musician)" title="Alex Harvey (musician)">Alex Harvey</a>) playing a guitar and a mandolin, respectively, on a small portable stage. They comment on a fictitious preceding event, such as a man dressed in 15th century attire laughing as he leads a trio of speed boats with the painted names <i>Nina</i>, <i>Pinta</i>, and <i>Santa Maria</i>. After cutting to the duo, one says to the other "You know, folks who save hundreds of dollars by switching to GEICO sure are happy." The other then replied, "How happy are they, (Jimmy/Ronnie)?" and in the case above, the response is "Happier than <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> with speedboats!"</li>
<li>CASH, the stack of cash that represents the money you could have saved by switching to GEICO.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also GEICO ads that feature stories from GEICO <a href="/wiki/Customer" title="Customer">customers</a> about situations in which the company assisted them, but are translated by celebrities like <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">Little Richard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Rivers" title="Joan Rivers">Joan Rivers</a>. Film trailer announcer <a href="/wiki/Don_LaFontaine" title="Don LaFontaine">Don LaFontaine</a> appeared in one such ad, shortly before his death. The tag announcer for these spots was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D.C._Douglas" title="D.C. Douglas">D.C. Douglas</a>. GEICO is also an official sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> and themed commercials for that always feature members of the hometown <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a>.</p>

<p>GEICO has long been involved in motorsports sponsorships. Since 2008, the company has sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Germain_Racing" title="Germain Racing">Germain Racing</a> team, first in the NASCAR Nationwide Series with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Wallace_(racing_driver)" title="Mike Wallace (racing driver)">Mike Wallace</a>, and later in the NASCAR Sprint Cup Series with <a href="/wiki/Max_Papis" title="Max Papis">Max Papis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Casey_Mears" title="Casey Mears">Casey Mears</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GEICO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GEICO's major competitors include <a href="/wiki/Amica_Mutual_Insurance" title="Amica Mutual Insurance">Amica Mutual Insurance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberty_Mutual_Insurance" title="Liberty Mutual Insurance">Liberty Mutual Insurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/State_Farm_Insurance" title="State Farm Insurance">State Farm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allstate" title="Allstate">Allstate</a>, <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Insurance" title="21st Century Insurance">21st Century Insurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reliance_Partners" title="Reliance Partners">Reliance Partners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Corporation" title="Progressive Corporation">Progressive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationwide_Mutual_Insurance_Company" title="Nationwide Mutual Insurance Company">Nationwide Insurance</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Services_Automobile_Association" title="United Services Automobile Association">United Services Automobile Association</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GEICO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December of 2016, a federal Miami jury awarded $2.7 million to a family who sued the company, claiming the company acted in bad faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November of 2015, a jury in Miami awarded a family $14.5 million after they sued the company for bad faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October of 2015, the Consumer Federation of California successfully sued the company for $6 million after alleged discrimination based on occupation, education level and other personal characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October of 2015, the United States Court of Appeals for the 11th Circuit upheld a verdict against the company for over $700k in a breach of contract suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Tony Dane of Las Vegas successfully sued the company for breach of contract following the theft of his car in which GEICO denied his claim, accused him of stealing his own vehicle, and put a private investigator on him. He feared he may have lost custody of his children. His original attorney dropped him, so he pursued the case pro se and won, gaining a verdict from a jury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December of 2010, the family of John Potts, who was killed in a traffic accident by a GEICO customer, successfully sued the company for $8.48 million after the company refused to pay an adequate settlement following the crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
