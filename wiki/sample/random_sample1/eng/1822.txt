<p>A <b>branch</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) or <b>tree branch</b> (sometimes referred to in <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> as a <b>ramus</b>) is a woody structural member connected to but not part of the central <a href="/wiki/Trunk_(botany)" title="Trunk (botany)">trunk</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> (or sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a>). Large branches are known as <b>boughs</b> and small branches are known as <b>twigs</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntrotoPoetry_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IntrotoPoetry-1">[1]</a></sup> Due to a broad range of species of trees, branches and twigs can be seen in many different shapes and sizes.</p>
<p>While branches can be nearly <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" title="Horizontal plane">horizontal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_direction" title="Vertical direction">vertical</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Diagonal" title="Diagonal">diagonal</a>, the majority of trees have upwardly diagonal branches.</p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Twig" title="Twig">twig</a>" often refers to a terminus, while "bough" refers only to branches coming directly from the trunk.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Words">Words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the enormous quantity of branches in the world, there are a variety of names in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> alone for them. In general however, unspecific words for a branch (such as <b>rise</b>) have been <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">replaced</a> by the word branch itself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_terms">Specific terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specific terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A bough can also be called a <b>limb</b> or <b>arm</b>, and though these are arguably <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphors</a>, both are widely accepted <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonyms</a> for bough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <b>crotch</b> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_forks" title="Tree forks"><b>fork</b></a> is an area where a trunk splits into two or more boughs.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Twig" title="Twig">twig</a> is frequently referred to as a <b>sprig</b> as well, especially when it has been plucked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other words for twig include <b>branchlet</b>, <b>spray</b>, and <b>surcle</b>, as well as the technical terms <b>surculus</b> and <b>ramulus</b>.</p>
<p>Branches found under larger branches can be called <b>underbranches</b>.</p>
<p>Some branches from specific trees have their own names, such as <b>osiers</b> and <b>withes</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Withy" title="Withy">withies</a></b>, which come from <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willows</a>. Often trees have certain words which, in English, are naturally <a href="/wiki/Collocation" title="Collocation">collocated</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mistletoe" title="Mistletoe">mistletoe</a>, which usually employ the phrase "sprig of" (as in, a "sprig of mistletoe"). Similarly, the branch of a <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry tree</a> is generally referred to as a "cherry branch", while other such formations (i.e., "<a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> branch" or "<a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">orange</a> branch") carry no such alliance. A good example of this versatility is <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>, which could be referred to as variously an "oak branch", an "oaken branch", a "branch of oak", or the "branch of an oak [tree]".</p>
<p>Once a branch has been cut or in any other way removed from its source, it is most commonly referred to as a <b>stick</b>, and a stick employed for some purpose (such as <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanking" title="Spanking">spanking</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beating_up" title="Beating up">beating</a>) is often called a <b>rod</b>. Thin, flexible sticks are called <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switch_(rod)" title="Switch (rod)">switches</a></b>, <b>wands</b>, <b>shrags</b>, or <b>vimina</b> (singular <b>vimen</b>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_etymology">History and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, there are numerous words for branch, including <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">seten</span></i>, <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">stofn</span></i>, <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">telgor</span></i>, and <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">hrīs</span></i>. There are also numerous descriptive words, such as <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">blēd</span></i> (that is, something that has bled, or "bloomed", out), <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">bōgincel</span></i> (literally "little bough"), <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">ōwæstm</span></i> (literally "on growth"), and <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">tūdornes</span></i> (literally "offspringing"). Numerous other words for twigs and boughs abound, including <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">tān</span></i>, which still survives as the "-toe" in <i>mistletoe</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latin words for branch are <i>ramus</i> or <i>cladus</i>. The second term is an affix found in other modern words such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cladodonts" title="Cladodonts">cladodonts</a> (prehistoric sharks with branched teeth) or <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a>.</p>
