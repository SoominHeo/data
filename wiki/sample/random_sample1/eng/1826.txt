<p><b>Shizuo Kakutani</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">角谷 静夫</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kakutani Shizuo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, August 28, 1911 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%8Csaka" title="Ōsaka">Ōsaka</a> – August 17, 2004 in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>)</span> was a Japanese-American <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, best known for his <a href="/wiki/Kakutani_fixed-point_theorem" title="Kakutani fixed-point theorem">eponymous fixed-point theorem</a>.</p>
<p>Kakutani attended <a href="/wiki/Tohoku_University" title="Tohoku University">Tohoku University</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sendai,_Miyagi" title="Sendai, Miyagi">Sendai</a>, where his advisor was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tatsujir%C5%8D_Shimizu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tatsujirō Shimizu (page does not exist)">Tatsujirō Shimizu</a>. At one point he spent two years at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Institute for Advanced Study">Institute for Advanced Study</a> in <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton</a> at the invitation of the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Weyl" title="Hermann Weyl">Hermann Weyl</a>. While there, he also met <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>.</p>
<p>Kakutani received his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in 1941 from <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and taught there through <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. He returned to the Institute for Advanced Study in 1948, and was given a professorship by <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> in 1949, where he won a students choice award for excellence in teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kakutani received two awards of the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Academy" title="Japan Academy">Japan Academy</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Imperial Prize (page does not exist)">Imperial Prize</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Academy_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Academy Prize (page does not exist)">Academy Prize</a> in 1982, for his scholarly achievements in general and his work on functional analysis in particular.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kakutani_fixed-point_theorem" title="Kakutani fixed-point theorem">Kakutani fixed-point theorem</a> is a generalization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brouwer%27s_fixed-point_theorem" title="Brouwer's fixed-point theorem">Brouwer's fixed-point theorem</a>, holding for generalized <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_(mathematics)" title="Correspondence (mathematics)">correspondences</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functions</a>. Its most important uses are in proving the existence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nash_equilibria" title="Nash equilibria">Nash equilibria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrow%E2%80%93Debreu%E2%80%93McKenzie_model" title="Arrow–Debreu–McKenzie model">Arrow–Debreu–McKenzie model</a> of <a href="/wiki/General_equilibrium_theory" title="General equilibrium theory">general equilibrium theory</a>.</p>
<p>Kakutani's other mathematical contributions include the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kakutani_skyscraper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kakutani skyscraper (page does not exist)">Kakutani skyscraper</a>, a concept in <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a> (a branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> that studies <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Invariant_measure" title="Invariant measure">invariant measure</a> and related problems). They also include his solution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisson_equation" title="Poisson equation">Poisson equation</a> <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_processes_and_boundary_value_problems" title="Stochastic processes and boundary value problems">using the methods of stochastic analysis</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Collatz_conjecture" title="Collatz conjecture">Collatz conjecture</a> is also known as the Kakutani conjecture.</p>
<p>His daughter, <a href="/wiki/Michiko_Kakutani" title="Michiko Kakutani">Michiko Kakutani</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning literary critic for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>.</p>
