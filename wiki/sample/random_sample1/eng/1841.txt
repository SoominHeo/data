<p><b>Angular acceleration</b> is the rate of change of <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> units, it is measured in <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">radians</a> per <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> squared (rad/s<sup>2</sup>), and is usually denoted by the Greek letter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_(letter)" title="Alpha (letter)">alpha</a> (<i>α</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-constant_acceleration">Non-constant acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angular_acceleration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-constant acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For any non-constant torque, the angular acceleration of an object will change with time. The equation becomes a differential equation instead of a constant value. This <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equation</a> is known as the equation of motion of the system and can completely describe the motion of the object. It is also the best way to calculate the angular velocity.</p>
