<p><b>Naoto Kan</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">菅 直人</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kan Naoto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born 10 October 1946)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> politician, and former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister of Japan</a>. In June 2010, then-Finance Minister Kan was elected as the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Democratic Party of Japan">Democratic Party of Japan</a> (DPJ) and designated Prime Minister by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" title="Diet of Japan">Diet</a> to succeed <a href="/wiki/Yukio_Hatoyama" title="Yukio Hatoyama">Yukio Hatoyama</a>. Kan was the first Prime Minister of Japan since the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Junichiro_Koizumi" title="Junichiro Koizumi">Junichiro Koizumi</a> in 2006 to serve for more than 1 year, with predecessors Yukio Hatoyama, <a href="/wiki/Tar%C5%8D_As%C5%8D" title="Tarō Asō">Tarō Asō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yasuo_Fukuda" title="Yasuo Fukuda">Yasuo Fukuda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> either resigning prematurely or losing an election. On 26 August 2011, Kan announced his resignation. <a href="/wiki/Yoshihiko_Noda" title="Yoshihiko Noda">Yoshihiko Noda</a> was formally appointed as Prime Minister on 2 September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ01_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On 1 August 2012, United Nations Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> announced Kan would be one of the members of the UN high-level panel on the post-2015 development agenda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kan was born in <a href="/wiki/Ube,_Yamaguchi" title="Ube, Yamaguchi">Ube City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamaguchi_Prefecture" title="Yamaguchi Prefecture">Yamaguchi Prefecture</a>, as the son of Hisao Kan, an executive for a glass manufacturer. He graduated in 1970 from the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology" title="Tokyo Institute of Technology">Tokyo Institute of Technology</a> and became a licensed <i><a href="/wiki/Benrishi" title="Benrishi">benrishi</a></i> (patent agent/attorney) in 1971.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_career">Diet career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diet career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from college, Kan worked at a patent office for four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He actively engaged in civic <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots movements</a> for years and also served on election campaign staff for <a href="/wiki/Fusae_Ichikawa" title="Fusae Ichikawa">Fusae Ichikawa</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a> activist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After having lost in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_1976" title="Japanese general election, 1976">1976</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_1979" title="Japanese general election, 1979">1979 general elections</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Councillors_election,_1977" title="Japanese House of Councillors election, 1977">1977 Upper House election</a>, Kan achieved a seat in the lower house in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_1980" title="Japanese general election, 1980">1980</a> as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Democratic_Federation_(Japan)" title="Socialist Democratic Federation (Japan)">Socialist Democratic Federation</a>. He gained national popularity in 1996, when serving as the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health,_Labour,_and_Welfare" title="Ministry of Health, Labour, and Welfare">Minister of Health and Welfare</a>, admitting the government's responsibility for the spread of <a href="/wiki/HIV-tainted_blood_scandal_(Japan)" title="HIV-tainted blood scandal (Japan)">HIV-tainted blood</a> in the 1980s and directly apologized to victims. At that time, he was a member of a small party forming the ruling <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)">Liberal Democratic Party</a> (LDP). His frank action was completely unprecedented and was applauded by the media and the public.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1998, his image was affected by allegations of an affair, vigorously denied by both parties, with a television newscaster and media consultant, Yūko Tonomoto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Yukio_Hatoyama" title="Yukio Hatoyama">Yukio Hatoyama</a> resigned as the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Democratic Party of Japan">Democratic Party of Japan</a> (DPJ), Kan again took over the position. In July 2003, the DPJ and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Japan,_1998)" title="Liberal Party (Japan, 1998)">Liberal Party</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Ichir%C5%8D_Ozawa" title="Ichirō Ozawa">Ichirō Ozawa</a> agreed to form a united opposition party to prepare for the <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Japan" title="Elections in Japan">general election</a> that was anticipated to take place in the fall.</p>
<p>During the campaign of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_2003" title="Japanese general election, 2003">election of 2003</a>, the DPJ called the election as the choice of the government between the ruling LDP-bloc and the DPJ, with Kan being presented as the alternative candidate to then Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Junichiro_Koizumi" title="Junichiro Koizumi">Junichiro Koizumi</a>. His face was used as the trademark of the campaign against the LDP.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, in 2004 Kan was accused of unpaid annuities and again resigned the position of leader. On 10 May 2004, he officially announced his resignation and made the <a href="/wiki/Shikoku_Pilgrimage" title="Shikoku Pilgrimage">Shikoku Pilgrimage</a>. Later, the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare spokesman apologized, saying the unpaid record was due to an administrative error.</p>
<p>In mid-October 2005, Kan, who turned 60 in 2006, proposed the creation of a new political party to be called the "Dankai (<a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">baby boomer</a>) Party". The initial intent of the party was to offer places of activity for the Japanese baby boomers – <span class="nowrap">2.7 million</span> of whom began to retire <i>en masse</i> in 2007.</p>
<p>He believes the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Self-Defense_Forces" title="Japan Self-Defense Forces">Japan Self-Defense Forces</a> should play a more prominent role on the international stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finance_minister">Finance minister</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Finance minister">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 6 January 2010, he was picked by <a href="/wiki/Yukio_Hatoyama" title="Yukio Hatoyama">Yukio Hatoyama</a> to be the new finance minister, assuming the post in addition to deputy prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He replaced <a href="/wiki/Hirohisa_Fujii" title="Hirohisa Fujii">Hirohisa Fujii</a> as finance minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his first news conference, Kan announced his priority was stimulating growth and took the unusual step of naming a specific dollar-yen level as optimal to help exporters and stimulate the economy: "There are a lot of voices in the business world saying that (the dollar) around ¥95 is appropriate in terms of trade".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Hatoyama appeared to rebuke Kan. "When it comes to foreign exchange, stability is desirable and rapid moves are undesirable. The government basically shouldn't comment on foreign exchange," he told reporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister">Prime Minister</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prime Minister">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On 2 June 2010 <a href="/wiki/Yukio_Hatoyama" title="Yukio Hatoyama">Yukio Hatoyama</a> announced his intention to resign as the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Democratic Party of Japan">Democratic Party of Japan</a> (DPJ) and as Prime Minister, also saying that he had urged his backer in the party, <a href="/wiki/Ichir%C5%8D_Ozawa" title="Ichirō Ozawa">Ichirō Ozawa</a>, to resign as Secretary General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT02_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT02-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Cabinet resigned <i>en masse</i> on 4 June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT02_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT02-12">[12]</a></sup> Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Katsuya_Okada" title="Katsuya Okada">Katsuya Okada</a> and Land and Transport Minister <a href="/wiki/Seiji_Maehara" title="Seiji Maehara">Seiji Maehara</a>, though once considered to be possible successors to Hatoyama, announced their support for Naoto Kan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kan, at his age of 63, won the leadership of the DPJ with 291 votes to 129, defeating a relatively unknown Ozawa-backed legislator <a href="/wiki/Shinji_Tarutoko" title="Shinji Tarutoko">Shinji Tarutoko</a>, 50,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT02_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT02-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> who was leading the environmental policy committee in the lower house of the Diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequently, on 4 June, Kan was designated Prime Minister by the Diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 8 June, <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Emperor Akihito</a> formally appointed Kan as the country's <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Japan" title="List of Prime Ministers of Japan">94th Prime Minister</a>, and the 29th postwar Prime Minister.</p>
<p>Kan's approval ratings fell in the month of June after he proposed an increase in the sales tax rate from 5% to 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg1-18">[18]</a></sup> His sales tax increase proposal was opposed by <a href="/wiki/Ichir%C5%8D_Ozawa" title="Ichirō Ozawa">Ichirō Ozawa</a>, amongst others in the DPJ, and the proposal was quickly scaled back by Kan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The botched sales tax increase proposal was partially blamed for the DPJ's disappointing results in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Councillors_election,_2010" title="Japanese House of Councillors election, 2010">July House of Councillors election</a>, where the DPJ lost its majority and were forced to work with smaller, unaffiliated parties (such as <a href="/wiki/Your_Party" title="Your Party">Your Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Communist_Party" title="Japanese Communist Party">JCP</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Social Democratic Party (Japan)">SDP</a>) in order to secure passage of bills in the House of Councillors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August, Kan apologised to the <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Republic of Korea</a> on the 100th anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan%E2%80%93Korea_Annexation_Treaty" title="Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty">Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ozawa <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Japan)_leadership_election,_September_2010" title="Democratic Party (Japan) leadership election, September 2010">challenged</a> Kan's leadership of the DPJ in September. Although it was initially believed that Ozawa had a slight edge among DPJ members of parliament,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters1-21">[21]</a></sup> in the final vote Kan garnered the support of 206 DPJ lawmakers to Ozawa's 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Local rank-and-file party members and activists overwhelmingly supported Kan, and according to opinion polls the wider Japanese public preferred Kan to Ozawa by as much as a 4–1 ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the leadership challenge, Kan reshuffled his cabinet, which left many prominent members of the pro-Ozawa faction of the DPJ without important posts in the new cabinet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The cabinet reshuffle also resulted in the promotion of long-time Kan ally <a href="/wiki/Yoshito_Sengoku" title="Yoshito Sengoku">Yoshito Sengoku</a> to Chief Cabinet Secretary. Sengoku was labeled by the opposition LDP as the "second" Prime Minister of the Kan cabinet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 September a Chinese fishing boat captain was arrested by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Coast_Guard" title="Japan Coast Guard">Japan Coast Guard (JCG)</a> after <a href="/wiki/2010_Senkaku_boat_collision_incident" title="2010 Senkaku boat collision incident">his trawler had collided with JCG patrol boats</a> in disputed waters near the <a href="/wiki/Senkaku_Islands" title="Senkaku Islands">Senkaku Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> China protested the arrest, as it <a href="/wiki/Senkaku_Islands_dispute" title="Senkaku Islands dispute">claims the islands as part of its sovereign territory</a>, and demanded the unconditional release of the captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The captain was released on 24 September, after China had cut off all ministerial-level contacts with Japan and threatened further action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The incident brought <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China%E2%80%93Japan_relations" title="People's Republic of China–Japan relations">Sino-Japanese relations</a> to its lowest point since the Koizumi administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Kan government intervened in mid-September to weaken the surging yen by buying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._dollars" title="U.S. dollars">U.S. dollars</a>, a move which temporarily relieved Japan's exporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yenintervention1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-yenintervention1-29">[29]</a></sup> The move proved popular with stock brokers, Japanese exporters, and the Japanese public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yenintervention1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-yenintervention1-29">[29]</a></sup> It was the first such move by a Japanese government since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yenintervention1_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-yenintervention1-29">[29]</a></sup> Later, in October, after the yen had offset the intervention and had reached a 15-year high, the Kan cabinet approved a stimulus package worth about 5.1 trillion yen ($62 billion) in order to weaken the yen and fight deflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November, Kan spoke out forcefully in support of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and in harsh criticism of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> in the wake of the latter's <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Yeonpyeong" title="Bombardment of Yeonpyeong">bombardment of Yeonpyeong</a>, meanwhile ignoring China's public comments which had not yet included denunciation of the North.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 12 March 2011, after the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">earthquake and tsunami in the northeast Japan</a>, Kan flew in a helicopter to observe the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_I_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Fukushima I Nuclear Power Plant">Fukushima I Nuclear Power Plant</a> and was heavily involved in efforts to effectively respond to the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Naoto Kan took an increasingly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear" title="Anti-nuclear">anti-nuclear</a> stance in the months following the Fukushima disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In May, he ordered the aging <a href="/wiki/Hamaoka_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Hamaoka Nuclear Power Plant">Hamaoka Nuclear Power Plant</a> be closed over earthquake and tsunami fears, and he said he would freeze plans to build new reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite falling popularity, Kan rejected calls to step down while the country continued to suffer from the earthquake, tsunami, and nuclear crises of spring 2011. One year into his premiership on 2 June 2011, Kan proposed his resignation, hours before the Diet put forward a vote of no-confidence. The motion was defeated by 293 to 152, bolstering the Prime Minister's position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2011, Kan said that Japan must reduce its dependence on nuclear energy, breaking with a decades-old Japanese government drive to build more nuclear power plants in the country. "We must scrap the plan to have nuclear power contribute 53 percent (of electricity supply) by 2030 and reduce the degree of reliance on nuclear power," Kan told a government panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Kan said Japan should abandon plans to build 14 new reactors by 2030. He wants to "pass a bill to promote <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and questioned whether private companies should be running atomic plants".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2012, Kan said the Fukushima disaster made it clear to him that "Japan needs to dramatically reduce its dependence on nuclear power, which supplied 30 percent of its electricity before the crisis, and has turned him into a believer of renewable energy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kan12_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-kan12-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kan announced his intention to resign on 10 August 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resignation_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-resignation-40">[40]</a></sup> On 26 August, with passage of a debt bill and the renewable energy bill as final conditions, Kan expected "to see his successor in office [within the] week, according to a Kyodo news report, which cited cabinet ministers". At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Seiji_Maehara" title="Seiji Maehara">Seiji Maehara</a>, who had supported Kan in 2010, was reported to have announced his intention to run to succeed Kan. Maehara is seen as the potential DPJ candidate most popular with the voters at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Several other cabinet members joined the race, and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Japan)_leadership_election,_2011" title="Democratic Party (Japan) leadership election, 2011">election of the DPJ successor</a> was scheduled for 29 August. At that time, <a href="/wiki/Yoshihiko_Noda" title="Yoshihiko Noda">Yoshihiko Noda</a>, most recently finance minister, was elected as the new DPJ leader and, as leader of the largest party in the Diet, became prime minister as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ01_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ01-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Kan was interviewed about the Fukushima nuclear disaster, and has said that at one point Japan faced a situation where there was a chance that people might not be able to live in the capital zone including Tokyo and would have to evacuate. He says he is haunted by the specter of an even bigger nuclear crisis forcing tens of millions of people to flee Tokyo and threatening the nation's existence. "If things had reached that level, not only would the public have had to face hardships but Japan's very existence would have been in peril".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k12_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-k12-42">[42]</a></sup> That convinced Kan to "declare the need for Japan to end its reliance on atomic power and promote renewable sources of energy such solar that have long taken a back seat in the resource-poor country's energy mix".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k12_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-k12-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_Prime_Ministership">Post Prime Ministership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post Prime Ministership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 16 December <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_gubernatorial_election,_2012" title="Tokyo gubernatorial election, 2012">2012 Tokyo gubernatorial election</a>, called due to the resignation of incumbent Governor <a href="/wiki/Shintaro_Ishihara" title="Shintaro Ishihara">Shintaro Ishihara</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Democratic Party of Japan">Democratic Party of Japan</a> itself did not endorse any candidate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> but Kan supported <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Utsunomiya" title="Kenji Utsunomiya">Kenji Utsunomiya</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> who shared Kan's anti-nuclear stance. Ultimately Utsunomiya came second in the election, losing to <a href="/wiki/Naoki_Inose" title="Naoki Inose">Naoki Inose</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> who had been vice-governor under Ishihara from 2007 to 2012, and then acting governor after Ishihara's abrupt resignation.</p>
<p>In the 16 December <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_2012" title="Japanese general election, 2012">2012 general election</a> called by Kan's successor Noda, Kan lost his seat in Tokyo's Number 18 District,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> but retained a seat in the diet through the proportional representation system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naoto_Kan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kan married his wife Nobuko in 1970. Nobuko, born in Okayama Prefecture, entered a relationship with the Tokyo-dwelling Kan after entering <a href="/wiki/Tsuda_College" title="Tsuda College">Tsuda College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> As the two are <a href="/wiki/Cousin_marriage" title="Cousin marriage">first cousins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> the engagement was met with parental opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> They have two sons, Gentarō and Shinjirō. Gentarō is a civil rights activist and lost in elections for the Lower House in 2003 and 2005. Shinjiro is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinarian</a> and works at an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_hospital" title="Animal hospital">animal hospital</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerima,_Tokyo" title="Nerima, Tokyo">Nerima, Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kan is nicknamed "Ira-Kan" (Fretful Kan") due to his reputed short temper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> His hobbies are <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">go</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shogi" title="Shogi">shogi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Origami" title="Origami">origami</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Kan built a machine to calculate the complicated mahjong point system and applied for a patent in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
