<p><b>Cirrhosis</b> is a condition in which the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> does not function properly due to long-term damage. Typically, the disease comes on slowly over months or years. Early on, there are often no symptoms. As the disease worsens, a person may become tired, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asthenia" title="Asthenia">weak</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pruritus" title="Pruritus">itchy</a>, have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedal_edema" title="Pedal edema">swelling in the lower legs</a>, develop <a href="/wiki/Jaundice" title="Jaundice">yellow skin</a>, bruise easily, have <a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">fluid build up in the abdomen</a>, or develop <a href="/wiki/Spider_angioma" title="Spider angioma">spider-like blood vessels on the skin</a>. The fluid build-up in the abdomen may become <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_bacterial_peritonitis" title="Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis">spontaneously infected</a>. Other complications include <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_encephalopathy" title="Hepatic encephalopathy">hepatic encephalopathy</a>, bleeding from <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_varices" title="Esophageal varices">dilated veins in the esophagus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gastric_varices" title="Gastric varices">dilated stomach veins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liver_cancer" title="Liver cancer">liver cancer</a>. Hepatic encephalopathy results in confusion and possibly <a href="/wiki/Unconsciousness" title="Unconsciousness">unconsciousness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cirrhosis is most commonly caused by <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcohol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B" title="Hepatitis B">hepatitis B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C" title="Hepatitis C">hepatitis C</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_fatty_liver_disease" title="Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease">non-alcoholic fatty liver disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Typically, more than two or three drinks per day over a number of years is required for alcoholic cirrhosis to occur. Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease is due to a number of reasons, including being <a href="/wiki/Overweight" title="Overweight">overweight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperlipidemia" title="Hyperlipidemia">high blood fats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">high blood pressure</a>. A number of less common causes include <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_hepatitis" title="Autoimmune hepatitis">autoimmune hepatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Primary_biliary_cirrhosis" title="Primary biliary cirrhosis">primary biliary cirrhosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemochromatosis" title="Hemochromatosis">hemochromatosis</a>, certain medications, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallstones" title="Gallstones">gallstones</a>. Cirrhosis is characterized by the replacement of normal liver <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> by <a href="/wiki/Fibrosis" title="Fibrosis">scar tissue</a>. These changes lead to loss of liver function. Diagnosis is based on blood testing, <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liver_biopsy" title="Liver biopsy">liver biopsy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some causes of cirrhosis, such as hepatitis B, can be prevented by <a href="/wiki/Vaccination" title="Vaccination">vaccination</a>. Treatment partly depends on the underlying cause. The goal is often to prevent worsening and complications. Avoiding alcohol is recommended. Hepatitis B and C may be treatable with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiviral_medication" title="Antiviral medication">antiviral medications</a>. Autoimmune hepatitis may be treated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">steroid medications</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ursodiol" title="Ursodiol">Ursodiol</a> may be useful if the disease is due to blockage of the <a href="/wiki/Bile_duct" title="Bile duct">bile ducts</a>. Other medications may be useful for complications such as swelling, hepatic encephalopathy, and dilated esophageal veins. In severe cirrhosis, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liver_transplant" title="Liver transplant">liver transplant</a> may be an option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cirrhosis resulted in 1.2 million deaths in 2013, up from 0.8 million deaths in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD2014_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Of these, alcohol caused 384,000, hepatitis C caused 358,000, and hepatitis B caused 317,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2014-2">[2]</a></sup> In the United States, more men die of cirrhosis than women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014-1">[1]</a></sup> The first known description of the condition is by <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> in the 5th century BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The word <i>cirrhosis</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κίρρωσις</span>; <i>kirrhos</i> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">κιρρός</span> "yellowish" and <i>-osis</i> (-<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ωσις</span>) meaning "condition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liver_Cirrhosis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Liver_Cirrhosis.png/310px-Liver_Cirrhosis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Liver_Cirrhosis.png/465px-Liver_Cirrhosis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Liver_Cirrhosis.png/620px-Liver_Cirrhosis.png 2x" width="310"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liver_Cirrhosis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Liver cirrhosis.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cirrhosis has many possible manifestations. These signs and symptoms may be either as a direct result of the failure of liver cells or secondary to the resultant <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a>. There are also some manifestations whose causes are nonspecific but may occur in cirrhosis. Likewise, the absence of any does not rule out the possibility of cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup> Cirrhosis of the liver is slow and gradual in its development. It is usually well advanced before its symptoms are noticeable enough to cause alarm. Weakness and loss of weight may be early symptoms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liver_dysfunction">Liver dysfunction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Liver dysfunction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following features are as a direct consequence of liver cells not functioning.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spider_angioma" title="Spider angioma">Spider angiomata</a> or spider nevi are vascular lesions consisting of a central arteriole surrounded by many smaller vessels (hence the name "spider") and occur due to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Estradiol" title="Estradiol">estradiol</a>. One study found that spider angiomata occur in about 1/3 of cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10423070_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10423070-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Palmar_erythema" title="Palmar erythema">Palmar erythema</a> is a reddening of palms at the thenar and hypothenar eminences also as a result of increased estrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gynecomastia" title="Gynecomastia">Gynecomastia</a>, or increase in breast gland size in men that is not cancerous, is caused by increased estradiol and can occur in up to 2/3 of patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primary_care_guidelines_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Primary_care_guidelines-10">[10]</a></sup> This is different from increase in breast fat in overweight people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liver_disease_and_the_hypothalamic_pituitary_gonadal_axis_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liver_disease_and_the_hypothalamic_pituitary_gonadal_axis-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypogonadism" title="Hypogonadism">Hypogonadism</a>, a decrease in sex hormones manifest as impotence, infertility, loss of sexual drive, and testicular atrophy, can result from primary gonadal injury or suppression of <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamic%E2%80%93pituitary%E2%80%93gonadal_axis" title="Hypothalamic–pituitary–gonadal axis">hypothalamic/pituitary function</a>. Hypogonadism is associated with cirrhosis due to alcoholism and hemochromatosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Liver size can be <a href="/wiki/Hepatomegaly" title="Hepatomegaly">enlarged</a>, normal, or shrunken in people with cirrhosis.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">Ascites</a>, accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity (space in the abdomen), gives rise to flank dullness (needs about 1500 ml to detect flank dullness). This may be visible as increase in abdominal girth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fetor_hepaticus" title="Fetor hepaticus">Fetor hepaticus</a> is a musty breath odor resulting from increased <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jaundice" title="Jaundice">Jaundice</a> is yellow discoloration of the skin and <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a> (with the eye being especially noticeable) due to increased bilirubin (at least 2–3 mg/dL or 30 µmol/L). Urine may also appear dark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_hypertension">Portal hypertension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Portal hypertension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Liver cirrhosis increases resistance to blood flow and higher pressure in the portal venous system, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a>. Effects of portal hypertension include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Splenomegaly" title="Splenomegaly">Splenomegaly</a> (increase in size of the spleen) is found in 35% to 50% of patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Esophageal_varices" title="Esophageal varices">Esophageal varices</a> result from collateral portal blood flow through vessels in the stomach and esophagus (a process called <a href="/wiki/Portacaval_anastomosis" title="Portacaval anastomosis">portacaval anastomosis</a>). When these blood vessels become enlarged, they are called varices and are more likely to rupture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup> Variceal rupture often leads to severe bleeding, which can be fatal.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caput_medusa" title="Caput medusa">Caput medusa</a> are dilated periumbilical collateral veins due to portal hypertension. Blood from the portal venous system may be shunted through the periumbilical veins and ultimately to the abdominal wall veins, manifesting as a pattern that may resemble the head of <a href="/wiki/Medusa" title="Medusa">Medusa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cruveilhier-Baumgarten murmur is a venous hum heard in the epigastric region (on examination by stethoscope) due to collateral connections forming between portal system and the periumbilical veins as a result of portal hypertension.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unestablished_cause">Unestablished cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unestablished cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are some changes seen in cirrhosis whose causes are not clearly known. They may also be a sign of other non-liver related causes.</p>
<ul>
<li>Nail changes.
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muehrcke%27s_lines" title="Muehrcke's lines">Muehrcke's lines</a> - paired horizontal bands separated by normal color resulting from <a href="/wiki/Hypoalbuminemia" title="Hypoalbuminemia">hypoalbuminemia</a> (inadequate production of <a href="/wiki/Human_serum_albumin" title="Human serum albumin">albumin</a>). It is not specific for cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terry%27s_nails" title="Terry's nails">Terry's nails</a> (double nails) - proximal two-thirds of the nail plate appears white with distal one-third red, also due to hypoalbuminemia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derm_Atlas_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Derm_Atlas-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nail_clubbing" title="Nail clubbing">Clubbing</a> - angle between the nail plate and proximal nail fold &gt; 180 degrees. It is not specific for cirrhosis and can therefore happen in a number of conditions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derm_Atlas_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Derm_Atlas-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypertrophic_osteoarthropathy" title="Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy">Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy</a>. Chronic proliferative <a href="/wiki/Periostitis" title="Periostitis">periostitis</a> of the long bones that can cause considerable pain. It is not specific for cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derm_Atlas_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Derm_Atlas-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dupuytren%27s_contracture" title="Dupuytren's contracture">Dupuytren's contracture</a>. Thickening and shortening of palmar fascia (tissue on the palm of the hands) that leads to flexion deformities of the fingers. Caused by fibroblastic proliferation (increased growth) and disorderly <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> deposition. It is relatively common (33% of patients).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derm_Atlas_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Derm_Atlas-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Other. Weakness, fatigue, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anorexia" title="Anorexia">anorexia</a>, weight loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_disease">Advanced disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advanced disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the disease progresses, complications may develop. In some people, these may be the first signs of the disease.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruising" title="Bruising">Bruising</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage">bleeding</a> resulting from decreased production of <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a> factors.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hepatic_encephalopathy" title="Hepatic encephalopathy">Hepatic encephalopathy</a> - the liver does not clear <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and related nitrogenous substances from the blood, which are carried to the brain, affecting cerebral functioning: neglect of personal appearance, unresponsiveness, forgetfulness, trouble concentrating, or changes in sleep habits. This can be seen on exam by <a href="/wiki/Asterixis" title="Asterixis">asterixis</a> which is bilateral asynchronous flapping of outstretched, dorsiflexed hands seen in patients with hepatic encephalopathy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sensitivity to medication caused by decreased metabolism of the active compounds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acute_kidney_injury" title="Acute kidney injury">Acute kidney injury</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Hepatorenal_syndrome" title="Hepatorenal syndrome">hepatorenal syndrome</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has many possible causes; sometimes more than one cause is present in the same person. Globally, 57% of cirrhosis is attributable to either hepatitis B (30%) or hepatitis C (27%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perz2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perz2006-18">[18]</a></sup> Alcohol consumption is another important cause, accounting for about 20% of the cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perz2006_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Perz2006-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_liver_disease" title="Alcoholic liver disease">Alcoholic liver disease</a> (ALD). Alcoholic cirrhosis develops for 10–20% of individuals who drink heavily for a decade or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Alcohol seems to injure the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> by blocking the normal metabolism of protein, fats, and carbohydrates. This injury happens through the formation of acetaldehyde from alcohol which itself is reactive, but also leads to the accumulation of products in the liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup> Patients may also have concurrent <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_hepatitis" title="Alcoholic hepatitis">alcoholic hepatitis</a> with fever, hepatomegaly, jaundice, and anorexia. AST and ALT are both elevated but less than 300 IU/liter with an AST:ALT ratio &gt; 2.0, a value rarely seen in other liver diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup> In the United States, 2/5 of cirrhosis-related deaths are due to alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_steatohepatitis" title="Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis">Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis</a> (NASH). In NASH, fat builds up in the liver and eventually causes scar tissue. This type of hepatitis appears to be associated with obesity (40% of NASH patients) diabetes, protein malnutrition, coronary artery disease, and treatment with corticosteroid medications. This disorder is similar to that of alcoholic liver disease but the patient does not have an alcohol history. A biopsy is needed for diagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Chronic <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C" title="Hepatitis C">hepatitis C</a>. Infection with the <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C_virus" title="Hepatitis C virus">hepatitis C virus</a> causes inflammation of the liver and a variable grade of damage to the organ. Over several decades this inflammation and grade change can lead to cirrhosis. Among patients with chronic hepatitis C, 20-30% will develop cirrhosis. Host risk factors include human promoter polymorphisms such as TGF-β1 and angiotensin as well as host immune phenotype variations, such as immunosuppressed patients .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Cirrhosis caused by hepatitis C and alcoholic liver disease are the most common reasons for liver transplant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Can be diagnosed with serologic assays that detect hepatitis C antibody or viral RNA. The enzyme immunoassay, EIA-2, is the most commonly used screening test in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Chronic <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B" title="Hepatitis B">hepatitis B</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B_virus" title="Hepatitis B virus">hepatitis B virus</a> causes liver inflammation and injury that over several decades can lead to cirrhosis. Hepatitis D is dependent on the presence of hepatitis B and accelerates cirrhosis in co-infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup> Chronic hepatitis B can be diagnosed with detection of HBsAg &gt; 6 months after initial infection. HBeAg and HBV DNA are determined to assess whether patient needs antiviral therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Primary_biliary_cirrhosis" title="Primary biliary cirrhosis">Primary biliary cirrhosis</a>. Damage of the bile ducts leading to secondary liver damage. May be asymptomatic or complain of fatigue, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pruritus" title="Pruritus">pruritus</a>, and non-jaundice skin hyperpigmentation with hepatomegaly. There is prominent alkaline phosphatase elevation as well as elevations in cholesterol and bilirubin. Gold standard diagnosis is antimitochondrial antibodies (positive in 90% of PBC patients). Liver biopsy if done shows bile duct lesions. It is more common in women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Primary_sclerosing_cholangitis" title="Primary sclerosing cholangitis">Primary sclerosing cholangitis</a>. PSC is a progressive cholestatic disorder presenting with pruritus, <a href="/wiki/Steatorrhea" title="Steatorrhea">steatorrhea</a>, fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies, and metabolic bone disease. There is a strong association with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), especially ulcerative colitis. Diagnosis is best with contrast cholangiography showing diffuse, multifocal strictures and focal dilation of <a href="/wiki/Bile_duct" title="Bile duct">bile ducts</a>, leading to a beaded appearance. Non-specific serum immunoglobulins may also be elevated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_hepatitis" title="Autoimmune hepatitis">Autoimmune hepatitis</a>. This disease is caused by the immunologic damage to the liver causing inflammation and eventually scarring and cirrhosis. Findings include elevations in serum globulins, especially gamma globulins. Therapy with <a href="/wiki/Prednisone" title="Prednisone">prednisone</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Azathioprine" title="Azathioprine">azathioprine</a> is beneficial. Cirrhosis due to autoimmune hepatitis still has 10-year survival of 80+ %.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-12"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary_hemochromatosis" title="Hereditary hemochromatosis">Hereditary hemochromatosis</a>. Usually presents with a family history of cirrhosis, skin hyperpigmentation, diabetes mellitus, pseudogout, and/or cardiomyopathy, all due to signs of <a href="/wiki/Iron_overload" title="Iron overload">iron overload</a>. Labs show fasting transferrin saturation of &gt; 60% and ferritin &gt; 300 ng/ml.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Genetic testing may be used to identify HFE mutations. If these mutations are present, a biopsy may not need to be performed. Treatment is with <a href="/wiki/Venipuncture" title="Venipuncture">phlebotomy</a> to lower total body iron levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-13"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_disease" title="Wilson's disease">Wilson's disease</a>. Autosomal recessive disorder characterized by low serum <a href="/wiki/Ceruloplasmin" title="Ceruloplasmin">ceruloplasmin</a> and increased hepatic copper content on liver biopsy and elevated 24-hour urine copper. May also have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kayser-Fleischer_rings" title="Kayser-Fleischer rings">Kayser-Fleischer rings</a> in the cornea and altered mental status. This condition affects 1 in 30,000 people.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Indian_childhood_cirrhosis" title="Indian childhood cirrhosis">Indian childhood cirrhosis</a> is a form of neonatal cholestasis characterized by deposition of copper in the liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9587155_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9587155-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alpha_1-antitrypsin_deficiency" title="Alpha 1-antitrypsin deficiency">Alpha 1-antitrypsin deficiency</a> (A1AD). Autosomal recessive disorder of decreased levels of the enzyme alpha 1--antitrypsin. Patients may also have <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">COPD</a>, especially if they have a history of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco smoking</a>. Serum AAT levels are low and liver biopsy is positive for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_acid-Schiff" title="Periodic acid-Schiff">Periodic acid-Schiff</a> Recombinant AAT is used to prevent lung disease due to AAT deficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-14"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_cirrhosis" title="Cardiac cirrhosis">Cardiac cirrhosis</a>. Due to chronic right sided heart failure which leads to liver congestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-15"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Galactosemia" title="Galactosemia">Galactosemia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease_type_IV" title="Glycogen storage disease type IV">Glycogen storage disease type IV</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">Cystic fibrosis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section_13-16"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison.27s_Cirrhosis_Section-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hepatotoxicity#Specific_drug_or_toxin" title="Hepatotoxicity">Hepatotoxic drugs or toxins</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The liver plays a vital role in synthesis of proteins (for example, <a href="/wiki/Serum_albumin" title="Serum albumin">albumin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">clotting factors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Complement_system" title="Complement system">complement</a>), detoxification, and storage (for example, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a>). In addition, it participates in the metabolism of <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.</p>
<p>Cirrhosis is often preceded by hepatitis and fatty liver (steatosis), independent of the cause. If the cause is removed at this stage, the changes are still fully reversible.</p>
<p>The pathological hallmark of cirrhosis is the development of scar tissue that replaces normal <a href="/wiki/Parenchyma" title="Parenchyma">parenchyma</a>. This scar tissue blocks the portal flow of blood through the organ therefore disturbing normal function. Recent research shows the pivotal role of the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_stellate_cell" title="Hepatic stellate cell">stellate cell</a>, a cell type that normally stores <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a>, in the development of cirrhosis. Damage to the hepatic parenchyma (due to inflammation) leads to activation of the stellate cell, which increases fibrosis (through production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myofibroblasts" title="Myofibroblasts">myofibroblasts</a>) and obstructs blood flow in the circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Path._of_cirrhosis_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Path._of_cirrhosis-24">[24]</a></sup> In addition, it secretes <a href="/wiki/TGF_beta_1" title="TGF beta 1">TGF-β<sub>1</sub></a>, which leads to a fibrotic response and proliferation of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>. Furthermore, it secretes <a href="/wiki/Tissue_inhibitor_of_metalloproteinase" title="Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase">TIMP</a> 1 and 2, naturally occurring inhibitors of <a href="/wiki/Matrix_metalloproteinase" title="Matrix metalloproteinase">matrix metalloproteinases</a>, which prevents them from breaking down fibrotic material in the extracellular <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(biology)" title="Matrix (biology)">matrix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fibrous tissue bands (septa) separate hepatocyte nodules, which eventually replace the entire liver architecture, leading to decreased blood flow throughout. The <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> becomes congested, which leads to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersplenism" title="Hypersplenism">hypersplenism</a> and increased sequestration of <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelets</a>. Portal hypertension is responsible for most severe complications of cirrhosis.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Micrograph" title="Micrograph">Micrograph</a> showing cirrhosis. <a href="/wiki/Trichrome_stain" title="Trichrome stain">Trichrome stain</a>.</div>

</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard_(test)" title="Gold standard (test)">gold standard</a> for diagnosis of cirrhosis is a <a href="/wiki/Liver_biopsy" title="Liver biopsy">liver biopsy</a>, through a <a href="/wiki/Percutaneous" title="Percutaneous">percutaneous</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jugular" title="Jugular">transjugular</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laparoscopic" title="Laparoscopic">laparoscopic</a>, or fine-needle approach. A biopsy is not necessary if the clinical, laboratory, and radiologic data suggests cirrhosis. Furthermore, there is a small but significant risk to liver biopsy, and cirrhosis itself predisposes for complications caused by liver biopsy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The best predictors of cirrhosis are ascites, platelet count &lt;160,000/mm3, spider angiomata, and Bonacini cirrhosis discriminant score greater than 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lab_findings">Lab findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lab findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following findings are typical in cirrhosis:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thrombocytopenia" title="Thrombocytopenia">Thrombocytopenia</a> - typically multifactorial. Due to alcoholic marrow suppression, sepsis, lack of folate, sequestering in the spleen as well as decreased <a href="/wiki/Thrombopoietin" title="Thrombopoietin">thrombopoietin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup> However, this rarely results in platelet count &lt; 50 000/mL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maddrey_1999_Evaluation_of_the_Liver_A:_Laboratory_Test_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maddrey_1999_Evaluation_of_the_Liver_A:_Laboratory_Test-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aminotransferase" title="Aminotransferase">Aminotransferases</a> - AST and ALT are moderately elevated, with AST &gt; ALT. However, normal aminotransferases do not preclude cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Book._Current_Med_Diagnosis-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alkaline_phosphatase" title="Alkaline phosphatase">Alkaline phosphatase</a> - slightly elevated but less than 2-3 times the upper limit of normal.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-glutamyl_transferase" title="Gamma-glutamyl transferase">Gamma-glutamyl transferase</a> – correlates with AP levels. Typically much higher in chronic liver disease from alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maddrey_1999_Evaluation_of_the_Liver_A:_Laboratory_Test_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maddrey_1999_Evaluation_of_the_Liver_A:_Laboratory_Test-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">Bilirubin</a> - Levels normal when compensated but may elevate as cirrhosis progresses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">Albumin</a> - levels fall as the synthetic function of the liver declines with worsening cirrhosis since albumin is exclusively synthesized in the liver</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prothrombin_time" title="Prothrombin time">Prothrombin time</a> - increases since the liver synthesizes clotting factors.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Globulin" title="Globulin">Globulins</a> - increased due to shunting of bacterial antigens away from the liver to lymphoid tissue.</li>
<li>Serum <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> - <a href="/wiki/Hyponatremia" title="Hyponatremia">hyponatremia</a> due to inability to excrete free water resulting from high levels of <a href="/wiki/Vasopressin" title="Vasopressin">ADH</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">aldosterone</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leukopenia" title="Leukopenia">Leukopenia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutropenia" title="Neutropenia">neutropenia</a> - due to splenomegaly with splenic margination.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">Coagulation</a> defects - the liver produces most of the coagulation factors and thus coagulopathy correlates with worsening liver disease.</li>
</ul>
<p>There is now a validated and patented combination of 6 of these markers as non-invasive biomarker of fibrosis (and so of cirrhosis): <a href="/wiki/FibroTest" title="FibroTest">FibroTest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18973844_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18973844-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other laboratory studies performed in newly diagnosed cirrhosis may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Serology for <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a> viruses, <a href="/wiki/Autoantibody" title="Autoantibody">autoantibodies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_antibody" title="Anti-nuclear antibody">ANA</a>, anti-smooth muscle, <a href="/wiki/Anti-mitochondrial_antibody" title="Anti-mitochondrial antibody">anti-mitochondria</a>, anti-LKM)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferritin" title="Ferritin">Ferritin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transferrin_saturation" title="Transferrin saturation">transferrin saturation</a>: markers of iron overload as in hemochromatosis, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceruloplasmin" title="Ceruloplasmin">ceruloplasmin</a>: markers of copper overload as in Wilson's disease</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunoglobulin" title="Immunoglobulin">Immunoglobulin</a> levels (IgG, IgM, IgA) - these immunoglobins are non-specific but may help in distinguishing various causes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">Cholesterol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_1-antitrypsin" title="Alpha 1-antitrypsin">Alpha 1-antitrypsin</a></li>
</ul>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">Ultrasound</a> is routinely used in the evaluation of cirrhosis. It may show a small and nodular liver in advanced cirrhosis along with increased echogenicity with irregular appearing areas. Other findings suggestive of cirrhosis in imaging are an enlarged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudate_lobe" title="Caudate lobe">caudate lobe</a>, widening of the liver fissures and enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>. An enlarged spleen (<a href="/wiki/Splenomegaly" title="Splenomegaly">splenomegaly</a>), which normally measures less than 11–12 cm in adults, is suggestive of cirrhosis with <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a> in the right clinical setting. Ultrasound may also screen for hepatocellular carcinoma, portal hypertension, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budd-Chiari_syndrome" title="Budd-Chiari syndrome">Budd-Chiari syndrome</a> (by assessing flow in the hepatic vein).</p>
<p>Cirrhosis is diagnosed with a variety of <a href="/wiki/Elastography" title="Elastography">elastography</a> techniques. Because a cirrhotic liver is generally stiffer than a healthy one, imaging the liver's stiffness can give diagnostic information about the location and severity of cirrhosis. Techniques used include <a href="/wiki/Transient_elastography" title="Transient elastography">transient elastography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_radiation_force_impulse_imaging" title="Acoustic radiation force impulse imaging">acoustic radiation force impulse imaging</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic_shear_imaging" title="Supersonic shear imaging">supersonic shear imaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_elastography" title="Magnetic resonance elastography">magnetic resonance elastography</a>. Compared to a biopsy, elastography can sample a much larger area and is painless. It shows reasonable correlation with the severity of cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other tests performed in particular circumstances include abdominal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">CT</a> and liver/bile duct <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a> (<a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_cholangiopancreatography" title="Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography">MRCP</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endoscopy">Endoscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Endoscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastroscopy" title="Gastroscopy">Gastroscopy</a> (endoscopic examination of the esophagus, stomach, and <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>) is performed in patients with established cirrhosis to exclude the possibility of esophageal varices. If these are found, prophylactic local therapy may be applied (sclerotherapy or banding) and <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">beta blocker</a> treatment may be commenced.</p>
<p>Rarely are diseases of the bile ducts, such as <a href="/wiki/Primary_sclerosing_cholangitis" title="Primary sclerosing cholangitis">primary sclerosing cholangitis</a>, causes of cirrhosis. Imaging of the bile ducts, such as <a href="/wiki/Endoscopic_retrograde_cholangiopancreatography" title="Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography">ERCP</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_cholangiopancreatography" title="Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography">MRCP</a> (MRI of biliary tract and pancreas) may aid in the diagnosis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathology">Pathology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pathology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="550" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg/220px-Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg/330px-Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg/440px-Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hepatocellular_carcinoma_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cirrhosis leading to hepatocellular carcinoma (autopsy specimen).</div>

<p>Macroscopically, the liver is initially enlarged, but with the progression of the disease, it becomes smaller. Its surface is irregular, the consistency is firm, and the color is often yellow (if associated <a href="/wiki/Steatosis" title="Steatosis">steatosis</a>). Depending on the size of the nodules there are three macroscopic types: micronodular, macronodular, and mixed cirrhosis. In micronodular form (Laennec's cirrhosis or portal cirrhosis) regenerating nodules are under 3 mm. In macronodular cirrhosis (post-necrotic cirrhosis), the nodules are larger than 3 mm. The mixed cirrhosis consists of nodules with different sizes.</p>
<p>However, cirrhosis is defined by its pathological features on microscopy: (1) the presence of regenerating nodules of hepatocytes and (2) the presence of <a href="/wiki/Fibrosis" title="Fibrosis">fibrosis</a>, or the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> between these nodules. The pattern of fibrosis seen can depend on the underlying insult that led to cirrhosis. Fibrosis can also proliferate even if the underlying process that caused it has resolved or ceased. The fibrosis in cirrhosis can lead to destruction of other normal tissues in the liver: including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinusoids" title="Sinusoids">sinusoids</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_of_Disse" title="Space of Disse">space of Disse</a>, and other vascular structures, which leads to altered resistance to blood flow in the liver and <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>As cirrhosis can be caused by many different entities which injure the liver in different ways, cause specific abnormalities may be seen. For example, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_hepatitis_B" title="Chronic hepatitis B">chronic hepatitis B</a>, there is infiltration of the liver parenchyma with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphocytes" title="Lymphocytes">lymphocytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada-32">[32]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_cirrhosis" title="Cardiac cirrhosis">cardiac cirrhosis</a> there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erythrocytes" title="Erythrocytes">erythrocytes</a> and a greater amount of fibrosis in the tissue surrounding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatic_vein" title="Hepatic vein">hepatic veins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CardiacCirrhosis_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-CardiacCirrhosis-33">[33]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Primary_biliary_cirrhosis" title="Primary biliary cirrhosis">primary biliary cirrhosis</a>, there is fibrosis around the bile duct, the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granulomas" title="Granulomas">granulomas</a> and pooling of <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heathcote_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heathcote-34">[34]</a></sup> Lastly in alcoholic cirrhosis, there is infiltration of the liver with <a href="/wiki/Neutrophil" title="Neutrophil">neutrophils</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grading">Grading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Grading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The severity of cirrhosis is commonly classified with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child-Pugh_score" title="Child-Pugh score">Child-Pugh score</a>. This score uses <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_serum_albumin" title="Human serum albumin">albumin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prothrombin_time" title="Prothrombin time">INR</a>, presence and severity of <a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">ascites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_encephalopathy" title="Hepatic encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a> to classify patients in class A, B, or C. Class A has a favourable prognosis, while class C is at high risk of death. It was devised in 1964 by Child and Turcotte and modified in 1973 by Pugh and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>More modern scores, used in the allocation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liver_transplant" title="Liver transplant">liver transplants</a> but also in other contexts, are the <a href="/wiki/Model_for_End-Stage_Liver_Disease" title="Model for End-Stage Liver Disease">Model for End-Stage Liver Disease</a> (MELD) score and its pediatric counterpart, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pediatric_End-Stage_Liver_Disease" title="Pediatric End-Stage Liver Disease">Pediatric End-Stage Liver Disease</a> (PELD) score.</p>
<p>The hepatic venous pressure gradient, (difference in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venous_pressure" title="Venous pressure">venous pressure</a> between afferent and efferent blood to the liver) also determines severity of cirrhosis, although hard to measure. A value of 16 mm or more means a greatly increased risk of dying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Key prevention strategies for cirrhosis and its compensation are population-wide interventions to reduce alcohol intake (through pricing strategies, public health campaigns, and personal counseling), programs to reduce the transmission of viral hepatitis, and screening of relatives of people with hereditary liver diseases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Little is known on modulators of cirrhosis risk and progression. <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">Coffee</a> consumption appears to help protect against cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally, liver damage from cirrhosis cannot be reversed, but treatment could stop or delay further progression and reduce complications. A healthy diet is encouraged, as cirrhosis may be an energy-consuming process. Close follow-up is often necessary. Antibiotics are prescribed for infections, and various medications can help with itching. Laxatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Lactulose" title="Lactulose">lactulose</a>, decrease the risk of constipation; their role in preventing encephalopathy is limited.</p>
<p>Alcoholic cirrhosis caused by alcohol abuse is treated by abstaining from alcohol. Treatment for hepatitis-related cirrhosis involves medications used to treat the different types of hepatitis, such as interferon for viral hepatitis and corticosteroids for autoimmune hepatitis. Cirrhosis caused by <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_disease" title="Wilson's disease">Wilson's disease</a>, in which copper builds up in organs, is treated with <a href="/wiki/Chelation_therapy" title="Chelation therapy">chelation therapy</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Penicillamine" title="Penicillamine">penicillamine</a>) to remove the copper.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preventing_further_liver_damage">Preventing further liver damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Preventing further liver damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regardless of the underlying cause of cirrhosis, alcohol and <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">paracetamol</a>, as well as other potentially damaging substances, are discouraged. Vaccination of susceptible patients should be considered for <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">Hepatitis A</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B" title="Hepatitis B">Hepatitis B</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transplantation">Transplantation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Transplantation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liver_transplantation" title="Liver transplantation">Liver transplantation</a></div>
<p>If complications cannot be controlled or when the liver ceases functioning, <a href="/wiki/Liver_transplantation" title="Liver transplantation">liver transplantation</a> is necessary. Survival from liver transplantation has been improving over the 1990s, and the five-year survival rate is now around 80%. The survival rate depends largely on the severity of disease and other medical problems in the recipient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Model_for_End-Stage_Liver_Disease" title="Model for End-Stage Liver Disease">MELD score</a> is used to prioritize patients for transplantation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamath_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamath-40">[40]</a></sup> Transplantation necessitates the use of immune suppressants (<a href="/wiki/Ciclosporin" title="Ciclosporin">ciclosporin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tacrolimus" title="Tacrolimus">tacrolimus</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decompensated_cirrhosis">Decompensated cirrhosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Decompensated cirrhosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In patients with previously stable cirrhosis, decompensation may occur due to various causes, such as <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> (of any source), increased alcohol intake, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">medication</a>, bleeding from esophageal varices or dehydration. It may take the form of any of the complications of cirrhosis listed below.</p>
<p>Patients with decompensated cirrhosis generally require admission to <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospital</a>, with close monitoring of the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_balance" title="Fluid balance">fluid balance</a>, mental status, and emphasis on adequate nutrition and medical treatment - often with <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxatives</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Enema" title="Enema">enemas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">thiamine</a> and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Glucocorticoid" title="Glucocorticoid">steroids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetylcysteine" title="Acetylcysteine">acetylcysteine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentoxifylline" title="Pentoxifylline">pentoxifylline</a>. Administration of <a href="/wiki/Saline_(medicine)" title="Saline (medicine)">saline</a> is avoided as it would add to the already high total body sodium content that typically occurs in cirrhosis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palliative_care">Palliative care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Palliative care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palliative_care" title="Palliative care">Palliative care</a> is specialized medical care that focuses on providing patients with relief from the symptoms, pain, and stress of a serious illness, such as cirrhosis. The goal of palliative care is to improve quality of life for both the patient and the patient's family and it is appropriate at any stage and for any type of cirrhosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Especially in the later stages, people with cirrhosis experience significant symptoms such as abdominal swelling, itching, leg edema, and chronic abdominal pain which would be amenable for treatment through palliative care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanchez_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanchez-42">[42]</a></sup> Because the disease is not curable without a transplant, palliative care can also help with discussions regarding the person's wishes concerning health care <a href="/wiki/Power_of_attorney" title="Power of attorney">power of attorney</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Do_Not_Resuscitate" title="Do Not Resuscitate">Do Not Resuscitate</a> decisions and life support, and potentially <a href="/wiki/Hospice" title="Hospice">hospice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanchez_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sanchez-42">[42]</a></sup> Despite proven benefit, people with cirrhosis are rarely referred to palliative care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascites">Ascites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ascites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Salt restriction is often necessary, as cirrhosis leads to accumulation of salt (sodium retention). <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">Diuretics</a> may be necessary to suppress <a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">ascites</a>. Diuretic options for inpatient treatment include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldosterone_antagonist" title="Aldosterone antagonist">aldosterone antagonists</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spironolactone" title="Spironolactone">spironolactone</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Loop_diuretic" title="Loop diuretic">loop diuretics</a>. Aldosterone antagonists are preferred for people who can take oral medications and are not in need of an urgent volume reduction. Loop diuretics can be added as additional therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSGascites_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSGascites-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>If a rapid reduction of volume is required, <a href="/wiki/Paracentesis" title="Paracentesis">paracentesis</a> is the preferred option. This procedure requires the insertion of a plastic tube into the peritoneal cavity. Human albumin solution is usually given to prevent complications from the rapid reduction. In addition to being more rapid than diuretics, 4–5 liters of paracentesis is more successful in comparison to diuretic therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSGascites_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-BSGascites-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Esophageal_variceal_bleeding">Esophageal variceal bleeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Esophageal variceal bleeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For portal hypertension, nonselective <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">beta blockers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Propranolol" title="Propranolol">propranolol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nadolol" title="Nadolol">nadolol</a> are commonly used to lower blood pressure over the portal system. In severe complications from portal hypertension, <a href="/wiki/Transjugular_intrahepatic_portosystemic_shunt" title="Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt">transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunting</a> (TIPS) is occasionally indicated to relieve pressure on the portal vein. As this shunting can worsen hepatic encephalopathy, it is reserved for those patients at low risk of encephalopathy. TIPS is generally regarded only as a bridge to liver transplantation or as a palliative measure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hepatic_encephalopathy">Hepatic encephalopathy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hepatic encephalopathy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High-protein food increases the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_balance" title="Nitrogen balance">nitrogen balance</a>, and would theoretically increase <a href="/wiki/Encephalopathy" title="Encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a>; in the past, this was therefore eliminated as much as possible from the diet. Recent studies show that this assumption was incorrect, and high-protein foods are even <i>encouraged</i> to maintain adequate nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sundaram_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sundaram-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hepatorenal_syndrome">Hepatorenal syndrome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Hepatorenal syndrome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hepatorenal_syndrome" title="Hepatorenal syndrome">hepatorenal syndrome</a> is defined as a urine sodium less than 10 mmol/L and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serum_creatinine" title="Serum creatinine">serum creatinine</a> &gt; 1.5 mg/dl (or 24 hour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatinine_clearance" title="Creatinine clearance">creatinine clearance</a> less than 40 ml/min) after a trial of volume expansion without diuretics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid3297907_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid3297907-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spontaneous_bacterial_peritonitis">Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People with <a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">ascites</a> due to cirrhosis are at risk of <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_bacterial_peritonitis" title="Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis">spontaneous bacterial peritonitis</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_hypertensive_gastropathy">Portal hypertensive gastropathy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Portal hypertensive gastropathy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Which refers to changes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mucosa" title="Mucosa">mucosa</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> in people with portal hypertension, and is associated with cirrhosis severity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20407828_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20407828-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infection">Infection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Infection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cirrhosis can cause immune system dysfunction, leading to <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>. Signs and symptoms of infection may be nonspecific and are more difficult to recognize (for example, worsening encephalopathy but no fever).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hepatocellular_carcinoma">Hepatocellular carcinoma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Hepatocellular carcinoma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hepatocellular_carcinoma" title="Hepatocellular carcinoma">Hepatocellular carcinoma</a> is a primary liver cancer that is more common in people with cirrhosis. People with known cirrhosis are often screened intermittently for early signs of this tumor, and screening has been shown to improve outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cirrhosis and chronic liver disease were the tenth leading cause of death for men and the twelfth for women in the United States in 2001, killing about 27,000 people each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Also, the cost of cirrhosis in terms of human suffering, hospital costs, and lost productivity is high. Cirrhosis is more common in men than in women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Established cirrhosis has a 10-year mortality of 34–66%, largely dependent on the cause of the cirrhosis; alcoholic cirrhosis has a worse prognosis than primary biliary cirrhosis and cirrhosis due to hepatitis. The risk of death due to all causes is increased twelvefold; if one excludes the direct consequences of the liver disease, there is still a fivefold increased risk of death in all disease categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrhosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "cirrhosis" is a neologism derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>kirrhós</i> meaning "yellowish, tawny" (the orange-yellow colour of the diseased liver) and the suffix <i>-osis</i>, i.e. "condition" in medical terminology. While the clinical entity was known before, it was <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Laennec" title="René Laennec">René Laennec</a> who gave it this name (in the same 1819 work in which he also described the <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
