<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ganguro</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><i><b>Ganguro</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ガングロ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is an alternative fashion trend among young Japanese women that started in the mid-1990s, distinguished by a dark tan and contrasting make-up liberally applied by fashionistas.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shibuya,_Tokyo" title="Shibuya, Tokyo">Shibuya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ikebukuro" title="Ikebukuro">Ikebukuro</a> districts of Tokyo were the centres of <i>ganguro</i> fashion; it was started by rebellious youth who contradicted the traditional Japanese concept of beauty; pale skin, dark hair and neutral makeup tones. <i>Ganguro</i> instead tanned their skin, bleached their hair and used a lot of colourful makeup in unusual ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Ganguro</i> has a connection to <a href="/wiki/Japanese_folklore" title="Japanese folklore">Japanese folklore</a> of ghosts and demons who are depicted with a similar appearance such as those in <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noh" title="Noh">noh</a> costumes. This connection is further underlined by the offshoot style <i>yamanba</i>, named after a <a href="/wiki/Yama-uba" title="Yama-uba">mountain witch</a> in Japanese folklore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmrlsi1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-jmrlsi1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>ganguro</i> trend started in the mid-1990s and reached its peak by the latter half of the decade; it purportedly became almost obsolete by 2000 when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bihaku" title="Bihaku">bihaku</a> craze emerged among young women who wanted to imitate the look of their favourite popular singers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryuukou-maro.net_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ryuukou-maro.net-3">[3]</a></sup> specifically <a href="/wiki/Ayumi_Hamasaki" title="Ayumi Hamasaki">Ayumi Hamasaki</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.A7.AB.E5.B3.B6_2001_page_86_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.A7.AB.E5.B3.B6_2001_page_86-4">[4]</a></sup> who debuted at the time. The <i>ganguro</i> trend faded out afterwards, although its influence can be observed in <i>yamanba</i> and <i>manba</i> styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganguro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ganguro appeared as a new fashion style in Japan in the early 1990s and was prevalent mostly among young women. In <i>ganguro</i> fashion, a deep tan is combined with hair dyed in shades of orange to blonde, or a silver grey known as "high bleached". Black ink is used as eye-liner and white concealer is used as lipstick and eyeshadow. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_eyelashes" title="False eyelashes">False eyelashes</a>, plastic facial gems, and <a href="/wiki/Pearl_powder" title="Pearl powder">pearl powder</a> are often added to this. <a href="/wiki/Platform_shoe" title="Platform shoe">Platform shoes</a> and brightly coloured outfits complete the <i>ganguro</i> look. Also typical of <i>ganguro</i> fashion are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tie_dye" title="Tie dye">tie-dyed</a> <a href="/wiki/Sarong" title="Sarong">sarongs</a>, miniskirts, stickers on the face, and many bracelets, rings, and necklaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmrlsi1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-jmrlsi1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ganguro falls into the larger subculture of <i><a href="/wiki/Gyaru" title="Gyaru">gyaru</a></i> (from English "gal"), a slang term used for various groups of young women, usually referring to overly childish women. Researchers in the field of Japanese studies believe that <i>ganguro</i> is a form of revenge against traditional Japanese society due to resentment of neglect, isolation, and constraint of Japanese society. This is their attempt at individuality, self-expression, and freedom, in open defiance of school standards and regulations.</p>
<p><i>Ganguro</i> can be used to describe girls, or gals, with a tan, lightened hair and some brand clothing; they can often be confused with <i>Oneegyaru</i> (Big Sister Gal) and <i>Serebu</i> (Celeb), although <i>Oneegyaru</i> is usually associated with expensive gal brands and <i>Serebu</i> focuses on expensive western fashions.</p>
<p>Fashion magazines like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_magazine" title="Egg magazine">Egg</a></i> and <i>Ageha</i> have had a direct influence on the <i>ganguro</i>. Other popular <i>ganguro</i> magazines include <i><a href="/wiki/Popteen" title="Popteen">Popteen</a></i> and <i>Ego System</i>. The <i>ganguro</i> culture is often linked with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Para_para" title="Para para">para para</a></i>, a Japanese dance style. However, most para para dancers are not ganguro, and most ganguro are not para para dancers, though there are many who are ganguro or gal and dance para para.</p>
<p>One of the most famous early <i>ganguro</i> girls was known as Buriteri, nicknamed after the black soy sauce used to flavor yellowtail fish in teriyaki cooking. <i>Egg</i> made her a star by frequently featuring her in its pages during the height of the <i>ganguro</i> craze. After modelling and advertising for the Shibuya tanning salon "Blacky", social pressure and negative press convinced Buriteri to retire from the <i>ganguro</i> lifestyle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yamanba_and_Manba">Yamanba and Manba <span id="Yamanba"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganguro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Yamanba and Manba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><i>Yamanba</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ヤマンバ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and <i>manba</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">マンバ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> are styles which developed from <i>ganguro</i>. Old school <i>yamanba</i> and <i>manba</i> (particularly known as 2004 Manba) featured dark tans and white lipstick, pastel eye make-up, tiny metallic or glittery adhesives below the eyes, brightly coloured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circle_lenses" title="Circle lenses">circle lenses</a>, plastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dayglo" title="Dayglo">dayglo</a>-coloured clothing, and incongruous accessories, such as Hawaiian <i>leis</i>. Stickers on the face died out shortly after 2004 and, for a while, <i>yamanba</i> died. <i>Manba</i> then became more extreme, with multicoloured and usually synthetic hair. <i>Manba</i> in 2008 saw a darker tan, and no facial stickers. Hair was usually neon/bright colours, with pink being a favourite. Wool emulating dreadlocks, extensions, and clips were worn to make hair appear longer. Clothing remained the same, although <i>leis</i> were worn less frequently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmrlsi1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-jmrlsi1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yamanba and manba are distinct from one another. Yamanba involves white make-up only above the eye, while manba makeup is applied below the eye also.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Stuffed animals, bracelets, bells and hibiscus flowers are worn.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The male equivalent is called a "Center guy",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a pun on the name of a popular pedestrian shopping street near <a href="/wiki/Shibuya_Station" title="Shibuya Station">Shibuya Station</a> in Tokyo called <a href="/wiki/Center_Gai" title="Center Gai">Center Gai</a> センター街 <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">Sentā-gai</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganguro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ganguro practitioners say that the term derives from the phrase <i>ganganguro</i> or "gangankuro" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ガンガン黒</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, exceptionally dark)</span>. The word <i>ganguro</i> can be translated as "burn-black look", "dark tanning", "black-face".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>yamanba</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Yama-uba" title="Yama-uba">Yama-uba</a>, the name of a mountain hag in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_folklore" title="Japanese folklore">Japanese folklore</a> whom the fashion is thought to resemble.</p>
