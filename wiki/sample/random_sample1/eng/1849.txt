<p><b>Jiandao</b> or <b>Chientao</b>, known in Korean as <b>Gando</b> or <b>Kando</b>, refers to a small piece of marsh land between the <a href="/wiki/Yanbian_Korean_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture">Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helong" title="Helong">Helong</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>. The original Chinese name of Jiandao is Jiajiang (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">假江</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Jiájiāng</span></i>).</p>
<p>Another way to define Jiandao was provided by the Imperial Japan in early 20th century. An expanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_empire" title="Japanese empire">Japanese empire</a> claimed that Jiandao included territory of four counties (<a href="/wiki/Yanji" title="Yanji">Yanji</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wangqing" title="Wangqing">Wangqing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helong" title="Helong">Helong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunchun" title="Hunchun">Hunchun</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> province and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Koreans_in_China" title="Ethnic Koreans in China">ethnic Koreans</a> living in this region should be placed under its influence, which would give Japan a chance to impose colonial jurisdiction on the region. As one of its first set of attempts to annex northeast China and conquer other parts of mainland China, Imperial Japanese forces in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> invaded Jiandao in 1907, but Japan withdrew its forces to Korea in 1909 and recognized the border that was present along <a href="/wiki/Tumen_River" title="Tumen River">Tumen River</a> before the invasion, under diplomatic pressure from China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the region defined by Japan in early 20th century as Jiandao is part of <a href="/wiki/Yanbian_Korean_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture">Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture</a>, a part of <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> Province in the northeast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC). The area of the prefecture is approximately 42,000 square kilometers in size and home to about 810,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Koreans_in_China" title="Ethnic Koreans in China">ethnic Koreans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In China, Yanbian is the name used, and Jiandao is not used, due to its association with Japanese colonial occupation. Both <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> recognize the region as a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, but there are some nationalist elements in South Korea that endorse the idea that the region should be a part of modern-day Korea. These groups claim what happened in Jiandao between 1907–1909 (Japan's invasion and subsequent withdrawal) was an "illegal transfer" of "Korean territory" between Japan and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiandao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many different states and tribes succeeded each other in ruling the area during ancient times. These included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buyeo_kingdom" title="Buyeo kingdom">Buyeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> and Goguryeo's successor state <a href="/wiki/Balhae" title="Balhae">Balhae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> was one of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms of Korea</a> and controlled northern Korea and southern Manchuria. Balhae was a state that existed in the area during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> in China and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Silla" title="Unified Silla">Unified Silla</a> Period in Korea. China emphasizes Balhae's temporary tributary relationship to the Tang, while Korea emphasizes that Balhae was a successor state and a cultural extension of Goguryeo.</p>
<p>Balhae was destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liao_Dynasty" title="Liao Dynasty">Liao Dynasty</a> in 926, and was formally annexed in 936. Over the next nine to ten centuries the region was administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liao_Dynasty" title="Liao Dynasty">Liao Dynasty</a> (Khitans), <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115%E2%80%931234)" title="Jin dynasty (1115–1234)">Jin dynasty</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchens</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongols</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> and finally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a>, which was established by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchu" title="Manchu">Manchus</a>. Eventually, the Qing dynasty succeeded in unifying <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> by replacing the Ming Dynasty.</p>
<p>In 1712, the border between Qing and Joseon was formally demarcated. For years, <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing</a> officials did not allow people to move to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">northeast China</a>, as it believed that should a <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a> majority government rise again in parts of China south of the Great Wall, the Manchus could retreat to their original homeland. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon</a> officials also did not allow its subjects to move to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">northeast China</a>. These governmental regulations, with the general marshy nature of the area, left these lands north of the <a href="/wiki/Tumen_River" title="Tumen River">Tumen River</a> relatively undeveloped and the region was sparsely populated by Manchu tribes for a long time. Qing officials regularly inspected this region and occasional Korean intruders were detained and sent back to Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, by the late 19th century, peasants in northern Korea migrated to northeast China to flee famine and poverty. Even more arrived as refugees when Japan invaded Korea in 1894.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, Japan began the process that led to the formal annexation of Korea. In 1905, the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a> became a <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Imperial Japan</a>, effectively losing diplomatic rights, and became a part of the Imperial Japan in 1910. In the early 20th century, Korean immigration to Manchuria steadily increased, either by refugees fleeing from Japanese rule, or from encouragement by the Japanese government for people to develop the land. Some local Chinese governments welcomed the Korean immigrants, as they were a source of labor and agricultural skill.</p>
<p>In the meantime, Japan began to expand into northeast China. One of the regions the Japanese targeted was Jiandao (known in Korean as Gando). Rather than being a small piece of marsh land, the Japanese claimed that Jiandao included territory of four counties (Yanji, Wangqing, Helong and Hunchun) of <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> province. The Japanese further claimed ethnic Koreans living in this region should be placed under the influence of Imperial Japan.</p>
<p>The Japanese first infiltrated Jiandao in April 1907 to collect information and data. On August 7, 1907, Japanese troops invaded Jiandao and claimed that the "Jiandao Issue" was "unsettled". (see reference: Jiandao Incident)</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Gando_Convention" title="Gando Convention">Jiandao Convention</a> of 1909, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> affirmed territorial rights of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing</a> over Jiandao after the Chinese foreign ministry issued a thirteen-point refutation statement regarding its rightful ownership. Japan agreed to withdraw its invading troops back to Korea in two months. The treaty also contained provisions for the protection and rights of ethnic Koreans under Chinese rule. Nevertheless, there were large <a href="/wiki/Koreans" title="Koreans">Korean</a> settlements and the area remained under significant Japanese influence.</p>
<p>Despite the agreement, Koreans in Jiandao continued to be a source of friction between the Chinese and Japanese governments. Japan maintained that all ethnic Koreans were Japanese nationals, subject to Japanese jurisdiction and law, and demanded rights to patrol and police the area. The Qing and subsequent local Chinese governments insisted on its territorial sovereignty over the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esselstrom_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-esselstrom-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Mukden_Incident" title="Mukden Incident">Mukden Incident</a> of 1931, the Japanese military (the Kwantung Army) invaded Manchuria. Between 1931 and 1945, <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> was under the control of <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo" title="Manchukuo">Manchukuo</a>, a Japanese puppet state. Jiandao was a province of Manchukuo. This period initiated a new wave of Korean immigration, as the Japanese government actively encouraged (or forced) Korean settlement in order to colonize and develop the region. The Japanese also moved to suppress resistance in the region. Within three and half years (from Sept 1931 to March 1935), Japanese regular forces and police murdered 4520 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>(also see reference: Jiandao Incident) During and after the 1930s, many ethnic Koreans in the region joined and participated in the Chinese Communist Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 1938, a counterinsurgency unit called the <a href="/wiki/Gando_Special_Force" title="Gando Special Force">Gando Special Force</a> was organized by the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Kwantung_Army" title="Kwantung Army">Kwantung Army</a> to combat communist guerrillas within the region. The top commander of this battalion-size force was Japanese. Historian Philip Jowett noted that during the Japanese occupation of Manchuria, the Gando Special Force had "earned a reputation for brutality and was reported to have laid waste to large areas which came under its rule."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the liberation of Korea, many Korean expatriates in the region moved back to Korea, but a significant majority still remained in Manchuria; descendants of these people form the Korean ethnic minority in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> today.</p>
<p>The area is now the <a href="/wiki/Yanbian_Korean_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture">Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin province</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_claims">Boundary claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiandao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Boundary claims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Korean claims over Gando stem from what is perceived as an ambiguity in the original Sino-Korean boundary agreement.</p>
<p>After several attempts by the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a> to negotiate the issue, in 1712, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon</a> of Korea and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing</a> of China agreed to delineate the boundaries of the two countries at the Yalu and Tumen Rivers. The Qing delegation was led by Mukedeng, and the Joseon delegation was led by Pak Kwon, and the two held a joint commission to survey and demarcate the boundaries between the two states. Efforts were taken to locate the sources of the Yalu and Tumen rivers at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baekdu_Mountain" title="Baekdu Mountain">Baekdu Mountain</a>. Owing to Pak's age, they agreed for Mukedeng's team to ascend the summit alone. Mukedeng's team quickly identified the source of the Yalu, but identification for the Tumen proved more complicated. At last a spot was decided, and a <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a> was erected as a boundary marker. Over the next year, a fence was built to demarcate the areas where the Tumen river ran underground.</p>
<p>Pak Kwon was instructed by the Joseon government to retain all territory south of the Yalu and Tumen rivers, a goal he accomplished. However, some Korean officials lamented the loss of claims on areas north of the river and criticized Pak Kwon for not accompanying Mukedeng to the summit. The territorial claims stem from the territories held by <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balhae" title="Balhae">Balhae</a>. (See history section regarding the description of these two states) Nonetheless, the border remained uncontentious for the next 150 years. Cross-border movements were forbidden, and was punishable by death after trespassers were detained and repatriated back to their respective countries.</p>
<p>In the 1870s the Qing government reversed its policy of prohibiting entry to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>, and began allowing <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> settlers into the territory in response to growing <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> encroachment. The area around Gando was opened up to settlement in 1881, but Chinese settlers quickly discovered some Korean farming communities already settled in the area. It was apparent that despite the decreed punishment, severe droughts in northern <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> had motivated Korean farmers to seek new lands. The <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> general-governor Ming-An's official response was to lodge a protest to the Joseon government and offer to allow the Korean population to stay if they agreed to become Manchu subjects and adopt Qing customs and dress. Joseon's response was to encourage the farmers not to register as Qing subjects but to return to Korea within the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The farmers, unwilling to abandon their homes, argued that because of the ambiguity in the naming of the Tumen river, they were actually already in Korean territory. The <a href="/wiki/Yalu_River" title="Yalu River">Yalu River</a> boundary is of little dispute, but the interpretation of the Tumen River boundary 土門 (토문) causes problems. The name of the river itself originates from the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_language" title="Jurchen language">Jurchen</a> word <i>tumen</i>, meaning "ten thousand". The official boundary agreement in 1712 identified the Tumen river using the characters 土門 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Tǔmen) for the phonetic transcription. However, the modern <a href="/wiki/Tumen_River" title="Tumen River">Tumen River</a> is written as 圖們 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Túmen) in modern Chinese and as 豆滿 (두만) "Duman" in both modern Korean and Japanese. Some Koreans hence claim that the "Tumen" referred to in the treaty is actually a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Songhua_River" title="Songhua River">Songhua River</a>. Under this interpretation, Gando (where the Koreans settled) would be part of Korean territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>This confusion arises as the two names sound identical, and neither name is of Chinese origin. The two rivers can be seen in the following map from the period. Korean claims are based on maps showing the border river as 土門 and the claim that this is a different river than the one used for the modern border. However, it is uncertain which modern river the Korean claim corresponds to, as there is no modern tributary of the Songhua River with that name:</p>

<p>This interpretation of the boundary gradually developed into Joseon official policy. O Yunjung, a Korean official appointed to review the claims made by the farmers and investigate the sources of the river, adopted the latter interpretation and declared that the region did not belong to China. Joseon and Qing officials met in 1885 and 1887 to resolve the dispute, but with little result. Korean officials suggested on starting from the stele and tracing the river downwards, while Qing officials proposed starting at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Tumen_River" title="Tumen River">Tumen River</a> and moving upstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref3-11">[11]</a></sup> From 1905 onwards, Korea came under the influence and control of Japan and was unable to effectively pursue these claims.</p>
<p>After the liberation of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> in 1945, many Koreans believed that Gando should be given to Korean rule, but the military control by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> in the south and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Socialist_Republics" title="Union of Soviet Socialist Republics">Union of Soviet Socialist Republics</a> in the north hindered any unified Korean claim to the territory. The chaos of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and the geopolitical situation of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> effectively diminished any opportunity for <a href="/wiki/Koreans" title="Koreans">Koreans</a> to highlight the Gando issue. In 1962, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> signed a boundary treaty with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> setting the Korean boundary at <a href="/wiki/Yalu_River" title="Yalu River">Yalu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tumen_River" title="Tumen River">Tumen</a>, effectively foregoing territorial claims to Gando. <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> also recognizes this as the boundary between Korea and China.</p>
<p>Today, none of the governments involved (<a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>) make the claim that Gando is Korean territory. In addition, there is very little enthusiasm for <a href="/wiki/Irredentism" title="Irredentism">irredentism</a> among the Korean minority in China. Although there are occasional arguments over historical interpretation, this issue arouses very little emotion or official interest on the part of any of the parties, and relations between China and both Koreas remain warm.</p>
<p>In 2004 the South Korean government issued a statement to the effect that it believed that the <a href="/wiki/Gando_Convention" title="Gando Convention">Gando Convention</a> was null and void. The resultant controversy and strong negative reaction from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">PRC</a> led to a retraction of the statement, along with an explanation that its issuance was an "administrative error."</p>
<p>A small number of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> activists believe that under a unified Korea, the treaties signed by <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> can be deemed null, allowing the unified Korea to actively seek regress for Gando. However, the current political situation make this a faint possibility at best. Also, some scholars claims that <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China's</a> efforts to incorporate the history of <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balhae" title="Balhae">Balhae</a> into Chinese history is an effectively pre-emptive move to squash any territorial disputes that might rise regarding Gando before a unified Korea can claim such or the Korean ethnic minority in the <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> region claim to become part of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>.</p>

Esselstrom (2000). <a class="external text" href="http://journals.cambridge.org/bin/bladerunner?REQUNIQ=1120901689&amp;REQSESS=5073687&amp;118200REQEVENT=&amp;REQINT1=288800&amp;REQAUTH=0" rel="nofollow">"Rethinking the Colonial Conquest of Manchuria: The Japanese Consular Police in Jiandao, 1909–1937"</a>. <i>Modern Asian Studies</i>. <b>39</b> (1): 39–75. <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">doi</a>:<a class="external text" href="//dx.doi.org/10.1017%2FS0026749X04001398" rel="nofollow">10.1017/S0026749X04001398</a>.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJiandao&amp;rft.atitle=Rethinking+the+Colonial+Conquest+of+Manchuria%3A+The+Japanese+Consular+Police+in+Jiandao%2C+1909%931937&amp;rft.au=Erik+W.+Esselstrom&amp;rft.date=2000&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fjournals.cambridge.org%2Fbin%2Fbladerunner%3FREQUNIQ%3D1120901689%26REQSESS%3D5073687%26118200REQEVENT%3D%26REQINT1%3D288800%26REQAUTH%3D0&amp;rft_id=info%3Adoi%2F10.1017%2FS0026749X04001398&amp;rft.issue=1&amp;rft.jtitle=Modern+Asian+Studies&amp;rft.pages=39-75&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.volume=39"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://wiki.chinabaike.com/index.php?doc-view-44215.html" rel="nofollow">"Jiandao Incident and Jiandao Convention 间岛事件和间岛协约"</a>.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJiandao&amp;rft.btitle=Jiandao+Incident+and+Jiandao+Convention+%E9%97%B4%E5%B2%9B%E4%BA%E4%BB%B6%E5%92%8C%E9%97%B4%E5%B2%9B%E5%8F%E7%BA%A6&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwiki.chinabaike.com%2Findex.php%3Fdoc-view-44215.html&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>

Jowett (2004). <i>Rays of the Rising Sun</i>. West Midlands: Helion &amp; Company Limited. p. 34.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJiandao&amp;rft.au=Philip+S.+Jowett&amp;rft.btitle=Rays+of+the+Rising+Sun&amp;rft.date=2004&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft.pages=34&amp;rft.place=West+Midlands&amp;rft.pub=Helion+%26+Company+Limited&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-ref1-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-ref1_9-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Schmid, pg. 226-227 The border did not become a bone of contention again until almost 150 years later—the second moment pointed out in Chang Chiyon’s work. In the 1870s Qing authorities began to open Manchuria, shut off from Han migration since the earliest years of the dynasty. In various stages between 1878 and 1906 the entire expanse of Manchuria opened to settlement; the Tumen River valley received its first legal Han settlers in 1881. When these Qing settlers arrived, however, they quickly discovered that many more Koreans had already begun farming much of the best land. By 1882 the presence of large Korean communities in the region came to the attention of the general of Jilin, Ming An, who proceeded to lodge a protest with the Choson court, laying down a number of conditions: so long as these Koreans paid taxes to the court, registered their households with local authorities, recognized the legal jurisdiction of the Jilin authorities, and shaved their heads in the Manchu style—in short, become Qing subjects—they were welcome to stay; otherwise they should return to Choson territory. Seoul responded by urging Ming An not to register their subjects, for within one year they would all be returned home—an agreement that seemed to accept Qing land claims. For the farmers themselves—people who had fled famine conditions and labored for more than ten years to bring land under cultivation—a move off the lands hardly proved a favorable scenario. Few left. By April of the following year the head of the Huichun Resettlement Bureau had again demanded of local Choson authorities that by the conclusion of the fall harvest the farmers be returned to the other side of the river.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-ref2-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-ref2_10-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Schmid, pg. 227 Their position centered on an interpretation of the stele erected by Mukedeng more than two centuries earlier. The farmers contended that they had never crossed any boundary and were in fact within Choson territory. Their argument skillfully played off the ambiguity surrounding the character engraved on the stele to represent the first syllable in the name of the Tumen River. They argued that Qing officials had failed to distinguish between two different rivers, both called something like Tumen but written with a different character signifying the first syllable. One, the character on the stele, indicated earth; the second, a character not on the stele, signified what today is considered the tu for Tumen River, meaning diagram. The river behind which the Qing officials demanded the farmers withdraw was the latter. As argued by the farmers, though the pronunciation was nearly identical, the different characters signified two distinct rivers. The first Tumen River delineated the northernmost extreme of Choson jurisdiction, while a second Tumen River flowed within Choson territory. Qing authorities mistakenly believed the two rivers were one and the same, the petition suggested, only because Chinese settlers had falsely accused the Korean farmers of crossing the border. In fact their homes were between the two rivers, meaning that they lived inside Choson boundaries. The way to substantiate their claims, they urged, was to conduct a survey of the Mt. Paektu stele, for in their opinion the stele alone could determine the boundary.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-ref3-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-ref3_11-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Schmid, pg. 227-228 At this time O Yunjung, who later became a famous reform official, was appointed as special inspector for the Northwest. Upon receiving his appointment, O informed the king in wonderful Confucian rhetoric that the farmers would “naturally return” as they learned of the king’s sagely virtue, but when he arrived at the frontier, he quickly learned sagely virtue was no match for land. He immediately heard the complaints of the farmers. In response, O undertook two investigations, the first to verify the position and text of the Mt. Paektu stele, the second to ascertain the sources of the river. The results of these efforts sufficiently confirmed the position of the farmers, and O, in an audience at court, confidently eased the king’s doubt about their claim to these lands. “That these lands are not the lands of China,” he stated, “is most clear.” From this point what had been a view circulating only at the local level among residents developed into official policy. By 1885 and 1887, when Choson and Qing delegates met along the border to survey the local topography with the hope of ending the disagreement, the Choson negotiators had adopted the interpretation of the stele as the basis of their negotiating stance. Start at the stele, they told their Qing counterparts, and trace the river downward from this point. The Qing side rejected this emphasis on the stele. Instead, the opposite method of locating the border was suggested: start at the mouth of the Tumen River and trace the river upstream, regardless of the positioning of the stele. A number of surveys were conducted, but more accurate information on the local topography did little to soften the opposing positions on determining the boundary.</span></li>
</ol>
