<p>A <b>spy ship</b> or reconnaissance vessel is a dedicated <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> intended to gather <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_(information_gathering)" title="Intelligence (information gathering)">intelligence</a>, usually by means of sophisticated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIGINT" title="SIGINT">electronic eavesdropping</a>. In a wider sense, any ship intended to gather information could be considered a spy ship.</p>
<p>Spy ships are usually controlled by a nation's <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, due to the high costs and advanced equipment required. They tend to be parts of the nation's <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navy</a>, though they may also be operated by <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">secret services</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_trawlers" title="Naval trawlers">Naval trawlers</a> masquerade as civilian ships such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_trawlers" title="Fishing trawlers">fishing trawlers</a>, which could be reasonably expected to remain in a certain area for a long time.</p>
<p>Ships which are used to infiltrate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy" title="Spy">spies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Special_forces" title="Special forces">special forces</a> are sometimes also called "spy ships".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spy ships in the modern sense have been used at least since the early <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and they are in use by all major powers. Their uses, in addition to listening in on communications and spy on enemy fleet movements, were to monitor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_test" title="Nuclear test">nuclear tests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> launches (especially of potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBMs</a>).</p>
<p>One of the most important functions for both Cold War spy ship fleets, especially in the 1960s, was the gathering of <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> "signatures" – the patterns of noise that could often identify the specific type of submarine and were thus valuable in <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a>. During that era, the USA fielded about 80 vessels, usually classified as "environmental research" craft, while the Soviets had around 60 ships, often converted trawlers or <a href="/wiki/Russian_Hydrographic_Service" title="Russian Hydrographic Service">hydrographic</a> research ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1980s, the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fisheries fleet</a> was known for having equipped many of their thousands of ships with sophisticated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIGINT" title="SIGINT">SIGINT</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ELINT" title="ELINT">ELINT</a> equipment, thus functioning as auxiliary spy ships tracking western naval vessels and electronic communications (though their main function remained commercial fishing).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USNS_Vanguard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="891" data-file-width="1263" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/USNS_Vanguard.jpg/220px-USNS_Vanguard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/USNS_Vanguard.jpg/330px-USNS_Vanguard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/USNS_Vanguard.jpg/440px-USNS_Vanguard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USNS_Vanguard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
While the <a href="/wiki/USNS_Mission_San_Fernando_(T-AO-122)" title="USNS Mission San Fernando (T-AO-122)">USNS <i>Vanguard</i></a> was not strictly a spy ship, being used for space tracking, there is some overlap between her capabilities and those of a spy ship.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A spy ship usually stays in <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (or at least outside territorial waters), so as to not violate territorial <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a>. From there, it will use its electronic equipment to monitor sea and air traffic, radio and radar<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-2">[2]</a></sup> frequencies and also try to intercept and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decrypt" title="Decrypt">decrypt</a> coded <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phone" title="Phone">phone</a> communications. This is mostly done via passive means such as <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> receivers or passive <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>. Sometimes however, active measures such as <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> may also be used to detect the movement of <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missiles" title="Missiles">missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> or other vehicles or troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, this risks revealing the ship's purpose.</p>
<p>As it is located much closer to the surveilled area than a fixed installation (given a close-by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoreline" title="Shoreline">shoreline</a>), the monitoring is usually much more efficient and in some respects better than even that of spy satellites.</p>
<p>Tracking vessels for space missions/control stations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">spy satellites</a> also have some of the capabilities of spy ships, and as they are controlled by their national governments, they are also intermittently used for similar purposes, such as tracking enemy missile tests.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_AGI_trawlers">Soviet AGI trawlers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soviet AGI trawlers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the United States Navy began deploying <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile submarines</a> in 1960, the Soviet Union attempted to obtain more information about the capabilities of the <a href="/wiki/UGM-27_Polaris" title="UGM-27 Polaris">UGM-27 Polaris</a> missile and the locations of the submarines capable of launching them. While the Soviet Navy requested more sophisticated ships, they were allocated trawlers (called <i>tral’shchiki</i>) from the fishing fleet equipped with more sophisticated sensors and communication equipment. Very capable crews were assigned to these little trawlers of unremarkable appearance, and they were assigned to patrol stations off United States Naval bases to photograph and report arrival and departure of United States warships and auxiliaries. Other trawlers of similar appearance would patrol weapons firing ranges used by the United States Navy to observe practice firings of modern weapons and record the acoustic and/or electromagnetic signature of the sonar, search radar, fire-control radar, guidance, and/or command electronics of each weapons system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enj_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-enj-8">[8]</a></sup> The United States Navy officially designated these trawlers as <b>Auxiliary, General Intelligence</b> or <b>AGI</b>, and they were informally known as <i>tattletales</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nip_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nip-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>An AGI might be assigned to a single patrol station for as long as six months before being relieved by a similar AGI. These AGIs were not fast enough to keep up with most warships, but they sometimes congregated around <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a> conducting <a href="/wiki/Modern_United_States_Navy_carrier_air_operations" title="Modern United States Navy carrier air operations">air operations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Sixth_Fleet" title="United States Sixth Fleet">United States Sixth Fleet</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_States_Seventh_Fleet" title="United States Seventh Fleet">United States Seventh Fleet</a> in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, or in suspected patrol areas of ballistic missile submarines. After the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> authorized a <i>counter AGI program</i> for United States <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a> to come alongside the AGIs to push against them, foul their <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">screws</a> with steel nets, and focus high power electromagnetic transmitters to burn out the amplifying circuitry of their electronic sensors. The AGI crews then revealed their ship-handling skills using superior maneuverability to evade the destroyers' intentions. This jousting in international waters continued until signing of the <a href="/wiki/U.S.%E2%80%93Soviet_Incidents_at_Sea_agreement" title="U.S.–Soviet Incidents at Sea agreement">U.S.–Soviet Incidents at Sea agreement</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enj_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-enj-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
