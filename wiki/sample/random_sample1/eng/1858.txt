<p><b>Simplified Chinese characters</b> (<span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AE%80%E5%8C%96%E5%AD%97" title="wikt:简化字">简化字</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">jiǎnhuàzì</span></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> are standardized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a> prescribed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Table_of_General_Standard_Chinese_Characters" title="Table of General Standard Chinese Characters">Table of General Standard Chinese Characters</a></i> for use in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>. Along with <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese characters</a>, it is one of the two standard character sets of the contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_written_language" title="Chinese written language">Chinese written language</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_China" title="Government of China">government of the People's Republic of China</a> in mainland China has promoted them for use in printing since the 1950s and 1960s in an attempt to increase literacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They are officially used in the People's Republic of China and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.</p>
<p>Traditional Chinese characters are currently used in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (<a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>). While traditional characters can still be read and understood by many mainland Chinese and the Chinese community in Malaysia and Singapore, these groups generally retain their use of Simplified characters. <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Overseas Chinese</a> communities generally tend to use traditional characters.</p>
<p>Simplified Chinese characters may be referred to by their official name above or colloquially (<span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AE%80%E4%BD%93%E5%AD%97" title="wikt:简体字">简体字</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Zh-jiantiz%C3%AC.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Zh-jiantiz%C3%AC.ogg" title="Zh-jiantizì.ogg">jiǎntǐzì</a></span></span></span></i>). Strictly, the latter refers to simplifications of character "structure" or "body", character forms that have existed for thousands of years alongside regular, more complicated forms. On the other hand, the official name refers to the modern systematically simplified character set, that (as stated by <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a> in 1952) includes not only structural simplification but also substantial reduction in the total number of standardized Chinese characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mao_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mao-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Simplified character forms were created by decreasing the number of strokes and simplifying the forms of a sizable proportion of traditional Chinese characters. Some simplifications were based on popular <a href="/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia)" title="Cursive script (East Asia)">cursive</a> forms embodying graphic or phonetic simplifications of the traditional forms. Some characters were simplified by applying regular rules, for example, by replacing all occurrences of a certain component with a simplified version of the component. Variant characters with the same pronunciation and identical meaning were reduced to a single standardized character, usually the simplest amongst all variants in form. Finally, many characters were left untouched by simplification, and are thus identical between the traditional and simplified Chinese orthographies.</p>
<p>Some simplified characters are very dissimilar to and unpredictably different from traditional characters, especially in those where a component is replaced by an arbitrary simple symbol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This often leads opponents not well-versed in the method of simplification to conclude that the 'overall process' of character simplification is also arbitrary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.BD.AD.E5.B0.8F.E6.98.8E_derivation_errors_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.BD.AD.E5.B0.8F.E6.98.8E_derivation_errors-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2015)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> In reality, the methods and rules of simplification are few and internally consistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On the other hand, proponents of simplification often flaunt a few choice simplified characters as ingenious inventions, when in fact these have existed for hundreds of years as ancient variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2015)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Second_round_of_simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Second round of simplified Chinese characters">second round of simplifications</a> was promulgated in 1977, but was later retracted in 1986 for a variety of reasons, but largely due to the confusion by and the unpopularity of the second round simplifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the Chinese government never officially dropped its goal of further simplification in the future.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In August 2009, the PRC began collecting public comments for a modified list of simplified characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The new <i><a href="/wiki/Table_of_General_Standard_Chinese_Characters" title="Table of General Standard Chinese Characters">Table of General Standard Chinese Characters</a></i> consisting of 8,105 (simplified and unchanged) characters was promulgated by the State Council of the People's Republic of China on June 5, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1949">Before 1949</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Before 1949">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although most of the simplified Chinese characters in use today are the result of the works moderated by the government of the People's Republic of China (PRC) in the 1950s and 60s, character simplification predates the PRC's formation in 1949. Cursive written text almost always includes character simplification. Simplified forms used in print have always existed; they date back to as early as the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> (221–206 BC).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ROC24_SC1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="624" height="423" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/ROC24_SC1.jpg/220px-ROC24_SC1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/ROC24_SC1.jpg/330px-ROC24_SC1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/ROC24_SC1.jpg/440px-ROC24_SC1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ROC24_SC1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The first batch of Simplified Characters introduced in 1935 consisted of 324 characters.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the earliest proponents of character simplification was <a href="/wiki/Lubi_Kui" title="Lubi Kui">Lubi Kui</a>, who proposed in 1909 that simplified characters should be used in education. In the years following the <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">May Fourth Movement</a> in 1919, many anti-imperialist Chinese intellectuals sought ways to modernise China. Traditional culture and values such as <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> were challenged. Soon, people in the Movement started to cite the traditional Chinese writing system as an obstacle in modernising China and therefore proposed that a reform be initiated. It was suggested that the Chinese writing system should be either simplified or completely abolished. <a href="/wiki/Fu_Sinian" title="Fu Sinian">Fu Sinian</a>, a leader of the May Fourth Movement, called Chinese characters the "writing of ox-demons and snake-gods" (<span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">牛鬼蛇神的文字</span>). <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">Lu Xun</a>, a renowned Chinese author in the 20th century, stated that, "If <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> are not destroyed, then China will die." (<span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">漢字不滅，中國必亡</span>) Recent commentators have claimed that Chinese characters were blamed for the economic problems in China during that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yen-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1930s and 1940s, discussions on character simplification took place within the <a href="/wiki/Kuomintang" title="Kuomintang">Kuomintang</a> government, and a large number of Chinese intellectuals and writers have long maintained that character simplification would help boost literacy in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1935, 324 simplified characters collected by <a href="/wiki/Qian_Xuantong" title="Qian Xuantong">Qian Xuantong</a> were officially introduced as the table of first batch simplified characters and suspended in 1936. In many world languages, literacy has been promoted as a justification for <a href="/wiki/Spelling_reform" title="Spelling reform">spelling reforms</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="People.27s_Republic_of_China">People's Republic of China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: People's Republic of China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The PRC issued its first round of official character simplifications in two documents, the first in 1956 and the second in 1964.</p>
<p>Within the PRC, further character simplification became associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leftists" title="Leftists">leftists</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>, culminating with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second-round_simplified_Chinese_character" title="Second-round simplified Chinese character">second-round simplified characters</a>, which were promulgated in 1977. In part due to the shock and unease felt in the wake of the Cultural Revolution and Mao's death, the second-round of simplifications was poorly received.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1986 the authorities retracted the second round completely. Later in the same year, the authorities promulgated a final list of simplifications, which is identical to the 1964 list except for six changes (including the restoration of three characters that had been simplified in the First Round: <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叠</span>, <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">覆</span>, <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">像</span>; note that the form <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">疊</span> is used instead of <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叠</span> in regions using Traditional Chinese).</p>
<p>There had been simplification initiatives aimed at eradicating characters entirely and establishing the <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Hanyu Pinyin</a> romanization as the official written system of the PRC, but the reform never gained quite as much popularity as the leftists had hoped.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After the retraction of the second round of simplification, the PRC stated that it wished to keep Chinese orthography stable. Years later in 2009, the Chinese government released a major revision list which included 8,300 characters. No new simplifications were introduced. However, six characters previously listed as "traditional" characters that have been simplified, as well as 51 other "variant" characters were restored to the standard list. In addition, orthographies (e.g., stroke shape) for 44 characters were proposed to be modified slightly to fit traditional calligraphic rules. Also, the practice of unrestricted simplification of rare and archaic characters by analogy using simplified radicals or components is now discouraged. A State Language Commission official cited "oversimplification" as the reason for restoring some characters. The language authority declared an open comment period until August 31, 2009 for feedback from the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The proposed orthographic changes to 44 characters were not implemented due to overwhelmingly negative public opinion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The officially promulgated version of the <i>List of Commonly Used Standardized Characters</i>, announced in 2013, contained 45 newly recognized standard characters that were previously considered variant forms, as well official sanction of 226 characters that had been simplified by analogy and had seen wide use but were not explicitly given in previous lists or documents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore_and_Malaysia">Singapore and Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Singapore and Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> underwent three successive rounds of character simplification, eventually arriving at the same set of simplified characters as Mainland China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first round, consisting of 498 Simplified characters from 502 Traditional characters, was promulgated by the Ministry of Education in 1969. The second round, consisting of 2287 Simplified characters, was promulgated in 1974. The second set contained 49 differences from the Mainland China system; those were removed in the final round in 1976. In 1993, Singapore adopted the six revisions made by Mainland China in 1986. However, unlike in mainland China where personal names may only be registered using simplified characters, parents have the option of registering their children's names in traditional characters in Singapore.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> promulgated a set of simplified characters in 1981, which were also completely identical to the simplified characters used in Mainland China. Chinese-language <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Malaysia" title="Education in Malaysia">schools</a> use these.</p>
<p>Traditional characters are still often seen in decorative contexts such as shop signs and calligraphy in both countries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small group called Dou Zi Sei (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">T:導字社; S:导字社</span>)/Dou Zi Wui (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">T:導字會; S:导字会</span>) attempted to introduce a special version of simplified characters using romanizations in the 1930s. Today, however, the <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional characters</a> remain dominant in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shinjitai" title="Shinjitai">Shinjitai</a></div>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Japan also simplified a number of Chinese characters (<i><a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a></i>) used in the Japanese language. The new forms are called <i><a href="/wiki/Shinjitai" title="Shinjitai">shinjitai</a></i>. Compared to Chinese, the Japanese reform was more limited, simplifying only a few hundred characters, most of which were already in use in cursive script. Further, the list of simplifications was exhaustive, unlike Chinese simplification – thus analogous simplifications of not explicitly simplified characters (<i><a href="/wiki/Extended_shinjitai" title="Extended shinjitai">extended shinjitai</a></i>) are not approved, and instead standard practice is to use the traditional forms.</p>
<p>The number of characters in circulation was also reduced, and formal lists of characters to be learned during each grade of school were established. The overall effect was to standardize teaching and the use of Kanji in modern literature and media.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_simplification">Method of simplification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Method of simplification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Structural simplification of characters</dt>
<dd>All characters simplified this way are enumerated in Chart 1 and Chart 2 in <i>Jianhuazi zong biao</i> (<a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/简化字总表"><span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">简化字总表</span></a>), "Complete List of Simplified Characters" announced in 1986.</dd>
<dd><a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/简化字总表#.E7.AC.AC.E4.B8.80.E8.A1.A8">Chart 1</a> lists all 350 characters that are used by themselves, and can never serve as 'simplified character components'.</dd>
<dd><a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/简化字总表#.E7.AC.AC.E4.BA.8C.E8.A1.A8">Chart 2</a> lists 132 characters that are used by themselves as well as utilized as simplified character components to further derive other simplified characters. Chart 2 also lists 14 'components' or 'radicals' that cannot be used by themselves, but can be generalized for derivation of more complex characters.</dd>
<dt>Derivation based on simplified character components</dt>
<dd><a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/简化字总表#.E7.AC.AC.E4.B8.89.E8.A1.A8">Chart 3</a> list 1,753 characters which are simplified based on the same simplification principles used for character components and radicals in Chart 2. This list is non-exhaustive, so if a character is not already found in Chart 1, 2 or 3, but can be simplified in accordance with Chart 2, the character should be simplified.</dd>
<dt>Elimination of variants of the same character</dt>
<dd><a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/第一批异体字整理表">Series One Organization List of Variant Characters</a> accounts for some of the orthography difference between Mainland China on the one hand, and Hong Kong and Taiwan on the other. These are not simplifications of character structures, but rather reduction in number of total standard characters. For each set of <a href="/wiki/Variant_Chinese_character" title="Variant Chinese character">variant characters</a> that share the identical pronunciation and meaning, one character (usually the simplest in form) is elevated to the standard character set, and the rest are obsoleted. After rounds of revisions, by 1993, some 1,027 variant characters have been declared obsolete by this list. Amongst the chosen variants, those that appear in the "Complete List of Simplified Characters" are also simplified in character structure accordingly.</dd>
<dt>Adoption of new standardized character forms</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Xin_Zixing" title="Xin Zixing">New standardized character forms</a> originated from the "List of character forms of General Used Chinese characters for Publishing" containing 6,196 characters, published in 1965. The new forms tend to adopt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulgar_variant" title="Vulgar variant">vulgar variant forms</a> for most of its characters. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xiandai_Hanyu_Tongyong_Zibiao" title="Xiandai Hanyu Tongyong Zibiao">List of Commonly Used Characters in Modern Chinese</a> list, published in 1988, contains <a class="external text" href="http://www.china-language.gov.cn/wenziguifan/shanghi/014c.htm" rel="nofollow">7,000 commonly used characters</a>, and replaces the 1965 list. Since the new forms take vulgar variants, many characters now appear slightly simpler compared to old forms, and as such are often mistaken as structurally simplified characters.</dd>
</dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation_based_on_simplified_character_components">Derivation based on simplified character components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Derivation based on simplified character components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on 132 characters and 14 components listed in Chart 2 of the <a class="external text" href="http://zh.wikisource.org/zh/简化字总表">Complete List of Simplified Characters</a>, the 1,753 'derived' characters found in the non-exhaustive Chart 3 can be created by systematically simplifying components using Chart 2 as a conversion table. While exercising such derivation, following rules should be observed:</p>
<ul>
<li>The "Complete List of Simplified Characters" employs <b>character components</b>, not the traditional definition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_character)" title="Radical (Chinese character)">radicals</a>. A component refers to any conceivable part of a character, regardless of its position within the character, or its relative size compared to other components in the same character. For instance, in the character <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">摆</span>, not only is <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">扌</span> (a traditional radical) considered a component, but also is <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">罢</span> a component.
<ul>
<li>Each of the 132 <i>simplified characters</i> in Chart 2, when used as a <i>component</i> in compound characters, systematically simplify compound characters in exactly the same way the Chart 2 character itself was simplified. For instance, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">單</span> is simplified in Chart 2 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">单</span>. Based on the same principle, these derivations can be made: <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">彈</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">弹</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嬋</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">婵</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">囅</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">冁</span>; etc.</li>
<li>The 14 <i>simplified components</i> in Chart 2 are never used alone as individual characters. They only serve as components. Example of derived simplification based on the component <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">𦥯</span>, simplified to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">龸</span>, include: <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">學</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">学</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">覺</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">觉</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">黌</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">黉</span>; <big>學 → 学</big>; etc.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Chart 1 collects 352 simplified characters that <i>generally cannot be used as components</i>. Even in rare cases where a Chart 1 character is found as a component in a compound character, the compound character cannot be simplified in the same way. For instance, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">習</span> is simplified in Chart 1 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">习</span>, but <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">褶</span> cannot be simplified to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">「衤习」</span>.</li>
<li>A character that is already explicitly listed as simplified character in the "Complete List of Simplified Characters" cannot be alternatively simplified based on derivation. For instance, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">戰</span> and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">誇</span> are simplified in Chart 1 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">战</span> and <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">夸</span> respectively, thus they cannot be simplified alternatively by derivation via <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">单</span> and <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">讠</span> in Chart 2 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">「单戈」</span> and <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">「讠夸」</span>. <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">過</span> is simplified in Chart 2 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">过</span>, thus it cannot be alternatively derived via <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">呙</span> in Chart 2 as <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">「辶呙」</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Sample Derivations</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">𦥯</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">龸</span>, thus <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">學</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">学</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">覺</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">觉</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">黌</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">黉</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">單</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">单</span>, thus <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">彈</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">弹</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嬋</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">婵</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">囅</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">冁</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">頁</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">页</span>, thus <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">顏</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">颜</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">頷</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">颔</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">順</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">顺</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">額</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">额</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">專</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">专</span>, thus <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">傳</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">传</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">轉</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">转</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">磚</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">砖</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">食</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">饣</span>, thus <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">飯</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">饭</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">飽</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">饱</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">飼</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">饲</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">餃</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">饺</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elimination_of_variants_of_the_same_character">Elimination of variants of the same character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elimination of variants of the same character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "Series One Organization List of Variant Characters" reduces the number of total standard characters. First, amongst each set of variant characters sharing identical pronunciation and meaning, one character (usually the simplest in form) is elevated to the standard character set, and the rest are obsoleted. Then amongst the chosen variants, those that appear in the "Complete List of Simplified Characters" are also simplified in character structure accordingly. Some examples follow:</p>
<p><b>Sample reduction of equivalent variants</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">姪</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">侄</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">蹤</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">踪</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">恆</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">恒</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">佇</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">伫</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">虖、嘑、謼</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">呼</span>; <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">夠</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">够</span></big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>In choosing standard characters, often ancient variants with simple structures are preferred</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">異</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">异</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">淚</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">泪</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">災、烖、菑</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">灾</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>Vulgar forms simpler in structure are also chosen</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">傑</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">杰</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">貓</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">猫</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">豬</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">猪</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">獃、騃</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">呆</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>The chosen variant was already simplified in Chart 1</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">裏</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">裡</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">里</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歎</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嘆</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叹</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">唘、啓</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">啟</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">启</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">鬦、鬪、鬭</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">鬥</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">斗</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">厤、暦</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">曆</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">历</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歴</span> → <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歷</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">历</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In some instance, the chosen variant is actually more complex than eliminated ones. This is often taken up by opponents of simplification who are not aware of the dual goals of simplification (i.e. in structure of characters as well as in total number of characters) to decry that simplification does not always simplify characters. An example is the character <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">搾</span> which is eliminated in favor of the variant form <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">榨</span>. Note that the "hand" radical <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">扌</span>, with three strokes, on the left of the eliminated <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">搾</span> is now "seen" as more complex, appearing as the "tree" radical <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">木</span>, with four strokes, in the chosen variant <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">榨</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption_of_new_standardized_character_forms">Adoption of new standardized character forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Adoption of new standardized character forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The new standardized character forms started in the "List of character forms of General Used Chinese characters for Publishing" and revised through the "List of Commonly Used Characters in Modern Chinese" tend to adopt vulgar variant character forms. Since the new forms take vulgar variants, many characters now appear slightly simpler compared to old forms, and as such are often mistaken as structurally simplified characters. Some examples follow:</p>
<p><b>The traditional component <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">釆</span> becomes <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">米</span></b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">粵</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">粤</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">奧</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">奥</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>The traditional component <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">囚</span> becomes <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">日</span></b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">溫</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">温</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">媼</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">媪</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>The traditional "Break" stroke becomes the "Dot" stroke</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">虛</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">虚</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">噓</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">嘘</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>The traditional components <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">⺥</span> and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">爫</span> become <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">⺈</span></b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">靜</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">静</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">睜</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">睁</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>The traditional component <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">奐</span> becomes <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">奂</span></b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><big><span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">換</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">换</span>; <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">煥</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">焕</span>;</big> etc.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consistency">Consistency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Consistency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is a common misconception that the simplification process is arbitrary and not based on consistent rules. These allegations are often made when people 'discover' their own 'principles of simplification' from anecdotal evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.BD.AD.E5.B0.8F.E6.98.8E_derivation_errors_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.BD.AD.E5.B0.8F.E6.98.8E_derivation_errors-5">[5]</a></sup> Note, however, that simplification by derivation must follow the rules mentioned earlier.</p>
<p>An often cited example of the apparent irregularity of simplification involves characters that appear to share the simple symbol <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">又</span> used in many simplified characters in Chart 1. Often it is intuited that <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">又</span> is a 'character component', after observing <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">漢</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">汉</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">難</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">难</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">癱</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">瘫</span>, etc. A student of simplification may infer that the same simplification mechanism also works for <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嘆</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叹</span> and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">灘</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">滩</span>. When observing that <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歎</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叹</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歡</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">欢</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">勸</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">劝</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">灌</span> (not simplified) and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">罐</span> (not simplified), one may come to the conclusion that the process of simplification is irregular.</p>
<p>However, in the Complete List of Simplified Characters, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">漢</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">汉</span> appears in Chart 1. <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">難</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">难</span> is listed in Chart 2. And <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">癱</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">瘫</span> is a derived character found in the non-exhaustive list in Chart 3. Therefore, <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">难</span> is defined as a 'simplified character component' according to the standard, while <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">又</span> is not. Based on <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">难</span>, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">癱</span> is simplified to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">瘫</span>, and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">灘</span> to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">滩</span>.</p>
<p>In the "Series One Organization List of Variant Characters", the variant character <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歎</span> is replaced by <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嘆</span>. The character <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">嘆</span> is simplified in Chart 1 to <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叹</span>. Therefore, <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歎</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">叹</span>.</p>
<p>Both <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">歡</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">欢</span> and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">勸</span> → <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">劝</span> appear in Chart 1. Thus they are not defined as derived characters.</p>
<p>There are no characters or components found in Chart 2 usable for derivation of <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">灌</span> and <span lang="zh-hant" xml:lang="zh-hant">罐</span>. Further investigation reveals that these two characters do not appear in Chart 1 nor in "Series One Organization List of Variant Characters". Thus they are not defined as simplified characters; they remain unchanged from traditional forms in the "List of Commonly Used Characters in Modern Chinese".</p>
<p>Not all new character forms result in simpler characters (i.e. fewer strokes). For instance, the old form <span lang="zh-cn" xml:lang="zh-cn">強</span>, with 11 strokes, now appears as <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">强</span>, with 12 strokes, in the new form. However, technically, these new character forms do not constitute simplified characters.</p>
<p>Strangely, some characters with the water radical with three dots 氵 in the traditional form were simplified into the ice radical with two dots 冫 in the simplified form. Some examples are <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">決</span> → <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">决</span>, <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">況</span> → <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">况</span>, and <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">湊</span> → <span lang="zh_cn" xml:lang="zh_cn">凑</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_use">Distribution and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Distribution and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The slogan <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">战无不胜的毛泽东思想万岁!</span> (Zhàn wúbù shèng de Máo Zédōng sīxiǎng wànsuì!; Long live the invincible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong_Thought" title="Mao Zedong Thought">Mao Zedong Thought</a>!) on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xinhua_Gate" title="Xinhua Gate">Xinhua Gate</a> in Beijing.</div>

<p>The People's Republic of China, Singapore and Malaysia generally use simplified characters. They appear very sparingly in printed text produced in Hong Kong, Macau, Taiwan, and <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a> communities, although they are becoming more prevalent as China opens to the world. Conversely, the mainland is seeing an increase in the use of traditional forms, where they are often used on signs, and in logos, blogs, dictionaries, and scholarly works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainland_China">Mainland China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mainland China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Law of the People's Republic of China on the National Common Language and Characters implies simplified Chinese as the standard script, and relegates traditional Chinese to certain aspects and purposes such as ceremonies, cultural purposes (e.g. calligraphy), decoration, publications and books on ancient literature and poetry, and research purposes. Traditional Chinese remains ubiquitous on buildings predating the promotion of simplified characters, such as former government buildings, religious buildings, educational institutions, and historical monuments. Traditional Chinese is also often used for commercial purposes, such as shopfront displays and advertisements, though this isn't officially encouraged.</p>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/One_country,_two_systems" title="One country, two systems">one country, two systems</a> model, the PRC has not attempted to force Hong Kong or Macau into using simplified characters. The PRC tends to print material intended for people in Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan, and overseas Chinese in traditional characters. For example, it prints versions of the <i><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Daily" title="People's Daily">People's Daily</a></i> in traditional characters and both the <i>People's Daily</i> and <a href="/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency" title="Xinhua News Agency">Xinhua</a> websites have versions in traditional characters using <a href="/wiki/Big5" title="Big5">Big5</a> encoding. Mainland companies selling products in Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan use Traditional characters on their displays and packaging to communicate with consumers (the reverse is true as well).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictionaries" title="Dictionaries">Dictionaries</a> published in mainland China generally show both simplified and their traditional counterparts. In digital media, many cultural phenomena imported from Hong Kong and Taiwan into mainland China, such as music videos, <a href="/wiki/Karaoke" title="Karaoke">karaoke</a> videos, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtitled" title="Subtitled">subtitled</a> movies, and subtitled dramas, use traditional Chinese characters, thereby exposing mainlanders to the use of traditional characters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong_2">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Textbooks, official statements, newspapers, including the PRC-funded media, show no signs of moving to simplified Chinese characters.</p>
<p>It is common for Hong Kong people to learn traditional Chinese characters in school, and some simplified Chinese in passing (either through reading mainland-published books or other media). For use on computers, however, people tend to type Chinese characters using a traditional character set such as <a href="/wiki/Big5" title="Big5">Big5</a>. In Hong Kong, as well as elsewhere, it is common for people who use both sets to do so because it is much easier to convert from the traditional character set to the simplified character set because of the usage of the aforementioned methods 8 and 9 of simplification.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simplified Chinese characters are not officially used in governmental and civil publications in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. However, it is legal to import simplified character publications and distribute them. Certain simplified characters that have long existed in informal writing for centuries also have popular usage, while those characters simplified originally by the Taiwanese government are much less common in daily appearance.</p>
<p>In all areas, most handwritten text will include informal simplifications (alternative script) which are not the same as the simplifications officially promulgated by the PRC and are often instead influenced by the <i>shinjitai</i> used in Japan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The informal simplification of the first character of "Taiwan" from <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">臺</span> to <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">台</span> rivals its orthodox form in commonality, even in print and in answers to school exams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ok_to_use_vulgar_forms_in_tests_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ok_to_use_vulgar_forms_in_tests-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore_and_Malaysia_2">Singapore and Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Singapore and Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Singapore, where Chinese is one of the official languages, simplified characters are the official standard and used in all official publications as well as government-controlled press. While simplified characters are taught exclusively in schools, the government does not officially discourage the use of traditional characters. While all official publications are in simplified characters, the government still allows parents to choose whether to have their child's Chinese name registered in simplified or traditional characters.</p>
<p>In Malaysia, Chinese is not an official language, but over 90% of ethnic-Chinese students are educated in Chinese schools, which have taught simplified characters since 1981. However, traditional characters are widely used by older generations, and are widespread on signboards, etc. Most of Malaysia's Chinese newspapers compromise by retaining traditional characters in article headlines, but using simplified characters for content.</p>
<p>As there is no restriction of the use of traditional characters in the mass media, television programmes, books, magazines and music CD's that have been imported from Hong Kong or Taiwan are widely available, and these almost always use traditional characters. Most karaoke discs, being imported from Hong Kong or Taiwan, have song lyrics in traditional characters as well. Many shop signs continue to be written in traditional characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reflection_on_use_of_simplified_Chinese_in_Singapore_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-reflection_on_use_of_simplified_Chinese_in_Singapore-23">[23]</a></sup> Menus in <a href="/wiki/Hawker_centre" title="Hawker centre">hawker centres</a> and coffeeshops are also commonly seen in traditional characters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, schools in Mainland China, Malaysia and Singapore use simplified characters exclusively, while schools in Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan use traditional characters exclusively.</p>
<p>Today, simplified Chinese characters predominate among college and university programs teaching Chinese as a foreign language outside of China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teaching_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Teaching-24">[24]</a></sup> such as those in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainland_China_2">Mainland China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mainland China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2004, Ministry of education authorities rejected a proposal from a Beijing <a href="/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Political_Consultative_Conference" title="Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference">CPPCC</a> political conference member that called for elementary schools to teach traditional Chinese characters in addition to the simplified ones. The conference member pointed out that most mainland Chinese, especially young people, have difficulties with traditional Chinese characters; this is especially important in dealing with non-mainland communities such as <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and Hong Kong. The educational authorities did not approve the recommendation, saying that it did not fit in with the "requirements as set out by the law" and it could potentially complicate the curricula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A similar proposal was delivered to the 1st Plenary Session of the 11th Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference in March 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong_3">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most, if not all, Chinese language text books in Hong Kong are written in traditional characters. Before 1997, the use of simplified characters was generally discouraged by educators. After 1997, while students are still expected to be proficient and utilize traditional characters in formal settings, they may sometimes adopt a hybrid written form in informal settings to speed up writing. With the exception of open examinations, Simplified Chinese characters are considered acceptable by the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Examinations_and_Assessment_Authority" title="Hong Kong Examinations and Assessment Authority">Hong Kong Examinations and Assessment Authority</a> for their speed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore_and_Malaysia_3">Singapore and Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Singapore and Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chinese textbooks in Singapore and Malaysia are written exclusively in simplified characters, and only simplified characters are taught in school. Traditional characters are usually only taught to those taking up calligraphy as a <a href="/wiki/Co-curricular_activity_(Singapore)" title="Co-curricular activity (Singapore)">co-curricular activity</a> or Cantonese as an elective course at school.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_as_a_foreign_language">Chinese as a foreign language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chinese as a foreign language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the source of many Mandarin Chinese textbooks is mainland China, the majority of textbooks teaching Chinese are now based on simplified characters and Hanyu Pinyin – although there are textbooks originating in China which have a traditional version. For practical reasons, universities and schools prepare students who will be able to communicate with mainland China, so their obvious choice is to use simplified characters.</p>
<p>In places where a particular set is not locally entrenched, e.g., Europe and the United States, instruction is now mostly simplified, as the economic importance of mainland China increases, and also because of the availability of textbooks printed in mainland China. Teachers of international students often recommend learning both systems.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the United Kingdom, universities mainly teach Mandarin Chinese at the undergraduate level using the simplified characters coupled with <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>. However, they will require the students to learn or be able to recognise the traditional forms if they are studying in Taiwan or Hong Kong (such as taking Cantonese courses). In Australia and New Zealand, schools, universities and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TAFE" title="TAFE">TAFEs</a> use predominantly simplified characters.</p>
<p>Russia and most East European nations are traditionally oriented on the education of the PRC's system for teaching Chinese, uses simplified characters but exposes the learners to both systems.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, universities have used predominantly simplified characters since 1990s. In high school, Chinese is one of the selective subjects. By the regulation of the national curricula standards, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhuyin" title="Zhuyin">MPS I</a> and traditional characters had been originally used before (since the 1940s), but by the change of regulation, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a> and simplified characters have been used to pupils who enter the school in 1996 or later. Therefore, MPS I and traditional characters disappeared after 1998 in South Korean high school Chinese curriculum.</p>
<p>In Japan there are two types of schools. Simplified Chinese is taught instead of traditional Chinese in pro-mainland China schools. They also teach Pinyin, a romanization system for standard Chinese, while the Taiwan-oriented schools teach <a href="/wiki/Bopomofo" title="Bopomofo">Zhuyin</a>, which uses phonetic symbols. However, the Taiwan-oriented schools are starting to teach simplified Chinese and Pinyin to offer a more well-rounded education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">Southeast Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Southeast Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, the use of simplified characters is getting more and more popular. Before the 1970s, Chinese schools in the Philippines were under the supervision of the Ministry of Education of the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Republic of China</a>. Hence, most books were using the Traditional Characters. Traditional Characters remained prevalent until the early 2000s. However, institutions like the <a href="/wiki/Confucius_Institute" title="Confucius Institute">Confucius Institute</a>, being the cultural arm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> are strong proponents of the use of Simplified Characters. Also, many new schools are now importing their Mandarin textbooks from Singapore instead of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.</p>
<p>Public universities, such as the Linguistics and Asian Languages Department of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Philippines" title="University of the Philippines">University of the Philippines</a> uses Simplified Characters in their teaching materials. On the other hand, private schools such as <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Kai_Shek_College" title="Chiang Kai Shek College">Chiang Kai Shek College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Jude_Catholic_School_(Manila)" title="Saint Jude Catholic School (Manila)">Saint Jude Catholic School</a> remain to be main proponents of the usage of Traditional Characters. However, some private universities, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ateneo_de_Manila_University" title="Ateneo de Manila University">Ateneo de Manila University</a> now use Simplified Characters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_encoding">Computer encoding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simplified_Chinese_characters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Computer encoding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In computer text applications, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guobiao_code" title="Guobiao code">GB encoding scheme</a> most often renders simplified Chinese characters, while <a href="/wiki/Big5" title="Big5">Big5</a> most often renders traditional characters. Although neither encoding has an explicit connection with a specific character set, the lack of a one-to-one mapping between the simplified and traditional sets established a de facto linkage.</p>
<p>Since simplified Chinese conflated many characters into one and since the initial version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guobiao_code" title="Guobiao code">GB encoding scheme</a>, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GB2312" title="GB2312">GB2312</a>-80, contained only one code point for each character, it is impossible to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GB2312" title="GB2312">GB2312</a> to map to the bigger set of traditional characters. It is theoretically possible to use Big5 code to map to the smaller set of simplified character glyphs, although there is little market for such a product. Newer and alternative forms of GB have support for traditional characters. In particular, mainland authorities have now established <a href="/wiki/GB_18030" title="GB 18030">GB 18030</a> as the official encoding standard for use in all mainland software publications. The encoding contains all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> characters included in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> 3.0. As such, GB 18030 encoding contains both simplified and traditional characters found in Big-5 and GB, as well as all characters found in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language_and_computers" title="Japanese language and computers">Japanese</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_writing_system" title="Korean writing system">Korean</a> encodings.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> deals with the issue of simplified and traditional characters as part of the project of <a href="/wiki/Han_unification" title="Han unification">Han unification</a> by including code points for each. This was rendered necessary by the fact that the linkage between simplified characters and traditional characters is not one-to-one. While this means that a Unicode system can display both simplified and traditional characters, it also means that different <a href="/wiki/Locale_(computer_software)" title="Locale (computer software)">localization</a> files are needed for each type.</p>
<p>The Chinese characters used in modern Japanese (called Kanji characters) have also undergone simplification, but generally to a lesser extent than with simplified Chinese. It's worth mentioning that Japan's writing system utilizes a reduced number of Chinese characters in daily use, resulting partly from the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_writing_system#Post-WWII" title="Japanese writing system">Japanese language reforms</a>; thus, a number of complex characters are written phonetically. Reconciling these different character sets in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> became part of the controversial process of <a href="/wiki/Han_unification" title="Han unification">Han unification</a>. Not surprisingly, some of the Chinese characters used in Japan are neither 'traditional' nor 'simplified'. In this case, these characters cannot be found in traditional/simplified Chinese dictionaries.</p>
