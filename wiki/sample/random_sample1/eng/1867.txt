<p>The <b>Galata Tower</b> (<i>Galata Kulesi</i> in <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>) — called <i>Christea Turris</i> (the <i>Tower of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Christ</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Latin</a>) by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> — is a medieval stone tower in the <a href="/wiki/Galata" title="Galata">Galata</a>/<a href="/wiki/Karak%C3%B6y" title="Karaköy">Karaköy</a> quarter of <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, just to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horn" title="Golden Horn">Golden Horn</a>'s junction with the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>. One of the city's most striking landmarks, it is a high, cone-capped cylinder that dominates the skyline and offers a panoramic vista of Istanbul's <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">historic peninsula</a> and its environs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galata_Tower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nine-story tower is 66.90 meters tall (62.59 m without the ornament on top, 51.65 m at the observation deck), and was the city's tallest structure when it was built. The elevation at ground level is 35 meters above sea-level. The tower has an external <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of 16.45 meters at the base, an 8.95 meters diameter inside, and walls that are 3.75 meters thick.</p>
<p>There is a restaurant and café on its upper floors which command a magnificent view of Istanbul and the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>. Also located on the upper floors is a night club which hosts a Turkish show. There are two operating elevators that carry visitors from the lower level to the upper levels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galata_Tower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galata_Tower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1153" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Galata_Tower.jpg/220px-Galata_Tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Galata_Tower.jpg/330px-Galata_Tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Galata_Tower.jpg/440px-Galata_Tower.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galata_Tower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Albumen print of the Galata Tower, taken by <a href="/wiki/Pascal_S%C3%A9bah" title="Pascal Sébah">Pascal Sébah</a> some time between 1875–1886. Here the tower has the cupola that was built after the storm of 1875. The present-day conical top is a reconstruction of a previous one, and was built during restoration works between 1965 and 1967.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque style</a> tower was built as <i>Christea Turris</i> (Tower of Christ) in 1348 during an expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> colony in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. Galata Tower was the tallest building in Istanbul at 219½ feet (66.9 m) when it was built in 1348.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katie_Hallam_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katie_Hallam-2">[2]</a></sup> It was built to replace the <a href="/wiki/Galata_Tower_(old)" title="Galata Tower (old)">old Tower of Galata</a>, an original Byzantine tower named <i><a href="/wiki/Galata_Tower_(old)" title="Galata Tower (old)">Megalos Pyrgos</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: <i>Great Tower</i>) which controlled the northern end of the massive sea chain that closed the entrance to the Golden Horn. That tower was on a different site and was largely destroyed in 1203, during the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> of 1202–1204.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB815_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODB815-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The upper section of the tower with the conical cap was slightly modified in several restorations during the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> period when it was used as an observation tower for spotting fires.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="663" data-file-width="1026" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg/220px-View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg/330px-View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg/440px-View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_of_constantinople_by_evening_light.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Galata Tower with its previous <a href="/wiki/Conical_roof" title="Conical roof">conical roof</a>, depicted in a painting by <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Aivazovsky" title="Ivan Aivazovsky">Ivan Aivazovsky</a> in 1846. The previous conical roof was destroyed by a storm in 1875. The current conical roof was built during restoration works between 1965 and 1967.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Seyahatname" title="Seyahatname">Seyahatname</a></i> of Ottoman historian and traveller <a href="/wiki/Evliya_%C3%87elebi" title="Evliya Çelebi">Evliya Çelebi</a>, in circa 1630-1632, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hezarfen_Ahmet_%C3%87elebi" title="Hezarfen Ahmet Çelebi">Hezarfen Ahmet Çelebi</a> flew as an early <i>intercontinental</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">aviator</a> using artificial wings for gliding from this tower over the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a> to the slopes of <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Csk%C3%BCdar" title="Üsküdar">Üsküdar</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolian</a> side, nearly six kilometres away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evliy.C3.A2_.C3.87elebi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evliy.C3.A2_.C3.87elebi-4">[4]</a></sup> Evliyâ Çelebi also tells of Hezarfen's brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagari_Hasan_%C3%87elebi" title="Lagari Hasan Çelebi">Lagari Hasan Çelebi</a>, performing the first flight with a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> in a conical cage filled with <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> in 1633.</p>
<p>Starting from 1717 the Ottomans began to use the tower for spotting fires in the city. In 1794, during the reign of Sultan <a href="/wiki/Selim_III" title="Selim III">Selim III</a>, the roof of the tower was made of lead and wood, and the stairs were severely damaged by a fire. Another fire damaged the building in 1831, upon which a new restoration work took place.</p>
<p>In 1875, during a storm, the conical roof on the top of the building was destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TimeOutIstanbul_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TimeOutIstanbul-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galatakulesi.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galatakulesi.org-6">[6]</a></sup> The tower remained without this conical roof for the rest of the Ottoman period. Many years later, during the restoration works between 1965 and 1967, the conical roof was reconstructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TimeOutIstanbul_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TimeOutIstanbul-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galatakulesi.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Galatakulesi.org-6">[6]</a></sup> During this final restoration in the 1960s, the wooden interior of the tower was replaced by a concrete structure and it was commercialized and opened to the public.</p>
<p>From the top of the tower, the first French panorama painter <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Pr%C3%A9vost_(painter)" title="Pierre Prévost (painter)">Pierre Prévost</a> drew in 1818 his "Panorama de <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>" which was later exhibited in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1825.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The panorama image shown below is composed of ten photos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> taken from the Galata Tower by the photographic firm of Sébah &amp; Joaillier, and is most likely to have been taken in the 1880s.</p>
