<p>The <b>Arch of Galerius</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Gr.</a>: αψίδα του Γαλερίου) or <b>Kamara</b>) <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Gr.</a>:καμάρα) and the <b>Rotunda</b> (ροτόντα) are neighbouring early 4th-century monuments in the city of <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a>, in the region of <a href="/wiki/Central_Macedonia" title="Central Macedonia">Central Macedonia</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arch_of_Galerius_and_Rotunda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="274" data-file-width="372" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg/220px-Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg/330px-Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ad/Saint_George_Rotunda_reconstruction.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The 4th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a> commissioned these two structures as elements of an imperial precinct linked to his Thessaloniki palace. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeologists" title="Archeologists">Archeologists</a> have found substantial remains of the palace to the southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These three monumental structures were connected by a road that ran through the arch, which rose above the major east-west road of the city.</p>
<p>At the crux of the major axes of the city, the Arch of Galerius emphasized the power of the emperor and linked the monumental structures with the fabric of 4th-century Thessaloniki. The arch was composed of a masonry core faced with <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> sculptural panels celebrating a victory over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_Empire" title="Sassanid Empire">Sassanid Persians</a>. About two-thirds the arch is preserved.</p>
<p>The Rotunda was a massive circular structure with a masonry core that had an <a href="/wiki/Oculus" title="Oculus">oculus</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in Rome. It has gone through multiple periods of use and modification as a polytheist temple, a Christian basilica, a Muslim mosque, and again a Christian church (and archaeological site). A minaret is preserved from its use as a mosque, and ancient remains are exposed on its southern side.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_description_of_the_Arch">Location and description of the Arch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arch_of_Galerius_and_Rotunda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location and description of the Arch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kamara.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2896" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kamara.jpg/260px-Kamara.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kamara.jpg/390px-Kamara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kamara.jpg/520px-Kamara.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kamara.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Arch of Galerius</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Arch of Galerius, stands on what is now <i>Egnatia &amp; Dimitrios Gounari Street</i>. The arch was built in 298 to 299 AD and dedicated in 303 AD to celebrate the victory of the <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">tetrarch</a> <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a> over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_Empire" title="Sassanid Empire">Sassanid Persians</a> and capture of their capital <a href="/wiki/Ctesiphon" title="Ctesiphon">Ctesiphon</a> in 298.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The structure was an octopylon (eight-pillared gateway) forming a triple arch that was built of a rubble masonry core faced first with brick and then with marble panels with sculptural relief. The central arched opening was 9.7 m wide and 12.5 m high, and the secondary openings on other side were 4.8 m wide and 6.5 m high. The central arch spanned the portion of the <i><a href="/wiki/Via_Egnatia" title="Via Egnatia">Via Egnatia</a></i> (primary Roman road from Dyrrhacium to <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>) that passed through the city as a <a href="/wiki/Decumanus_Maximus" title="Decumanus Maximus"><i>Decumanus</i></a> (east-west major street). A road connecting the Rotunda (125m northeast) with the Palace complex (235m southwest) passed through the arch along its long axis.</p>
<p>Only the northwestern three of the eight pillars and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> cores of the arches above survive: i.e., the entire eastern side (4 pillars) and the southernmost one of the western pillars are lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Extensive consolidation with modern brick has been performed on the exposed masonry cores to protect the monument. The two pillars flanking the central arched passageway retain their sculpted <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> slabs, which depict the wars of Galerius against the Persians in broadly panegyric terms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculptural_program_of_the_Arch">Sculptural program of the Arch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arch_of_Galerius_and_Rotunda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sculptural program of the Arch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Understanding of the sculptural program of the arch is limited by the loss of the majority of the marble panels, but the remains give an impression of the whole. Four vertically stacked registers of sculpted decoration were carved on each pillar, each separated by elaborate moldings. A label for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigris_River" title="Tigris River">Tigris River</a> indicates that there were likely labels on other representations as the builders deemed necessary. Artistic license was taken in the representations, for instance, the Caesar Galerius is shown in personal combat with the Sassanid Shah Narses in one of the panels; although they never met in battle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the arch a mounted Galerius attacks a similarly mounted Narses with a lance as an eagle bearing a victory wreath in its talons approaches Galerius. The Caesar sits securely on his rearing horse, while the Persian king appears nearly unhorsed. Terrified Persians cower under the hooves of the Caesar’s horse in the chaos of battle. The panel expresses the power of the Caesar Galerius.</p>
<p>The relief of the imperial family conjoined in a sacrifice of thanksgiving owes its distant prototype to the Augustan reliefs on the <i><a href="/wiki/Ara_Pacis" title="Ara Pacis">Ara Pacis</a></i> in Rome.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Galerius' wife, Diocletian's daughter Valeria, is shown at his side, helping authenticate his connection to his predecessor. Here as elsewhere all the faces have been carefully chiselled off, whether as <i><a href="/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae" title="Damnatio memoriae">damnatio memoriae</a></i> or in later cultural intolerance of images.</p>
<p>In another panel, the tetrarchs are all arrayed in the toga as a <i>Victoria</i> holds a victory wreath out to the heads of the two <i>Augusti</i>. A third panel celebrates the unity of the <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">tetrarchy</a>, with a depiction of the tetrarchs standing together; the depersonalized manner in which the tetrarchs are portrayed is reminiscent of the schematic statues of the tetrarchs in porphyry at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Basilica" title="St. Mark's Basilica">St. Mark's Basilica</a> in Venice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Only Galerius is dressed in armor, and he makes the offering upon the altar.</p>
<p>What remains of the arch asserts the glory of the tetrarchy and the prominence of Galerius within that system. The arch celebrates the Roman Empire as part of Galerius’ victory over the Sassanid king.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Galerius is also pictured on his horse at the right, while attacking a Sassanid guard.</p>

<p>The Rotunda of Galerius is 125m northeast of the Arch of Galerius at 40°37'59.77"N, 22°57'9.77"E. It is also known (by its consecration and use) as the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Greece" title="Church of Greece">Greek Orthodox</a> Church of <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Agios Georgios</a></i>, and is informally called the Church of the Rotunda (or simply <i>The Rotunda</i>). The cylindrical structure was built in 306 AD on the orders of the tetrarch <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a>, who was thought to have intended it to be his <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausoleum</a>. It was more likely intended as a temple; it is not known to what god it would have been dedicated.</p>
<p>The Rotunda has a diameter of 24.5 m. Its walls are more than 6 m thick, which is why it has withstood Thessaloniki's earthquakes. The walls are interrupted by eight rectangular bays, with the south bay forming the entrance. A flat brick dome, 30 m high at the peak, crowns the cylindrical structure. In its original design, the dome of the Rotunda had an <i><a href="/wiki/Oculus" title="Oculus">oculus</a></i>, as does the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in Rome.</p>

<p>After Galerius's death in 311, he was buried at <a href="/wiki/Gamzigrad" title="Gamzigrad">Gamzigrad</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felix_Romuliana" title="Felix Romuliana">Felix Romuliana</a>) near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zajecar" title="Zajecar">Zajecar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>. The Rotunda stood empty for several years until the Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a> ordered it converted into a Christian church in 326. The church was embellished with very high quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosaics" title="Mosaics">mosaics</a>. Only fragments have survived of the original decoration, for example, a band depicting saints with hands raised in prayer, in front of complex architectural fantasies.</p>
<p>The building was used as a church for over 1,200 years until the city fell to the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a>. In 1590 it was converted into a <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a>, called the Mosque of Suleyman Hortaji Effendi, and a <a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">minaret</a> was added to the structure. It was used as a mosque until 1912, when the Greeks captured the city during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_War" title="Balkan War">Balkan War</a>. Greek Orthodox officials reconsecrated the structure as a church, and they left the minaret. The structure was damaged during an earthquake in 1978 but was subsequently restored. As of 2004<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arch_of_Galerius_and_Rotunda&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the minaret was still being stabilized with scaffolding. The building is now a historical monument under the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ephorate_of_Byzantine_Antiquities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ephorate of Byzantine Antiquities (page does not exist)">Ephorate of Byzantine Antiquities</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Ministry_of_Culture" title="Greek Ministry of Culture">Greek Ministry of Culture</a>, although the Greek Orthodox Church has access to the church for various festivities.</p>
<p>The Rotunda is the oldest of Thessaloniki's churches. Some Greek publications claim it is the oldest Christian church in the world, although there are competitors for that title. It is the most important surviving example of a church from the early Christian period of the Greek-speaking part of the Roman Empire.</p>
