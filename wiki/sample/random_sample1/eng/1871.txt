<p><b>Galeazzo Maria Sforza</b> (24 January 1444 – assassinated, 26 December 1476) was the fifth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Milan" title="Duke of Milan">Duke of Milan</a> from 1466 until his death. He was famous for being lustful, cruel and tyrannical.</p>
<p>He was born to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a>, a popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condottiero" title="Condottiero">condottiero</a> and ally of <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de' Medici">Cosimo de' Medici</a> who would gain the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> in 1450, and <a href="/wiki/Bianca_Maria_Visconti" title="Bianca Maria Visconti">Bianca Maria Visconti</a>. He married into the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">Gonzaga</a> family; on the death of his first wife <a href="/wiki/Dorotea_Gonzaga" title="Dorotea Gonzaga">Dorotea Gonzaga</a>, he married <a href="/wiki/Bona_of_Savoy" title="Bona of Savoy">Bona of Savoy</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Galeazzo Maria Sforza was born in <a href="/wiki/Fermo" title="Fermo">Fermo</a>, near the family's castle of <a href="/wiki/Girifalco" title="Girifalco">Girifalco</a>, the first son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bianca_Maria_Visconti" title="Bianca Maria Visconti">Bianca Maria Visconti</a>. At the death of his father (8 March 1466), Galeazzo was in France at the head of a military expedition to help King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI of France</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Burgundy" title="Charles I of Burgundy">Charles I of Burgundy</a>. Called back home by his mother, Galeazzo returned to Italy by an adventurous trip under a false name. The false identity was necessary as he had to pass by the territories of the family enemy, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counts_and_Dukes_of_Savoy#Dukes_of_Savoy" title="Counts and Dukes of Savoy">Duke of Savoy</a>, who made an unsuccessful attempt on Galeazzo's life. He entered Milan on 20 March, acclaimed by the populace.</p>
<p>In his first years Galeazzo and his mother ruled jointly, but later his ruthless character pushed him to oust Bianca Maria from Milan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage">Patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sforza was famous as a patron of music. Under his direction, financial backing and encouragement, his chapel grew into one of the most famous and historically significant musical ensembles in Europe. Composers from the north, especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_School_(music)" title="Dutch School (music)">Franco-Flemish</a> composers from the present-day <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, came to sing in his chapel and write <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">masses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motet" title="Motet">motets</a> and secular music for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Some of the figures associated with the Sforza chapel include <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Agricola" title="Alexander Agricola">Alexander Agricola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Martini" title="Johannes Martini">Johannes Martini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loyset_Comp%C3%A8re" title="Loyset Compère">Loyset Compère</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_van_Weerbeke" title="Gaspar van Weerbeke">Gaspar van Weerbeke</a>. However, most of the singers at the Sforza chapel fled after Galeazzo's murder and took positions elsewhere; as a result, there was soon a rise in musical standards in other cities such as <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation">Reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Galeazzo Sforza is also known to have had a cruel streak. He was a notorious womanizer who often passed his women on to his courtiers once he was tired of them. Sforza once had a poacher executed by forcing him to swallow an entire <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hare</a> (with fur intact), and had another man nailed alive to his coffin. He also had a <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priest</a> who predicted a short reign for Sforza, punished by being starved to death. This made him many enemies in Milan. It was also said of Galeazzo Sforza that he had raped the wives and daughters of numerous Milanese nobles, that he took sadistic pleasure in devising tortures for men who had offended him, and that he enjoyed pulling apart the limbs of his enemies with his own hands.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza,_1444-1473-,_1476.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1015" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg/220px-Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg/330px-Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg/440px-Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza%2C_1444-1473-%2C_1476.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frontespizio_Lamento_del_duca_Galeazzo_Maria_-Sforza,_1444-1473-,_1476.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>There were three principal assassins involved in Sforza's death: <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Visconti" title="Carlo Visconti">Carlo Visconti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Olgiati" title="Gerolamo Olgiati">Gerolamo Olgiati</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Andrea_Lampugnani" title="Giovanni Andrea Lampugnani">Giovanni Andrea Lampugnani</a>, all fairly high-ranking officials at the Milanese court.</p>
<p>Lampugnani, descended from Milanese nobility, is recognized as the leader of the conspiracy. His motives were based primarily on a land dispute, in which Galeazzo had failed to intervene in a matter which saw the Lampugnani family lose considerable properties. Visconti and Olgiati also bore the duke enmity - Olgiati was a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">Republican</a> idealist, whereas Visconti believed Sforza to have taken his sister's virginity.</p>
<p>After carefully studying Sforza's movements, the conspirators made their move on the day after <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, 1476, the official day of Santo Stefano, the namesake of the church where the deed was to be committed. Supported by about thirty friends, the three men waited in the church for the duke to arrive for mass. When Galeazzo Sforza arrived, Lampugnani knelt before him; after some words were exchanged, Lampugnani rose suddenly and stabbed Sforza in the groin and breast. Olgiati and Visconti soon joined in, as did a servant of Lampugnani's.</p>
<p>Sforza was dead within a matter of seconds. All the assassins quickly escaped in the ensuing mayhem save for Lampugnani, who became entangled in some of the church's cloth and was killed by a guard. His body soon fell into the hands of a mob, which dragged the corpse through the streets, slashing and beating at it; finally, they hung the body upside-down outside Lampugnani's house. The beheaded corpse was cut down the next day and, in an act of symbolism, the "sinning" right hand was removed, burnt and put on display.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_of_the_assassination">Aftermath of the assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath of the assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite the initial public reaction, the government brought swift justice, soon encouraged by the public as well.</p>
<p>The conspirators had given little thought to the repercussions of their crime, and were apprehended within days. Visconti and Olgiati were soon found and executed, as was the servant of Lampugnani who had participated in the slaying. The executions took place in a public ceremony that culminated in the display of their corpses as a warning to others.</p>
<p>Evidence from the conspirators' confessions indicated that the assassins had been encouraged by the humanist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cola_Montano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cola Montano (page does not exist)">Cola Montano</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> who had left Milan some months before, and who bore malice against the duke for a public whipping some years before. While being tortured, Olgiati also uttered the famous words, "Mors acerba, fama perpetua, stabit vetus memoria facti" (Death is bitter, but glory is eternal, the memory of my deed will endure).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistFlorence_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistFlorence-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar elements indicate that this assassination was likely influential in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pazzi_Conspiracy" title="Pazzi Conspiracy">Pazzi Conspiracy</a>, a subsequent attempt to dethrone the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> family in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> and to replace them with <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Riario" title="Girolamo Riario">Girolamo Riario</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With his second wife, <a href="/wiki/Bona_of_Savoy" title="Bona of Savoy">Bona of Savoy</a>, Sforza had four children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Sforza" title="Gian Galeazzo Sforza">Gian Galeazzo Sforza</a> (1469–1494), who became duke upon his father's death; he married his cousin <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_Aragon,_Duchess_of_Milan" title="Isabella of Aragon, Duchess of Milan">Isabella of Aragon, Duchess of Milan</a> and had issue</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hermes_Maria_Sforza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hermes Maria Sforza (page does not exist)">Hermes Maria Sforza</a> (1470–1503), Marquis of <a href="/wiki/Tortona" title="Tortona">Tortona</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bianca_Maria_Sforza" title="Bianca Maria Sforza">Bianca Maria Sforza</a> (1472–1510), who married <a href="/wiki/Philibert_I,_Duke_of_Savoy" title="Philibert I, Duke of Savoy">Philibert I, Duke of Savoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Sforza" title="Anna Sforza">Anna Sforza</a> (1476–1497), who married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_I_d%27Este" title="Alfonso I d'Este">Alfonso I d'Este</a></li>
</ul>
<p>With his mistress <a href="/wiki/Lucrezia_Landriani" title="Lucrezia Landriani">Lucrezia Landriani</a>, he had at least one illegitimate daughter:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caterina_Sforza" title="Caterina Sforza">Caterina Sforza</a>, who married 3 times: <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Riario" title="Girolamo Riario">Girolamo Riario</a>; <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Feo" title="Giacomo Feo">Giacomo Feo</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_de%27_Medici_il_Popolano" title="Giovanni de' Medici il Popolano">Giovanni de' Medici il Popolano</a></li>
<li>Sforza reportedly had at least 4 other illegitimate children by Lucrezia, and 1 other child by another woman. <a class="external free" href="http://www.kleio.org/en/history/famtree/sforza/631.html" rel="nofollow">http://www.kleio.org/en/history/famtree/sforza/631.html</a></li>
</ul>
