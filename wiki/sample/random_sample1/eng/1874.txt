<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cisalpine Gaul</h1>

<p><b>Cisalpine Gaul</b> (<i>Gallia Cisalpina</i>), also called <i>Gallia Citerior</i> or <i>Gallia Togata</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCC-1">[1]</a></sup> was the part of <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>) during the 4th and 3rd centuries BC. Conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> in the 220s BC, it was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> from c. 81 BC until 42 BC, when it was merged into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italy_(Roman_Empire)" title="Italy (Roman Empire)">Roman Italy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DRR-2">[2]</a></sup> Until that time, it was considered part of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, precisely that part of Gaul on the "hither side of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>" (from the perspective of the Romans), as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis" title="Gallia Narbonensis">Transalpine Gaul</a> ("on the far side of the Alps").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRG_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRG-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gallia Cisalpina was further subdivided into <i>Gallia Cispadana</i> and <i>Gallia Transpadana</i>, i.e. its portions south and north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po River</a>, respectively. The Roman province of the 1st century BC was bounded on the north and west by the Alps, in the south as far as <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">Placentia</a> by the river <a href="/wiki/Po_(river)" title="Po (river)">Po</a>, and then by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apennines" title="Apennines">Apennines</a> and the river <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">Rubicon</a>, and in the east by the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LES_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LES-4">[4]</a></sup> In 49 BC all inhabitants of Cisalpine Gaul received <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">Roman citizenship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGL_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGL-5">[5]</a></sup> and eventually the province was divided among four of the eleven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustan_regions_of_Italy" title="Augustan regions of Italy">regions of Italy</a>: <i>Regio VIII Gallia Cispadana</i>, <i>Regio IX Liguria</i>, <i>Regio X Venetia et Histria</i> and <i>Regio XI Gallia Transpadana</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGR-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cisalpine_Gaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cisalpine_Gaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_peoples_of_Italy" title="Ancient peoples of Italy">Ancient peoples of Italy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="717" data-file-width="949" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg/220px-Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg/330px-Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg/440px-Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallia_Cisalpina-fr.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Peoples of northern Italy during the 4th to 3rd centuries BC.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Canegrate culture (13th century BC) may represent the first migratory wave of the proto-Celtic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> population from the northwest part of the Alps that, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_passes" title="Alpine passes">Alpine passes</a>, penetrated and settled in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po</a> valley between <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">Lake Como</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scamozzina_culture" title="Scamozzina culture">Scamozzina culture</a>). They brought a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funerary" title="Funerary">funerary</a> practice—<a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremation</a>—which supplanted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhumation" title="Inhumation">inhumation</a>. It has also been proposed that a more ancient proto-Celtic presence can be traced back to the beginning of the Middle <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> (XVI-XV century BC), when North Western Italy appears closely linked regarding the production of bronze artifacts, including ornaments, to the western groups of the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus_culture" title="Tumulus culture">Tumulus culture</a> (<a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, 1600 BC - 1200 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The bearers of the Canegrate culture maintained its homogeneity for only a century, after which it melded with the <a href="/wiki/Ligures" title="Ligures">Ligurian</a> aboriginal populations and with this union gave rise to a new phase called the <a href="/wiki/Golasecca_culture" title="Golasecca culture">Golasecca culture</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which is nowadays identified with the Celtic Lepontii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a> (v. 34) has the <a href="/wiki/Insubres" title="Insubres">Insubres</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Bellovesus" title="Bellovesus">Bellovesus</a>, arrive in northern Italy during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarquinius_Priscus" title="Tarquinius Priscus">Tarquinius Priscus</a> (7th-6th century BC), occupying the area between <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cremona" title="Cremona">Cremona</a>. Milan (<i>Mediolanum</i>) itself is presumably a Gaulish foundation of the early 6th century BC, its name having a Celtic etymology of "[city] in the middle of the [Padanic] plain". <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a> in the 2nd century BC wrote about co-existence of the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> with Etruscan nations in the period before the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Allia" title="Battle of the Allia">Sack of Rome</a> in 390 BC.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ligures" title="Ligures">Ligures</a> lived in Northern Mediterranean Coast straddling South-east French and North-west Italian coasts, including parts of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a> island and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>. Ligurian tribes were also present in Latium (see <a href="/wiki/Rutuli" title="Rutuli">Rutuli</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in Samnium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> they called themselves <i>Ambrones</i>, which could indicate a relationship with the <a href="/wiki/Ambrones" title="Ambrones">Ambrones</a> of northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Little is known of the Ligurian language. Only place-names and personal names remain. It appears to be an <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> branch with both <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">Italic</a> and particularly strong <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> affinities. Because of the strong Celtic influences on their language and culture, they were known in antiquity as <b>Celto-Ligurians</b> (in Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Κελτολίγυες</span>, Keltolígues).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baldi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-baldi-16">[16]</a></sup> Modern linguists, like Xavier Delamarre argues that <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(ancient)" title="Ligurian language (ancient)">Ligurian</a> was a Celtic language, similar to, but not the same as Gaulish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Ligurian-Celtic question is also discussed by Barruol (1999). Ancient Ligurian is either listed as Celtic (epigraphic),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kruta1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kruta1-18">[18]</a></sup> or Para-Celtic (onomastic).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kruta2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-kruta2-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Veneti" title="Adriatic Veneti">Veneti</a> were an <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European people</a> who inhabited north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, in an area corresponding to the modern-day region of the <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ven3_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ven3-20">[20]</a></sup> By the 4th century BC the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Veneti" title="Adriatic Veneti">Veneti</a> had been so Celticized that <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a> wrote that the Veneti of the 2nd century BC were identical to the Gauls except for language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> (64 BC–AD 24), on the other hand, conjectured that the Adriatic Veneti were descendant from Celts who in turn were related to later <a href="/wiki/Veneti_(Gaul)" title="Veneti (Gaul)">Celtic tribe of the same name</a> who lived on the Belgian coast and fought against <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>. He further suggested that the identification of the Adriatic Veneti with the Paphlagonian Enetoi led by Antenor — which he attributes to <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a> (496–406 BC) — was a mistake due to the similarity of the names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strabo_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-strabo-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 391 BC, Celts "who had their homes beyond the Alps, streamed through the passes in great strength and seized the territory that lay between the <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">Appennine mountains</a> and the Alps" according to <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> was routed in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Allia" title="Battle of the Allia">battle of Allia</a>, and Rome was sacked in 390 BC by the <a href="/wiki/Senones" title="Senones">Senones</a>.</p>
<p>The defeat of the combined <a href="/wiki/Samnium" title="Samnium">Samnite</a>, Celtic and Etruscan alliance by the Romans in the <a href="/wiki/Samnite_Wars" title="Samnite Wars">Third Samnite War</a> ending in 290 BC sounded the beginning of the end of the Celtic domination in mainland Europe. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Telamon" title="Battle of Telamon">Battle of Telamon</a> in 225 BC, a large Celtic army was trapped between two Roman forces and crushed.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Boii" title="Boii">Boii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insubres" title="Insubres">Insubres</a> allied themselves with the Carthaginians, laying siege to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutina" title="Mutina">Mutina</a> (<a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">Modena</a>). In response, Rome sent an expedition led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L._Manlius_Vulso" title="L. Manlius Vulso">L. Manlius Vulso</a>. Vulso's army was ambushed twice, and the Senate sent <a href="/wiki/Publius_Cornelius_Scipio" title="Publius Cornelius Scipio">Scipio</a> with an additional force to provide support. These were the Roman forces encountered by <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> after his crossing of the Alps. The Romans were defeated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ticinus" title="Battle of the Ticinus">Battle of the Ticinus</a>, leading to all the Gauls except for the <a href="/wiki/Cenomani" title="Cenomani">Cenomani</a> to join the insurgency. Rome then sent the army of <a href="/wiki/Tiberius_Sempronius_Longus_(consul_218_BC)" title="Tiberius Sempronius Longus (consul 218 BC)">Tiberius Sempronius Longus</a> who engaged Hannibal in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Trebia" title="Battle of the Trebia">Battle of the Trebia</a>, also resulting in a Roman defeat, forcing Rome to temporarily abandon Gallia Cisalpina altogether, returning only after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Zama" title="Battle of Zama">defeat of Carthage</a> in 202 BC. Rome conquered the last remaining independent Celtic kingdom in Italy<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2013)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> in 192 BC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_province">Roman province</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cisalpine_Gaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman province">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republican_governors_of_Gaul" title="Roman Republican governors of Gaul">Roman Republican governors of Gaul</a></div>
<p>Sometimes referred to as <i>Gallia Citerior</i> ("Hither Gaul"), <i>Provincia Ariminum</i>, or <i>Gallia Togata</i> ("Toga-wearing Gaul", indicating the region's early Romanization). <i>Gallia Transpadana</i> denoted that part of Cisalpine Gaul between the Padus (now the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po River</a>) and the Alps, while <i>Gallia Cispadana</i> was the part to the south of the river.</p>
<p>Probably officially established around 81 BC, the province was governed from Mutina (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">Modena</a>), where, in 73 BC, forces under <a href="/wiki/Spartacus" title="Spartacus">Spartacus</a> defeated the legion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaius_Cassius_Longinus_2" title="Gaius Cassius Longinus 2">Gaius Cassius Longinus</a>, the provincial governor.</p>
<p>In 49 BC, with the <a href="/wiki/Lex_Roscia" title="Lex Roscia">Lex Roscia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> granted to the populations of the province the full Roman citizenship.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">Rubicon</a> River marked its southern boundary with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italia_(Roman_Empire)" title="Italia (Roman Empire)">Italia</a> proper. By crossing this river in 49 BC with his battle-hardened legions, returning from the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, Julius Caesar precipitated the civil war within the Roman Republic which led, eventually, to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. To this day the term "crossing the Rubicon" means, figuratively, "reaching the point of no return".</p>
<p>The province was merged into Italia about 42 BC, as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Octavian's</a> "Italicization" program during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Triumvirate" title="Second Triumvirate">Second Triumvirate</a>. The dissolution of the <i>provincia</i> required a new governing law or <i>lex</i>, although its contemporary title is unknown. The parts of it inscribed on a bronze tablet preserved in the museum at <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a> are entirely concerned with arranging the judiciary: the law appoints two <i>viri</i> and four <i>viri juri dicundo</i>, and also mentions a <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">Prefect</a> of Mutina.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titus_Livius" title="Titus Livius">Livy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> three famous sons of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Province" title="Roman Province">province</a>, were born in <i>Gallia Cisalpina</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Canegrate_culture">The Canegrate culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cisalpine_Gaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Canegrate culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canegrate_culture" title="Canegrate culture">Canegrate culture</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canegrate_culture" title="Canegrate culture">Canegrate culture</a> reflects a late Bronze Age to early Iron Age culture in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pianura_Padana" title="Pianura Padana">Pianura Padana</a>. These areas are now known as western <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_Ticino" title="Canton Ticino">Canton Ticino</a>.</p>
<p>The Canegrate culture testifies to the arrival of a first "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">proto-Celtic</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> migratory wave of populations from the northwest part of the Alps that, crossing the alpine passes, had not yet infiltrated and settled down in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po</a> area between <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_of_Como" title="Lake of Como">Lake of Como</a>. They were bearers of a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funerary" title="Funerary">funerary</a> practice, which supplanted the old culture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhumation" title="Inhumation">inhumation</a> instead introducing <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremation</a>.</p>
<p>The population of Canegrate maintained its own homogeneity for a limited period of time, approximately a century, after which they blended with the <a href="/wiki/Ligures" title="Ligures">Ligurian</a> aboriginal populations to create a new culture called the <a href="/wiki/Golasecca_culture" title="Golasecca culture">Golasecca culture</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golasecca_culture">Golasecca culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cisalpine_Gaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Golasecca culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Golasecca_culture" title="Golasecca culture">Golasecca culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_of_Golasecca" title="Culture of Golasecca">Culture of Golasecca</a> (9th to 4th centuries BC) spread between the end of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> in the areas of northwestern <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_Ticino" title="Canton Ticino">Canton Ticino</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://img641.imageshack.us/f/italiabronz.png/" rel="nofollow">[3]</a>. At the end of the <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric period</a>, this was an area where travelers frequently stopped and had contact with the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Urnfield_culture" title="Urnfield culture">Urnfield culture</a> to the north and with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villanova_culture" title="Villanova culture">Villanova culture</a> to the south. The Golasecca culture was initially concentrated in the foothills area south of the Alps. It later spread throughout the lakes area, and established many settlements representing this original culture. The oldest remains found thus far can be dated from the 9th century BC.</p>

<p>There is some debate whether the <a href="/wiki/Lepontic_language" title="Lepontic language">Lepontic language</a> should be considered as a Gaulish dialect or an independent branch within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Celtic" title="Continental Celtic">Continental Celtic</a>. Apart from Lepontic, the "Cisalpine Gaulish language" proper would be the Gaulish language as spoken by the Gauls invading northern Italy in the 4th century BC. This is a dialect of the larger Gaulish language, with some known phonetic features distinguishing it from Transalpine dialects, such as <i>-nn-</i> replacing <i>-nd-</i> and <i>s(s)</i> replacing <i>-χs-</i>.</p>
