<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Galilean moons</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>Galilean moons</b> are the four largest <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">moons of Jupiter</a>—<a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto</a>. They were first seen by <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> in January 1610, and recognized by him as satellites of Jupiter in March 1610.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They are the first objects found to orbit another planet. Their names derive from the lovers of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>. They are among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_moons_by_diameter" title="List of moons by diameter">the largest objects</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and the eight <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, with radii larger than any of the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a>. Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System, and is even bigger than the planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>. The three inner moons—Io, Europa, and Ganymede—are in a 4:2:1 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a> with each other.</p>
<p>The Galilean moons were discovered in either 1609 or 1610 when Galileo made improvements to his <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>, which enabled him to observe celestial bodies more distinctly than ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup> Galileo's discovery showed the importance of the telescope as a tool for astronomers by proving that there were objects in space that cannot be seen by the naked eye. More importantly, the incontrovertible discovery of celestial bodies orbiting something other than Earth dealt a serious blow to the then-accepted <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">Ptolemaic world system</a>, or the geocentric theory in which everything orbits around Earth.</p>
<p>Galileo initially named his discovery the <b>Cosmica Sidera</b> ("<a href="/wiki/Cosimo_II_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo</a>'s stars"), but the names that eventually prevailed were chosen by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marius" title="Simon Marius">Simon Marius</a>. Marius discovered the moons independently at the same time as Galileo, and gave them their present names, which were suggested by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a>, in his <i>Mundus Jovialis</i>, published in 1614.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="330" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg/170px-Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg/255px-Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>, the discoverer of the four moons</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a result of improvements <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> made to the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>, with a magnifying capability of 20×,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> he was able to see celestial bodies more distinctly than was ever possible before. This allowed Galilei to discover in either December 1609 or January 1610 what came to be known as the Galilean moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 7, 1610, Galileo wrote a letter containing the first mention of Jupiter's moons. At the time, he saw only three of them, and he believed them to be fixed stars near Jupiter. He continued to observe these <a href="/wiki/Celestial_spheres" title="Celestial spheres">celestial orbs</a> from January 8 to March 2, 1610. In these observations, he discovered a fourth body, and also observed that the four were not fixed stars, but rather were orbiting Jupiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Galileo's discovery proved the importance of the telescope as a tool for astronomers by showing that there were objects in space to be discovered that until then had remained unseen by the naked eye. More importantly, the discovery of celestial bodies orbiting something other than Earth dealt a blow to the then-accepted <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">Ptolemaic world system</a>, which held that Earth was at the center of the universe and all other celestial bodies revolved around it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Galileo's <i><a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">Sidereus Nuncius</a></i> (<i>Starry Messenger</i>), which announced celestial observations through his telescope, does not explicitly mention <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">Copernican heliocentrism</a>, a theory that placed the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> at the center of the universe. Nevertheless, Galileo accepted the Copernican theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Chinese historian of astronomy, <a href="/wiki/Xi_Zezong" title="Xi Zezong">Xi Zezong</a>, has claimed that a "small reddish star" observed near Jupiter in 362 BCE by Chinese astronomer <a href="/wiki/Gan_De" title="Gan De">Gan De</a> may have been <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>, predating Galileo's discovery by around two millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedication_to_the_Medicis">Dedication to the Medicis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dedication to the Medicis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="334" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg/170px-Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg/255px-Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sidereus_Nuncius_Medicean_Stars.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Medician stars in the <i><a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">Sidereus Nuncius</a></i> (the 'starry messenger'), 1610. The moons are drawn in changing positions.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1605, Galileo had been employed as a mathematics tutor for <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_II_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo de' Medici</a>. In 1609, Cosimo became Grand Duke Cosimo II of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>. Galileo, seeking patronage from his now-wealthy former student and his powerful family, used the discovery of Jupiter's moons to gain it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup> On February 13, 1610, Galileo wrote to the Grand Duke's secretary:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"God graced me with being able, through such a singular sign, to reveal to my Lord my devotion and the desire I have that his glorious name live as equal among the stars, and since it is up to me, the first discoverer, to name these new planets, I wish, in imitation of the great sages who placed the most excellent heroes of that age among the stars, to inscribe these with the name of the Most Serene Grand Duke."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Galileo asked whether he should name the moons the "Cosmian Stars", after Cosimo alone, or the "Medician Stars", which would honor all four brothers in the Medici clan. The secretary replied that the latter name would be best.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 12, 1610, Galileo wrote his dedicatory letter to the Duke of Tuscany, and the next day sent a copy to the Grand Duke, hoping to obtain the Grand Duke's support as quickly as possible. On March 19, he sent the telescope he had used to first view Jupiter's moons to the Grand Duke, along with an official copy of <i><a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">Sidereus Nuncius</a></i> (<i>The Starry Messenger</i>) that, following the secretary's advice, named the four moons the Medician Stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup> In his dedicatory introduction, Galileo wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Scarcely have the immortal graces of your soul begun to shine forth on earth than bright stars offer themselves in the heavens which, like tongues, will speak of and celebrate your most excellent virtues for all time. Behold, therefore, four stars reserved for your illustrious name ... which ... make their journeys and orbits with a marvelous speed around the star of Jupiter ... like children of the same family ... Indeed, it appears the Maker of the Stars himself, by clear arguments, admonished me to call these new planets by the illustrious name of Your Highness before all others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galileo89_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Galileo89-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Galileo initially called his discovery the <b>Cosmica Sidera</b> ("Cosimo's stars"), in honour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosimo_II_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo II de' Medici">Cosimo II de' Medici</a> (1590–1621).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At Cosimo's suggestion, Galileo changed the name to <b>Medicea Sidera</b> ("the <b>Medician stars</b>"), honouring all four Medici brothers (Cosimo, Francesco, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_de%27_Medici_(cardinal)" title="Carlo de' Medici (cardinal)">Carlo</a>, and Lorenzo). The discovery was announced in the <i><a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">Sidereus Nuncius</a></i> ("Starry Messenger"), published in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in March 1610, less than two months after the first observations.</p>
<p>Other names put forward include:</p>
<ul>
<li>I. <i>Principharus</i> (for the "prince" of Tuscany), II. <i>Victripharus</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Vittoria_della_Rovere" title="Vittoria della Rovere">Vittoria della Rovere</a>), III. <i>Cosmipharus</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo de' Medici</a>) and IV. <i>Fernipharus</i> (after Duke <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_II_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Ferdinando II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Ferdinando de' Medici</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> – by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Hodierna" title="Giovanni Battista Hodierna">Giovanni Battista Hodierna</a>, a disciple of Galileo and author of the first <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">ephemerides</a> (<i>Medicaeorum Ephemerides</i>, 1656);</li>
<li><i>Circulatores Jovis</i>, or <i>Jovis Comites</i> – by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">Johannes Hevelius</a>;</li>
<li><i>Gardes</i>, or <i>Satellites</i> (from the Latin <i>satelles, satellitis</i>, meaning "escorts") – by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Ozanam" title="Jacques Ozanam">Jacques Ozanam</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The names that eventually prevailed were chosen by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marius" title="Simon Marius">Simon Marius</a>, who discovered the moons independently at the same time as Galileo: he named them at the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> after lovers of the god Zeus (the Greek equivalent of Jupiter): <i><a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto</a></i>, in his <i>Mundus Jovialis</i>, published in 1614.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marazzini_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-marazzini-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Galileo steadfastly refused to use Marius' names and invented as a result the numbering scheme that is still used nowadays, in parallel with proper moon names. The numbers run from Jupiter outward, thus I, II, III and IV for Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marazzini_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-marazzini-11">[11]</a></sup> Galileo used this system in his notebooks but never actually published it. The numbered names (Jupiter <i>x</i>) were used until the mid-20th century when other inner moons were discovered, and Marius' names became widely used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marazzini_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-marazzini-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination_of_longitude">Determination of longitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Determination of longitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_longitude#Galileo.27s_proposal_.E2.80.94_Jovian_moons" title="History of longitude">History of longitude</a></div>
<p>Galileo was able to develop a method of determining <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> based on the timing of the orbits of the Galilean moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The times of the eclipses of the moons could be precisely calculated in advance, and compared with local observations on land or on ship to determine the local time and hence longitude. The main problem with the technique was that it was difficult to observe the Galilean moons through a telescope on a moving ship; a problem that Galileo tried to solve with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Celatone" title="Celatone">celatone</a>. The method was used by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">Cassini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Picard" title="Jean Picard">Picard</a> to re-map <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Members">Members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some models predict that there may have been several generations of Galilean satellites in Jupiter's early history. Each generation of moons to have formed would have spiraled into Jupiter and been destroyed, due to tidal interactions with Jupiter's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-satellite_disk" title="Proto-satellite disk">proto-satellite disk</a>, with new moons forming from the remaining debris. By the time the present generation formed, the gas in the proto-satellite disk had thinned out to the point that it no longer greatly interfered with the moons' orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv0812_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv0812-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-15">[15]</a></sup> Other models suggest that Galilean satellites formed in a proto-satellite disk, in which formation timescales were comparable to or shorter than orbital migration timescales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dangelo_podolak_2015_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dangelo_podolak_2015-16">[16]</a></sup> Io is <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> and likely has an interior of rock and metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv0812_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv0812-14">[14]</a></sup> Europa is thought to contain 8% ice and water by mass with the remainder rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv0812_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv0812-14">[14]</a></sup> These moons are, in increasing order of distance from Jupiter:</p>

<p>Io is the innermost of the four Galilean moons of Jupiter and, with a diameter of 3,642 kilometers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_moons_by_diameter" title="List of moons by diameter">fourth-largest moon</a> in the Solar System. It was named after <a href="/wiki/Io_(mythology)" title="Io (mythology)">Io</a>, a priestess of <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> who became one of the lovers of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>. Nevertheless, it was simply referred to as "Jupiter I", or "The first satellite of Jupiter", until the mid-20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marazzini_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-marazzini-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>With over 400 active volcanos, Io is the most geologically active object in the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopes2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lopes2004-20">[20]</a></sup> Its surface is dotted with more than 100 mountains, some of which are taller than Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schenk2001_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk2001-21">[21]</a></sup> Unlike most satellites in the outer Solar System (which have a thick coating of ice), Io is primarily composed of silicate rock surrounding a molten iron or iron sulfide core.</p>
<p>Although not proven, recent data from the Galileo orbiter indicate that Io might have its own magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Io has an extremely thin atmosphere made up mostly of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> If a surface data or collection vessel were to land on Io in the future, it would have to be extremely tough (similar to the <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a>-like bodies of the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a> landers) to survive the radiation and magnetic fields that originate from Jupiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europa">Europa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Europa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa (moon)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Europa, the second of the four Galilean moons, is the second closest to Jupiter and the smallest at 3121.6 kilometers in diameter, which is slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>. The name comes from a mythical <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> noblewoman, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(mythology)" title="Europa (mythology)">Europa</a>, who was courted by <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and became the queen of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, though the name did not become widely used until the mid-20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marazzini_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-marazzini-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is one of the smoothest objects in the Solar System,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> with a layer of water surrounding the mantle of the planet, thought to be 100 kilometers thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The smooth surface includes a layer of ice, while the bottom of the ice is theorized to be liquid water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EuropaAlbedo_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-EuropaAlbedo-27">[27]</a></sup> The apparent youth and smoothness of the surface have led to the hypothesis that a water ocean exists beneath it, which could conceivably serve as an abode for <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Heat energy from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_flexing" title="Tidal flexing">tidal flexing</a> ensures that the ocean remains liquid and drives geological activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geology_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-geology-29">[29]</a></sup> Life may exist in Europa's under-ice ocean, perhaps subsisting in an environment similar to Earth's deep-ocean <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> or the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Life in such an ocean could possibly be similar to <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microbial</a> life on Earth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_ocean" title="Deep ocean">deep ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> So far, there is no evidence that life exists on Europa, but the likely presence of liquid water has spurred calls to send a probe there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prominent markings that criss-cross the moon seem to be mainly <a href="/wiki/Albedo_feature" title="Albedo feature">albedo features</a>, which emphasize low topography. There are few <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">craters</a> on Europa because its surface is tectonically active and young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Some theories suggest that Jupiter's gravity is causing these markings, as one side of Europa is constantly facing Jupiter. Also, volcanic water eruptions splitting the surface of Europa, and even geysers have been considered as a cause. The color of the markings, reddish-brown, is theorized to be caused by sulfur, but scientists cannot confirm that, because no data collection devices have been sent to Europa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Europa is primarily made of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> rock and likely has an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> core. It has a tenuous atmosphere composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ganymede">Ganymede</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ganymede">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede (moon)</a></div>
<p>Ganymede, the third Galilean moon is named after the mythological <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(mythology)" title="Ganymede (mythology)">Ganymede</a>, cupbearer of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek gods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>'s beloved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Ganymede is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_natural_satellites_by_diameter" title="List of natural satellites by diameter">largest natural satellite</a> in the Solar System at 5262.4 kilometers in diameter, which makes it larger than the planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> – although only at about half of its mass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nineplanets.org-Ganymede_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-nineplanets.org-Ganymede-36">[36]</a></sup> since Ganymede is an icy world. It is the only satellite in the Solar System known to possess a <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>, likely created through <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> within the liquid iron core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ganymede is composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate rock</a> and water ice, and a salt-water ocean is believed to exist nearly 200 km below Ganymede's surface, sandwiched between layers of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The metallic core of Ganymede suggests a greater heat at some time in its past than had previously been proposed. The surface is a mix of two types of terrain—highly cratered dark regions and younger, but still ancient, regions with a large array of grooves and ridges. Ganymede has a high number of craters, but many are gone or barely visible due to its icy crust forming over them. The satellite has a thin <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> that includes O, O<sub>2</sub>, and possibly O<sub>3</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>), and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_hydrogen" title="Atomic hydrogen">atomic hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Callisto">Callisto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Callisto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto (moon)</a></div>
<p>Callisto is the fourth and last Galilean moon, and is the second largest of the four, and at 4820.6 kilometers in diameter, it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_moons_by_diameter" title="List of moons by diameter">the third largest moon</a> in the Solar System. Callisto was a daughter of the Arkadian King Lykaon and a hunting companion of the goddess Artemis. It does not form part of the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a> that affects three inner Galilean satellites and thus does not experience appreciable <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">tidal heating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Callisto is composed of approximately equal amounts of <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">ices</a>, which makes it the least dense of the Galilean moons. It is one of the most heavily cratered satellites in the Solar System, and one major feature is a basin around 3000 km wide called <a href="/wiki/Valhalla_(crater)" title="Valhalla (crater)">Valhalla</a>.</p>
<p>Callisto is surrounded by an extremely thin atmosphere composed of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and probably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_oxygen" title="Molecular oxygen">molecular oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Investigation revealed that Callisto may possibly have a subsurface ocean of liquid water at depths greater than 100 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The likely presence of an ocean within Callisto indicates that it can or could harbor <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">life</a>. However, this is less likely than on nearby <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Callisto has long been considered the most suitable place for a human base for future exploration of the Jupiter system since it is furthest from the intense radiation of Jupiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Fluctuations in the orbits of the moons indicate that their mean density decreases with distance from Jupiter. Callisto, the outermost and least dense of the four, has a density intermediate between ice and rock whereas Io, the innermost and densest moon, has a density intermediate between rock and iron. Callisto has an ancient, heavily cratered and unaltered ice surface and the way it rotates indicates that its density is equally distributed, suggesting that it has no rocky or metallic core but consists of a homogeneous mix of rock and ice. This may well have been the original structure of all the moons. The rotation of the three inner moons, in contrast, indicates differentiation of their interiors with denser matter at the core and lighter matter above. They also reveal significant alteration of the surface. Ganymede reveals past tectonic movement of the ice surface which required partial melting of subsurface layers. Europa reveals more dynamic and recent movement of this nature, suggesting a thinner ice crust. Finally, Io, the innermost moon, has a sulfur surface, active volcanism and no sign of ice. All this evidence suggests that the nearer a moon is to Jupiter the hotter its interior. The current model is that the moons experience tidal heating as a result of the gravitational field of Jupiter in inverse proportion to the square of their distance from the giant planet. In all but Callisto this will have melted the interior ice, allowing rock and iron to sink to the interior and water to cover the surface. In Ganymede a thick and solid ice crust then formed. In warmer Europa a thinner more easily broken crust formed. In Io the heating is so extreme that all the rock has melted and water has long ago boiled out into space.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution">Origin and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jupiter's regular satellites are believed to have formed from a circumplanetary disk, a ring of accreting gas and solid debris analogous to a <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2009_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2009-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alibert2005_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alibert2005-49">[49]</a></sup> They may be the remnants of a score of Galilean-mass satellites that formed early in Jupiter's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2009_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2009-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="286" data-file-width="456" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png/300px-Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png/450px-Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/01/Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Masses_of_Jovian_moons.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The relative masses of the Jovian moons. Those smaller than Europa are not visible at this scale, and combined would only be visible at 100× magnification.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Simulations suggest that, while the disk had a relatively high mass at any given moment, over time a substantial fraction (several tenths of a percent) of the mass of Jupiter captured from the Solar nebula was processed through it. However, the disk mass of only 2% that of Jupiter is required to explain the existing satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2009_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2009-48">[48]</a></sup> Thus there may have been several generations of Galilean-mass satellites in Jupiter's early history. Each generation of moons would have spiraled into Jupiter, due to drag from the disk, with new moons then forming from the new debris captured from the Solar nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2009_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2009-48">[48]</a></sup> By the time the present (possibly fifth) generation formed, the disk had thinned out to the point that it no longer greatly interfered with the moons' orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-15">[15]</a></sup> The current Galilean moons were still affected, falling into and being partially protected by an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a> which still exists for Io, Europa, and Ganymede. Ganymede's larger mass means that it would have migrated inward at a faster rate than Europa or Io.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2009_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2009-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visibility">Visibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galilean_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Visibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter(2)_(by).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="485" data-file-width="961" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg/180px-Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg/270px-Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg/360px-Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter%282%29_%28by%29.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Bresson_-_Jupiter(2)_(by).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Galilean moons seen with an amateur telescope.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All four Galilean moons are bright enough that they could be sighted from Earth without a telescope, if they were farther away from Jupiter. (They are, however, easily visible with even low-powered <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>.) They have <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitudes</a> between 4.6 and 5.6 when Jupiter is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy)" title="Opposition (astronomy)">opposition</a> with the Sun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplfact_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-jplfact-50">[50]</a></sup> and are about one unit of magnitude dimmer when Jupiter is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">conjunction</a>. The main difficulty in observing the moons from Earth is their proximity to Jupiter since they are obscured by its brightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The maximum angular separations of the moons are between 2 and 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">minutes of arc</a> from Jupiter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> which is close to the limit of human <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">visual acuity</a>. Ganymede and Callisto, at their maximum separation, are the likeliest targets for potential naked-eye observation.</p>
