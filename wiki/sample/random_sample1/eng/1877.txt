<p><b>Galba</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Servius Sulpicius Galba Caesar Augustus</i></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> 24 December 3 BC – 15 January 69), pron. <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> for seven months from 68 to 69. Galba was the governor of <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Tarraconensis" title="Hispania Tarraconensis">Hispania Tarraconensis</a>, and made a bid for the throne during the rebellion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vindex" title="Vindex">Julius Vindex</a>. He was the first emperor of the <a href="/wiki/Year_of_the_Four_Emperors" title="Year of the Four Emperors">Year of the Four Emperors</a> and the last emperor born in the First Century BC.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_family_life">Origins and family life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and family life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born as <b>Servius Sulpicius Galba</b> near <a href="/wiki/Terracina" title="Terracina">Terracina</a>, "on the left as you go towards <a href="/wiki/Fondi" title="Fondi">Fundi</a>" in the words of <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through his paternal grandfather ("more eminent for his learning than for his rank — for he did not advance beyond the grade of praetor" and who "published a voluminous and painstaking history", and, according to Suetonius, predicted his rise to power),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> he was descended from <a href="/wiki/Servius_Sulpicius_Galba_(praetor)" title="Servius Sulpicius Galba (praetor)">Servius Sulpicius Galba</a>. Galba's father attained the consulship, and although he was short, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunchback" title="Hunchback">hunchbacked</a> and only an indifferent speaker, was an industrious pleader at the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(law)" title="Bar (law)">bar</a>.</p>
<p>His mother was <a href="/wiki/Mummia_Achaica" title="Mummia Achaica">Mummia Achaica</a>, the granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Lutatius_Catulus_(Capitolinus)" title="Quintus Lutatius Catulus (Capitolinus)">Lutatius Catulus</a> (cos. 78) and great-granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Mummius_Achaicus" title="Lucius Mummius Achaicus">Lucius Mummius Achaicus</a>. They only had one other child, an elder son called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gaius_Sulpicius_Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gaius Sulpicius Galba (page does not exist)">Gaius</a> who left Rome after squandering the greater part of his estate, and committed suicide because Tiberius dishonored him by preventing him from taking part in the <a href="/wiki/Senatorial_province" title="Senatorial province">allotment of the provinces</a> <a href="/wiki/Cursus_honorum" title="Cursus honorum">in his year</a>. His father married a second wife, <a href="/wiki/Livia_Ocellina" title="Livia Ocellina">Livia Ocellina</a>, a distant kinswoman of the empress Livia. She later adopted Galba, so he took her names, remaining <b>Lucius Livius Ocella Sulpicius Galba</b> until becoming emperor.</p>
<p>His was a noble family, and he was a man of great wealth, but was unconnected by birth and only very, very remotely by <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a> with any of the first six Caesars. In his early years he was regarded as a youth of remarkable abilities, and it is said that both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> prophesied his future eminence (<a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_(Tacitus)" title="Annals (Tacitus)">Annals</a></i>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/cgi-bin/ptext?lookup=Tac.+Ann.+6.20" rel="nofollow">vi. 20</a>; <a class="external text" href="http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Suetonius/12Caesars/Galba*.html#4" rel="nofollow">Suet. Galba 4</a>; Dio 57.19.4).</p>
<p>His wife, Aemilia Lepida, however, was connected by the marriages of some of her relatives to some of the Julii-Claudii. They had two sons, probably Gaius and Servius (most likely Livius Ocella Galba), who died during their father's life. The elder son was born circa 25 AD. Hardly anything is known about his life as he died young. He was engaged to his step-sister Antonia Postuma, but they never wed, which leads modern historians to believe that he died during this time. Their engagement is dated to 48, and that is generally believed to be his time of death.</p>
<p>The date of birth of the younger son occurred later than 25 but before 30. This Galba outlived his older brother. He was a quaestor in 58, but he was never seen in politics after that. His time of death is generally believed to be around 60 AD. Galba Minor was never married and had no children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a>'s description of Galba was that "In sexual matters he was more inclined to males, and then none but the hard bodied and those past their prime".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This seems to be the only case in Roman history where a named individual male is stated to prefer adult males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service">Public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He became <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">Praetor</a> in 20, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consul</a> in 33; he earned a reputation in the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa_(Roman_province)" title="Africa (Roman province)">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> for his military capability, strictness and impartiality. On the death of <a href="/wiki/Caligula" title="Caligula">Caligula</a>, he refused the invitation of his friends to make a bid for the empire, and loyally served <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a>. For the first half of <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a>'s reign he lived in retirement, until 61, when the emperor bestowed on him the province of <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Tarraconensis" title="Hispania Tarraconensis">Hispania Tarraconensis</a>.</p>
<p>In the spring of 68, Galba was informed of Nero's intention to put him to death, and of the insurrection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vindex" title="Vindex">Julius Vindex</a> in Gaul. He was at first inclined to follow the example of Vindex, but the defeat at Vesontio (Besançon) and suicide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> of the latter renewed Galba's hesitation. It was said that the <a href="/wiki/Courtier" title="Courtier">courtier</a> <a href="/wiki/Calvia_Crispinilla" title="Calvia Crispinilla">Calvia Crispinilla</a> was behind his defection from Nero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-abit-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The news that <a href="/wiki/Nymphidius_Sabinus" title="Nymphidius Sabinus">Nymphidius Sabinus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">Prefect</a>, had given him his favour revived Galba's spirits. Until now, he had only dared to call himself the <a href="/wiki/Legatus" title="Legatus">legate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">senate</a> and Roman people; after Nero's <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, he assumed the title of <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">Caesar</a></i>, and marched straight for <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.</p>
<p>Following Nero's death, Nymphidius Sabinus sought to seize power prior to the arrival of Galba, but he could not win the loyalty of the Praetorian guard and was killed. Upon Galba's approach to the city in October, he was met by soldiers presenting demands; Galba replied by killing many of them.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule">Rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Galba's primary concern during his brief reign was restoring state finances, and to this end he undertook a number of unpopular measures, the most dangerous was his refusal to pay the praetorians the reward promised in his name. Galba scorned the notion that soldiers should be "<a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribed</a>" for their loyalty. He was notoriously cruel throughout the Empire; according to the historian <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a>, Galba levied massive taxes against areas that were slow to receive him as Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suetonius_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suetonius-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also sentenced many to death without trial, and rarely accepted requests for citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suetonius_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suetonius-10">[10]</a></sup> He further disgusted the populace by his meanness and dislike of pomp and display. Advanced age destroyed his energy, and he was entirely in the hands of favourites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suetonius_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Suetonius-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three of these — <a href="/wiki/Titus_Vinius" title="Titus Vinius">Titus Vinius</a>, who became Galba's colleague as <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Laco" title="Cornelius Laco">Cornelius Laco</a>, the commander of the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guard</a>, and Galba's <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedman</a> Icelus Marcianus — were said to virtually control the emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suetonius_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Suetonius-10">[10]</a></sup> The three were called "The Three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedagogue" title="Pedagogue">Pedagogues</a>" because of their influence on Galba. All this made the new emperor gravely unpopular.</p>
<p>During the later period of his provincial administration, Galba was indolent and apathetic, but this was due either to a desire not to attract Nero's notice or to the growing infirmities of age. <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> says all pronounced him worthy of the empire, until he became emperor ("<i>omnium consensū cāpax imperiī nisi imperasset</i>").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_mutiny_on_the_frontier">Military mutiny on the frontier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military mutiny on the frontier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 1 January 69, two legions in <a href="/wiki/Germania_Superior" title="Germania Superior">Germania Superior</a> refused to swear loyalty to Galba. They toppled his statues, demanding that a new emperor be chosen. On the following day, the soldiers of <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Germania Inferior</a> also rebelled and took the decision of who should be the next emperor into their own hands, proclaiming the governor of the province, <a href="/wiki/Vitellius" title="Vitellius">Vitellius</a>, as Emperor.</p>
<p>This outbreak of revolt made Galba aware of his own unpopularity and of the general discontent. In order to check the rising storm, he adopted as his heir and successor <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Calpurnius_Piso_Licinianus" title="Lucius Calpurnius Piso Licinianus">L. Calpurnius Piso</a>. The populace regarded the choice of successor as a sign of fear and the Praetorians were indignant, because the usual donative was not forthcoming. Furthermore, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._Salvius_Otho" title="M. Salvius Otho">M. Salvius Otho</a>, who was expecting to be adopted, was alienated by the choice of Piso.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination_.28January_69.29">Assassination (January 69)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Assassination (January 69)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Otho had governed <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a> and was one of Galba's earliest supporters, he was disappointed at the selection of Piso and entered into communication with the discontented Praetorians, who hailed him as their emperor on 15 January 69. Galba at once set out to meet the rebels, though he was so feeble that he had to be carried in a litter. According to Suetonius, Galba prior to his death had put on a linen corset—although remarking that it had little protection against so many swords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was met by a troop of Otho's cavalry and was killed near <a href="/wiki/Lacus_Curtius" title="Lacus Curtius">Lacus Curtius</a>. One guard, <a href="/wiki/Centurion" title="Centurion">centurion</a> <a href="/wiki/Sempronius_Densus" title="Sempronius Densus">Sempronius Densus</a>, died defending him. Piso was killed shortly afterwards. According to <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, during Galba's last moments he offered his neck, and said, "Strike, if it be for the good of the Romans!"</p>
<p>After his death, Galba's head was brought to Otho, who gave it to his camp followers who paraded and mocked it—the camp followers' mocking was their angry response to a remark by Galba that his strength was unimpaired. The head was then bought by a freedman so he could throw it on the place where his former master had been executed on Galba's orders. Galba's steward buried both head and trunk in a tomb by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurelian_Road" title="Aurelian Road">Aurelian Road</a>.</p>
