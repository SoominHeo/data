<p>The <b>brown antechinus</b> (<i>Antechinus stuartii</i>), also known as <b>Stuart's antechinus</b> and <b>Macleay's marsupial mouse</b>, is a species of small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivorous" title="Carnivorous">carnivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Dasyuridae" title="Dasyuridae">Dasyuridae</a>. The males die after their first breeding season, and the species holds the world record for being the world's smallest <a href="/wiki/Semelparity_and_iteroparity" title="Semelparity and iteroparity">semelparous</a> mammal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_antechinus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Antechinus stuartii</i> is mostly light brown above, including the upper surfaces of its feet, and a lighter brown below and on its tail. Its body length is 93â130Â mm (3.7â5.1 in) and its tail 92â120Â mm (3.6â4.7 in), and it weighs 16â44 grams (0.5â1.5 oz). Unlike in other members of <i><a href="/wiki/Antechinus" title="Antechinus">Antechinus</a></i>, no pale-coloured eye ring occurs. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antechinus_agilis" title="Antechinus agilis">Antechinus agilis</a></i> is similar in appearance and difficult to distinguish except by its distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Menkhorst_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Menkhorst-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_antechinus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brown antechinus was only the third in its genus to be described and as such, until recently, has included species such as the <a href="/wiki/Agile_antechinus" title="Agile antechinus">agile antechinus</a> (<i>Antechinus agilis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_antechinus" title="Subtropical antechinus">subtropical antechinus</a> (<i>Antechinus subtropicus</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_antechinus" title="Tropical antechinus">tropical antechinus</a> (<i>Antechinus adustus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Menkhorst_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Menkhorst-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56</sup> It has also been included itself with the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-footed_antechinus" title="Yellow-footed antechinus">yellow-footed antechinus</a> as the subspecies <i>A. flavipes burrelli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strahan_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strahan-5">[5]</a></sup> It was described in 1841 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Sharp_Macleay" title="William Sharp Macleay">William Sharp Macleay</a>, who named the species in honour of his friend and fellow naturalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Stuart_(1802-1842)" title="James Stuart (1802-1842)">James Stuart</a>, who had discovered the animal at Spring Cove (North Head) in 1837 while working as surgeon in charge of the Quarantine Station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_antechinus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brown antechinus is mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, and females build large communal nests shared by many individuals. Like all <a href="/wiki/Antechinus" title="Antechinus">antechinuses</a>, the males die after their first breeding season (which lasts two weeks) as a result of stress and exhaustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Menkhorst_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Menkhorst-4">[4]</a></sup> Female brown antechinuses do not possess a <a href="/wiki/Pouch_(marsupial)" title="Pouch (marsupial)">pouch</a>; the young must attach themselves to the teats (of which there are usually eight). Its diet includes <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphipod" title="Amphipod">amphipods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a>, although it is an opportunistic feeder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strahan_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strahan-5">[5]</a></sup> The litter size is six or seven young.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_antechinus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brown antechinus is found east of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dividing_Range" title="Great Dividing Range">Great Dividing Range</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, from southeastern <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> to around <a href="/wiki/Kioloa,_New_South_Wales" title="Kioloa, New South Wales">Kioloa, New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strahan_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strahan-5">[5]</a></sup> It is mostly found in forested habitats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strahan_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strahan-5">[5]</a></sup> with dense lower ground cover and low fire frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
