<p>The <b>sensory nervous system</b> is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> responsible for processing <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sensory</a> information. A sensory system consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_receptor" title="Sensory receptor">sensory receptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neural_pathway" title="Neural pathway">neural pathways</a>, and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> involved in sensory perception. Commonly recognized sensory systems are those for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_(sense)" title="Vision (sense)">vision</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory</a> (hearing), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatic_sensation" title="Somatic sensation">somatic sensation</a> (touch), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustatory_system" title="Gustatory system">gustatory</a> (taste), <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">olfaction</a> (smell) and <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">vestibular</a> (balance/movement). In short, senses are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transducers" title="Transducers">transducers</a> from the physical world to the realm of the mind where we interpret the information, creating our <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> of the world around us.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Receptive_field" title="Receptive field">receptive field</a> is the specific part of the world to which a receptor organ and receptor cells respond. For instance, the part of the world an eye can see, is its receptive field; the light that each <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone</a> can see, is its receptive field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Receptive_field" title="Receptive field">Receptive fields</a> have been identified for the <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">visual system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensory system</a>, so far.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stimulus">Stimulus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stimulus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sensory systems code for four aspects of a <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimulus</a>; type (<a href="#Modality">modality</a>), intensity, location, and duration. Arrival <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> of a sound <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> differences of continuous sound are used for localization of sound sources. Certain receptors are sensitive to certain types of stimuli (for example, different <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">mechanoreceptors</a> respond best to different kinds of touch stimuli, like sharp or blunt objects). Receptors <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">send impulses</a> in certain patterns to send information about the intensity of a stimulus (for example, how loud a sound is). The location of the receptor that is stimulated gives the brain information about the location of the stimulus (for example, stimulating a mechanoreceptor in a finger will send information to the brain about that finger). The duration of the stimulus (how long it lasts) is conveyed by firing patterns of receptors. These impulses are transmitted to the brain through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber" title="Afferent nerve fiber">afferent neurons</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Senses_and_receptors">Senses and receptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Senses and receptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While there is debate among neurologists as to the specific number of senses due to differing definitions of what constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> classified five ‘traditional’ human senses which have become universally accepted: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch" title="Touch">touch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">sight</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">hearing</a>. Other senses that have been well-accepted in most mammals, including humans, include <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">nociception</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">equilibrioception</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinaesthesia" title="Kinaesthesia">kinaesthesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thermoception" title="Thermoception">thermoception</a>. Furthermore, some non-human animals have been shown to possess alternate senses, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetoception" title="Magnetoception">magnetoception</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electroreception" title="Electroreception">electroreception</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Receptors">Receptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Receptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_receptor" title="Sensory receptor">sensory receptor</a></div>
<p>The initialization of sensation stems from the response of a specific receptor to a physical <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimulus</a>. The receptors which react to the stimulus and initiate the process of sensation are commonly characterized in four distinct categories: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoreceptors" title="Chemoreceptors">chemoreceptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">photoreceptors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptors" title="Mechanoreceptors">mechanoreceptors</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoreceptors" title="Thermoreceptors">thermoreceptors</a>. All receptors receive distinct physical stimuli and transduce the signal into an electrical <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>. This action potential then travels along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber" title="Afferent nerve fiber">afferent neurons</a> to specific brain regions where it is processed and interpreted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemoreceptors">Chemoreceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemoreceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor" title="Chemoreceptor">Chemoreceptor</a></div>
<p>Chemoreceptors, or chemosensors, detect certain chemical stimuli and transduce that signal into an electrical <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>. There are two primary types of chemoreceptors:</p>
<ul>
<li>Distance chemoreceptors are integral to receiving stimuli in the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a> through both olfactory receptor neurons and neurons in the <a href="/wiki/Vomeronasal_organ" title="Vomeronasal organ">vomeronasal organ</a>.</li>
<li>Direct chemoreceptors include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taste_buds" title="Taste buds">taste buds</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustatory_system" title="Gustatory system">gustatory system</a> as well as receptors in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aortic_bodies" title="Aortic bodies">aortic bodies</a> which detect changes in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoreceptors">Photoreceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photoreceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">Photoreceptor cell</a></div>
<p>Photoreceptors are capable of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phototransduction" title="Phototransduction">phototransduction</a>, a process which converts light (<a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>) into, among other types of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">membrane potential</a>. There are three primary types of photoreceptors: <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">Cones</a> are photoreceptors which respond significantly to <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a>. In humans the three different types of cones correspond with a primary response to short wavelength (blue), medium wavelength (green), and long wavelength (yellow/red).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_2010-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">Rods</a> are photoreceptors which are very sensitive to the intensity of light, allowing for vision in dim lighting. The concentrations and ratio of rods to cones is strongly correlated with whether an animal is <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>. In humans rods outnumber cones by approximately 20:1, while in nocturnal animals, such as the <a href="/wiki/Tawny_owl" title="Tawny owl">tawny owl</a>, the ratio is closer to 1000:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_2010_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_2010-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">Ganglion Cells</a> reside in the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_medulla" title="Adrenal medulla">adrenal medulla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> where they are involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sympathetic_response" title="Sympathetic response">sympathetic response</a>. Of the ~1.3 million ganglion cells present in the retina, 1-2% are believed to be <a href="/wiki/Intrinsically_photosensitive_retinal_ganglion_cells" title="Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells">photosensitive ganglia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These photosensitive ganglia play a role in conscious vision for some animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ecker_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ecker-8">[8]</a></sup> and are believed to do the same in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoriguchiWinawer2012_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoriguchiWinawer2012-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanoreceptors">Mechanoreceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mechanoreceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">Mechanoreceptor</a></div>
<p>Mechanoreceptors are sensory receptors which respond to mechanical forces, such as <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Distortion" title="Distortion">distortion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While mechanoreceptors are present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hair_cells" title="Hair cells">hair cells</a> and play an integral role in the <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">vestibular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory system</a>, the majority of mechanoreceptors are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutaneous" title="Cutaneous">cutaneous</a> and are grouped into four categories:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Slowly Adapting type 1 Receptors</i> have small receptive fields and respond to static stimulation. These receptors are primarily used in the sensations of <a href="/wiki/Shape" title="Shape">form</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_roughness" title="Surface roughness">roughness</a>.</li>
<li><i>Slowly Adapting type 2 Receptors</i> have large receptive fields and respond to stretch. Similarly to type 1, they produce sustained responses to a continued stimuli.</li>
<li><i>Rapidly Adapting Receptors</i> have small receptive fields and underlie the perception of slip.</li>
<li><i>Pacinian Receptors</i> have large receptive fields and are the predominant receptors for high frequency vibration.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoreceptors">Thermoreceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thermoreceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermoreceptor" title="Thermoreceptor">Thermoreceptor</a></div>
<p>Thermoreceptors are sensory receptors which respond to varying <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a>. While the mechanisms through which these receptors operate is unclear, recent discoveries have shown that <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> have at least two distinct types of thermoreceptors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krantz_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-krantz-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The End-Bulb of Krause, or <a href="/wiki/Bulboid_corpuscle" title="Bulboid corpuscle">bulboid corpuscle</a>, detects temperatures above body temperature</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruffini_corpuscle" title="Ruffini corpuscle">Ruffini’s end organ</a> detects temperatures below body temperature</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nociceptors">Nociceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nociceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nociceptor" title="Nociceptor">Nociceptor</a></div>
<p>Nociceptors respond to potentially damaging stimuli by sending signals to the spinal cord and brain. This process, called <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">nociception</a>, usually causes the perception of <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They are found in internal organs as well as on the surface of the body. Nociceptors detect different kinds of damaging stimuli or actual damage. Those that only respond when tissues are damaged are known as "sleeping" or "silent" nociceptors.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Thermal</i> nociceptors are activated by noxious heat or cold at various temperatures.</li>
<li><i>Mechanical</i> nociceptors respond to excess pressure or mechanical deformation.</li>
<li><i>Chemical</i> nociceptors respond to a wide variety of chemicals, some of which are signs of tissue damage. They are involved in the detection of some spices in food.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensory_cortex">Sensory cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sensory cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> received by the <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptors</a> listed above are <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">transduced</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>, which is carried along one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber" title="Afferent nerve fiber">afferent neurons</a> towards a specific area of the brain. While the term <a href="/wiki/Sensory_cortex" title="Sensory cortex">sensory cortex</a> is often used informally to refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex" title="Somatosensory cortex">somatosensory cortex</a>, the term more accurately refers to the multiple areas of the brain at which <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a> are received to be processed. For the five <a href="/wiki/Sense#Traditional_senses" title="Sense">traditional senses</a> in humans, this includes the primary and secondary <a href="/wiki/Sensory_cortex" title="Sensory cortex">cortexes of the different senses</a>: the somatosensory cortex, the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Primary_olfactory_cortex" title="Primary olfactory cortex">primary olfactory cortex</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gustatory_cortex" title="Gustatory cortex">gustatory cortex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brynie.2C_F.H._2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brynie.2C_F.H._2009-13">[13]</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_modality" title="Stimulus modality">modalities</a> have corresponding sensory cortex areas as well, including the <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_cortex" title="Vestibular cortex">vestibular cortex</a> for the sense of balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Somatosensory_cortex">Somatosensory cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Somatosensory cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in the <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal lobe</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex" title="Somatosensory cortex">somatosensory cortex</a> is the primary receptive area for the sense of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch" title="Touch">touch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">proprioception</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensory system</a>. This cortex is further divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brodmann_areas" title="Brodmann areas">Brodmann areas</a> 1, 2, and 3. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_3" title="Brodmann area 3">Brodmann area 3</a> is considered the primary processing center of the somatosensory cortex as it receives significantly more input from the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>, has neurons highly responsive to somatosensory stimuli, and can evoke somatic sensations through electrical <a href="/wiki/Stimulation" title="Stimulation">stimulation</a>. Areas 1 and 2 receive most of their input from area 3. There are also pathways for <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">proprioception</a> (via the <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Motor_neuron" title="Motor neuron">motor</a> control (via <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_4" title="Brodmann area 4">Brodmann area 4</a>). See also: S2 <a href="/wiki/Secondary_somatosensory_cortex" title="Secondary somatosensory cortex">Secondary somatosensory cortex</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_cortex">Visual cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Visual cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The visual cortex refers to the primary visual cortex, labeled V1 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_17" title="Brodmann area 17">Brodmann area 17</a>, as well as the extrastriate visual cortical areas V2-V5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Located in the <a href="/wiki/Occipital_lobe" title="Occipital lobe">occipital lobe</a>, V1 acts as the primary relay station for visual input, transmitting information to two primary pathways labeled the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsal_stream" title="Dorsal stream">dorsal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral_stream" title="Ventral stream">ventral streams</a>. The dorsal stream includes areas V2 and V5, and is used in interpreting visual ‘where’ and ‘how.’ The ventral stream includes areas V2 and V4, and is used in interpreting ‘what.’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Increases in <a href="/wiki/Task-negative" title="Task-negative">Task-negative</a> activity are observed in the ventral attention network, after abrupt changes in sensory stimuli,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> at the onset and offset of task blocks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and at the end of a completed trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auditory_cortex">Auditory cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Auditory cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>, the auditory cortex is the primary receptive area for sound information. The auditory cortex is composed of Brodmann areas 41 and 42, also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior_transverse_temporal_area_41" title="Anterior transverse temporal area 41">anterior transverse temporal area 41</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_transverse_temporal_area_42" title="Posterior transverse temporal area 42">posterior transverse temporal area 42</a>, respectively. Both areas act similarly and are integral in receiving and processing the signals transmitted from <a href="/wiki/Hair_cell" title="Hair cell">auditory receptors</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_olfactory_cortex">Primary olfactory cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Primary olfactory cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in the temporal lobe, the <a href="/wiki/Primary_olfactory_cortex" title="Primary olfactory cortex">primary olfactory cortex</a> is the primary receptive area for <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">olfaction</a>, or smell. Unique to the olfactory and gustatory systems, at least in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, is the implementation of both <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central</a> mechanisms of action. The peripheral mechanisms involve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor_neurons" title="Olfactory receptor neurons">olfactory receptor neurons</a> which <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">transduce</a> a chemical signal along the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_nerve" title="Olfactory nerve">olfactory nerve</a>, which terminates in the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a>. The chemo-receptors involved in olfactory nervous cascade involve using G-protein receptors to send their chemical signals down said cascade. The central mechanisms include the convergence of olfactory nerve <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> into <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus" title="Glomerulus">glomeruli</a> in the olfactory bulb, where the signal is then transmitted to the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_olfactory_nucleus" title="Anterior olfactory nucleus">anterior olfactory nucleus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Piriform_cortex" title="Piriform cortex">piriform cortex</a>, the medial <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Entorhinal_cortex" title="Entorhinal cortex">entorhinal cortex</a>, all of which make up the primary olfactory cortex.</p>
<p>In contrast to vision and hearing, the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulbs</a> are not cross-hemispheric; the right bulb connects to the right hemisphere and the left bulb connects to the left hemisphere.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gustatory_cortex">Gustatory cortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gustatory cortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gustatory_cortex" title="Gustatory cortex">gustatory cortex</a> is the primary receptive area for <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>. The word <i>taste</i> is used in a technical sense to refer specifically to sensations coming from taste buds on the tongue. The five qualities of taste detected by the tongue include sourness, bitterness, sweetness, saltiness, and the protein taste quality, called <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">umami</a>. In contrast, the term <i>flavor</i> refers to the experience generated through integration of taste with smell and tactile information. The gustatory cortex consists of two primary structures: the <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">anterior insula</a>, located on the <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">insular lobe</a>, and the frontal <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(brain)" title="Operculum (brain)">operculum</a>, located on the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">frontal lobe</a>. Similarly to the olfactory cortex, the gustatory pathway operates through both peripheral and central mechanisms. Peripheral <a href="/wiki/Taste_receptor" title="Taste receptor">taste receptors</a>, located on the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">soft palate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, transmit the received signal to primary sensory axons, where the signal is projected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleus_of_the_solitary_tract" title="Nucleus of the solitary tract">nucleus of the solitary tract</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla</a>, or the gustatory nucleus of the solitary tract complex. The signal is then transmitted to the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>, which in turn projects the signal to several regions of the <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" title="Neocortex">neocortex</a>, including the gustatory cortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The neural processing of taste is affected at nearly every stage of processing by concurrent somatosensory information from the tongue, that is, <a href="/wiki/Mouthfeel" title="Mouthfeel">mouthfeel</a>. Scent, in contrast, is not combined with taste to create flavor until higher cortical processing regions, such as the insula and orbitofrontal cortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_sensory_system">Human sensory system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_nervous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Human sensory system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The human sensory system consists of the following sub-systems:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">Visual system</a> consists of the <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">photoreceptor cells</a>, optic nerve, and V1.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">Auditory system</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">Somatosensory system</a> consists of the receptors, transmitters (pathways) leading to S1, and S1 that experiences the sensations labelled as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactition" title="Tactition">touch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> (warm or cold), <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Itch" title="Itch">itch</a> and tickle), and the sensations of muscle movement and joint position including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_position" title="Human position">posture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_musculoskeletal_system" title="Human musculoskeletal system">movement</a>, and facial expression (collectively also called proprioception).</li>
