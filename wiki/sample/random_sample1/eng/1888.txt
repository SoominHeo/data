<p><b>Gambela</b> or <b>Gambella</b>, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a>: <span lang="am" xml:lang="am">ጋምቤላ</span><a href="/wiki/Help:Multilingual_support_(Ethiopic)" title="Help:Multilingual support (Ethiopic)"><sup><span class="t nihongo icon" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;text-decoration:none;padding:0 .1em;">?</span></sup></a>) also official known as <b>Gambela Peoples' Region</b>, is one of the nine <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Ethiopia" title="Regions of Ethiopia">ethnic divisions</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kililoch" title="Kililoch">kililoch</a></i>) of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>. Previously known as "Region 12", its capital is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambela_(city)" title="Gambela (city)">Gambela</a>. The Region is situated between the <a href="/wiki/Baro_River" title="Baro River">Baro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akobo_River" title="Akobo River">Akobo Rivers</a>, with its western part including the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baro_salient&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baro salient (page does not exist)">Baro salient</a>.</p>
<p>Located in Gambela is <a href="/wiki/Gambela_National_Park" title="Gambela National Park">Gambela National Park</a>, which covers approximately 5,061 square kilometers or 17% of the Region's territory.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gambela_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on the 2007 Census conducted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Statistical_Agency_(Ethiopia)" title="Central Statistical Agency (Ethiopia)">Central Statistical Agency</a> of Ethiopia (CSA), the Gambela Region has total population of 307,096, consisting of 159,787 men and 147,309 women; urban inhabitants number 77,925 or 25.37% of the population. With an estimated area of 29,782.82 square kilometers, this region has an estimated density of 10 people per square kilometer. For the entire Region 66,467 households were counted, which results in an average for the Region of 4.6 persons to a household, with urban households having on average 3.8 and rural households 4.9 people. The Gambela Region is mainly inhabited by various <a href="/wiki/Nilotic_peoples" title="Nilotic peoples">Nilotic</a> ethnic minority populations (<a href="/wiki/Nuer_people" title="Nuer people">Nuer</a> 46.66%, <a href="/wiki/Anuak_people" title="Anuak people">Anuak</a> 21.16%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mezhenger" title="Mezhenger">Mezhenger</a> 4%), as well as some <a href="/wiki/Omotic_languages" title="Omotic languages">Omotic</a> groups (<a href="/wiki/Kafficho_people" title="Kafficho people">Kafficho</a> 5.04%, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shakacho_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shakacho people (page does not exist)">Shakacho</a> 2.27%), <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic</a> populations (<a href="/wiki/Amhara_people" title="Amhara people">Amhara</a> 8.42%, <a href="/wiki/Oromo_people" title="Oromo people">Oromo</a> 4.83%, <a href="/wiki/Kambaata_people" title="Kambaata people">Kambaata</a> 1.44%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigray-Tigrinya_people" title="Tigray-Tigrinya people">Tigray</a> 1.32%), and other ethnic groups predominantly from southern Ethiopia 4.86%. <a href="/wiki/Nuer_language" title="Nuer language">Nuer</a> is spoken as a first language by 48.35%, 22.02% speak <a href="/wiki/Anuak_language" title="Anuak language">Anuak</a>, 11.11% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a>, 4.85% <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language"><b>Afaan Oromoo</b></a>, 4.65% <a href="/wiki/Kafa_language" title="Kafa language">Kafa</a>, 2.48% <a href="/wiki/Shakacho_language" title="Shakacho language">Shakacho</a>, 1.47% speak <a href="/wiki/Kambaata_language" title="Kambaata language">Kambaata</a>, and 1.32% speak <a href="/wiki/Tigrinya_language" title="Tigrinya language">Tigrinya</a>; the remaining 3.75% spoke all other primary languages reported. 70.1% of the region's population are <a href="/wiki/P%27ent%27ay" title="P'ent'ay">Protestant</a>, 16.8% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahido_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahido Church">Orthodox Christian</a>, 4.9% <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Ethiopia" title="Islam in Ethiopia">Muslim</a>, 3.8% practice <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">traditional religions</a>, 3.4% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Ethiopia" title="Roman Catholicism in Ethiopia">Catholic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Gambela is the historic home of the indigenous Anuak. In recent years there has been significant violence between the Anuak and more recently arrived Ethiopians, who are generally referred to as "Highlanders".</p>
<p>The 1994 national census reported the regions population to be 181,862 in 35,940 households, of whom 92,902 were men and 88,960 women; 27,424 or 15.08% of the population were urban inhabitants. (This total also includes an estimate for all 19 <a href="/wiki/Kebele" title="Kebele">kebeles</a> of one woreda and 6 kebeles in two other woredas, which were not counted; these areas were estimated to have 19,465 inhabitants, of whom 9,203 were men and 10,262 women.) The six largest ethnic groups of the region were the Nuer (39.7%), the Anuak (27.45%), Amhara (7.74%), Oromo (6.49%), Mezhenger (5.76%), and Kafficho (4.18%); all other ethnic groups made up 8.68% of the population. <a href="/wiki/Nuer_language" title="Nuer language">Nuer</a> is spoken as a first language by 39.72%, 27.47% speak <a href="/wiki/Anuak_language" title="Anuak language">Anuak</a>, 8.44% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a>, 6.45% <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">'<i><b>Afaan Oromoo'</b></i></a> , and 5.75% speak <a href="/wiki/Majang_language" title="Majang language">Majang</a>; the remaining 12.17% spoke all other primary languages reported. A plurality of the inhabitants said they were Protestant, with 44.01% of the population reporting answers in that category, while 24.13% professed Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity, 10.28% practiced traditional religions, 5.15% were Muslim, and 3.21% were Catholic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Values for reported common indicators of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a> for Gambela as of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gambela_Region&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> include the following: 44% of the inhabitants fall into the lowest wealth quintile; adult literacy for men is 57.5% and for women 22.8%; and the Regional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infant_mortality_rate" title="Infant mortality rate">infant mortality rate</a> is 92 infant deaths per 1,000 live births, which is greater than the nationwide average of 77; at least half of these deaths occurred in the infants’ first month of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Refugee_camps">Refugee camps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gambela_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Refugee camps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camp" title="Refugee camp">refugee camps</a> located in Gambela Region housing around 268,000 refugees from <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Pugnido camp: ~62,800</li>
<li>Tierkidi camp: ~54,750</li>
<li>Kule camp: ~49,410</li>
<li>Jewi camp: ~42,570</li>
<li>Leitchuor camp: ~4,480</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gambela_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The CSA reported that for 2004-2005 3,734 tons of coffee were produced in Gambela, based on inspection records from the Ethiopian Coffee and Tea authority. This represents 1.64% of the total production in Ethiopia. The CSA could not provide livestock estimates for Gambela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In a 26 May 2000 report, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> observed that at the time <a href="/wiki/Trypanosomiasis" title="Trypanosomiasis">Trypanosomiasis</a> was a major problem in cattle for this Region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There was an epidemic of this disease in the area during 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gambela is believed to have major oil resources. In June 2003, the Ethiopian government signed an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">Petronas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> for the joint exploration and development of oil resources in Gambella region. Petronas then awarded a contract for <a href="/wiki/Exploration_geophysics" title="Exploration geophysics">seismic data acquisition</a> to China’s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhongyuan_Petroleum_Exploration_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhongyuan Petroleum Exploration Bureau (page does not exist)">Zhongyuan Petroleum Exploration Bureau</a> (ZPEB) October of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Water and Mines Resources Development Bureau of Gambela announced January 2007 that it was initiating a program that would drill 13 new manually operated wells, 54 new deep water wells, and develop four springs. This would provide access to <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_and_sanitation_in_Ethiopia" title="Water supply and sanitation in Ethiopia">drinking water</a> for 26,000 inhabitants, increasing coverage for the state to 42 percent from the existing 27 percent, at a cost of 6 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Birr" title="Ethiopian Birr">Birr</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Construction of an asphalt road 102 kilometers in length and connecting Gambela City with <a href="/wiki/Jikawo" title="Jikawo">Jikawo</a> by way of <a href="/wiki/Itang" title="Itang">Itang</a> was begun in 2008 with a budget over 446 million Birr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_subdivisions">Administrative subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gambela_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Administrative subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While Gambela is subdivided into administrative zones and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woreda" title="Woreda">woredas</a> as other Regions in Ethiopia are, this region has seen the most changes in these subdivisions of any region, to the point they can confuse anyone tracing their development.</p>
<p>Originally, Gambela was subdivided into four administrative zones and one special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woreda" title="Woreda">woreda</a> (an administrative subdivision which is similar to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_area" title="Autonomous area">autonomous area</a> and is not part of a zone) as follows, although these zones were not been given proper names.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Administrative_Zone_1_(Gambela)" title="Administrative Zone 1 (Gambela)">Administrative Zone 1</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Administrative_Zone_2_(Gambela)" title="Administrative Zone 2 (Gambela)">Administrative Zone 2</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Administrative_Zone_3_(Gambela)" title="Administrative Zone 3 (Gambela)">Administrative Zone 3</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_Zone_4_(Gambela)" title="Administrative Zone 4 (Gambela)">Administrative Zone 4</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godere_special_woreda" title="Godere special woreda">Godere special woreda</a></li>
</ul>
<p>By 2001, when the CSA released its <i>Sample Agricultural Enumeration</i>, these four zones had been combined into two, and Godere had been merged into the second administrative zone. By the 2007 census, Gambela had been redivided into three zones (named for the three largest ethnic groups), and <a href="/wiki/Itang" title="Itang">Itang</a> had been made a special woreda; borders of the existing woredas were moved around to create several new ones. These zones are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anuak_Zone" title="Anuak Zone">Anuak Zone</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mezhenger_Zone" title="Mezhenger Zone">Mezhenger Zone</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuer_Zone" title="Nuer Zone">Nuer Zone</a></li>
</ul>
