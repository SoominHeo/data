<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sentimental novel</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>sentimental novel</b> or the <b>novel of sensibility</b> is an <a href="/wiki/18th_century_in_literature" title="18th century in literature">18th-century literary genre</a> which celebrates the emotional and intellectual concepts of <a href="/wiki/Sentimentality" title="Sentimentality">sentiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sentimentalism_(literature)" title="Sentimentalism (literature)">sentimentalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sensibility" title="Sensibility">sensibility</a>. Sentimentalism, which is to be distinguished from sensibility, was a fashion in both <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> and prose fiction beginning in the eighteenth century in reaction to the rationalism of the <a href="/wiki/Augustan_literature" title="Augustan literature">Augustan Age</a>.</p>
<p>Sentimental novels relied on <b>emotional response</b>, both from their readers and characters. They feature scenes of distress and tenderness, and the plot is arranged to advance both emotions and actions. The result is a valorization of "fine feeling," displaying the characters as a model for refined, sensitive emotional effect. The ability to display feelings was thought to show character and experience, and to shape social life and relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentimental_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the most famous sentimental novels in English are <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Richardson" title="Samuel Richardson">Samuel Richardson</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamela,_or_Virtue_Rewarded" title="Pamela, or Virtue Rewarded">Pamela, or Virtue Rewarded</a></i> (1740), <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Goldsmith" title="Oliver Goldsmith">Oliver Goldsmith</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicar_of_Wakefield" title="Vicar of Wakefield">Vicar of Wakefield</a></i> (1766), <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Sterne" title="Laurence Sterne">Laurence Sterne</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tristram_Shandy" title="Tristram Shandy">Tristram Shandy</a></i> (1759–67), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Sentimental_Journey_Through_France_and_Italy" title="A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy">Sentimental Journey</a></i> (1768), <a href="/wiki/Henry_Brooke_(writer)" title="Henry Brooke (writer)">Henry Brooke</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fool_of_Quality" title="The Fool of Quality">The Fool of Quality</a></i> (1765–70), <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mackenzie" title="Henry Mackenzie">Henry Mackenzie</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_of_Feeling" title="The Man of Feeling">The Man of Feeling</a></i> (1771) and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Edgeworth" title="Maria Edgeworth">Maria Edgeworth</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Castle_Rackrent" title="Castle Rackrent">Castle Rackrent</a></i> (1800). Continental examples are <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Julie,_or_the_New_Heloise" title="Julie, or the New Heloise">Julie, or the New Heloise</a></i>, his autobiography <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confessions_(Jean-Jacques_Rousseau)" title="Confessions (Jean-Jacques Rousseau)">The Confessions</a></i> (1764–70) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sorrows_of_Young_Werther" title="The Sorrows of Young Werther">The Sorrows of Young Werther</a></i> (1774).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> American examples are <a href="/wiki/Susan_Warner" title="Susan Warner">Susan Warner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wide,_Wide_World" title="The Wide, Wide World">The Wide, Wide World</a></i> (1850), while the "death of Little Eva in <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">Harriet Beecher Stowe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">Uncle Tom's Cabin</a></i> (1852)" is also an example of this sensibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tobias_Smollett" title="Tobias Smollett">Tobias Smollett</a> tried to imply a darker underside to the "cult of sensibility" in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Expedition_of_Humphry_Clinker" title="The Expedition of Humphry Clinker">The Expedition of Humphry Clinker</a></i> (1771). Another example of this type of <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> is Frances Burney's <i><a href="/wiki/Evelina" title="Evelina">Evelina</a></i> (1778), wherein the heroine, while naturally good, in part for being country-raised, hones her politeness when, while visiting London, she is educated into propriety. This novel also is the beginning of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a>", though it is most appropriately labeled a conduct novel and a forerunner of the female <a href="/wiki/Bildungsroman" title="Bildungsroman">Bildungsroman</a> in the English tradition exemplified by later writers such as <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte Brontë</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While this genre is particularly associated with the second half of the 18th century, it continued in a modified form into the 19th century, especially in the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mrs_Henry_Wood" title="Mrs Henry Wood">Mrs Henry Wood</a>, who is remembered especially for <i><a href="/wiki/East_Lynne" title="East Lynne">East Lynne</a></i> (1861).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, the question as to whether <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> is a sentimental novelist is more debatable. Valerie Purton in her recent <i>Dickens and the Sentimental Tradition</i>, sees him continuing aspects of this tradition, and argues that his "sentimental scenes and characters [are] as crucial to the overall power of the novels as his darker or comic figures and scenes", and that "<i><a href="/wiki/Dombey_and_Son" title="Dombey and Son">Dombey and Son</a></i> is [ ... ] Dickens's greatest triumph in the sentimentalist tradition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> on the other hand, the Encyclopædia Britannica online comments, that despite "patches of emotional excess", such as the reported death of Tiny Tim in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Christmas_Carol" title="A Christmas Carol">A Christmas Carol</a></i> (1843), "Dickens cannot really be termed a sentimental novelist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sentimental novels also gave rise to the subgenre of <b>domestic fiction</b> in the early nineteenth century, commonly called <b>conduct novels</b>. The story's hero in domestic fiction is generally set in a domestic world and centers on a woman going through various types of hardship, and who is juxtaposed with either a foolish and passive or a woefully undereducated woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The contrast between the heroic woman's actions and her foils is meant to draw sympathy to the character's plight and to instruct them about expected conduct of women. The domestic novel uses sentimentalism as a tool to convince readers of the importance of its message.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of the 19th century, sentimental literature faced complaints about the abundance of "cheap sentiment" and its excessive bodily display. Critics, and eventually the public, began to see sentimentalism manifested in society as unhealthy physical symptoms such as nervousness and being overly sensitive, and the genre began declining sharply in popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_satirizing_of_sentimentalism">The satirizing of sentimentalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentimental_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The satirizing of sentimentalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novelist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fielding" title="Henry Fielding">Henry Fielding</a>, known later for his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Tom_Jones,_a_Foundling" title="The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling">The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirized</a> the sentimental style in his early novels <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shamela" title="Shamela">Shamela</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Andrews" title="Joseph Andrews">Joseph Andrews</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sense_and_Sensibility" title="Sense and Sensibility">Sense and Sensibility</a></i> is most often seen as a "witty <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a> of the sentimental novel",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> by juxtaposing values of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> (sense, reason) with those of the later eighteenth century (sensibility, feeling) while exploring the larger realities of women's lives, especially through concerns with <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a>. This reading of <i>Sense and Sensibility</i> specifically and Austen's fiction in general has been complicated and revised by recent critics such as Claudia L. Johnson (<i>Jane Austen: Women, Politics and the Novel</i> [1988] and <i>Equivocal Beings: Politics, Gender, and Sentimentality in the 1790s</i> [1995]), Jillian Heydt-Stevenson (<i>Austen's Unbecoming Conjunctions</i> [2005], and Christopher C. Nagle (<i>Sexuality and the Culture of Sensibility in the British Romantic Era</i> [2007]), all of whom see unruly and even subversive energies at play in her work, inspired by the sentimental tradition.</p>
<p>James Joyce parodies the sentimental novel in the "Nausicaa" episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i>. The character of Gerty MacDowell was inspired by the protagonist of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lamplighter" title="The Lamplighter">The Lamplighter</a></i>, a nineteenth century best-seller.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_aspects">Cultural aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentimental_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sentimental novel complemented social trends of the time toward <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a> and the heightened value of human life. The literature focused on weaker members of society, such as orphans and condemned criminals, and allowed readers to identify and sympathize with them. This translated to growing sentimentalism within society, and led to social movements calling for change, such as the abolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_penalty" title="Death penalty">death penalty</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>. Instead of the death penalty, popular sentiment called for the rehabilitation of criminals, rather than harsh punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Frederick Douglass himself was inspired to stand against his own bondage and slavery in general in his famous <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrative_of_the_Life_of_Frederick_Douglass" title="Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass">Narrative</a></i> by the speech by the sentimentalist playwright <a href="/wiki/Richard_Brinsley_Sheridan" title="Richard Brinsley Sheridan">Sheridan</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Columbian_Orator" title="The Columbian Orator">The Columbian Orator</a> detailing a fictional dialogue between a master and slave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a>'s 1774 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sorrows_of_Young_Werther" title="The Sorrows of Young Werther">The Sorrows of Young Werther</a></i> was highly sentimental and immediately extremely popular throughout Europe, and even inspired young people who could relate to Werther's sorrows to commit suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is also an excellent example of an <a href="/wiki/Epistolary_novel" title="Epistolary novel">epistolary novel</a>, an especially typical form for eighteenth-century novels of sensibility, beginning with the influential novels of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Richardson" title="Samuel Richardson">Samuel Richardson</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamela,_or_Virtue_Rewarded" title="Pamela, or Virtue Rewarded">Pamela</a></i> (1740), <i><a href="/wiki/Clarissa" title="Clarissa">Clarissa</a></i> (1748), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Sir_Charles_Grandison" title="The History of Sir Charles Grandison">The History of Sir Charles Grandison</a></i> (1753). The latter was an especially important influence on <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>, who references it repeatedly in her letters and began a dramatic adaptation of the work for the amusement of her family.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gothic_novel">Gothic novel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentimental_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gothic novel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">gothic fiction</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">Gothic novel</a>'s story occurs in a distant time and place, often Medieval or Renaissance Europe (especially Italy and Spain), and involved the fantastic exploits of a virtuous heroine imperiled by dark, tyrannical forces beyond her control. The first Gothic novel is Horace Walpole’s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Castle_of_Otranto" title="The Castle of Otranto">The Castle of Otranto</a></i> (1764), but its most famous and popular practitioner was <a href="/wiki/Ann_Radcliffe" title="Ann Radcliffe">Ann Radcliffe</a>. As in other Gothic novels, the notion of the <a href="/wiki/Sublime_(philosophy)" title="Sublime (philosophy)">sublime</a> is central. Eighteenth-century aesthetic theory, following <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, held that the sublime and the beautiful were juxtaposed. The sublime was awful (awe-inspiring) and terrifying while the beautiful was calm and reassuring. The characters and landscapes of the Gothic rest almost entirely within the sublime, with the heroine serving as the great exception. The “beautiful” heroine’s susceptibility to supernatural elements, integral to these novels, both celebrates and problematizes what came to be seen as hyper-sensibility.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_the_Gothic_novel">Relation to the Gothic novel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentimental_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation to the Gothic novel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gothic and sentimental novels are considered a form of popular fiction, reaching their height of popularity in the late 18th Century. They reflected a popular shift from <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassical</a> ideas of order and reason to emotion and imagination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Popular stylistic elements, such as the "discovery" of the original manuscript by the author (as in Walpole's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Castle_of_Otranto" title="The Castle of Otranto">The Castle of Otranto</a></i>) or creating fragmented works by combining disjointed tales (seen in Sterne's <i><a href="/wiki/A_Sentimental_Journey_Through_France_and_Italy" title="A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy">A Sentimental Journey</a></i>) were meant to suggest to the reader that there was no act of artistic creation to distort reality between the reader and the work, or that the emotional intensity and sincerity remained intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
