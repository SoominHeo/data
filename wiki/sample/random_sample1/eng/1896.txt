<p><b>Blackacre</b>, <b>Whiteacre</b>, <b>Greenacre</b>, <b>Brownacre</b>, and variations are the <a href="/wiki/Placeholder_name" title="Placeholder name">placeholder names</a> used for fictitious <a href="/wiki/Estate_(law)" title="Estate (law)">estates in land</a>.</p>
<p>The names are used by professors of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> in <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions, particularly in the area of <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> and occasionally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contracts" title="Contracts">contracts</a>, to discuss the rights of various parties to a piece of land. A typical <a href="/wiki/Law_school" title="Law school">law school</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_exam" title="Bar exam">bar exam</a> question on real property might say:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Adam, owner of a <a href="/wiki/Fee_simple" title="Fee simple">fee simple</a> in Blackacre, conveyed the property "to Bill for life, <a href="/wiki/Future_interest" title="Future interest">remainder</a> to Charles, provided that if any person should consume alcohol on the property before the first born son of Charles turns twenty-one, then the property shall go to Dwight in fee simple." Assume that neither Bill, Charles, or Dwight is an heir of Adam, and that Adam's only heir is his son, Edward. Discuss the ownership interests in Blackacre of Adam, Bill, Charles, Dwight and Edward.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Where more than one estate is needed to demonstrate a point – perhaps relating to a dispute over boundaries, <a href="/wiki/Easement" title="Easement">easements</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian_rights" title="Riparian rights">riparian rights</a> – a second estate will usually be called Whiteacre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a third, Greenacre, and a fourth, Brownacre.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jesse_Dukeminier" title="Jesse Dukeminier">Jesse Dukeminier</a>, author of one of the leading series of <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbooks</a> on property, traces the use of Blackacre and Whiteacre for this purpose to a 1628 treatise by Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Coke" title="Edward Coke">Edward Coke</a>. Dukeminier suggests that the term might originate with references to colors associated with certain <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peas" title="Peas">peas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beans" title="Beans">beans</a> are black, <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">corn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potatoes" title="Potatoes">potatoes</a> are white, <a href="/wiki/Hay" title="Hay">hay</a> is green"), or with the means by which rents were to be paid, with black rents payable in <a href="/wiki/Produce" title="Produce">produce</a> and white rents in <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A 1790 treatise by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Buller,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Francis Buller, 1st Baronet">Francis Buller</a> similarly uses these placeholder names, stating: "If A. have Black Acre and C. have White Acre, and A. has a way over White Acre to Black Acre, and then purchases White Acre, the way will be extinct; and if A. afterwards enfeoff C. of White Acre without excepting the road, it is gone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternate theory is that the term arises from <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> policy in which plots of <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">plantation</a> land given to recently <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">emancipated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedmen" title="Freedmen">freedmen</a> (freed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaves" title="Slaves">slaves</a>) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction Era of the United States">Reconstruction</a> period under <a href="/wiki/William_Tecumseh_Sherman" title="William Tecumseh Sherman">Sherman</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Forty_acres_and_a_mule" title="Forty acres and a mule">forty acres and a mule</a>" policy. Such plots were colloquially called "Blackacres". Unfortunately, since most of the freed slaves were illiterate, many were cheated out of their land through shady loan contracts with former plantation owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These disputes, along with questions of the proper contractual entitlement of the slaves to the land in the first place, provided the basis for the development of modern American <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Property_law" title="Property law">property law</a> and has come to refer to situations where there exists some contention or ambiguity surrounding the rightful owner or respective rights of the parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In various law journals and treatises in Louisiana, which uses a <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Louisiana" title="Law of Louisiana">unique form</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> influenced by but not identical to the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Code" title="Napoleonic Code">Napoleonic Code</a>, authors have used the term "<a href="/wiki/Arpent" title="Arpent">arpent</a> noir" as a placeholder name for the purpose of discussing rights concerning immovables.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackacre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of its association with legal education, a number of legal publications and events utilize the name. For example, <i>Blackacre</i> was adopted as the name of the literary journal at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_School_of_Law" title="University of Texas School of Law">University of Texas School of Law</a>, founded by Matt Matthews, Ben Love, and Meera George. <i>Blackacre</i> is also the name of a journal at the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Law_School" title="Sydney Law School">University of Sydney Law School</a>, published annually by the Sydney University Law Society, the name of the open-air courtyard and weekly student social at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_Law_School" title="Vanderbilt Law School">Vanderbilt Law School</a>, and the name of a <a href="/wiki/William_Mitchell_College_of_Law" title="William Mitchell College of Law">William Mitchell College of Law</a> formal.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Blackacre_Nature_Preserve_and_Historic_Homestead" title="Blackacre Nature Preserve and Historic Homestead">Blackacre Nature Preserve and Historic Homestead</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> was so named by the donor of the land, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Macauley_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Macauley Smith (page does not exist)">Macauley Smith</a>, who had been a judge on the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Court_of_Appeals" title="Kentucky Court of Appeals">Kentucky Court of Appeals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In July 2010, a legal humor website wrote an article chronicling the foreclosure sale of Blackacre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A group of law students in Indianapolis founded a brewery named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Acre_Brewing_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black Acre Brewing Co. (page does not exist)">Black Acre Brewing Co.</a> in late 2010 as a homage to their legal schooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monica_Youn" title="Monica Youn">Monica Youn</a>'s 2016 book of poetry from <a href="/wiki/Graywolf_Press" title="Graywolf Press">Graywolf Press</a> is titled <i>Blackacre</i>, in reference to the legal concept (Youn has a law degree from Yale).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
