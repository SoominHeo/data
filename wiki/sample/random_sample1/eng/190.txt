<p>The domain name <b>org</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Generic_top-level_domain" title="Generic top-level domain">generic top-level domain</a> (gTLD) of the <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> (DNS) used in the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. The name is truncated from <i>organization</i>. It was one of the original domains established in 1985, and has been operated by the <a href="/wiki/Public_Interest_Registry" title="Public Interest Registry">Public Interest Registry</a> since 2003. The domain was originally intended for non-profit entities, but this restriction was not enforced and has been removed. The domain is commonly used by schools, open-source projects, and communities, as well as by for-profit entities. The number of registered domains in org has increased from fewer than one million in the 1990s, to ten million as of June, 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.org&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The domain ".org" was one of the original top-level domains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">com</a>, <a href="/wiki/.us" title=".us">us</a>, <a href="/wiki/.edu" title=".edu">edu</a>, <a href="/wiki/.gov" title=".gov">gov</a>, <a href="/wiki/.mil" title=".mil">mil</a> and <a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">net</a>, established in January 1985. It was originally intended for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organizations" title="Non-profit organizations">non-profit organizations</a> or organizations of a non-commercial character that did not meet the requirements for other gTLDs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MITRE" title="MITRE">The MITRE Corporation</a> was the first group to register an org domain with <i>mitre.org</i> in July 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The TLD has been operated since January 1, 2003 by <a href="/wiki/Public_Interest_Registry" title="Public Interest Registry">Public Interest Registry</a>, who assumed the task from VeriSign Global Registry Services, a division of <a href="/wiki/Verisign" title="Verisign">Verisign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Registrations">Registrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.org&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Registrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Registrations of subdomains are processed via accredited registrars worldwide. Anyone can register a <a href="/wiki/Second-level_domain" title="Second-level domain">second-level domain</a> within org, without restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In some instances subdomains are being used also by commercial sites, such as craigslist.org. According to the <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> Dashboard (Domain Name) report, the composition of the TLD is diverse, including cultural institutions, associations, sports teams, religious, and civic organizations, <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a> projects, schools, environmental initiatives, social, and fraternal organizations, health organizations, legal services, as well as clubs, and community-volunteer groups. In some cases subdomains have been created for crisis management.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2011)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> Some cities, among them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rybnitsa" title="Rybnitsa">Rybnitsa</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://rybnitsa.org" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> in <a href="/wiki/Transnistria" title="Transnistria">Transnistria</a>) also have org domain names.</p>

<p>Although organizations anywhere in the world may register subdomains, many countries, such as Australia (<a href="/wiki/.au" title=".au">au</a>), Japan (<a href="/wiki/.jp" title=".jp">jp</a>), Argentina (<a href="/wiki/.ar" title=".ar">ar</a>), Bolivia (<a href="/wiki/.bo" title=".bo">bo</a>), Uruguay (<a href="/wiki/.uy" title=".uy">uy</a>), Turkey(<a href="/wiki/.tr" title=".tr">tr</a>), Somalia (<a href="/wiki/.so" title=".so">so</a>), Sierra Leone (<a href="/wiki/.sl" title=".sl">sl</a>), Russia (<a href="/wiki/.ru" title=".ru">ru</a>), Bangladesh (<a href="/wiki/.bd" title=".bd">bd</a>), and the United Kingdom (<a href="/wiki/.uk" title=".uk">uk</a>), have established a second-level domain with a similar purpose under their <a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">ccTLD</a>. Such second-level domains are usually named <i>org</i> or <i>or</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2009, the org domain consisted of more than 8 million registered domain names,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fiftythree_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fiftythree-6">[6]</a></sup> 8.8 million in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortyone_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fortyone-7">[7]</a></sup> and 9.6 million in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirty_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thirty-8">[8]</a></sup> The Public Interest Registry registered the ten millionth .ORG domain in June, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When the 9.5 millionth .org was registered in December 2011, .org, became the third largest gTLD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internationalized_domain_names">Internationalized domain names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.org&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internationalized domain names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The org domain registry allows the registration of selected <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">internationalized domain names</a> (IDNs) as second-level domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For German, Danish, Hungarian, Icelandic, Korean, Latvian, Lithuanian, Polish, and Swedish IDNs this has been possible since 2005. Spanish IDN registrations have been possible since 2007.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain_name_security">Domain name security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.org&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Domain name security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 2, 2009, The Public Interest Registry announced<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> that the <span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">org</span> domain is the first open generic top-level domain and the largest registry overall that has signed its <a href="/wiki/DNS_zone" title="DNS zone">DNS zone</a> with <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System_Security_Extensions" title="Domain Name System Security Extensions">Domain Name System Security Extensions</a> (DNSSEC). This allows the verification of the origin authenticity and integrity of DNS data by conforming DNS clients.</p>
<p>As of June 23, 2010, DNSSEC was enabled for individual second-level domains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> starting with 13 registrars.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_of_registration">Cost of registration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.org&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cost of registration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Public_Interest_Registry" title="Public Interest Registry">Public Interest Registry</a> (PIR) charges its accredited registrars US $7.70<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> for each domain name. The registrars may set their charges to end users without restrictions.</p>
