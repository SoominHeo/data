<p><a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">Rape</a> can be categorized in different ways: for example, by reference to the situation in which it occurs, by the identity or characteristics of the victim, and by the identity or characteristics of the perpetrator. These categories are referred to as <b>types of rape</b>. The types of rape described below are not mutually exclusive: a given rape can fit into multiple categories, by for example by being both a prison rape and a gang rape, or both a custodial rape and the rape of a child.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date_rape">Date rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Date rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Date_rape" title="Date rape">Date rape</a></div>
<p>The term "date rape" is used to refer to several types of rape, broadly <a href="/wiki/Acquaintance_rape" title="Acquaintance rape">acquaintance rape</a>, which is a non-domestic rape committed by someone who knows the victim,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphreys_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humphreys-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Drug_facilitated_sexual_assault" title="Drug facilitated sexual assault">drug facilitated sexual assault</a> (DFSA), where the rapist intentionally drugs the victim with a <a href="/wiki/Date_rape_drug" title="Date rape drug">date rape drug</a> so that they are incapacitated. Acquaintance rape constitutes the vast majority of reported rapes, while <a href="/wiki/Drug_facilitated_sexual_assault#Frequency_of_occurrence" title="Drug facilitated sexual assault">DFSA is infrequent</a>. A frequently overlapping category is <i>incapacitated rape,</i> where the victim is incapacitated and unable to give consent – this is often the result of intoxication, but can also simply be because the victim is asleep or has a medical condition. DFSA is when the rapist intentionally incapacitates the victim via drugs, while acquaintance rape can occur when the victim is not incapacitated.</p>
<p>Acquaintance rape can occur between two people who know one another usually in social situations, between people who are dating as a couple and have had consensual sex in the past, between two people who are starting to date, between people who are just friends, and between acquaintances. They include rapes of co-workers, schoolmates, family, friends, teachers and other acquaintances, providing they are dating;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annual_Crime_Report_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Annual_Crime_Report-2">[2]</a></sup> it is sometimes referred to as "hidden rape" and has been identified as a growing problem in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_society" title="Western society">western society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A college survey conducted by the United States' National Victim Center reported that one in four college women have been raped or experienced attempted rape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bureau_of_Justice_Statistics_1996_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bureau_of_Justice_Statistics_1996-4">[4]</a></sup> This report indicates that young women are at considerable risk of becoming a victim of date rape while in college. In addition, there have been reported incidents of colleges questioning accounts of alleged victims, further complicating documentation and policing of student assaults, despite such preventative legislation as the <a href="/wiki/Clery_Act" title="Clery Act">Clery Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gang_rape">Gang rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gang rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gang_rape" title="Gang rape">Gang rape</a></div>
<p>Gang rape occurs when a group of people participate in the rape of a single victim. Rape involving at least two or more violators (usually at least three<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-7">[7]</a></sup>) is widely reported to occur in many parts of the world. Systematic information on the extent of the problem, however, is scant.</p>
<p>One study showed that offenders and victims in gang rape incidents were younger with a higher possibility of being unemployed. Gang rapes involved more alcohol and other <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">drug use</a>, night attacks and severe sexual assault outcomes and less victim resistance and fewer weapons than individual rapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullman1999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman1999-8">[8]</a></sup> Another study found that group sexual assaults were more violent and had greater resistance from the victim than individual sexual assaults and that victims of group sexual assaults were more likely to seek crisis and police services, contemplate <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, and seek therapy than those involved in individual assaults. The two groups were about the same in the amount of drinking and other drug use during the assault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gidycz1990_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gidycz1990-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spousal_rape">Spousal rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spousal rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marital_rape" title="Marital rape">Marital rape</a></div>
<p>Also known as marital rape, wife rape, husband rape, partner rape or intimate partner sexual assault (IPSA), is rape between a married or <i>de facto</i> couple. Research reveals that victims of marital/partner rape suffer longer lasting trauma than victims of stranger rape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rape_of_children">Rape of children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rape of children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">Child sexual abuse</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">Incest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child-on-child_sexual_abuse" title="Child-on-child sexual abuse">Child-on-child sexual abuse</a></div>
<p>Rape of a child is a form of <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a>. When committed by another child (usually older or stronger) or adolescent, it is called <a href="/wiki/Child-on-child_sexual_abuse" title="Child-on-child sexual abuse">child-on-child sexual abuse</a>. When committed by a parent or other close relatives such as grandparents, aunts and uncles, it is also <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a> and can result in serious and long-term psychological <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">trauma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtois_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Courtois-11">[11]</a></sup> When a child is raped by an adult who is not a family member but is a caregiver or in a position of authority over the child, such as school teachers, religious authorities, or therapists, to name a few, on whom the child is dependent, the effects can be similar to incestual rape.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_rape">Statutory rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statutory rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Statutory_rape" title="Statutory rape">Statutory rape</a></div>
<p>National and regional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governments" title="Governments">governments</a>, citing an interest in protecting "young people" (variously defined but sometimes synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minors</a>) from sexual exploitation, treat any sexual contact with such a person as an offense (not always categorized as "rape"), even if he or she agrees to or initiates the sexual activity.</p>
<p>The offense is often based on a presumption that people under a certain age do not have the capacity to give consent. The age at which individuals are considered competent to give consent, called the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_consent" title="Age of consent">age of consent</a>, varies in different countries and regions; in the US, the age ranges from 16 to 18. Sexual activity that violates age-of-consent law, but is neither violent nor physically coerced, is sometimes described as "<a href="/wiki/Statutory_rape" title="Statutory rape">statutory rape</a>," a legally-recognized category in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Most states, however, allow persons younger than the age of consent to engage in sexual activity if the age difference between the partners is small; these are called close in age exemptions or a <a href="/wiki/Statutory_rape#Romeo_and_Juliet_laws" title="Statutory rape">Romeo and Juliet exemption</a> and even in countries where there is no official legal exemption prosecutions are infrequent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prison_rape">Prison rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prison rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prison_rape" title="Prison rape">Prison rape</a></div>
<p>Rates of <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a> rape have been reported as affecting between 3% and 12% of prison inmates in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Although prison rapes are more commonly same-sex crimes (since prisons are usually separated by sex), the attacker usually does not identify as homosexual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This phenomenon is much less common elsewhere in the western world. This is partly because of the differences in the structure of the prison system in the US as compared to the prison systems in Canada, Australia and Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The attacker is most commonly another inmate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serial_rape">Serial rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Serial rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Serial_rapist" title="Serial rapist">Serial rapist</a></div>
<p>Serial rape is rape committed by a person over a relatively long period of time and committed on a number of victims. Most times this type of rapist is unknown to the victim and follows a specific and predictable pattern of targeting and assaulting victims.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Payback_rape">Payback rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Payback rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Payback rape", also called "punishment rape" or "revenge rape", is a form of rape specific to certain cultures, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a>. It consists of the rape of a female, usually by a group of several males, as revenge for acts committed by members of her family, such as her father or brothers. The rape is meant to humiliate the father or brothers, as punishment for their prior behavior towards the perpetrators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Payback rape is sometimes connected to tribal fighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_rape">War rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: War rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_rape" title="War rape">War rape</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="843" data-file-width="709" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg/220px-Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg/330px-Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg/440px-Paul_Jamin_-_Le_Brenn_et_sa_part_de_butin_1893.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><b>War rapes</b> are rapes committed by soldiers, other combatants or civilians during armed conflict or war, or during military occupation. It also covers the situation where girls and women are forced into prostitution or <a href="/wiki/Sexual_slavery" title="Sexual slavery">sexual slavery</a> by an occupying power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During war, rape is often used as a means of <a href="/wiki/Psychological_warfare" title="Psychological warfare">psychological warfare</a> in order to humiliate the enemy and undermine their morale. Rapes in war are often systematic and thorough, and military leaders may actually encourage their soldiers to rape civilians. Likewise, systematic rapes are often employed as a form of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">ethnic cleansing</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>War rape has been considered a <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crime</a> only since 1949. Article 27 of the Fourth Geneva Convention explicitly prohibits wartime rape and enforced prostitution. These prohibitions were reinforced by the 1977 Additional Protocols to the 1949 Geneva Conventions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Therefore, during the post-war <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" title="Nuremberg Trials">Nuremberg Trials</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Military_Tribunal_for_the_Far_East" title="International Military Tribunal for the Far East">Tokyo Trials</a>, mass war rape was not prosecuted as a war crime.</p>
<p>In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Tribunal_for_Rwanda" title="International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda">International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda</a> established by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> made landmark decisions that rape is a crime of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> under <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. In one judgement, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navanethem_Pillay" title="Navanethem Pillay">Navanethem Pillay</a> said: "From time immemorial, rape has been regarded as spoils of war. Now it will be considered a war crime. We want to send out a strong message that rape is no longer a trophy of war."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word <i>rape</i> only began to be used to refer to sexual assault in the early 15th century, and its dominant usage remained to refer to abduction and robbery without any connotation of sexual assault until the modern period. Many classical references to rape during war do not refer explicitly to instances of sexual assault, but rather to the practice of abducting the women or property of the enemy during warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rape_by_deception">Rape by deception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rape by deception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rape_by_deception" title="Rape by deception">Rape by deception</a></div>
<p>Rape by deception occurs when the perpetrator gains the victim's consent through <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrective_rape">Corrective rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Corrective rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Corrective_rape" title="Corrective rape">Corrective rape</a></div>
<p>Corrective rape is targeted rape against <a href="/wiki/Non-heterosexual" title="Non-heterosexual">non-heterosexuals</a> as a punishment for violating <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartle_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bartle-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Di_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Di-21">[21]</a></sup> It is a form of <a href="/wiki/Hate_crime" title="Hate crime">hate crime</a> against <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> individuals, mainly lesbians, in which the rapist justifies the act as an acceptable response to the victim's perceived sexual or gender orientation and a form of punishment for being gay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartle_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bartle-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Di_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Di-21">[21]</a></sup> Often, the stated argument of the corrective rapist is that the rape will turn the person straight, "correcting" their sex or gender, i.e. make them conform to societal norms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartle_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bartle-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Di_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Di-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The term was first coined in South Africa after well-known cases of corrective rape, such as that of sports star <a href="/wiki/Eudy_Simelane" title="Eudy Simelane">Eudy Simelane</a>, became public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Custodial_rape">Custodial rape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Custodial rape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Custodial rape is rape perpetrated by a person employed by the state in a supervisory or custodial position, such as a police officer, public servant or jail or hospital employee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It includes the rape of children in institutional care such as orphanages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Custodial rape has been reported in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In India custodial rape has been a major focus of women's rights organizations, and has been an official category of rape defined under law since 1983. Indian law says this type of rape takes advantage of the rapist's position of authority and is therefore subject to extra penalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term custodial rape is sometimes used broadly to include rape by anyone in a position of authority such as an employer, money-lender, contractor or landlord, but under Indian law it refers only to government employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Victims of custodial rape are frequently minorities, people who are poor, or low-status for example because of their caste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> Researchers say custodial rape is part of a broader pattern of custodial abuse, which can also include <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perpetrator_types">Perpetrator types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Perpetrator types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Types_of_rapist" title="Types of rapist">Types of rapist</a></div>
<p>Nicholas Groth has described three types of rape, based on the goal of the rapist. This includes the anger rapist, power rapist and sadistic rapist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSOM_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSOM-38">[38]</a></sup> According to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_Barbaree&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Howard Barbaree (page does not exist)">Howard Barbaree</a>, a psychologist at <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University" title="Queen's University">Queen's University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">Kingston, Ontario</a>, most rapes are impulsive and opportunistic, and committed by people who may commit other impulsive acts, including impulsive crimes. These rapists tend to show no anger except in response to their victim's resistance, and use little unnecessary force.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_classification">Further classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Types_of_rape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Further classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As mentioned above, when differing acts of rape are defined and typified, whether by popular convention, research of the subject, or otherwise, the results are often neither exclusive nor exhaustive in scope. Some instances may fit two or more definitions, while others may remain as yet undefined. One example of this are the following sub-classifications created by American researcher Patricia Rozee:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>exchange rape</b> - rape occurring as the result of bargaining or solidarity-displaying among men</li>
<li><b>punitive rape</b> - rape used to punish or discipline</li>
<li><b>theft rape</b> - rape that happens when a woman is abducted, in most cases to be used as a slave or a prostitute</li>
<li><b>ceremonial rape</b> - rape involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deflower" title="Deflower">defloration</a> rituals</li>
<li><b>status rape</b> - rape resulting from differences in hierarchy or social class</li>
</ul>
<p>Where they are not already typed above, many of these otherwise distinct forms could equally apply to other classifications (e.g. prison rape that is also punitive rape, war rape that is also theft rape, etc.)</p>
