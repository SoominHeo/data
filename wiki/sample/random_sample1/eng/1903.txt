<p><b>River dolphins</b> are a widely distributed group of fully <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_mammal" title="Aquatic mammal">aquatic mammals</a> that reside exclusively in freshwater or <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a>. They are an informal grouping of <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly">paraphyletic</a> group within the infraorder <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">Cetacea</a>. The river dolphins comprise the extant families <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platanistidae" title="Platanistidae">Platanistidae</a> (the Indian dolphins), <a href="/wiki/Iniidae" title="Iniidae">Iniidae</a> (the Amazonian dolphins), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontoporiidae" title="Pontoporiidae">Pontoporiidae</a> (the brackish dolphins). There are five <a href="/wiki/Neontology" title="Neontology">extant</a> species of river dolphins, and two subspecies. River dolphins, alongside other cetaceans, belong to the clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetartiodactyla" title="Cetartiodactyla">Cetartiodactyla</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Even-toed_ungulate" title="Even-toed ungulate">even-toed ungulates</a>, and their closest living relatives the <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamidae" title="Hippopotamidae">hippopotamuses</a>, having diverged about 40 million years ago.</p>
<p>River dolphins are relatively small compared to other dolphins, having evolved to survive in warm, shallow water and strong river currents. They range in size from the 5-foot (1.5 m) long <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_river_dolphin" title="South Asian river dolphin">South Asian river dolphin</a> to the 8-foot (2.4 m) and 220-pound (100 kg) <a href="/wiki/Amazon_river_dolphin" title="Amazon river dolphin">Amazon river dolphin</a>. Several species exhibit <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, in that the males are larger than the females. They have streamlined bodies and two limbs that are modified into flippers. River dolphins use their conical-shaped teeth and long beaks to capture fast-moving prey in murky water. They have well-developed hearing that is adapted for both air and water; they do not really rely on vision since the water they swim in is usually very muddy. These species are well-adapted to living in warm, shallow waters, and, unlike other cetaceans, have little to no <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a>.</p>
<p>River dolphins are not very widespread; they are all restricted to certain rivers or deltas. This makes them extremely vulnerable to habitat destruction. River dolphins feed primarily on fish. Male river dolphins typically mate with multiple females every year, but females only mate every two to three years. Calves are typically born in the spring and summer months and females bear all the responsibility for raising them. River dolphins produce a variety of vocalizations, usually in the form of clicks and whistles.</p>
<p>River dolphins are rarely kept in captivity; breeding success has been poor and the animals often die within a few months of capture. As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there are only three river dolphins in captivity.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_cetaceans" title="List of cetaceans">List of cetaceans</a></div>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a> of river dolphins (Iniidae, Pontoporiidae, Lipotidae and Platanistidae) are currently recognized, comprising three superfamilies (Inioidea, Lipotoidea and Platanistoidea). Platanistidae, containing the two subspecies of <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_river_dolphin" title="South Asian river dolphin">South Asian river dolphin</a>, is the only accepted family of Platanistoidea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice1998_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rice1998-1">[1]</a></sup> Previously, many taxonomists had assigned all river dolphins to a single family, Platanistidae, and treated the Ganges and Indus River dolphins as separate species. A December 2006 survey found no members of <i><a href="/wiki/Baiji" title="Baiji">Lipotes vexillifer</a></i> (commonly known as the baiji, or Chinese river dolphin) and declared the species <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functionally_extinct" title="Functionally extinct">functionally extinct</a>. With their disappearance, one of the recently accepted superfamilies, Lipotoidea, has become extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turvey2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turvey2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2012 the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Marine_Mammalogy" title="Society for Marine Mammalogy">Society for Marine Mammalogy</a> began considering the Bolivian (<i>Inia geoffrensis boliviensis</i>) and Amazonian (<i>Inia geoffrensis geoffrensis</i>) subspecies as full species <i>Inia boliviensis</i> and <i>Inia geoffrensis</i>, respectively; however, much of the scientific community, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>, continue to consider the Bolivian population to be a subspecies of <i>Inia geoffrensis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_cetaceans" title="Evolution of cetaceans">Evolution of cetaceans</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>River dolphins are members of the infraorder <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">Cetacea</a>, which are descendants of land-dwelling mammals of the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artiodactyla" title="Artiodactyla">Artiodactyla</a> (even-toed <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a>). They are related to the <i><a href="/wiki/Indohyus" title="Indohyus">Indohyus</a></i>, an extinct <a href="/wiki/Chevrotain" title="Chevrotain">chevrotain-like</a> ungulate, from which they split approximately 48 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancestors_Tale_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestors_Tale-7">[7]</a></sup> The primitive cetaceans, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeocetes" title="Archaeocetes">archaeocetes</a>, first took to the sea approximately 49 million years ago and became fully aquatic by 5–10 million years later. It is unknown when river dolphins first ventured back into fresh water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>River dolphins are thought to have relictual distributions, that is, their ancestors originally occupied marine habitats, but were then displaced from these habitats by modern dolphin lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassens2000_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cassens2000-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamilton2001_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton2001-10">[10]</a></sup> Many of the <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> similarities and adaptations to freshwater habitats arose due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>; thus, a grouping of all river dolphins is <a href="/wiki/Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly">paraphyletic</a>. Amazon river dolphins are actually more closely related to <a href="/wiki/Delphinoidea" title="Delphinoidea">oceanic dolphins</a> than to <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_river_dolphin" title="South Asian river dolphin">South Asian river dolphins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hrbek2014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hrbek2014-11">[11]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isthminia_panamensis" title="Isthminia panamensis">Isthminia panamensis</a></i> is an extinct genus and species of river dolphin, living 5.8 to 6.1 million years ago. Its fossils were discovered near Piña, Panama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>River dolphin has been considered a taxonomic description, suggesting an evolutionary relationship among the group, although it is now known that they form two distinct clades. 'True' river dolphins are descendants of ancient evolutionary lineages that evolved in freshwater environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassens2000_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cassens2000-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some species of cetacean live in rivers and lakes, but are more closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_dolphins" title="Oceanic dolphins">oceanic dolphins</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porpoises" title="Porpoises">porpoises</a> and entered fresh water more recently. Such species are considered facultative freshwater cetaceans as they can use both marine and freshwater environments. These include species such as the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_dolphin" title="Irrawaddy dolphin">Irrawaddy dolphin</a>, <i>Orcaella brevirostris</i>, found in the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahakam" title="Mahakam">Mahakam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_River" title="Irrawaddy River">Irrawaddy Rivers</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> <a href="/wiki/Finless_porpoise" title="Finless porpoise">finless porpoise</a> <i>Neophocaena phocaenoides asiaeorientalis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tucuxi" title="Tucuxi">tucuxi</a> (<i>Sotalia fluviatilis</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> is another species descended from oceanic dolphins; however, it does not perfectly fit the label of 'facultative' either, as it occurs only in fresh water. The tucuxi was until recently considered conspecific with the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_dolphin" title="Guiana dolphin">Guiana dolphin</a> (<i>Sotalia guianensis</i>), which inhabits marine waters. It may also be true for the Irrawaddy dolphin and the finless porpoise that, although the species may be found in both freshwater and marine environments, individual animals found in rivers may not be able to survive in the ocean, and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The tucuxi is currently classified as an oceanic dolphin (Delphinidae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscana" title="Franciscana">Franciscana</a> (<i>Pontoporia blainvillei</i>) has shown a converse evolutionary pattern, and has an ancient evolutionary lineage in freshwater, but inhabits estuarine and coastal waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="768" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg/220px-Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg/330px-Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg/440px-Indus_River_dolphin_skull_cast.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>River dolphins have a torpedo shaped body with a flexible neck, limbs modified into flippers, non-existent external ear flaps, a tail fin, and a small bulbous head. River dolphin skulls have small eye orbits, a long snout and eyes placed on the sides of the head. River dolphins are rather small, ranging in size from the 5-foot (1.5 m) long <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_river_dolphin" title="South Asian river dolphin">South Asian river dolphin</a> to the 8-foot (2.4 m) and 220-pound (100 kg) <a href="/wiki/Amazon_river_dolphin" title="Amazon river dolphin">Amazon river dolphin</a>. They all have female-biased <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, with the females being larger than the males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup> River dolphins are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polygynous" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a>, meaning male river dolphins typically mate with multiple females every year, but females only mate every two to three years. Calves are typically born in the spring and summer months and females bear all the responsibility for raising them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>River dolphins have conical teeth, used to catch swift prey such as small river fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup> They also have very long snouts, with some measuring 23 inches (58 cm), four times longer than most of their <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_dolphin" title="Oceanic dolphin">oceanic counterparts</a>. They have a two-chambered stomach that is similar in structure to that of terrestrial carnivores. They have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundic_stomach" title="Fundic stomach">fundic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyloric_stomach" title="Pyloric stomach">pyloric</a> chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Breathing involves expelling stale air from their <a href="/wiki/Blowhole_(anatomy)" title="Blowhole (anatomy)">blowhole</a>, followed by inhaling fresh air into their lungs. They do not have the iconic <a href="/wiki/Dolphin#Anatomy" title="Dolphin">spout</a>, as this only forms when the warm air exhaled from the lungs meets cold external air, which does not occur in their tropical habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>River dolphins have a relatively thin layer of <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a>. Blubber can help with buoyancy, protection from predators (they would have a hard time getting through a thick layer of fat), energy for leaner times, and insulation from harsh climates. The habitats of river dolphins lack these needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotion">Locomotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locomotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>River dolphins have two flippers and a tail fin. These flippers contain four digits. Although river dolphins do not possess fully developed hind limbs, some possess discrete rudimentary appendages, which may contain feet and digits. River dolphins are slow swimmers in comparison to oceanic dolphins, which can travel at speeds up to 35 miles per hour (56 km/h); the <a href="/wiki/Tucuxi" title="Tucuxi">tucuxi</a> can only travel at about 14 miles per hour (23 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Unlike other cetaceans, their neck vertebrae are not fused together, meaning they have greater flexibility than other non-terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_mammal" title="Aquatic mammal">aquatic mammals</a>, at the expense of speed. This means they can turn their head without actually moving their entire body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> When swimming, river dolphins rely on their tail fins to propel themselves through the water. Flipper movement is continuous. River dolphins swim by moving their tail fins and lower bodies up and down, propelling themselves through vertical movement, while their flippers are mainly used for steering. All species have a <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The ears of river dolphins have specific adaptations to their aquatic environment. In humans, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_ear" title="Middle ear">middle ear</a> works as an impedance equalizer between the outside air's low <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_impedance" title="Acoustic impedance">impedance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cochlea" title="Cochlea">cochlear</a> fluid's high impedance. In river dolphins, and other cetaceans, there is no great difference between the outer and inner environments. Instead of sound passing through the outer ear to the middle ear, river dolphins receive sound through the throat, from which it passes through a low-impedance fat-filled cavity to the inner ear. The ear is acoustically isolated from the skull by air-filled sinus pockets, which allows for greater directional hearing underwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Dolphins send out high frequency clicks from an organ known as a <a href="/wiki/Melon_(cetacean)" title="Melon (cetacean)">melon</a>. This melon consists of fat, and the skull of any such creature containing a melon will have a large depression. This allows river dolphins to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosonar" title="Biosonar">biosonar</a> for orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cetacean_anatomy_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-Cetacean_anatomy-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:203–427</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thewissen-Hearing_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thewissen-Hearing-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> They are so dependent on echolocation that they can survive even if they are blind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Beyond locating an object, echolocation also provides the animal with an idea on the object's shape and size, though how exactly this works is not yet understood. The small hairs on the rostrum of the Amazon river dolphin are believed to function as a <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">tactile sense</a>, possibly to compensate for their poor eyesight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>River dolphins have very small eyes for their size, and do not have a very good sense of sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice1998_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rice1998-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, the eyes are placed on the sides of the head, so the vision consists of two fields, rather than a <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular</a> view like humans have. When river dolphins surface, their lens and cornea correct the nearsightedness that results from the refraction of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass07_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mass07-31">[31]</a></sup> They have both <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone</a> cells, meaning they can see in both dim and bright light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass07_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mass07-31">[31]</a></sup> Most river dolphins have slightly flattened eyeballs, enlarged pupils (which shrink as they surface to prevent damage), slightly flattened corneas and a <a href="/wiki/Tapetum_lucidum" title="Tapetum lucidum">tapetum lucidum</a>; these adaptations allow for large amounts of light to pass through the eye and, therefore, a very clear image of the surrounding area. They also have glands on their eyelids and an <a href="/wiki/Corneal_epithelium" title="Corneal epithelium">outer corneal layer</a> that act as protection for the cornea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:505–519</sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olfactory_lobes" title="Olfactory lobes">Olfactory lobes</a> are absent in river dolphins, suggesting that they have no sense of smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:481–505</sup></p>
<p>River dolphins are not thought to have a sense of taste, as their taste buds are atrophied or missing altogether. However, some dolphins have preferences between different kinds of fish, indicating some sort of attachment to taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:447–454</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Development and agriculture have had devastating impacts on the habitats on river dolphins. The total population of <a href="/wiki/Araguaian_river_dolphin" title="Araguaian river dolphin">Araguaian river dolphins</a> is estimated to be between 600 and 1,500 individuals, and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> is limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hrbek2014_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hrbek2014-11">[11]</a></sup> The ecology of their habitat has been adversely affected by agricultural, ranching and industrial activities, as well as by the use of dams for hydroelectric power. The inhabited section of the Araguaia River probably extends over about 900 miles (1,400 km) out of a total length of 1,300 miles (2,100 km). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tocantins_river" title="Tocantins river">Tocantins river</a> habitat is <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">fragmented</a> by six hydroelectric dams, so the population there is at particular risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hrbek2014_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hrbek2014-11">[11]</a></sup> Its probable eventual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> status is <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> or worse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hrbek2014_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hrbek2014-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54–58</sup></p>
<p>Both subspecies of <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_river_dolphin" title="South Asian river dolphin">South Asian river dolphins</a> have been very adversely affected by human use of the river systems in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">subcontinent</a>. Irrigation has lowered water levels throughout both subspecies' ranges. Poisoning of the water supply from industrial and agricultural chemicals may have also contributed to population decline. Perhaps the most significant issue is the building of more than 50 dams along many rivers, causing the segregation of populations and a narrowed <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a> in which the dolphins can breed. Currently, three subpopulations of Indus river dolphins are considered capable of long-term survival if protected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31–32, 37–38</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braulik_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braulik-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>As China developed economically, pressure on the <a href="/wiki/Baiji" title="Baiji">baiji river dolphin</a> grew significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41–46</sup> <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">Industrial</a> and residential waste flowed into the Yangtze. The riverbed was dredged and reinforced with concrete in many locations. Ship traffic multiplied, boats grew in size, and fishermen employed wider and more lethal nets. Noise pollution caused the nearly blind animal to collide with propellers. Stocks of the dolphin's prey declined drastically in the late 20th century, with some fish populations declining to one thousandth of their pre-industrial levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-34">[34]</a></sup> In the 1950s, the population was estimated at 6,000 animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> but declined rapidly over the subsequent five decades. Only a few hundred were left by 1970. Then the number dropped down to 400 by the 1980s and then to 13 in 1997 when a full-fledged search was conducted. On December 13, 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Baiji" title="Baiji">baiji</a> (<i>Lipotes vexillifer</i>) was declared "functionally extinct", after a 45-day search by leading experts in the field failed to find a single specimen. The last verified sighting was in September 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turvey2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turvey2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amazon_river_dolphin#Human_interaction" title="Amazon river dolphin">Amazon river dolphin § Human interaction</a></div>
<p>The region of the Amazon in Brazil has an extension of 3,100,000 sq mi (8,000,000 km<sup>2</sup>) containing diverse fundamental ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> One of these ecosystems is a <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1rzea_forest" title="Várzea forest">várzea forest</a>, and is home to a large number of fish species which are an essential resource for human consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup> The várzea is also a major source of income through excessive local commercialized fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Várzea consist of muddy river waters containing a vast number and diversity of nutrient-rich species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171.66.127.192_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-171.66.127.192-41">[41]</a></sup> The abundance of distinct fish species lures the Amazon River dolphin into the várzea areas of high water occurrences during the seasonal flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to attracting predators such as the Amazon river dolphin, these high-water occurrences are an ideal location to draw in the local fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54–58</sup> Human fishing activities directly compete with the dolphins for the same fish species, the <a href="/wiki/Tambaqui" title="Tambaqui">tambaqui</a> (<i>Colossoma macropomum</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Piaractus_brachypomus" title="Piaractus brachypomus">pirapitinga</a> (<i>Piaractus brachypomus</i>), resulting in deliberate or unintentional catches of the Amazon river dolphin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_2004_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loch_2009_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loch_2009-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Silvano_et_al..2C_2009-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crespo_2010b_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crespo_2010b-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_.26_Marmontel_2011_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_.26_Marmontel_2011-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alves_2012_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alves_2012-49">[49]</a></sup> The local fishermen overfish, and when the Amazon River dolphins remove the commercialized fish from the nets and lines, it damages the equipment and the capture and causes a negative reaction from the local fishermen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loch_2009_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loch_2009-45">[45]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crespo_2010b_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crespo_2010b-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_.26_Marmontel_2011_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_.26_Marmontel_2011-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Institute_of_Environment_and_Renewable_Natural_Resources" title="Brazilian Institute of Environment and Renewable Natural Resources">Brazilian Institute of Environment and Renewable Natural Resources</a> prohibit fishermen from killing the Amazon river dolphin, yet they are not compensated for the damage to their equipment and the loss of their catch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alves_2012_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alves_2012-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bycatch">Bycatch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bycatch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the process of catching the commercialized fish, the Amazon river dolphins get caught in the nets and exhaust themselves until they die, or the local fishermen deliberately kill the dolphins that become entangled in their nets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup> The carcasses are discarded, consumed, or used as bait to attract a scavenger catfish, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piracatinga" title="Piracatinga">piracatinga</a> (<i>Calophysus macropterus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Estupi.C3.B1.C3.A1n_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Estupi.C3.B1.C3.A1n-50">[50]</a></sup> The use of the Amazon river dolphin carcass as bait for the piracatinga dates back from 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Estupi.C3.B1.C3.A1n_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Estupi.C3.B1.C3.A1n-50">[50]</a></sup> The increasing consumption demand by the local inhabitants and Colombia for the piracatinga has created a market for distribution of the Amazon river dolphin carcasses to be used as bait throughout these regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54–58</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alves_2012_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alves_2012-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, of the 15 dolphin carcasses found in the <a href="/wiki/Japur%C3%A1_River" title="Japurá River">Japurá River</a> in 2010–2011 surveys, 73% of the dolphins were killed for bait, disposed of, or abandoned in entangled gillnets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup> The data does not fully represent the actual overall number of deaths of the Amazon river dolphins, whether accidental or intentional, because a variety of factors make it extremely complicated to record and medically examine all the carcasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_2004_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crespo_2010b_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Crespo_2010b-47">[47]</a></sup> Scavenger species feed upon them and the complexity of the river currents makes it nearly impossible to locate all the carcasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-5"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup> More importantly, the local fishermen do not report these deaths out of fear that legal action will be taken against them,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iriarte_2013_38-6"><a href="#cite_note-Iriarte_2013-38">[38]</a></sup> as the Amazon river dolphin and other cetaceans are protected under the Brazilian federal law, prohibiting any takes, harassments, and kills of the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_captivity">In captivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In captivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A baiji conservation <a href="/wiki/Dolphinarium" title="Dolphinarium">dolphinarium</a> was established at the Institute of Hydrobiology (IHB) in Wuhan in 1992. This was planned as a backup to any other conservation efforts by producing an area completely protected from any threats, and where the baiji could be easily observed. The site includes an indoor and outdoor holding pool, a water filtration system, food storage and preparation facilities, research labs and a small museum. The aim is to also generate income from tourism which can be put towards the baiji plight. The pools are not very large, only kidney shaped tanks with dimensions of 82 feet (25 m) arc 23 feet (7.0 m) width and 11 feet (3.4 m) depth, 33 feet (10 m) diameter, 6.6 feet (2.0 m) deep and 39 feet (12 m) diameter, 11 feet (3.4 m) deep, and are not capable of holding many baijis at one time. <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Adams" title="Douglas Adams">Douglas Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Carwardine" title="Mark Carwardine">Mark Carwardine</a> documented their encounters with the endangered animals on their conservation travels for the BBC programme <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Chance_to_See" title="Last Chance to See">Last Chance to See</a></i>. The book by the same name, published in 1990, included pictures of a captive specimen, a male named Qi Qi (淇淇) that lived in the Wuhan Institute of Hydrobiology <a href="/wiki/Dolphinarium" title="Dolphinarium">dolphinarium</a> from 1980 to July 14, 2002. Discovered by a fisherman in <a href="/wiki/Dongting_Lake" title="Dongting Lake">Dongting Lake</a>, he became the sole resident of the Baiji Dolphinarium (白鱀豚水族馆) beside East Lake. A sexually mature female was captured in late 1995, but died after half a year in 1996 when the Shishou Tian-e-Zhou Baiji Semi-natural Reserve (石首半自然白鱀豚保护区), which had contained only <a href="/wiki/Finless_porpoise" title="Finless porpoise">finless porpoises</a> since 1990, was flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Amazon river dolphin has historically been kept in dolphinariums. Today, only three exist in captivity: one in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acuario_de_Valencia" title="Acuario de Valencia">Acuario de Valencia</a> in Venezuela, one in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zoologico_de_Guistochoca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zoologico de Guistochoca (page does not exist)">Zoologico de Guistochoca</a> in Peru, and one in <a href="/wiki/Duisburg_Zoo" title="Duisburg Zoo">Duisburg Zoo</a> in Germany. Several hundred were captured between the 1950s and 1970s, and were distributed in dolphinariums throughout the US, Europe, and Japan. Around 100 went to US dolphinariums, and of that, only 20 survived; the last died in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Zoo" title="Pittsburgh Zoo">Pittsburgh Zoo</a> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolphins_32-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dolphins-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:58–59</sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_World">Old World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Old World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">Hindu mythology</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganges_River_Dolphin" title="Ganges River Dolphin">Ganges River Dolphin</a> is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganga_in_Hinduism" title="Ganga in Hinduism">Ganga</a>, the deity of the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> river. The dolphin is said to be among the creatures which heralded the goddess' descent from the heavens and her mount, the <a href="/wiki/Makara_(Hindu_mythology)" title="Makara (Hindu mythology)">Makara</a>, is sometimes depicted as a dolphin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Chinese mythology, the baiji has many origin stories. For example, near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baiji" title="Baiji">baiji</a> was a princess that had lost her parents and had lived with her step-father, whom she had longed to get away from. The step-father wanted to trade her since she would have been sold for a great sum of money, but as they were crossing the river to get to the trader, a storm rolled in, and they were drenched. The step-father, enraged, tried to take her, but she plunged herself into the river. Before being drowned in the river, she was transformed into a dolphin, and swam away from her abusive step-father, who also fell in and was transformed into a <a href="/wiki/Finless_porpoise" title="Finless porpoise">porpoise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turvy_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-turvy-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In another story further upstream the Yangtze, the baiji was the daughter of a general who was deported from the city of <a href="/wiki/Wuhan" title="Wuhan">Wuhan</a> during a war. During his duty, the daughter ran away. Later, the general met a woman who told him how her father was a general, and when he realized that she was his daughter, he threw himself into the river out of shame. The daughter ran after him and also fell into the river. Before they were drowned, the daughter was transformed into a dolphin, and the general a porpoise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turvy_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-turvy-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_World">New World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: New World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amazon_river_dolphin" title="Amazon river dolphin">Amazon river dolphins</a>, known by the natives as the boto or encantados, are very prevalent in the mythology of the native South Americans. They are often characterized in their mythology as wielding superior musical ability, their seductiveness and love of sex, often resulting in illegitimate children, and their attraction to parties. Despite the fact that the Encante are said to come from a <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopia</a> full of wealth and without pain or death, the encantados crave the pleasures and hardships of human societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transformation into human form is said to be rare, and usually occurs at night. The encantado will often be seen running from a <a href="/wiki/Festival" title="Festival">festa</a>, despite protests from the others for it to stay, and can be seen by pursuers as it hurries to the river and reverts to dolphin form. When it is under human form, it wears a hat to hide its <a href="/wiki/Blowhole_(anatomy)" title="Blowhole (anatomy)">blowhole</a>, which does not disappear with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shapeshift" title="Shapeshift">shapeshift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides the ability to shapeshift into human form, encantados frequently wield other magical abilities, such as the power to control storms, enchant humans into doing their will, transform humans into encantados themselves, and inflict <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illness" title="Illness">illness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">insanity</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shamans" title="Shamans">Shamans</a> often intervene in these situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">Kidnapping</a> is also a common theme in such <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. Encantados are said to be fond of abducting humans with whom they fall in love, children born of their illicit love affairs, or just about anyone near the river who can keep them company, and taking them back to the Encante. The fear of this is so great among people who live in the Amazon river area that both children and adults are terrified of going near the water between dusk and dawn, or entering water-bodies alone. Some who supposedly have encountered encantados while out in their <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a> have been said to have gone insane, although in fact, the creatures seem to have done little more than follow their boats and nudge them from time to time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
