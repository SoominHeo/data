<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Accretion disk</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>accretion disk</b> is a structure (often a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumstellar_disk" title="Circumstellar disk">circumstellar disk</a>) formed by diffused material in <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital</a> motion around a massive central body. The central body is typically a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">Gravity</a> causes material in the disk to spiral inward towards the central body. Gravitational and frictional forces compress and raise the temperature of the material, causing the emission of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>. The frequency range of that radiation depends on the central object's mass. Accretion disks of young stars and <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a> radiate in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>; those around <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectrum</a>. The study of oscillation modes in accretion disks is referred to as <b>diskoseismology</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manifestations">Manifestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Manifestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<td><b>Unsolved problem in physics</b>:<br/>
<div style="padding:5px"><i>Accretion disk jets: Why do the disks surrounding certain objects, such as the nuclei of active <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>, emit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">jets</a> along their polar axes? These jets are invoked by astronomers to do everything from getting rid of angular momentum in a forming star to reionizing the universe (in <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nuclei</a>), but their origin is still not well understood.</i><br/>
<small><a href="/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="List of unsolved problems in physics">(more unsolved problems in physics)</a></small></div>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><br/>
Accretion disks are a ubiquitous phenomenon in astrophysics; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">active galactic nuclei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst" title="Gamma ray burst">gamma ray bursts</a> all involve accretion disks. These disks very often give rise to <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jet" title="Astrophysical jet">astrophysical jets</a> coming from the vicinity of the central object. Jets are an efficient way for the star-disk system to shed <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> without losing too much <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>.</p>
<p>The most spectacular accretion disks found in nature are those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">active galactic nuclei</a> and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasars" title="Quasars">quasars</a>, which are believed to be massive black holes at the center of galaxies. As matter enters the accretion disc, it follows a trajectory called a <a href="/wiki/Tendex_line" title="Tendex line">tendex line</a>, which describes an inward spiral. This is because particles rub and bounce against each other in a turbulent flow, causing frictional heating which radiates energy away, reducing the particles' angular momentum, allowing the particle to drift inwards, driving the inward spiral. The loss of angular momentum manifests as a reduction in velocity; at a slower velocity, the particle wants to adopt a lower orbit. As the particle falls to this lower orbit, a portion of its gravitational potential energy is converted to increased velocity and the particle gains speed. Thus, the particle has lost energy even though it is now travelling faster than before; however, it has lost angular momentum. As a particle orbits closer and closer, its velocity increases, as velocity increases frictional heating increases as more and more of the particle's potential energy (relative to the black hole) is radiated away; the accretion disk of a black hole is hot enough to emit <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> just outside the <a href="/wiki/Event_horizon" title="Event horizon">event horizon</a>. The large <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of quasars is believed to be a result of gas being accreted by supermassive black holes (Lynden-Bell, 1969).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Elliptical accretion disks formed at tidal disruption of stars can be typical in galactic nuclei and quasars (Gurzadyan and Ozernoy, 1979).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Accretion process can convert about 10 percent to over 40 percent of the mass of an object into energy as compared to around 0.7 percent for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In close <a href="/wiki/Binary_system" title="Binary system">binary systems</a> the more massive primary component evolves faster and has already become a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a>, a neutron star, or a black hole, when the less massive companion reaches the giant state and exceeds its <a href="/wiki/Roche_lobe" title="Roche lobe">Roche lobe</a>. A gas flow then develops from the companion star to the primary. Angular momentum conservation prevents a straight flow from one star to the other and an accretion disk forms instead.</p>
<p>Accretion disks surrounding <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri stars</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbig_Ae/Be_stars" title="Herbig Ae/Be stars">Herbig stars</a> are called <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disks</a> because they are thought to be the progenitors of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a>. The accreted gas in this case comes from the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular cloud</a> out of which the star has formed rather than a companion star.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accretion_disk_physics">Accretion disk physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Accretion disk physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Accretion_disk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Accretion_disk.jpg/250px-Accretion_disk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Accretion_disk.jpg/375px-Accretion_disk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Accretion_disk.jpg/500px-Accretion_disk.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>In the 1940s, models were first derived from basic physical principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W1948_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-W1948-6">[6]</a></sup> In order to agree with observations, those models had to invoke a yet unknown mechanism for angular momentum redistribution. If matter is to fall inwards it must lose not only gravitational energy but also lose <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. Since the total angular momentum of the disk is conserved, the angular momentum loss of the mass falling into the center has to be compensated by an angular momentum gain of the mass far from the center. In other words, angular momentum should be <i>transported</i> outwards for matter to accrete. <p>On one hand, it was clear that viscous stresses would eventually cause the matter towards the center to heat up and radiate away some of its gravitational energy. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> itself was not enough to explain the transport of angular momentum to the exterior parts of the disk. <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">Turbulence</a>-enhanced viscosity was the mechanism thought to be responsible for such angular-momentum redistribution, although the origin of the turbulence itself was not well understood. <p>The Shakura-Sunyaev model assumes that the disk is in local thermal equilibrium, and can radiate its heat efficiently. In this case, the disk radiates away the viscous heat, cools, and becomes geometrically thin. However, this assumption may break down. In the radiatively inefficient case, the disk may "puff up" into a <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a> or some other three-dimensional solution like an Advection Dominated Accretion Flow (ADAF). The ADAF solutions usually require that the accretion rate is smaller than a few percent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddington_limit" title="Eddington limit">Eddington limit</a>. Another extreme is the case of <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">Saturn's rings</a>, where the disk is so gas poor that its angular momentum transport is dominated by solid body collisions and disk-moon gravitational interactions. The model is in agreement with recent astrophysical measurements using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_lensing" title="Gravitational lensing">gravitational lensing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetorotational_instability">Magnetorotational instability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Magnetorotational instability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Balbus and Hawley (1991)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BH1991_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BH1991-9">[9]</a></sup> proposed a mechanism which involves magnetic fields to generate the angular momentum transport. A simple system displaying this mechanism is a gas disk in the presence of a weak axial magnetic field. Two radially neighboring fluid elements will behave as two mass points connected by a massless spring, the spring tension playing the role of the magnetic tension. In a Keplerian disk the inner fluid element would be orbiting more rapidly than the outer, causing the spring to stretch. The inner fluid element is then forced by the spring to slow down, reduce correspondingly its angular momentum causing it to move to a lower orbit. The outer fluid element being pulled forward will speed up, increasing its angular momentum and move to a larger radius orbit. The spring tension will increase as the two fluid elements move further apart and the process runs away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.22.22B2003_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-.22.22B2003-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_fields_and_jets">Magnetic fields and jets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Magnetic fields and jets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Accretion disks are usually assumed to be threaded by the external magnetic fields present in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. These fields are typically weak (about few micro-Gauss), but they can get anchored to the matter in the disk, because of its high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a>, and carried inward toward the central <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>. This process can concentrate the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a> around the centre of the disk giving rise to very strong magnetic fields. Formation of powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jets" title="Astrophysical jets">astrophysical jets</a> along the rotation axis of accretion disks requires a large scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poloidal" title="Poloidal">poloidal</a> magnetic field in the inner regions of the disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP1982_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BP1982-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such magnetic fields may be advected inward from the interstellar medium or generated by a magnetic dynamo within the disk. Magnetic fields strengths at least of order 100 Gauss seem necessary for the magneto-centrifugal mechanism to launch powerful jets. There are problems, however, in carrying external magnetic flux inward towards the central star of the disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> High electric conductivity dictates that the magnetic field is frozen into the matter which is being accreted onto the central object with a slow velocity. However, the plasma is not a perfect electric conductor, so there is always some degree of dissipation. The magnetic field diffuses away faster than the rate at which it is being carried inward by accretion of matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A simple solution is assuming a <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> much larger than the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_diffusivity" title="Magnetic diffusivity">magnetic diffusivity</a> in the disk. However, numerical simulations, and theoretical models, show that the viscosity and magnetic diffusivity have almost the same order of magnitude in magneto-rotationally turbulent disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Some other factors may possibly affect the advection/diffusion rate: reduced turbulent magnetic diffusion on the surface layers; reduction of the <a href="/wiki/Shakura" title="Shakura">Shakura</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunyaev" title="Sunyaev">Sunyaev</a> viscosity by magnetic fields;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the generation of large scale fields by small scale MHD turbulence –a large scale dynamo.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic_models_of_sub-Eddington_accretion_disks_.28thin_disks.2C_ADAFs.29">Analytic models of sub-Eddington accretion disks (thin disks, ADAFs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Analytic models of sub-Eddington accretion disks (thin disks, ADAFs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the accretion rate is sub-Eddington and the opacity very high, the standard thin accretion disk is formed. It is geometrically thin in the vertical direction (has a disk-like shape), and is made of a relatively cold gas, with a negligible radiation pressure. The gas goes down on very tight spirals, resembling almost circular, almost free (Keplerian) orbits. Thin disks are relatively luminous and they have thermal electromagnetic spectra, i.e. not much different from that of a sum of black bodies. Radiative cooling is very efficient in thin disks. The classic 1974 work by Shakura and Sunyaev on thin accretion disks is one of the most often quoted papers in modern astrophysics. Thin disks were independently worked out by Lynden-Bell, Pringle and Rees. Pringle contributed in the past thirty years many key results to accretion disk theory, and wrote the classic 1981 review that for many years was the main source of information about accretion disks, and is still very useful today.</p>

<p>A fully general relativistic treatment, as needed for the inner part of the disk when the central object is a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>, has been provided by Page and Thorne ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and used for producing simulated optical images by Luminet <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and Marck,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> in which it is to be noted that, although such a system is intrinsically symmetric its image is not, because the relativistic rotation speed needed for centrifugal equilibrium in the very strong gravitational field near the black hole produces a strong Doppler redshift on the receding side (taken here to be on the right) whereas there will be a strong blueshift on the approaching side. It is also to be noted that due to light bending, the disk appears distorted but is nowhere hidden by the black hole (in contrast with what is shown in the misinformed artist's impression presented below).</p>
<p>When the accretion rate is sub-Eddington and the opacity very low, an ADAF is formed. This type of accretion disk was predicted in 1977 by Ichimaru. Although Ichimaru's paper was largely ignored, some elements of the ADAF model were present in the influential 1982 ion-tori paper by Rees, Phinney, Begelman and Blandford. ADAFs started to be intensely studied by many authors only after their rediscovery in the mid-1990 by Narayan and Yi, and independently by Abramowicz, Chen, Kato, Lasota (who coined the name ADAF), and Regev. Most important contributions to astrophysical applications of ADAFs have been made by Narayan and his collaborators. ADAFs are cooled by advection (heat captured in matter) rather than by radiation. They are very radiatively inefficient, geometrically extended, similar in shape to a sphere (or a "corona") rather than a disk, and very hot (close to the virial temperature). Because of their low efficiency, ADAFs are much less luminous than the Shakura-Sunyaev thin disks. ADAFs emit a power-law, non-thermal radiation, often with a strong Compton component.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic_models_of_super-Eddington_accretion_disks_.28slim_disks.2C_Polish_doughnuts.29">Analytic models of super-Eddington accretion disks (slim disks, Polish doughnuts)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Analytic models of super-Eddington accretion disks (slim disks, Polish doughnuts)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The theory of highly super-Eddington black hole accretion, M&gt;&gt;M<sub>Edd</sub>, was developed in the 1980s by Abramowicz, Jaroszynski, <a href="/wiki/Bohdan_Paczy%C5%84ski" title="Bohdan Paczyński">Paczyński</a>, Sikora and others in terms of "Polish doughnuts" (the name was coined by Rees). Polish doughnuts are low viscosity, optically thick, radiation pressure supported accretion disks cooled by <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>. They are radiatively very inefficient. Polish doughnuts resemble in shape a fat torus (a doughnut) with two narrow funnels along the rotation axis. The funnels collimate the radiation into beams with highly super-Eddington luminosities.</p>
<p>Slim disks (name coined by Kolakowska) have only moderately super-Eddington accretion rates, M≥M<sub>Edd</sub>, rather disk-like shapes, and almost thermal spectra. They are cooled by advection, and are radiatively ineffective. They were introduced by Abramowicz, Lasota, Czerny and Szuszkiewicz in 1988.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Excretion_disk">Excretion disk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accretion_disk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Excretion disk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The opposite of an accretion disk is an excretion disk where instead of material accreting from a disk on to a central object, material is excreted from the center outwards on to the disk. Excretion disks are formed when stars merge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
