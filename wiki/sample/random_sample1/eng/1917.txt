<p><b>Canine distemper</b> (sometimes termed <i>hardpad disease</i> in canine) is a <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> that affects a wide variety of animal families, including domestic and wild species of dogs, coyotes, foxes, pandas, wolves, ferrets, skunks, raccoons, and large cats, as well as pinnipeds, some primates, and a variety of other species. It was long believed that animals in the family <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">Felidae</a>, including many species of large cat as well as domestic cats, were resistant to canine distemper, until some researchers reported the prevalence of CDV infection in large felids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ikeda2001_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ikeda2001-1">[1]</a></sup> It is now known that both large <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">Felidae</a> and domestic cats can be infected, usually through close housing with dogs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ikeda2001_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ikeda2001-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreeneAppel2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreeneAppel2006-2">[2]</a></sup> or possibly blood transfusion from infected cats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ikeda2001_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ikeda2001-1">[1]</a></sup> but such infections appear to be self-limiting and largely without symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreeneAppel2006_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GreeneAppel2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In canines, distemper impacts several body systems, including the gastrointestinal and respiratory tracts and the spinal cord and brain, with common symptoms that include high fever, eye inflammation and eye/nose discharge, labored breathing and coughing, vomiting and diarrhea, loss of appetite and lethargy, and hardening of nose and footpads. The viral infection can be accompanied by secondary bacterial infections and can present eventual serious neurological symptoms.</p>
<p>Canine distemper is caused by a single-stranded <a href="/wiki/RNA_virus" title="RNA virus">RNA virus</a> of the family <i><a href="/wiki/Paramyxoviridae" title="Paramyxoviridae">Paramyxoviridae</a></i> (the same family of the viruses that causes <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumps" title="Mumps">mumps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_respiratory_syncytial_virus" title="Human respiratory syncytial virus">bronchiolitis</a> in humans). The disease is highly contagious via inhalation and fatal 50% of the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2014)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (December 2014)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup> Despite extensive vaccination in many regions, it remains a major disease of dogs, and is the leading cause of infectious disease death in dogs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (December 2014)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The origin of the word <i>distemper</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>distemperen</i>, meaning to upset the balance of the humors, which is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>destemprer</i>, meaning to disturb, which is from the <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> <i>distemperare</i>: Latin <i>dis-</i> and Latin <i>temperare</i>, meaning to not mix properly.</p>

<p>Distemper is caused by a single-stranded <a href="/wiki/RNA_virus" title="RNA virus">RNA virus</a> of the family <i><a href="/wiki/Paramyxoviridae" title="Paramyxoviridae">Paramyxoviridae</a></i>, which is a close relative of the viruses that cause <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> in humans and <a href="/wiki/Rinderpest" title="Rinderpest">rinderpest</a> in animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Distemper, or hardpad disease in canines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RadostitsEtalMerck_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-RadostitsEtalMerck-5">[5]</a></sup> affects animals in the families <a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">Canidae</a> (dog, fox, wolf, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon_dog" title="Raccoon dog">Raccoon dog</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mustelidae" title="Mustelidae">Mustelidae</a> (ferret, mink, skunk, wolverine, marten, badger, otter),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RadostitsEtalMerck_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-RadostitsEtalMerck-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Procyonidae" title="Procyonidae">Procyonidae</a> (raccoon, coati), <a href="/wiki/Ailuridae" title="Ailuridae">Ailuridae</a> (red panda), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ursidae" title="Ursidae">Ursidae</a> (bear), <a href="/wiki/Elephantidae" title="Elephantidae">Elephantidae</a> (Asian elephant), and some primates (e.g., Japanese monkey),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Viverridae" title="Viverridae">Viverridae</a> (raccoon-like South Asian<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> binturong, palm civet),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyaenidae" title="Hyaenidae">Hyaenidae</a> (hyena),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnipedia" title="Pinnipedia">Pinnipedia</a> (seals, walrus, sea lion, etc.),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and large <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">Felidae</a> (cats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> though not <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">domestic cats</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As McLeod notes, "Canine distemper causes symptoms in multiple body systems, including the gastrointestinal tract, respiratory tract, and the brain and spinal cord", and common symptoms in that species can include high fever, eye and nose discharge and eye inflammation, vomiting and diarrhea, lethargy and loss of appetite, labored breathing and/or coughing, and hardening of footpads and nose, with accompanying bacterial infection and eventual serious neurological symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A relative of the measles virus, canine distemper <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> (CDV) had its first recorded case described in 1905 by French veterinarian Henri Carré.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pomeroy2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pomeroy2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was first thought to be related to the <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a> and was attributed to several species of bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore1902_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore1902-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2013)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (December 2014)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It now affects all populations of domestic <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> and some populations of <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>. The first vaccine against canine distemper was developed in 1923 and 1924 by an Italian named Puntoni. Although he did not use a large population of dogs for his trials, his work shows that dogs can be vaccinated against this disease, producing solid immunity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A commercial vaccine was developed in 1950, yet owing to limited use, the virus remains prevalent in many populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pomeroy2008_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pomeroy2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The domestic dog has largely been responsible for introducing canine distemper to previously unexposed wildlife and now causes a serious conservation threat to many species of <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">carnivores</a> and some species of <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupials</a>. The virus contributed to the near-extinction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-footed_Ferret" title="Black-footed Ferret">black-footed ferret</a>. It also may have played a considerable role in the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Thylacine" title="Thylacine">thylacine (Tasmanian tiger)</a> and recurrently causes mortality among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Wild_Dog" title="African Wild Dog">African wild dogs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy2007-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1991, the lion population in Serengeti, Tanzania, experienced a 20% decline as a result of the disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessment2005_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment2005-12">[12]</a></sup> The disease has also mutated to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phocid_distemper_virus" title="Phocid distemper virus">phocid distemper virus</a>, which affects <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The virus, a single-stranded negative RNA, can cause systemic infection in the host carnivore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Puppies from three to six months old are particularly susceptible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remedys_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-remedys-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (December 2014)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> CDV spreads through aerosol droplets and through contact with infected bodily fluids, including nasal and ocular secretions, <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>, six to 22 days after exposure. It can also be spread by food and water contaminated with these fluids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carter_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carter-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2014)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1999_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1999-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2013)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The time between infection and disease is 14 to 18 days, although a fever can appear from three to six days after infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carmichael_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carmichael-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2014)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Canine distemper virus tends to orient its infection towards the <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphoid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelial</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous tissues</a>. The virus initially replicates in the lymphatic tissue of the respiratory tract. The virus then enters the blood stream and infects the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory" title="Respiratory">respiratory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal" title="Gastrointestinal">gastrointestinal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urogenital" title="Urogenital">urogenital</a> <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelial</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Optic_nerve" title="Optic nerve">optic nerves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> Therefore, the typical pathologic features of canine distemper include lymphoid depletion (causing <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppression</a> and leading to secondary infections), interstitial <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">encephalitis</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demyelination" title="Demyelination">demyelination</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hyperkeratosis" title="Hyperkeratosis">hyperkeratosis</a> of the nose and foot pads.</p>
<p>The mortality rate of the virus largely depends on the immune status of the infected dogs. Puppies experience the highest mortality rate, where complications such as <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">encephalitis</a> are more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1999_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1999-16">[16]</a></sup> In older dogs that develop distemper <a href="/wiki/Encephalomyelitis" title="Encephalomyelitis">encephalomyelitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestibular_disease" title="Vestibular disease">vestibular disease</a> may present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dewey2003_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey2003-18">[18]</a></sup> Around 15% of canine inflammatory central nervous system diseases are a result of CDV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elia2008_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elia2008-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease_progression">Disease progression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disease progression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The virus first appears in bronchial lymph nodes and tonsils two days after exposure. The virus then enters the blood stream on the second or third day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1999_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1999-16">[16]</a></sup> A first round of acute fever tends to begin around three to eight days after infection, which is often accompanied by a low white blood cell count, especially of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphocytes" title="Lymphocytes">lymphocytes</a>, as well as low platelet count. These signs may or may not be accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_(symptom)" title="Anorexia (symptom)">anorexia</a>, a runny nose, and discharge from the eye. This first round of fever typically recedes rapidly within 96 hours, and then a second round of fever begins around the 11th or 12th day and lasts at least a week. Gastrointestinal and respiratory problems tend to follow, which may become complicated with secondary bacterial infections. Inflammation of the brain and spinal cord, otherwise known as <a href="/wiki/Encephalomyelitis" title="Encephalomyelitis">encephalomyelitis</a>, either is associated with this, subsequently follows, or comes completely independent of these problems. A thickening of the footpads sometimes develops, and vesicularpustular lesions on the abdomen usually develop. Neurological signs typically are found in the animals with thickened footpads from the virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup> About half of sufferers experience <a href="/wiki/Meningoencephalitis" title="Meningoencephalitis">meningoencephalitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup> Less than 50% of the adult dogs that contract the disease die from it. Among puppies, the death rate often reaches 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastrointestinal_and_respiratory_signs">Gastrointestinal and respiratory signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gastrointestinal and respiratory signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commonly observed signs are a runny nose, vomiting and diarrhea, dehydration, excessive salivation, coughing and/or labored breathing, loss of appetite, and weight loss. When and if the neurological signs develop, incontinence may ensue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remedys_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-remedys-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (December 2014)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurological_signs">Neurological signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neurological signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The signs within the central nervous system include a localized involuntary twitching of muscles or groups of muscles, seizures often distinguished by salivation, and jaw movements commonly described as "chewing gum fits", or more appropriately as "distemper myoclonus". As the condition progresses, the seizures worsen and advance to grand mal convulsions followed by death of the animal. The animal may also show signs of sensitivity to light, incoordination, circling, increased sensitivity to sensory stimuli such as pain or touch, and deterioration of motor capabilities. Less commonly, they may lead to blindness and paralysis. The length of the systemic disease may be as short as 10 days, or the start of neurological signs may not come until several weeks or months later. Those few that survive usually have a small tic or twitch of varying levels of severity. With time, this tic will usually diminish somewhat in its severity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lasting_signs">Lasting signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lasting signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A dog that survives distemper will continue to have both nonlife-threatening and life-threatening signs throughout its lifespan. The most prevalent nonlife-threatening symptom is hard pad disease. This occurs when a dog experiences the thickening of the skin on the pads of its paws as well as on the end of its nose. Another lasting symptom commonly is <a href="/wiki/Enamel_hypoplasia" title="Enamel hypoplasia">enamel hypoplasia</a>. Puppies, especially, will have damage to the enamel of teeth that are not completely formed or those that have not yet grown through the gums. This is a result of the virus's killing the cells responsible for manufacturing the tooth enamel. These affected teeth tend to erode quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Insider2012_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Insider2012-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Life-threatening signs usually include those due to the degeneration of the nervous system. Dogs that have been infected with distemper tend to suffer a progressive deterioration of mental abilities and motor skills. With time, the dog can acquire more severe seizures, paralysis, reduction in sight and incoordination. These dogs are usually humanely euthanized because of the immense pain and suffering they face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Insider2012_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Insider2012-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The above signs, especially fever, respiratory signs, neurological signs, and thickened footpads occurring in unvaccinated dogs strongly indicate canine distemper. However, several febrile diseases match many of the signs of the disease and only recently has distinguishing between canine <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpes" title="Herpes">herpes</a> virus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parainfluenza" title="Parainfluenza">parainfluenza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leptospirosis" title="Leptospirosis">leptospirosis</a> been possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus, finding the virus by various methods in the dog's conjunctival cells gives a definitive diagnosis. In older dogs that develop distemper encephalomyelitis, diagnosis may be more difficult, since many of these dogs have an adequate vaccination history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dewey2003_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey2003-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>An additional test to confirm distemper is a <a href="/wiki/Brush_border" title="Brush border">brush border</a> slide of the bladder transitional epithelium of the inside lining from the bladder, stained with Dif-Quick. These infected cells have inclusions which stain a carmine red color, found in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">paranuclear</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> readability. About 90% of the bladder cells will be positive for inclusions in the early stages of distemper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DA2PPC_Vaccine" title="DA2PPC Vaccine">DA2PPC Vaccine</a></div>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a> against canine distemper exist for <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATCvet" title="ATCvet">ATCvet</a> code: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATC_code_I07" title="ATC code I07">QI07AD05</a> (<span title="www.whocc.no/atcvet"><a class="external text" href="http://www.whocc.no/atcvet/atcvet_index/?code=QI07AD05" rel="nofollow">WHO</a></span>) and combinations) and <a href="/wiki/Ferret" title="Ferret">domestic ferrets</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATC_code_I20" title="ATC code I20">QI20DD01</a> (<span title="www.whocc.no/atcvet"><a class="external text" href="http://www.whocc.no/atcvet/atcvet_index/?code=QI20DD01" rel="nofollow">WHO</a></span>)), which in many jurisdictions are mandatory for pets. Infected animals should be <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantined</a> from other dogs for several months owing to the length of time the animal may shed the virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> is destroyed in the environment by routine cleaning with disinfectants, detergents, or drying. It does not survive in the environment for more than a few hours at room temperature (20–25 °C), but can survive for a few weeks in shady environments at temperatures slightly above freezing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_Koret_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis_Koret-23">[23]</a></sup> It, along with other labile viruses, can also persist longer in serum and tissue debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1999_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1999-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no specific treatment for the canine distemper. As with measles, the treatment is symptomatic and supportive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CreevyMerck_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-CreevyMerck-3">[3]</a></sup> The supportive care is geared towards treating fluid/electrolyte imbalances, neurological symptoms, and preventing any secondary bacterial infections. Examples include administering fluids, electrolyte solutions, analgesics, anticonvulsants, broad spectrum antibiotics, antipyretics, parenteral nutrition and nursing care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">Vitamin A</a> supplements have been shown to reduce measles associated mortality in humans. Similar findings were made for ferrets infected with CDV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RODENEFFER.2C_C._2007_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-RODENEFFER.2C_C._2007-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The distemper virus was observed to be susceptible to ribavirin <i>in vitro</i>, and 0.02 to 0.05 micromols are needed to induce its mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Error_catastrophe" title="Error catastrophe">error catastrophe</a> and the inhibitory effect on virus replication by 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The main concern in the use of ribavirin was the result of its interaction with the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>. As the brain is an immunologically privileged area, the concern was the capacity of ribavirin to overcome this barrier. In a study using mice with encephalitis due to measles, once the virus has become established in the nervous phase, the blood–brain barrier prevents ribavirin from acting in the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The verification of all these results <i>in vivo</i> resulted in an effectiveness of 80%, versus 50% of the control group, in animals that had already reached the nervous phase of viral infection. Survival rates were recorded at the end of the 15-day treatment protocol. No post treatment survival data was recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (December 2014)">self-published source?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (December 2014)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The application of ribavirin demands a close monitoring of the animal due the risk of <a href="/wiki/Leukopenia" title="Leukopenia">leukopenia</a>; also, the ingestion of long-chain tryglicerides (fats) is needed to better absorb the drug,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and for preservation of gastric tissues, which are quite susceptible to it.</p>
<p>The medical and veterinary communities widely accept, while possible treatments are still being investigated as to their efficacy, prevention using vaccination as the most reliable way of preventing disease spread among the population.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">Prevalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Prevalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prevalence of canine distemper in the community has decreased dramatically due to the availability of vaccinations. However, the disease continues to spread among unvaccinated populations, such as animal shelters and pet stores. This provides a great threat to both the rural and urban communities throughout the United States, affecting both shelter and domestic canines. Despite the effectiveness of the vaccination, outbreaks of this disease continue to occur nationally. In April 2011, the Arizona Humane Society released a valley-wide pet health alert throughout the Phoenix, Arizona area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHS_ISSUES_VALLEYWIDE_PET_HEALTH_ALERT_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHS_ISSUES_VALLEYWIDE_PET_HEALTH_ALERT-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributing_factors">Contributing factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canine_distemper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Contributing factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outbreaks of canine distemper continue to occur throughout the United States and elsewhere, and are caused by many factors. These factors include the overpopulation of dogs and the irresponsibility of pet owners. The overpopulation of dogs is a national problem that organizations such as the Humane Society and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a> face every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pet_Overpopulation_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pet_Overpopulation-31">[31]</a></sup> This problem is even greater within areas such as Arizona, owing to the vast amount of rural land. An unaccountable number of strays that lack vaccinations reside in these areas and are therefore more susceptible to diseases such as canine distemper. These strays act as a host for the virus, spreading it throughout the surrounding area, including urban areas. Puppies and dogs that have not received their shots can then be infected if in a place where many dogs interact, such as a dog park.</p>
<p>The irresponsibility of pet owners is another factor that contributes to ongoing outbreaks of canine distemper. Puppies should begin vaccination at six to eight weeks of age and then continue getting the “booster shot” every two to four weeks until they are 16 weeks of age. Without the full series of shots, the vaccination will not provide protection against the virus. Since puppies are typically sold at the age of eight to ten weeks, they typically receive the first shot while still with their breeder, but the new owner often does not finish the series. These dogs are not protected against the virus and so are susceptible to canine distemper infection, continuing the downward spiral that leads to outbreaks throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canine_Distemper:_Prevention_of_Infections_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canine_Distemper:_Prevention_of_Infections-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
