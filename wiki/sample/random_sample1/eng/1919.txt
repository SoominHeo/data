<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Visual gag</h1>

<p>In comedy, a <b>visual gag</b> or <b>sight gag</b> is anything which conveys its <a href="/wiki/Humour" title="Humour">humour</a> visually, often without words being used at all. The gag may involve a physical impossibility or an unexpected occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LifeSightGagRevival_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-LifeSightGagRevival-1">[1]</a></sup> The humor is caused by alternative interpretations of the goings-on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll146_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll146-2">[2]</a></sup> Visual gags are used in magic, plays, and acting on television / movies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_gag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common type of visual gag is based on multiple interpretations of a series of events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll148_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll148-3">[3]</a></sup> This type is used in the 1935 <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_39_Steps_(1935_film)" title="The 39 Steps (1935 film)">The 39 Steps</a></i>. Lead actor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Donat" title="Robert Donat">Robert Donat</a> was kidnapping actress <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Carroll" title="Madeleine Carroll">Madeleine Carroll</a> and they were handcuffed. When they checked into an inn, the innkeeper assumed that they were passionate lovers because of the handcuffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll146_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll146-2">[2]</a></sup> The film used dialogue that could be interpreted both ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll148_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll148-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another visual gag is a switched image, often at the beginning of a film. A subsequent view of the scene shows something not viewed before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll152_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll152-4">[4]</a></sup> Switched movement may be the gag, such as <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pawnshop" title="The Pawnshop">The Pawnshop</a></i>. Chaplin fights with his coworker and punches at him once. Their boss walks in mid-swing and Chaplin changes the motion to act like he was dropping to his knees to scrub the floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll152_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll152-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noel Carroll established the most influential taxonomy of sight gags, breaking down the varieties into six types, two of which are enumerated below.</p>
<p>Mutual Interference: The audience is fully aware of the on-screen situation, but a character comically misunderstands</p>
<p>Mimed Metaphor: A variety of virtual simile an object may be treated as if it is a different object or be used in an unconventional way, such as acting like a <a href="/wiki/Doughnut" title="Doughnut">doughnut</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Barbell" title="Barbell">barbell</a> or using a tuba as an umbrella holder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_gag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous examples in cinema history of directors who based most of the humor in their films on visual gags, even to the point of using no or minimal dialogue. Visual gags began in live theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll146_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll146-2">[2]</a></sup> The first known use of a visual gag in a film was in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumi%C3%A8re_brothers" title="Lumière brothers">Lumière brothers</a>' 1895 short, <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Arroseur_Arros%C3%A9" title="L'Arroseur Arrosé">L'Arroseur Arrosé</a></i> ("The Waterer Watered"), in which a gardener watering his plants becomes the subject of a boy's prank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> An early pioneer in visual gags was <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">Georges Méliès</a>. The filmmaker experimented with techniques in the then-new film media creating techniques to trick viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll146_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll146-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">Vaudeville</a> actors often used gags in their routines. A classic vaudeville visual gag was for two actors to mirror each other's actions around a prop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesHolidayBooks_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimesHolidayBooks-7">[7]</a></sup> Visual gags were continued into <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">Silent films</a> and are considered a hallmark of the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll146_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll146-2">[2]</a></sup> In silent films, the actors in the mirror bit performed in silence with no music playing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesHolidayBooks_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimesHolidayBooks-7">[7]</a></sup> Comedians such as <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buster_Keaton" title="Buster Keaton">Buster Keaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lloyd" title="Harold Lloyd">Harold Lloyd</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">Marx Brothers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesHolidayBooks_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimesHolidayBooks-7">[7]</a></sup> often used visual humour because the technology used to record voices in film (and play it back in a synchronized presentation) did not yet exist. Often the differences between people are part of the comic duos, especially thin and fat actors are used such as <a href="/wiki/Abbott_and_Costello" title="Abbott and Costello">Abbott and Costello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurel_and_Hardy" title="Laurel and Hardy">Laurel and Hardy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> cites the fourth <i><a href="/wiki/Gilligan%27s_Island" title="Gilligan's Island">Gilligan's Island</a></i> episode, "Goodnight, Sweet Skipper", as a classic American sight gag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesNewSitcoms_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimesNewSitcoms-9">[9]</a></sup> The castaways were trying to contact civilization with a <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>. In the episode, Skipper can recall only how he converted the radio into a <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> when he is <a href="/wiki/Sleepwalking" title="Sleepwalking">sleepwalking</a>. After Skipper was unsuccessful, Gilligan got it to work by pounding on the radio; he used it to briefly contact a pilot flying overhead. Gilligan retrieved Skipper and demonstrated how he pounded on the radio, causing the guts of the radio to fall out. Their rescue was foiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesNewSitcoms_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimesNewSitcoms-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
