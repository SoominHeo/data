<p><b><span class="nowrap">(225088) 2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian object</a> (TNO) orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scattered_disc" title="Scattered disc">scattered disc</a>, approximately 1500 kilometers in diameter. It is the third-largest known body in the Solar System beyond Neptune,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P.C3.A1l2016_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-P.C3.A1l2016-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scida2015_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-scida2015-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL2016-05-11_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPL2016-05-11-14">[13]</a></sup> and the largest known body in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> without a name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownblog_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brownblog-15">[14]</a></sup> According to current estimates as of May 2016, it is slightly larger than <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">Makemake</a> or <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a>, and is hence almost certainly a <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">dwarf planet</a>. It has one known moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla2016a_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla2016a-6">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="nowrap">(225088) 2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> was discovered by <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> astronomers as part of the PhD thesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megan_E._Schwamb" title="Megan E. Schwamb">Megan E. Schwamb</a>, who was at the time a graduate student of <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael E. Brown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scida2011_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-scida2011-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brown nicknamed the object "<a href="/wiki/Snow_White" title="Snow White">Snow White</a>" for its presumed white color,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scida2011_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-scida2011-16">[15]</a></sup> because it would have to be very large or very bright to be detected by their survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownblog_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brownblog-15">[14]</a></sup> It was also the "<a href="/wiki/Seven_Dwarfs" title="Seven Dwarfs">seventh dwarf</a>" discovered by Brown's team, after Quaoar in 2002, Sedna in 2003, Haumea and Orcus in 2004, and Makemake and Eris in 2005. However, <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> turned out to be one of the reddest objects in the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a>, comparable only to <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">Quaoar</a>, so the nickname was dropped.</p>
<p><span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> is currently the largest known object in the Solar System without an official name. In 2011 Brown decided he finally had enough information to justify giving it one, because the discovery of water ice and the possibility of methane makes it noteworthy enough to warrant further study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scida2011_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-scida2011-16">[15]</a></sup> However, as of 2015, Brown had yet to propose a name, though he notes that in 2017 anyone will be able to make a proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="nowrap">(225088) 2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> came to <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a> around 1857.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[2]</a></sup> As of February 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> it is located 87.5 AU from the Sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDyS-distances_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AstDyS-distances-18">[17]</a></sup> and moving at 2.7 kilometers per second (6,000 miles per hour) with respect to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horizons_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-horizons-19">[18]</a></sup> This makes it the third-farthest known large body in the Solar System, after <a href="/wiki/V774104" title="V774104">V774104</a> (103 AU) and <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a> (96.3 AU), and farther out than <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">Sedna</a> (85.7 AU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownblog_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brownblog-15">[14]</a></sup> It has been farther from the Sun than Sedna since 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horizons_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-horizons-19">[18]</a></sup> <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> will be farther than both Sedna and Eris by 2045,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons2076_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons2076-20">[19]</a></sup> and it will reach aphelion in 2130.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horizons_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-horizons-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolute_magnitude">Absolute magnitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Absolute magnitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The size of an object can be calculated from its <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude#Solar_System_bodies_.28H.29" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute magnitude</a> (H) and the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (the amount of light it reflects). <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> has an absolute magnitude (H) of 1.92,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[2]</a></sup> which makes it the fifth-brightest <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">TNO</a> known,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownRedemption2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownRedemption2-21">[20]</a></sup> a little less bright than <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">Sedna</a> (H=1.5; D≈1,000 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sednajpl_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sednajpl-22">[21]</a></sup> and brighter than <a href="/wiki/90482_Orcus" title="90482 Orcus">Orcus</a> (H=2.2; D≈800 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orcusjpl_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orcusjpl-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> is among the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object#Colors" title="Trans-Neptunian object">reddest</a> objects known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2011_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011-11">[10]</a></sup> This is probably in part due to methane frosts, which turn red when bombarded by sunlight and cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2011_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_composition_and_atmosphere">Surface composition and atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surface composition and atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spectrum of <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> shows signatures for both water <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> and methane, which makes it similar in composition to <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">Quaoar</a>. The presence of red methane frost on the surfaces of both <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> and Quaoar implies the existence of a tenuous methane atmosphere on both objects, slowly evaporating into space. Although <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> comes closer to the Sun than Quaoar, and is thus warm enough that a methane atmosphere should evaporate, its larger mass makes retention of an atmosphere just possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2011_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011-11">[10]</a></sup> In particular, 2007 OR10's large size means that it is likely to retain even nitrogen, which almost all TNOs lose over the course of their existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> The presence of water ice on the surface of <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> implies a brief period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanism" title="Cryovolcanism">cryovolcanism</a> in its distant past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownRedemption3_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownRedemption3-25">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarf-planet_status">Dwarf-planet status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dwarf-planet status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on the most recent size estimates as of May 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scida2015_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-scida2015-8">[7]</a></sup> <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> would be the third-largest dwarf planet, after Pluto and Eris, and before Makemake, Haumea, and Ceres, though the error bars overlap with those of Makemake. The IAU has not addressed the possibility of accepting additional dwarf planets since before the discovery of <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> was announced. Brown states that it "must be a dwarf planet even if predominantly rocky",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-27">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">Scott Sheppard</a> and colleagues think that it is "likely" to be a dwarf planet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheppard2011_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheppard2011-28">[27]</a></sup> based on its minimum possible diameter (552 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> and what is understood of the conditions for <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">hydrostatic equilibrium</a> in cold icy–rocky bodies. <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> is too distant for its diameter to be resolved directly; Brown's estimate of 1,000–1,500 km is based on calculating the albedo that is the best fit in his model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2011_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011-11">[10]</a></sup> which agrees with the <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006128000000000000♠</span>1280<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>210 km</span> determined from observations by the <i>Herschel</i> space observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool4_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool4-9">[8]</a></sup> If the orbit of OR10's small <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">satellite</a> (see below) can be well determined, its mass could be calculated directly; mass is also a factor in hydrostatic equilibrium.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite">Satellite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(225088)_2007_OR10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Satellite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, analysis of Hubble images of <span class="nowrap">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></span> taken in 2010 revealed a satellite around 300 km in diameter and orbiting at a distance of at least 15,000 km. It was announced at the DPS48 meeting on 17 October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla2016a_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla2016a-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS48_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-DPS48-30">[29]</a></sup> The satellite is probably too small and dark to affect size estimates for the likely dwarf planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla2016a_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla2016a-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
