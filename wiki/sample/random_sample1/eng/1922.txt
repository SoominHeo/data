<p><b>Opening Day</b> is the day on which professional <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> leagues begin their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_season" title="Regular season">regular season</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> and most of the <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">minor leagues</a>, this day typically falls during the first week of April.</p>
<p>For baseball fans, Opening Day serves as a symbol of rebirth; writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Boswell" title="Thomas Boswell">Thomas Boswell</a> once penned a book titled, <i>Why Time Begins on Opening Day</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opening_day_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-opening_day-1">[1]</a></sup> Many feel that the occasion represents a newness or a chance to forget last season, in that all 30 of the major league clubs and their millions of fans begin with 0–0 records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opening_day_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-opening_day-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Opening Day festivities extend throughout the sport of baseball, from hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">Minor League Baseball</a> franchises to <a href="/wiki/College_baseball" title="College baseball">college</a>, high school, and youth leagues in North America and beyond. Since <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> generally starts their season first among professional leagues, their Opening Day is the one most commonly recognized by the general public. Most of the minor leagues start a few days later, but within the same week; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_season" title="Short season">short season</a> Class A and Rookie leagues are exceptions, since they begin play in June. (College, high school and youth baseball seasons vary widely depending on location and weather conditions.) Opening Day ignores the <a href="/wiki/Exhibition_game" title="Exhibition game">exhibition games</a> played during <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a> in the month leading up to Opening Day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Opening_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FindlayMarketParade.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/FindlayMarketParade.jpg/300px-FindlayMarketParade.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/FindlayMarketParade.jpg/450px-FindlayMarketParade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/FindlayMarketParade.jpg/600px-FindlayMarketParade.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FindlayMarketParade.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Findlay_Market" title="Findlay Market">Findlay Market</a> Parade at <a href="/wiki/Fountain_Square,_Cincinnati" title="Fountain Square, Cincinnati">Fountain Square</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Cincinnati" title="Downtown Cincinnati">Downtown Cincinnati</a> in 2015. The parade has been held annually since <a href="/wiki/1920_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="1920 Cincinnati Reds season">1920</a> to celebrate Opening Day in Cincinnati.</div>

<p>For generations, Opening Day has arrived amid pageantry. In <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, home of the sport's <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Red_Stockings" title="Cincinnati Red Stockings">first professional team</a>, the annual <a href="/wiki/Findlay_Market" title="Findlay Market">Findlay Market</a> Parade marks an official "city holiday" with young and old alike taking the day off to cheer on the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Reds</a>. For decades, the first pitch of every major league season officially took place in Cincinnati, and the Reds remain the only major league team to always open the season with a home game (save for 1966, when they started the season at Chicago after rain washed out the opening series in Cincinnati; and 1990, when due to a <a href="/wiki/1990_Major_League_Baseball_lockout" title="1990 Major League Baseball lockout">lockout</a> affecting the schedule they opened the season at Houston).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> have been the Reds' most common Opening Day opponent, visiting Cincinnati 36 times on Opening Day, most recently in 2007. The <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, whom the current Reds organization played their first Opening Day against in 1882, is a close second with 31, most recently in 2015; no other team has more than 19 (by the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>, most recently in 2014), largely due to the Cubs and Pirates rotating as the Opening Day opponents from 1899 to 1916, then the two teams and the Cardinals rotated from 1917 to 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Fittingly, the Reds were also the first team to host an <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">Interleague game</a> on Opening Day when the team hosted the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim</a> in the first year of year-round Interleague play in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1994 <a href="/wiki/ESPN_Major_League_Baseball" title="ESPN Major League Baseball">ESPN</a> has televised a regular-season game the night before "Opening Day" and recent years have seen the staging of season-opening series in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, Japan, and Australia. While these are technically "opening games", Major League Baseball still reserves the title "Opening Day" for the first day in which multiple games are played. (For the first time ever, three televised games were played on Sunday, April 3, 2016, before the traditional "Opening Day" slate of games on Monday, April 4.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Opening Day is a state of mind as well, with countless baseball fans known to recognize this unofficial holiday as a good reason to call in sick at work or be <a href="/wiki/Truancy" title="Truancy">truant</a> from school (as most teams typically play their home opener in the afternoon) and go out to the ballpark for the first of 162 regular season games. Teams' home openers serve as the only regular season games during the year in which the entire rosters of both teams as well as coaches and clubhouse staff are introduced to the crowd prior to the games; for the rest of the year, ballparks only introduce the starting lineups (including the <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> in <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> ballparks) and the team's <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a>. Some teams, among them the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>, have had their broadcasters as the master of pre-game ceremonies for their home openers, which also typically feature appearances by retired players, local celebrities or media personalities, politicians, and other dignitaries.</p>

<p>Hall of Fame pitcher <a href="/wiki/Early_Wynn" title="Early Wynn">Early Wynn</a>, who played for the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901–60)">Washington Senators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>, once said: "An opener is not like any other game. There's that little extra excitement, a faster beating of the heart. You have that anxiety to get off to a good start, for yourself and for the team. You know that when you win the first one, you can't lose 'em all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opening_day_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-opening_day-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to Opening Day, the teams' <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">managers</a> have to decide the starting <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitchers</a> for the game, an assignment typically given to the <a href="/wiki/Ace_(baseball)" title="Ace (baseball)">ace</a> of each team's staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For a pitcher to start on Opening Day is considered an honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, <a href="/wiki/Ozzie_Smith" title="Ozzie Smith">Ozzie Smith</a>, with the support of <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch" title="Anheuser-Busch">Anheuser-Busch</a>, began a campaign using the <i><a href="/wiki/We_the_People_(petitioning_system)" title="We the People (petitioning system)">We the People</a></i> site on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WhiteHouse.gov" title="WhiteHouse.gov">WhiteHouse.gov</a> to petition the U.S. government to make Opening Day an official <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Public holidays in the United States">national holiday</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorable_moments">Memorable moments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Opening_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Memorable moments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="728" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg/300px-Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg/450px-Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg/600px-Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wilson_opening_day_1916.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> throws out the ceremonial first pitch on Opening Day. Photo 1916</div>

<p>In 1907, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(NL)" title="History of the New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> forfeited their game at the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>, 9–0, after rowdy fans made and threw snowballs. Without police available to restore order, umpire <a href="/wiki/Bill_Klem" title="Bill Klem">Bill Klem</a> awarded the game to the Phillies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1940, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Bob_Feller" title="Bob Feller">Bob Feller</a> threw a <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> to open the season against the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>. It remains the only no-hitter in Opening Day history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Twelve <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. Presidents</a> have thrown the first ball of the season. On April 14, 1910, baseball enthusiast <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a> attended the home opener in Washington, D.C., becoming the first U.S. President to <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_first_pitch" title="Ceremonial first pitch">throw out the first pitch</a> to start a season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> threw first pitches with both his right and left arm in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opening_day_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-opening_day-1">[1]</a></sup> On April 4, 1994, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> inaugurated the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a>' new ballpark, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobs_Field" title="Jacobs Field">Jacobs Field</a>, with the first pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">.449 hitter</a> in openers, with three <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> and fourteen <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a> during fourteen such games and at least one hit in each game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseball-almanac1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-almanac1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 4, 1974, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> ignited the Opening Day crowd at Cincinnati's <a href="/wiki/Riverfront_Stadium" title="Riverfront Stadium">Riverfront Stadium</a> with his 714th career home run, tying <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> on Major League Baseball's all-time list. Aaron finished his career with 755 home runs.</p>
<p>In 14 season openers for the Washington Senators, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a> pitched a record nine shutouts. Two of his more famous starts include a 3–0 victory over the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philadelphia_Athletics" title="History of the Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a> in 1910 and a 1–0 marathon victory while battling the A's <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Rommel" title="Eddie Rommel">Eddie Rommel</a> for 15 innings.</p>
<p>On April 4, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Young" title="Dmitri Young">Dmitri Young</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> hit three home runs in his team's opener against the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> at Detroit's <a href="/wiki/Comerica_Park" title="Comerica Park">Comerica Park</a>. He became the third major leaguer with three home runs on Opening Day, following the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>' <a href="/wiki/George_Bell_(outfielder)" title="George Bell (outfielder)">George Bell</a> in 1988 and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>' <a href="/wiki/Tuffy_Rhodes" title="Tuffy Rhodes">Tuffy Rhodes</a> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> were the first major league team to open their home season with a <a href="/wiki/Night_game" title="Night game">night game</a>, beating the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> 4–2 at <a href="/wiki/Sportsman%27s_Park" title="Sportsman's Park">Sportsman's Park</a> on April 18, 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The longest Opening Day game in major league history was played on April 5, 2012 between the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>. The game, played at Cleveland's <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Field" title="Progressive Field">Progressive Field</a>, ended with the Blue Jays beating the Indians, 7–4, in 16 innings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The previous record for longest Opening Day game was on April 19, 1960, again at Cleveland. That game, lasting 15 innings, also saw the Indians in a losing effort, 4–2, versus the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philadelphia_Athletics" title="History of the Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901–60)">Washington Senators</a> also played a 15-inning season opener on April 13, 1926, with Washington winning, 1–0, at home.</p>
<p>On rare occasions, predominantly in the early 20th century, a team would open its home season with a <a href="/wiki/Doubleheader_(baseball)" title="Doubleheader (baseball)">doubleheader</a>. The first of these came when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Americans" title="Boston Americans">Boston Americans</a> hosted the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philadelphia_Athletics" title="History of the Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a> for two games on April 20, 1903, with Boston winning the first game, 9–4, and Philadelphia taking the second game, 10–7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The most recent Opening Day doubleheader in the major leagues came on April 7, 1971, with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> defeating the host <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> in both games (6–5 and 12–4, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Hall of Famer</a> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Seaver" title="Tom Seaver">Tom Seaver</a> has been a starting pitcher for the most Opening Day games in Major League history, doing the honors 16 times for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_Opening_Days">Recent Opening Days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Opening_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recent Opening Days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Major League Baseball had most of its teams open the 2011 season on a Thursday (March 31) or Friday (April 1) rather than the traditional Monday, in order to prevent the World Series from extending into November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Similarly, most teams opened the 2012 season on Thursday (April 5) or Friday (April 6). However, subsequent seasons have returned to Monday openers for most teams through at least 2017.</p>
