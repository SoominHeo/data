<p><b>Formicariidae</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of smallish <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> of subtropical and tropical <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> known as formicariids. They are between 10 and 20 cm (4 and 8 in) in length, and are related to the <a href="/wiki/Antbird" title="Antbird">antbirds</a>, Thamnophilidae, and <a href="/wiki/Gnateater" title="Gnateater">gnateaters</a>, Conopophagidae. This family contains probably (see below) some 12 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in two fairly small <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>.</p>
<p>These are forest birds that tend to feed on insects at or near the ground since many are specialist <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> eaters. Most are drab in appearance with shades of (rusty) brown, black, and white being their dominant tones. Compared to other birds that specialize in following ants, this family is the most tied to the ground. The long, powerful legs (which lend the birds a distinctive upright posture) and an essentially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestigial" title="Vestigial">vestigial</a> tail aid this lifestyle.</p>
<p>They lay two or three eggs in a nest in a tree, both sexes incubating.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics">Systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Formicariidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>antthrushes</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> <i>Formicarius</i> and <i>Chamaeza</i> are similar in appearance to small <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" title="Rallidae">rails</a>. Their sexes are alike in plumage, and they walk like <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starlings</a>. The <i>thrush</i> part of the name refers only to the similarity in size (and in <i>Chamaeza</i> also coloration) to true <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a>, not to an <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> relationship.</p>
<p>Recent research (Irestedt <i>et al.</i> 2002, Rice 2005a,b) indicates that the Formicariidae as previously delimited are highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>, judging from comparison of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA">mt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NDNA" title="NDNA">nDNA</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">sequences</a>. The aberrant bar-bellied "antpittas" of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Pittasoma" title="Pittasoma">Pittasoma</a>,</i> which were formerly placed here, belong to the <a href="/wiki/Gnateater" title="Gnateater">gnateater</a> family (which initially was also considered part of the Formicariidae); as the gnateaters proper, they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_dichromatism" title="Sexual dichromatism">sexually dichromatic</a>. In addition, the true antpittas formerly placed in this family are now found in their own family, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grallariidae" title="Grallariidae">Grallariidae</a>. On the other hand, at least a large proportion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinocryptidae" title="Rhinocryptidae">Rhinocryptidae</a> (<b>tapaculos</b>), including the <a href="/wiki/Type_genus" title="Type genus">type genus</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinocrypta" title="Rhinocrypta">Rhinocrypta</a></i>, seem to be closer to the antthrushes, but are still considered a distinct family.</p>
<p>Very little molecular data is currently available for rhinocryptids, which are moreover rather non-diagnostic morphologically. As relationships with related groups such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furnariidae" title="Furnariidae">ovenbirds</a> have not been fully resolved, it is certain that the systematics of the group will be revised soon, but it is not clear how exactly the new systematic lineup will be. On the other hand, morphological, behavioral and molecular analyses of the true antpittas agree astoundingly well as regards their internal systematics, considering the high amount of lifestyle-related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homoplasies" title="Homoplasies">homoplasies</a> in the birds discussed here. Recent taxonomic favor has moved three major lineages – antthrushes, tapaculos and allies, and antpittas into separate families.</p>
