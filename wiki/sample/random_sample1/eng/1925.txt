<p><i><b>Nepeta cataria</b></i>, commonly known as <b>catnip</b>, <b>catswort</b>, or <b>catmint</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Nepeta" title="Nepeta">Nepeta</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Lamiaceae" title="Lamiaceae">Lamiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native</a> to southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">central Asia</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It is also widely <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation_(biology)" title="Naturalisation (biology)">naturalized</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRIN-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The common name <a href="/wiki/Catmint" title="Catmint">catmint</a> can also refer to the genus as a whole. The names catnip and catmint are derived from the intense attraction most <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">cats</a> have towards them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nepeta cataria</i> is a short-lived <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a>, growing 50â100Â cm (20â39Â in) tall and wide. It resembles a typical <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a> family member in appearance by having the characteristic square stem that members of the <a href="/wiki/Lamiaceae" title="Lamiaceae">Lamiaceae</a> plant family have,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but with brown-green foliage. The coarse-toothed leaves are triangular to ovate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbot_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mbot-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The small bilabiate <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> can be white and finely spotted with pale purple or pink. They are showy and fragrant. The plant blooms from late spring through autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbot_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-mbot-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nepeta cataria</i> was one of the many species <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by Linnaeus in 1753 in his landmark work <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He had previously described it in 1738 as <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Nepeta floribus interrupte spicatis pedunculatis</span></i> (meaning "<i>Nepeta</i> with flowers in a stalked, interrupted spike"), before the commencement of <a href="/wiki/Linnaean_taxonomy" title="Linnaean taxonomy">Linnaean taxonomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The plant <a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Nepetalactone" title="Nepetalactone">nepetalactone</a> is the main chemical constituent of the essential oil of <i>Nepeta cataria</i>. Nepetalactone can be extracted from catnip by <a href="/wiki/Steam_distillation" title="Steam distillation">steam distillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Nepeta cataria</i> is cultivated as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> for use in gardens. It is also grown for its attractant qualities to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_cat" title="House cat">house cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbot_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-mbot-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plant is <a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">drought-tolerant</a> and deer-resistant. It can be a repellent for certain insects, including <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squash_bug" title="Squash bug">squash bugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbot_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-mbot-7">[7]</a></sup> Catnip is best grown in full sunlight and grows as a loosely branching, low <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Varieties include <i>Nepeta cataria</i> var. <i>citriodora</i> (or <i>N. cataria</i> subsp. <i>citriodora</i>), or "lemon catnip".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractant">Attractant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Attractant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The compound iridodial, extracted from catnip oil, has been found to attract <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacewings" title="Lacewings">lacewings</a> which eat aphids and mites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repellent">Repellent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Repellent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nepetalactone is a mosquito and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fly_repellent" title="Fly repellent">fly repellent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingsley_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kingsley-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Oil isolated from catnip by steam distillation is a repellent against insects, in particular mosquitoes, cockroaches and termites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Research suggests that, while ten times more effective than <a href="/wiki/DEET" title="DEET">DEET</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> it is not as effective as a repellent when used on the skin when compared with DEET or <a href="/wiki/SS220" title="SS220">SS220</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_humans">Effect on humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catnip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effect on humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Nepeta cataria</i> can be brewed to produce a herbal tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It is also used as a culinary <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herb</a> for many dishes. Catnip can also be ingested through smoking the herb. It has only a negligible effect on relaxation, like other <a href="/wiki/Herbal_cigarette" title="Herbal cigarette">herbal cigarettes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (March 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Catnip has a history of medicinal use for a variety of ailments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup> The plant has been consumed as a tea, juice, <a href="/wiki/Tincture" title="Tincture">tincture</a>, infusion or poultice, and has also been smoked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup> However, its medicinal use has fallen out of favor with the development of more commonplace pharmaceutical drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Catnip contains the feline attractant <a href="/wiki/Nepetalactone" title="Nepetalactone">nepetalactone</a>. <i>Nepeta cataria</i> (and some other species within the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nepeta" title="Nepeta">Nepeta</a></i>) are known for their behavioral effects on the <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">cat</a> family, not only on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_cat" title="Domestic cat">domestic cats</a> but also other species of cats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup> Several tests showed that <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">cougars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serval" title="Serval">servals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a> often reacted strongly to catnip in a manner similar to domestic cats and while <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigers" title="Tigers">tigers</a> can react strongly as well, they do not react as consistently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Durand_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Durand-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>With domestic cats, <i>N. cataria</i> is used as a recreational substance for pet cats' enjoyment, and catnip and catnip-laced products designed for use with domesticated cats are available to consumers. Common behaviors cats display when they sense the bruised leaves or stems of catnip are rubbing on the plant, rolling on the ground, pawing at it, licking it, and chewing it. Consuming much of the plant is followed by drooling, sleepiness, anxiety, leaping about and <a href="/wiki/Purr" title="Purr">purring</a>. Some growl, <a href="/wiki/Meow" title="Meow">meow</a>, scratch or bite at the hand holding it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The main response period after exposure is generally between five and fifteen minutes, after which <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_fatigue" title="Olfactory fatigue">olfactory fatigue</a> usually sets in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2007_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2007-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.107</sup></p>
<p>Cats detect nepetalactone through their <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_epithelium" title="Olfactory epithelium">olfactory epithelium</a>, not through their <a href="/wiki/Vomeronasal_organ" title="Vomeronasal organ">vomeronasal organ</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> At the olfactory epithelium, the nepetalactone binds to one or more <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">olfactory receptors</a>.</p>
<p>Not all cats are affected by catnip;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup> roughly 33% are not affected by the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The behavior is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grognet_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Grognet-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other plants that also have this effect on cats include <a href="/wiki/Valerian_(herb)" title="Valerian (herb)">valerian</a> (<i>Valeriana officinalis</i>) and plants that contain <a href="/wiki/Actinidine" title="Actinidine">actinidine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some domestic house cats that do not react to catnip may react to <a href="/wiki/Lonicera_tatarica" title="Lonicera tatarica">Tartarian honeysuckle</a> in a manner similar to those that do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2007_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2007-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.108</sup></p>
