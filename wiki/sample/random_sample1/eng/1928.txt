<p>The <b>ocean sunfish</b> or <b>common mola</b> (<i>Mola mola</i>) is the heaviest known <a href="/wiki/Osteichthyes" title="Osteichthyes">bony fish</a> in the world. It has an average adult weight between 247 and 1,000 kg (545–2,205 lb). The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is native to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_fish" title="Tropical fish">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> waters around the globe. It resembles a fish head with a tail, and its main body is flattened laterally. Sunfish can be as tall as they are long when their <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal</a> and ventral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fins" title="Pelvic fins">fins</a> are extended.</p>
<p>Sunfish live on a diet consisting mainly of <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>, but because this diet is nutritionally poor, they consume large amounts to develop and maintain their great bulk. Females of the species can produce more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> than any other known <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> up to 300,000,000 at a time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sunfish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fry_(biology)" title="Fry (biology)">fry</a> resemble miniature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pufferfish" title="Pufferfish">pufferfish</a>, with large pectoral fins, a tail fin, and body spines uncharacteristic of adult sunfish.</p>
<p>Adult sunfish are vulnerable to few natural predators, but <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">sea lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> will consume them. Among humans, sunfish are considered a delicacy in some parts of the world, including <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU" title="EU">EU</a>, regulations ban the sale of fish and fishery products derived from the family <a href="/wiki/Molidae" title="Molidae">Molidae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BannedinEEC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BannedinEEC-5">[5]</a></sup> Sunfish are frequently caught in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>.</p>
<p>A member of the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Tetraodontiformes" title="Tetraodontiformes">Tetraodontiformes</a>, which also includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pufferfish" title="Pufferfish">pufferfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porcupinefish" title="Porcupinefish">porcupinefish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Filefish" title="Filefish">filefish</a>, the sunfish shares many traits common to members of this order. The ocean sunfish, <i>Mola mola</i>, is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_and_taxonomy">Naming and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Naming and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:257px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mola_mola2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mola_mola2.jpg/255px-Mola_mola2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mola_mola2.jpg/383px-Mola_mola2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mola_mola2.jpg/510px-Mola_mola2.jpg 2x" width="255"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mola_mola2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ocean sunfish is the heaviest of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fishes</a>. It has a flattened body and is as tall as it is long. It feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many of the sunfish's various names allude to its flattened shape. Its specific name, <i>mola</i>, is Latin for "millstone", which the fish resembles because of its gray color, rough texture, and rounded body. Its common English name, sunfish, refers to the animal's habit of sunbathing at the surface of the water. The Dutch-, Portuguese-, French-, Catalan-, Spanish-, Italian-, Russian-, Greek- and German-language names, respectively <i>maanvis</i>, <i>peixe lua</i>, <i>poisson lune</i>, <i>peix lluna</i>, <i>pez luna</i>, <i>pesce luna</i>, <i>рыба-луна</i>, <i>φεγγαρόψαρο</i> and <i>Mondfisch</i>, mean "moon fish", in reference to its rounded shape. In German, the fish is also known as <i>Schwimmender Kopf</i>, or "swimming head". In Polish, it is named <i>samogłów</i>, meaning "head alone", because it has no true tail. The Chinese translation of its academic name is <i>fan-che yu</i> 翻車魚, meaning "toppled wheel fish". The ocean sunfish has various superseded <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> synonyms, and was originally classified in the pufferfish genus, as <i><a href="/wiki/Tetraodon" title="Tetraodon">Tetraodon</a> mola</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CatalogOfFishes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CatalogOfFishes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnotatedChecklist_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnnotatedChecklist-7">[7]</a></sup> It is now placed in its own <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, <i>Mola</i>, with two <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>: <i>Mola mola</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mola_ramsayi" title="Mola ramsayi">Mola ramsayi</a></i>. The ocean sunfish, <i>Mola mola</i>, is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EvolutionSunfishPaper_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EvolutionSunfishPaper-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The genus <i>Mola</i> belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Molidae" title="Molidae">Molidae</a>. This family comprises three genera: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masturus" title="Masturus">Masturus</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mola_(genus)" title="Mola (genus)">Mola</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Slender_sunfish" title="Slender sunfish">Ranzania</a></i>. The common name "sunfish" without qualifier is used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">marine</a> family Molidae as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> sunfishes in the family <a href="/wiki/Centrarchidae" title="Centrarchidae">Centrarchidae</a> which are unrelated to Molidae. On the other hand, the name "ocean sunfish" and "mola" refer only to the family Molidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The family Molidae belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Tetraodontiformes" title="Tetraodontiformes">Tetraodontiformes</a>, which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pufferfish" title="Pufferfish">pufferfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porcupinefish" title="Porcupinefish">porcupinefish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Filefish" title="Filefish">filefish</a>. It shares many traits common to members of this order, including the four fused teeth that form the characteristic beak and give the order its name (<i>tetra</i>=four, <i>odous</i>=tooth, and <i>forma</i>=shape). Indeed, sunfish fry resemble spiky pufferfish more than they resemble adult molas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishEvolution_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishEvolution-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a> of the ocean sunfish is replaced by a rounded clavus, creating the body's distinct truncated shape. The body is flattened laterally, giving it a long oval shape when seen head-on. The pectoral fins are small and fan-shaped, while the dorsal fin and the anal fin are lengthened, often making the fish as tall as it is long. Specimens up to 3.2 m (10.5 ft) in height have been recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZHeraldVisitor_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZHeraldVisitor-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mature ocean sunfish has an average length of 1.8 m (5.9 ft) and a fin-to-fin length of 2.5 m (8.2 ft). The average weight of mature specimens can range from 247 to 1,000 kg (545 to 2,205 lb),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but even larger individuals are not unheard of. The maximum size is up to 3.3 m (10.8 ft) in length<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZHeraldVisitor_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NZHeraldVisitor-10">[10]</a></sup> 4.2 m (14 ft) across the fins<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-13">[13]</a></sup> and up to 2,300 kg (5,100 lb) in mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The spinal column of <i>M. mola</i> contains fewer <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a> and is shorter in relation to the body than that of any other fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> Although the sunfish descended from <a href="/wiki/Osteichthyes" title="Osteichthyes">bony ancestors</a>, its skeleton contains largely <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilaginous</a> tissues, which are lighter than <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, allowing it to grow to sizes impractical for other bony fishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Its teeth are fused into a beak-like structure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_teeth" title="Pharyngeal teeth">pharyngeal teeth</a> located in the throat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sunfish lacks a <a href="/wiki/Swim_bladder" title="Swim bladder">swim bladder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> Some sources indicate the internal organs contain a concentrated <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxin" title="Neurotoxin">neurotoxin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetrodotoxin" title="Tetrodotoxin">tetrodotoxin</a>, like the organs of other poisonous tetraodontiformes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-14">[14]</a></sup> while others dispute this claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishResearch_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishResearch-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fins">Fins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the course of its evolution, the caudal fin (tail) of the sunfish disappeared, to be replaced by a lumpy pseudotail, the clavus. This structure is formed by the convergence of the dorsal and anal fins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StrangeTail_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-StrangeTail-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leis.27Conundrum_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leis.27Conundrum-20">[20]</a></sup> and is used by the fish as a rudder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineFieldGuide_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineFieldGuide-21">[21]</a></sup> The smooth-denticled clavus retains 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fin_ray" title="Fin ray">fin rays</a>, and terminates in a number of rounded ossicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ocean sunfish often swim near the surface, and their protruding dorsal fins are sometimes mistaken for those of <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo-23">[23]</a></sup> However, the two can be distinguished by the motion of the fin. Sharks, like most fish, swim by moving the tail sideways while keeping the dorsal fin stationary. The sunfish, though, swings its dorsal fin and anal fin in a characteristic <a href="/wiki/Sculling" title="Sculling">sculling</a> motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skin">Skin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Skin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adult sunfish range from brown to silvery-grey or white, with a variety of mottled skin patterns; some of these patterns may be region-specific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> Coloration is often darker on the dorsal surface, fading to a lighter shade ventrally as a form of countershading camouflage. <i>M. mola</i> also exhibits the ability to vary skin coloration from light to dark, especially when under attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> The skin, which contains large amounts of reticulated collagen, can be up to 7.3 cm (2.9 in) thick on the ventral surface, and is covered by denticles and a layer of <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a>. The skin on the clavus is smoother than that on the body, where it can be as rough as sandpaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than 40 species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a> may reside on the skin and internally, motivating the fish to seek relief in a number of ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup> One of the most frequent ocean sunfish parasites is the flatworm, <a href="/wiki/Accacoelium_contortum" title="Accacoelium contortum">Accacoelium contortum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In temperate regions, drifting kelp fields harbor <a href="/wiki/Cleaner_fish" title="Cleaner fish">cleaner wrasses</a> and other fish which remove parasites from the skin of visiting sunfish. In the tropics, <i>M. mola</i> solicits cleaning help from reef fishes. By basking on its side at the surface, the sunfish also allows seabirds to feed on parasites from its skin. Sunfish have been reported to <a href="/wiki/Cetacean_surfacing_behaviour#Breaching_and_lunging" title="Cetacean surfacing behaviour">breach</a>, clearing the surface by approximately 3 m (9.8 ft), in an effort to dislodge embedded parasites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_behavior">Range and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Range and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ocean sunfish are native to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_fish" title="Tropical fish">tropical</a> waters of every ocean in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Mola</i> <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotypes</a> appear to vary widely between the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, but genetic differences between individuals in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern hemispheres</a> are minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrackingSunfish_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrackingSunfish-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although early research suggested sunfish moved around mainly by drifting with ocean currents, individuals have been recorded swimming 26 km (16 mi)in a day, at a top speed of 3.2 km/h (2.0 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineFieldGuide_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineFieldGuide-21">[21]</a></sup> Sunfish are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> and swim at depths to 600 m (2,000 ft). Contrary to the perception that sunfish spend much of their time basking at the surface, <i>M. mola</i> adults actually spend a large portion of their lives submerged at depths greater than 200 m (660 ft), occupying both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epipelagic" title="Epipelagic">epipelagic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopelagic" title="Mesopelagic">mesopelagic</a> zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsPreliminary_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsPreliminary-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunfish are most often found in water warmer than 10 °C (50 °F);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsPreliminary_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsPreliminary-28">[28]</a></sup> prolonged periods spent in water at temperatures of 12 °C (54 °F) or lower can lead to disorientation and eventual death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup> Surface basking behavior, in which a sunfish swims on its side, presenting its largest profile to the sun, may be a method of "thermally recharging" following dives into deeper, colder water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrackingSunfish_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-TrackingSunfish-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biogeography_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biogeography-29">[29]</a></sup> Sightings of the fish in colder waters outside of its usual habitat, such as those southwest of England, may be evidence of increasing marine temperatures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> although the proximity of England's southwestern coast to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> means that many of these sightings may also be the result of the fish being carried to Europe by the current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Sunfish have been found as far north as off the northern coast of Scotland, and have been caught from areas around the United Kingdom in smaller numbers. Especially large specimens have occasionally been found in the North Atlantic, with a fish caught in 1976 off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> being over 108 lb (49 kg) in weight.</p>
<p>Sunfish are usually found alone, but occasionally in pairs or in large groups while being cleaned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> They swim primarily in open waters, but are sometimes seen near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelp_bed" title="Kelp bed">kelp beds</a>, taking advantage of resident populations of smaller fish which remove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectoparasite" title="Ectoparasite">ectoparasites</a> from their skin. Because sunfish must consume a large volume of prey, their presence in a given area may be used as an indicator of nutrient-rich waters where endangered species may be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diet of the ocean sunfish consists primarily of various <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>. It also consumes <a href="/wiki/Salp" title="Salp">salps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, small fish, fish larvae, and <a href="/wiki/Zostera" title="Zostera">eel grass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> This range of food items indicates that the sunfish feeds at many levels, from the surface to deep water, and occasionally down to the seafloor in some areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> The diet is nutritionally poor, forcing the sunfish to consume a large amount of food to maintain its size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle">Lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ocean sunfish may live up to ten years in captivity, but their lifespan in a natural habitat has not yet been determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo-23">[23]</a></sup> Their growth rate is also undetermined. However, a young specimen at the <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium">Monterey Bay Aquarium</a> increased in weight from 26 to 399 kg (57 to 880 lb) and reached a height of nearly 1.8 m (5.9 ft) in 15 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sheer size and thick skin of an adult of the species deters many smaller predators, but younger fish are vulnerable to predation by <a href="/wiki/Bluefin_tuna" title="Bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahi_mahi" title="Mahi mahi">mahi mahi</a>. Adults are consumed by sea lions, orca, and sharks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> Sea lions appear to hunt sunfish for sport, tearing the fins off, tossing the body around, and then simply abandoning the still-living but helpless fish to die on the seafloor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mating practices of the ocean sunfish are poorly understood, but spawning areas have been suggested in the North Atlantic, South Atlantic, North Pacific, South Pacific, and Indian oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup> Females can produce as many as 300 million eggs at a time, more than any other known vertebrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishLifeHistory_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishLifeHistory-3">[3]</a></sup> Sunfish eggs are released into the water and <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">externally fertilized</a> by sperm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newly hatched sunfish larvae are only 2.5 mm (0.098 in) long and weigh a fraction of a gram. They grow to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fry_(biology)" title="Fry (biology)">fry</a>, and those which survive grow many millions of times their original size before reaching adult proportions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineFieldGuide_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineFieldGuide-21">[21]</a></sup> Sunfish fry, with large pectoral fins, a tail fin, and body spines uncharacteristic of adult sunfish, resemble miniature pufferfish, their close relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOGM_Mola_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOGM_Mola-33">[33]</a></sup> Young sunfish school for protection, but this behavior is abandoned as they grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SwimWithSunfish_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-SwimWithSunfish-34">[34]</a></sup> By adulthood, they have the potential to grow more than 60 million times their birth size, arguably the most extreme size growth of any vertebrate animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genome">Genome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, researchers from China National Genebank and <a href="/wiki/Agency_for_Science,_Technology_and_Research" title="Agency for Science, Technology and Research">A*STAR</a> Singapore, including Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Brenner" title="Sydney Brenner">Sydney Brenner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whole_genome_sequencing" title="Whole genome sequencing">sequenced the genome</a> of the ocean sunfish and discovered several genes which might explain its fast growth rate and large body size. As member of the order Tetraodontiformes, like Fugu the sunfish has quite a compact genome, at 730 Mb in size. Analysis from this data suggests that sunfish and pufferfishes separated approximately 68 million years ago, which corroborates the results of other recent studies based on smaller datasets. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_interaction">Human interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Human interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

A sunfish caught in 1910, with an estimated weight of 1600 kg (3500 lb)</div>

<p>Despite their size, ocean sunfish are docile, and pose no threat to human divers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup> Injuries from sunfish are rare, although a slight danger exists from large sunfish leaping out of the water onto boats; in one instance, a sunfish landed on a 4-year-old boy when the fish leaped onto the boy's family's boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Areas where they are commonly found are popular destinations for sport dives, and sunfish at some locations have reportedly become familiar with divers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-14">[14]</a></sup> The fish is more of a problem to boaters than to swimmers, as it can pose a hazard to watercraft due to its large size and weight. Collisions with sunfish are common in some parts of the world and can cause damage to the hull of a boat,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoatAlarm_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoatAlarm-38">[38]</a></sup> or to the propellers of larger ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AustMuseumAdult_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-AustMuseumAdult-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The flesh of the ocean sunfish is considered a delicacy in some regions, the largest markets being Taiwan and Japan. All parts of the sunfish are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_(food)" title="Fish (food)">used in cuisine</a>, from the fins to the internal organs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishResearch_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishResearch-18">[18]</a></sup> Some parts of the fish are used in some areas of traditional medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-14">[14]</a></sup> If the body does contain toxins, then the marketing and sale of ocean sunfish is forbidden in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> according to Regulation (EC) No 853/2004 of the European Parliament and of the Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BannedinEEC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BannedinEEC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SunfishDish1_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2222" data-file-width="3152" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/SunfishDish1_2.jpg/220px-SunfishDish1_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/SunfishDish1_2.jpg/330px-SunfishDish1_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/SunfishDish1_2.jpg/440px-SunfishDish1_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SunfishDish1_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A dish made from the ocean sunfish</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sunfish are accidentally but frequently caught in <a href="/wiki/Gillnetting#Drift_nets" title="Gillnetting">drift gillnet</a> fisheries, making up nearly 30% of the total catch of the swordfish fishery employing drift gillnets in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineFieldGuide_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineFieldGuide-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> rate is even higher for the Mediterranean swordfish industry, with 71% to 90% of the total catch being sunfish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishResearch_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishResearch-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SwimWithSunfish_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-SwimWithSunfish-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fishery bycatch and destruction of ocean sunfish are unregulated worldwide. In some areas, the fish are "finned" by fishermen who regard them as worthless bait thieves; this process, in which the fins are cut off, results in the eventual death of the fish, because it can no longer propel itself without its dorsal and anal fins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MolaFishery_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-MolaFishery-39">[39]</a></sup> The species is also threatened by floating litter such as plastic bags which resemble jellyfish, its main food. Bags can choke and suffocate a fish or fill its stomach to the extent that it starves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeo_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeo-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many areas of sunfish biology remain poorly understood, and various research efforts are underway, including aerial surveys of populations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsResearch_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsResearch-40">[40]</a></sup> satellite surveillance using pop-off satellite tags,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishResearch_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishResearch-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsResearch_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsResearch-40">[40]</a></sup> genetic analysis of tissue samples,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanSunfishResearch_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-OceanSunfishResearch-18">[18]</a></sup> and collection of amateur sighting data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> A decrease in sunfish populations may be caused by more frequent bycatch and the increasing popularity of sunfish in human diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LargePelagicsLifeHistory_15-9"><a href="#cite_note-LargePelagicsLifeHistory-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, a <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">video went viral</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> man profusely expressing his amazement when encountering a basking sunfish for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_captivity">In captivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_sunfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In captivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg/220px-Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg/330px-Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg/440px-Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mola_mola_ocean_sunfish_Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A tank at the Monterey Bay Aquarium provides a size comparison between an ocean sunfish and humans.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sunfish are not widely held in aquarium exhibits, due to the unique and demanding requirements of their care. Some Asian aquaria display them, particularly in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Aquarium_Kaiyukan" title="Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan">Kaiyukan Aquarium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> is one of few aquariums with <i>M. mola</i> on display, where it is reportedly as popular an attraction as the larger <a href="/wiki/Whale_shark" title="Whale shark">whale sharks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OsakaAquarium_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-OsakaAquarium-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Oceanarium" title="Lisbon Oceanarium">Lisbon Oceanarium</a> in Portugal has sunfish showcased in the main tank,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oceanario_Lisbon_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oceanario_Lisbon-45">[45]</a></sup> and in Spain, the <a href="/wiki/L%27Oceanogr%C3%A0fic" title="L'Oceanogràfic">Valencia Oceanogràfic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> has specimens of sunfish. The <a href="/wiki/Nords%C3%B8en_Oceanarium" title="Nordsøen Oceanarium">Nordsøen Oceanarium</a> in the northern town of <a href="/wiki/Hirtshals" title="Hirtshals">Hirtshals</a> in Denmark is also famous for its sunfish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the first ocean sunfish to be held in an aquarium in the United States is claimed to have arrived at the <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium">Monterey Bay Aquarium</a> in August 1986,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> other specimens have previously been held at other locations. <a href="/wiki/Marineland_of_the_Pacific" title="Marineland of the Pacific">Marineland of the Pacific</a>, closed since 1998 and located on the Palos Verdes Peninsula in Los Angeles County, California, held an ocean sunfish in its warm-water tank as early as 1957,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and in 1964 held a 650-pound (290 kg) specimen, claimed as the largest ever captured at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, another 1,000-pound (450 kg) specimen was brought alive to Marineland Studios Aquarium, near St. Augustine, Florida, in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>Because sunfish had not been kept in captivity on a large scale before, the staff at Monterey Bay was forced to innovate and create their own methods for capture, feeding, and parasite control. By 1998, these issues were overcome, and the aquarium was able to hold a specimen for more than a year, later releasing it after its weight increased by more than 14 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Mola mola</i> has since become a permanent feature of the Open Sea exhibit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineFieldGuide_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineFieldGuide-21">[21]</a></sup> Monterey Bay Aquarium's largest sunfish specimen was euthanized on February 14, 2008, after an extended period of poor health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major concern to curators is preventive measures taken to keep specimens in captivity from injuring themselves by rubbing against the walls of a tank, since ocean sunfish cannot easily maneuver their bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OsakaAquarium_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-OsakaAquarium-44">[44]</a></sup> In a smaller tank, hanging a vinyl curtain has been used as a stopgap measure to convert a cuboid tank to a rounded shape and prevent the fish from scraping against the sides. A more effective solution is simply to provide enough room for the sunfish to swim in wide circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-7"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup> The tank must also be sufficiently deep to accommodate the vertical height of the sunfish, which may reach 3.2 m (10 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZHeraldVisitor_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-NZHeraldVisitor-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Feeding captive sunfish in a tank with other faster-moving, more aggressive fish can also present a challenge. Eventually, the fish can be taught to respond to a floating target to be fed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_in_the_slow_lane_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Life_in_the_slow_lane-53">[53]</a></sup> and to take food from the end of a pole or from human hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnderwaterPioneer_24-8"><a href="#cite_note-UnderwaterPioneer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
