<p>A <b>custom car</b> is a passenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">vehicle</a> that has been substantially modified in either of the following two ways</p>
<ol>
<li>a custom car may be altered to <a href="/wiki/Engine_tuning" title="Engine tuning">improve its performance</a>, often by altering or replacing the <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">transmission</a></li>
<li>a custom car may be a personal "styling" statement, making the car look unlike any car as delivered from the factory.</li>
</ol>
<p>Although the two are related, custom cars are distinct from <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rods</a>. The extent of this difference has been the subject of debate among customizers and rodders for decades. Additionally, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_rod_(car)" title="Street rod (car)">street rod</a> can be considered a custom.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A development of hot rodding, the change in name corresponded to the change in the <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> of the cars being modified. The first hot rods were pre-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> cars, with <a href="/wiki/Running_board" title="Running board">running boards</a> and simple fenders over the wheels. Early model cars (1929 to 1934) were modified by removing the running boards and either removing the fenders entirely or replacing them with very light <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cycle_fender&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cycle fender (page does not exist)">cycle fenders</a>. Later models usually had fender skirts installed.</p>
<p>Coachcraft Ltd. in Hollywood, California, built several modified cars that are generally regarded as the first examples of "custom cars", in contrast to a custom-bodied coachbuilt car that was commonly purchased new by wealthy people in the 1930s. The firm was started by ex-employees of Howard "Dutch" Darrin, who had designed and built the custom-bodied luxury cars that came before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coachcraft_history_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coachcraft_history-1">[1]</a></sup> Strother MacMinn called the "Yankee Doodle Roadster" by Coachcraft the “first American custom sports car." Many pictures of this car can be seen by looking at the web pages in these references:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YankeeDoodle1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-YankeeDoodle1-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YankeeDoodle2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-YankeeDoodle2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many cars were "hopped up" with engine modifications such as adding additional carburetors, high compression heads and dual exhausts. <a href="/wiki/Engine_swap" title="Engine swap">Engine swaps</a> were done, the object of which was to put the most powerful engine in the lightest possible frame and body combination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Custom_history_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Custom_history-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The suspension was usually altered. Initially this involved lowering the rear end as much as possible with the use of lowering blocks on the rear springs. Later cars were given a rake job either adding a dropped front axle or heating front coil springs to make the front end of the car much lower than the rear.</p>
<p>Much later some hot rods and custom cars swapped the old solid rear axle for an independent rear axle, often from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaguar_(car)" title="Jaguar (car)">Jaguar</a>. Sometimes the grille of one make of car replaced by another; the 1937 <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a> grille was often used on a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a>. In the 1950s and 1960s, the grille swap of choice was the 1953 <a href="/wiki/DeSoto_(automobile)" title="DeSoto (automobile)">De Soto</a>.</p>
<p>The original hot rods were plainly painted like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Model_A_(1927-1931)" title="Ford Model A (1927-1931)">Model A Fords</a> from which they had been built up, and only slowly begun to take on colors, and eventually fancy orange-yellow flamed hoods or "candy-like" deep acrylic finishes in the various colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Custom_history_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Custom_history-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the change in automobile design to encase the wheels in fenders and to extend the hood to the full width of the car, the former practices were no longer possible. In addition, there was tremendous automotive advertising and subsequent public interest in the new models in the 1950s. Hence custom cars came into existence, swapping <a href="/wiki/Headlamp" title="Headlamp">headlamp</a> rings, grilles, bumpers, <a href="/wiki/Chrome_plating" title="Chrome plating">chrome</a> side strips, and tail lights, as well as frenching and tunnelling head- and taillights. The bodies of the cars were changed by cutting through the sheet metal, removing bits to make the car lower, welding it back together, and adding a lot of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> to make the resulting form smooth (hence the term <a href="/wiki/Lead_sled" title="Lead sled">lead sled</a>; lead has since been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Bondo_(putty)" title="Bondo (putty)">Bondo</a>). By this means, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chopped_and_channeled#Chopping" title="Chopped and channeled">chopping</a> made the roof lower;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chopped_and_channeled#Sectioning" title="Chopped and channeled">sectioning</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> made the body thinner from top to bottom. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chopped_and_channeled#Channeling" title="Chopped and channeled">Channeling</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> was cutting notches in the floorpan where the body touches the frame to lower the whole body. Fins were often added from other cars, or made up from sheet steel. In the custom car culture, someone who merely changed the appearance without also substantially improving the performance was looked down upon.</p>
<p>More recently, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juxtapoz_Magazine" title="Juxtapoz Magazine">Juxtapoz Magazine</a></i>, founded by the artist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Williams_(artist)" title="Robert Williams (artist)">Robert Williams</a>, has covered <a href="/wiki/Kustom_Kulture" title="Kustom Kulture">Kustom Kulture</a> art.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customization_style">Customization style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Customization style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Custom cars are distinct from cars in stock condition. Builders may adopt the visual and performance characteristics of some relevant modification styles, and combine these as desired. There are now several different custom themes, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rat_rod" title="Rat rod">Rat rod</a>: imitates (or exaggerates) the "unfinished" and amateur-built appearance of hot rods of the 1930s, 1940s, and 1950s</li>
<li>Restomod - restored and modernized. Safety and convenience upgrades, such as disc brakes, AC, etc., but can include fuel injection and overdrive upgrades, etc. Externally might resemble a stock car with period correct mods rather than customs.</li>
<li>Street machines: Typically American cars with large-displacement engines modified for speed and often appearance.</li>
<li>Street rod - consist largely of period specific vehicles and components, or emmulate visual characteristics of cars through the'40s vintage. There is a great deal of overlap here with <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rods</a>. See street rod definition below.</li>
<li>Muscle Cars are the Cars Before the year 2000 that were Customized and Modified for the Best Performance</li>
<li>Tweeners - early 1960s cars between the styles of 1950s and the muscle cars.</li>
<li>Modern: The style of contemporary cars. Most often contemporary components and paint finishes of modern cars are used.</li>
<li>NOTE: Types overlap and blend often impossible to classify.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paint">Paint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Paint was an important concern. Once bodywork was done, the cars were painted unusual colors. Transparent but wildly colored candy-apple paint, applied atop a metallic undercoat, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalflake" title="Metalflake">metalflake</a> paint, with aluminum glitter within candy-apple paint, appeared in the 1960s. These took many coats to produce a brilliant effect – which in hot climates had a tendency to flake off. This process and style of paint job was invented by Joe Bailon, a customizer from Northern California.</p>
<p>Customizers also continued the habit of adding decorative paint after the main coat was finished, of flames extending rearward from the front wheels, scallops, and hand-painted pinstripes of a contrasting color. The base color, most often a single coat, would be expected to be of a simpler paint. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flame_job_(painting_technique)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flame job (painting technique) (page does not exist)">Flame jobs</a> later spread to the hood, encompassing the entire front end, and have progressed from traditional reds and yellows to blues and greens and body-color "ghost" flames. One particular style of flames, called "crab claw flames", which is still prevalent today, is attributed to Dean Jeffries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Painting has become such a part of the custom car scene that now in many custom car competitions, awards for custom paint are as highly sought after as awards for the cars themselves.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_swaps">Engine swaps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Engine swaps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Engine swaps have always been commonplace. Once, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Flathead_engine" title="Ford Flathead engine">flathead</a>, or "flatty", was the preference, supplanted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_FirePower_engine" title="Chrysler FirePower engine">early hemi</a> in the 1950s and 1960s. By the 1970s, the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_small-block_engine#Small_block_Chevrolet_V8_.281955.E2.80.931998.29" title="Chevrolet small-block engine">small-block Chevy</a> was the most common option, and since the 1980s, the 350 cu in (5.7 l) Chevy has been almost ubiquitous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> More recently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the 325 cu in (5.3 l) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GM_small-block_engine#Generation_III_.281997.E2.80.932007.29" title="GM small-block engine">Chevrolet LS</a> has begun replacing the 350.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Flatheads and early hemis have not entirely disappeared, but ready availability, ease of maintenance, and low cost of parts make the Chevrolet V8, in particular the first and third generation small block, the most frequent engine of choice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Once customizing post-war cars caught on, some of the practices were extended to pre-war cars, which would have been called fendered rods, with more body work done on them. An alternate rule for disambiguation developed: hot rods had the engine behind the front suspension, while customs had the engine over the front suspension. The clearest example of this is <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Fords</a> prior to 1949 had <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a>'s old transverse front suspension, while 1949 models had a more modern suspension with the engine moved forward. However, an American museum has what could be the first true custom, built by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cletus_Clobes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cletus Clobes (page does not exist)">Cletus Clobes</a> in 1932, among its exhibits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the coming of the <a href="/wiki/Muscle_car" title="Muscle car">muscle car</a>, and further to the high-performance luxury car, customization declined. One place where it persisted was the U.S. Southwest, where <a href="/wiki/Lowrider" title="Lowrider">lowriders</a> were built similar in concept to the earlier customs, but of post-1950s cars.</p>
<p>As the supply of usable antique steel bodies has dried up, companies such <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Westcott_Auto_Restyling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Westcott Auto Restyling (page does not exist)">Westcott's</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harwood_Street_Rods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harwood Street Rods (page does not exist)">Harwood</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gibbon_Fiberglass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gibbon Fiberglass (page does not exist)">Gibbon Fiberglass</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_2.2F78.2C_p.43_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_2.2F78.2C_p.43-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Speedway_Motors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Speedway Motors (page does not exist)">Speedway Motors</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_2.2F78.2C_p.43_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_2.2F78.2C_p.43-12">[12]</a></sup> have begun to fabricate new <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> copies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> while <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Manufacturing_and_Supply&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Classic Manufacturing and Supply (page does not exist)">Classic Manufacturing and Supply</a>, for one example, has been making a variety of new steel bodies since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> California's "junker" (or "crusher") law, which pays a nominal sum to take "gross polluters" off the road, has been criticized by enthusiasts (and by SEMA) for accelerating this trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in the 1950s, it became popular among customizers to display their vehicles at drive-in restaurants. Among the largest and longest lasting was <a href="/wiki/Johnie%27s_Broiler" title="Johnie's Broiler">Johnie's Broiler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downey,_California" title="Downey, California">Downey, California</a>. The practice continues today, especially in Southern California.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highest award for customizers is the AMBR (<a href="/wiki/America%27s_Most_Beautiful_Roadster" title="America's Most Beautiful Roadster">America's Most Beautiful Roadster</a>) trophy, presented annually at the Grand National Roadster Show since 1948 (also known within the customizer community as the Oakland Roadster Show until it was moved to Southern California in 2003). This competition has produced famous, and radical, customs.</p>
<p>Another is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ridler_Award" title="Ridler Award">Ridler Award</a>, presented at the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Autorama" title="Detroit Autorama">Detroit Autorama</a> since 1964 in honor of show promoter Don Ridler. With one of the most unusual of car show entry requirements, winners of the prestigious Ridler Award are selected as the most outstanding from among cars being shown for the first time. This prompts builders of many high-end roadsters to first enter the Autorama first and then the Grand National show in order to have the chance to win top honors at both shows. Few cars and owners can claim this achievement.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_customs">Notable customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some customs gained attention for winning the AMBR trophy, or for their outlandish styling. Notable among these is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silhouette_(custom_car)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silhouette (custom car) (page does not exist)"><i>Silhouette</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Ed_Roth" title="Ed Roth">Ed Roth</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mysterion_(custom_car)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mysterion (custom car) (page does not exist)"><i>Mysterion</i></a>. Some of these more unusual projects turned into <a href="/wiki/Hot_Wheels" title="Hot Wheels">Hot Wheels</a> cars, among them <a href="/wiki/The_Red_Baron_(custom_car)" title="The Red Baron (custom car)"><i>The Red Baron</i></a>.</p>
<p>Other custom cars became notable for appearances in film (such as <i>Ala Kart</i> {1958},<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>The California Kid</i> three-window {1973},<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> or the yellow deuce from "<a href="/wiki/American_Graffiti" title="American Graffiti">American Graffiti</a>" {1973}) or television (such as <i>The <a href="/wiki/Monkeemobile" title="Monkeemobile">Monkeemobile</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/The_Munsters" title="The Munsters">"Munsters"</a> hearse, or, more recently, Boyd's full-custom <a href="/wiki/Home_Improvement_(TV_series)" title="Home Improvement (TV series)"><i>Tool Time</i></a> '34, or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Thelan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Don Thelan (page does not exist)">Don Thelan</a>'s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> '33 three-window, <i>Eliminator</i>, built for the <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Top" title="ZZ Top">ZZ Top</a> video<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>). Specialist vehicles, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontiac_Trans-Am" title="Pontiac Trans-Am">T/A</a>, <a href="/wiki/KITT" title="KITT">KITT</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Knight_Rider_(1982_TV_series)" title="Knight Rider (1982 TV series)"><i>Knight Rider</i></a>, are not usually considered customs, but movie or TV cars, because they retain a mostly stock exterior.</p>
<p>Still others exemplified a trend. One of these is the '51 Merc built by the Barris brothers for Bob Hirohata in 1953, known forever after as the <a href="/wiki/Hirohata_Merc" title="Hirohata Merc">Hirohata Merc</a>. Even without an appearance in film ("Runnin' Wild"), it is iconic of 1950s customs, and of how to do a Merc right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The same year, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_Emory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neil Emory (page does not exist)">Neil Emory</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clayton_Jensen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clayton Jensen (page does not exist)">Clayton Jensen</a> built <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Polynesian_(custom_car)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polynesian (custom car) (page does not exist)"><i>Polynesian</i></a> for Jack Stewart, starting with a '50 <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_98#1949" title="Oldsmobile 98">Olds sedan</a>. <i>Polynesian</i> made the cover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Rod_Magazine" title="Hot Rod Magazine"><i>Hot Rod</i></a> in August, and saw 54 pages of construction details in <i><a href="/wiki/Motor_Trend" title="Motor Trend">Motor Trend</a> Custom Car Annual</i> in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain linguistic conventions are followed among rodders and customizers:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Model_year" title="Model year">model year</a> is rarely given in full,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> except when it might be confused, so a 1934 model is a '34, while a 2005 might be an '05 or not.
<ul>
<li>A '32 is usually a Deuce and most often a roadster, unless <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a> is specified, and almost always a Ford.</li>
<li>A 1955, 1956, or 1957 is usually a Chevrolet.</li>
<li>A 1955, 1956, or 1957 Chevrolet is often called a <a href="/wiki/Tri-Five" title="Tri-Five">Tri-Five</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>A 3- or 5-window is usually a Ford, unless specified.</li>
<li>A flatty is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Flathead_engine" title="Ford Flathead engine">flathead V8</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> (always Ford, unless specified); a late (or late model) flatty is probably a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(automobile)" title="Mercury (automobile)">Merc</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Hemi_engine#Hemi_design_reintroduced" title="Chrysler Hemi engine">hemi</a> ("<i>hem</i> ee") is always a 426, unless displacement (331, 354, or 392) is specified;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> a 426 is a hemi, unless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_RB_engine#426" title="Chrysler RB engine">Wedge</a> is specified. See baby hemi.</li>
<li>A 392 is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_FirePower_engine#392" title="Chrysler FirePower engine">early hemi</a>.
<ul>
<li>A 331 or 354 is known to be an (early) hemi, but rarely referred to as such</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>A 270 "Jimmy" was a 270 cubic inch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Straight-6_engine#270" title="Chevrolet Straight-6 engine">GMC truck engine</a> often used to replace a smaller displacement Chevrolet six cylinder.</li>
<li>Units are routinely dropped, unless they are unclear, so a 426 cubic inch (in³) displacement engine is simply referred to as a 426, a 5-liter (litre) displacement engine is a 5.0 ("five point oh"), and a 600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_feet_per_minute" title="Cubic feet per minute">cubic feet per minute</a> (cfm) carburetor is a 600. Engine displacement can be described in cubic inches or liters (for example, a 5.7-liter engine is also known as a 350 {"three fifty"}); this frequently depends on which units the user is most comfortable or familiar with.</li>
</ul>
<p>The "cutoff year" as originally promoted by the <i>National Street Rod Association</i> (<a href="/wiki/National_Street_Rod_Association" title="National Street Rod Association">NSRA</a>) is 1949. Many custom car shows will only accept 1948 and earlier models as entries, and many custom car organizations will not admit later model cars or trucks (also with some imports - this has been a gray area of what's acceptable e.g. an aircooled VW Beetle, a Big Three product manufactured overseas e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Capri" title="Ford Capri">Ford Capri</a> built in the UK or a <a href="/wiki/Holden" title="Holden">General Motors - Holden's</a> product, not to mention <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captive_imports" title="Captive imports">captives</a>), and/or a vintage import automobile with an American driveline transplant) but this practice is subject to change. Modern day custom car shows which allow the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Muscle_car" title="Muscle car">musclecars</a> have used the 1972 model year as the cutoff (usually known by the car show organizers as a pre-72) since it is considered the end of the musclecar era prior to the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> and stricter pollution controls to meet emission standards. This means that a 1973 or newer automobile (especially those manufactured during the <a href="/wiki/Malaise_era" title="Malaise era">malaise era</a> including musclecar survivors e.g. Camaro/Firebird) is prohibited from participating with some exceptions. The NSRA has announced that starting in 2011 it will switch to a shifting year method where any owner with a car 30 years or older will be allowed membership. So in 2011 the owner of a 1981 model year vehicle will qualify, then in 2012 the owner of a 1982 model year vehicle will quality, and so on. Additionally, the Goodguys car show organization has moved the year limit for its "rod" shows from 1949 to 1954 in recent years - Goodguys organizers also have taken into consideration that the younger generation who could not afford a vintage hot rod or pre-1972 musclecar now include their Super Sunday events where it encompasses all model years including American-powered transplants e.g. a Jaguar with a Chevrolet V8 or a Ford-powered Mazda.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_terms">Common terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Custom_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Common terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some other common terms:</p>
<ul>
<li>3 deuces — arrangement of three 2-<a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">barrel</a> (twin-choke) <a href="/wiki/Carburetor" title="Carburetor">carburetors</a>; distinct from <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_LA_engine" title="Chrysler LA engine">Six Pak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac" title="Pontiac">Pontiac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile" title="Oldsmobile">Olds</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Tri-Power<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> (also 3x2 arrangements)</li>

<li>3 on the tree — three-speed manual transmission operated by a steering column mounted shifter.</li>
<li>4 on the floor — four-speed manual transmission operated by a floor mounted shifter.</li>
<li>5-window — 2-door coupé; so named for having rear window plus one door window and one quarter window on each side<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>

Not much traffic either, especially at night.</li>
<li>Ardun - Ford flathead V8 hemi heads designed by Zora Arkus Duntov (later known as THE Corvette guy)</li>
<li>Baby moons - chrome small smooth convex hubcaps covering the wheel lug area. Full moons covered the entire wheel.</li>

<li>Barn fresh, barn find— a newly discovered vehicle typically found in storage (i.e. a barn, etc.), either long forgotten or abandoned, still in its original condition from when it was first stored<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li>Bugcatcher intake — large scoop intake protruding through hood opening, or on cars with no hood.</li>
<li>Bullnosing — Replacing the hood ornament with a "bullnose" chrome strip or filling the mounting hole with lead.</li>

<li>Hiboy (or highboy) — fenderless, but not lowered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Distinct from <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod#Language" title="Hot rod">gasser</a>.</li>

</li>

<li>Street rod - A modified car licensed for use on streets and highways. --Traditionally designated as '48 and older by enthusiasts, SEMA, and states that issue specific street rod license plates. '49 and newer receive custom car plates. Antique, street rods, custom rod, and normal plates carry different rules under SEMA rules for state applied licenses.</li>
<li>Studillac ("<i>stewed</i> i lack") — <a href="/wiki/Studebaker" title="Studebaker">Studebaker</a> with transplanted <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_V8_engine#OHV" title="Cadillac V8 engine">Cadillac V8 engine</a></li>
<li>Suicided — changed from front- to rear-hinged ("<a href="/wiki/Suicide_door" title="Suicide door">suicide door</a>")</li>
<li>Suicide front end — a front axle configuration where it is mounted forward of the front cross member or the end of the frame rails.</li>
<li>Suicide knob - see spinner knob</li>
<li>Taildragger - A vehicle lowered more in the rear than front. Often seen on leadsleds. Often a regionalized trend.</li>
<li>Tin Indian — <a href="/wiki/Pontiac" title="Pontiac">Pontiac</a> (for the grille badge)</li>
<li>Toploader — Ford 4-speed manual transmission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>Touring — see Phaeton</li>
<li>Track T — <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Model T</a> <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a> built in the style of a dirt track race car<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Trailer queen — pejorative term used in some circles for pure show cars which are never driven</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tri-Five" title="Tri-Five">Tri-Five</a> — 1955, 1956, or 1957 Chevrolet</li>
<li>Tuck-and-roll — upholstery technique creating a "pleated" look<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>Tunneled — deeply sunken into fender<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_12.2F98.2C_p.212_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-.252526quot.25253BStreet_Rodder.2C_12.2F98.2C_p.212-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>V-butted (or vee-butted) — with windshield center post deleted, original panes meeting in the middle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> (distinct from fitting a one-piece windshield), or to make such a change ("the windshield was vee-butted", "he vee-butted the windshield")</li>
<li>Vicky — Victoria body style<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li>Weedburners - downward pointing zoomies</li>
<li>Wide whites — wide-stripe <a href="/wiki/Whitewall_tire" title="Whitewall tire">whitewall tires</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> typical of the 1950s, as opposed to modern ones</li>
<li>Woodie — Typically a station wagon manufactured by most of the major manufacturers where much of the body behind the firewall was replaced with wood construction.</li>
<li>Zoomie pipes (or zoomies) — short exhaust pipes with no mufflers, used for racing, or just for show (not street legal)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Usually one pipe per cylinder. Pointing upwards called zoomies. Downwards are weed burners.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some terms have an additional, different meaning among <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rodders</a> than among customizers: NOS, for instance, is a reference to <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, rather than new old stock.</p>
