<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mudflat</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Mudflats</b> or <b>mud flats</b>, also known as <b>tidal flats</b>, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_wetland" title="Coastal wetland">coastal wetlands</a> that form when mud is deposited by tides or rivers. They are found in sheltered areas such as <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bayou" title="Bayou">bayous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>. Mudflats may be viewed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic" title="Geologic">geologically</a> as exposed layers of <a href="/wiki/Bay_mud" title="Bay mud">bay mud</a>, resulting from deposition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuarine" title="Estuarine">estuarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a> and marine animal <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. Most of the sediment within a mudflat is within the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a>, and thus the flat is submerged and exposed approximately twice daily.</p>
<p>In the past tidal flats were considered unhealthy, economically unimportant areas and were often dredged and developed into agricultural land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Several especially shallow mudflat areas, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wadden_Sea" title="Wadden Sea">Wadden Sea</a>, are now popular among those practising the sport of <a href="/wiki/Mudflat_hiking" title="Mudflat hiking">mudflat hiking</a>.</p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in places, mudflats are exposed not by tidal action, but by wind-action driving water away from the shallows into the sea. These wind-affected mudflats are called <i><a href="/wiki/Windwatt" title="Windwatt">windwatts</a></i> in German.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudflat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg/220px-Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg/330px-Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg/440px-Stewart_Island_Oban_Mudflats.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Mudflats near <a href="/wiki/Oban,_New_Zealand" title="Oban, New Zealand">Oban</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stewart_Island/Rakiura" title="Stewart Island/Rakiura">Stewart Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a></div>

<p>Tidal flats, along with intertidal <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forests, are important <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They usually support a large population of wildlife, and are a key habitat that allows tens of millions of migratory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shorebirds" title="Shorebirds">shorebirds</a> to migrate from breeding sites in the northern hemisphere to non-breeding areas in the southern hemisphere. They are often of vital importance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migratory_bird" title="Migratory bird">migratory birds</a>, as well as certain species of <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> mudflats have been classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> priority habitat.</p>
<p>The maintenance of mudflats is important in preventing coastal erosion. However, mudflats worldwide are under threat from predicted <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rises</a>, land claims for development, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dredge" title="Dredge">dredging</a> due to shipping purposes, and <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">chemical pollution</a>. In some parts of the world, such as East and South-East Asia, mudflats have been reclaimed for <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, and industrial development. For example, around the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> region of East Asia, more than 65% of mudflats have been destroyed over the past 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudflat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mudflat sediment deposits are focused into the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a> which is composed of a barren zone, marsh and salt pan. Within these areas are various ratios of sand and mud that make up the sedimentary layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reineck_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reineck-7">[7]</a></sup> The associated growth of coastal sediment deposits can be attributed to rates of subsidence along with rates of deposition (example: silt transported via river) and changes in sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reineck_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reineck-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Barren zones extend from the lowest portion of the intertidal zone to the marsh areas. Beginning in close proximity to the tidal bars, sand dominated layers are prominent and become increasingly muddy throughout the tidal channels. Common bedding types include laminated sand, ripple bedding, and bay mud. <a href="/wiki/Bioturbation" title="Bioturbation">Bioturbation</a> also has a strong presence in barren zones.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshes" title="Marshes">Marshes</a> contain an abundance of <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plants</a> while the sediment layers consist of thin sand and mud layers. <a href="/wiki/Mudcrack" title="Mudcrack">Mudcracks</a> are a common as well as wavy bedding planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reineck_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reineck-7">[7]</a></sup> Marshes are also the origins of coal/peat layers because of the abundant decaying plant life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reineck_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reineck-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salt pans can be distinguished in that they contain thinly laminated layers of clayey silt. The main source of the silt comes from rivers. Dried up mud along with wind erosion forms silt dunes. When flooding, rain or tides come in, the dried sediment is then re-distributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reineck_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Reineck-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
