<p><b>Objectivity</b> is a central <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> concept, related to <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>, which has been variously defined by sources. Generally, objectivity means the state or quality of being true even outside of a <a href="/wiki/Subject_(philosophy)" title="Subject (philosophy)">subject</a>'s individual biases, interpretations, feelings, and imaginings. A proposition is generally considered objectively true (to have <b>objective truth</b>) when its truth conditions are met without biases caused by feelings, ideas, opinions, etc., of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentient" title="Sentient">sentient</a> subject. A second, broader meaning of the term refers to the ability in any context to judge fairly, without partiality or external influence. This second meaning of <i>objectivity</i> is sometimes used <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymously</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Neutrality_(philosophy)" title="Neutrality (philosophy)">neutrality</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectivism">Objectivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Objectivity_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>"Objectivism" is a term that describes a branch of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> that originated in the early nineteenth century. <a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Gottlob Frege</a> was the first to apply it, when he expounded an <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> theory contrary to that of <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>. Kant's <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalism</a> attempted to reconcile the failures he perceived in <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">philosophical realism</a>.</p>
<p>Stronger versions of this claim hold that there is only one correct description of this reality. If it is true that reality is mind-independent, then reality might include objects that are unknown to consciousness and thus might include objects not the subject of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensionality" title="Intensionality">intensionality</a>. Objectivity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/References" title="References">referring</a> requires a definition of <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>. According to metaphysical objectivists, an object may truthfully be said to have this or that attribute, as in the statement "This object exists," whereas the statement "This object is true" or "false" is meaningless. For them, only propositions have <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth values</a>. The terms "objectivity" and "objectivism" are not synonymous, with objectivism being an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological" title="Ontological">ontological</a> theory that incorporates a <a href="/wiki/Ontological_commitment" title="Ontological commitment">commitment</a> to the objectivity of objects.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Platonic_idealism" title="Platonic idealism">Plato's idealism</a> was a form of metaphysical objectivism, holding that the Ideas exist objectively and independently. <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">Berkeley's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empiricist" title="Empiricist">empiricist</a> <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a>, on the other hand, could be called a <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">subjectivism</a>: he held that things only exist to the extent that they are perceived. Both theories claim methods of objectivity. Plato's definition of objectivity can be found in his <a href="/wiki/Platonic_epistemology" title="Platonic epistemology">epistemology</a>, which takes as a model <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and his metaphysics, where knowledge of the ontological status of objects and ideas is resistant to change.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> considered knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> a condition of philosophical knowledge, both being concerned with <a href="/wiki/Universal_(metaphysics)" title="Universal (metaphysics)">universal</a> truths. Plato's opposition between objective knowledge and <i><a href="/wiki/Doxa" title="Doxa">doxa</a></i> (opinions) became the basis for later philosophies intent on resolving the problem of reality, knowledge, and human existence. Personal opinions belong to the changing sphere of the sensible, opposed to a fixed and eternal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incorporeal" title="Incorporeal">incorporeal</a> realm that is mutually intelligible.</p>
<p>Where Plato distinguishes between what and how we know things (epistemology), and their ontological status as things (metaphysics), <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">subjectivism</a> such as Berkeley's and a mind dependence of knowledge and reality fails to distinguish between what one knows and what is to be known, or at least explains the distinction superficially. In Platonic terms, a criticism of <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">subjectivism</a> is that it is difficult to distinguish between knowledge, <i>doxa</i>, and subjective knowledge (true belief), distinctions that Plato makes.</p>
<p>The importance of perception in evaluating and understanding objective reality is debated. Realists argue that perception is key in directly observing objective reality, while <a href="/wiki/Instrumentalism" title="Instrumentalism">instrumentalists</a> hold that perception is not necessarily useful in directly observing objective reality, but is useful in interpreting and predicting reality. The concepts that encompasses these ideas are important in the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectivity_in_ethics">Objectivity in ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Objectivity_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Objectivity in ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_subjectivism">Ethical subjectivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Objectivity_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ethical subjectivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-cognitivism" title="Non-cognitivism">Non-cognitivism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">Subjectivism</a></div>
<p>The term, "ethical subjectivism," covers two distinct theories in ethics. According to cognitive versions of ethical subjectivism, the truth of moral statements depends upon people's values, attitudes, feelings, or beliefs. Some forms of cognitivist ethical subjectivism can be counted as forms of realism, others are forms of anti-realism. David Hume is a foundational figure for cognitive ethical subjectivism. On a standard interpretation of his theory, a trait of character counts as a moral virtue when it evokes a sentiment of approbation in a sympathetic, informed, and rational human observer. Similarly, Roderick Firth's <a href="/wiki/Ideal_observer_theory" title="Ideal observer theory">ideal observer theory</a> held that right acts are those that an impartial, rational observer would approve of. William James, another ethical subjectivist, held that an end is good (to or for a person) just in the case it is desired by that person (see also <a href="/wiki/Ethical_egoism" title="Ethical egoism">ethical egoism</a>). According to non-cognitive versions of ethical subjectivism, such as emotivism, prescriptivism, and expressivism, ethical statements cannot be true or false, at all: rather, they are expressions of personal feelings or commands. For example, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Ayer" title="Alfred Ayer">A. J. Ayer's</a> emotivism, the statement, "Murder is wrong" is equivalent in meaning to the emotive, "Murder, Boo!"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_objectivism">Ethical objectivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Objectivity_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethical objectivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moral_realism" title="Moral realism">Moral realism</a></div>
<p>According to the ethical objectivist, the <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a> or falsehood of typical moral judgments does not depend upon the beliefs or feelings of any person or group of persons. This view holds that moral propositions are analogous to propositions about chemistry, biology, or history, in so much as they are true despite what anyone believes, hopes, wishes, or feelings. When they fail to describe this mind-independent moral reality, they are false—no matter what anyone believes, hopes, wishes, or feels.</p>
<p>There are many versions of ethical objectivism, including various religious views of morality, Platonistic intuitionism, <a href="/wiki/Kantianism" title="Kantianism">Kantianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a>, and certain forms of <a href="/wiki/Ethical_egoism" title="Ethical egoism">ethical egoism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contractualism" title="Contractualism">contractualism</a>. Note that Platonists define ethical objectivism in an even more narrow way, so that it requires the existence of intrinsic value. Consequently, they reject the idea that contractualists or egoists could be ethical objectivists. Objectivism, in turn, places primacy on the origin of the frame of reference—and, as such, considers any arbitrary frame of reference ultimately a form of ethical subjectivism by a transitive property, even when the frame incidentally coincides with reality and can be used for measurements.</p>
