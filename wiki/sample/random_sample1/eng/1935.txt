<p>The <b>Ganges-Brahmaputra Delta</b> in Bangladesh (also known as the <b>Brahmaputra Delta</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMerriam-Webster1997412_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMerriam-Webster1997412-1">[1]</a></sup> the <i>Sunderbans Delta</i>, or the <b>Bengal Delta</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BengalDelta_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BengalDelta-2">[2]</a></sup>) and in <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river delta</a> in the South Asia region of <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and the state of West Bengal. It is the world's largest delta, and empties into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>. It is also one of the most fertile regions in the world, thus earning the nickname <b>The Green Delta</b>. The delta stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Hooghly_River" title="Hooghly River">Hooghly River</a> on the west to the <a href="/wiki/Meghna_River" title="Meghna River">Meghna River</a> on the east. It is approximately 354 km (220 mi) across at the Bay of Bengal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMerriam-Webster1997412_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMerriam-Webster1997412-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a> (formerly Calcutta) and <a href="/wiki/Haldia" title="Haldia">Haldia</a> in India and <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Mongla" title="Port of Mongla">Mongla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chittagong" title="Chittagong">Chittagong</a> in Bangladesh are the principal seaports of the delta.</p>
<p>A number of large rivers flow through the Brahmaputra Delta, including the <a href="/wiki/Padma_River" title="Padma River">Padma</a> (main <a href="/wiki/Distributary" title="Distributary">distributary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Jamuna_River_(Bangladesh)" title="Jamuna River (Bangladesh)">Jamuna</a> (main distributary of the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_River" title="Brahmaputra River">Brahmaputra</a>), which merge and then join the Meghna before entering the sea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Ganges Delta has the shape of a triangle, and is considered to be an "arcuate" delta (arc-shaped). It covers more than 105,000 km<sup>2</sup> (41,000 sq mi), and although the delta lies mostly in Bangladesh and India, rivers from <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>, India, and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> drain into it from the north. Approximately two-thirds of the delta is in Bangladesh. Most of the delta is composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> soils made up by small sediment particles that finally settle down as river currents slow down in the estuary. Rivers carry these fine particles with them, even from their sources at glaciers as <a href="/wiki/Fluvio-glacial" title="Fluvio-glacial">fluvio-glacial</a>. Red and red-yellow <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterite</a> soils are found as one heads farther east. The soil has large amounts of minerals and nutrients, which is good for agriculture.</p>
<p>It is composed of a labyrinth of <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">channels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, and flood plain sediments (chars). The <a href="/wiki/Gorai-Madhumati_River" title="Gorai-Madhumati River">Gorai-Madhumati River</a>, one of the distributaries of the Ganges, divides the Ganges Delta into two parts: the geologically young, active, eastern delta, and the older, less active, western delta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BengalDelta_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BengalDelta-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 125 and 143 million people live on the delta,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> despite risks from <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>, heavy runoff from the melting snows of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, and tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>. A large part of the nation of Bangladesh lies in the Ganges Delta, and many of the country's people depend on the delta for survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200339_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200339-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is believed that upwards of 300 million people are supported by the Ganges Delta, and approximately 400 million people live in the Ganges River Basin, making it the most populous river basin in the world. Most of the Ganges Delta has a population density of more than 200 people per km<sup>2</sup> (520 people per square mile), making it one of the most densely populated regions in the world.</p>

<p>Three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregion" title="Terrestrial ecoregion">terrestrial ecoregions</a> cover the delta. The <a href="/wiki/Lower_Gangetic_plains_moist_deciduous_forests" title="Lower Gangetic plains moist deciduous forests">Lower Gangetic plains moist deciduous forests</a> ecoregion covers most of the delta region, although the forests have mostly been cleared for agriculture and only small enclaves remain. Thick stands of tall grass, known as canebrakes, grow in wetter areas. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sundarbans_freshwater_swamp_forests" title="Sundarbans freshwater swamp forests">Sundarbans freshwater swamp forests</a> ecoregion lies closer to the Bay of Bengal; this ecoregion is flooded with slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brackish" title="Brackish">brackish</a> water during the dry season, and fresh water during the monsoon season. These forests, too, have been almost completely converted to intensive agriculture, with only 130 square kilometres (50 sq mi) of the ecoregion's 14,600 square kilometres (5,600 sq mi) protected. Where the delta meets the Bay of Bengal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sundarbans_mangroves" title="Sundarbans mangroves">Sundarbans mangroves</a> form the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> ecoregion, covering an area of 20,400 square kilometres (7,900 sq mi) in a chain of 54 islands. They derive their name from the predominant mangrove species, <i><a href="/wiki/Heritiera" title="Heritiera">Heritiera</a> fomes</i>, which are known locally as <i>sundri</i> or <i>sundari</i>.</p>
<p>Animals in the delta include the Indian python (<i><a href="/wiki/Python_molurus" title="Python molurus">Python molurus</a></i>), clouded leopard (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neofelis_nebulosa" title="Neofelis nebulosa">Neofelis nebulosa</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Indian_elephant" title="Indian elephant">Indian elephant</a> (<i>Elephas maximus indicus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, which live in the Sundarbans. Approximately 1,020 endangered <a href="/wiki/Bengal_tiger" title="Bengal tiger">Bengal tigers</a> (<i>Panthera tigris tigris</i>) are believed to inhabit the Sundarbans. The Ganges–Brahmaputra basin has tropical deciduous forests that yield valuable timber: <a href="/wiki/Shorea_robusta" title="Shorea robusta">sal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peepal" title="Peepal">peepal</a> trees are found in these areas. The delta region has mangrove trees.</p>
<p>It is estimated that 30,000 <a href="/wiki/Chital" title="Chital">chital</a> (<i>Axis axis</i>) are in the Sundarbans part of the delta. Birds found in the delta include <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfishers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpeckers</a>, the shalik (<i><a href="/wiki/Common_myna" title="Common myna">Acridotheres tristis</a></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Swamp_francolin" title="Swamp francolin">swamp francolin</a> (<i>Francolinus gularis</i>), and the doel (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_magpie_robin" title="Oriental magpie robin">Copsychus saularis</a></i>). Two species of <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphin</a> can be found in the delta: the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_dolphin" title="Irrawaddy dolphin">Irrawaddy dolphin</a> (<i>Orcaella brevirostris</i>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganges_and_Indus_river_dolphin" title="Ganges and Indus river dolphin">Ganges river dolphin</a> (<i>Platanista gangetica gangetica</i>). The Irrawaddy dolphin is an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_dolphin" title="Oceanic dolphin">oceanic dolphin</a> which enters the delta from the Bay of Bengal. The Ganges river dolphin is a true <a href="/wiki/River_dolphin" title="River dolphin">river dolphin</a>, but is extremely rare and considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a>.</p>
<p>Trees found in the delta include <a href="/wiki/Heritiera_littoralis" title="Heritiera littoralis">sundari</a>, garjan (<i><a href="/wiki/Rhizophora" title="Rhizophora">Rhizophora</a></i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>, mangrove palm (<i><a href="/wiki/Nypa_fruticans" title="Nypa fruticans">Nypa fruticans</a></i>), and mangrove date palm (<i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_paludosa" title="Phoenix paludosa">Phoenix paludosa</a></i>). Many endangered species live here.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ganges Delta lies at the junction of three tectonic plates: the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian Plate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Burma_Plate" title="Burma Plate">Burma Plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The edge of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> paleoshelf runs approximately from <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a> to the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Shillong_Plateau" title="Shillong Plateau">Shillong Plateau</a>. The edge of the paleoshelf marks the transition from the thick continental crust in the northwest to the thin continental or oceanic crust in the southeast. The enormous sediment supply from the Himalayan collision has extended the delta about 400 kilometres (250 mi) seaward since the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>. The sediment thickness southeast of the edge of the paleoshelf beneath the Ganges Delta can exceed 16 kilometres (9.9 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Approximately two-thirds of the Bangladesh people work in agriculture, and grow crops on the fertile floodplains of the delta. The major crops that are grown in the Ganges Delta are <a href="/wiki/Jute" title="Jute">jute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200339_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200339-3">[3]</a></sup> Fishing is also an important activity in the delta region, with fish being a major source of food for many of the people in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200344_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200344-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years, scientists have been helping the poor people of the delta to improve fish farming methods. By turning unused ponds into viable fish farms, and improving methods of raising fish in existing ponds, many people can now earn a living raising and selling fish. Using new systems, fish production in existing ponds has increased 800%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Shrimp are farmed in containers or cages that are submerged in open water. Most are exported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200344_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200344-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>As there is a maze of many river branches, the area is difficult to pass. Most islands are only connected with the mainland by simple wooden ferryboats. Bridges are rare. Some islands are not yet connected to the electric grid, so island residents tend to use solar cells for a bit of electric supply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ganges Delta lies mostly in the tropical wet <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> zone, and receives between 1,500 to 2,000 mm (59 to 79 in) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> each year in the western part, and 2,000 to 3,000 mm (79 to 118 in) in the eastern part.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclones_and_flooding">Cyclones and flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cyclones and flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1970, the deadliest tropical cyclone of the twentieth century hit the Ganges Delta region. The <a href="/wiki/1970_Bhola_cyclone" title="1970 Bhola cyclone">1970 Bhola cyclone</a> killed 500,000 people (official death toll), with another 100,000 missing. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="The Guinness Book of World Records">The Guinness Book of World Records</a> estimated the total loss of human life from the Bhola cyclone at 1,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_Bangladesh_Cyclone" title="1991 Bangladesh Cyclone">Another cyclone</a> hit the delta in 1991, killing about 139,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200343_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200343-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>People have to be careful on the river delta as severe flooding also occurs. In 1998, the Ganges flooded the delta, killing about 1,000 people and leaving more than 30 million people homeless. The Bangladesh government asked for $900 million to help feed the people of the region, as the entire rice crop was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200340_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200340-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_the_delta">Future of the delta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganges_Delta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future of the delta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the greatest challenges people living on the Ganges Delta may face in coming years is the threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">rising sea levels caused by climate change</a>. An increase in sea level of 0.5 metres (1 ft 8 in) could result in six million people losing their homes in Bangladesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200344-45_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200344-45-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Important gas reserves have been discovered in the delta, such as in the Titas and Bakhrabad gas fields. Several major oil companies have invested in exploration of the Ganges Delta region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBowden200341_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBowden200341-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
