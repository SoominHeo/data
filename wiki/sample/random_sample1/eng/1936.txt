<p><b>The Gap, Inc.</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> commonly known as <b>Gap Inc.</b> or <b>Gap</b>, (stylized as <b>GAP</b>) is an American worldwide clothing and accessories retailer.</p>
<p>It was founded in 1969 by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fisher" title="Donald Fisher">Donald Fisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doris_F._Fisher" title="Doris F. Fisher">Doris F. Fisher</a> and is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The company operates five primary divisions: the namesake banner, <a href="/wiki/Banana_Republic" title="Banana Republic">Banana Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Navy" title="Old Navy">Old Navy</a>, Intermix, and Athleta. Gap Inc. is the largest specialty retailer in the United States, and is 3rd in total international locations, behind <a href="/wiki/Inditex" title="Inditex">Inditex</a> Group and <a href="/wiki/H%26M" title="H&amp;M">H&amp;M</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As of September 2008, the company has approximately 135,000 employees and operates 3,727 stores worldwide, of which 2,406 are located in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Fisher family remains deeply involved in the company, collectively owning much of its stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fisher" title="Donald Fisher">Donald Fisher</a> served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Board" title="Chairman of the Board">Chairman of the Board</a> until 2004, playing a role in the ouster of then-CEO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millard_Drexler" title="Millard Drexler">Millard Drexler</a> in 2002, and remained on the board until his death on September 27, 2009. Fisher's wife and their son, <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Fisher" title="Robert J. Fisher">Robert J. Fisher</a>, also serve on Gap's board of directors. Robert succeeded his father as chairman in 2004 and also served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer">CEO</a> on an interim basis following the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pressler" title="Paul Pressler">Paul Pressler</a> in 2007, before being succeeded by Glenn K. Murphy up until 2014. On February 1, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Art_Peck" title="Art Peck">Art Peck</a> took over as CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Donald and Doris Fisher, who had a difficult time finding jeans that fit, opened the first Gap store on Ocean Avenue in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> on August 21, 1969; its merchandise consisted of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levi%27s" title="Levi's">Levi's</a> and <a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">LP records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> They had raised US$63,000 to open the store,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gapinc.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gapinc.com-12">[12]</a></sup> and reached US$2 million in sales in the second full year of operation. In 1970, Gap opened its second store in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>; its back room serves as the company's first replenishment warehouse. In 1971, Gap established its corporate headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Burlingame,_California" title="Burlingame, California">Burlingame, California</a> with four employees. By 1973, the company had over 25 locations and had expanded into the East Coast market with a store in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echelon_Mall" title="Echelon Mall">Echelon Mall</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voorhees,_New_Jersey" title="Voorhees, New Jersey">Voorhees, New Jersey</a>. In 1974, Gap began to sell private-label merchandise.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, Gap assumed an upscale identity and revamped its inventory under the direction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millard_Drexler" title="Millard Drexler">Millard Drexler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Drexler was removed from his position after 19 years of service in 2002 after over-expansion, a 29-month slump in sales, and tensions with the Fisher family. Drexler refused to sign a non-compete agreement and eventually became CEO of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Crew" title="J. Crew">J. Crew</a>. One month after his departure, merchandise that he had ordered was responsible for a strong rebound in sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Fisher" title="Robert J. Fisher">Robert J. Fisher</a> recruited <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pressler" title="Paul Pressler">Paul Pressler</a> as the new CEO; he was credited with closing under-performing locations and paying off debt. However, his focus groups failed to recover the company's leadership in its market.</p>
<p>In 2007, Gap announced that it would "focus [its] efforts on recruiting a chief executive officer who has deep retailing and merchandising experience ideally in apparel, understands the creative process and can effectively execute strategies in large, complex environments while maintaining strong financial discipline". That January, Pressler resigned after two disappointing holiday sales seasons and was succeeded by Robert J. Fisher on an interim basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He began working with the company in 1980 and joined the board in 1990, and would later assume several senior executive positions, including president of Banana Republic and the Gap units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The board's search committee was led by Adrian Bellamy, chairman of <a href="/wiki/The_Body_Shop" title="The Body Shop">The Body Shop International</a> and included founder Donald Fisher. On February 2, Marka Hansen, the former head of the Banana Republic division, replaced <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Harriss" title="Cynthia Harriss">Cynthia Harriss</a> as the leader of the Gap division. The executive president for marketing and merchandising Jack Calhoun became interim president of Banana Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In May, Old Navy laid off approximately 300 managers in lower volume locations to help streamline costs. That July, Glenn Murphy, previously CEO of <a href="/wiki/Shoppers_Drug_Mart" title="Shoppers Drug Mart">Shoppers Drug Mart</a> in Canada, was announced as the new CEO of Gap, Inc. New lead designers were also brought on board to help define a fashionable image, including <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Robinson_(fashion_designer)" title="Patrick Robinson (fashion designer)">Patrick Robinson</a> for Gap Adult, Simon Kneen for Banana Republic, and <a href="/wiki/Todd_Oldham" title="Todd Oldham">Todd Oldham</a> for Old Navy. Robinson was hired as chief designer in 2007, but was dismissed in May 2011 after sales failed to increase. However, he enjoyed commercial success in international markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2011, Gap Inc. announced plans to close 189 US stores, nearly 21 percent, by the end of 2013; however, it also plans to expand its presence in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The company announced it would open its first stores in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in the Fall of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2015, Gap Inc announced plans to close their subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Piperlime" title="Piperlime">Piperlime</a> in order to focus on their core brands. The first and only Piperlime store, based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoHo" title="SoHo">SoHo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, closed in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_identity">Corporate identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo">Logo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Logo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gap Inc. owns a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> to its name, "Gap". The Gap's original trademark was a <a href="/wiki/Service_mark" title="Service mark">service mark</a> for <i>retail clothing store services</i>. The application was filed with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">United States Patent and Trademark Office</a> on February 29, 1972, by The Gap Stores; registration was granted on October 10, 1972. The first use of the trademark was on August 23, 1969, and expanded to commercial usage on October 17, 1969. A second application was filed by Gap Stores, Inc. on September 12, 1974, this time for a trademark filed for <a href="/wiki/Shirt" title="Shirt">shirts</a>. The first usage for shirts and clothing products was on June 25, 1974. Trademark registration was granted on December 28, 1976. Both the service mark and trademark are registered and owned by Gap (Apparel), LLC of San Francisco, California.</p>
<p>On October 6, 2010, in an effort to establish a contemporary presence, Gap introduced a new logo. It was designed with the <a href="/wiki/Helvetica" title="Helvetica">Helvetica</a> font and reduced the prominence of the brand's iconic blue box. After much public outcry, the company reverted to its previous "blue box" logo on October 12, after less than a week in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Marka Hansen, the executive who oversaw the logo change, resigned February 1, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Banana_Republic" title="Banana Republic">Banana Republic</a>, a small safari-themed clothing retailer, was purchased by Gap in 1983 and was rebranded as an upscale clothing retailer in the late 1980s. <a href="/wiki/Old_Navy" title="Old Navy">Old Navy</a> was launched in 1994 as a value chain with a specialty flair. <a href="/wiki/Forth_%26_Towne" title="Forth &amp; Towne">Forth &amp; Towne</a>, the company's fourth traditional retail concept, was launched on August 24, 2005, featuring apparel targeted toward women 35 years and older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On February 26, 2007, after an 18-month trial period, it was discontinued, and the 19 stores were closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A fifth brand, the online clothing and accessories retailer <a href="/wiki/Piperlime" title="Piperlime">Piperlime</a>, was created in 2006; however, as of April 2015, the brand has been retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A sixth brand, Athleta, a women's athletic wear line, was added in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Intermix, a multi-brand fashion retailer founded in 1993, was acquired by Gap Inc. in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Gap originally targeted the younger generation when it opened, with its name referring to the <a href="/wiki/Generation_gap" title="Generation gap">generation gap</a> of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It originally sold everything that Levi Strauss &amp; Co made in every style, size, and color, and organized the stock by size. The Gap was the first of many shops that carried only Levi's, which led to a worldwide shortage of indigo denim.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Gap eventually started making their own jeans and selling them at the expense of jeans from Levi's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Gap's current marketing works to appeal to a broad demographic of customers, whereas <a href="/wiki/Banana_Republic" title="Banana Republic">Banana Republic</a> presents a sophisticated image and <a href="/wiki/Old_Navy" title="Old Navy">Old Navy</a> focuses "fun, fashion, and value" for families and younger customers. While the company has been criticized for blandness and uniformity in its selling environments, it maintains that it tailors its stores "to appeal to unique markets" by developing multiple formats and designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The domain <i>www.gap.com</i> attracts over 18 million visitors annually, according to a 2008 <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The brand is being criticized in the UK because the merchandise that is offered to the UK customers cost double the prices (or even a direct $/£ swap) found in the United States. Gap also does not offer XXL or larger sizes in the UK stating the UK market does not require them in contrast to market leader NEXT who offer a variety of larger sizes in the UK.</p>

<p>Gap operates company-owned stores in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, France, Italy, Indonesia, Japan, China, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Mexico as of May 2016; it also has franchise agreements with unaffiliated franchisees to operate Gap, Banana Republic, or Old Navy stores in 43 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> As of May 2016, Gap Inc. operates 3,727 company-owned and franchise locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In January 2008, Gap signed a deal with Marinopoulos Group to open Gap and Banana Republic stores in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In February 2009, Elbit Imaging, Ltd. secured a franchise to open and operate Gap and Banana Republic stores in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In August 2010, the company opened its first store in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Australia" title="Commonwealth of Australia">Australia</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chadstone_Shopping_Centre" title="Chadstone Shopping Centre">Chadstone Shopping Centre</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In September 2011, Komax opened the first Gap store in Chile, due to a franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In October 2011, the first GAP store opened in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> but shut it down and two other locations in <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">Wrocław</a> and <a href="/wiki/Katowice" title="Katowice">Katowice</a> in 2015. Gap now has a store in New Delhi, India which opened in May 2015. On 20 February 2016, Gap launched stores in Mumbai at Oberoi Mall and Infinity-2.</p>
<p>In May 2016, Gap Inc. announced it would shutter all Old Navy stores in Japan in response to poor Q1 performance for Old Navy and consistent losses across the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2006, Gap took part in the Product Red campaign with the launch of a special RED collection, including a T-shirt manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a> from African cotton. The expanded Gap Product Red collection was released on October 13, 2006. 50 to 100 percent of the profits went to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Fund" title="Global Fund">Global Fund</a>, depending on the item.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The company continued the products into 2007, especially in the lead up to Valentine's Day, using slogans such as "Admi(RED)" and "Desi(RED)." National Labor Committee for Worker and Human Rights activists criticized Gap's partnerships because Gap has historically been accused of sweatshop-like conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Product Red has contributed over $45 million to the Global Fund, more than any other private donation received to date. Other launch partners included <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Converse_(shoe_company)" title="Converse (shoe company)">Converse</a>, Hallmark, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emporio_Armani" title="Emporio Armani">Emporio Armani</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_controversies">Labour controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Labour controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> lawsuit against Gap and 21 other companies alleged that <a href="/wiki/Sweatshop" title="Sweatshop">sweatshop</a> workers in <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a> were not paid for overtime work, were subjected to forced <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, and were required to work in unsafe working conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> of 20 million dollars was reached whereby The Gap did not admit liability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethisphere_Magazine" title="Ethisphere Magazine">Ethisphere Magazine</a> chose Gap from among thousands of companies evaluated as one of 100 "World’s Most Ethical Companies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In May 2006, adult and child employees of Western, a supplier in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, were found to have worked up to 109 hours per week and to have gone six months without being paid. Some employees claimed they had been raped by managers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NLCNET_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-NLCNET-54">[54]</a></sup> Most of these allegations were directed at Wal-Mart (who mostly ignored the claims), while Gap immediately looked into the matter to remedy the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NLCNET_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-NLCNET-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (April 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On October 28, 2007, <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> footage showed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">child labor</a> in Indian Gap factories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The company denied knowledge of the happenings; it subsequently removed and destroyed the single piece of clothing in question, a smock blouse, from a British store. Gap promised to investigate breaches in its ethical policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bangladeshi and international labor groups in 2011 put forth a detailed safety proposal which entailed the establishment of independent inspections of garment factories. The plan called for inspectors to have the power to close unsafe factories. The proposal entailed a legally binding contract between suppliers, customers and unions. At a meeting in 2011 in <a href="/wiki/Dhaka" title="Dhaka">Dhaka</a>, major European and North American retailers, including Gap, rejected the proposal. Further efforts by unions to advance the proposal after numerous and deadly factory fires have been rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Big_brands_rejected_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Big_brands_rejected-57">[57]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/2013_Savar_building_collapse" title="2013 Savar building collapse">2013 Savar building collapse</a>, Kohl's became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Bangladesh_Worker_Safety" title="Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety">Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety</a>.</p>
<p>On February 19, 2014, Glenn Murphy, CEO of Gap Inc., announced Gap will raise the minimum wages for its 65,000 U.S. store employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gap_Readies_Minimum_Wage_Increase_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gap_Readies_Minimum_Wage_Increase-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
