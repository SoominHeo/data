<p><b>Gangsta rap</b> or <b>gangster rap</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenre" title="Subgenre">subgenre</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a> with themes and lyrics that generally emphasize the "<a href="/wiki/Gangster" title="Gangster">gangsta</a>" lifestyle. The genre evolved from <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore hip hop</a> into a distinct form, pioneered in the mid-1980s by rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Schoolly_D" title="Schoolly D">Schoolly D</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>, and was popularized in the later part of the 1980s by groups like <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> After the national attention that Ice-T and N.W.A attracted in the late 1980s and early 1990s, gangsta rap became the most commercially lucrative subgenre of hip hop. Many (if not most) gangsta rap artists openly boast of their associations with various active street gangs as part of their artistic image, with the <a href="/wiki/Bloods" title="Bloods">Bloods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crips" title="Crips">Crips</a> being the most commonly represented. Gangsta rap is closely related to other indigenous gang and crime-oriented forms of music, such as the <a href="/wiki/Narcocorrido" title="Narcocorrido">narcocorrido</a> genre of northern Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The subject matter inherent in gangsta rap has caused a great deal of controversy. Criticism has come from both left wing and right wing commentators, as well as <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious leaders</a>, who have accused the genre of promoting <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_addiction" title="Sexual addiction">sex addiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Promiscuity" title="Promiscuity">promiscuity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogyny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">street gangs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disorderly_conduct" title="Disorderly conduct">disorderly conduct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drive-by_shooting" title="Drive-by shooting">drive-by shootings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">thievery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">driving under the influence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">drug dealing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol abuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contempt_of_cop" title="Contempt of cop">disregarding law enforcement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Narcissism" title="Narcissism">narcissism</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_White_House" title="The White House">The White House</a> administrations of both <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> criticized the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship-3">[3]</a></sup> "Many black rappers—including Ice-T and <a href="/wiki/Sister_Souljah" title="Sister Souljah">Sister Souljah</a>—contend that they are being unfairly singled out because their music reflects deep changes in society not being addressed anywhere else in the public forum. The white politicians, the artists complain, neither understand the music nor desire to hear what's going on in the devastated communities that gave birth to the art form," wrote journalist <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Philips" title="Chuck Philips">Chuck Philips</a> in a review of the battle between "the Establishment" and defenders of rap music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship-3">[3]</a></sup> "The reason why rap is under attack is because it exposes all the contradictions of American culture ...What started out as an underground art form has become a vehicle to expose a lot of critical issues that are not usually discussed in American politics. The problem here is that the White House and wanna-bes like Bill Clinton represent a political system that never intends to deal with inner city urban chaos," Sister Souljah told Philips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-rap.2C_race.2C_and_censorship-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, some commentators (for example, <a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">Spike Lee</a> in his satirical film <i><a href="/wiki/Bamboozled" title="Bamboozled">Bamboozled</a></i>) have criticized gangsta rap as analogous to black <a href="/wiki/Minstrel_show" title="Minstrel show">minstrel shows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">blackface</a> performance, in which performers – both black and white – were made to look African American, and acted in a stereotypical uncultured and ignorant manner for entertainment. Gangsta rappers often defend themselves by arguing they are describing the reality of inner-city life, and that they are only adopting a character which behaves in ways they do not necessarily endorse. Gangsta rappers are also famous (or infamous) for appearing more hardcore compared to early concepts and themes of hip-hop artists, and are known for saying things that are often considered <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>; for instance, the gangsta rap group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> produced the famous <a href="/wiki/Fuck_tha_Police" title="Fuck tha Police">Fuck tha Police</a> protest song about <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racial_profiling" title="Racial profiling">racial profiling</a>.</p>
<p>In high-crime areas, putting on these made up personas is life-threatening, but the fact that gangsta rappers told the stories of others is often seen as having earned them respect for raising awareness of the severity of inner-city crime. Many gangsta rappers argue that in the world of their genre exists the emotions and perspectives of a people whose suffering is too often overlooked and belittled by society. Gangsta rap, some argue, was an effect of the various wrongdoings perpetrated against African-Americans in underprivileged neighborhoods. The various riots sparked by the <a href="/wiki/Rodney_King" title="Rodney King">Rodney King</a> beating and the acquittal of the police officers responsible for the beating sparked anger and outrage in an area that was already on the bubble. Gangsta rap acted as an outlet so such people could express themselves angrily and not in fear that they were going to be silenced for telling the truth. They often used gangsta rap to tell the stories of their lives, which sometimes included strong violence, hypersexuality, and drug abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 1973 album <i>Hustler's Convention</i> by Lightnin' Rod and Jaren Clark featured lyrics that deal with street life, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pimp" title="Pimp">pimping</a> and the hustling of drugs. <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Poets" title="The Last Poets">The Last Poets</a> member <a href="/wiki/Jalal_Mansur_Nuriddin" title="Jalal Mansur Nuriddin">Jalal Mansur Nuriddin</a> delivers rhyming vocals in the urban slang of his time, and together with the other Last Poets members, was quite influential on later <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> groups, such as <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>. Many rappers, such as <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mac_Dre" title="Mac Dre">Mac Dre</a>, have credited pimp and writer <a href="/wiki/Iceberg_Slim" title="Iceberg Slim">Iceberg Slim</a> with influencing their rhymes.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudy_Ray_Moore" title="Rudy Ray Moore">Rudy Ray Moore</a>'s stand-up comedy and films based on his <a href="/wiki/Dolemite" title="Dolemite">Dolemite</a> hustler-pimp alter ego also influenced gangsta rap and are still a popular source for samples. Finally, <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">blaxploitation</a> films of the 1970s, with their vivid depictions of black underworld figures, were a major inspiration as well. For example, the opening skit on <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i> is a homage to the famous bathtub scene in the 1972 film <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Fly_(film)" title="Super Fly (film)">Super Fly</a></i>, while the rapper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notorious_B.I.G." title="Notorious B.I.G.">Notorious B.I.G.</a> took his alias "Biggie Smalls" from a character in the 1975 film <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Do_It_Again_(1975_film)" title="Let's Do It Again (1975 film)">Let's Do It Again</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins:_1984.E2.80.931990">Origins: 1984–1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins: 1984–1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings:_Ice-T_.26_Schoolly_D">Beginnings: Ice-T &amp; Schoolly D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beginnings: Ice-T &amp; Schoolly D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tracy "<a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>" Morrow, was born in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>, in 1958. As a teenager, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> where he rose to prominence in the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop scene</a>. In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> released "<a href="/wiki/6_in_the_Mornin%27" title="6 in the Mornin'">6 in the Mornin'</a>", which is often regarded as among the first gangsta rap songs. Ice-T had been MCing since the early '80s, but first turned to gangsta rap themes after being influenced by <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Schoolly_D" title="Schoolly D">Schoolly D</a> and his 1985 album <i><a href="/wiki/Schoolly_D_(album)" title="Schoolly D (album)">Schoolly D</a></i>, often considered to be the originator of gangsta rap. In an interview with PROPS magazine, Ice-T said:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Here's the exact chronological order of what really went down: The first record that came out along those lines was <a href="/wiki/Schoolly_D" title="Schoolly D">Schoolly D</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/P.S.K._What_Does_It_Mean%3F" title="P.S.K. What Does It Mean?">P.S.K.</a>" Then the syncopation of that rap was used by me when I made "6 in the Mornin'". The vocal delivery was the same: '...P.S.K. is makin' that green', '...six in the morning, police at my door'. When I heard that record I was like "Oh @#!*% !" and call it a bite or what you will but I dug that record. My record didn't sound like P.S.K., but I liked the way he was flowing with it. P.S.K. was talking about Park Side Killers but it was very vague. That was the only difference, when Schoolly did it, it was "...one by one, I'm knockin' em out." All he did was represent a gang on his record. I took that and wrote a record about guns, beating people down, and all that with "6 in the Mornin'". At the same time my single came out, <a href="/wiki/Boogie_Down_Productions" title="Boogie Down Productions">Boogie Down Productions</a> hit with <i><a href="/wiki/Criminal_Minded" title="Criminal Minded">Criminal Minded</a></i>, which was a gangster-based album. It wasn't about messages or "You Must Learn", it was about gangsterism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In 2011, Ice-T repeated in his autobiography that Schoolly D was his inspiration for gangsta rap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Ice-T continued to release gangsta albums for the remainder of the 1980s: <i><a href="/wiki/Rhyme_Pays" title="Rhyme Pays">Rhyme Pays</a></i> in 1987, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(Ice_T)" title="Power (Ice T)">Power</a></i> in 1988, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Iceberg/Freedom_of_Speech...Just_Watch_What_You_Say" title="The Iceberg/Freedom of Speech...Just Watch What You Say">The Iceberg/Freedom of Speech...Just Watch What You Say</a></i> in 1989. Ice-T's lyrics also contained strong political commentary, and often played the line between glorifying the gangsta lifestyle and criticizing it as a <a href="/wiki/No-win_situation" title="No-win situation">no-win situation</a>.</p>
<p>Schoolly D's debut album, <i>Schoolly D</i>, and especially the song "<a href="/wiki/P.S.K._What_Does_It_Mean%3F" title="P.S.K. What Does It Mean?">P.S.K. What Does It Mean?</a>", would heavily influence not only Ice-T, but also <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a> and <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> (most notably in the song "<a href="/wiki/Boyz-n-the-Hood" title="Boyz-n-the-Hood">Boyz-n-the-Hood</a>") as well as the <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a> on their seminal hardcore hip hop-influenced album <i><a href="/wiki/Licensed_to_Ill" title="Licensed to Ill">Licensed to Ill</a></i> (1986).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hess_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hess-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boogie_Down_Productions_.26_N.W.A">Boogie Down Productions &amp; N.W.A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boogie Down Productions &amp; N.W.A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boogie_Down_Productions" title="Boogie Down Productions">Boogie Down Productions</a> released their first single, "Say No Brother (Crack Attack Don't Do It)", in 1986. It was followed by "South-Bronx/P is Free" and "9mm Goes Bang" in the same year. The latter is the most gangsta-themed song of the three; in it, <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a> boasts about shooting a crack dealer and his posse to death (in self-defense).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The album <i><a href="/wiki/Criminal_Minded" title="Criminal Minded">Criminal Minded</a></i> followed in 1987, and was the first rap album to have fire arms on its cover. Shortly after the release of this album, BDP's DJ, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scott_LaRock" title="Scott LaRock">Scott LaRock</a> was shot and killed. After this, BDP's subsequent records were more focused with the inadequate rationale removed.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N.W.A.StraightOuttaComptonalbumcover.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/N.W.A.StraightOuttaComptonalbumcover.jpg/220px-N.W.A.StraightOuttaComptonalbumcover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d2/N.W.A.StraightOuttaComptonalbumcover.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N.W.A.StraightOuttaComptonalbumcover.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
N.W.A's best-selling album <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first blockbuster gangsta rap album was N.W.A's <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, released in 1988. <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> would establish West Coast hip hop as a vital genre, and establish Los Angeles as a legitimate rival to hip hop's long-time capital, New York City. <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> sparked the first major controversy regarding hip hop lyrics when their song "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_tha_Police" title="Fuck tha Police">Fuck tha Police</a>" earned a letter from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> Assistant Director, Milt Ahlerich, strongly expressing <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">law enforcement</a>'s resentment of the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to the influence of Ice T and N.W.A, gangsta rap is often credited as being an originally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Coast_rap" title="West Coast rap">West Coast</a> phenomenon, despite the contributions of East Coast acts like Boogie Down Productions in shaping the genre.</p>
<p>In the early 1990s, former N.W.A member <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> would further influence gangsta rap with his hardcore, socio-political solo albums, which suggested the potential of gangsta rap as a political medium to give voice to inner-city youth. N.W.A's second album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efil4zaggin" title="Efil4zaggin">Efil4zaggin</a></i> (1991) (released after Ice Cube's departure from the group), broke ground as the first gangsta rap album to reach #1 on the Billboard pop charts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aside from N.W.A and Ice T, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>), <a href="/wiki/Frost_(rapper)" title="Frost (rapper)">Kid Frost</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/South_Gate,_California" title="South Gate, California">South Gate</a>-based Latino group <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hill" title="Cypress Hill">Cypress Hill</a> were pioneering West Coast rappers. <a href="/wiki/Above_the_Law_(group)" title="Above the Law (group)">Above the Law</a> also played an important role in the gangsta rap movement, as their 1990 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Livin%27_Like_Hustlers" title="Livin' Like Hustlers">Livin' Like Hustlers</a></i>, as well as their guest appearance on N.W.A's 1991 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efil4zaggin" title="Efil4zaggin">Efil4zaggin</a></i>, foreshadowing the dominance of the genre in 1990s starting with Dr. Dre's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a> were one of the first groups to identify themselves as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangsters" title="Gangsters">gangsters</a>", and one of the first popular rap groups to talk about violence and drug and alcohol use, though largely in a more humorous manner. They had started out as a <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> band, but after introduction to producer <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a> and the exit of <a href="/wiki/Kate_Schellenbach" title="Kate Schellenbach">Kate Schellenbach</a> they became a rap group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> Magazine, their 1986 album <i><a href="/wiki/Licensed_to_Ill" title="Licensed to Ill">Licensed to Ill</a></i> is "filled with enough references to guns, drugs, and empty sex (including the pornographic deployment of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiffleball" title="Wiffleball">Wiffleball</a> bat in "<a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere_(song)" title="Paul Revere (song)">Paul Revere</a>") to qualify as a gangsta-rap cornerstone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Beasties' 1989 album <i><a href="/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique" title="Paul's Boutique">Paul's Boutique</a></i> included the similarly themed tracks "Car Thief," "Looking Down the Barrel of a Gun," and "High-Plains Drifter." In 1986, the Los Angeles-based group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.A._(group)" title="C.I.A. (group)">C.I.A.</a> rapped over Beastie Boy tracks for songs such as "My Posse" and "Ill-Legal", and the Beastie Boys' influence can be seen significantly in N.W.A's early albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The New York rap group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Run_DMC" title="Run DMC">Run DMC</a> is often credited with popularizing hardcore and confrontational attitudes and lyrics in hip hop culture, and were one of the first rap groups to dress in flashy, gang-like street clothing. Their stripped-down, rock-inspired beats were also important in establishing the early gangsta rap production style. The seminal Long Island-based group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">Public Enemy</a> featured aggressive, politically charged lyrics, which had an especially strong influence on gangsta rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. East Coast hardcore rappers like <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">Rakim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">Big Daddy Kane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slick_Rick" title="Slick Rick">Slick Rick</a>, <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a>, and <a href="/wiki/EPMD" title="EPMD">EPMD</a> also reflected the trend in hip-hop music in the late 1980s towards hard-hitting, angry, aggressive, and politically conscious lyrics, revolving around crime, violence, poverty, war and gunplay.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>-based group known as the <a href="/wiki/Geto_Boys" title="Geto Boys">Geto Boys</a> came out around the late 1980s and made songs containing both gangsta themes of crime and violence and sociopolitical commentary. The group notably released proto-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafioso_rap" title="Mafioso rap">mafioso rap</a> music with the song "Scarface", a track centered on selling <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> and killing rival gang members. The Geto Boys are also known for being the first rap group to sample from the movie <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scarface_(1983_movie)" title="Scarface (1983 movie)">Scarface</a></i>, a film which became the basis for various mafioso rap samples in the 1990s. Furthermore, the Geto Boys, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam_Master_J" title="Jam Master J">Jam Master J</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Erick_Sermon" title="Erick Sermon">Erick Sermon</a>'s group <a href="/wiki/Flatlinerz" title="Flatlinerz">Flatlinerz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Paul_(producer)" title="Prince Paul (producer)">Prince Paul</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>'s group <a href="/wiki/Gravediggaz" title="Gravediggaz">Gravediggaz</a>, are often cited as pioneers of "<a href="/wiki/Horrorcore" title="Horrorcore">horrorcore</a>" rap, a <a href="/wiki/Transgressive_art" title="Transgressive art">transgressive</a> and abrasive subgenre of hardcore rap or gangsta rap which focuses on common <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror</a> themes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a>, often with <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">gothic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Macabre" title="Macabre">macabre</a> lyrics, <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">satanic</a> imagery, and <a href="/wiki/Slasher_film" title="Slasher film">slasher film</a> or <a href="/wiki/Splatter_film" title="Splatter film">splatter film</a>-like violence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_.E2.80.93_present">1990 – present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990 – present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice-T">Ice-T</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ice-T">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> released one of the seminal albums of the genre, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OG:_Original_Gangster" title="OG: Original Gangster">OG: Original Gangster</a></i> in 1991. It also contained a song by his new <a href="/wiki/Thrash_metal" title="Thrash metal">thrash metal</a> group <a href="/wiki/Body_Count" title="Body Count">Body Count</a>, who released a <a href="/wiki/Body_Count_(album)" title="Body Count (album)">self titled album</a> in 1992. Particular controversy surrounded one of its songs "<a href="/wiki/Cop_Killer_(song)" title="Cop Killer (song)">Cop Killer</a>". The rock song was intended to speak from the viewpoint of a police target seeking revenge on racist, brutal cops. Ice-T's rock song infuriated government officials, the <a href="/wiki/National_Rifle_Association" title="National Rifle Association">National Rifle Association</a> and various police advocacy groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_Schwarzenegger_blew_away_dozens_of_cops_as_the_Terminator._But_I_don.27t_hear_anybody_complaining_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold_Schwarzenegger_blew_away_dozens_of_cops_as_the_Terminator._But_I_don.27t_hear_anybody_complaining-14">[14]</a></sup> Consequently, Time Warner Music refused to release Ice-T's upcoming album <i>Home Invasion</i> and dropped Ice-T from the label. Ice-T suggested that the furor over the song was an overreaction, telling journalist <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Philips" title="Chuck Philips">Chuck Philips</a> "...they've done movies about nurse killers and teacher killers and student killers. Arnold Schwarzenegger blew away dozens of cops as the Terminator. But I don't hear anybody complaining about that." In the same interview, Ice-T suggested to Philips that the misunderstanding of <i>Cop Killer</i>, the misclassification of it as a rap song (not a rock song), and the attempts to censor it had racial overtones: "The Supreme Court says it's OK for a white man to burn a cross in public. But nobody wants a black man to write a record about a cop killer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_Schwarzenegger_blew_away_dozens_of_cops_as_the_Terminator._But_I_don.27t_hear_anybody_complaining_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold_Schwarzenegger_blew_away_dozens_of_cops_as_the_Terminator._But_I_don.27t_hear_anybody_complaining-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ice-T's next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Invasion_(album)" title="Home Invasion (album)">Home Invasion</a></i> was postponed as a result of the controversy, and was finally released in 1993. While it contained gangsta elements, it was his most <a href="/wiki/Political_hip_hop" title="Political hip hop">political</a> album to date. After a proposed censoring of the <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Invasion_(album)" title="Home Invasion (album)">Home Invasion</a></i> album cover art, he left <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>. Ice-T's subsequent releases went back to straight gangsta-ism, but were never as popular as his earlier releases. He had alienated his core audience with his involvement in metal, his emphasis on politics and with his uptempo Bomb-Squad style beats during a time when <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> was popular. He published a book "The Ice Opinion: Who Gives a @#!*% ?" in 1994.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="G-funk_and_Death_Row_Records">G-funk and Death Row Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: G-funk and Death Row Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-Funk" title="G-Funk">G-Funk</a></div>
<p>In 1992, former N.W.A member <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>, a massive seller (eventually going triple platinum) which showed that explicit gangsta rap could hold mass commercial appeal just like more pop-oriented rappers such as <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">MC Hammer</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Jazzy_Jeff_%26_The_Fresh_Prince" title="DJ Jazzy Jeff &amp; The Fresh Prince">The Fresh Prince</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tone_L%C5%8Dc" title="Tone Lōc">Tone Lōc</a>. The album established the dominance of West Coast gangsta rap and Dre's new post-N.W.A label, <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> (owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marion_%22Suge%22_Knight" title='Marion "Suge" Knight'>Marion "Suge" Knight</a>), as Dre's album showcased a stable of promising new Death Row rappers. The album also began the subgenre of G-funk, a slow, drawled form of hip hop that dominated the rap charts for some time.</p>
<p>Extensively sampling <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> bands, especially <a href="/wiki/Parliament_(band)" title="Parliament (band)">Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funkadelic" title="Funkadelic">Funkadelic</a>, G-funk was multi-layered, yet simple and easy to dance to. The simple message of its lyrics, that life's problems could be overcome by guns, alcohol, and marijuana, endeared it to a teenage audience. The single "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuthin%27_but_a_%22G%22_Thang" title="Nuthin' but a &quot;G&quot; Thang">Nuthin' but a "G" Thang</a>" became a crossover hit, with its humorous, <i><a href="/wiki/House_Party_(film)" title="House Party (film)">House Party</a></i>-influenced video becoming an MTV staple despite that network's historic orientation towards rock music.</p>
<p>Another success was <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Predator" title="The Predator">Predator</a></i> album, released at about the same time as <i>The Chronic</i> in 1992. It sold over 5 million copies and was #1 in the charts, propelled by the hit single "<a href="/wiki/It_Was_a_Good_Day" title="It Was a Good Day">It Was a Good Day</a>", despite the fact that Ice Cube was not a Death Row artist. One of the genre's biggest crossover stars was Dre's protégé <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Doggy Dogg</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i>), whose exuberant, party-oriented themes made songs such as "<a href="/wiki/Gin_and_Juice" title="Gin and Juice">Gin and Juice</a>" club anthems and top hits nationwide. In 1996, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac">2Pac</a> signed with Death Row and released the multi-platinum double album <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me" title="All Eyez on Me">All Eyez on Me</a></i>. Not long afterward, his shocking murder brought gangsta rap into the national headlines and propelled his posthumous <i><a href="/wiki/The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory" title="The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory">The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</a></i> album (released under the alias "Makaveli") (which eerily featured an image of 2Pac being crucified on the front cover) to the top of the charts. <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a> was another G-funk musician along with the now deceased <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>. Other successful G-Funk influenced artists included <a href="/wiki/Spice_1" title="Spice 1">Spice 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/MC_Eiht" title="MC Eiht">MC Eiht</a> and <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a>, all of them reaching decent positions on the Billboard 100, in spite of not being associated with Death Row.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mafioso_rap">Mafioso rap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mafioso rap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mafioso rap is a <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore hip hop</a> subgenre founded by <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a> in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is the pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Mafia" title="Mafia">Mafia</a> extension of East Coast hardcore rap, and is considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> the counterpart of West Coast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-Funk" title="G-Funk">G-Funk</a> rap. Mafioso rap is characterized by references to famous <a href="/wiki/Gangster" title="Gangster">mobsters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafiosi" title="Mafiosi">mafiosi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racketeering" title="Racketeering">racketeering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a> in general (but especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian-American_Mafia" title="Italian-American Mafia">Italian-American Mafia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_Mafia" title="Sicilian Mafia">Sicilian Mafia</a>, <a href="/wiki/African-American_organized_crime" title="African-American organized crime">African-American organized crime</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">Latin American</a> organized crime or <a href="/wiki/Drug_cartel" title="Drug cartel">drug cartels</a>). Though some mafioso rap was more gritty and street-oriented, many other mafioso rap artists frequently focused on lavish, self-indulgent, <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialistic</a>, and luxurious subject matter, such as expensive drugs, cars, and expensive <a href="/wiki/Champagne" title="Champagne">champagne</a>. Though the genre died down for several years, it re-emerged in 1995 when <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> member <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a> released his critically acclaimed solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</a></i>. 1995 also saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Doe_or_Die" title="Doe or Die">Doe or Die</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>' protégé <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a> and the release of the album <i><a href="/wiki/4,5,6" title="4,5,6">4,5,6</a></i> by subgenre originator <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>. This album featured other mafioso rap artists <a href="/wiki/MF_Grimm" title="MF Grimm">MF Grimm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> and B-1. These three albums brought the genre to mainstream recognition, and inspired other East Coast artists, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notorious_B.I.G." title="Notorious B.I.G.">Notorious B.I.G.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, to adopt the same themes as well with their albums <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Life_After_Death" title="Life After Death">Life After Death</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/It_Was_Written" title="It Was Written">It Was Written</a></i> (respectively). Though Mafioso rap declined in the mainstream by the late 1990s, it saw somewhat of a revival in the mid 2000s<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fishscale" title="Fishscale">Fishscale</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/American_Gangster_(album)" title="American Gangster (album)">American Gangster</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..._Pt._II" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx... Pt. II">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx... Pt. II</a></i>. Similarly, in recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> many rappers, such as <a href="/wiki/T.I." title="T.I.">T.I.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross" title="Rick Ross">Rick Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones" title="Jim Jones">Jim Jones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cassidy_(rapper)" title="Cassidy (rapper)">Cassidy</a> have maintained popularity with lyrics about self-centered urban criminal lifestyles or "hustling". <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Kim" title="Lil' Kim">Lil' Kim</a>'s mafioso album <i><a href="/wiki/La_Bella_Mafia" title="La Bella Mafia">La Bella Mafia</a></i>, released in 2003, was a commercial success, receiving platinum certification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Coast_hardcore_hip_hop_and_the_East_Coast-West_Coast_feud">East Coast hardcore hip hop and the East Coast-West Coast feud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: East Coast hardcore hip hop and the East Coast-West Coast feud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meanwhile, rappers from New York City, such as <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papoose_(rapper)" title="Papoose (rapper)">Papoose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remy_Ma" title="Remy Ma">Remy Ma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onyx_(band)" title="Onyx (band)">Onyx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_L" title="Big L">Big L</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Kim" title="Lil' Kim">Lil' Kim</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_L.O.X" title="The L.O.X">The L.O.X</a>, among others, pioneered a grittier sound known as East Coast <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore hip hop</a>. In 1994, both <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> released their debut albums <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i> respectively, which paved the way for New York City to take back dominance from the West Coast. In an interview for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> in 1994, the Wu-Tang Clan's <a href="/wiki/GZA" title="GZA">GZA</a> commented on the term "gangsta rap" and its association with his group's music and hip hop at the time:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Our music is not 'gangsta rap'. There's no such thing. The label was created by the media to limit what we can say. We just deliver the truth in a brutal fashion. The young black male is a target. Snoop (Doggy Dogg) has gone four times platinum and makes more money than the president. They don't like that, so you hear 'ban this, ban that'. We attack people's emotions. It's a real live show that brings out the inside in people. Like I said, intense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALewis_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALewis-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— GZA</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>It is widely speculated that the ensuing "East Coast/West Coast" battle between <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a> resulted in the deaths of <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac">2Pac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a>' <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> Even before the murders, Death Row had begun to unravel, as co-founder Dr. Dre had left earlier in 1996; in the aftermath of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac">2Pac</a>'s death, label owner <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a> was sentenced to prison for a parole violation, and Death Row proceeded to sink quickly as most of its remaining artists, including <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, left. Dr. Dre, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="MTV Video Music Awards">MTV Video Music Awards</a>, claimed that "gangsta rap was dead". While Puff Daddy's Bad Boy Entertainment fared better than its West Coast rival, it eventually began to lose popularity and support by the end of the decade, due to its pursuit of a more mainstream sound, as well as challenges from <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and New Orleans-based labels, especially, <a href="/wiki/Master_P" title="Master P">Master P</a>'s <a href="/wiki/No_Limit_Records" title="No Limit Records">No Limit</a> stable of popular rappers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_and_Midwestern_gangsta_rap">Southern and Midwestern gangsta rap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Southern and Midwestern gangsta rap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the deaths of <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biggie_Smalls" title="Biggie Smalls">Biggie Smalls</a> and the media attention the murders generated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> gangsta rap became an even greater commercial force. However, most of the industry's major labels were in turmoil, bankrupt, or creatively stagnant, and new labels representing the rap scenes in new locations sprang up.</p>
<p>Master P's No Limit Records label, based out of New Orleans, became quite popular in the late 1990s, though critical success was very scarce, with the exceptions of some later additions like <a href="/wiki/Mystikal" title="Mystikal">Mystikal</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ghetto_Fabulous_(album)" title="Ghetto Fabulous (album)">Ghetto Fabulous</a></i>, 1998). No Limit had begun its rise to national popularity with Master P's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ghetto_Is_Trying_to_Kill_Me!" title="The Ghetto Is Trying to Kill Me!">The Ghetto Is Trying to Kill Me!</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1994_in_music" title="1994 in music">1994</a>), and had major hits with <a href="/wiki/Silkk_the_Shocker" title="Silkk the Shocker">Silkk the Shocker</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charge_It_2_Da_Game" title="Charge It 2 Da Game">Charge It 2 Da Game</a></i>, 1998) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C_Miller" title="C Miller">C-Murder</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Life_or_Death_(C-Murder_album)" title="Life or Death (C-Murder album)">Life or Death</a></i>, 1998). <a href="/wiki/Cash_Money_Records" title="Cash Money Records">Cash Money Records</a>, also based out of New Orleans, had enormous commercial success beginning in the late 1990s with a similar musical style but utilized a quality-over-quantity business approach unlike No Limit.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a> collective <a href="/wiki/Hypnotize_Minds" title="Hypnotize Minds">Hypnotize Minds</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Three_6_Mafia" title="Three 6 Mafia">Three 6 Mafia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_Pat" title="Project Pat">Project Pat</a>, have taken gangsta rap to some of its darker extremes. Led by in-house producers <a href="/wiki/DJ_Paul" title="DJ Paul">DJ Paul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juicy_J" title="Juicy J">Juicy J</a>, the label became known for its pulsating, menacing beats and uncompromisingly thuggish lyrics. However, in the mid-2000s, the group began attaining more mainstream popularity, eventually culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Three_6_Mafia" title="Three 6 Mafia">Three 6 Mafia</a> winning an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for the song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/It%27s_Hard_Out_Here_for_a_Pimp" title="It's Hard Out Here for a Pimp">It's Hard Out Here for a Pimp</a>" from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hustle_and_Flow" title="Hustle and Flow">Hustle and Flow</a></i>.</p>
<p>Midwest gangsta rap originated in the mid-1990s and rose to major prominence in the 2000s. However, in the 2010s a new form of gangsta rap known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drill_(music_genre)" title="Drill (music genre)">drill</a> emerged from the Midwest. Drill gained popularity by rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Lil_Durk" title="Lil Durk">Lil Durk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chief_Keef" title="Chief Keef">Chief Keef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil_Reese" title="Lil Reese">Lil Reese</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lil_Herb" title="Lil Herb">Lil Herb</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest_Hip_Hop" title="Midwest Hip Hop">Midwest Hip Hop</a> was originally distinctive for its faster-paced flow. This is evident in the styles of the earliest Midwestern rappers to release albums, Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a> and Cleveland's <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-n-Harmony" title="Bone Thugs-n-Harmony">Bone Thugs-n-Harmony</a>. Bone Thugs, known for their fast, harmonizing vocals coupled with an ultra-quick rap delivery, would achieve major success with their critically acclaimed 1995 album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E_1999_Eternal" title="E 1999 Eternal">E 1999 Eternal</a></i>, which featured a major hit in the Grammy-winning "<a href="/wiki/Tha_Crossroads" title="Tha Crossroads">Tha Crossroads</a>".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> first came on to the national scene in the late 1980s with the violent and disturbing stories told by the <a href="/wiki/Geto_Boys" title="Geto Boys">Geto Boys</a>, with member <a href="/wiki/Scarface_(rapper)" title="Scarface (rapper)">Scarface</a> achieving major solo success in the mid-90s.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chopped_and_Screwed" title="Chopped and Screwed">Chopped and Screwed</a> genre was developed in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas which remains the location most associated with the style. The late <a href="/wiki/DJ_Screw" title="DJ Screw">DJ Screw</a>, a South Houston DJ, is credited with the creation of and early experimentation with the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> DJ Screw began making mixtapes of the slowed-down music in the early 1990s and began the <a href="/wiki/Screwed_Up_Click" title="Screwed Up Click">Screwed Up Click</a>. This provided a significant outlet for MCs in the South-Houston area, and helped local rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Big_Moe" title="Big Moe">Big Moe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Flip" title="Lil' Flip">Lil' Flip</a>, <a href="/wiki/E.S.G." title="E.S.G.">E.S.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/UGK" title="UGK">UGK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Keke" title="Lil' Keke">Lil' Keke</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Park_Mexican" title="South Park Mexican">South Park Mexican</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice_1" title="Spice 1">Spice 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Z-Ro" title="Z-Ro">Z-Ro</a> gain regional and sometimes national prominence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narco-rap">Narco-rap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Narco-rap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Narco-rap is a music scene, similar to the early underground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangsta-rap" title="Gangsta-rap">gangsta-rap</a> scene, that emerged in northeastern Mexico and southern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Its lyrical content, popular among Latino youth, is violent and focuses on the power of drug cartels and the gruesomeness of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Drug_War" title="Mexican Drug War">drug war</a> in the border region. Narco-rap emerged in the urban area of Tamaulipas, a turf currently under armed dispute between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Zetas" title="The Zetas">the Zetas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Cartel" title="Gulf Cartel">Gulf Cartel</a>. Narco-rappers sing about the life of mobsters and the reality of the cities under the cartel's rule. Some of the key players of the genre are <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Flores_Borrego#Narco-Rap_legacy" title="Samuel Flores Borrego">Cano y Blunt</a>, DemenT and Big Los.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainstream_rap">Mainstream rap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mainstream rap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the late 1990s, gangsta rap, while a huge-selling genre, had been regarded as well outside of the pop mainstream, committed to representing the experience of the inner-city and not "selling out" to the pop charts. However, the rise of Bad Boy Records, propelled by the massive crossover success of Bad Boy head <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_%22Puffy%22_Combs" title='Sean "Puffy" Combs'>Sean "Puffy" Combs</a>'s 1997 ensemble album, <i><a href="/wiki/No_Way_Out_(album)" title="No Way Out (album)">No Way Out</a></i>, on the heels of the media attention generated by the murders of <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, signaled a major stylistic change in gangsta rap (or as it is referred to on the East Coast, hardcore rap), as it morphed into a new subgenre of hip hop which would become even more commercially successful and popularly accepted.</p>
<p>The earlier, somewhat controversial crossover success enjoyed by popular gangsta rap songs like "<a href="/wiki/Gin_and_Juice" title="Gin and Juice">Gin and Juice</a>" gave way to gangsta rap's becoming a widely accepted staple on the pop charts in the late 1990s. For example, between the release of The Notorious B.I.G.'s debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i> in 1994 and his follow-up, the posthumous <i><a href="/wiki/Life_After_Death" title="Life After Death">Life After Death</a></i> in 1997, his sound changed from a darker, tense production, with lyrics projecting desperation and paranoia, to a cleaner, more laid-back sound, fashioned for popular consumption (though the references to guns, drug dealing and life as a thug on the street remained).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>-styled hooks and instantly recognizable samples of well-known <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> and pop songs from the 1970s and 1980s were the staples of this sound, which was showcased primarily in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_%22Puffy%22_Combs" title='Sean "Puffy" Combs'>Sean "Puffy" Combs</a>'s latter-day production work for The Notorious B.I.G. ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mo_Money,_Mo_Problems" title="Mo Money, Mo Problems">Mo Money, Mo Problems</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Mase" title="Mase">Mase</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Feel_So_Good_(song)" title="Feel So Good (song)">Feels So Good</a>"), and non-Bad Boy artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Can_I_Get_A..." title="Can I Get A...">Can I Get A...</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Street_Dreams_(song)" title="Street Dreams (song)">Street Dreams</a>"). Also achieving similar levels of success with a similar sound at the same time as Bad Boy was <a href="/wiki/Master_P" title="Master P">Master P</a> and his No Limit label in New Orleans, as well as the New Orleans upstart Cash Money label. While Souljah Boy sings in his raps, he has had some hard-hitting records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the artists who achieved such mainstream success in the 2000s, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a>, then <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> and <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>, originated from the gritty 1990s East Coast rap scene and were influenced by hardcore artists such as <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mase" title="Mase">Mase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam'ron">Cam'ron</a> were typical of a more relaxed, casual flow that became the pop-gangsta norm. By contrast, other rappers like <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> and <a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a> enjoyed commercial success in the late 1990s by rapping about ever-more macabre tales of death and violence, maintaining commercial relevance by attempting to be controversial and subversive, growing on the <a href="/wiki/Horrorcore" title="Horrorcore">Horrorcore</a> rap style born in the late 1980s [Insane Poetry - 12' Strokes Til Midnight 12" 1988] to early 1990's [Esham - Boomin' Words From Hell aka "Boomin' '90."].</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_debate">Criticism and debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta_rap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism and debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The explicit nature of gangsta rap's lyrics has made it heavily controversial. There is also debate about the relationship between gangsta rap and real-world crime taking the form of a <a href="/wiki/Chicken_or_the_egg" title="Chicken or the egg">chicken or the egg</a> scenario.</p>
<p>Critics of gangsta rap hold that it glorifies and encourages criminal behavior, and may be at least partially to blame for the problem of street gangs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Although this view is often stereotyped as that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_American" title="White American">white</a> <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatives</a>, it has been shared by members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">black</a> community, most notably <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cosby" title="Bill Cosby">Bill Cosby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Those who are supportive or at least less critical of gangsta rap hold that crime on the street level is for the most part a reaction to <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and that gangsta rap reflects the reality of lower class life. Many believe that the blaming of crime on gangsta rap is a form of unwarranted <a href="/wiki/Moral_panic" title="Moral panic">moral panic</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_World_Development_Report_2011" title="The World Development Report 2011">The World Development Report 2011</a>, for instance, confirmed that most street gang members maintain that poverty and unemployment is what drove them to crime; none made reference to music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Cube_(rapper)" title="Ice Cube (rapper)">Ice Cube</a> famously satirized the blame placed on gangsta rap for social ills in his song "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_Rap_Made_Me_Do_It" title="Gangsta Rap Made Me Do It">Gangsta Rap Made Me Do It</a>".</p>
<p>Moreover, English scholar Ronald A.T. Judy has argued that gangsta rap reflects the experience of blackness at the end of political economy, when capital is no longer wholly produced by human labor but in a globalized system of commodities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In this economy, gangsta rap traffics blackness as a commodifiable affect of "being a nigga."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In other words, gangsta rap defines the experience of blackness, in which he locates in gangsta rap's deployment of the word "nigga," in this new global economic system as "adaptation to the force of commodification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> For Judy, <i>nigga</i> (and gangsta rap) becomes an ontologically authentic category for describing the condition of being black in the modern "realm of things."</p>
<p>Despite this, many who hold that gangsta rap is not responsible for social ills are critical of the way many gangsta rappers intentionally exaggerate their criminal pasts for the sake of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_credibility" title="Street credibility">street credibility</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rick_Ross_(rapper)" title="Rick Ross (rapper)">Rick Ross</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Slim_Jesus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Slim Jesus (page does not exist)">Slim Jesus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> among others have been heavily criticized for this.</p>
