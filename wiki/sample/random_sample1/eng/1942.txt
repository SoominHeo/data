<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Great Attractor</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Great Attractor</b> is a gravitational anomaly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intergalactic_space" title="Intergalactic space">intergalactic space</a> at the center of the <a href="/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster" title="Laniakea Supercluster">Laniakea Supercluster</a> that reveals the existence of a localised concentration of mass tens of thousands of times more massive than the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. This mass is observable by its effect on the motion of galaxies and their associated clusters over a region hundreds of millions of <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> across. The Great Attractor is moving towards the <a href="/wiki/Shapley_Supercluster" title="Shapley Supercluster">Shapley Supercluster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>These galaxies are all <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifted</a>, in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Flow" title="Hubble Flow">Hubble Flow</a>, indicating that they are receding relative to us and to each other, but the variations in their redshift are sufficient to reveal the existence of the anomaly. The variations in their redshifts are known as <a href="/wiki/Peculiar_velocity" title="Peculiar velocity">peculiar velocities</a>, and cover a range from about +700 km/s to −700 km/s, depending on the angular deviation from the direction to the Great Attractor.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first indications of a deviation from uniform expansion of the universe were reported in 1973 and again in 1978. The location of the Great Attractor was finally determined in 1986: it is situated at a distance of somewhere between 150 and 250 Mly (million light years) (47–79 M<a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">pc</a>) (the latter being the most recent estimate) from the Milky Way, in the direction of the constellations <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Australe" title="Triangulum Australe">Triangulum Australe</a> (The Southern Triangle) and <a href="/wiki/Norma_(constellation)" title="Norma (constellation)">Norma</a> (The Carpenter’s Square).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While objects in that direction lie in the <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance" title="Zone of Avoidance">Zone of Avoidance</a> (the part of the night sky obscured by the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way galaxy</a>) and are thus difficult to study with visible wavelengths, X-ray observations have revealed that the region of space is dominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norma_cluster" title="Norma cluster">Norma cluster (ACO 3627)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a massive cluster of galaxies containing a preponderance of large, old galaxies, many of which are colliding with their neighbours and/or radiating large amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_over_apparent_mass">Debate over apparent mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Debate over apparent mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992, much of the apparent signal of the Great Attractor was attributed to the effect of <a href="/wiki/Malmquist_bias" title="Malmquist bias">Malmquist bias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2005, astronomers conducting an X-ray survey of part of the sky known as the Clusters in the <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance" title="Zone of Avoidance">Zone of Avoidance</a> (CIZA) project reported that the Great Attractor was actually only one tenth the mass that scientists had originally estimated. The survey also confirmed earlier theories that the Milky Way galaxy is in fact being pulled towards a much more massive cluster of galaxies near the <a href="/wiki/Shapley_Supercluster" title="Shapley Supercluster">Shapley Supercluster</a>, which lies beyond the Great Attractor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_flow">Dark flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dark flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dark_flow" title="Dark flow">Dark flow</a></div>
<p>"Dark flow" is an <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> term that describes a possible non-random component of the <a href="/wiki/Peculiar_velocity" title="Peculiar velocity">peculiar velocity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a>. The measured velocity is the sum of that predicted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_Law" title="Hubble's Law">Hubble's Law</a> added to a possible small, unexplained, "dark" velocity that flows in a direction common to the galaxy clusters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laniakea_Supercluster">Laniakea Supercluster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Laniakea Supercluster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The proposed <a href="/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster" title="Laniakea Supercluster">Laniakea Supercluster</a> is defined as the Great Attractor's basin, encompassing the former superclusters of Virgo and Hydra-Centaurus. Thus the Great Attractor would be the core of the new supercluster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
