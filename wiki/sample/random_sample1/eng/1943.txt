<p><b>Megathrust earthquakes</b> occur at <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction zones</a> at destructive <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent plate boundaries</a>, where one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> is forced underneath another. These <a href="/wiki/Interplate_earthquake" title="Interplate earthquake">interplate earthquakes</a> are the planet's most powerful, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude" title="Moment magnitude">moment magnitudes</a> (<i>M<sub>w</sub></i>) that can exceed 9.0. Since 1900, all <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> of magnitude 9.0 or greater have been megathrust earthquakes. No other type of known terrestrial source of tectonic activity has produced earthquakes of this scale.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megathrust_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the rupture, one side of the fault is pushed upwards relative to the other, and it is this type of movement that is known as <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThrustDefinition_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ThrustDefinition-1">[1]</a></sup> They are a type of <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Dip-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">dip-slip fault</a>. A thrust fault is a reverse fault with a dip of 45° or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DipSlipDefinition_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DipSlipDefinition-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Oblique-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">Oblique-slip faults</a> have significant components of different slip styles. The term <i>megathrust</i> does not have a widely accepted rigorous definition, but is used to refer to an extremely large thrust fault, typically formed at the plate interface along a subduction zone such as the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_megathrust" title="Sunda megathrust">Sunda megathrust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ParkButler2005_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ParkButler2005-3">[3]</a></sup> It is mostly American terminology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas">Areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megathrust_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The major subduction zone is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Oceans</a> and is responsible for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> activity associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire" title="Pacific Ring of Fire">Pacific Ring of Fire</a>. Since these earthquakes deform the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_floor" title="Ocean floor">ocean floor</a>, they often generate a significant series of <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> waves. They are known to produce intense shaking for periods of time that can last for up to a few minutes.</p>
<p>In Japan, the Nankai megathrust under the <a href="/wiki/Nankai_Trough" title="Nankai Trough">Nankai Trough</a> is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Nankai_megathrust_earthquakes" title="Nankai megathrust earthquakes">Nankai megathrust earthquakes</a> and associated tsunamis.</p>
<p>A study reported in 2016 found that the largest megathrust quakes are associated with downgoing slabs with the shallowest dip, so-called "flat slab subduction". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
