<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Veto</h1>

<p>A <b>veto</b> – Latin for "I forbid" – is the power (used by an officer of the state, for example) to unilaterally stop an official action, especially the enactment of legislation. A veto can be absolute, as for instance in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_veto_power" title="United Nations Security Council veto power">United Nations Security Council</a>, whose permanent members (China, France, Russia, United Kingdom, United States of America) can block any resolution. Or it can be limited, as in the legislative process of the United States, where a two-thirds vote in both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> may <b>override</b> a Presidential veto of legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A veto gives power only to stop changes, not to adopt them (except for the rare "amendatory veto"). Thus a veto allows its holder to protect the <a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">status quo</a>.</p>
<p>The concept of a veto body originated with the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consuls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tribune" title="Tribune">tribunes</a>. Either of the two consuls holding office in a given year could block a military or civil decision by the other; any tribune had the power to unilaterally block legislation passed by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitzer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_veto">Roman veto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roman veto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The institution of the veto, known to the Romans as the <i>intercessio</i>, was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> in the 6th century BC to enable the tribunes to protect the interests of the <a href="/wiki/Plebs" title="Plebs">plebs</a> (common citizenry) from the encroachments of the <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(ancient_Rome)" title="Patrician (ancient Rome)">patricians</a>, who dominated the Senate. A tribune's veto did not prevent the senate from passing a bill, but meant that it was denied the force of law. The tribunes could also use the veto to prevent a bill from being brought before the plebeian assembly. The consuls also had the power of veto, as decision-making generally required the assent of both consuls. If one disagreed, either could invoke the <i>intercessio</i> to block the action of the other. The veto was an essential component of the Roman conception of power being wielded not only to manage state affairs but to moderate and restrict the power of the state's high officials and institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitzer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spitzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Westminster_systems">Westminster systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Westminster systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Westminster_system" title="Westminster system">Westminster systems</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchies</a>, the power to veto legislation by withholding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> is a rarely used <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power" title="Reserve power">reserve power</a> of the monarch. In practice, the Crown follows the convention of exercising its prerogative on the advice of its chief advisor, the prime minister.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster</a> (1931), the United Kingdom Parliament may not repeal any Act of the Parliament of the Commonwealth of Australia on the grounds that is repugnant to the laws and interests of the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.au_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov.au-3">[3]</a></sup> Other countries in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> (not to be confused with the Commonwealth of Australia), such as Canada and New Zealand, are likewise affected. However, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Constitution" title="Australian Constitution">Australian Constitution</a> (sec. 59), the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen</a> may veto a bill that has been given royal assent by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor-General" title="Governor-General">Governor-General</a> within one year of the legislation being assented to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.au_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gov.au-3">[3]</a></sup> This power has never been used. The Australian Governor-General himself or herself has, in theory, power to veto, or more technically, withhold assent to, a bill passed by both houses of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Parliament" title="Australian Parliament">Australian Parliament</a>, and contrary to the advice of the prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This may be done without consulting the sovereign as per Section 58 of the constitution:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When a proposed law passed by both Houses of the Parliament is presented to the Governor-General for the Queen's assent, he shall declare, according to his discretion, but subject to this Constitution, that he assents in the Queen's name, or that he withholds assent, or that he reserves the law for the Queen's pleasure. The Governor-General may return to the house in which it originated any proposed law so presented to him, and may transmit therewith any amendments which he may recommend, and the Houses may deal with the recommendation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power" title="Reserve power">reserve power</a> is however, constitutionally arguable, and it is difficult to foresee an occasion when such a power would need to be exercised. It is possible that a Governor-general might so act if a bill passed by the Parliament was in violation of the Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One might argue, however, that a government would be hardly likely to present a bill which is so open to rejection. Many of the viceregal reserve powers are untested, because of the brief constitutional history of the Commonwealth of Australia, and the observance of the convention that the head of state acts upon the advice of his or her chief minister. The power may also be used in a situation where the parliament, usually a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>, passes a bill without the blessing of the executive. The governor general on the advice of the executive could withhold consent from the bill thereby preventing its passage into law.</p>
<p>With regard to the six governors of the states which are federated under the Australian Commonwealth, a somewhat different situation exists. Until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia_Act" title="Australia Act">Australia Act</a> 1986, each state was constitutionally dependent upon the British Crown directly. Since 1986, however, they are fully independent entities, although the Queen still appoints governors on the advice of the state head of government, the <a href="/wiki/Premier" title="Premier">premier</a>. So the Crown may not veto (nor the UK Parliament overturn) any act of a state governor or state legislature. Paradoxically, the states are more independent of the Crown than the federal government and legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> State constitutions determine what role a governor plays. In general the governor exercises the powers the sovereign would have, including the power to withhold the Royal Assent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Canada" title="Constitution of Canada">Constitution Act, 1867</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General of Canada</a> may veto a bill by refusing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a>. If the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a> withholds the Queen's assent, the sovereign may within two years disallow the bill, thereby vetoing the law in question. However, this power has never been used.</p>
<p>Provincial viceroys, called "<a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_governor_(Canada)" title="Lieutenant governor (Canada)">Lieutenant Governors</a>" (plural), however are able to reserve Royal Assent to provincial bills for the governor general; this clause was last invoked in 1961 by the Lieutenant Governor of Saskatchewan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/President_of_India#Important_presidential_interventions_in_the_past" title="President of India">President of India § Important presidential interventions in the past</a></div>
<p>In India, the president has three veto powers i.e. absolute, suspension &amp; pocket. The president can send the bill back to parliament for changes, which constitutes a limited veto that can be overridden by a simple majority. But the Bill reconsidered by the parliament becomes a law with or without the assents of President after 14 days. The president can also take no action indefinitely on a bill, sometimes referred to as a pocket veto.The president can refuse to assent, which constitutes an absolute veto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_india_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-intro_india-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-india_times_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-india_times-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Spain, Section 91 of the Constitution provides that the King shall give his assent to laws passed by the General Courts within 15 days after their final passing by them. The absence of the royal assent, although not constitutionally provided<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, would mean the bill did not become a part of the law. Section 90 of the Constitution states that "Within two months after receiving the text, the Senate may, by a message stating the reasons for it, adopt a veto or approve amendments thereto. The veto must be adopted by overall majority."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the royal veto ("withholding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a>") was last exercised in 1708 by <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Militia_Bill_1708" title="Scottish Militia Bill 1708">Scottish Militia Bill 1708</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> used to have an effective power of veto by refusing to concur in bills adopted by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons" title="House of Commons">House of Commons</a>. However, reform first by a Liberal government and then by a Labour government has limited its powers. The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Acts of 1911 and 1949</a> reduced its powers: they can now only amend and delay legislation. They can delay legislation for up to one year. Under the 1911 Act, money bills (those concerning finance) cannot be delayed, and under the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Convention" title="Salisbury Convention">Salisbury Convention</a>, the Lords cannot delay any bills set out in the governing party's manifesto.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="According_to_the_Constitution">According to the Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: According to the Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_presidential_vetoes" title="List of United States presidential vetoes">List of United States presidential vetoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line-item_veto_in_the_United_States" title="Line-item veto in the United States">Line-item veto in the United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pocket_veto" title="Pocket veto">Pocket veto</a></div>
<p>All legislation passed by both houses of Congress must be presented to the President. This presentation is in the President's capacity as Head of State.</p>
<p>If the President approves of the legislation, then the President signs it into law. According to <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution#Section_7:_Bills" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article 1. Section 7</a> of the Constitution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> when the President chooses not to sign or does not approve of the bill, the President must return the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" title="Bill (proposed law)">bill</a>, unsigned, within ten days, excluding Sundays, to the house of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> in which it originated, while the Congress is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_session" title="wikt:in session">in session</a>. The President is constitutionally required to state any objections to the bill in writing, and the Congress is constitutionally required to consider them, and to reconsider the legislation. This action, in effect, is a veto.</p>
<p>If the Congress overrides the veto by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-thirds_majority" title="Two-thirds majority">two-thirds vote</a> in each house, it becomes law without the President's signature. Otherwise, the bill fails to become law unless it is presented to the President again and the President chooses to sign it. Historically, the Congress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veto_override" title="Veto override">overrides</a> the Presidential veto less than 10% of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_Senate_Glossary_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_Senate_Glossary-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A bill can also become law without the President's signature if, after its presentment, the President simply fails to sign it within the ten days noted. If there are fewer than ten days left in the session before Congress <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adjourn" title="wikt:adjourn">adjourns</a>, and if Congress does so <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adjourn" title="wikt:adjourn">adjourn</a> before the ten days have expired in which the President might sign the bill, then the bill fails to become law. This procedure, when used as a formal device, is called a <a href="/wiki/Pocket_veto" title="Pocket veto">pocket veto</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modifications_declared_unconstitutional">Modifications declared unconstitutional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modifications declared unconstitutional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1983, the Supreme Court had struck down the one-house <a href="/wiki/Legislative_veto" title="Legislative veto">legislative veto</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a> grounds and on grounds that the action by one house of Congress violated the Constitutional requirement of bicameralism. The case was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INS_v._Chadha" title="INS v. Chadha">INS v. Chadha</a></i>, concerning a foreign exchange student in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> who had been born in Kenya but whose parents were from India. Because he was not born in India, he was not an Indian citizen. Because his parents were not Kenyan citizens, he was not Kenyan. Thus, he <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stateless_person" title="Stateless person">had nowhere to go</a> when his student visa expired because neither country would take him, so he overstayed his visa and was ordered to show cause why he should not be deported from the United States.</p>
<p>The Immigration and Nationality Act was one of many acts of Congress passed since the 1930s, which contained a provision allowing either house of that legislature to nullify decisions of agencies in the executive branch simply by passing a resolution. In this case, Chadha's deportation was suspended and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> passed a resolution overturning the suspension, so that the deportation proceedings would continue. This, the Court held, amounted to the House of Representatives passing legislation without the concurrence of the Senate, and without presenting the legislation to the President for consideration and approval (or veto). Thus, the Constitutional principle of bicameralism and the separation of powers doctrine were disregarded in this case, and this legislative veto of executive decisions was struck down.</p>
<p>In 1996, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> passed, and President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> signed, the <a href="/wiki/Line_Item_Veto_Act_of_1996" title="Line Item Veto Act of 1996">Line Item Veto Act of 1996</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">act</a> allowed the President to veto individual items of budgeted expenditures from appropriations bills instead of vetoing the entire bill and sending it back to the Congress. However, this <a href="/wiki/Line-item_veto" title="Line-item veto">line-item veto</a> was immediately challenged by members of Congress who disagreed with it. In 1998, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">Supreme Court</a> declared the line-item veto unconstitutional. The Court found the language of the Constitution required each bill presented to the President to be either approved or rejected as a whole. An action by which the President might pick and choose which parts of the bill to approve or not approve amounted to the President acting as a legislator instead of an executive and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>—and particularly as a single legislator acting in place of the entire Congress—thereby violating the <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a> doctrine. (See <i><a href="/wiki/Clinton_v._City_of_New_York" title="Clinton v. City of New York">Clinton v. City of New York</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_524" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 524">524</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">U.S.</a> <a class="external text" href="https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/524/417/" rel="nofollow">417</a> (1998).)</p>
<p>In 2006, Senator <a href="/wiki/Bill_Frist" title="Bill Frist">Bill Frist</a> introduced the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Legislative_Line_Item_Veto_Act_of_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Legislative Line Item Veto Act of 2006 (page does not exist)">Legislative Line Item Veto Act of 2006</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>. Rather than provide for an actual legislative veto, however, the procedure created by the Act provides that, if the President should recommend rescission of a budgetary line item from a budget bill he previously signed into law—a power he already possesses pursuant to U.S. Const. Art. II—the Congress must vote on his request within ten days. Because the legislation that is the subject of the President's request (or "Special Message", in the language of the bill) was already enacted and signed into law, the vote by the Congress would be ordinary legislative action, not any kind of veto—whether line-item, legislative or any other sort. The House passed this measure, but the Senate never considered it, so the bill expired and never became law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Intent_of_the_Framers">Intent of the Framers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Intent of the Framers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">Constitutional Convention</a>, the veto was routinely referred to as a 'revisionary power'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Veto was constructed not as an absolute veto, but rather with limits. Such as that Congress can override a veto, and that a President must state their objections in writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These limits would have been important in the minds of the Founders, given that in Britain the monarch retained an absolute veto (though by this time the power had become a formality). Further, as <a href="/wiki/Elbridge_Gerry" title="Elbridge Gerry">Elbridge Gerry</a> explained in the final days of the Convention: "The primary object of the revisionary check of the President is not to protect the general interest, but to defend his own department."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Constitutional Convention the framers overwhelmingly rejected three proposals for an absolute veto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_the_Articles_and_Constitution">Under the Articles and Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Under the Articles and Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Continental_Congress" title="President of the Continental Congress">Presidents</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> (1774–81) did not have the power of veto. The President could not veto an act of Congress under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a> (1781–89), but he possessed certain recess and reserve powers that were not necessarily available to the predecessor President of the Continental Congress. It was only with the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> (drafted 1787; ratified 1788; fully effective since 4 March 1789) that veto power was conferred upon the person titled "President of the United States".</p>
<p>The presidential veto power was first exercised on 5 April 1792<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> when President <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> vetoed a bill outlining a new apportionment formula submitted by then Secretary of State Thomas Jefferson. Apportionment described how Congress divides seats in the House of Representatives among the states based on the US census figures. President Washington thought the bill gave an unfair advantage to the northern states.</p>
<p>The Congress first overrode a presidential veto (passed a bill into law notwithstanding the President's objections) on 3 March 1845.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veto_history_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-veto_history-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._states.2C_veto_powers.2C_and_override_authority">U.S. states, veto powers, and override authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: U.S. states, veto powers, and override authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All U.S. states also have a provision by which legislative decisions can be vetoed by the governor. In addition to the ability to veto an entire bill as a "package," many states allow the governor to exercise specialty veto authority to strike or revise portions of a bill without striking the whole bill.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Amendatory veto</dt>
<dd>Allows a governor to amend bills that have been passed by the legislature. Revisions are subject to confirmation or rejection by the legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vock_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vock-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Line item veto</dt>
<dd>Allows a governor to remove specific sections of a bill (usually only spending bills) that has been passed by the legislature. Deletions can be overridden by the legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vock_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vock-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Pocket veto</dt>
<dd>Any bill presented to a governor after a session has ended must be signed to become law. A governor can refuse to sign such a bill and it will expire. Such vetoes cannot be overridden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vock_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vock-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Reduction veto</dt>
<dd>Allows a governor to reduce the amounts budgeted for spending items. Reductions can be overridden by the legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vock_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vock-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Package veto</dt>
<dd>Allows a governor to veto the entire bill. Package vetoes can be overridden by the legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vock_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Vock-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_republican_systems">European republican systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: European republican systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_veto">Presidential veto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Presidential veto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many European republics allow some form of presidential veto on legislation, which may vary, according to their constitutional form or by convention. These include France, Italy, Portugal, the Republic of Ireland, Latvia, Lithuania, Ukraine, and Hungary.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Austria" title="President of Austria">President of Austria</a> has no veto power, but signs bills into law.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Iceland" title="President of Iceland">President of Iceland</a> may refuse to sign a bill, which is then put to universal adult <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">suffrage</a>. This right was not exercised until 2004, by President <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lafur_Ragnar_Gr%C3%ADmsson" title="Ólafur Ragnar Grímsson">Ólafur Ragnar Grímsson</a>, who has since refused to sign two other bills. The first bill was withdrawn, but the latter two resulted in referenda.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">President of France</a> has only a very limited form of suspensive veto: when presented with a law, he or she can request another reading of it by the Assembly, but only once per law. Aside from it, the President can only refer bills to the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Council_(France)" title="Constitutional Council (France)">Constitutional Council</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_Hungary" title="President of Hungary">President of Hungary</a> has two options to veto a bill: submit it to the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Hungary" title="Constitutional Court of Hungary">Constitutional Court</a> if he suspects that it violates the constitution or send it back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Hungary" title="National Assembly of Hungary">Parliament</a> and ask for a second debate and vote on the bill. If the Court rules that the bill is constitutional or it is passed by the Parliament again, respectively, the President must sign it.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> may refuse to grant assent to a bill that he or she considers to be unconstitutional, after consulting the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Council of State (Ireland)">Council of State</a>; in this case, the bill is referred to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Ireland" title="Supreme Court of Ireland">Supreme Court</a>, which finally determines the matter. This is the most widely used reserve power. The President may also, on request of a majority of <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad Éireann</a> (the upper house of parliament) and a third of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a> (the lower house of parliament), after consulting the Council of State, decline to sign a bill "of such national importance that the will of the people thereon ought to be ascertained" in an <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_referendum" title="Ordinary referendum">ordinary referendum</a> or a new Dáil reassembling after a general election held within eighteen months. This latter power has never been used because the government of the day almost always commands a majority of the Seanad, preventing the third of the Dáil that usually makes up the opposition from combining with it.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Italy" title="President of Italy">President of Italy</a> may request a second deliberation of a bill passed by Parliament before it is promulgated. This is a very weak form of veto as the Parliament can override the veto by an ordinary majority. The same provision exists in France and Latvia. While such a limited veto cannot thwart the will of a determined parliamentary majority, it may have a delaying effect and may cause the parliamentary majority to reconsider the matter. The President of Republic can also call a new election for parliament.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Estonia" title="President of Estonia">President of Estonia</a> may effectively veto a law adopted by <a href="/wiki/Riigikogu" title="Riigikogu">Estonian parliament</a> by refusing to proclaim it and demanding a new debate and decision. The parliament, in its turn, may override this veto by passing the law unamended for the second time (a simple majority is enough). In this case the President is obliged to proclaim the law or to request the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Estonia" title="Supreme Court of Estonia">Supreme Court of Estonia</a> to declare the law unconstitutional. If the Supreme Court rules that the law does not violate the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Estonia" title="Constitution of Estonia">Constitution</a>, the President may not object any more and is obliged to finally proclaim the law.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Latvia" title="President of Latvia">President of Latvia</a> may suspend a bill for a period of two months, during which it may be referred to the people in a referendum if a certain number of signatures are gathered. This is potentially a much stronger form of veto, as it enables the President to appeal to the people against the wishes of the Parliament and Government.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Poland" title="President of Poland">President of Poland</a> may submit a bill to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Tribunal_of_the_Republic_of_Poland" title="Constitutional Tribunal of the Republic of Poland">Constitutional Tribunal</a> if he suspects that bill is unconstitutional or send it back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_of_the_Republic_of_Poland" title="Sejm of the Republic of Poland">Sejm</a> for a second voting. If the Tribunal says that the bill is constitutional or if Sejm passes it by at least three-fifths of the votes, the President must sign the bill.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">President of Portugal</a> may refuse to sign a bill or refer it, or parts of it, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitutional_Court" title="Portuguese Constitutional Court">Constitutional Court</a>. If the President refuses to sign bill without it being declared unconstitutional, the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Assembly of the Republic</a> (parliament) may pass it again, in which case it becomes law.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ukraine" title="President of Ukraine">President of Ukraine</a> may refuse to sign a bill and return it to Parliament with his proposals. If the parliament agrees on his proposals, the President must sign the bill. Parliament may overturn a veto by a two-thirds majority. If Parliament overturns his veto, the President must sign the bill within 10 days.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberum_veto">Liberum veto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Liberum veto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the constitution Poland or the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> in the 17th and 18th centuries, there was an institution called the <a href="/wiki/Liberum_veto" title="Liberum veto">liberum veto</a>. All bills had to pass the <i>Sejm</i> or "Seimas" (Parliament) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unanimous" title="Unanimous">unanimous</a> consent, and if any legislator voted <i>nay</i> on anything, this not only vetoed that bill but also dissolved that legislative session itself. The concept originated in the idea of "Polish democracy" as any Pole of noble extraction was considered as good as any other, no matter how low or high his material condition might be. It was never exercised, however, under the rule of the strong Polish royal dynasties, which came to an end in the mid-17th century. These were followed by an elective kingship. As might be expected, the more and more frequent use of this veto power paralyzed the power of the legislature and, combined with a string of weak figurehead kings, led ultimately to the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">partitioning and the dissolution of the Polish state</a> in the late 18th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">President of the Philippines</a> may refuse to sign a bill, sending the bill back to the house where it originated along with his objections. <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Philippines" title="Congress of the Philippines">Congress</a> can override the veto via a two-thirds vote with both houses voting separately, after which the bill becomes law. The president may also veto specific provisions on <a href="/wiki/Money_bill" title="Money bill">money bills</a> without affecting other provisions on the same bill. The president cannot veto a bill due to inaction; once the bill has been received by the president, the chief executive has thirty days to veto the bill. Once the thirty-day period expires, the bill becomes law as if the president had signed it.</p>
