<p><b>Gertrude Lawrence</b> (4 July 1898 – 6 September 1952) was an English actress, singer, dancer and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_comedy" title="Musical comedy">musical comedy</a> performer known for her stage appearances in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_End_Theatre" title="West End Theatre">West End</a> of London and on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> in New York.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lawrence was born <b>Gertrude Alice Dagmar Klasen</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BetzBetz2002_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BetzBetz2002-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Alexandre Dagmar Lawrence-Klasen</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowie2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowie2002-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>Gertrude Alexandra Dagmar Klasen</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BetzBetz2002_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BetzBetz2002-1">[1]</a></sup> or some variant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clarke_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-3">[3]</a></sup> (sources differ), of English and <a href="/wiki/Danes" title="Danes">Danish</a> extraction, in the <a href="/wiki/Newington,_London" title="Newington, London">Newington</a> area of <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Southwark" title="London Borough of Southwark">London Borough of Southwark</a>. Her father was a <a href="/wiki/Bass_(voice_type)" title="Bass (voice type)">basso profundo</a> who performed under the name Arthur Lawrence. His heavy drinking led her mother Alice to leave him soon after Gertrude's birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1904, her stepfather took the family to <a href="/wiki/Bognor_Regis" title="Bognor Regis">Bognor</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> coast for the August <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a>. While there, they attended a concert where audience members were invited to entertain. At her mother's urging, young Gertrude sang a song and was rewarded with a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_(British_coin)" title="Sovereign (British coin)">gold sovereign</a> for her effort. It was her first public performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1908, to augment the family's meagre income, Alice accepted a job in the chorus of the Christmas <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a> at <a href="/wiki/Brixton" title="Brixton">Brixton</a> Theatre. A child who could sing and dance was needed to round out the troupe, and Alice volunteered her daughter. While working in the production Alice heard of <a href="/wiki/Italia_Conti_Academy_of_Theatre_Arts" title="Italia Conti Academy of Theatre Arts">Italia Conti</a>, who taught dance, elocution and the rudiments of acting. Gertrude auditioned for Conti, who thought the child was talented enough to warrant free lessons.</p>
<p>Lawrence joined Italia Conti's production of <i><a href="/wiki/Where_the_Rainbow_Ends" title="Where the Rainbow Ends">Where the Rainbow Ends</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Her training led to appearances in <a href="/wiki/Max_Reinhardt" title="Max Reinhardt">Max Reinhardt</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Miracle_(play)" title="The Miracle (play)">The Miracle</a></i> in London and <i>Fifinella</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Basil_Dean" title="Basil Dean">Basil Dean</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Playhouse" title="Liverpool Playhouse">Liverpool Repertory Theatre</a>. At some point during this period, the child decided to adopt her father's professional surname as her own. Dean then cast her in his next production, <a href="/wiki/Gerhart_Hauptmann" title="Gerhart Hauptmann">Gerhart Hauptmann</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Assumption_of_Hannele" title="The Assumption of Hannele">Hannele</a></i>, where she first met <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a>. Their meeting was the start of a close and sometimes tempestuous friendship and the most important professional relationship in both their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_stage_career">Early stage career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early stage career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following <i>Hannele</i>, Lawrence reconnected with her father, who was living with a chorus girl. They agreed to let her tour with them in two successive <a href="/wiki/Revue" title="Revue">revues</a>, after which Arthur announced he had signed a year-long contract with a variety show in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, leaving the two young women to fend for themselves. Lawrence, now aged sixteen, opted to live at the Theatrical Girls' Club in <a href="/wiki/Soho" title="Soho">Soho</a> rather than return to her mother and stepfather.</p>
<p>She worked steadily with various touring companies until 1916, when she was hired by famed <a href="/wiki/Impresario" title="Impresario">impresario</a> <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Charlot" title="André Charlot">André Charlot</a> to <a href="/wiki/Understudy" title="Understudy">understudy</a> <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Lillie" title="Beatrice Lillie">Beatrice Lillie</a> and appear in the chorus of his latest production in London's West End.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> When it closed, she assumed Lillie's role on tour, then returned to London once again to understudy the star in another Charlot production, where she met dance director Francis Gordon-Howley. Although he was twenty years her senior, the two wed and soon after had a daughter Pamela, Lawrence's only child. The marriage was not a success, and Lawrence took Pamela with her to her mother's home in <a href="/wiki/Clapham" title="Clapham">Clapham</a>. The couple remained separated but did not divorce until ten years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1918, Lawrence contracted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumbago" title="Lumbago">lumbago</a> and was given a fortnight to recuperate by Charlot, who then saw her at an opening night party at Ivor Novello's invitation two days before she was cleared to return to work by her doctor, and immediately sacked her. When the apparent reason for her dismissal became common knowledge among other West End producers, she was unable to find work, and in early 1919 she accepted a job singing in the show at Murray's, a popular London <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclub</a>, where she remained for the better part of the next two years. While performing there she met Captain Philip Astley, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Household_Cavalry" title="Household Cavalry">Household Cavalry</a>. He became her friend, escort, and ultimately lover, and taught her how to dress and behave in high society.</p>
<p>When Lawrence became romantically involved with <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_Manhattan" title="Financial District, Manhattan">Wall Street</a> banker Bert Taylor in 1927, Astley proposed marriage, an offer Lawrence refused because she knew Astley would expect her to leave the stage and settle in rural England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The two remained close until he married actress <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Carroll" title="Madeleine Carroll">Madeleine Carroll</a> in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When Lawrence divorced Gordon-Howley, she and Taylor became engaged and remained so for two years, with each free to enjoy a social life separate from the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the end of 1920, Lawrence left Murray's and began to ease her way back into legitimate theatre while touring in a <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> act as the partner of popular singer Walter Williams. In October 1921, Charlot asked her to replace an ailing Beatrice Lillie as star of his latest production, <i>A to Z</i>, opposite <a href="/wiki/Jack_Buchanan" title="Jack Buchanan">Jack Buchanan</a>. In it the two introduced the song "<a href="/wiki/Limehouse_Blues_(song)" title="Limehouse Blues (song)">Limehouse Blues</a>," which went on to become one of Lawrence's signature tunes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1923, <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a> developed his first musical revue, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Calling!" title="London Calling!">London Calling!</a></i>, specifically for Lawrence. Charlot agreed to produce it, but brought in more experienced writers and composers to work on the book and score. One of Coward's surviving songs was "Parisian Pierrot", a tune that would be identified closely with Lawrence throughout her career.</p>
<p>The show's success led its producer to create <i>André Charlot's London Revue of 1924</i>, which he took to Broadway with Lawrence, Lillie, Buchanan and <a href="/wiki/Constance_Carpenter" title="Constance Carpenter">Constance Carpenter</a>. It was so successful it moved to a larger theatre to accommodate the demand for tickets and extended its run. After it closed, the show toured the US and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, although Lawrence was forced to leave the cast when she contracted <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">double pneumonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pleurisy" title="Pleurisy">pleurisy</a> and was forced to spend fourteen weeks in a <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> hospital recuperating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Charlot's Revue of 1926</i>, starring Lawrence, Lillie, and Buchanan, opened on Broadway in late 1925. In his review, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Woollcott" title="Alexander Woollcott">Alexander Woollcott</a> singled out Lawrence, calling her "the personification of style and sophistication" and "the ideal star."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Like its predecessor, it toured following the Broadway run. It proved to be Lawrence's last project with Charlot. In November 1926, she became the first British performer to star in an American musical on Broadway when she opened in <i><a href="/wiki/Oh,_Kay!" title="Oh, Kay!">Oh, Kay!</a></i>, with music by <a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin</a>, lyrics by <a href="/wiki/Ira_Gershwin" title="Ira Gershwin">Ira Gershwin</a>, and a book by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Bolton" title="Guy Bolton">Guy Bolton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.G._Wodehouse" title="P.G. Wodehouse">P.G. Wodehouse</a>. Following a run of 256 performances, the musical opened in the West End, where it ran for 213 performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1928, Lawrence returned to Broadway opposite <a href="/wiki/Clifton_Webb" title="Clifton Webb">Clifton Webb</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Girl" title="Treasure Girl">Treasure Girl</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> a Gershwin work she was confident would be a huge hit. Anticipating a long run, she arrived in New York with her daughter Pamela, a personal maid and two cars, and settled into a flat on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Park Avenue (Manhattan)">Park Avenue</a>. Her instincts about the musical were wrong; audiences had difficulty accepting her as an avaricious woman who double-crosses her lover, and it ran for only 68 performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> She starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Howard_(actor)" title="Leslie Howard (actor)">Leslie Howard</a> in <i>Candle Light</i>, an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> play adapted by Wodehouse, in 1929,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and in 1931 she and Noël Coward triumphed in his play <i><a href="/wiki/Private_Lives" title="Private Lives">Private Lives</a></i>, first in the UK, and later on Broadway.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_stage_career">Later stage career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later stage career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1936, Lawrence and Coward starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Tonight_at_8:30" title="Tonight at 8:30">Tonight at 8:30</a></i>, a cycle of ten one-act plays he had written specifically for the two of them. In 1937, she appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Crothers" title="Rachel Crothers">Rachel Crothers</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/Susan_and_God" title="Susan and God">Susan and God</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and in 1939 starred in <i>Skylark</i>, a comedy by <a href="/wiki/Samson_Raphaelson" title="Samson Raphaelson">Samson Raphaelson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Lawrence felt the play needed work prior to opening on Broadway, and a run at the Cape Playhouse in <a href="/wiki/Dennis,_Massachusetts" title="Dennis, Massachusetts">Dennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> was arranged. The theatre was run by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> graduate Richard Aldrich, and he and the actress became involved in a romantic relationship. The two wed on her birthday in 1940 and remained married until her death in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-22">[22]</a></sup> They had homes in Dennis and in <a href="/wiki/Turtle_Bay,_Manhattan" title="Turtle Bay, Manhattan">Turtle Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1941, Lawrence's daughter Pamela married a New York doctor named William G. Cahan. The ceremony was held at the Dennis, Massachusetts home of Lawrence and Aldrich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Lawrence was friendly with her son-in-law but lost contact with him after his 1950 divorce from Pamela, according to Cahan's memoir that was published in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Lawrence did not have any grandchildren during her lifetime.</p>
<p>Lawrence returned to the musical stage in <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_in_the_Dark" title="Lady in the Dark">Lady in the Dark</a></i> in 1941. It originally had been planned as a play with recurrent musical themes for <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Cornell" title="Katharine Cornell">Katharine Cornell</a> by <a href="/wiki/Moss_Hart" title="Moss Hart">Moss Hart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Weill" title="Kurt Weill">Kurt Weill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ira_Gershwin" title="Ira Gershwin">Ira Gershwin</a>, but by the time the first act was completed it was clear it was very much a musical that Cornell agreed was beyond her capability as a performer. Soon after this, Hart met Lawrence at a rehearsal for a revue designed to raise funds for <a href="/wiki/British_War_Relief_Society" title="British War Relief Society">British War Relief</a>, and he offered her the role of Liza Elliott, a magazine editor undergoing <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a> to better understand why both her professional and personal lives are filled with indecision.</p>
<p>The show was very ambitious and stretched the star's talents for singing, dancing and acting. Her performance prompted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Watts,_Jr." title="Richard Watts, Jr.">Richard Watts</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald_Tribune" title="New York Herald Tribune">New York Herald Tribune</a></i> to call her "the greatest feminine performer in the American theatre," and <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Atkinson" title="Brooks Atkinson">Brooks Atkinson</a> described her as "a goddess" in his review in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>. She remained with the show throughout its Broadway run and its subsequent national tour over the next three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decades later, Ira Gershwin told American songwriting historian Sheila Davis about a contribution Lawrence made to honing the lyrics of the song "My Ship" in <i>Lady in the Dark</i>. "During a <i>Lady in the Dark</i> rehearsal," Davis wrote, "Gertrude Lawrence suddenly stopped singing midline and called out to Gershwin, who was monitoring from the orchestra, 'Why does she say 'I could wait <i>four</i> years' – why not five or six?' Of course, the line was 'I could wait <i>for</i> years.' The painstaking lyricist immediately substituted <i>the</i> to clarify the aural ambiguity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1945, Lawrence starred as <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Doolittle" title="Eliza Doolittle">Eliza Doolittle</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Massey" title="Raymond Massey">Raymond Massey</a> as Henry Higgins in a revival of <i><a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_(play)" title="Pygmalion (play)">Pygmalion</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>, who initially resisted the idea of Lawrence playing the role. Following the Broadway run, she toured the United States (including a stint in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>) and Canada in the play until May 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autobiography">Autobiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Autobiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1945, Lawrence published the <a href="/wiki/Autobiography" title="Autobiography">autobiography</a> <i>A Star Danced</i>. Her long-term friend <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a> later suggested it was a romanticised and less than wholly factual account of her life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Although Lawrence claimed the work was solely hers, many suspected her business manager and attorney <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Holtzmann" title="Fanny Holtzmann">Fanny Holtzmann</a> had written much of it. The author embarked on a cross-country tour of the United States to publicise her book, the first person ever to engage in such a promotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lawrence's second husband Richard Aldrich became a lieutenant in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, during which time Lawrence had a standing invitation to perform for British troops from the head of the UK's <a href="/wiki/Entertainments_National_Service_Association" title="Entertainments National Service Association">Entertainments National Service Association</a>. Her chief obstacle was getting from her home in <a href="/wiki/Dennis,_Massachusetts" title="Dennis, Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">Britain</a>. Aldrich was overseas at the time. In her 1945 memoir <i>A Star Danced</i>, she recalled, "After weeks of more or less patient waiting, repeated timid, pleading, urgent and finally importunate requests to the authorities who rule such matters in Washington and London, and a rapid-fire barrage of telegrams, cables and telephone calls, it had happened. At last I had permission to do what I had been wanting desperately to do for four years—go to England and do my bit on a tour for E.N.S.A."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lawrence's attorney booked the actress on a <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> charter flight from <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> to an airfield near London that lasted 36 hours, including two refuelling stops. When Lawrence boarded the plane, she discovered that she, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a> and Beatrice Lillie were among the few passengers without diplomatic passports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Lawrence and Lillie were the only female passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Hours after landing near London, she performed with E.N.S.A. for British and American troops who, it turned out, had been deployed for the imminent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-Day" title="D-Day">D-Day</a> landings in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. Aldrich was in one of the squadrons of the US Navy.</p>
<p>Aldrich wrote in his 1954 biography of his recently deceased wife:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>She went over with the first E.N.S.A. unit to go into France, making the crossing in an LST (<a href="/wiki/Landing_Ship,_Tank" title="Landing Ship, Tank">Landing Ship, Tank</a>). Others in the party included <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello" title="Ivor Novello">Ivor Novello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Rutherford" title="Margaret Rutherford">Margaret Rutherford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diana_Wynyard" title="Diana Wynyard">Diana Wynyard</a> and Bobbie Andrews. In her autobiography, <i>A Star Danced</i>, she has given a graphic account of their landing on a Normandy beach and of the progress of her unit through the wrecked towns, where there was still no water or electricity. Shows were given in shell-torn cinemas and hastily lighted casinos.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>The physical discomforts – the sleeping in attics, the total lack of sanitation, the scanty and poor food – Gertrude could and did take as fortunes of war. What bothered her more was the breakdown in communications with me. Always dependent upon getting frequent letters from those she loved, she chafed and worried because no mail reached her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As Allied forces scored more victories in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South West Pacific theatre of World War II">South Pacific</a> later that year, Lawrence endured long plane rides and dangerous conditions to perform for troops there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Aldrich book includes a photograph of Lawrence and two unidentified performers standing next to a military plane in <a href="/wiki/Angaur" title="Angaur">Angaur</a> that had just transported them there from <a href="/wiki/Ulithi" title="Ulithi">Ulithi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_and_personal_connection_to_Daphne_du_Maurier">Professional and personal connection to Daphne du Maurier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Professional and personal connection to Daphne du Maurier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1948, Lawrence returned to Britain to star in <i>September Tide</i>, a play written specifically for her by <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>. Her role was that of a middle-aged <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornish</a> woman whose son-in-law, a bohemian artist, falls in love with her. The playwright had intended her to open the play on Broadway, but Lawrence's husband thought it was too British for the American market. The London press paid little attention to her return to the stage, and she was distressed to discover that in a country struggling to recover from the effects of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the public no longer was as interested in the private lives of stage stars as it once had been.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Prior to opening in the West End, the play toured <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, where the frequently sparse audiences consisted primarily of elderly people who remembered Lawrence from her heyday. While on the road, she underwent erratic mood swings and frequently clashed with her fellow cast members, including actors <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gough" title="Michael Gough">Michael Gough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Forbes" title="Bryan Forbes">Bryan Forbes</a>, and the crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The play opened in London in mid-December 1948.</p>
<p>Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i>, Eric Keown called her return "an occasion for rejoicing" but dismissed the play as "an artificial piece of conventional sentiment which leaves the actress's talents unused." She remained with the play until July 1949, then returned to the United States, where she performed her role for one week at her husband's theatre in Dennis, Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to an authorised 1993 biography of the author and playwright by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Forster" title="Margaret Forster">Margaret Forster</a>, Lawrence and du Maurier became close friends during the London production of <i>September Tide</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The nature of their relationship remained unclear following the 1989 death of du Maurier. Forster quotes du Maurier as saying the following about Lawrence c. 1949, "To be blatantly vulgar, anyone with a spice of imagination would prefer a divan with Gertrude to a double-bed with her."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lawrence biographer <a href="/wiki/Sheridan_Morley" title="Sheridan Morley">Sheridan Morley</a> interviewed du Maurier for his 1981 book <i>Gertrude Lawrence</i>. Du Maurier was quoted as saying she called Lawrence by the nickname "Cinders," short for <a href="/wiki/Cinderella" title="Cinderella">Cinderella</a>. Either while negotiating to appear in <i>September Tide</i> or rehearsing it, Lawrence stayed in "a flat in London somewhere," according to what du Maurier told Morley decades later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-41">[41]</a></sup> Boiling water in her tea kettle for a visitor was stressful for Lawrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-41">[41]</a></sup> Du Maurier also told the biographer that she had forgotten all the dialogue she had written for <i>September Tide</i> and that shortly before her interview with Morley she had "been searching my shelves for a copy of the play. ... I cannot remember how Cinders looked, what she wore, far less what she said."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-41">[41]</a></sup> Du Maurier's contribution to the Morley biography of Lawrence consists of little more than that.</p>
<p>Nothing about a personal connection between Gertrude Lawrence and Daphne du Maurier was published during Lawrence's lifetime. Two years after Lawrence's death, her widower Richard Aldrich had this to say in a best-selling biography of his late wife, <i>Gertrude Lawrence as Mrs. A.</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>All her ingenuous traits, which could be annoying as well as endearing, would be swept away by her courage, her clear perception of truth, and the divine compassion which could flood her heart and lift her to the heights of nobility.</p>
<p>I am sure that she was frequently bewildered by the rapidity and mutability of her own impulses. Possessed, as she was, of an intuitive rather than an analytical intelligence, I doubt that she really understood herself clearly, any more than did most of those who thought they knew her intimately. An exception in this regard was Daphne du Maurier.</p>
<p>During those months in England [when <i>September Tide</i> was in production], Gertrude and Daphne formed a warm friendship, which continued unbroken after Gertrude's return to America. Daphne later returned the visit by being Gertrude's guest in New York. Daphne's subsequent best-selling novel <i>Mary Anne</i> was originally planned as a possible starring vehicle for Gertrude.</p>
It was chiefly from comments made later by Daphne that I was able to reconstruct the full picture of Gertrude's inner conflict during her stay in London. Daphne spoke of Gertrude's moodiness, her variability, her sense of vague self-dissatisfaction. To other English friends, Gertrude talked wistfully of wanting to remain in England, "where I belong."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_King_and_I"><i>The King and I</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The King and I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Yul_Brynner" title="Yul Brynner">Yul Brynner</a> and Gertrude Lawrence in the stage musical <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_and_I" title="The King and I">The King and I</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1950, Lawrence's business manager and attorney Fanny Holtzmann was looking for a new property for her client when the 1944 <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Landon" title="Margaret Landon">Margaret Landon</a> book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anna_and_the_King_of_Siam_(book)" title="Anna and the King of Siam (book)">Anna and the King of Siam</a></i> was sent to her by the <a href="/wiki/William_Morris_Agency" title="William Morris Agency">William Morris</a> agent who represented Landon. He thought a stage adaptation of the book would be an ideal vehicle for the actress. Holtzmann agreed, but proposed a musical version would be better. Lawrence wanted <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a> to write the score, but when he proved to be unenthused by the suggestion, Holtzmann sent the book to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodgers" title="Richard Rodgers">Richard Rodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hammerstein_II" title="Oscar Hammerstein II">Oscar Hammerstein II</a>. Rodgers initially demurred because he felt Lawrence's vocal range was limited and she had a tendency to sing flat. But he realised the story had strong potential, and the two men agreed to write what ultimately became <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_and_I" title="The King and I">The King and I</a></i>.</p>
<p>It opened on Broadway in March 1951, and Lawrence won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_a_Leading_Actress_in_a_Musical" title="Tony Award for Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Musical">Tony Award for Best Actress in a Musical</a> for her performance. Her triumph was short-lived; her health deteriorated rapidly, forcing her to miss numerous performances until she finally was hospitalised. While bedridden in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NewYork-Presbyterian_Hospital" title="NewYork-Presbyterian Hospital">NewYork-Presbyterian Hospital</a>, on Friday afternoon, 5 September 1952, less than 24 hours before her death, she instructed Holtzmann to arrange for co-star <a href="/wiki/Yul_Brynner" title="Yul Brynner">Yul Brynner</a>'s name to be added to the marquee of the <a href="/wiki/St._James_Theatre" title="St. James Theatre">St. James Theatre</a>, which included only Lawrence's name at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated410_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated410-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_career">Film career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Film career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the course of twenty-one years, Lawrence appeared in only nine films. She made her screen debut in 1929 in <i>The Battle of Paris</i>, which featured two songs by Cole Porter. <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> offered her the film shortly after the Broadway production of <i>Treasure Girl</i> unexpectedly closed and, with no prospects of stage work in the immediate future, she accepted the offer. The film, co-starring <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Treacher" title="Arthur Treacher">Arthur Treacher</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Ruggles" title="Charles Ruggles">Charles Ruggles</a>, was shot in Paramount's <a href="/wiki/Kaufman_Astoria_Studios" title="Kaufman Astoria Studios">Astoria Studio</a> complex in <a href="/wiki/Astoria,_Queens" title="Astoria, Queens">Astoria, Queens</a>. Lawrence was cast as Georgie, an artist living in pre-<a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, who becomes a <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabaret</a> singer and falls in love with an American soldier. Publicity for the film emphasised Lawrence's songs and costumes rather than the story, which was so weak that director <a href="/wiki/Robert_Florey" title="Robert Florey">Robert Florey</a> had threatened to resign midway through filming. Described by one critic as a "floperetta," it was not a success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1932, she appeared in three features: an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Lonsdale" title="Frederick Lonsdale">Frederick Lonsdale</a> play <i><a href="/wiki/Aren%27t_We_All%3F" title="Aren't We All?">Aren't We All?</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Lachman" title="Harry Lachman">Harry Lachman</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Lord_Camber%27s_Ladies" title="Lord Camber's Ladies">Lord Camber's Ladies</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>, directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benn_W._Levy" title="Benn W. Levy">Benn W. Levy</a>, and co-starring <a href="/wiki/Gerald_du_Maurier" title="Gerald du Maurier">Gerald du Maurier</a>; and <i>No Funny Business</i> with <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a>. In 1935, she appeared in <i>Mimi</i>, based on <i><a href="/wiki/La_Vie_de_Boh%C3%A8me" title="La Vie de Bohème">La Vie de Bohème</a></i>. The following year she was cast opposite <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elsa_Lanchester" title="Elsa Lanchester">Elsa Lanchester</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Rembrandt_(1936_film)" title="Rembrandt (1936 film)">Rembrandt</a></i> and co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Rex_Harrison" title="Rex Harrison">Rex Harrison</a> in <i>Men are Not Gods</i>, both produced by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a>.</p>
<p>Lawrence's best-known American film role was that of Amanda Wingfield, the overbearing mother in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glass_Menagerie_(1950_film)" title="The Glass Menagerie (1950 film)">The Glass Menagerie</a></i> (1950), which both <a href="/wiki/Bette_Davis" title="Bette Davis">Bette Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tallulah_Bankhead" title="Tallulah Bankhead">Tallulah Bankhead</a> had sought. The role required her to wear padding and affect a <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern American</a> accent and friends and critics questioned her decision to accept it. <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>, who had written the play, thought casting Lawrence was "a dismal error" and, after the film's release, called it the worst adaptation of his work he had seen thus far. <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">Bosley Crowther</a> of the <i>New York Times</i> called her Amanda "a farcically exaggerated shrew with the zeal of a burlesque comedienne" and "a perfect imitation of a nervous Mama in domestic comedy". Writing about her performance in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(US_magazine)" title="Saturday Review (US magazine)">Saturday Review</a></i>, Richard Griffith was generous in his praise, saying "Not since <a href="/wiki/Greta_Garbo" title="Greta Garbo">Garbo</a> has there been anything like the naked eloquence of her face, with its amazing play of thought and emotion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_radio">Television and radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Television and radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1938, Lawrence took a night off from <i>Susan and God</i> to perform the play for <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s emerging television audience, which then consisted mostly of customers at bars, hotels, pedestrians in other public places in New York City and NBC employees. Probably less than 100–200 receivers could pick up the telecast. Photos of the 1938 live broadcast are featured in a major article in <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life (magazine)</a> published a week later, as it was one of the first full-length plays done on live television. In 1943, Lawrence hosted a weekly series of American radio shows, some of them featuring discussions with guests and others adaptations of Hollywood hit films. In 1947, she returned to NBC for a production of the 1913 Shaw play <i>The Great Catherine</i>. To promote <i>The King and I</i>, she appeared on various television programmes, including the <a href="/wiki/Ed_Sullivan" title="Ed Sullivan">Ed Sullivan</a>-hosted <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">Toast of the Town</a></i>, with Rodgers and Hammerstein joining her to perform selections from the show. Additionally, she appeared on several <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> interview and variety shows before and after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_difficulties">Financial difficulties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financial difficulties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout her adult life, except during World War II, Lawrence spent far more than she earned. Philip Astley had persuaded her to place £1,000 in a trust fund for her daughter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> but aside from that she had no savings of her own. During her engagement to Bert Taylor he managed her finances and encouraged her to invest in the productions in which she starred, but although she earned a considerable amount of money from <i>Private Lives</i>, she still was deeply in debt, at one point owing fashion entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Hattie_Carnegie" title="Hattie Carnegie">Hattie Carnegie</a> more than $10,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> She opened accounts with dozens of shop owners but assumed she had unlimited credit and paid little attention to the invoices they sent.</p>
<p>Finally, two London laundry owners, whose bills totalled just under £50, filed a writ demanding she declare <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> if she was unable to settle her accounts, and Lawrence's financial affairs came under the scrutiny of the <a href="/wiki/Official_Receiver" title="Official Receiver">Official Receiver</a>. On 26 February 1935, the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a></i> reported her assets were valued at £1,879 but her liabilities were nearly £35,000, with an additional £10,000 owed to the <a href="/wiki/Inland_Revenue" title="Inland Revenue">Inland Revenue</a> on her earnings in the United States.</p>
<p>Later in 1935, Lawrence's flat, cars, clothing, and jewellery were seized by the "London bankruptcy court" (words used by the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and Lawrence, her maid and her dog were forced to move into a flat owned by her agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> On 8 November 1935, accused of "gross extravagance," she was ordered "to pay £50 weekly from the proceeds of her present [nightclub] engagement, and 25 per cent of anything earned any other way should the engagement end," according to the same Associated Press report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was later discovered that Lawrence had never paid American taxes, either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Her attorney Fanny Holtzmann worked out an agreement whereby $150 would be deducted from her salary each week she worked in the United States until her American tax debt was settled.</p>
<p>Refusing to lower her standard of living, she decided to take film work during the day, appear on stage at night, and perform in late-night cabarets to support her spending habits. Much to the distress of her agent, she purchased a country house and farm in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, then left it vacant while she remained in the United States for a lengthy stay. When her agent questioned the wisdom of such a move, she reportedly asked him to investigate the cost of a swimming pool installation on the property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Columbia_University">Columbia University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Columbia University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Early in September [1951]", wrote Lawrence's widower, "she calmly announced that she had accepted an appointment to the Faculty of Columbia University, in the School of Dramatic Arts, of which Dr. Milton Smith was Director. Her particular post was to conduct Class 107 in the Study of Roles and Scenes. The class met on Thursday afternoons in the Brander Matthews Theatre on Morningside Heights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated383_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated383-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>"I shall be teaching an advanced, not an elementary course," Aldrich quoted her as saying in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated383_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated383-54">[54]</a></sup> "Dr. Smith and I have screened all the students. They've had preliminary work in voice, speech and pantomime. Many of them are already working professionally in radio and television. But, more than that, if I can find one person of real talent and encourage and train him, I'll feel that I've done something worthwhile."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated383_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated383-54">[54]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported on 28 September 1951 that Lawrence "suffered an attack of stage fright yesterday and refused to let reporters observe her in her new role of teacher at Columbia University."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>She taught the class again in the spring 1952 semester at Columbia, this time allowing a <i>New York Times</i> reporter and photographer to attend and take pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_funeral">Death and funeral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death and funeral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 16 August 1952, Lawrence fainted backstage immediately after finishing a Saturday matinee of <i>The King and I</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated406_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated406-57">[57]</a></sup> After "a few days at home," wrote Aldrich, she was admitted to New York-Presbyterian Hospital for tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated406_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated406-57">[57]</a></sup> Doctors said she was suffering from <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, and she was admitted to a room on the 16th floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated410_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated410-43">[43]</a></sup> Her former son-in-law, Dr. William G. Cahan, recalled in his 1992 memoir (by then he was a globally recognised medical expert on cancer) what happened next in 1952:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>She was admitted to New York Hospital [located across the street from the hospital where Cahan worked on the staff]. Her doctors were puzzled by what was described in the press as "a liver problem," and suspected that she might have cancer. Hearing this, and without Gertrude's knowledge, her husband consulted me. Not wanting to alarm her by appearing in person (she knew, of course, that by now I was a cancer specialist), I sent some of my colleagues as consultants. They, too, were puzzled, and ordered an exploratory laparotomy (abdominal operation).</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>At dawn of the day the operation was to take place [Saturday, September 6], Dick Aldrich called [Cahan on the telephone]: Gertrude had become comatose; would I please come to the hospital at once? I found her surrounded by interns frantically pumping intravenous fluids and stimulants into her. As I bent over her, she opened her eyes for a second or two, looked up at me, and made a face as if to say, "What are <i>you</i> doing here?"</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>A few minutes later, she died.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Concerned and curious about what had caused her death, I was present at her autopsy. This showed that she had widespread liver and abdominal cancer, the source of which was never clarified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to the <i>New York Times</i>, 5,000 people crowded the intersection of East 55th Street and Fifth Avenue in Manhattan, while 1,800 others, including Yul Brynner, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Davis_Lodge" title="John Davis Lodge">John Davis Lodge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marlene_Dietrich" title="Marlene Dietrich">Marlene Dietrich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Silvers" title="Phil Silvers">Phil Silvers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luise_Rainer" title="Luise Rainer">Luise Rainer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss_Hart" title="Moss Hart">Moss Hart</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Carlisle" title="Kitty Carlisle">Kitty Carlisle</a> filled <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue_Presbyterian_Church" title="Fifth Avenue Presbyterian Church">Fifth Avenue Presbyterian Church</a> for Lawrence's funeral. In his <a href="/wiki/Eulogy" title="Eulogy">eulogy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hammerstein_II" title="Oscar Hammerstein II">Oscar Hammerstein II</a> quoted from an essay on death written by poet and novelist <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Lawrence was buried in the champagne-coloured gown worn for the "Shall We Dance?" number in the second act of <i>The King and I</i>, and she was interred in the Aldrich family plot in Lakeview Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Upton,_Massachusetts" title="Upton, Massachusetts">Upton, Massachusetts</a>. She was the first person for whom the house lights were dimmed in all <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway (Manhattan)</a> theatres due to her death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early 1953, Lawrence's name was included on a list of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> professors who had died the previous year and were honoured with a memorial service and flags on the campus lowered to half-mast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Richard Aldrich's biography of his late wife became a best-seller in late 1954 and 1955, and was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a> at a New York bookstore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lawrence's biographer Sheridan Morley wrote in 1981 that throughout the 1950s, 1960s and 1970s, "...most traces of Gertrude Lawrence ... disappeared; she died before television had begun to immortalise its artists on (video)tape, before radio shows were regularly recorded, and though she made half a dozen films, her appearances in them are mostly undistinguished and give no clear impression of a radiance which could hold theatre audiences spellbound."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a> portrayed Lawrence in the musical <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Star!_(film)" title="Star! (film)">Star!</a></i>, released to cinemas in 1968. It was loosely based on the period of her life from her days as an unknown aspiring performer until her wedding to Richard Aldrich. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Crenna" title="Richard Crenna">Richard Crenna</a> appeared as Aldrich. The real Aldrich, who in the 1960s no longer worked in the entertainment business, was a consultant on the film. Noël Coward was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Massey_(actor)" title="Daniel Massey (actor)">Daniel Massey</a>. <i>Star!</i> was a box office flop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Its failure damaged the movie career of Julie Andrews, who three years earlier had been the most visible movie actress worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Glass Menagerie</i> was Gertrude Lawrence's only film that was a box-office success and in which she worked with an American studio and an entirely American cast: <a href="/wiki/Jane_Wyman" title="Jane Wyman">Jane Wyman</a> (Laura), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Kennedy_(actor)" title="Arthur Kennedy (actor)">Arthur Kennedy</a> (Tom), <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Douglas" title="Kirk Douglas">Kirk Douglas</a> (The Gentleman Caller). The director was an Englishman whose entire career was in American cinema. The movie was rarely shown on American television until 1992, however. During that year, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMC_(TV_network)" title="AMC (TV network)">American Movie Classics</a> revived it with an introduction and postscript from the channel's host, Bob Dorian. He provided information about Lawrence for American viewers who were not familiar with her.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Janet_McTeer" title="Janet McTeer">Janet McTeer</a> portrayed Lawrence opposite <a href="/wiki/Geraldine_Somerville" title="Geraldine Somerville">Geraldine Somerville</a> as Daphne du Maurier and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Sinclair" title="Malcolm Sinclair">Malcolm Sinclair</a> as Noël Coward in <i>Daphne</i>, a 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_movie" title="Television movie">television movie</a> broadcast by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.</p>
<p>Three years after Lawrence's death, her daughter Pamela gave birth to Benn Clatworthy, by her second husband, the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Clatworthy_(sculptor)" title="Robert Clatworthy (sculptor)">Robert Clatworthy</a>. The first of Lawrence's three grandchildren, he is now a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenor_saxophonist" title="Tenor saxophonist">tenor saxophonist</a> based in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Lawrence's other two grandchildren, Sarah Hunt and Tom Clatworthy, are both residents of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gertrude_Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the classic <a href="/wiki/Moss_Hart" title="Moss Hart">Moss Hart</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Kaufman" title="George Kaufman">George Kaufman</a> play <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Came_to_Dinner" title="The Man Who Came to Dinner">The Man Who Came to Dinner</a>, the character Lorraine Sheldon was inspired by Gertrude Lawrence.</p>
<ul>
<li>Lawrence's recording of the song "<a href="/wiki/Getting_to_Know_You_(song)" title="Getting to Know You (song)">Getting to Know You</a>" from <i>The King and I</i> was featured on the soundtrack of the sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_of_Queens" title="The King of Queens">The King of Queens</a></i> in the episode "Arthur, Spooner." It was originally telecast in the United States on <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> on 23 September 2002 as the season premiere. It has been repeated many times in <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_syndication" title="Broadcast syndication">syndication</a>, on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TBS_(US_TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TBS (US TV channel) (page does not exist)">TBS (US TV channel)</a> in the United States and is available on the <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> for the Complete Fifth Season.</li>
</ul>
