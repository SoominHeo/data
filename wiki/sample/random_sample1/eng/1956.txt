<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dry cell</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>Dry cell</b> is a type of electricity-producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_cell" title="Chemical cell">chemical cell</a>, commonly used today for many home and portable devices, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>. It was developed in 1886 by the German scientist Carl Gassner.</p>
<p>A dry cell uses a paste <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>, with only enough <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> to allow current to flow. Unlike a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_cell" title="Wet cell">wet cell</a>, a dry cell can operate in any orientation without spilling, as it contains no free liquid, making it suitable for portable equipment. By comparison, the first wet cells were typically fragile glass containers with lead rods hanging from the open top and needed careful handling to avoid <a href="/wiki/Spillage" title="Spillage">spillage</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_batteries" title="Lead–acid batteries">Lead-acid battery</a> did not achieve the safety and portability of the dry cell until the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gel_battery" title="Gel battery">gel battery</a>. Wet cells have continued to be used for high drain applications such as starting <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>, because inhibiting electrolyte flow tends to reduce the current capability.</p>
<p>A common dry cell is the <a href="/wiki/Zinc%E2%80%93carbon_battery" title="Zinc–carbon battery">zinc-carbon cell</a>, sometimes called the dry <a href="/wiki/Leclanch%C3%A9_cell" title="Leclanché cell">Leclanché cell</a>, with a nominal voltage of 1.5 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a>, the same as the <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_battery" title="Alkaline battery">alkaline cell</a> (since both use the same <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>–<a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a> combination).</p>
<p>A standard dry cell comprises a <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>, usually in the form of a cylindrical pot, with a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> in the form of a central rod. The <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> is <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> in the form of a paste next to the zinc anode. The remaining space between the electrolyte and carbon cathode is taken up by a second paste consisting of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>, the latter acting as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depolariser" title="Depolariser">depolariser</a>. In some designs, the ammonium chloride is replaced by <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many experimenters tried to immobilize the electrolyte of an electrochemical cell to make it more convenient to use. The <a href="/wiki/Zamboni_pile" title="Zamboni pile">Zamboni pile</a> of 1812 was a high-voltage dry battery but capable of delivering only minute currents. Various experiments were made with cellulose, sawdust, spun glass, asbestos fibers, and gelatine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1886, Carl Gassner obtained a German patent (No. 37,758) on a variant of the Leclanché cell, which came to be known as the dry cell because it did not have a free liquid electrolyte. Instead, the ammonium chloride was mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plaster_of_Paris" title="Plaster of Paris">Plaster of Paris</a> to create a paste, with a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a> added in to extend the shelf life. The <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a> cathode was dipped in this paste, and both were sealed in a zinc shell, which also acted as the anode. In November 1887, he obtained <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US373064" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 373,064</a></span> for the same device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike previous wet cells, Gassner's dry cell was more solid, did not require maintenance, did not spill, and could be used in any orientation. It provided a potential of 1.5 volts. The first mass-produced model was the Columbia dry cell, first marketed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Carbon_Company" title="National Carbon Company">National Carbon Company</a> in 1896.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The NCC improved Gassner's model by replacing the plaster of Paris with coiled cardboard, an innovation that left more space for the cathode and made the battery easier to assemble. It was the first convenient battery for the masses and made portable electrical devices practical.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc-carbon_battery" title="Zinc-carbon battery">zinc-carbon cell</a> (as it came to be known) is still manufactured today.</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Primary_cell" title="Primary cell">Primary cell</a>

<li>Zinc-carbon cells, also known as <a href="/wiki/Leclanch%C3%A9_cell" title="Leclanché cell">Leclanché cells</a></li>

<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">Nickel-cadmium cell</a></li>

<p>Primary cells are not <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable</a> and are generally disposed of after their chemicals have been used up through the cell's internal reaction.</p>
<p>Secondary cells are rechargeable, and may be reused multiple times.</p>
