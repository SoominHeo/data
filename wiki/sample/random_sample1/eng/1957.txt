<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coat of arms of Guernsey</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>coat of arms of Guernsey</b> is the official symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>. It is a red shield with three gold lions (historically described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopards" title="Leopards">leopards</a>) passant guardant surmounted by a small branch of leaves. It is very similar to the arms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Normandy" title="Coat of arms of Normandy">Normandy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Jersey" title="Coat of arms of Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_England" title="Coat of arms of England">England</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seal_of_Guernsey">Seal of Guernsey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Guernsey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Seal of Guernsey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Seal of Guernsey closely follows the Coat of Arms, it originates from 1279 when a single seal was provided by Edward I for joint use in Guernsey and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>. The seal comprised 3 <i>Luparts</i>, leopards (or lions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup> By 1304 separate seals were provided for each Bailiwick.</p>
<p>The shape of the sprig or "Rameau" on the top of the seal has changed over the centuries. Bailiff Daniel de L'Isle Brock commissioned a replacement seal in 1832 the lions or leopards becoming a caricature of true heraldic beasts. The head of the beasts took on a shape approximating to that of a shield, the mane was virtually non-existent, the body was somewhat extended and the legs were so thin they could not carry an animal. Bailiff Sir Edgar McCulloch in 1884 reverted to the traditional heraldic representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
