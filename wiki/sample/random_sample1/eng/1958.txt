<p><b>Architectural photography</b> is the photographing of buildings and similar structures that are both aesthetically pleasing and accurate representations of their subjects. <a href="/wiki/Architectural_photographers" title="Architectural photographers">Architectural photographers</a> are usually skilled in the use of specialized techniques and equipment.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first permanent photograph, <i><a href="/wiki/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras" title="View from the Window at Le Gras">View from the Window at Le Gras</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce" title="Nicéphore Niépce">Nicéphore Niépce</a>, was also the first architectural photograph as it was a view of buildings. Similarly, photographs taken by early photographer William <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fox_Talbot" title="Henry Fox Talbot">Henry Fox Talbot</a> were of architecture, including his photograph of a Latticed window in Lacock Abbey taken in 1835.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Throughout the history of photography, buildings have been highly valued photographic subjects, mirroring society's appreciation for architecture and its cultural significance. By the 1860s, architectural photography started to become an established visual medium.</p>
<p>Much as building designs changed and broke with traditional forms, architectural photography also evolved. During the early-to-mid-20th century, architectural photography became more creative as photographers used diagonal lines and bold shadows in their compositions, and experimented with other techniques.</p>
<p>By the early 1950s, architects were hiring more photographers for commissioned work, resulting in architectural photography being viewed as more of an art form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-Lowe_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-Lowe-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinar_P.jpg"><img alt="Large, boxy camera with bellows" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="462" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Sinar_P.jpg/220px-Sinar_P.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Sinar_P.jpg/330px-Sinar_P.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Sinar_P.jpg/440px-Sinar_P.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinar_P.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sinar" title="Sinar">Sinar</a> P</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A tenet of architectural photography is the use of <a href="/wiki/Perspective_control" title="Perspective control">perspective control</a>, with an emphasis on vertical lines that are non-converging (parallel). This is achieved by positioning the focal plane of the camera at so that it is perpendicular to the ground, regardless of the elevation of the camera eye. This result can be achieved by the use of <a href="/wiki/View_camera" title="View camera">view camera</a>s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tilt-shift_lens" title="Tilt-shift lens">tilt/shift lens</a>es, or post-processing.</p>
<p>Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/View_camera" title="View camera">view cameras</a> have been used for architectural photography as they allow for the lens to be tilted or shifted relative to the film plane. This allows for control of perspective, as well as a variety of creative possibilities.</p>
<p>In a similar fashion to landscape photography, a deep <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">depth of field</a> is usually employed so that both the foreground and background (to infinity) are in sharp focus.</p>
<p>More recently, <a href="/wiki/Digital_single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Digital single-lens reflex camera">digital single lens reflex (DSLR)</a> cameras have been used in the field of architectural photography. These cameras also employ detachable, tilt-shift lenses of varying (usually fixed) focal lengths.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior_and_interior">Exterior and interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exterior and interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Architectural photography typically shows either the exterior or the interior of buildings. The techniques used in each of these types of photography are similar, but do have some difference and sometimes require different equipment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior">Exterior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exterior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Exterior architectural photography usually takes advantage of <a href="/wiki/Available_light" title="Available light">available light</a> by day, or at night it uses ambient light from adjacent street lights, landscape lights, exterior building lights, moonlight and even <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> present in the sky in all but the darkest situations.</p>
<p>In many cases, the landscaping surrounding a building is important to the overall composition of a photograph, and even necessary to communicate the aesthetic harmony of a building with its environment. The photographer will often include flowers, trees, fountains or statues in the foreground of a composition, taking advantage of their ability to help lead the eye into the composition and to its main subject, the building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interior architectural photography can also be performed with ambient light transmitted through windows and skylights, as well as interior lighting fixtures. Frequently though, architectural photographers will use supplemental lighting to improve the illumination within a building. Either electronic flash "strobes" or incandescent "hot lights" can be used. A feature of architectural photography is that the principal subjects rarely move. It is therefore possible to use post-processing editing to achieve a balanced lighting scheme, even in the absence of additional lighting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_organizations">Trade organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trade organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The architectural photography profession is primarily represented by three trade organizations, which strive to spread best practices amongst architectural photographers, as well as promote the sound business practices, consistency, quality and copyright protection.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Media_Photographers" title="American Society of Media Photographers">American Society of Media Photographers</a> (ASMP)</li>
<li>The Association of Independent Architectural Photographers (AIAP)</li>
<li>The International Association of Architectural Photographers (IAAP)</li>
<li>The Association of Photographers (AOP)</li>
</ul>
