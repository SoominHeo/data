<p>The <b>sooty mangabey</b> (<i>Cercocebus atys</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_monkey" title="Old World monkey">Old World monkey</a> found in forests from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> in a margin along the coast down to <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup> While overall rated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a>, the eastern race <i>lunulatus</i>, also known as the <b>white-crowned</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>white-naped</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <b>white-collared mangabey</b> (leading to confusion with the <a href="/wiki/Collared_mangabey" title="Collared mangabey">collared mangabey</a>), is considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">Endangered</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The White-collared mangabey was declared its own species by the IUCN in 2016, but it retains the endangered conservation status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_ecology">Habitat and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_mangabey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sooty mangabey is native to tropical West Africa, being found in Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, Liberia, Senegal, Sierra Leone and Ivory Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> It lives in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth_forest" title="Old growth forest">old growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_forest" title="Secondary forest">secondary forests</a> as well as in flooded, dry, swamp, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a>, and gallery forests. The primate is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> whose diet includes primarily fruits and seeds, sometimes feeding on small animals. They live in social groups of four to twelve individuals, but occasionally groups as large as 95 individuals have been recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_mangabey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two distinctive <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mangabey_(disambiguation)" title="Mangabey (disambiguation)">mangabey</a>, and it is possible they should be considered separate species. Both were formerly considered <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of a widespread <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cercocebus_torquatus" title="Cercocebus torquatus">Cercocebus torquatus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Cercocebus atys atys</i> (west of the <a href="/wiki/Sassandra_River" title="Sassandra River">Sassandra River</a>)</li>
<li>White-collared (or White-crowned) mangabey, <i>Cercocebus atys lunulatus</i> (east of the Sassandra River).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_mangabey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sooty mangabeys are naturally infected with a strain of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simian_Immunodeficiency_Virus" title="Simian Immunodeficiency Virus">Simian Immunodeficiency Virus</a> (SIV), known as SIVsmm. Due to extensive human-mangabey contact in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>, SIVsmm has <a href="/wiki/Zoonosis" title="Zoonosis">jumped from this species</a> into <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> on multiple occasions, resulting in <a href="/wiki/HIV#HIV-2" title="HIV">HIV-2</a> <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a>. The <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV-1</a> strain by contrast came from the <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzee</a> strain of SIV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sooty mangabey can also contract <a href="/wiki/Leprosy" title="Leprosy">leprosy</a>, as can humans, the <a href="/wiki/Nine-banded_armadillo" title="Nine-banded armadillo">nine-banded armadillo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzee</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Crab-eating_macaque" title="Crab-eating macaque">crab-eating macaque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_mangabey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sooty mangabey is believed to be decreasing in numbers as its forest habitat is degraded, with trees being felled for firewood and timber, and it is hunted for food in some parts of its range. It is more terrestrial than some of its relatives and sometimes raids farms, which brings it into conflict with humans. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed its conservation status as "<a href="/wiki/Near-threatened_species" title="Near-threatened species">near-threatened</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
