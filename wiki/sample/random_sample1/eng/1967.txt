<p><b>Black Widow</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">чёрная вдова</span>, <i>chyornaya vdova</i>) or <b>shahidka</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">шахидка</span>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Language" title="Russian Language">Russian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminine_gender" title="Feminine gender">feminine gender</a> derivation from <i><a href="/wiki/Shahid" title="Shahid">shahid</a></i>), is a term for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> <a href="/wiki/Chechens" title="Chechens">Chechen</a> <a href="/wiki/Female_suicide_bomber" title="Female suicide bomber">female suicide bombers</a>, willing to be a manifestation of violent <a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">jihad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tt29_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tt29-1">[1]</a></sup> They became known at the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_theater_hostage_crisis" title="Moscow theater hostage crisis">Moscow theater hostage crisis</a> of October 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tgmel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tgmel-2">[2]</a></sup> The commander <a href="/wiki/Shamil_Basayev" title="Shamil Basayev">Shamil Basayev</a> referred to the shahidkas as a part of force of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_bomber" title="Suicide bomber">suicide bombers</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Riyad-us_Saliheen_Brigade_of_Martyrs" title="Riyad-us Saliheen Brigade of Martyrs">Riyad-us Saliheen Brigade of Martyrs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup> Basayev also stated that he himself trained at least fifty of the black widows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term of "Black Widows" probably originates from the fact that many of these women are <a href="/wiki/Widow" title="Widow">widows</a> of men killed by the Russian forces in <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>. (The toxic connotation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_widow_spider" title="Black widow spider">black widow spider</a> is intended.) In 2003, the Russian journalist Julia Jusik coined the phrase <b>"Brides of Allah"</b> (Невесты Аллаха) when she described the process by which Chechen women were recruited by Basayev and his associates;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the phrase was also used again after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beslan_school_hostage_crisis" title="Beslan school hostage crisis">Beslan attack</a>, as the title of an installment of the Russian <a href="/wiki/NTV_(Russia)" title="NTV (Russia)">NTV</a> programme <i>Top Secret</i> (Совершенно секретно).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_motives">Background and motives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Widow_(Chechnya)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and motives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been claims that many of the women have been sold by their parents to be used as shahidkas, others have been kidnapped or tricked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjusik_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-jjusik-5">[5]</a></sup> She also claims that many have been prepared for the suicide by way of <a href="/wiki/Narcotic" title="Narcotic">narcotics</a> and rape. Several were pregnant at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjusik_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-jjusik-5">[5]</a></sup> Independent journalists like Robert W. Kurz and Charles K. Bartles reject this view, stating that in most cases female Chechen suicide bombers do not fit this model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup> Mostly they are given no training at all in preparation for the suicides as no weapon skill is needed to strap on the explosives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjusik_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-jjusik-5">[5]</a></sup> Many do not even blow themselves up, but are blown up by remote control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjusik_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-jjusik-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On the other hand, Besayev, as stated above, reported that the women are trained for their mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Radu" title="Michael Radu">Michael Radu</a> argued that these women are specifically trained for suicide attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-radu-8">[8]</a></sup> Additionally, some black widows have brothers or close relatives who were killed in one of the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chechen_separatism" title="Chechen separatism">Chechen wars</a> between Russia and Islamist rebels since 1994 or in clashes with Russian-backed forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tt29_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tt29-1">[1]</a></sup> Kurz and Bartles offer another view of their motives, arguing that these women are much more motivated by revenge, despair, and their drive for an independent state than by religious fundamentalism or individual honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_attacks">Notable attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Widow_(Chechnya)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Khava Barayeva is the first known 'Black Widow' who blew herself up at a <a href="/wiki/Russian_Ground_Forces" title="Russian Ground Forces">Russian Army</a> base in <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a> in June 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strat_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-strat-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Medna Bayrokova, a resident of <a href="/wiki/Grozny" title="Grozny">Grozny</a>, said that she remembers the day a middle aged woman came to her front door asking to speak to her 26-year-old daughter, Zareta Bayrokova, who was a tuberculosis patient. Bayrokova let the woman in. Her daughter spent an hour in her bedroom with the woman, before leaving the house. Zareta Bayrokova died in the attack on the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_theater_hostage_crisis" title="Moscow theater hostage crisis">Dubrovka Theater in Moscow</a> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nivat_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nivat-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>In May 2003, Shakhida Baimuratova, a Black Widow suicide bomber, killed 16 people and wounded 150 in an assassination attempt on then Moscow-appointed Chechen president <a href="/wiki/Akhmad_Kadyrov" title="Akhmad Kadyrov">Akhmad Kadyrov</a> at a crowded Muslim festival in Ilishkan Yurt. A second woman bomber was also present but killed only herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 5 June 2003, a woman detonated a bomb in a bus carrying Russian Air Force pilots in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Ossetia" title="North Ossetia">North Ossetia</a>, killing twenty (besides herself) and injuring 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 5 July 2003, two suicide bombers killed 16 people and injured six others at a rock concert at <a href="/wiki/Tushino_Airfield" title="Tushino Airfield">Tushino Airfield</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timelinett_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-timelinett-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>In December 2003, a male and female suicide bomber killed 46 people and injured 100 others by detonating explosives on a packed commuter train, which had just left <a href="/wiki/Yessentuki" title="Yessentuki">Yessentuki</a> in Southern Russia. The woman is believed to have carried explosives in a bag, whereas the man had <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenades</a> strapped to his leg.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>On 9 December 2003, a bomb exploded outside the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_National,_Moscow" title="Hotel National, Moscow">Hotel National, Moscow</a> just a few hundred metres from the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Kremlin" title="Moscow Kremlin">Moscow Kremlin</a>. It is thought that the target was the <a href="/wiki/State_Duma" title="State Duma">State Duma</a> building and that the bomb had detonated prematurely. Six people died and 13 were injured in the blast. The suicide bomber was later identified as Khadishat Mangeriyeva.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>On 6 February 2004, Georgi Trofimov, a Russian <a href="/wiki/Bomb_disposal" title="Bomb disposal">bomb disposal</a> officer, was killed as he tried to defuse a device at a Moscow cafe. The failed bomber, ethnic <a href="/wiki/Ingush_people" title="Ingush people">Ingush</a> <a href="/wiki/Zarema_Muzhakhoyeva" title="Zarema Muzhakhoyeva">Zarema Muzhakhoyeva</a>, was sentenced to 20 years of imprisonment for terrorism in April 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2005, she participated in the trial of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beslan_hostage_crisis" title="Beslan hostage crisis">Beslan hostage crisis</a> terrorist <a href="/wiki/Nur-Pashi_Kulayev" title="Nur-Pashi Kulayev">Nur-Pashi Kulayev</a> as a witness for the prosecution, but she withdrew all her statements about Kulayev that she made in pre-trial depositions and said she didn't know he was a militant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2004_Russian_aircraft_bombings" title="2004 Russian aircraft bombings">Two Russian passenger aircraft disasters in 2004</a> are believed to have been the work of the Black Widows. The smaller of the planes, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TU-134" title="TU-134">TU-134</a> which crashed near <a href="/wiki/Tula,_Russia" title="Tula, Russia">Tula</a> had been carrying a Chechen woman called Amanat Nagayeva who had bought her ticket just an hour before the flight took off. The larger plane exploded near the city of <a href="/wiki/Rostov" title="Rostov">Rostov</a> killing 46 people. Among the wreckage, investigators found traces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexogen" title="Hexogen">hexogen</a>, a powerful explosive. Another Chechen woman, Satsita Djerbikhanova, was also a last-minute passenger on this flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwkckb07_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-rwkckb07-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 1 September 2004, two Chechen women, Roza Nagayeva and Mairam Taburova, were involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beslan_school_massacre" title="Beslan school massacre">Beslan school massacre</a>. The attack which killed 334 civilians, including 186 children, was masterminded by <a href="/wiki/Shamil_Basayev" title="Shamil Basayev">Shamil Basayev</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>On 29 March 2010, nearly 40 people were killed and another 100 injured when two suicide bombers detonated explosives at two stations of the Moscow subway, the Park Kultury metro station and at the Lubyanka station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuguy_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuguy-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/2010_Moscow_Metro_bombings" title="2010 Moscow Metro bombings">The attacks</a> were linked to shahidkas by the Russian Government, although an investigation has yet to be undertaken. One of the perpetrators was Dagestani-born Dzhennet Abdurakhmanova (1992-2010), the widow of 30-year-old Umalat Magomedov who was killed by Russian forces on 31 December 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuguy_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuguy-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 24 January 2011, 35 were <a href="/wiki/Domodedovo_International_Airport_bombing" title="Domodedovo International Airport bombing">killed</a> and 180 wounded in <a href="/wiki/Domodedovo_International_Airport" title="Domodedovo International Airport">Domodedovo</a>, Russia's busiest airport. Although the identity of those responsible for carrying out the attacks has not been officially confirmed, initial reports suggested that at least one Black Widow was involved, likely accompanied by a man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 7 March 2012, a widow of a militant killed on 10–11 February 2012 near a village, Karabudakhkent, 40 km (24 miles) south of <a href="/wiki/Dagestan_Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Dagestan Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic">Dagestan</a> capital <a href="/wiki/Makhachkala" title="Makhachkala">Makhachkala</a> killed herself and five police officers and wounded two others in Karabudakhkent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc7mar_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc7mar-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 28 August 2012, Sufi leader <a href="/wiki/Said_Afandi_al-Chirkawi" title="Said Afandi al-Chirkawi">Said Afandi</a> and six other people were killed in a suicide bomb attack in <a href="/wiki/Dagestan" title="Dagestan">Dagestan</a>. The attack was allegedly perpetrated by Russian Aminat Kurbanova who had converted to Islam. Her two former spouses were Islamic militants, and her third husband also believed to be a militant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwj_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lwj-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 25 May 2013, a female suicide bomber, Madina Alieva, blew herself up in Dagestan, injuring at least 18. She was the widow of an Islamist killed in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 21 October 2013, a female suicide bomber, Naida Asiyalova, <a href="/wiki/October_2013_Volgograd_bus_bombing" title="October 2013 Volgograd bus bombing">blew up a Volgograd bus</a>, killing six of the forty passengers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>On 29 December 2013, a female suicide bomber <a href="/wiki/December_2013_Volgograd_bombings" title="December 2013 Volgograd bombings">killed 16 people at a train station in Volgograd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
