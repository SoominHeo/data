<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Black Wednesday</h1>

<p>In politics and economics, <b>Black Wednesday</b> refers to 16 September 1992, when the British <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> government was forced to withdraw the <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound sterling</a> from the <a href="/wiki/European_Exchange_Rate_Mechanism" title="European Exchange Rate Mechanism">European Exchange Rate Mechanism</a> (ERM) after it was unable to keep the pound above its agreed lower limit in the ERM. In 1997, the <a href="/wiki/HM_Treasury" title="HM Treasury">UK Treasury</a> estimated the cost of Black Wednesday at £3.4 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2005, documents released under the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_2000" title="Freedom of Information Act 2000">Freedom of Information Act</a> revealed that the actual cost may have been slightly less, £3.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The trading losses in August and September were estimated at £800 million, but the main loss to taxpayers arose because <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devaluation</a> could have made them a profit. The papers <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> show that if the government had maintained $24 billion foreign currency reserves and the pound had fallen by the same amount, the UK would have made a £2.4 billion profit on the pound sterling's devaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2012)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Wednesday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the ERM was set up in 1979, the United Kingdom declined to join. This was a controversial decision, as the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Howe" title="Geoffrey Howe">Geoffrey Howe</a>, was staunchly pro-European. His successor, <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Lawson" title="Nigel Lawson">Nigel Lawson</a>, a believer in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_exchange_rate" title="Fixed exchange rate">fixed exchange rate</a>, admired the low inflationary record of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>. He attributed it to the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">Deutsche Mark</a> and the management of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bundesbank" title="Bundesbank">Bundesbank</a>. Thus, although the UK had not joined the ERM, from early 1987 to March 1988 the Treasury followed a semi-official policy of 'shadowing' the Deutsche Mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Matters came to a head in a clash between Lawson and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>'s economic adviser <a href="/wiki/Alan_Walters" title="Alan Walters">Alan Walters</a>, when Walters claimed that the Exchange Rate Mechanism was "half baked".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This led to Lawson's resignation as chancellor to be replaced by his old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prot%C3%A9g%C3%A9" title="Protégé">protégé</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>, who, with <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hurd" title="Douglas Hurd">Douglas Hurd</a>, the then <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a>, convinced the Cabinet to sign Britain up to the ERM in October 1990, effectively guaranteeing that the British government would follow an economic and <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> preventing the <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> between the pound and other member currencies from fluctuating by more than 6%. On 8 October 1990, Thatcher entered the pound into the ERM mechanism at <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">DM</a> 2.95 to the pound. Hence, if the exchange rate ever neared the bottom of its permitted range, DM 2.773, the government would be obliged to intervene. With UK inflation at three times the rate of Germany's, interest rates at 15% and the "<a href="/wiki/Lawson_Boom" title="Lawson Boom">Lawson Boom</a>" about to bust, the conditions for joining the ERM were not favourable at that time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From the beginning of the 1990s, high German <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a>, set by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bundesbank" title="Bundesbank">Bundesbank</a> to counteract inflationary effects related to excess expenditure on <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, caused significant stress across the whole of the ERM. The UK and Italy had additional difficulties with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_deficit_(economics)" title="Double deficit (economics)">double deficits</a>, while the UK was also hurt by the rapid depreciation of the United States Dollar – a currency in which many British exports were priced – that summer. Issues of national prestige and the commitment to a doctrine that the fixing of exchange rates within the ERM was a pathway to a single European currency inhibited the adjustment of exchange rates. In the wake of the rejection of the <a href="/wiki/Maastricht_Treaty" title="Maastricht Treaty">Maastricht Treaty</a> by the Danish electorate in a referendum in the spring of 1992, and announcement that there would be a referendum in France as well, those ERM currencies that were trading close to the bottom of their ERM bands came under pressure from foreign exchange traders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the months leading up to Black Wednesday, <a href="/wiki/George_Soros" title="George Soros">Soros</a> had been building a huge short position in pounds sterling that would become immensely profitable if the pound fell below the lower band of the ERM. Soros recognized the unfavourable position at which the United Kingdom joined the ERM, believing the rate at which the UK was brought into the Exchange Rate Mechanism was too high, their inflation was also much too high (triple the German rate), and British interest rates were hurting their asset prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amazon1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-amazon1-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Soros" title="George Soros">George Soros</a>, the most high-profile of the currency market speculators, made over £1 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in profit by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_selling" title="Short selling">short selling</a> sterling.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_currency_traders_act">The currency traders act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Wednesday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The currency traders act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The UK government attempted to prop up the depreciating pound to avoid withdrawal from the monetary system the country had joined two years previously. John Major raised interest rates to 10 percent and authorised the spending of billions worth of foreign currency reserves to buy up the sterling being sold on the currency markets but the measures failed to prevent the pound falling below its minimum level in the ERM. The Treasury took the decision to defend the sterling's position, believing that to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devalue" title="Devalue">devalue</a> would be to promote inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soros' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_Fund" title="Quantum Fund">Quantum Fund</a> began a massive sell-off of pounds on Tuesday, 15 September 1992. The Exchange Rate Mechanism stated that the Bank of England was required to accept any offers to sell pounds. However, the Bank of England only accepted orders during the trading day. When the markets opened in London the next morning, the Bank of England began their attempt to prop up their currency as per the decision made by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Lamont" title="Norman Lamont">Norman Lamont</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robin_Leigh-Pemberton" title="Robin Leigh-Pemberton">Robin Leigh-Pemberton</a>, the then Chancellor of the Exchequer and Governor of the Bank of England respectively. They began buying orders to the amount of 300 million pounds twice before 8:30 AM to little effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Bank of England's intervention was ineffective because Soros' Quantum Fund was dumping pounds far faster. The Bank of England continued to buy and Quantum continued to sell until Lamont told Prime Minister John Major that their pound purchasing was failing to produce results.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At 10:30 AM on 16 September, the British government announced a rise in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_base_rate#Interest_rates" title="Bank base rate">base interest rate</a> from an already high 10 to 12 percent to tempt speculators to buy pounds. Despite this and a promise later the same day to raise base rates again to 15 percent, dealers kept selling pounds, convinced that the government would not stick with its promise. By 7:00 that evening, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Lamont,_Baron_Lamont_of_Lerwick" title="Norman Lamont, Baron Lamont of Lerwick">Norman Lamont</a>, then Chancellor, announced Britain would leave the ERM and rates would remain at the new level of 12 percent; however, on the next day interest rate was back on 10%. It was later revealed that the decision to withdraw had been agreed at an emergency meeting during the day between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Lamont,_Baron_Lamont_of_Lerwick" title="Norman Lamont, Baron Lamont of Lerwick">Norman Lamont</a>, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>, Foreign Secretary <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hurd" title="Douglas Hurd">Douglas Hurd</a>, President of the Board of Trade <a href="/wiki/Michael_Heseltine" title="Michael Heseltine">Michael Heseltine</a>, and Home Secretary <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clarke" title="Kenneth Clarke">Kenneth Clarke</a> (the latter three all being staunch pro-<a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">Europeans</a> as well as senior Cabinet Ministers), and that the interest rate hike to 15% had only been a temporary measure to prevent a rout in the pound that afternoon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Wednesday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other ERM countries such as <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, whose currencies had breached their bands during the day, returned to the system with broadened bands or with adjusted central parities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Even in this relaxed form, ERM-I proved vulnerable, and ten months later the rules were relaxed further to the point of imposing very little constraint on the domestic monetary policies of member states.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The effect of the high German interest rates, and high British interest rates, had arguably put Britain into recession as large numbers of businesses failed and the housing market crashed. Some commentators, following <a href="/wiki/Norman_Tebbit" title="Norman Tebbit">Norman Tebbit</a>, took to referring to ERM as an "Eternal Recession Mechanism"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> after the UK fell into recession during the early 1990s. While many people in the UK recall Black Wednesday as a national disaster, some conservatives claim that the forced ejection from the ERM was a "Golden Wednesday"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> or "White Wednesday",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaletsky_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaletsky-11">[11]</a></sup> the day that paved the way for an economic revival, with the Conservatives handing <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>'s New Labour a much stronger economy in 1997 than had existed in 1992<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaletsky_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-kaletsky-11">[11]</a></sup> as the new economic policy swiftly devised in the aftermath of Black Wednesday led to re-establishment of economic growth with falling unemployment and inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">Monetary policy</a> switched to <a href="/wiki/Inflation_targeting" title="Inflation targeting">inflation targeting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party government</a>'s image had been damaged to the extent that the electorate was more inclined to believe opposition arguments of the time – that the economic recovery ought to be credited to external factors, as opposed to good government policies. The Conservatives had recently won the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1992" title="United Kingdom general election, 1992">1992 general election</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallup_poll" title="Gallup poll">Gallup poll</a> for September showed a 2.5% Conservative lead. By the October poll, following Black Wednesday, their share of the intended vote in the poll had plunged from 43% to 29%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>The stock market rose about 8% over the next two days, so it was worth about the same for outsiders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
