<p><i><b>Black Skin, White Masks</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Peau noire, masques blancs</i></span>) is a 1952 book by <a href="/wiki/Frantz_Fanon" title="Frantz Fanon">Frantz Fanon</a>, in which Fanon studies the psychology of the racism and dehumanization inherent in situations of colonial domination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the application of historical interpretation, and the concomitant underlying social indictment, the psychiatrist Frantz Fanon formulated <i>Black Skin, White Masks</i> to combat the oppression of black people. He applied <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalytic_theory" title="Psychoanalytic theory">psychoanalytic theory</a> to explain the feelings of <a href="/wiki/Dependent_personality_disorder" title="Dependent personality disorder">dependency</a> and inadequacy that Black people experience in a White world. That the divided <a href="/wiki/Self-perception_theory" title="Self-perception theory">self-perception</a> of the Black Subject who has lost his native cultural origin, and embraced the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Homeland#Motherland" title="Homeland">Mother Country</a>, produces an <a href="/wiki/Inferiority_complex" title="Inferiority complex">inferiority complex</a> in the mind of the Black Subject, who then will try to <a href="/wiki/Cultural_appropriation" title="Cultural appropriation">appropriate</a> and imitate the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonizer</a>. Such behavior is more readily evident in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upward_mobility" title="Upward mobility">upwardly mobile</a> and educated black people who <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">can afford</a> to acquire <a href="/wiki/Status_symbol" title="Status symbol">status symbols</a> within the world of the colonial <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">ecumene</a>, such as an education abroad and mastery of the language of the colonizer, the white masks.</p>
<p>Based upon, and derived from, the concepts of the <a href="/wiki/Collective_unconscious" title="Collective unconscious">collective unconscious</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catharsis" title="Catharsis">collective catharsis</a>, the sixth chapter, “The Negro and Psychopathology”, presents brief, deep psychoanalyses of colonized black people, and thus proposes the inability of black people to fit into the norms (social, cultural, racial) established by white society. That “a normal Negro child, having grown up in a normal Negro family, will become abnormal on the slightest contact of the white world.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> That, in a white society, such an extreme psychological response originates from the unconscious and unnatural training of black people, from early childhood, to associate “blackness” with “wrongness”. That such unconscious mental training of black children is effected with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_books" title="Comic books">comic books</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartoons" title="Cartoons">cartoons</a>, which are cultural media that instil and affix, in the mind of the white child, the society’s cultural representations of black people as villains. Moreover, when black children are exposed to such images of villainous black people, the children will experience a psychopathology (psychological trauma), which mental wound becomes inherent to their individual, behavioral make-up; a part of his and her personality. That the early-life suffering of said psychopathology — black skin associated with villainy — creates a collective nature among the men and women who were reduced to colonized populations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Skin,_White_Masks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After its initial publication in the mid-twentieth century, <i>Black Skin, White Masks</i> (1952) remained an obscure book about the <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">servile</a> psychology imposed upon the colonised man, woman, and child — a <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">post-colonial</a> cultural legacy of the <a href="/wiki/Homeland#Motherland" title="Homeland">Mother Country</a> to her former <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperial</a> subjects. Since the 1980s, it has become an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-colonial" title="Anti-colonial">anti-colonial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-racist" title="Anti-racist">anti-racist</a> work in Anglophone countries; yet, in Francophone countries, the book is a relatively minor Fanon work, despite the subject’s explicit cultural connection with the societies of the black-skinned and other non-white peoples who were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Colonial_Empire" title="French Colonial Empire">French Colonial Empire</a> (1534–1980).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman-3">[3]</a></sup> The medical insights (psychological and psychiatric) remain valid sociologically, especially as applied by peoples of diverse colonial and imperial histories, such as the <a href="/wiki/Palestinians" title="Palestinians">Palestinians</a> in the Middle East, the <a href="/wiki/Tamils" title="Tamils">Tamils</a> in Sri Lanka, and <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> in the US, in their contemporary struggles for cultural and political autonomy. Contemporary theorists of <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a> and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-colonialism" title="Anti-colonialism">anti-colonialism</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">liberation theology</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>, have preferred Frantz Fanon’s later, culturally and politically <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutionary</a> works, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wretched_of_the_Earth" title="The Wretched of the Earth">The Wretched of the Earth</a></i> (1962), rather than the psychoanalytic explanation of colonial relations — between the colonizer and the colonized — that is <i>Black Skin, White Masks</i> (1952).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Black Skin, White Masks continues to spark intrigue and debate. In 2015, leading African studies scholar <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Gordon" title="Lewis Gordon">Lewis R. Gordon</a> published a book titled <i>What Fanon Said: A Philosophical Introduction To His Life And Thought.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anthony Elliott writes that <i>Black Skin, White Masks</i> is a "seminal" work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
