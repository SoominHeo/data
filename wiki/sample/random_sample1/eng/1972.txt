<p>The <b>black mamba</b> (<i>Dendroaspis polylepis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Venomous_snake" title="Venomous snake">venomous snake</a> <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan" title="Sub-Saharan">sub-Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Specimens vary in color from grey to dark brown, but not black. Juvenile black mambas tend to be lighter in color than adults and darken with age. It is the longest species of venomous snake indigenous to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_continent" title="African continent">African continent</a>; mature specimens generally exceed 2 meters (6.6 ft) and commonly attain 3 meters (9.8 ft). Specimens of 4.3 to 4.5 meters (14.1 to 14.8 ft) have been reported.</p>
<p>Although most <a href="/wiki/Mamba" title="Mamba">mamba</a> species are tree-dwelling snakes, the black mamba is not generally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, preferring lairs in <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> habitats in a range of terrains. These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">savannah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, rocky slopes and in some regions dense forest. It is <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> and chiefly an ambush predator, known to prey on <a href="/wiki/Hyrax" title="Hyrax">hyrax</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushbabies" title="Bushbabies">bushbabies</a> and other small mammals as well as birds. It is also a pursuit predator; in this it resembles some other long, speedy, highly-venomous species with well-developed vision. Over suitable surfaces it is possibly the speediest species of snake, capable of at least 11 km/h (6.8 mph) over short distances. Adult mambas have few natural predators.</p>
<p>In a threat display, the mamba usually opens its inky black mouth, spreads its narrow neck-flap and sometimes hisses. It is capable of striking at considerable range and occasionally may deliver a series of bites in rapid succession. Its venom is primarily composed of potent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxins" title="Neurotoxins">neurotoxins</a> which may cause fast onset of symptoms. Despite its reputation for being formidable and highly aggressive, like most snakes, it usually attempts to flee from humans unless threatened or cornered. Not being proximal to humans, bites from the black mamba are not frequent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The black mamba, <i>Dendroaspis polylepis</i>, is an <a href="/wiki/Elapidae" title="Elapidae">elapid</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendroaspis" title="Dendroaspis">Dendroaspis</a></i>. Although it had been known to missionaries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and residents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> prior to 1860, by the name "mamba". Which was already established in the <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> and taken from the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> word "<i>imamba</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> was made by <a href="/wiki/Albert_G%C3%BCnther" title="Albert Günther">Albert Günther</a> in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITIS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Database_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Database-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1873, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Peters" title="Wilhelm Peters">Wilhelm Peters</a> described two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>: the nominotypical <i>D.polylepis polylepis</i> and also <i>D.polylepis antinorii</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Database_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Database-3">[3]</a></sup> However, these are no longer held to be distinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITIS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1896, <a href="/wiki/George_Albert_Boulenger" title="George Albert Boulenger">Boulenger</a> combined the species (<i>Dendroaspis polylepis</i>) as a whole with the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_green_mamba" title="Eastern green mamba">eastern green mamba</a> (<i>Dendroaspis angusticeps</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">lumping</a> diagnosis that remained in force until 1946, when <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Frederick_Maynard_FitzSimons" title="Vivian Frederick Maynard FitzSimons">FitzSimons</a> <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">split</a> them again into separate species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haagner_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.BCnther_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.BCnther-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">generic name</a>, <i>Dendroaspis</i>, derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>dendro</i> (<i>δένδρο</i>), meaning "tree",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dendro_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dendro-10">[10]</a></sup> and <i>aspis</i> (<i>ασπίς</i>), which is understood to mean "shield",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but also denotes "cobra" or simply "snake", in particular "snake with hood (shield)". Via <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>aspis</i>, it is the source of the English word "asp". In ancient texts, <i>aspis</i> or asp often referred to the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_cobra" title="Egyptian cobra">Egyptian cobra</a> (<i>Naja haje</i>), in reference to its shield-like hood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asp_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-asp-12">[12]</a></sup> Thus, "Dendroaspis" literally means <i>tree asp</i>, reflecting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a> nature of most of the species within the genus. The genus was first described by the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist">ornithologist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpetologist" title="Herpetologist">herpetologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schlegel" title="Hermann Schlegel">Hermann Schlegel</a> in 1848.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlegel_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel-13">[13]</a></sup> Slowinski <i>et al.</i> (1997) pointed out that the relationships of the African genus <i>Dendroaspis</i> are problematical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slowinski_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slowinski-14">[14]</a></sup> However, evidence suggests that <i>Dendroaspis</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophiophagus" title="Ophiophagus">Ophiophagus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bungarus" title="Bungarus">Bungarus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Hemibungarus" title="Hemibungarus">Hemibungarus</a></i> form a solid non-<a href="/wiki/Coral_snake" title="Coral snake">coral snake</a> Afro-Asiatic clade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castoe_et_al_07_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castoe_et_al_07-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">specific epithet</a> <i>polylepis</i> is derived from the Ancient Greek <i>poly</i> (<i>πολύ</i>) meaning "many" and <i>lepis</i> (<i>λεπίς</i>) meaning "scale".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It apparently refers to the scale count of this species, which is higher than some other species in the genus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2001" data-file-width="1904" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG/220px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG/330px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG/440px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_striking.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The black interior of the mouth of a black mamba</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Dendroaspis polylepis</i> is a large, round-bodied, slender, but powerful snake. It tapers smoothly towards the tail, but is of markedly more <a href="/wiki/Robustness_(morphology)" title="Robustness (morphology)">robust</a> build than its distinctly <a href="/wiki/Gracility" title="Gracility">gracile</a> <a href="/wiki/Biological_specificity" title="Biological specificity">congeners</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendroaspis_angusticeps" title="Dendroaspis angusticeps">Dendroaspis angusticeps</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendroaspis_viridis" title="Dendroaspis viridis">Dendroaspis viridis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> The head is often said to be "coffin-shaped" with a somewhat pronounced brow ridge and a medium-sized eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROEA_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ROEA-18">[18]</a></sup> It is a highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteroglyphous" title="Proteroglyphous">proteroglyphous</a> snake, with <a href="/wiki/Fang" title="Fang">fangs</a> up to 6.5 millimeters (0.26 in) in length<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup> located at the front of the <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">maxilla</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> The adult snake's length ranges from 2 meters (6.6 ft) to 3 meters (9.8 ft) routinely<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> but, according to some sources, specimens have grown to lengths of 4.3 to 4.5 meters (14.1 to 14.8 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haagner_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> Black mambas weigh about 1.6 kilograms (3.5 lb)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> on average. A specimen of 1.41 meters (4.6 ft) was found to have weighed 651.7 g (1.437 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Dendroaspis polylepis</i> is the second longest venomous snake species, exceeded in length only by the <a href="/wiki/King_cobra" title="King cobra">king cobra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Specimens vary considerably in color; some are olive-brown to khaki, many are grey, but none are black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haagner_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> Some individuals display dark mottling towards the posterior, which may appear in the form of oblique bars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattison_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-Mattison-17">[17]</a></sup> The underbody is often pale yellow or cream colored and the eyes are dark brown to black with a silver or pale yellow corona surrounding the pupil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_div_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-animal_div-23">[23]</a></sup> Juvenile snakes are lighter in color than adults, typically grey or olive green in appearance, and they darken with age.</p>
<p>The black mamba has been said to take its name from the color of the inside of its mouth, but this claim is an <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Factoid" title="Factoid">factoid</a> that lacks objective or historical support. In particular, it was referred to as the "black mamba" decades before knowledge of the color of its oral <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucosa</a> was in common circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie1875_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie1875-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">Common names</a> for mambas are to be taken no more seriously than any other common names. We find for example that attempts to impose the more logical common name, "common mamba", for the "black mamba",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> have been abandoned in favor of applying "common mamba" to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_green_mamba" title="Eastern green mamba">Eastern green mamba</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eastern_green_mamba" title="Eastern green mamba">Dendroaspis angusticeps</a></i>. In the mean time, since the late twentieth century, some authors have unquestioningly copied the claim that the name derived from the color of its open mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The black mamba has a wide and fragmented range within <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>. Specifically, it has been observed in: north east <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, south western <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, southwards to <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> to <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a> in South Africa, and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>; then north easterly through <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> to south eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup> The black mamba's distribution contains gaps within the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>. These gaps may lead physicians to misidentify black mamba bites and administer an inappropriate antivenom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The black mamba was also recorded in 1954 in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup> However, this observation, and a subsequent observation that identified a second specimen in the region in 1956, has not been confirmed and thus the snake's distribution in this area is inconclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup> The black mamba is not commonly found at altitudes above 1,000 metres (3,300 ft), although its distribution does reach 1,800 metres (5,900 ft) in Kenya and 1,650 metres (5,410 ft) in Zambia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The black mamba is primarily <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a>, but occasionally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, especially where it occurs in forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maina_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maina-29">[29]</a></sup> Typically it inhabits neglected areas of scrub, termite mounds, abandoned burrows and rock crevices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> It is adapted to terrain ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">savannah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> to rocky slopes and dense forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-5"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup> The black mamba prefers moderately dry environments such as light woodland and scrub, rocky outcrops, and semi-arid dry savannah, to dense forest or arid desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_28-6"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1488" data-file-width="1055" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg/220px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg/330px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg/440px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_by_Bill_Love.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A black mamba in defensive posture</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The black mamba is graceful but skittish and often unpredictable. It is agile and can move quickly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> while being shy and secretive by nature and, like most snakes, avoids threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> In the wild, a black mamba seldom tolerates humans approaching more closely than about 40 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> When confronted it can display great truculence and is likely to gape in a threat display, exposing its black mouth and flicking its tongue. It also is likely to form a hood by spreading its neck-flap as <a href="/wiki/Cobra" title="Cobra">cobras</a> do. The mamba's hood however, is narrower than that of a typical cobra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> The threat display may be accompanied by audible hissing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the threat display, any sudden movement by the intruder may provoke the mamba into a series of rapid strikes leading to severe <a href="/wiki/Envenomation" title="Envenomation">envenomation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> Also, the size of the black mamba, plus its ability to raise its head well off the ground, enable it to launch as much as 40% of its body length upwards, so mamba bites in humans quite often are on the upper body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IWE_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IWE-30">[30]</a></sup> This behaviour also permits the snake to land a strike at unexpectedly long range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> The black mamba's reputed readiness to attack is often much exaggerated and usually is provoked by perceived threats, such as blocking its intended retreat, accidentally or otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed">Speed</span></h3>
<p>For its slender body, the black mamba is deceptively powerful; it is the fastest moving snake indigenous to Africa, and perhaps the fastest anywhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_D_V_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Van_D_V-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SevenDeadly_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SevenDeadly-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldBook_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorldBook-33">[33]</a></sup> It does however, move rather clumsily over soft sand, a surface to which it is not adapted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosew_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosew-34">[34]</a></sup> There have been many unrealistic stories concerning the black mamba's speed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JOLKKONEN_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-JOLKKONEN-35">[35]</a></sup> perhaps partly because the slender body exaggerates the impression of speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-36">[36]</a></sup> These stories include the myth that it can outrun a galloping horse or a running human. On 23 April 1906, on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serengeti_Plains" title="Serengeti Plains">Serengeti Plains</a>, an intentionally provoked black mamba was recorded at a speed of 11 km/h (6.8 mph), over a distance of 43 m (141 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A black mamba would almost certainly not be able to exceed 16 km/h (9.9 mph),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JOLKKONEN_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-JOLKKONEN-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-36">[36]</a></sup> and like most reptiles it can maintain such relatively high speeds only for short distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>The black mamba is <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> and chiefly an ambush predator, though it has been recorded as hunting partly by pursuit, such as catching pigeons at a watering hole before they can gain sufficient height to escape. It usually goes hunting from a permanent lair, to which it will regularly return, providing that the hunting is good and it is not disturbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup> It hunts mainly by sight and in doing so it commonly will raise much of its length well off the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup> The black mamba does not typically hold onto prey after biting, instead releasing its quarry and waiting for it to succumb to paralysis and die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_div_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-animal_div-23">[23]</a></sup> This however depends on the type of prey; for example, it typically will hold onto a bird till it stops struggling to escape. If prey attempts to escape or defend itself, the black mamba often may follow up its initial bite with a rapid series of strikes to incapacitate and quickly kill its prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marais_27-9"><a href="#cite_note-Marais-27">[27]</a></sup> The snake has been known to prey on <a href="/wiki/Hyrax" title="Hyrax">hyrax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyrax</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushbabies" title="Bushbabies">bushbabies</a>, and bats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haagner_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-8">[8]</a></sup> The black mamba has a potent digestive system and has been observed to digest prey fully within eight to ten hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-8"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Mongoose.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1848" data-file-width="2515" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Yellow_Mongoose.JPG/220px-Yellow_Mongoose.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Yellow_Mongoose.JPG/330px-Yellow_Mongoose.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Yellow_Mongoose.JPG/440px-Yellow_Mongoose.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Mongoose.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Yellow_mongoose" title="Yellow mongoose">yellow mongoose</a> will prey on black mambas</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Not many predators challenge an adult black mamba although it does face a few threats such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" title="Birds of prey">birds of prey</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snake_eagle" title="Snake eagle">snake eagles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branch1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Branch1-40">[40]</a></sup> Although all species of snake eagle commonly prey on snakes, there are two species in particular that do so with high frequency, including preying on black mambas. These are the <a href="/wiki/Black-chested_snake_eagle" title="Black-chested snake eagle">black-chested snake eagle</a> (<i>Circaetus pectoralis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Brown_snake_eagle" title="Brown snake eagle">brown snake eagle</a> (<i>Circaetus cinereus</i>). The <a href="/wiki/Cape_file_snake" title="Cape file snake">Cape file snake</a> (<i>Mehelya capensis</i>), which is apparently immune to all African snake venoms and preys on other snakes including venomous ones, is a common predator of black mambas (limited only by the size it can swallow).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haagner_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bauchot_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bauchot-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongooses" title="Mongooses">Mongooses</a> which also are partially immune to venom, and are often quick enough to evade a bite, will sometimes tackle a black mamba for prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Humans do not usually consume black mambas, but they often kill them out of fear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_mamba,_male-male_combat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="591" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg/220px-Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg/330px-Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg/440px-Black_mamba%2C_male-male_combat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_mamba,_male-male_combat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Male black mambas engaged in combat</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Black mambas breed annually and mating occurs in the early spring, when male mambas locate a female by following her scent trail. After finding a potential mate the male will inspect the female by flicking his tongue over her entire body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-9"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup> As in other species of snakes and many other reptiles, fertilization is internal and the <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organs</a> of the males are in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemipenes" title="Hemipenes">hemipenes</a>. Females lay clutches of eggs with an <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubation</a> period of some 80 to 90 days. Like most snakes female mambas are both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iteroparous" title="Iteroparous">iteroparous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-10"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup> Egg-laying typically occurs during the middle of summer and egg clutches range from 6 to 17 eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-11"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the mating season rival males may compete by wrestling, not by biting. Opponents attempt to subdue each other by intertwining their bodies and raising their heads high off the ground. Observers have on occasion mistaken such wrestling for mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.26B95_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.26B95-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black mambas are generally solitary, but not strictly so; as a rule they interact very little except in male rivalry during the mating season. However, black mambas are well known to share retreats occasionally, either with other mambas, or sometimes with other species of snakes.</p>
<p>In hatching, mambas break though the egg shell with an <a href="/wiki/Egg_tooth" title="Egg tooth">egg tooth</a> and are born with fully developed venom glands; capable of inflicting a potentially lethal bite minutes after hatching. The body of the newly hatched snake contains the residues of the egg yolk, and assimilates them to sustain the young snake until it finds its first prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-12"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifespan">Lifespan</span></h3>
<p>There is little information available concerning the lifespan of wild black mambas, but the longest surviving captive example had a recorded lifespan of 11 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-13"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20">[20]</a></sup> It is possible that wild snakes may live significantly longer than this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-14"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Venom">Venom</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> of the black mamba is extremely toxic, commonly causing collapse in humans within 45 minutes or less from a single bite. Without effective <a href="/wiki/Antivenom" title="Antivenom">antivenom</a> therapy, death typically occurs in 7–15 hours. The venom is chiefly composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxins" title="Neurotoxins">neurotoxins</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Dendrotoxin" title="Dendrotoxin">dendrotoxin</a>. The black mamba is capable of striking at considerable range and occasionally may deliver a series of bites in rapid succession. Despite its reputation for being highly aggressive, like most snakes, it usually attempts to flee from humans unless threatened or cornered.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1932" data-file-width="2648" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG/220px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG/330px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG/440px-Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dendroaspis_polylepis_eating.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The black mamba feeding</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The black mamba's venom is composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxins" title="Neurotoxins">neurotoxins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dendrotoxin" title="Dendrotoxin">dendrotoxin</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiotoxin" title="Cardiotoxin">cardiotoxins</a> as well as other toxins such as <a href="/wiki/Fasciculin" title="Fasciculin">fasciculins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FitzSimons70_19-15"><a href="#cite_note-FitzSimons70-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branch1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Branch1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CME_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-CME-46">[46]</a></sup> In an experiment, the most abundant toxin found in black mamba venom was observed to be able to kill a mouse in as little as 4.5 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniel_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daniel-47">[47]</a></sup> Based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice">murine</a> <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) values, the black mamba's toxicity from all published sources is as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>(SC) <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_injection" title="Subcutaneous injection">subcutaneous</a> (most applicable to real bites): 0.32 mg/kg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.26B95_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-S.26B95-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry.2C_Bryan_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fry.2C_Bryan-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShermanMinton_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShermanMinton-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> 0.28 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.26B95_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-S.26B95-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JerryWalls_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-JerryWalls-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>(IV) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous" title="Intravenous">intravenous</a>: 0.25 mg/kg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry.2C_Bryan_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fry.2C_Bryan-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShermanMinton_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShermanMinton-49">[49]</a></sup> 0.011 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>(IP) <a href="/wiki/Intraperitoneal_injection" title="Intraperitoneal injection">intraperitoneal</a>: 0.30 mg/kg (average),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> 0.941 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry.2C_Bryan_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fry.2C_Bryan-48">[48]</a></sup> 0.05 mg/kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErZug_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErZug-54">[54]</a></sup> (the last quote doesn't make it clear if is either intravenous or intraperitoneal).</li>
</ul>
<p>Its bites can deliver about 100–120 mg of venom on average and the maximum dose recorded is 400 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branch1_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Branch1-40">[40]</a></sup> It is reported that before <a href="/wiki/Antivenom" title="Antivenom">antivenom</a> was widely available, the mortality rate from a bite was nearly 100%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20">[20]</a></sup> The bite of a black mamba can potentially cause collapse in humans within 45 minutes, or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V.26C78_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-V.26C78-55">[55]</a></sup> Without effective antivenom therapy, death typically occurs in 7–15 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branch1_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Branch1-40">[40]</a></sup> Presently, there is a <a href="/wiki/Antivenom" title="Antivenom">polyvalent antivenom</a> produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_African_Institute_for_Medical_Research" title="South African Institute for Medical Research">South African Institute for Medical Research</a> to treat black mamba bites from many localities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-56">[56]</a></sup> and a new antivenom is currently being developed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_de_Costa_Rica" title="Universidad de Costa Rica">Universidad de Costa Rica</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instituto_Clodomiro_Picado" title="Instituto Clodomiro Picado">Instituto Clodomiro Picado</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez_et_al_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez_et_al-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>A bite from a black mamba causes initial neurological and neuromuscular symptoms which may commonly include headache and a metallic taste in the mouth, which may be accompanied by a triad of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paresthesias" title="Paresthesias">paresthesias</a>, profuse <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">perspiration</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivation" title="Salivation">salivation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECAJS_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECAJS-58">[58]</a></sup> Other symptoms may include <a href="/wiki/Ptosis_(eyelid)" title="Ptosis (eyelid)">ptosis</a> and gradual <a href="/wiki/Bulbar_palsy" title="Bulbar palsy">bulbar palsy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECAJS_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-ECAJS-58">[58]</a></sup> Localised pain or numbness around the bite site is common but not typically severe;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prague_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prague-59">[59]</a></sup> therefore, application of a <a href="/wiki/Tourniquet" title="Tourniquet">tourniquet</a> proximal to the bite site is feasible and may assist in slowing the onset of prominent <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxicity" title="Neurotoxicity">neurotoxicity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECAJS_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-ECAJS-58">[58]</a></sup> Without appropriate treatment, symptoms typically progress to more severe reactions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tachydysrhythmias" title="Tachydysrhythmias">tachydysrhythmias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neurogenic_shock" title="Neurogenic shock">neurogenic shock</a>, leading to death by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_collapse" title="Cardiovascular collapse">cardiovascular collapse</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_failure" title="Respiratory failure">respiratory failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branch1_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Branch1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CME_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-CME-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECAJS_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-ECAJS-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attacks_on_humans">Attacks on humans</span></h2>
<p>The black mamba is popularly regarded as the most dangerous and feared snake in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniel_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Daniel-47">[47]</a></sup> to South African locals the black mamba's bite is known as the "kiss of death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KissOfDeath_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-KissOfDeath-60">[60]</a></sup> However, attacks on humans by black mambas are rare, as they usually try to avoid confrontation, and their occurrence in highly populated areas is not very common compared with some other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-new-61">[61]</a></sup> Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Echis_ocellatus" title="Echis ocellatus">ocellated carpet viper</a> is responsible for more human fatalities due to <a href="/wiki/Snakebite" title="Snakebite">snakebite</a> than all other African species combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JerryWalls_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-JerryWalls-51">[51]</a></sup> A survey of snakebites in South Africa from 1957 to 1963 recorded over 900 venomous snakebites, but only seven of these were confirmed black mamba bites, at a time when effective antivenom was not widely available. Out of more than 900 bites, only 21 ended in fatalities, including all seven black mamba bites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oshea05_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oshea05-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reported_bite_cases">Reported bite cases</span></h3>
<p>Danie Pienaar, now head of <a href="/wiki/South_African_National_Parks" title="South African National Parks">South African National Parks</a> Scientific Services,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAT13_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAT13-63">[63]</a></sup> survived the bite of a black mamba without antivenom in 1998. Although no antivenom was administered, Pienaar was in serious condition, despite the hospital physicians having declared it a "moderate" black mamba envenomation. At one point, Pienaar lapsed into a coma and his prognosis was declared "poor". Upon arrival at hospital Pienaar was immediately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intubated" title="Intubated">intubated</a>, given supportive drug therapy, put on mechanical ventilation and placed on <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a> for three days, until the toxins were flushed out of his system. He was released from hospital on the fifth day. Pienaar believes he survived for several reasons. In an article in <i>Kruger Park Times</i> he said "Firstly, it was not my time to go." The article went on to state, "The fact that he stayed calm and moved slowly definitely helped. The tourniquet was also essential."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAKNP_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAKNP-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In another case, 28-year-old <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> student Nathan Layton was bitten by a black mamba and died of a heart attack in less than an hour in March 2008. The black mamba had been found near a classroom at the Southern African Wildlife College in <a href="/wiki/Hoedspruit" title="Hoedspruit">Hoedspruit</a>, where Layton was training to be a safari guide. Layton was bitten by the snake on his index finger while it was being put into a jar, but he didn't realize he'd been bitten. He thought the snake had only brushed his hand. Approximately 30 minutes after being bitten Layton complained of blurred vision. He collapsed and died of a heart attack, nearly an hour after being bitten. Attempts to revive him failed, and he was pronounced dead at the scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Layton_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Layton-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Layton2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Layton2-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a rare case of survival without treatment, American professional photographer Mark Laita was bitten on the leg by a black mamba during a photo-shoot of a black mamba at a facility in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. The bite ruptured an artery in his calf, which bled profusely. Laita did not go to the doctor or the hospital, and except for the swollen fang marks giving him intense pain during the night, he was not affected and was fine physically. This led him to believe that either the snake gave him a "<a href="/wiki/Dry_bite" title="Dry bite">dry bite</a>" (meaning without injecting venom) or the heavy bleeding pushed the venom out. Some commenters on the story suggested that it was a <a href="/wiki/Venomoid" title="Venomoid">venomoid</a> snake (in which the venom glands are surgically removed), but Laita responded that it was not. Only later, he found that he had captured the snake biting his leg in a photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooper_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hooper-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
