<p>The <b>black tamarin</b> (<i>Saguinus niger</i>) or <b>black-handed tamarin</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tamarin" title="Tamarin">tamarin</a> endemic to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black-handed tamarins are among the smallest primates, weighing approximately 500 grams. As with other tamarins, the hind limbs are longer than the forelimbs and the thumbs are not opposable. With the exception of the big toe, there are claws on all fingers and toes, as well as two molars on each side of the jaw. The face of the black tamarin is generally hairless. The fur is blackish-brown with lighter markings on the back, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Red-handed_tamarin" title="Red-handed tamarin">red-handed tamarin</a> but without the reddish-orange feet and hands of that species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowak_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nowak-4">[4]</a></sup> The black-handed tamarin was previously regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the red-handed tamarin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The geographic range of black-handed tamarins, which are endemic to Pará, Brazil, is limited by the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Rio Amazonas</a> (Amazon River) to the North, the <a href="/wiki/Tocantins_River" title="Tocantins River">Rio Tocantins</a> (Tocantins River) to the East, the <a href="/wiki/Xingu_River" title="Xingu River">Rio Xingu</a> (Xingu River) to the West,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> and until recently, the Rio Gradaus (Gradaus River) to the South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> Photographic evidence of a black-handed tamarin in Confresa, a town in the northeastern region of the state of Mato Grosso, indicates an increase in the species' geographic range to the South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some marmosets occupy small areas of forest in the Tapajós and Madeira Basins, but the black-handed tamarin is absent from these regions due to lack of food resources. However, this species is considered to be more tolerant of habitat disturbances than other Callitrichides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black-handed tamarins are arboreal primates that prefer semi-deciduous forest for ease of foraging and thick growth as cover from aerial and terrestrial predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to deforestation in eastern Brazil, black-handed tamarins regularly rely on fragmented and disturbed secondary forest habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup> Although much of the forest canopy has been destroyed, and black-handed tamarins have been seen moving through all levels of the forest, they are most active in the lower to middle (5-15m) strata of the forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> analysis, black-handed tamarins were found to be closer related to populations on the same side of the <a href="/wiki/Tocantins_River" title="Tocantins River">Tocantins River</a> than on the other, showing that the river constitutes an effective gene flow barrier. The closest living relative of the black-handed tamarin is thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Red-handed_tamarin" title="Red-handed tamarin">red-handed tamarin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foraging_Behavior">Foraging Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foraging Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species makes use of both primary and secondary forest during foraging, keeping primarily to the canopy and spending minimal time on the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup> Black-handed tamarins subsist largely on fruit. During the dry season (November through January), when fruit is scarcer, they will also take <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> such as grasshoppers and crickets, and eat the gum of <i><a href="/wiki/Parkia" title="Parkia">Parkia</a> pendula</i>, an evergreen tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup> Even during the dry season, however, fruit from <i><a href="/wiki/Tetragastris" title="Tetragastris">Tetragastris</a> altissima</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Inga_alba" title="Inga alba">Inga alba</a></i> trees is available. During the wet season, over 90 percent of the diet is fruit from as many as nine different species of trees, including <i>Inga stipularis</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Inga_edulis" title="Inga edulis">Inga edulis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bagassa" title="Bagassa">Bagassa</a> guianensis</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pouteria_lucuma" title="Pouteria lucuma">Pouteria lucuma</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pourouma" title="Pourouma">Pourouma</a> guianesis</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Manilkara_bidentata" title="Manilkara bidentata">Manilkara bidentata</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Chrysophyllum" title="Chrysophyllum">Chrysophyllum</a> sp.</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-12">[12]</a></sup> The seeds of many of these fruits are ingested and defecated. This process plays an important role in the regeneration of forests that are continuously threatened by logging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the black-handed tamarin’s small size, they are considered prey by many other animals. Their predators include man, diurnal birds of prey, snakes, and various cat species including <a href="/wiki/Margay" title="Margay">margay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocelot" title="Ocelot">ocelot</a>, which are capable arboreal hunters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-14">[14]</a></sup> Because black-handed tamarins have both aerial and terrestrial predators, they utilize any tree or bundled, thick foliage into which they can climb, and they maintain a group sleep system to keep safe while resting at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The black-handed tamarin's social structure serves as an important line of defense against their predators. While much of their day is spent foraging, each individual keeps a lookout for predators and uses calls to alert their social unit. Although intimidating displays may be made toward predators or in territorial defense, the black-handed tamarin's primary defense is flight. The group aspect of their social structure is important to survival, as a single black-handed tamarin would not be able to forage and keep watch for predators safely and effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_behavior">Breeding behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tamarins typically live in extended family groups of 4 to 15 individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-15">[15]</a></sup> Although groups of tamarins may have more than one female per group, only one typically breeds (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Goeldi%27s_marmoset" title="Goeldi's marmoset">Goeldi's marmoset</a>). Dominant female callitrichids suppress the reproductive activity of their daughters and other subordinate females within their family group. Because daughters of dominant females and other females born and remaining in a group tend not to ovulate, when a female becomes sexually mature, there are two options: either find another group or stay in the natal group. In the latter case, the daughter may someday become the dominant female, or she may cease to ovulate. Daughters may compete for the role of dominant female with their siblings. Subordinate members do not generally engage in sexual reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>All adult black-handed tamarin participate in raising and caring for the young, including non-reproductive members of the group (which may number 2-20 members). Group members coordinate and work together to find sufficient food items for infants and juveniles. Unlike many primate groups that provide juveniles with small amounts of food, this species has a cooperative breeding system that shares a substantial portion of food with the juveniles. When group size is large, the breeding male often spends less time attending to his own young because of the universal group effort. When there is sufficient food, or if the dominant female is close to the end of her reproductive cycle, multiple females may be allowed to reproduce in a group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of mating variations have been observed: <a href="/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals" title="Monogamy in animals">monogamy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polygyny_in_animals" title="Polygyny in animals">polygyny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyandry_in_nature" title="Polyandry in nature">polyandry</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Polygynandry" title="Polygynandry">polygynandy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:6-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifetime_reproduction">Lifetime reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lifetime reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ovulation in female tamarins typically occurs between 12 and 17 months of age, whereas in male tamarins, sperm production occurs slightly later at about 13 to 18 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:6-16">[16]</a></sup> The birth of infants has been observed in both early January and mid July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup> Black-handed tamarins (like all other Callitrichids) usually produce fraternal twins, but litters may consist of 2-4 infants. Triplets and quadruplets usually only occur in captivity. The growth of infants is very rapid; infants become independent within five months. Female callitrichids are able to conceive again after 2–4 weeks of giving birth to their previous offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-:6-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tamarin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The black-handed tamarin is classified as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> as a result of a 30% decrease in population size over the last 18 years, and is likely to become endangered if conservation measures are not taken. Because the major threat to the species appears to be a loss of suitable habitat, habitat conservation is thought to be key in their survival. The range of the black-handed tamarin is within one of the most densely inhabited areas of Brazil, where logging has removed a significant amount of the original forest, causing a decrease in food availability and refuges from predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-3">[3]</a></sup> However, black-handed tamarins are adaptable foragers that can make use of primary, secondary and logged forests, which increases the potential available range of habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-12">[12]</a></sup> Populations are present in several reserves where logging is prohibited, including <a href="/wiki/Gurupi_Biological_Reserve" title="Gurupi Biological Reserve">Gurupí Biological Reserve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tapirap%C3%A9_Biological_Reserve" title="Tapirapé Biological Reserve">Tapirapé Biological Reserve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caxiuan%C3%A3_National_Forest" title="Caxiuanã National Forest">Caxiuanã National Forest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-3">[3]</a></sup> In central-western Brazil, in the southern part of their range, most black-handed tamarins are only able to survive in the remains of forested areas on private properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
