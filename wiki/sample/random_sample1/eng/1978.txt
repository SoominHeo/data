<p><b>Crabs</b> are <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">decapod</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infraorder" title="Infraorder">infraorder</a> <b>Brachyura</b>, which typically have a very short projecting "<a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>) (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CF%81%CE%B1%CF%87%CF%8D%CF%82" title="wikt:βραχύς">βραχύς</a> / <i>brachys</i></span> = short,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BF%E1%BD%90%CF%81%CE%AC" title="wikt:οὐρά">οὐρά</a> / <i>οura</i></span> = tail<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), usually entirely hidden under the <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a>. They live in all the world's oceans, in fresh water, and on land, are generally covered with a thick exoskeleton and have a single pair of claws. Many other animals with similar names – such as <a href="/wiki/Hermit_crab" title="Hermit crab">hermit crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_crab" title="King crab">king crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porcelain_crab" title="Porcelain crab">porcelain crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_crab" title="Horseshoe crab">horseshoe crabs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crab_louse" title="Crab louse">crab lice</a> – are not true crabs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gecarcinus_quadratus_(Nosara).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg/220px-Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg/330px-Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg/440px-Gecarcinus_quadratus_%28Nosara%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gecarcinus_quadratus_(Nosara).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Gecarcinus_quadratus" title="Gecarcinus quadratus">Gecarcinus quadratus</a></i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_crab" title="Land crab">land crab</a> from <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Crabs are generally covered with a thick <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>, composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and armed with a single pair of <a href="/wiki/Chela_(organ)" title="Chela (organ)">chelae</a> (claws). Crabs are found in all of the world's oceans, while many crabs live in <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> and on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_crab" title="Land crab">land</a>, particularly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> regions. Crabs vary in size from the <a href="/wiki/Pea_crab" title="Pea crab">pea crab</a>, a few millimetres wide, to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_spider_crab" title="Japanese spider crab">Japanese spider crab</a>, with a leg span of up to 4 metres (13 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 850 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of crab are freshwater, terrestrial or semi-terrestrial species;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> they are found throughout the world's tropical and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">semi-tropical</a> regions. They were previously thought to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic_group" title="Monophyletic group">monophyletic group</a>, but are now believed to represent at least two distinct <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(evolution)" title="Lineage (evolution)">lineages</a>, one in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and one in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest unambiguous crab <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> date from the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> <i>Imocaris</i>, known only from its <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>, may be a primitive crab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_radiation" title="Adaptive radiation">radiation</a> of crabs in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> and afterward may be linked either to the break-up of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> or to the concurrent radiation of <a href="/wiki/Osteichthyes" title="Osteichthyes">bony fish</a>, crabs' main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sexual dimorphism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Crabs often show marked <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>. Males often have larger <a href="/wiki/Chela_(organ)" title="Chela (organ)">claws</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> a tendency which is particularly pronounced in the <a href="/wiki/Fiddler_crab" title="Fiddler crab">fiddler crabs</a> of the genus <i>Uca</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ocypodidae" title="Ocypodidae">Ocypodidae</a>). In fiddler crabs, males have one claw which is greatly enlarged and which is used for communication, particularly for attracting a mate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Another conspicuous difference is the form of the pleon (<a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>); in most male crabs, this is narrow and triangular in form, while females have a broader, rounded abdomen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This is because female crabs brood fertilised eggs on their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleopod" title="Pleopod">pleopods</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction_and_lifecycle">Reproduction and lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reproduction and lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crab_(Pachygrapsus_marmoratus)_on_Istrian_coast_(Adriatic_sea).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2459" data-file-width="4162" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg/220px-Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg/330px-Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg/440px-Crab_%28Pachygrapsus_marmoratus%29_on_Istrian_coast_%28Adriatic_sea%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>Crabs attract a mate through chemical (<a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>), visual, acoustic, or vibratory means. Pheromones are used by most fully aquatic crabs, while <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_crab" title="Terrestrial crab">terrestrial</a> and semiterrestrial crabs often use visual signals, such as <a href="/wiki/Fiddler_crab" title="Fiddler crab">fiddler crab</a> males waving their large claws to attract females. The vast number of brachyuran crabs have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_fertilisation" title="Internal fertilisation">internal fertilisation</a> and mate belly-to-belly. For many aquatic species, mating takes place just after the female has moulted and is still soft. Females can store the <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> for a long time before using it to fertilise their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">eggs</a>. When fertilisation has taken place, the eggs are released onto the female's abdomen, below the tail flap, secured with a sticky material. In this location, they are protected during embryonic development. Females carrying eggs are called "berried" since the eggs resemble round berries.</p>
<p>When development is complete, the female releases the newly hatched <a href="/wiki/Crustacean_larvae" title="Crustacean larvae">larvae</a> into the water, where they are part of the <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>. The release is often timed with the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>. The free-swimming tiny <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoea" title="Zoea">zoea larvae</a> can float and take advantage of water currents. They have a spine, which probably reduces the rate of predation by larger animals. The zoea of most species must find food, but some crabs provide enough <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_yolk" title="Egg yolk">yolk</a> in the eggs that the larval stages can continue to live off the yolk.</p>

<p>Each species has a particular number of zoeal stages, separated by <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moults</a>, before they change into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalopa" title="Megalopa">megalopa</a> stage, which resembles an adult crab, except for having the abdomen (tail) sticking out behind. After one more moult, the crab is a <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">juvenile</a>, living on the bottom rather than floating in the water. This last moult, from megalopa to juvenile, is critical, and it must take place in a habitat that is suitable for the juvenile to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:63–77</sup></p>
<p>Most species of terrestrial crabs must <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">migrate</a> down to the ocean to release their larvae; in some cases, this entails very extensive migrations. After living for a short time as larvae in the ocean, the juveniles must do this migration in reverse. In many tropical areas with land crabs, these migrations often result in considerable <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">roadkill</a> of migrating crabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:113–114</sup></p>
<p>Once crabs have become juveniles, they will still have to keep moulting many more times to become adults. They are covered with a hard shell, which would otherwise prevent growth. The moult cycle is coordinated by <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>. When preparing for moult, the old shell is softened and partly eroded away, while the rudimentary beginnings of a new shell form under it. At the time of moulting, the crab takes in a lot of water to expand and crack open the old shell at a line of weakness along the back edge of the <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>. The crab must then extract all of itself – including its legs, <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_mouthparts" title="Arthropod mouthparts">mouthparts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eyestalk" title="Eyestalk">eyestalks</a>, and even the lining of the front and back of the digestive tract – from the old shell. This is a difficult process that takes many hours, and if a crab gets stuck, it will die. After freeing itself from the old shell (now called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exuvia" title="Exuvia">exuvia</a>), the crab is extremely soft and hides until its new shell has hardened. While the new shell is still soft, the crab can expand it to make room for future growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:78–79</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Crabs typically walk sideways<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sleinis_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sleinis-15">[15]</a></sup> (a behaviour which gives us the word <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crabwise" title="wikt:crabwise">crabwise</a>), because of the articulation of the legs which makes a sidelong gait more efficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vidal-Gadea_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vidal-Gadea-16">[16]</a></sup> However, some crabs walk forwards or backwards, including <a href="/wiki/Raninidae" title="Raninidae">raninids</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Libinia_emarginata" title="Libinia emarginata">Libinia emarginata</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mictyris_platycheles" title="Mictyris platycheles">Mictyris platycheles</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sleinis_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sleinis-15">[15]</a></sup> Some crabs, notably the <a href="/wiki/Portunidae" title="Portunidae">Portunidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matutidae" title="Matutidae">Matutidae</a>, are also capable of swimming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ng-19">[19]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Portunidae" title="Portunidae">Portunidae</a> especially so as their last pair of walking legs is flattened into swimming paddles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:96</sup></p>
<p>Crabs are mostly active animals with complex behaviour patterns. They can communicate by drumming or waving their <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">pincers</a>. Crabs tend to be aggressive towards one another, and males often fight to gain access to females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On rocky <a href="/wiki/Shore" title="Shore">seashores</a>, where nearly all caves and crevices are occupied, crabs may also fight over hiding holes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fiddler_crab" title="Fiddler crab">Fiddler crabs</a> (genus <i>Uca</i>) dig burrows in sand or mud, which they use for resting, hiding, and mating, and to defend against intruders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:28–29, 99</sup></p>
<p>Crabs are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a>, feeding primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and taking any other food, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>, depending on their availability and the crab species. For many crabs, a mixed diet of plant and animal matter results in the fastest growth and greatest <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, some species are more specialised in their diets. Some eat plankton, some eat primarily shellfish like clams, and some even catch fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weis_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Weis-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:85</sup></p>
<p>Crabs are known to work together to provide food and protection for their family, and during mating season to find a comfortable spot for the female to release her eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crab_fisheries" title="Crab fisheries">Crab fisheries</a></div>
<p>Crabs make up 20% of all marine <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> caught, farmed, and consumed worldwide, amounting to 1.5 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> annually. One species, <i><a href="/wiki/Portunus_trituberculatus" title="Portunus trituberculatus">Portunus trituberculatus</a></i>, accounts for one-fifth of that total. Other commercially important <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> include <i><a href="/wiki/Portunus_pelagicus" title="Portunus pelagicus">Portunus pelagicus</a></i>, several species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Chionoecetes" title="Chionoecetes">Chionoecetes</a></i>, the blue crab (<i><a href="/wiki/Callinectes_sapidus" title="Callinectes sapidus">Callinectes sapidus</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Charybdis_(genus)" title="Charybdis (genus)"><i>Charybdis</i> spp.</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edible_crab" title="Edible crab">Cancer pagurus</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Dungeness_crab" title="Dungeness crab">Dungeness crab</a> (<i>Metacarcinus magister</i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Scylla_serrata" title="Scylla serrata">Scylla serrata</a></i>, each of which yields more than 20,000 tonnes annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some species, crab meat is harvested by manually twisting and <a href="/wiki/Declawing_of_crabs" title="Declawing of crabs">pulling off</a> one or both claws and returning the live crab to the water in the belief the crab will survive and regenerate the claws, thereby making it a sustainable industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cookery">Cookery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cookery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Crab_meat" title="Crab meat">Crab meat</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_crab_dishes" title="List of crab dishes">List of crab dishes</a></div>

Crab <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masala_(spice)" title="Masala (spice)">masala</a> from <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div>

<p>Crabs are prepared and eaten as a dish in several different ways all over the world. Some species are eaten whole, including the shell, such as <a href="/wiki/Soft-shell_crab" title="Soft-shell crab">soft-shell crab</a>; with other species, just the claws or legs are eaten. The latter is particularly common for larger crabs, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_crab" title="Snow crab">snow crab</a>. Mostly in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> cultures, the <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a> of the female crab is also eaten, which usually appears orange or yellow in fertile crabs.</p>
<p>In some regions, spices improve the culinary experience. In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indosphere" title="Indosphere">Indosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice_mix" title="Spice mix">masala</a> crab and <a href="/wiki/Chilli_crab" title="Chilli crab">chilli crab</a> are examples of heavily spiced dishes. In the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> region, blue crab is often eaten with <a href="/wiki/Old_Bay_Seasoning" title="Old Bay Seasoning">Old Bay Seasoning</a>. Alaskan king crab or snow crab legs are usually simply boiled and served with garlic or lemon butter.</p>
<p>For the British dish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cromer_crab" title="Cromer crab">Cromer crab</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crab_meat" title="Crab meat">crab meat</a> is extracted and placed inside the hard shell. One American way to prepare crab meat is by extracting it and adding a flour mix, creating a <a href="/wiki/Crab_cake" title="Crab cake">crab cake</a>. Crabs are also used in <a href="/wiki/Bisque_(food)" title="Bisque (food)">bisque</a>, a global dish of French origin.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crab_stick" title="Crab stick">Imitation crab</a>, made from minced fish products that are crafted to resemble crab meat, is disdained throughout the culinary industry as an unacceptably low-quality substitute for real crab. This does not hinder its popularity in Japan (where it originated) and in home cooking, where cost is often a chief concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pain">Pain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pain_in_crustaceans" title="Pain in crustaceans">Pain in crustaceans</a></div>
<p>Crabs are often boiled alive. In 2005, Norwegian scientists concluded that crustaceans could not feel pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, a study by Bob Elwood and Mirjam Appel of Queens University in Belfast, found that hermit crabs reacted to electric shocks. This may indicate that some crustaceans are able to feel and remember pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infraorder" title="Infraorder">infraorder</a> Brachyura contains 6,793 species in 93 <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ng-19">[19]</a></sup> as many as the remainder of the <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">Decapoda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_.26_Davis_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_.26_Davis-35">[35]</a></sup> The evolution of crabs is characterised by an increasingly robust body, and a reduction in the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>. Although many other groups have undergone similar processes, <a href="/wiki/Carcinisation" title="Carcinisation">carcinisation</a> is most advanced in crabs. The <a href="/wiki/Telson" title="Telson">telson</a> is no longer functional in crabs, and the <a href="/wiki/Uropod" title="Uropod">uropods</a> are absent, having probably evolved into small devices for holding the reduced abdomen tight against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sternum_(arthropod)" title="Sternum (arthropod)">sternum</a>.</p>
<p>In most decapods, the <a href="/wiki/Gonopore" title="Gonopore">gonopores</a> (sexual openings) are found on the legs. However, since crabs use the first two pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleopod" title="Pleopod">pleopods</a> (abdominal appendages) for sperm transfer, this arrangement has changed. As the male abdomen evolved into a narrower shape, the gonopores have moved towards the midline, away from the legs, and onto the sternum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A similar change occurred, independently, with the female gonopores. The movement of the female gonopore to the sternum defines the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> <a href="/wiki/Eubrachyura" title="Eubrachyura">Eubrachyura</a>, and the later change in the position of the male gonopore defines the <a href="/wiki/Thoracotremata" title="Thoracotremata">Thoracotremata</a>. It is still a subject of debate whether those crabs where the female, but not male, gonopores are situated on the sternum, form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_.26_Davis_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_.26_Davis-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_influences">Cultural influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Cancer_(constellation)" title="Cancer (constellation)">Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Astrological_sign" title="Astrological sign">astrological sign</a> <a href="/wiki/Cancer_(astrology)" title="Cancer (astrology)">Cancer</a> are named after the crab, and depicted as a crab. <a href="/wiki/William_Parsons,_3rd_Earl_of_Rosse" title="William Parsons, 3rd Earl of Rosse">William Parsons, 3rd Earl of Rosse</a> drew the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a> in 1848 and noticed its similarity to the animal; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crab_pulsar" title="Crab pulsar">Crab pulsar</a> lies at the centre of the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moche_(culture)" title="Moche (culture)">Moche</a> people of ancient <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> worshipped nature, especially the sea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and often depicted crabs in their art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karkinos" title="Karkinos">Karkinos</a> was a crab that came to the aid of the <a href="/wiki/Lernaean_Hydra" title="Lernaean Hydra">Lernaean Hydra</a> as it battled <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>. One of <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Just_So_Stories" title="Just So Stories">Just So Stories</a></i>, <i>The Crab that Played with the Sea</i>, tells the story of a gigantic crab who made the waters of the sea go up and down, like the tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
