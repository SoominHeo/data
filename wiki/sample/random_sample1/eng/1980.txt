<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ancient Germanic law</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>Several Latin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_code" title="Law code">law codes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a> written in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> (also known as <i><b>leges barbarorum</b></i> "laws of the <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_kingdoms" title="Barbarian kingdoms">barbarians</a>") survive, dating to between the 5th and 9th centuries. They are influenced by <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon law</a>, and earlier tribal customs.</p>
<p>Germanic law was codified in writing under the influence of Roman law; previously it was held in the memory of designated individuals who acted as judges in confrontations and meted out justice according to customary rote, based on careful memorization of <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a>. Among the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> they were called <i>rachimburgs</i>. "Living libraries, they were law incarnate, unpredictable and terrifying."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When justice is oral, the judicial act is personal and subjective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Power, whose origins were at once said to be magical, divine and military was, according to Michel Rouche, exercised jointly by the "throne-worthy" elected king and his free warrior companions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Oral law sufficed as long as the <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warband</a> was not settled in one place. Germanic law made no provisions for the public welfare, the <i><a href="/wiki/Res_publica" title="Res publica">res publica</a></i> of Romans.</p>
<p>The language of all these continental codes was <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>; the only known codes drawn up in any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic language</a> were the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_law" title="Anglo-Saxon law">Anglo-Saxon laws</a>, beginning with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_%C3%86thelberht" title="Laws of Æthelberht">Laws of Æthelberht</a> (7th century). In the 13th century customary Saxon law was codified in the vernacular as the <i><a href="/wiki/Sachsenspiegel" title="Sachsenspiegel">Sachsenspiegel</a></i>.</p>
<p>All these laws may be described in general as codes of governmental procedure and <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> of compositions. They all present somewhat similar features with <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a>, the best-known example, but often differ from it in the date of compilation, the amounts of fines, the number and nature of the crimes, the number, rank, duties and titles of the officers, etc.</p>
<p>In Germanic Europe in the Early Middle Ages, every man was tried according to the laws of his own race, whether Roman, Salian or Ripuarian Frank, Frisian, Burgundian, Visigoth, Bavarian etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of separate codes were drawn up specifically to deal with cases between ethnic Romans. These codes differed from the normal ones that covered cases between Germanic peoples, or between Germanic people and Romans. The most notable of these are the <i>Lex Romana Visigothorum</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Breviary_of_Alaric" title="Breviary of Alaric">Breviary of Alaric</a></i> (506), the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lex_Romana_Curiensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lex Romana Curiensis (page does not exist)">Lex Romana Curiensis</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lex_Romana_Burgundionum" title="Lex Romana Burgundionum">Lex Romana Burgundionum</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tacitus">Tacitus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tacitus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Germania_(book)" title="Germania (book)">Germania</a></i> gives an account of the legal practice of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a> of the 1st century. Tacitus reports that criminal cases were put before the <a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a> (tribal assembly). Lighter offenses were regulated with <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a> (paid in livestock), paid in part to the victim (or their family) and in part to the king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_penalty" title="Death penalty">death penalty</a> was reserved for two kinds of capital offenses: military treason or desertion was punished by <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a>, and corporal infamy <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (rape) by throwing the condemned into a <a href="/wiki/Bog_body" title="Bog body">bog</a>.</p>
<p>The difference in punishment is explained by the idea that "glaring iniquities" must be exposed in plain sight, while "effeminacy and pollution" should best be buried and concealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Minor legal disputes were settled on a day-to-day basis by elected chiefs assisted by elected officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weregeld" title="Weregeld">weregeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_combat" title="Trial by combat">trial by combat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kn%C3%A9setja" title="Knésetja">knésetja</a></div>
<p>The Germanic law codes are designed for a clearly <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">stratified society</a> fixated on castes determined by descent or <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>. Legal status, and therefore freedom, was based on a person's caste, discriminating between royals and two or three successive castes of nobility, where the lower were reconned as peasants or freemen (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">OE</a> <i>freo man</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">OHG</a> <i>frīhals</i>), and those who are laymen, or bondmen (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">ON</a> <i>þræll</i>). Accordingly, descent (nativitate) was determining who would attend the various things (house-things, local things, regional things and inter-regional or royal things). Thus the bondmen were <i>ipso facto</i> represented by their family-heads - the local nobility - on the regional things of the nobles. The same differentiation (in castes) were defining who could convey and inherit property. In regulation of tribal feuds and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weregeld" title="Weregeld">weregeld</a> a similar discrimination is seen.</p>
<p>At the head of the nobility (adalmen, eaorls, ceorls and freemen) was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_king" title="Germanic king">king</a> overseeing the laws, rights and privileges. Under the king came the highborn nobility (OE <i>æþelu</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">OS</a> <i>aðali</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">Germ</a> <i>Adel</i>) and the middle nobility (OE <i>eorl</i>, OS/OHG <i>eorl</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">ON</a> <i>jarl</i>). The lower nobility were the ordinary freemen (OE <i>ċeorl</i>, Frankish <i>baro</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundian_language_(Germanic)" title="Burgundian language (Germanic)">Burgundian</a> <i>leudis</i>). Under these ceorls (peasants or freemen) came the serfs - as in 'laymen'. Skilled serfs permitted to leave their homesteads were often called 'leysing' or 'free-men' (OE <i>læt</i>, <i>freolæta</i>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">MDu</a> <i>laet</i>, <i>vrilaet</i>, ON <i>leysíngr</i>). Otherwise common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laymen" title="Laymen">laymen</a> were addressed as 'tjod' (OE <i>þēow</i>, OHG <i>diut</i>, OMG <i>deut</i>, ON <i>þjod</i>, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Goth</a> <i>þius</i>). As the Roman church gained political power in Europe this system was augmented by incorporating a separate class of <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, where their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishops" title="Bishops">bishops</a> were to be considered of equal status as a nobleman.</p>
<p>The Germanic law system is in principle based on <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">compensation</a> rather than revenge. Any injury must be compensated according to the damage done, regardless of motive or intent. Even for capital crimes like murder, the compensation is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weregeld" title="Weregeld">weregeld</a>, a fixed amount depending on the sex and social status of the victim. The practice of paying part of the damages to the king survives in the earliest Anglo-Saxon law code (Laws of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelberht_of_Kent" title="Æthelberht of Kent">Æthelberht</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>), under the term <i>drihtinbeah</i>, but seems to have been discontinued after Christianisation. As thralls are considered the property of their lord, crimes committed by thralls must be compensated by their headmasters just like damage caused by animals.</p>
<p>The most extreme punishment for crimes considered irredeemable seems to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outlawry" title="Outlawry">outlawry</a>, i.e. the declaration of the guilty party as beyond the protection of the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In most instances this may have been equivalent to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_sentence" title="Death sentence">death sentence</a> in practice, but the actual death penalty seems to have been foreseen only for very rare cases, such as sexual crimes (rape, promiscuity), religious crimes (incest) or crimes against the king (treason). <a href="/wiki/Lex_Alamannorum" title="Lex Alamannorum">Alamannic law</a> also foresees the death penalty for plotting to assassinate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Alemannia" title="Duke of Alemannia">duke</a>, and for military treason (assisting enemies or causing rebellion in the army), but in these cases the penalty may also be outlawry or a fine, depending on the judgement of the duke or the chieftains.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weregeld" title="Weregeld">weregeld</a> was set at a basic amount of 200 <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a>, which could be multiplied depending on the status (descent, caste) of the victim. In Anglo-Saxon law, the regular freeman is known as a <i>two-hynde man</i> ("a man worth 200"), and noblemen are either <i>six-hynde man</i> (threefold weregeld) or <i>twelve-hynde man</i> (sixfold weregeld). In <a href="/wiki/Lex_Alamannorum" title="Lex Alamannorum">Alamannic law</a>, the basic weregeld for a freeman is likewise 200 shillings. Alamannic tradition is particular in doubling the fee if the victim was a woman, so that the weregeld for a free woman is 400 shillings. The weregeld for a priest is threefold, i.e. 600 shillings. Alamannic law further introduces the concept of premeditated <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> (as opposed to deaths by accident or in combat), which is fined by ninefold weregeld. The Anglo-Saxon <i>Norðleoda laga</i> ("North-people's law") is unique in setting an explicit amount for a king's weregeld, at 30,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tremisses" title="Tremisses">tremisses</a>, explaining that 15,000 tremises is for the man (the same amount as for an atheling or an archbishop) and another 15,000 for the damage to the kingdom.</p>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, Germanic law mentions <a href="/wiki/Cruentation" title="Cruentation">cruentation</a> as a means to prove guilt or innocence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_law_codes">Individual law codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Individual law codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal ancient Germanic law codes are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Code_of_Euric" title="Code of Euric">Code of Euric</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>) - c. 480</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lex_Burgundionum" title="Lex Burgundionum">Lex Burgundionum</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gundobad" title="Gundobad">Gundobad</a>) - c. 500</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lex_Salica" title="Lex Salica">Lex Salica</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Salian_Franks" title="Salian Franks">Salian Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a>) - c. 500</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lex_Alamannorum" title="Lex Alamannorum">Pactus Alamannorum</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>) - c. 620</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visigothic_law_codes">Visigothic law codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visigothic law codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Euric" title="Code of Euric">Code of Euric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Code" title="Visigothic Code">Visigothic Code</a></div>
<p>Compared with other barbarian tribes, the Goths had the longest time of contact with Roman civilization, from migration in 376 to trade interactions years beforehand. The Visigothic legal attitude held that laws were created as new offenses of justice arose, and that the king's laws originated from God and His justice-scriptural basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Mercifulness (<i>clementia</i>) and a paternal feeling (<i>pietas</i>) were qualities of the king exhibited through the laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The level of severity of the law was "tempered" by this mercy, specifically for the poor; it was thought that by showing paternal love in formation of law, the legislator gained the love of citizen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> While the monarch's position was implicitly supreme and protected by laws, even kings were subject to royal law, for royal law was thought of as God's law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In theory, enforcement of the law was the duty of the king, and as the sovereign power he could ignore previous laws if he desired, which often led to complications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> To regulate the king's power, all future kings took an oath to uphold the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> While the Visigoths' law code reflected many aspects of Roman law, over time it grew to define a new society's requirements and opinions of law's significance to a particular people.</p>
<p>It is certain that the earliest written code of the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> dates to <a href="/wiki/Euric" title="Euric">Euric</a> (471). Code of Euric (<i>Codex Euricianus</i>), issued between 471 and 476, has been described as "the best legislative work of the fifth century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was created to regulate the Romans and Goths living in Euric's kingdom, where Romans greatly outnumbered Goths. The code borrowed heavily from the Roman Theodosian Code (<i>Codex Theodosianus</i>) from the early 5th century, and its main subjects were Visigoths living in Southern France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It contained about 350 clauses, organized by chapter headings; about 276 to 336 of these clauses remain today. Besides his own constitutions, Euric included in this collection the unwritten constitutions of his predecessors <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_I" title="Theodoric I">Theodoric I</a> (419-451), <a href="/wiki/Thorismund" title="Thorismund">Thorismund</a> (451-453), and <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_II" title="Theodoric II">Theodoric II</a> (453-466), and he arranged the whole in a logical order. Of the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Euric" title="Code of Euric">Code of Euric</a>, fragments of chapters 276 to 337 have been discovered in a palimpsest manuscript in the Bibliothèque Nationale at <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> (Latin coll, No. 12161), proving that the code ran over a large area. Euric's code was used for all cases between <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, and between them and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>; in cases between Romans, <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> was used.</p>
<p>At the insistence of Euric's son, <a href="/wiki/Alaric_II" title="Alaric II">Alaric II</a>, an examination was made of the Roman laws in use among Romans in his dominions, and the resulting compilation was approved in 506 at an assembly at Aire, in <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">Gascony</a>, and is known as the <a href="/wiki/Breviary_of_Alaric" title="Breviary of Alaric">Breviary of Alaric</a>, and sometimes as the <i>Liber Aniani</i>, from the fact that the authentic copies bear the signature of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Referendarius" title="Referendarius">referendarius</a></i> Anian. organized by chapter headings; about 276 to 336 of these clauses remain today. In 506 CE, Alaric II, son of Euric, assembled the council of Agde to issue the Breviary of Alaric (<i>Lex Romana Visigothorum</i>), applying specifically to Hispano-Roman residents of the Iberian Peninsula,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> where Alaric had migrated the Visigoth population. Both the Code of Euric and Breviary of Alaric borrowed heavily from the Theodosian Code. Euric, for instance, forbade intermarriage between Goths and Romans, which was already expressed in the <i>Codex Theodosianus</i>. The <i>Lex Romana Visigothorum</i> remained a source of law in the area that later became southern France long after it had been superseded in the Iberian peninsula by the <i>Lex Visigothorum</i> (see below).</p>
<p>Euric's code remained in force among the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Kingdom</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>) until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Liuvigild" title="Liuvigild">Liuvigild</a> (568-586), who made a new one, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codex_Revisus" title="Codex Revisus">Codex Revisus</a>, improving upon that of his predecessor. This work is lost, and we have no direct knowledge of any fragment of it. In the 3rd codification, however, many provisions have been taken from the 2nd, and these are designated by the word <i>antiqua</i>; by means of these <i>antiqua</i> we are enabled in a certain measure to reconstruct the work of Leovigild.</p>
<p>After the reign of Leovigild, the legislation of the Visigoths underwent a transformation. New laws made by the kings were declared to be applicable to all subjects in the kingdom, of whatever race; in other words, they became territorial; and this principle of territoriality was gradually extended to the ancient code. Moreover, the conversion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reccared" title="Reccared">Reccared</a> (586-601) from <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arianism</a> to orthodox Christianity effaced the religious differences among his subjects, and all subjects, being <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a>, had to submit to the canons of the councils, made obligatory by the kings.</p>
<p>In 643, Visigoth king <a href="/wiki/Chindasuinth" title="Chindasuinth">Chindasuinth</a> (642-653) proposed a new <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Code" title="Visigothic Code">Visigothic Code</a>, the <i>Lex Visigothorum</i> (also called the <i>Liber Iudiciorum</i> or <i>Forum Iudicium</i>), which replaced both the Code of Euric and the Breviary of Alaric. His son, <a href="/wiki/Recceswinth" title="Recceswinth">Recceswinth</a> (649-672), refined this code in its rough form and issued it officially in 654. This code applied equally to both Goths and Romans, presenting "a sign of a new society of Hispania developing in the seventh century, distinctly different from Gothic or Roman".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <i>Liber Iudiciorum</i> also marked a shift in the view of the power of law in reference to the king. It stressed that the <i>Liber Iudiciorum</i> alone is law, absent of any relation to any kingly authority, instead of the king being the law and the law merely an expression of his decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The lacunae in these fragments have been filled by the aid of the law of the <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarians</a>, where the chief Divisions are reintroduced, divided into 12 books, and subdivided into <i>tituli</i> and chapters (<i>aerae</i>). It comprises 324 constitutions taken from Leovigild's collection, a few of the laws of Reccared and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sisebur" title="Sisebur">Sisebur</a>, 99 laws of Chindasuinth, and 87 of Reccasuinth. A recension of this code of Reccasuinth was made in 681 by King <a href="/wiki/Erwig" title="Erwig">Erwig</a> (680-687), and is known as the <i>Lex Wisigothorum renovate</i>; and, finally, some <i>additamenta</i> were made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergica" title="Ergica">Ergica</a> (687-702).</p>
<p>The <i>Liber Iudiciorum</i> makes several striking differences from Roman law, especially concerning the issue of inheritance. According to the <i>Liber Iudiciorum</i>, if incest is committed, the children can still inherit, whereas in Roman law the children were disinherited and could not succeed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Title II of Book IV outlines the issue of inheritance under the newly united Visigothic Code: section 1, for instance, states that sons and daughters inherit equally if their parents die instate, section 4 says that all family members should inherit if no will exists to express the intentions of the deceased, and the final section expresses a global law of Recceswinth, stating that anyone left without heirs has the power to do what they want with their possessions. This statement recalls the Roman right for a person to leave his possessions to anyone in his will, except this Visigothic law emphasizes males and females equally, whereas, in Roman law, only males (particularly the <i>pater familias</i>) are allowed to make a will.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Burgundionum">Lex Burgundionum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lex Burgundionum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lex_Burgundionum" title="Lex Burgundionum">Lex Burgundionum</a></div>
<p>This is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_code" title="Law code">law code</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, probably issued by king <a href="/wiki/Gundobad" title="Gundobad">Gundobad</a>. It is influenced by <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> and deals with domestic laws concerning marriage and inheritance as well as regulating <a href="/wiki/Weregild" title="Weregild">weregild</a> and other penalties. Interaction between Burgundians is treated separately from interaction between Burgundians and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman" title="Gallo-Roman">Gallo-Romans</a>. The oldest of the 14 surviving manuscripts of the text dates to the 9th century, but the code's institution is ascribed to king Gundobad (died 516), with a possible revision by his successor <a href="/wiki/Sigismund" title="Sigismund">Sigismund</a> (died 523). The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lex_Romana_Burgundionum" title="Lex Romana Burgundionum">Lex Romana Burgundionum</a></i> is a separate code, containing various laws taken from Roman sources, probably intended to apply to the <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>' Gallo-Roman subjects. The oldest copy of this text dates to the 7th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Salica">Lex Salica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lex Salica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a></div>
<p>The exact origins of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> are uncertain: they were a group of Germanic peoples that settled in the lower regions of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> river. They were not a unified people at the start of the 3rd century but consisted of many tribes which were loosely connected with one another. Although they were intertwined with the Roman Empire the Franks were not a part of it. "No large body of Franks was admitted into the Empire, but individuals and small groups did cross."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-21">[21]</a></sup> The Romans were seen as a lower rank in Frankish society. With larger numbers the Franks over took the region of the Rhine. Latin became the secondary language to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Frankish" title="Old Frankish">the Germanic one of the Franks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankish_law" title="Frankish law">Frankish law</a> took precedence among the people. The Romans even embraced the "Barbarians" to the north at times, making them allies to fight off the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>.</p>
<p>The Franks were broken down into east and west regions. The Eastern Franks were known as the <a href="/wiki/Ripuarian_Franks" title="Ripuarian Franks">Ripuarians</a> and those west of the Rhine were known as the <a href="/wiki/Salian_Franks" title="Salian Franks">Salian Franks</a>. It was King <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis</a> who united the Franks under one law after defeating his rivals in 509 CE. It is during this time of unification that King Clovis developed the Salic Law.</p>
<p>The Lex Salica was a similar body of law to the Lex Burgundionum. It was compiled between 507 and 511 CE. The body of law deals with many different aspects of Frank society. The charges range from inheritance to murder and theft. The Salic law was used to bring order to Frank society, the main punishment for crimes being a fine with a worth designated to the type of crime. The law uses capital punishment only in cases of witchcraft and poisoning. This absence of violence is a unique feature of the Salic Law.</p>
<p>The code was originally brought about by the Frankish King Clovis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-21">[21]</a></sup> The code itself is a blue print for Frankish society and how the social demographics were assembled. One of the main purposes of the Salic Law is to protect a family's inheritance in the agnatic succession. This emphasis on inheritance made the Salic Law a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality#Agnatic_succession" title="Patrilineality">agnatic succession</a>, and in particular for the "fundamental law" that no woman could be king of France.</p>
<p>The use of fines as the main reparation made it so that those with the money to pay the fine had the ability to get away with the most heinous of crimes. "Those who commit rape shall be compelled to pay 2500 denars, which makes 63 shillings." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-21">[21]</a></sup> Rape was not the only detailed violent crime. The <a href="/wiki/Child_murder" title="Child murder">murder of children</a> is broken down by age and gender, and so is the murder of women.</p>
<p>Paying fines broke the society into economic and social demographics in that the wealthy were free to do as much as they could afford, whereas the fines themselves placed different values on the gender and racial demographics. This social capital is evident in the differences in the Salic Law's punishment for murder based on a woman's ability to bear children. Women who could bear children were protected by a 600 shilling fine while the fine for murdering a woman who could no longer bear children was only 200 shillings. It is also interesting that all crimes committed against Romans had lesser fines than other social classes. In the case of inheritance, it is made very clear that all property belongs to the males in the family. This also means that all debt also belongs to the males of the family.</p>
<p>The Salic Law outlines a unique way of securing the payment of money owed. It is called the <i>Chrenecruda</i> (or <i>crenecruda</i>, <i>chren ceude</i>, <i>crinnecruda</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-21">[21]</a></sup> In cases where the debtor could not pay back a loan in full they were forced to clear out everything from their home. If the debt still could not be paid off the owner could collect dust from all four corners of the house and cross the threshold. The debtor then turned and face the house with their next of kin gathered behind them. The debtor threw the dust over their shoulder. The person (or persons) that the dust fell upon was then responsible for the payment of the debt. The process continued through the family until the debt was paid. <i>Chrenecruda</i> helped secure loans within the Frankish society. It intertwined the loosely gathered tribes and helped to establish government authority. The process made a single person part of a whole group.</p>
<p>The Salic Law exists in two forms: the <i>Pactus Legis Salicae</i>, which is near to the original form approved by Clovis, and the <i>Lex Salica</i>, which is the edited form approved by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>. Both are published in the <a href="/wiki/Monumenta_Germaniae_Historica" title="Monumenta Germaniae Historica">Monumenta Germaniae Historica</a>'s <i>Leges</i> series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Ripuaria">Lex Ripuaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lex Ripuaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lex_Ripuaria" title="Lex Ripuaria">Lex Ripuaria</a></div>
<p>In the first half of the 7th century the <a href="/wiki/Ripuarian_Franks" title="Ripuarian Franks">Ripuarian Franks</a> received the Ripuarian law, a law code applying only to them, from the dominating <a href="/wiki/Salian_Franks" title="Salian Franks">Salian Franks</a>. The Salians, following the custom of the Romans before them, were mainly re-authorizing laws already in use by the Ripuarians, so that the latter could retain their local constitution.</p>
<p>The law of the Ripuarians contains 89 chapters and falls into three heterogeneous divisions. Chapters 1-31 consist of a scale of compositions; but, although the fines are calculated, not on the unit of 15 <i>solidi</i>, as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salic_Law" title="Salic Law">Salic Law</a>, but on that of 18 <i>solidi</i>, it is clear that this part is already influenced by the Salic Law. Chapters 32-64 are taken directly from the Salic Law; the provisions follow the same arrangement; the unit of the compositions is 15 <i>solidi</i>; but capitularies are interpolated relating to the affranchisement and sale of immovable property. Chapters 65-89 consist of provisions of various kinds, some taken from lost capitularies and from the Salic Law, and others of unknown origin.</p>
<p>The compilation apparently goes back to the reign of <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_I" title="Dagobert I">Dagobert I</a> (629-639)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pactus_Alamannorum_and_Lex_Alamannorum">Pactus Alamannorum and Lex Alamannorum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pactus Alamannorum and Lex Alamannorum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pactus_Alamannorum" title="Pactus Alamannorum">Pactus Alamannorum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lex_Alamannorum" title="Lex Alamannorum">Lex Alamannorum</a></div>
<p>Of the laws of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, who dwelt between the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lech_(river)" title="Lech (river)">Lech</a>, and spread over <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> and what is now <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> to the south of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a>, we possess two different texts.</p>
<p>The earlier text, of which five short fragments have come down to us, is known as the <i>Pactus Alamannorum</i>, and judging from the persistent recurrence of the expression <i>et sic convenit</i>, was most probably drawn up by an official commission. The reference to <i>aifranchisement in ecciesia</i> shows that it was composed after the conversion of the Alamanni to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. There is no doubt that the text dates back at least to the reign of the Frankish king <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_I" title="Dagobert I">Dagobert I</a>, i.e. to the first half of the 7th century.</p>
<p>The later text, known as the <i>Lex Alamannorum</i>, dates from a period when Alamannia was independent under national <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">dukes</a>, but recognized the theoretical suzerainty of the Frankish kings. There seems no reason to doubt the St. Gall manuscript, which states that the law had its origin in an agreement between the great Alamannic lords and Duke <a href="/wiki/Lantfrid" title="Lantfrid">Lantfrid</a>, who ruled the <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">duchy</a> from 709 to 730.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leges_Langobardorum">Leges Langobardorum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Leges Langobardorum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Edictum_Rothari" title="Edictum Rothari">Edictum Rothari</a></div>
<p>We possess a fair amount of information on the origin of the code of laws of the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>. The first part, consisting of 388 chapters, also known as the <i>Edictus Langobardorum</i>, and was promulgated by King <a href="/wiki/Rothari" title="Rothari">Rothari</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Diet_(assembly)" title="Diet (assembly)">diet</a> held at <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a> on 22 November 643. This work, composed at one time and arranged on a systematic plan, is very remarkable. The compilers knew Roman law, but drew upon it only for their method of presentation and for their terminology; and the document presents Germanic law in its purity. Rothar's edict was augmented by his successors: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimoald_I_of_Benevento" title="Grimoald I of Benevento">Grimwald</a> (668) added nine chapters; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liutprand_the_Lombard" title="Liutprand the Lombard">Liutprand</a> (713-735), fifteen volumes, containing a great number of ecclesiastical enactments; <a href="/wiki/Ratchis" title="Ratchis">Ratchis</a> (746), eight chapters; and <a href="/wiki/Aistulf" title="Aistulf">Aistulf</a> (755), thirteen chapters. After the union of the Lombards to the Frankish kingdom, the capitularies made for the entire kingdom were applicable to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. There were also special capitularies for Italy, called <i>Capitula Italica</i>, some of which were appended to the edict of Rothar.</p>
<p>At an early date, compilations were formed in Italy for the use of legal practitioners and jurists. <a href="/wiki/Eberhard" title="Eberhard">Eberhard</a>, duke and margrave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhaetia" title="Rhaetia">Rhaetia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">Friuli</a>, arranged the contents of the edict with its successive <i>additamenta</i> into a <i>Concordia de singulis causis</i> (829-832). In the 10th century a collection was made of the capitularies in use in Italy, and this was known as the <i>Capitulare Langobardorum</i>. Then appeared, under the influence of the school of law at <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>, the <i>Liber legis Langobardorum</i>, also called <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Papiensis" title="Liber Papiensis">Liber Papiensis</a></i> (beginning of 11th century), and the <i>Lombarda</i> (end of 11th century), in two forms, that given in a <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a> manuscript and known as the <i>Lombarda Casinensis</i> and the <i>Lombarda Vulgata</i>. In some, but not all, manuscripts of the <i>Liber Papiensis</i> each section of the edict is accompanied by specimen pleadings setting out the cause of action: in this way it comes near to being a treatment of substantive law as opposed to a simple tariff of penalties as found in the other <i>Leges barbarorum</i></p>
<p>There are editions of the <i>Edictus</i>, the <i>Concordia</i>, and the <i>Liber Papiensis</i> by F. Bluhme and A. Boretius in the <i><a href="/wiki/Monumenta_Germaniae_Historica" title="Monumenta Germaniae Historica">Monumenta Germaniae Historica</a></i> series, Leges (in folio) vol. iv. Bluhme also gives the rubrics of the <i>Lombardae</i>, which were published by F. Lindenberg in his <i>Codex legum antiquarum</i> in 1613. For further information on the laws of the Lombards see J. Merkel, <i>Geschichte des Langobardenrechts</i> (1850); A. Boretius, <i>Die Kapitularien im Langobardenreich</i> (1864); and C. Kier, <i>Edictus Rotari</i> (Copenhagen, 1898). Cf. R. Dareste in the <i>Nouvelle Revue historique de droit français et étranger</i> (1900, p. 143).</p>
<p>Lombard law, as developed by the Italian jurists, was by far the most sophisticated of the early Germanic systems, and some (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Frederic_William_Maitland" title="Frederic William Maitland">Frederic William Maitland</a>) have seen striking similarities between it and early English law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It remained living law, subject to modifications, both in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Lombards" title="Kingdom of the Lombards">Kingdom of the Lombards</a> that became the Carolingian Kingdom of Italy and in the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Benevento" title="Duchy of Benevento">Duchy of Benevento</a> that became the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> and continued to play a role in the latter as late as the 18th century. The <i><a href="/wiki/Libri_Feudorum" title="Libri Feudorum">Libri Feudorum</a></i>, explaining the distinctive Lombard version of feudalism, were frequently printed together with the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a> and were considered the academic standard for feudal law, influencing other countries including Scotland.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Baiuvariorum">Lex Baiuvariorum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lex Baiuvariorum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lex_Baiuvariorum" title="Lex Baiuvariorum">Lex Baiuvariorum</a></div>
<p>We possess an important law of the Bavarians, whose duchy was situated in the region east of the river <a href="/wiki/Lech_(river)" title="Lech (river)">Lech</a>. Parts of this law have been taken directly from the Visigothic law of Euric and from the law of the Alamanni. The Bavarian law, therefore, is later than that of the Alamanni. It dates unquestionably from a period when the Frankish authority was very strong in Bavaria, when the dukes were subjects of the Frankish kings. The law's compilation is most commonly dated between 744 and 748, by the following argument; Immediately after the revolt of Bavaria in 743 the Bavarian Duke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odilo_of_Bavaria" title="Odilo of Bavaria">Odilo</a> (died 748) was forced to submit to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pippin_the_Younger" title="Pippin the Younger">Pippin the Younger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carloman_(mayor_of_the_palace)" title="Carloman (mayor of the palace)">Carloman</a>, the sons of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Martel" title="Charles Martel">Charles Martel</a>, and to recognize Frankish suzerainty. A little earlier, in 739, the church of Bavaria had been organized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Boniface" title="St. Boniface">St. Boniface</a>, and the country divided into several bishoprics; and we find frequent references to these bishops (in the plural) in the law of the Bavarians. On the other hand, we know that the law is anterior to the reign of Duke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tassilo_III" title="Tassilo III">Tassilo III</a> (749-788). The date of compilation must, therefore, be placed between 744 and 748. Against this argument, however, it is very likely that Odilo recognized Frankish authority before 743; he took refuge at Charles Martel's court that year and married one of Martel's daughters. His "revolt" may have been in support of the claims of Pippin and Carloman's half-brother Grifo, not opposition to Frankish rule per se. Also, it is not clear that the Lex Baiuvariorum refers to multiple bishops in the duchy at the same time; when a bishop is accused of a crime, for instance, he is to be tried by the duke, and not by a council of fellow bishops as canon law required. So, it is possible that the Bavarian law was compiled earlier, perhaps between 735 (the year of Odilo's succession) and 739.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Frisionum">Lex Frisionum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lex Frisionum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lex_Frisionum" title="Lex Frisionum">Lex Frisionum</a></div>
<p>The <i>Lex Frisionum</i> of the duchy of <a href="/wiki/Frisia" title="Frisia">Frisia</a> consists of a medley of documents of the most heterogeneous character. Some of its enactments are purely <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a>, thus one paragraph allows the mother to kill her new-born child, and another prescribes the immolation to the gods of the defiler of their temple; others are purely Christian, such as those that prohibit <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incestuous</a> marriages and working on Sunday. The law abounds in contradictions and repetitions, and the compositions are calculated in different moneys. From this it appears the documents were merely materials collected from various sources and possibly with a view to the compilation of a homogeneous law. These materials were apparently brought together at the beginning of the 9th century, at a time of intense legislative activity at the court of Charlemagne.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lex_Saxonum">Lex Saxonum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Germanic_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lex Saxonum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lex_Saxonum" title="Lex Saxonum">Lex Saxonum</a></div>
<p>The <i>Lex Saxonum</i> has come down to us in two manuscripts and two old editions (those of B. J. Herold and du Tillet), and the text has been edited by Karl von Richthofen in the <i>Mon. Germ. hist, Leges</i>, v. The law contains ancient customary enactments of <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, and, in the form in which it reached us, is later than the conquest of Saxony by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>. It is preceded by two capitularies of Charlemagne for Saxony, the <i>Capitulatio de partibus Saxoniae</i> (A. Boretius i. 68), which dates undoubtedly from 782, and is characterized by great severity, death being the penalty for every offence against the Christian religion; and the <i>Capitulare Saxonicum</i> (A. Boretius i. 71), of the 28 October 797, in which Charlemagne shows less brutality and pronounces simple compositions for misdeeds that formerly warranted death. The <i>Lex Saxonum</i> apparently dates from 803, since it contains provisions that are in the <i>Capitulare legi Ribuariae additum</i> of that year. The law established the ancient customs, at the same time eliminating anything that was contrary to the spirit of Christianity; it proclaimed the peace of the churches, whose possessions it guaranteed and whose right of asylum it recognized.</p>
