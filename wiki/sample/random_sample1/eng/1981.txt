<p><b>Gerhard Ertl</b> (born 10 October 1936) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and a Professor <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">emeritus</a> at the Department of Physical Chemistry, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber_Institute_of_the_MPG" title="Fritz Haber Institute of the MPG">Fritz-Haber-Institut der Max-Planck-Gesellschaft</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Ertl’s research laid the foundation of modern surface chemistry, which has helped explain how fuel cells produce energy without pollution, how catalytic converters clean up car exhausts and even why iron rusts, the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences said.</p>
<p>His work has paved the way for development of cleaner energy sources and will guide the development of fuel cells, said Astrid Graslund, secretary of the Nobel Committee for Chemistry.</p>
<p>He was awarded the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> for his studies of chemical processes on solid surfaces. The Nobel academy said Ertl provided a detailed description of how chemical reactions take place on surfaces. His findings applied in both academic studies and industrial development, the academy said. “Surface chemistry can even explain the destruction of the ozone layer, as vital steps in the reaction actually take place on the surfaces of small crystals of ice in the stratosphere,” the award citation reads.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Ertl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ertl was born in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, Germany, where he studied physics from 1955 to 1957 at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stuttgart" title="University of Stuttgart">Technical University of Stuttgart</a> and then at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> (1957–1958) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilians_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilians University of Munich">Ludwig Maximilians University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> (1958–1959). He completed his <a href="/wiki/Diploma" title="Diploma">Diploma</a> in Physics at the Technical University of Stuttgart in 1961, followed his thesis advisor <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Gerischer" title="Heinz Gerischer">Heinz Gerischer</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Metals_Research" title="Max Planck Institute for Metals Research">Max Planck Institute for Metals Research</a> in Stuttgart to Munich and received his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> degree from the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Munich" title="Technical University of Munich">Technical University of Munich</a> in 1965.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Ertl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After completing his PhD, he became an assistant and lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Munich" title="Technical University of Munich">Technical University of Munich</a> (1965–1968). From 1968 to 1973, he was Professor and Director at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leibniz_University_Hannover" title="Leibniz University Hannover">Technical University of Hannover</a>. Then, he became a Professor at Institute for Physical Chemistry, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilians_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilians University of Munich">Ludwig Maximilians University of Munich</a> (1973–1986). During the 1970s and 80s, he was also a Visiting Professor at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Milwaukee" title="University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee">University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>. In 1986 he became professor at the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Berlin" title="Technical University of Berlin">Technical University of Berlin</a>. He was director at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber_Institute_of_the_MPG" title="Fritz Haber Institute of the MPG">Fritz Haber Institute of the MPG</a> from 1986 till his retirement in 2004. He became professor at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Humboldt University of Berlin</a> in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ertl now serves in his second term as a member of the university council of <a href="/wiki/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt" title="Technische Universität Darmstadt">Technische Universität Darmstadt</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Ertl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gerhard Ertl is known for determining the detailed molecular mechanisms of the catalytic synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> over <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber_Bosch_process" title="Haber Bosch process">Haber Bosch process</a>) and the catalytic oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> over <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a>). During his research he discovered the important phenomenon of oscillatory reactions on platinum surfaces and, using photoelectron microscopy, was able to image for the first time, the oscillating changes in surface structure and coverage that occur during reaction.</p>
<p>He always used new observation techniques like <a href="/wiki/Low-energy_electron_diffraction" title="Low-energy electron diffraction">low-energy electron diffraction</a> (LEED) at the beginning of his career, later <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_photoelectron_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy">ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy</a> (UPS) and <a href="/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope" title="Scanning tunneling microscope">scanning tunneling microscope</a> (STM) yielding ground breaking results. He won the <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Wolf Prize in Chemistry">Wolf Prize in Chemistry</a> in 1998 along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabor_A._Somorjai" title="Gabor A. Somorjai">Gabor A. Somorjai</a> of the University of California, Berkeley for "their outstanding contributions to the field of the <a href="/wiki/Surface_science" title="Surface science">surface science</a> in general and for their elucidation of fundamental mechanisms of heterogeneous catalytic reactions at single crystal surface in particular."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gerhard Ertl was awarded the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> for his studies of chemical processes on solid surfaces. The award, worth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Krona" title="Swedish Krona">SEK</a> 10 million (<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>1.7 million, <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">GB£</a>1.15 million), was announced on Ertl's 71st birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> "I am speechless", Ertl told Associated Press from his office in Berlin. "I was not counting on this."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Ertl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ertl and his wife Barbara have two children and several grandchildren. His hobbies include playing the piano and also playing with his cats when he is not doing experiments. He is a Christian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cicero_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cicero-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Ertl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ertl is one of the editors of the <i>Handbook of Heterogeneous Catalysis</i>.</p>
<p>Ertl is the co-editor of <i>Engineering Of Chemical Complexity</i>. 2013, World Scientific Publishing. (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9789814390453">ISBN 9789814390453</a>)</p>
