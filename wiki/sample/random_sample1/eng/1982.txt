<p><b>Gerhard von Rad</b> (21 October 1901 – 31 October 1971) was a German theologian, academic, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a> professor.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life.2C_education.2C_career">Early life, education, career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_von_Rad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education, career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2005_uni-03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/2005_uni-03.jpg/200px-2005_uni-03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/2005_uni-03.jpg/300px-2005_uni-03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/2005_uni-03.jpg/400px-2005_uni-03.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2005_uni-03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a>, where Rad taught.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rad was born in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, to Lutheran parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He then went on to be educated at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen" title="University of Erlangen">University of Erlangen</a> and further at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tübingen">University of Tübingen</a>.</p>
<p>In 1925, he became a <a href="/wiki/Curate" title="Curate">curate</a> in the Lutheran <i>Landeskirche</i> (i.e. the church in the federal state) of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ibid_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ibid-2">[2]</a></sup> Later, he taught at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen" title="University of Erlangen">University of Erlangen</a> in 1929 as <a href="/wiki/Tutor" title="Tutor">tutor</a>. In 1930 he was a <a href="/wiki/Privatdozent" title="Privatdozent">privatdozent</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" title="University of Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ibid_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ibid-2">[2]</a></sup> From 1934 to 1945 he served as a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a> and later at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a> from 1945 to 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After that, he became Professor of Old Testament at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruprecht_Karl_University_of_Heidelberg" title="Ruprecht Karl University of Heidelberg">Ruprecht Karl University of Heidelberg</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> and taught there until his death in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ibid_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ibid-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was conferred honorary doctorates from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Lund" title="University of Lund">University of Lund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oral_tradition_and_the_Pentateuch">Oral tradition and the Pentateuch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_von_Rad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Oral tradition and the Pentateuch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Noth" title="Martin Noth">Martin Noth</a>, Rad applied <a href="/wiki/Form_criticism" title="Form criticism">form criticism</a>, originated by <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Gunkel" title="Hermann Gunkel">Hermann Gunkel</a>, to the <a href="/wiki/Documentary_hypothesis" title="Documentary hypothesis">documentary hypothesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the experience of two World Wars, the German-speaking world began to turn "anti-Old Testament", as anti-Semitic feeling grew. Disturbed by this, Rad turned to the study of the Old Testament and gradually started to bring back its message.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>His lively papers achieved a renewal of interest and research in Old Testament studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Along with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Noth" title="Martin Noth">Martin Noth</a>, he applied research into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentateuch" title="Pentateuch">Pentateuch</a>'s oral tradition to the explanation of its origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1960, Rad traveled to the United States where he was a visiting scholar at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Theological_Seminary" title="Princeton Theological Seminary">Princeton Theological Seminary</a>. Rad was greatly influential during this period. While at Princeton, he took on <a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Jensen" title="Richard A. Jensen">Richard A. Jensen</a> as an understudy, who would also further his research and application.</p>

<p>Gerhard von Rad and his wife are buried in Heidelberg's <cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://www.heidelberg.de/servlet/PB/menu/1200304_%7C1/index.html" rel="nofollow">"Handschusheim Cemetery"</a>.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AGerhard+von+Rad&amp;rft.btitle=Handschusheim+Cemetery&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.heidelberg.de%2Fservlet%2FPB%2Fmenu%2F1200304_%257C1%2Findex.html&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span> On their gravestone is minimalist artwork depicting <a href="/wiki/Jonah" title="Jonah">Jonah</a> emerging from the great fish, an Old Testament symbol of <a href="/wiki/Resurrection" title="Resurrection">resurrection</a>.</p>
