<p><i><b>Gemeinschaft</b></i> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ɡəˈmaɪnʃaft]</a></span>) and <i><b>Gesellschaft</b></i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ɡəˈzɛlʃaft]</a></span>), generally translated as "<a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>", are categories which were used by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_T%C3%B6nnies" title="Ferdinand Tönnies">Ferdinand Tönnies</a> in order to categorize social ties into two dichotomous sociological types which define each other. <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a>, who is generally recognized as being a founding figure in sociology, also wrote extensively about the relationship between <i>Gemeinschaft</i> and <i>Gesellschaft</i>. Weber wrote in direct response to Tönnies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gemeinschaft.E2.80.93Gesellschaft_dichotomy"><i>Gemeinschaft</i>–<i>Gesellschaft</i> dichotomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemeinschaft_and_Gesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gemeinschaft–Gesellschaft dichotomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Gemeinschaft–Gesellschaft</i> <a href="/wiki/Dichotomy" title="Dichotomy">dichotomy</a> was proposed by Tönnies as a purely conceptual tool rather than as an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_type" title="Ideal type">ideal type</a> in the way it was used by <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> to accentuate the key elements of a historic/social change. According to the dichotomy, social ties can be categorized, on one hand, as belonging to personal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interaction" title="Social interaction">social interactions</a>, and the roles, values, and beliefs based on such interactions (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemeinschaft" title="Gemeinschaft">Gemeinschaft</a></i>, German, commonly translated as "<a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>"), or on the other hand as belonging to indirect interactions, impersonal roles, formal values, and beliefs based on such interactions (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft" title="Gesellschaft">Gesellschaft</a></i>, German, commonly translated as "<a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tönnies was a <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> scholar—he edited the standard modern editions of Hobbes's <i>The Elements of Law</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <i>Behemoth</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was his study of Hobbes that encouraged Tönnies to devote himself wholly to the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_law" title="Philosophy of law">philosophy of law</a>. And it has been argued that he derived both categories from Hobbes's concepts of "concord" and "union".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second edition, published in 1912, of the work in which Tönnies further promoted the concepts turned out to be an unexpected but lasting success<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> after the first edition was published in 1887 with the subtitle "Treatise on Communism and Socialism as Empirical Patterns of Culture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Seven more German editions followed, the last in 1935,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and it became part of the general stock of ideas with which pre-1933 German intellectuals were quite familiar. The book sparked a revival of <a href="/wiki/Corporatism" title="Corporatism">corporatist</a> thinking, including the rise of <a href="/wiki/Neo-medievalism" title="Neo-medievalism">neo-medievalism</a>, the rise of support for <a href="/wiki/Guild_socialism" title="Guild socialism">guild socialism</a>, and caused major changes in the field of sociology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_F._Klar.C3.A9n_1986._Pp._221_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_F._Klar.C3.A9n_1986._Pp._221-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concepts <i>Gemeinschaft</i> and <i>Gesellschaft</i> were also used by Max Weber in <i>Economy and Society</i>, which was first published in 1921. Weber wrote in direct response to Tönnies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and argued that <i>Gemeinschaft</i> is rooted in a "subjective feeling" that may be "affectual or traditional". <i>Gesellschaft</i>-based relationships, according to Weber, are rooted in "rational agreement by mutual consent", the best example of which is a commercial contract. To emphasize the fluidity and amorphousness of the relationship between <i>Gemeinschaft</i> and <i>Gesellschaft</i>, Weber modified the terms in German to <i>Vergemeinschaftung</i>, and <i>Vergesellschaftung</i>, which are the <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">gerund</a> forms of the German words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Weber's distinction between <i>Gemeinschaft</i> and <i>Gesellschaft</i> is highlighted in the essay "Classes, Stände, Parties",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which is the basis for Weber's <a href="/wiki/Three-component_theory_of_stratification" title="Three-component theory of stratification">three-component theory of stratification</a>.</p>
<p>Having propounded his conception of the <i>Gemeinschaft</i>–<i>Gesellschaft</i> dichotomy, Tönnies was drawn into a sharp polemic with <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="Émile Durkheim">Émile Durkheim</a>. In a lengthy, and on the whole positive review of Tönnies' book in 1889, Durkheim interpreted <i>Gemeinschaft</i> as an organic community, and <i>Gesellschaft</i> as a mechanical one, reproaching Tönnies for considering the second type of social organisation artificial, and not seeing the transition from the one type to the other. Tönnies did not agree with such an interpretation of his views, and in turn, when reviewing Durkheim's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Division_of_Labour_in_Society" title="The Division of Labour in Society">The Division of Labour in Society</a></i> (1896), wrote that Durkeim's whole sociology was a modification and variant of Spencer's, which was also unjust.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Globalization">Globalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemeinschaft_and_Gesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Globalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">Eric Hobsbawm</a> argued that, as <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> turns the entire planet into an increasingly remote kind of <i>Gesellschaft</i>, so too collective <a href="/wiki/Identity_politics" title="Identity politics">identity politics</a> seeks for a fictitious remaking of the qualities of <i>Gemeinschaft</i> by artificially reforging group bonds and identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fredric_Jameson" title="Fredric Jameson">Fredric Jameson</a> highlights the ambivalent envy felt by those constructed by <i>Gesellschaft</i> for remaining enclaves of <i>Gemeinschaft</i>, even as they inevitably corrode their existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_sociology">Outside sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemeinschaft_and_Gesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Outside sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> usage, <i>Gesellschaft</i> is the German term for "<a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a>", as in <i><a href="/wiki/Aktiengesellschaft" title="Aktiengesellschaft">Aktiengesellschaft</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_mit_beschr%C3%A4nkter_Haftung" title="Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung">Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung</a></i> (<i>GmbH</i>). "Gemeinschaft" is used to identify groups which have or are claimed to have an element of affective loyalty: one important usage is in the German name for the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, <i>Europäische Wirtschaftsgemeinschaft</i>.</p>
