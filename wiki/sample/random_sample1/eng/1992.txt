<p><b>Georg Theodor August Gaffky</b> (17 February 1850 – 23 September 1918) was a <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacteriologist" title="Bacteriologist">bacteriologist</a> best known for identifying <a href="/wiki/Bacillus" title="Bacillus">bacillus</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salmonella_typhi" title="Salmonella typhi">salmonella typhi</a> as the cause of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> in 1884.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Theodor_August_Gaffky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gaffky's parents were the shipping agent Georg Friedrich Wilhelm Gaffky, and Emma Schumacher. His medical studies at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Friedrich Wilhelm University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> were completed in 1873 after an interruption by the Franco-Prussian War in 1870. His dissertation postulated a relationship between <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kidney_disease" title="Kidney disease">kidney disease</a>. He worked as an assistant at the Berlin Charité hospital and passed the state medical exams in 1875. He then worked as an army surgeon.</p>
<p>Gaffky worked as an assistant to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Koch" title="Robert Koch">Robert Koch</a> in Berlin. Under Koch's leadership, Gaffky and others developed <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriological</a> protocols and achieved progress in identifying causes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_discoveries">Principal discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Theodor_August_Gaffky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principal discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Karl_Joseph_Eberth" title="Karl Joseph Eberth">Karl Joseph Eberth</a>'s description of a bacillus suspected as the cause of typhus, and Koch's independent work on the same organism, Gaffky dedicated years to improving culture methods to isolate the bacillus. He was able to produce cultures in gelatin and other hosts.</p>
<p>In 1884, Gaffky published results reporting that he had isolated the eberthella or Gaffky-Eberth bacillus in 26 of 28 cases of typhoid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gaffky was later part of an expedition to Egypt in which Koch identified transmission methods of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>. He became a government advisor during the 1892 cholera outbreak in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. He led an investigation of the 1897 outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a> in India.</p>
<p>Gaffky served as director of the Berlin Institute for Infectious Diseases from 1904–1913.</p>
