<p><b>Georg Karl Friedrich Wilhelm von Küchler</b> (30 May 1881 – 25 May 1968) was a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Generalfeldmarschall" title="Generalfeldmarschall">Field Marshal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_criminal" title="War criminal">war criminal</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. He was a recipient of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight%27s_Cross_of_the_Iron_Cross_with_Oak_Leaves" title="Knight's Cross of the Iron Cross with Oak Leaves">Knight's Cross of the Iron Cross with Oak Leaves</a>.</p>
<p>After the end of the war he was tried in the <a href="/wiki/High_Command_Trial" title="High Command Trial">High Command Trial</a>, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsequent_Nuremberg_Trials" title="Subsequent Nuremberg Trials">Subsequent Nuremberg Trials</a>. On 27 October 1948 was sentenced to twenty years' imprisonment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> committed in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. He was released in 1953.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I_and_interwar_years">World War I and interwar years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_K%C3%BCchler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: World War I and interwar years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in 1881, Küchler entered the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(German_Empire)" title="German Army (German Empire)">Imperial Army</a> in 1900. He studied at the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Military_Academy" title="Prussian Military Academy">Prussian Military Academy</a>, before joining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Staff" title="General Staff">General Staff</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> in 1913. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> he commanded an artillery battery on the Western Front and then became a staff officer of an infantry division. In 1919 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Freikorps" title="Freikorps">Freikorps</a> and fought the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. Promoted to colonel, Küchler became deputy commander of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(Wehrmacht)" title="1st Infantry Division (Wehrmacht)">1st Infantry Division</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a> in 1932. In 1938 he supported <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> in his removal of <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Blomberg" title="Werner von Blomberg">Werner von Blomberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Fritsch" title="Werner von Fritsch">Werner von Fritsch</a> from power.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_K%C3%BCchler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland_and_Western_campaign">Poland and Western campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_K%C3%BCchler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poland and Western campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Küchler assumed command of the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="3rd Army (Wehrmacht)">3rd Army</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland" title="Invasion of Poland">invasion of Poland</a>, Küchler’s troops captured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danzig" title="Danzig">Danzig</a>. In 1940 he was supportive of Nazi racial policy and ordered on 22 February a halt to any criticism of "ethnic struggle being carried out in the General Government, for instance that of the Polish minorities, of the Jews and those regarding Church matters". His order explained that the "final ethnic solution" required unique and harsh measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Western campaign he fought under Field Marshal <a href="/wiki/Fedor_von_Bock" title="Fedor von Bock">Fedor von Bock</a>. His <a href="/wiki/18th_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="18th Army (Wehrmacht)">18th Army</a> invaded the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, fighting at <a href="/wiki/Moerdijk" title="Moerdijk">Moerdijk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague" title="Hague">Hague</a>. Küchler’s forces took Antwerp on 18 May 1940 and then moved into France, attempting to cut off the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_II)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War II)">British Expeditionary Force</a> (BEF) from the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dunkirk" title="Dunkirk">Dunkirk</a>, which ended in failure. The <a href="/wiki/18th_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="18th Army (Wehrmacht)">18th Army</a> ended this phase of the war at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pas_de_Calais" title="Pas de Calais">Pas de Calais</a>.</p>
<p>Küchler was an active supporter of the planned war of annihilation (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vernichtungskrieg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vernichtungskrieg (page does not exist)">Vernichtungskrieg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernichtungskrieg" title="de:Vernichtungskrieg">de</a>)</span></i>) against the Soviet Union. After meeting Hitler in March 1941 to plan for <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Operation Barbarossa</a>, Küchler told his divisional commanders on 25 April 1941:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We are separated from Russia, ideologically and racially, by a deep abyss. Russia is, if only by the mass of her territory, an Asian state...The <i>Führer</i> does not wish to palm off responsibility for Germany's existence on to a later generation; he has decided to force the dispute with Russia before the year is out. If Germany wishes to live in peace for generations, safe from a threatening danger in the East, this cannot be a case of pushing Russia back a little-or even hundreds of kilometers-but the aim must be to annihilate European Russia, to dissolve the Russian state in Europe"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Küchler went on to call Red Army commissars "criminals" who should all be shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Front">Eastern Front</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_K%C3%BCchler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern Front">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During Barbarossa, the 18th Army forced its way to <a href="/wiki/Ostrov,_Ostrovsky_District,_Pskov_Oblast" title="Ostrov, Ostrovsky District, Pskov Oblast">Ostrov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pskov" title="Pskov">Pskov</a> after the Soviet troops of the <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Front" title="Northwestern Front">Northwestern Front</a> retreated towards Leningrad. On 10 July 1941, both Ostrov and Pskov were captured and the 18th Army reached <a href="/wiki/Narva" title="Narva">Narva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingisepp" title="Kingisepp">Kingisepp</a>, from where advance toward Leningrad continued from the <a href="/wiki/Luga_River" title="Luga River">Luga River</a> line. This had the effect of creating siege positions from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>, with the eventual aim of isolating Leningrad from all directions. The Finnish Army was then expected to advance along the eastern shore of Lake Ladoga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Küchler was directly involved in the murder of mental patients. In December 1941, with his express consent, units of the <a href="/wiki/Sicherheitsdienst" title="Sicherheitsdienst">SD</a> shot 240 mental patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHebert201095_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHebert201095-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 January 1942, Küchler succeeded Field Marshal <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ritter_von_Leeb" title="Wilhelm Ritter von Leeb">Wilhelm Ritter von Leeb</a> as commander of <a href="/wiki/Army_Group_North" title="Army Group North">Army Group North</a> after the latter was relieved of command. Küchler was seen as politically compliant and was liked by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, who hoped that Küchler would succeed where he believed Leeb had failed. Küchler commanded Army Group North from December 1941 through January 1944, maintaining the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leningrad" title="Siege of Leningrad">siege of Leningrad</a>. On 30 June 1942 Hitler promoted Küchler to field marshal (<a href="/wiki/Generalfeldmarschall" title="Generalfeldmarschall">Generalfeldmarschall</a>). In January 1944 Soviet troops broke the siege, and Küchler was sacked when he demanded the withdrawal to the <a href="/wiki/Luga_River" title="Luga River">Luga River</a>. While in retirement Küchler was approached by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Goerdeler" title="Carl Goerdeler">Carl Goerdeler</a> who tried to persuade him to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/July_Plot" title="July Plot">July Plot</a>. He refused to participate in the attempt to assassinate Hitler.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_and_conviction">Trial and conviction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_K%C3%BCchler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trial and conviction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of the Second World War, Küchler was arrested by <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> occupation authorities. He was tried in the <a href="/wiki/High_Command_Trial" title="High Command Trial">High Command Trial</a>, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsequent_Nuremberg_Trials" title="Subsequent Nuremberg Trials">Subsequent Nuremberg Trials</a>. In his testimony regarding the <a href="/wiki/German_mistreatment_of_Soviet_prisoners_of_war" title="German mistreatment of Soviet prisoners of war">crimes against the Soviet prisoners of war</a>, Küchler admitted that the conditions in the POW camps were harsh, but insisted that the main cause of that was the winter conditions of 1941–42, which he called an "act of God" and insisted that the army exaggerated POW mortality in their reports in an effort to receive more supplies for the prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHebert2010123_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHebert2010123-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 October 1948 Küchler was sentenced to twenty years' imprisonment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> committed in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The sentence was reviewed in 1951 and reduced to 12 years. He was released 1953 on medical grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHebert2010216.E2.80.93217_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHebert2010216.E2.80.93217-7">[7]</a></sup> Küchler died in 1968.</p>
