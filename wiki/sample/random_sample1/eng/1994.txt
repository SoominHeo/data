<p><b>Georg von Frundsberg</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> (24 September 1473 – 20 August 1528) was a <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">South German</a> military and <a href="/wiki/Landsknecht" title="Landsknecht">Landsknecht</a> leader in the service of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire_of_the_German_Nation" title="Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation">Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Imperial</a> <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern</a> proponent of <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> tactics, he established his reputation in active service during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> under Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> and his successor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_Frundsberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frundsberg was born to Ulrich von Frundsberg, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hauptmann_(officer)" title="Hauptmann (officer)">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_League" title="Swabian League">Swabian League</a> forces, and his wife Barbara von Rechberg at <a href="/wiki/Mindelheim" title="Mindelheim">Mindelheim</a>, into an old line of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrolean</a> knights who had settled in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Swabia" title="Upper Swabia">Upper Swabia</a>.</p>
<p>In 1492 he followed his father in the campaign of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> margrave <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach" title="Frederick I, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach">Frederick I of Brandenburg-Ansbach</a>, authorized to execute the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_ban" title="Imperial ban">Imperial ban</a> against Duke <a href="/wiki/Albert_IV,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Albert IV, Duke of Bavaria">Albert IV of Bavaria</a>. As Albert gave in, the expedition was cancelled. Frundsberg fought for the Habsburg emperor Maximilian I against the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Swiss Confederacy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_War" title="Swabian War">Swabian War</a> of 1499, where he had to realize that the era of the heavy armoured <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a> was well and truly over. In the same year he was among the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Imperial</a> troops sent to the aid of <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a>, who had been deposed as <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Milan" title="List of rulers of Milan">Duke of Milan</a> by King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII of France</a>. When Maximilian appointed him Tyrolean military captain, he recruited a powerful army of <a href="/wiki/Pike_square" title="Pike square">pike square</a> infantry formations following the Swiss example.</p>
<p>Still serving Maximilian, he took part in the 1504 <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Succession_of_Landshut" title="War of the Succession of Landshut">War of the Succession of Landshut</a>, fighting against Count <a href="/wiki/Ruprecht_of_the_Palatinate_(Bishop_of_Freising)" title="Ruprecht of the Palatinate (Bishop of Freising)">Ruprecht of the Palatinate</a> and his father Elector Palatine <a href="/wiki/Philip,_Elector_Palatine" title="Philip, Elector Palatine">Philip</a>. Frundsberg distinguished himself leading a Landsknecht regiment into the decisive Battle of <a href="/wiki/Wenzenbach" title="Wenzenbach">Wenzenbach</a>, whereafter Maximilian I personally bestowed knighthood on him: armed with muskets and culverines, the Frundsberg regiment broke a breach into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laager" title="Laager">wagon-wall</a> of the bohemian mercenaries (composed 300+ wagons), which were then routed. Convinced of the necessity of a native body of trained infantry, Frundsberg assisted Maximilian in the organization of the Landsknecht troops. One year later, he became the commander of the Landsknechts in the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Netherlands" title="Habsburg Netherlands">Habsburg Netherlands</a>.</p>
<p>Thereafter, Frundsberg lived an uninterrupted life of war, campaigning for the Empire and the Habsburgs. In 1509, he was appointed "Highest Field Captain" of the Landsknecht Regiment (occupation force) and participated in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">War of the League of Cambrai</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>, winning fame for himself and his men after defending the city of <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a> against numerous attacks. In 1512 he was, together with Jakob von Ems, leading the Imperial contingent sent to aid <a href="/wiki/Gaston_of_Foix,_Duke_of_Nemours" title="Gaston of Foix, Duke of Nemours">Gaston de Foix</a> to retake <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>.</p>
<p>After a short visit to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a>, he returned to the Italian peninsula, where he gained fresh laurels by his enterprises against the Venetians and the French. He was heading the Landsknechts at the side of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Spanish</a> commander <a href="/wiki/Fernando_d%27Avalos" title="Fernando d'Avalos">Fernando d'Avalos</a> at the 1513 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_La_Motta_(1513)" title="Battle of La Motta (1513)">Battle of La Motta</a>, routing the vastly outnumbering Venetian troops according to Frundsberg's motto <i>Viel Feind', viel Ehr'</i> ("Many foes, much honor"). Peace being made, he returned to Germany, and at the head of the infantry of the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_League" title="Swabian League">Swabian League</a> assisted in driving <a href="/wiki/Ulrich,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Ulrich, Duke of Württemberg">Ulrich, Duke of Württemberg</a>, from his duchy in 1519.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Worms" title="Diet of Worms">Diet of Worms</a> in 1521, he allegedly spoke words of encouragement to the "little monk" <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>, and during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_War_of_1521%E2%80%9326" title="Italian War of 1521–26">Italian War of 1521–26</a>, Frundsberg helped lead the Imperial Army into <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">Picardy</a>. When King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a> appeared on the battlefield with a force of approximately 40,000 men, the clever withdrawal of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V's</a> army saved its existence. Frundsberg considered the withdrawal on <a href="/wiki/Valenciennes" title="Valenciennes">Valenciennes</a> as "the greatest luck and most appropriate measure during war."</p>
<p>After the French campaign in 1522 ended and Frundsberg resigned from the leadership of the Landesknechts, he returned to lead the march of 6,000 men on upper Italy. A difficult alpine crossing through deep snow led to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bicocca" title="Battle of Bicocca">Battle of Bicocca</a> near <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> in April. Swiss nationals on foot fought alongside Frundsberg, who led and fought from the front. The emperor's victory at Bicocca allowed the return of the old Kingdom's Parliamentary Cabinet Lands of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> and Milan and brought the greater part of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a> under the influence of Charles V.</p>
<p>In 1525, after a brief stop in <a href="/wiki/Mindelheim" title="Mindelheim">Mindelheim</a> as the "Highest Field Captain" of the entire German Nation (with a force consisting of 12,000 men and twenty-nine flag bearers), Frundsberg moved again towards upper Italy to relieve Pavia and to save the Empire's <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a>. Despite an additional 6,000 men, of whom some were Spanish, in battle against an enemy that was twice as strong, Frundsberg won his most famous <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">victory at Pavia</a>, with the capture of the French king.</p>
<p>Only one year later, when the war in Italy was renewed in 1526, Frundsberg received a call for help from the Emperor's Army in Lombardy, to help decide the war. Albeit an insufficient amount, he obtained 36,000 German <a href="/wiki/Thaler" title="Thaler">Thaler</a> to organize the new army. During his occupation of Mindelheim, Frundsberg borrowed money and sold off his silver table settings and his wife's jewelry, in order to acquire the remaining funds to raise the army. In less than three weeks, Frundsberg organized over 12,000 men and crossed the Alps during the middle of November. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Charles_III,_Duke_of_Bourbon" title="Charles III, Duke of Bourbon">Constable de Bourbon</a> near <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">Piacenza</a> and marched towards <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. However, order and discipline broke down near <a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">Modena</a> on 13 March 1527, when no decisive battle developed after months of campaigning in Italy. Payment for the mercenaries remained overdue and, in the end, even Frundsberg was unable to rally the Landsknechts and restore order. The matter shook the old commander to such an extent that he suffered a stroke. Unable to regain his physical strength, Frundsberg was moved to Germany after a long struggle in Italian hospitals. Tormented by great anxiety over the situation with his mercenaries or "beloved sons", the loss of his personal estate and death of one of his sons, Frundsberg died in his castle in Mindelheim.</p>
<p>Though there is no evidence that he ever left the Catholic Church, and in spite of his constant loyalty to the Catholic Emperor, Frundsberg was quite receptive to the message of Martin Luther and according to some stories, before the campaign of 1527, he motivated his troops (mainly Lutherans) by promising to hang the pope with a golden rope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_von_Frundsberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frundsberg was considered a capable and chivalrous soldier, and a devoted servant of the Habsburgs. His son Caspar (1500–1536) and his grandson Georg (died 1586) were both soldiers of some distinction. With the latter's death, the family became extinct.</p>
<p>In 1841 a bust by <a href="/wiki/Max_von_Widnmann" title="Max von Widnmann">Max von Widnmann</a> was added to the <a href="/wiki/Walhalla_memorial" title="Walhalla memorial">Walhalla memorial</a>. Emperor <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph I of Austria</a> had a statue erected in his honour at the Hall of Generals (<i>Feldherrenhalle</i>) of the Vienna <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Military_History,_Vienna" title="Museum of Military History, Vienna">Museum of Military History</a>, inaugurated in 1866, and the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Navy" title="Austro-Hungarian Navy">Austro-Hungarian</a> corvette <a href="/wiki/SMS_Frundsberg" title="SMS Frundsberg">SMS Frundsberg</a> launched in 1873 was named after him. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <i>Waffen-SS</i> <a href="/wiki/10th_SS_Panzer_Division_Frundsberg" title="10th SS Panzer Division Frundsberg">10th Panzer Division</a> by order of 3 October 1943 was given the honorific title <i>Frundsberg</i>.</p>
