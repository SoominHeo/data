<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nuckelavee</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>nuckelavee</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>) or <b>nuckalavee</b> is a horse-like demon from <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orcadian</a> mythology that combines <a href="/wiki/Equus_(genus)" title="Equus (genus)">equine</a> and human elements. It has its origins in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, and is the most horrible of all the demons of the Scottish islands. The <i>nuck</i> component of its name may be cognate with <i>Nick</i> in Old Nick, a name for the Christian <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">Devil</a>. The nuckelavee's breath was thought to wilt crops and sicken livestock, and the creature was held responsible for droughts and epidemics on land despite its being predominantly a sea-dweller.</p>
<p>A graphic description of the nuckelavee as it appears on land was given by an islander who claimed to have had a confrontation with it, but accounts describing the details of the creature's appearance are inconsistent. In common with many other sea monsters it is unable to tolerate fresh water, therefore those it is pursuing have only to cross a river or stream to be rid of it. The nuckelavee is kept in confinement during the summer months by the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Mither" title="Sea Mither">Mither o' the Sea</a>, an ancient Orcadian spirit, and the only one able to control it.</p>
<p>Orcadian folklore had a strong Scandinavian influence, and it may be that the nuckelavee is a composite of a <a href="/wiki/Water_horse" title="Water horse">water horse</a> from <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic mythology</a> and a creature imported by the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a>. As with similar malevolent entities such as the <a href="/wiki/Kelpie" title="Kelpie">kelpie</a>, it possibly offered an explanation for incidents that islanders in ancient times could not otherwise understand.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuckelavee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The late 19th century saw an upsurge of interest in transcribing folklore, but the recorders used inconsistent spelling and frequently <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicised</a> words, thus the same entity could be given different names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonaghan2009ix.2C_xi.2C_xv_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonaghan2009ix.2C_xi.2C_xv-1">[1]</a></sup> The term <i>nuckelavee</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Orcadian_dialect" title="Orcadian dialect">Orcadian</a> <i>knoggelvi</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orkneyjar_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyjar-2">[2]</a></sup> and according to Orkney resident and 19th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folklorist" title="Folklorist">folklorist</a> <a href="/wiki/Walter_Traill_Dennison" title="Walter Traill Dennison">Walter Traill Dennison</a> means "Devil of the Sea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131-3">[3]</a></sup> The same demon is called a <i>mukkelevi</i> in <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, where it was considered a nasty sea <a href="/wiki/Trow_(folklore)" title="Trow (folklore)">trow</a> or sea devil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hibbert-Ware" title="Samuel Hibbert-Ware">Samuel Hibbert</a>, an antiquarian of the early nineteenth century, considered the component <i>nuck</i> of the nuckelavee's name to be cognate with both the <i>Nick</i> in Old Nick, a name sometimes given to the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">Devil</a> of Christian belief, and with the Latin <i>necare</i>, to kill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHibbert1891233_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHibbert1891233-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_beliefs">Folk beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuckelavee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Folk beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_common_attributes">Description and common attributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuckelavee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description and common attributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stories of mythical Orcadian demons are recorded in the 16th-century Latin manuscripts of Jo Ben,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup> who may have been referring to the nuckelavee in his description of the Orkney island of <a href="/wiki/Stronsay" title="Stronsay">Stronsay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OJ_Stronsay_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-OJ_Stronsay-10">[9]</a></sup> Dennison transcribed much of the information available about traditional tales told on Orkney, but to an extent romanticised and systematically altered certain elements of the stories in the process of transforming them into prose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-University-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[b]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nuckelavee is a mythical sea creature that appears as a horse-like demon when it ventures onto land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012387_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012387-14">[12]</a></sup> Writer and folklorist <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Marwick" title="Ernest Marwick">Ernest Marwick</a> considered it very similar to the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Neck_(water_spirit)" title="Neck (water spirit)">nøkk</a>, the nuggle of the Shetlands and the <a href="/wiki/Kelpie" title="Kelpie">kelpie</a>. A unique and solitary creature possessing extensive evil powers, its malevolent behaviour can influence events throughout the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup> Islanders were terrified of the creature and would not speak its name without immediately saying a prayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> It was often found in the vicinity of a beach, but would never come ashore if it was raining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>No tales describe what form the nuckelavee takes when in the sea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131-3">[3]</a></sup> but its appearance on land has been recounted in graphic detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBriggs200267_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBriggs200267-17">[15]</a></sup> An islander, Tammas, claimed to have survived a confrontation with the beast and, after much cajoling from Dennison, reluctantly gave his description of the monster, the only known first-hand account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[c]</a></sup> According to Tammas, the nuckelavee has a man's torso attached to a horse's back as if it were a rider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891131-3">[3]</a></sup> The male torso has no legs, but its arms can reach the ground from its position on top of the equine body, the legs of which have <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fin</a> like appendages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> The torso has a large head – possibly as much as 3 feet (90 centimetres) in diameter – that rolls back and forth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022-16">[14]</a></sup> The monster described by Tammas has two heads;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891133_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891133-20">[17]</a></sup> the equine head has an enormous gaping mouth that exudes a smelly toxic vapour, and a single giant eye like a burning red flame. A particularly gruesome detail is that the nuckelavee has no skin;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012388_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012388-21">[18]</a></sup> black blood courses through yellow veins, and the pale sinews and powerful muscles are visible as a pulsating mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> Other reports state that the creature resembles a <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaur</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMackMack201057_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMackMack201057-22">[19]</a></sup> narratives are inconsistent in the finer details of the demon's description however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orkneyjar_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyjar-2">[2]</a></sup> Traill Dennison only describes a man's head with a "mouth projected like that of a pig".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> Marwick also only mentions one head with a single red eye, and he borrows some of Tammas's characterisation by recording the creature's mouth as "like a whale's".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200022-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nuckelavee's breath was thought to wilt crops and sicken livestock, and it was considered responsible for epidemics and drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> Seaweed burning to create what was known at the time as <i>kelp</i> began on Stronsay in 1722. The product – <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">soda ash</a> – was an alkali mainly used to treat <a href="/wiki/Soil_pH#Acid_affected_soils" title="Soil pH">acidic soil</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891132-15">[13]</a></sup> although as time went on its commercial importance in soap and glass manufacture increased. The pungent smoke emitted during the process was believed to enrage the nuckelavee,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup> resulting in a wild rampage of <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">plague</a>, the deaths of cattle and the destruction of crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scapa_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scapa-23">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scran_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scran-24">[21]</a></sup> The nuckelavee was said to have infected horses on Stronsay with the deadly disease known as mortasheen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[d]</a></sup> to demonstrate its fury and exact its revenge against the islanders for burning seaweed; the infection subsequently spread to all the other islands involved in the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFenton199761_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFenton199761-27">[23]</a></sup> The creature was also blamed for prolonged periods of abnormally low rainfall, leading to water shortages and poor harvests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200023-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confinement">Confinement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuckelavee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Confinement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nuckelavee is the most malevolent of the demons in and around the Scottish islands, without any redeeming characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBriggs200268_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBriggs200268-28">[24]</a></sup> The only entity able to control it is the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Mither" title="Sea Mither">Mither o' the Sea</a>, an ancient spirit in Orcadian mythology who keeps the nuckelavee confined during the summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBane2013252_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBane2013252-29">[25]</a></sup> In common with other mythical sea monsters, with the possible exception of kelpies and the <a href="/wiki/Nuggle" title="Nuggle">nuggle</a> of Shetland, it is unable to wade through fresh flowing water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012388_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012388-21">[18]</a></sup> therefore those it is chasing have only to cross a stream to be rid of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBriggs200253_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBriggs200253-30">[26]</a></sup> Tammas managed to escape from the nuckelavee after he inadvertently splashed it with water from the <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">loch</a> he was alongside; this briefly distracted the monster, allowing Tammas to run over to a nearby channel of fresh water and jump to safety on the opposite bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891133_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison1891133-20">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuckelavee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Malevolent creatures possibly served to provide explanations for incidents that islanders from bygone times were otherwise unable to account for; many ancient myths were based upon the natural elements of the turbulent and ever changing sea around Orkney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison189070_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETraill_Dennison189070-31">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OJ_The_Sea_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-OJ_The_Sea-32">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarwick200019_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarwick200019-33">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012ix_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestwoodKingshill2012ix-34">[30]</a></sup> Established Orcadian tales were strongly influenced by Scandinavian mythology with a blending of traditional <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> stories,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMuir201410_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuir201410-35">[31]</a></sup> so the nuckelavee may have its roots in a mythical creature imported by the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> fused with a traditional Celtic <a href="/wiki/Water_horse" title="Water horse">water horse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orkneyjar_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyjar-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
