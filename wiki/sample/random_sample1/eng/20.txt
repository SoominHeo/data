<p><b>Dennis Joseph "Dan" Brouthers</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/uË/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uË</span><span title="/Î¸/ 'th' in 'thigh'">Î¸</span><span title="/Ér/ 'er' in 'finger'">Ér</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billjames438_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-billjames438-1">[1]</a></sup> May 8, 1858 â August 2, 1932) was an American <a href="/wiki/First_baseman" title="First baseman">first baseman</a> in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> whose career spanned the period from <a href="/wiki/1879_in_baseball" title="1879 in baseball">1879</a> to <a href="/wiki/1896_in_baseball" title="1896 in baseball">1896</a>, with a brief return in <a href="/wiki/1904_in_baseball" title="1904 in baseball">1904</a>. Nicknamed "Big Dan" for his size, he was 6Â feet 2Â inches (1.88Â m) and weighed 207 pounds (94Â kg), which was large by 19th-century standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-library_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-library-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recognized as the first great slugger in baseball history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fame-3">[3]</a></sup> and among the greatest sluggers of his era, he held the record for career <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> from <a href="/wiki/1887_in_baseball" title="1887 in baseball">1887</a> to <a href="/wiki/1889_in_baseball" title="1889 in baseball">1889</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-homerunprogress_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-homerunprogress-4">[4]</a></sup> with his final total of 106 tying for the fourth most of the 19th century. His career <a href="/wiki/Slugging_percentage" title="Slugging percentage">slugging percentage</a> of .519 remained the Major League record for a player with at least 4,000 <a href="/wiki/At_bat" title="At bat">at bats</a> until <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a> edged ahead of him in 1922. At the time of his initial retirement, he also ranked second in career <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a> (205), and third in <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a> (1,296) and <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">hits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dominant hitter during the prime of his career, he led (or was in the top of) the league in most offensive categories, including <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a>, <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">runs scored</a>, runs batted in (RBI), <a href="/wiki/On-base_percentage" title="On-base percentage">on-base percentage</a> and hits. He led the league in batting average five times, the most by a 19th-century player, and his career .342 batting average still ranks ninth all-time. Brouthers is one of only 29 players in baseball history to date who have appeared in Major League games in four decades.</p>
<p>He was also an active players' union member, and was elected <a href="/wiki/Vice_president" title="Vice president">vice president</a> of the Brotherhood of Professional Base Ball Players. Brouthers was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in 1945 by the Veterans Committee.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Sylvan_Lake,_New_York" title="Sylvan Lake, New York">Sylvan Lake, New York</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrosheet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-retrosheet-6">[6]</a></sup> he played organized baseball from the time that he was a child, from playing in the local sandlots<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">[7]</a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Semi-professional" title="Semi-professional">semi-professional</a> Actives of <a href="/wiki/Wappingers_Falls,_New_York" title="Wappingers Falls, New York">Wappingers Falls, New York</a>. On July 7, <a href="/wiki/1877_in_baseball" title="1877 in baseball">1877</a>, while running the bases, he collided at home plate with a catcher, named Johnny Quigley, of the Clippers of Harlem. Quigley was knocked unconscious, having suffered a traumatic head injury, and later died from these injuries on August 12. The 19-year-old Brouthers was cleared of any wrongdoing by the authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_League_career">Major League career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major League career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Troy">Troy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Troy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brouthers made his Major League debut on June 23, <a href="/wiki/1879_in_baseball" title="1879 in baseball">1879</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Troy_Trojans_(MLB_team)" title="Troy Trojans (MLB team)">Troy Trojans</a>, and contributed a <a href="/wiki/Single_(baseball)" title="Single (baseball)">single</a> in a come-from-behind victory against the <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_Stars_(National_League)" title="Syracuse Stars (National League)">Syracuse Stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup> Although he was a <a href="/wiki/First_baseman" title="First baseman">first baseman</a>, he was called upon to pitch that season with the Trojans in three games, one of which was on August 21 against <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Bond_(baseball)" title="Tommy Bond (baseball)">Tommy Bond</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Caps" title="Boston Red Caps">Boston Red Caps</a>. Brouthers lost 16â0, and within two weeks he was released from the club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup> He hit .274 that first season, with four home runs, and had 17 RBIs in 39 <a href="/wiki/Games_played" title="Games played">games played</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrosheet_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-retrosheet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his release, Brouthers played for a <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">minor league</a> team in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, and on one occasion in <a href="/wiki/1880_in_baseball" title="1880 in baseball">1880</a>, he hit a game-winning home run in an exhibition game versus the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bisons_(NL)" title="Buffalo Bisons (NL)">Buffalo Bisons</a>, off future Hall of Fame pitcher <a href="/wiki/Pud_Galvin" title="Pud Galvin">Pud Galvin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup> He hit well enough in the minors to get another shot with the Trojans, which lasted just three games when he had only two hits in 12 <a href="/wiki/At_bat" title="At bat">at bats</a>, and he was released again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buffalo">Buffalo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Buffalo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He got his first chance to be an everyday player in <a href="/wiki/1881_in_baseball" title="1881 in baseball">1881</a>, when he was signed by the Bisons, the team that he did well against the previous year. That season he batted .319, and played with them until the team folded after the <a href="/wiki/1885_in_baseball" title="1885 in baseball">1885</a> season. In his first season with the Bisons, he led the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) in home runs and <a href="/wiki/Slugging_percentage" title="Slugging percentage">slugging percentage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup> Brouthers, along with teammates <a href="/wiki/Jack_Rowe" title="Jack Rowe">Jack Rowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardy_Richardson" title="Hardy Richardson">Hardy Richardson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deacon_White" title="Deacon White">Deacon White</a>, became known as the "Big Four".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1882 and <a href="/wiki/1883_in_baseball" title="1883 in baseball">1883</a> he won his first two batting titles, posting .368 and .374 averages, respectively. Along with his two batting titles, during his time in Buffalo he also led the NL in slugging five times, hits and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_bases" title="Total bases">total bases</a> twice each, and triples and RBIs once each,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> with his 1883 total of 97 RBIs setting a new Major League record; <a href="/wiki/Cap_Anson" title="Cap Anson">Cap Anson</a> had set the previous mark of 83 the year before, and retook the record the following year with a total of 102. On July 19, <a href="/wiki/1883_in_baseball" title="1883 in baseball">1883</a>, Brouthers went 6-for-6 with two <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">doubles</a> in a 25â5 defeat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Quakers_(NL)" title="Philadelphia Quakers (NL)">Philadelphia Quakers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detroit_and_The_Brotherhood">Detroit and The Brotherhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Detroit and The Brotherhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of the <a href="/wiki/1885_in_baseball" title="1885 in baseball">1885</a> season, Buffalo was going through financial trouble and were forced to sell off their players, so "The Big Four" were sold to the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Wolverines" title="Detroit Wolverines">Detroit Wolverines</a> of the NL for US$7,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1886_in_baseball" title="1886 in baseball">1886</a>, his first season in Detroit, he again led the league in slugging percentage, the sixth year in a row, and led the league in total bases and doubles and claimed his first home run title. He finished within the top 10 in most offensive categories, including a third-place finish in the batting race with a lofty .370 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> On September 10, <a href="/wiki/1886_in_baseball" title="1886 in baseball">1886</a>, Brouthers hit three home runsâ along with a double and a single, to set the NL record with 15 total bases in one game. This mark tied the Major League record at the time, as <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hecker" title="Guy Hecker">Guy Hecker</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Colonels" title="Louisville Colonels">Louisville Colonels</a> totaled 15 the previous month in the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Detroit team was filled with stars from the era, including future Hall of Famers <a href="/wiki/Sam_Thompson" title="Sam Thompson">Sam Thompson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ned_Hanlon_(baseball)" title="Ned Hanlon (baseball)">Ned Hanlon</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Second_baseman" title="Second baseman">second baseman</a> <a href="/wiki/Fred_Dunlap" title="Fred Dunlap">Fred Dunlap</a>, the "Big Four", and the pitching of <a href="/wiki/Lady_Baldwin" title="Lady Baldwin">Lady Baldwin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlie_Getzien" title="Charlie Getzien">Charlie Getzien</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1886detroit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-1886detroit-10">[10]</a></sup> The team finished with a record of 87 wins and 36 losses, but finished in second place behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Stockings_(1870%E2%80%9389)" title="Chicago White Stockings (1870â89)">Chicago White Stockings</a> by <span class="nowrap">â</span><span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">Â </span><sup>1</sup>â<sub>2</sub></span>Â games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1886nl_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-1886nl-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the off-season, on November 11, <a href="/wiki/1886_in_baseball" title="1886 in baseball">1886</a>, The Executive Council of the Brotherhood of Professional Base Ball Playersâ formed in 1885 as the first organized players' union, met and re-elected <a href="/wiki/John_Montgomery_Ward" title="John Montgomery Ward">John Montgomery Ward</a> as president, and elected Brouthers as vice president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/1887_in_baseball" title="1887 in baseball">1887</a>, with the 1886 roster intact,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1887detroit_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-1887detroit-12">[12]</a></sup> the Wolverines finished in first place, besting the Quakers by <span class="nowrap">â</span><span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide">Â </span><sup>1</sup>â<sub>2</sub></span> games. Brouthers batted .338, and led the league in runs scored with 153, doubles with 36, and on-base percentage, while again finishing in the top 10 in most offensive categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> The Wolverines, behind the bats of Brouthers, Thompson and Richardson, led the League in batting, runs scored and slugging,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MHAL_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MHAL-13">[13]</a></sup> and went on to face the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis_Browns_(NL)" title="St. Louis Browns (NL)">St. Louis Browns</a> in a best-of-15 post-season tournament, the "World's Series". The Wolverines sealed a series championship with their eighth victory in 11 games; however, the two teams finished the series anyway, with Detroit winning 10 games to the Browns' five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1887nl_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-1887nl-14">[14]</a></sup> Brouthers only played in one of those games, getting two hits in three at bats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1887ws_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-1887ws-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the season, on November 17, <a href="/wiki/1887_in_baseball" title="1887 in baseball">1887</a>, members of the NL officially recognized the Brotherhood and met with a Brotherhood committee that consisted of three players â Wardâ Hanlon and Brouthers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1888_in_baseball" title="1888 in baseball">1888</a> Detroit team did not fare as well, finishing in fifth place with a record of 68â63, which was a full 16 games behind the first-place <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1888nl_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-1888nl-16">[16]</a></sup> Brouthers' numbers declined as well, as he did not produce at the same level of his previous seasons. Even with the lower numbers, he still led the league in runs scored with 118, and doubles for the third year in a row.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> The team's decline is attributed to prolonged injuries sustained by key players, while turmoil that unfolded concerning veteran stars' salary demands, and with falling attendance numbers, the club was forced to fold at the season's end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MHAL_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-MHAL-13">[13]</a></sup> Brouthers was then purchased by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Beaneaters" title="Boston Beaneaters">Boston Beaneaters</a> of the NL on October 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston">Boston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1889_in_baseball" title="1889 in baseball">1889</a>, his only season with the Beaneaters, he batted a league-leading .373, along with 105 runs scored and 118 runs batted in; he <a href="/wiki/Strikeout" title="Strikeout">struck out</a> only six times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> The first strikeout occurred on June 11, when he fell victim to <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Welch" title="Mickey Welch">Mickey Welch</a> of the Giants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the season, he â along with many Major League players â jumped to the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players' League">Players' League</a>, a league established by the Brotherhood which competed against the two other Major Leagues already in existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-players_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-players-17">[17]</a></sup> Brouthers signed with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Reds_(1890%E2%80%9391)" title="Boston Reds (1890â91)">Boston Reds</a>, and batted .330 while leading the league in on-base percentage and slugging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrosheet_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-retrosheet-6">[6]</a></sup> The Reds, behind the talents of Brouthers, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Stovey" title="Harry Stovey">Harry Stovey</a>, Hardy Richardson, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Radbourn" title="Charles Radbourn">Charles Radbourn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Player-manager" title="Player-manager">player-manager</a> <a href="/wiki/King_Kelly" title="King Kelly">King Kelly</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1890reds_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-1890reds-18">[18]</a></sup> finished in first place, <span class="nowrap">â</span><span class="frac nowrap">6<span class="visualhide">Â </span><sup>1</sup>â<sub>2</sub></span> games ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Ward%27s_Wonders" title="Brooklyn Ward's Wonders">Brooklyn Ward's Wonders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1890pl_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-1890pl-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Players' League lasted just the one season, and the Reds merged into the American Association, carrying many of the championship team's previous players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1891reds_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-1891reds-20">[20]</a></sup> Again, the team won the league's championship, finishing <span class="nowrap">â</span><span class="frac nowrap">8<span class="visualhide">Â </span><sup>1</sup>â<sub>2</sub></span> games ahead of the St. Louis Browns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1891aa_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-1891aa-21">[21]</a></sup> Brouthers led the league in batting average (.350), on-base percentage and slugging, while finishing second in triples with 19, sixth in doubles with 26, and third in RBIs with 109.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_career">Late career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Brouthers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the American Association folded following the <a href="/wiki/1891_in_baseball" title="1891 in baseball">1891</a> season, Brouthers was sent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Grooms" title="Brooklyn Grooms">Brooklyn Grooms</a> of the NL,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> where he played two seasons. Most of his success came in that first season, when he led the league in batting average, hits, RBIs and total bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/1893_in_baseball" title="1893 in baseball">1893</a> season, he played in only 77 of the team's games, but did well, hitting .337.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> After the season, Brouthers was traded along with <a href="/wiki/Willie_Keeler" title="Willie Keeler">Willie Keeler</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(19th_century)" title="Baltimore Orioles (19th century)">Baltimore Orioles</a> for <a href="/wiki/Billy_Shindle" title="Billy Shindle">Billy Shindle</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Treadway" title="George Treadway">George Treadway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrono_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-chrono-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>This trade brought in two future Hall of Fame players, which added to the already established Orioles core of players including <a href="/wiki/Third_baseman" title="Third baseman">third baseman</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a>, catcher <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Robinson" title="Wilbert Robinson">Wilbert Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shortstop" title="Shortstop">shortstop</a> <a href="/wiki/Hughie_Jennings" title="Hughie Jennings">Hughie Jennings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Center_fielder" title="Center fielder">center fielder</a> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Kelley" title="Joe Kelley">Joe Kelley</a>, all future Hall of Fame members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1894orioles_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-1894orioles-22">[22]</a></sup> The Orioles won the league's championship that season, and it was Brouthers' last full season in the majors, as he again produced great numbers, batting .347, finishing seventh in total bases, fifth in RBIs (128), fourth in doubles (39), and fifth in triples (23).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his career, and most notably during his time in Baltimore, he was known to always have his dog, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_setter" title="Irish setter">Irish setter</a> named Kelly, and had him sit in the players' area. It is claimed that the players never minded much, as he was very well-behaved and never left the area to run out on the field or made very much noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billjames438-9_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-billjames438-9-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in the <a href="/wiki/1895_in_baseball" title="1895 in baseball">1895</a> season, Baltimore sold Brouthers to the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Colonels" title="Louisville Colonels">Louisville Colonels</a> for $500, as his skills seemed to have diminished, and he only played in 24 of Louisville's games that season; he came back to hit .309 for them, ending the year with a .300 overall mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> Following the season, Louisville sold him to the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> for $500, where he played in 57 games in <a href="/wiki/1896_in_baseball" title="1896 in baseball">1896</a>, batting .344.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-14"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> It was his last season in the majors until he appeared for the <a href="/wiki/1904_in_baseball" title="1904 in baseball">1904</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a>, where he was hitless in a two-game stint before retiring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-15"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brouthers is still among the all-time leaders in many offensive categories. His .342 batting average ranks ninth, 205 triples ranks eighth, and his .423 on-base percentage ranks 15th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reference_9-16"><a href="#cite_note-reference-9">[9]</a></sup> He is tied with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Tiernan" title="Mike Tiernan">Mike Tiernan</a> for fourth among 19th-century home run hitters with a total of 106, behind <a href="/wiki/Roger_Connor" title="Roger Connor">Roger Connor</a> (138), Sam Thompson (127) and Stovey (122).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dimitry_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-dimitry-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>Brouthers played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">minor league baseball</a> for the <a href="/wiki/1898_in_baseball" title="1898 in baseball">1898</a> <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs_(International_League)" title="Toronto Maple Leafs (International League)">Toronto Maple Leafs</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_League_(1884%E2%80%931912)" title="Eastern League (1884â1912)">Eastern League</a>, where he won a batting title with a .415 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-library_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-library-2">[2]</a></sup> Later he played for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie_(city),_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie (city), New York">Poughkeepsie, New York</a>, team of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River_League" title="Hudson River League">Hudson River League</a>, batting a league-leading .373 at age 46.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>He remained near baseball for many years, working for his former teammate and New York Giants <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> John McGraw, who placed him in charge of the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a> press gate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-library_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-library-2">[2]</a></sup> He was with the Giants for nearly 20 years in this and other capacities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brouthers died at the age of 74 of a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findagrave_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-findagrave-25">[25]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/East_Orange,_New_Jersey" title="East Orange, New Jersey">East Orange, New Jersey</a>, and is interred at Saint Mary's Cemetery in Wappingers Falls, New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrosheet_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-retrosheet-6">[6]</a></sup> There is a <a class="external text" href="http://www.publicearth.com/places/memorials/dennis-big-dan-brouthers-memorial" rel="nofollow">statue dedicated to him</a> located in Veteran's Park in this small village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballpark_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ballpark-26">[26]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1945_in_baseball" title="1945 in baseball">1945</a>, Brouthers and several other stars of the era prior to 1910 were elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame by the Veterans Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-library_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-library-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-fame-3">[3]</a></sup> In honor of his achievements in Buffalo, he was inducted into the newly formed Buffalo Baseball Hall of Fame in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1999, a survey of the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_American_Baseball_Research" title="Society for American Baseball Research">Society for American Baseball Research</a> ranked him as the sixth-greatest player of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
