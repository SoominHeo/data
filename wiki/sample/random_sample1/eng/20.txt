<p><i><b>Thunderball</b></i> (1965) is the fourth <a href="/wiki/Spy_film" title="Spy film">spy film</a> in the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond_in_film" title="James Bond in film"><i>James Bond</i> series</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> as the fictional <a href="/wiki/Secret_Intelligence_Service" title="Secret Intelligence Service">MI6</a> agent <a href="/wiki/James_Bond_filmography" title="James Bond filmography">James Bond</a>. It is an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Thunderball_(novel)" title="Thunderball (novel)">novel of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Fleming" title="Ian Fleming">Ian Fleming</a>, which in turn <a href="/wiki/Thunderball_(novel)#Writing_and_copyright" title="Thunderball (novel)">was based on an original screenplay</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Whittingham" title="Jack Whittingham">Jack Whittingham</a>. It was directed by <a href="/wiki/Terence_Young_(director)" title="Terence Young (director)">Terence Young</a> with its screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Maibaum" title="Richard Maibaum">Richard Maibaum</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hopkins_(writer)" title="John Hopkins (writer)">John Hopkins</a>.</p>
<p>The film follows Bond's mission to find two <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> atomic bombs stolen by <a href="/wiki/SPECTRE" title="SPECTRE">SPECTRE</a>, which holds the world to ransom for £100 million in diamonds, in exchange for not destroying an unspecified major city in either England or the United States (later revealed to be <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>). The search leads Bond to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, where he encounters <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Largo" title="Emilio Largo">Emilio Largo</a>, the card-playing, eye patch-wearing SPECTRE Number Two. Backed by <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a> agent <a href="/wiki/Felix_Leiter" title="Felix Leiter">Felix Leiter</a> and Largo's mistress, <a href="/wiki/Domino_Vitali" title="Domino Vitali">Domino Derval</a>, Bond's search culminates in an underwater battle with Largo's henchmen. The film had a complex production, with four different <a href="/wiki/Second_unit" title="Second unit">units</a> and about a quarter of the film consisting of underwater scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Thunderball</i> was the first Bond film shot in <a href="/wiki/Widescreen" title="Widescreen">widescreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Panavision" title="Panavision">Panavision</a> and the first to have running time of over two hours.</p>
<p><i>Thunderball</i> was associated with a legal dispute in 1961 when former <a href="/wiki/Ian_Fleming" title="Ian Fleming">Ian Fleming</a> collaborators <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McClory" title="Kevin McClory">Kevin McClory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Whittingham" title="Jack Whittingham">Jack Whittingham</a> sued him shortly after the 1961 publication of the novel, claiming he based it upon the screenplay the trio had earlier written in a failed cinematic translation of James Bond. The lawsuit was settled out of court and Bond film series producers <a href="/wiki/Albert_R._Broccoli" title="Albert R. Broccoli">Albert R. Broccoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Saltzman" title="Harry Saltzman">Harry Saltzman</a>, fearing a rival McClory film, allowed him to retain certain screen rights to the novel's story, plot, and characters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universalexp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-universalexp-2">[2]</a></sup> and for McClory to receive sole producer credit on this film; Broccoli and Salzman were instead credited as Executive Producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was a success, earning a total of $141.2 million worldwide, exceeding the earnings of the three previous Bond films. In 1966, <a href="/wiki/John_Stears" title="John Stears">John Stears</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Academy Award for Best Visual Effects</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-academy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-academy-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">production designer</a> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Adam" title="Ken Adam">Ken Adam</a> was also nominated for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bafta_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bafta-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Thunderball</i> is the second-most financially successful film of the series when adjusting for inflation, after <i><a href="/wiki/Skyfall" title="Skyfall">Skyfall</a></i>. Some critics and viewers showered praise on the film and branded it a welcome addition to the series, while others complained of the repetitively monotonous aquatic action and prolonged length. In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> released a second film adaptation of the novel under the title <i><a href="/wiki/Never_Say_Never_Again" title="Never Say Never Again">Never Say Never Again</a></i>, with McClory as executive producer.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>James Bond — "007" — attends the funeral of Colonel Jacques Bouvar, a <a href="/wiki/SPECTRE" title="SPECTRE">SPECTRE</a> operative (Number 6).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouvar_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouvar-6">[6]</a></sup> Bouvar is alive and disguised as his own widow, but Bond identifies him. Following him to a château, Bond fights and kills him, escaping using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jetpack" title="Jetpack">jetpack</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">Aston Martin DB5</a>, conveniently driven up by confederate and minor <a href="/wiki/Bond_girl" title="Bond girl">Bond girl</a> Madame La Porte (<a href="/wiki/Maryse_Guy_Mitsouko" title="Maryse Guy Mitsouko">Maryse Guy Mitsouko</a>).</p>
<p>Bond is sent by <a href="/wiki/M_(James_Bond)" title="M (James Bond)">M</a> to the Shrublands clinic to improve his health. While being massaged by physiotherapist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patricia_Fearing" title="Patricia Fearing">Patricia Fearing</a>, he notices Count Lippe, a suspicious man with a criminal tattoo (from a <a href="/wiki/Tong_(organization)" title="Tong (organization)">Tong</a>). He searches Lippe's room, but is seen leaving by Lippe's clinic neighbour who is bandaged after <a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">plastic surgery</a>. Lippe tries to murder Bond with a <a href="/wiki/Traction_(orthopedics)" title="Traction (orthopedics)">spinal traction</a> machine, but is foiled by Fearing, whom Bond then seduces. Bond finds a dead bandaged man, François Derval. Derval was a <a href="/wiki/French_Air_Force" title="French Air Force">French Air Force</a> pilot on the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> staff working with the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>. As part of this work, Derval is slated to fly as an additional flight crewmember aboard an <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Avro_Vulcan" title="Avro Vulcan">Avro Vulcan</a> strategic jet bomber loaded with two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bombs" title="Atomic bombs">atomic bombs</a> for a training mission. However, prior to the flight, Derval is murdered by Angelo, a SPECTRE henchman qualified to pilot military jet aircraft and surgically altered to match Derval's appearance.</p>
<p>Angelo takes Derval's place on the flight, sabotaging the plane and killing its RAF crew through poison gas introduced into the aircraft's oxygen system. He then executes a controlled water landing, ditching the Vulcan in an area that SPECTRE has prepared near the Bahamas. After ditching, once the aircraft comes to a stop, Angelo lowers the jet's landing gear, sinking it to a depth readily accessible by SCUBA-equipped SPECTRE operatives and mini-submersibles. While still strapped into the jet's cockpit, Angelo is then killed by <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Largo" title="Emilio Largo">Emilio Largo</a> (SPECTRE No. 2) for trying to extort more money than offered to him. Largo and his henchmen retrieve the stolen atomic bombs from the seabed. After leaving the clinic for a conference, Bond is pursued by Count Lippe through the countryside roads until Lippe and his car are destroyed by SPECTRE agent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiona_Volpe" title="Fiona Volpe">Fiona Volpe</a> as punishment for failing to foresee Angelo's greed. SPECTRE has demanded £100 million in white flawless uncut diamonds from NATO in exchange for returning the bombs. If their demands are not met, SPECTRE will destroy a major city in the United States or the United Kingdom. At the meeting, Bond recognises Derval from a photograph. Since Derval's sister, <a href="/wiki/Domino_Vitali" title="Domino Vitali">Domino</a>, is in <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a>, Bond asks M to send him there, where he discovers Domino is Largo's mistress.</p>
<p>Bond takes a boat to where Domino is snorkelling. After Bond saves her life, the two have lunch together. Later, Bond goes to a party, where he sees Largo and Domino gambling. Bond enters the game against Largo and wins, and subsequently takes Domino to a dance. Recognising each other as adversaries, Bond and Largo begin a tense cat-and-mouse game of attempting to gain advantage over each other while still pretending ignorance of their adversary's true nature.</p>
<p>Bond meets <a href="/wiki/Felix_Leiter" title="Felix Leiter">Felix Leiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Q_(James_Bond)" title="Q (James Bond)">Q</a>, and is issued a collection of gadgets, including an underwater infrared camera, a distress beacon, underwater breathing apparatus, a flare gun, and a <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a>. Bond attempts to swim underwater beneath Largo's boat, but is nearly killed. Bond visits Largo at his estate, <i>Palmyra</i>. Bond's assistant, Paula, is later abducted by Largo for questioning and kills herself before Bond can rescue her.</p>
<p>Bond is kidnapped by Fiona but escapes. He is chased through a <a href="/wiki/Junkanoo" title="Junkanoo">Junkanoo</a> celebration and enters the Kiss Kiss club. Fiona finds and attempts to kill him, but she is shot by her own bodyguard. Bond and Felix search for the Vulcan, finding it underwater. Bond meets Domino scuba-diving and tells her that Largo killed her brother, asking for help finding the bombs. She tells him where to go to replace a henchman on Largo's mission to retrieve them from an underwater bunker. Bond gives her his Geiger counter, asking her to look for them on Largo's ship. She is discovered and captured. Disguised as Largo's henchman, Bond uncovers Largo's plan to destroy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_Beach" title="Miami Beach">Miami Beach</a>.</p>
<p>Bond is discovered and rescued by Leiter, who orders <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">United States Navy SEALs</a> to parachute to the area via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> transport aircraft. After an underwater battle, the henchmen surrender. Largo escapes to his ship, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disco_Volante_(ship)" title="Disco Volante (ship)">Disco Volante</a></i>, which has one of the bombs on board. Largo attempts to escape by jettisoning the rear of the ship, which has come under fire by pursuing <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_Guard" title="U.S. Coast Guard">U.S. Coast Guard</a> vessels. The front section, a <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">hydrofoil</a>, escapes. Bond, also aboard, and Largo fight; Largo is about to shoot him when Domino, freed by Largo's nuclear physicist Ladislav Kutze, kills Largo with a harpoon. Bond and Domino jump overboard, the boat runs aground and explodes. A <a href="/wiki/Fulton_surface-to-air_recovery_system" title="Fulton surface-to-air recovery system">sky hook</a>-equipped CIA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-17" title="B-17">B-17</a> aircraft then rescues them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_disputes">Legal disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Thunderball_(novel)#Controversy" title="Thunderball (novel)">Rights controversy of <i>Thunderball</i></a></div>
<p>Originally meant as the first <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> film, <i>Thunderball</i> was the centre of legal disputes that began in 1961 and ran until 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost_Bond_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lost_Bond-11">[11]</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/Ian_Fleming" title="Ian Fleming">Ian Fleming</a> collaborators <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McClory" title="Kevin McClory">Kevin McClory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Whittingham" title="Jack Whittingham">Jack Whittingham</a> sued Fleming shortly after the 1961 publication of the <i>Thunderball</i> novel, claiming he based it upon the screenplay the trio had earlier written in a failed cinematic translation of James Bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universalexp_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-universalexp-2">[2]</a></sup> The lawsuit was settled out of court; McClory retained certain screen rights to the novel's story, plot, and characters. By then, <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> was a box office success, and series producers Broccoli and Saltzman feared a rival McClory film beyond their control; they agreed to McClory's producer's credit of a cinematic <i>Thunderball</i>, with them as executive producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mkbb_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mkbb-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sources for <i>Thunderball</i> are controversial among film aficionados. In 1961, Ian Fleming published his novel based upon a television screenplay that he and others developed into the film screenplay; the efforts were unproductive, and Fleming expanded the script into his ninth James Bond novel. Consequently, one of his collaborators, Kevin McClory, sued him for plagiarism; they settled out of court in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, in 1964, Eon producers Broccoli and Saltzman agreed with McClory to cinematically adapt the novel; it was promoted as "Ian Fleming's <i>Thunderball</i>". Yet, along with the <a href="/wiki/WGA_screenwriting_credit_system" title="WGA screenwriting credit system">official credits</a> to screenwriters <a href="/wiki/Richard_Maibaum" title="Richard Maibaum">Richard Maibaum</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hopkins_(writer)" title="John Hopkins (writer)">John Hopkins</a>, the screenplay is also identified as <i>based on an original screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Whittingham" title="Jack Whittingham">Jack Whittingham</a></i> and as <i>based on the original story by Kevin McClory, Jack Whittingham, and Ian Fleming</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mkbb_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mkbb-12">[12]</a></sup> To date, the novel has twice been adapted cinematically; the 1983 <a href="/wiki/Jack_Schwartzman" title="Jack Schwartzman">Jack Schwartzman</a>-produced <i><a href="/wiki/Never_Say_Never_Again" title="Never Say Never Again">Never Say Never Again</a></i> features Sean Connery as James Bond, but is not an Eon production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Broccoli's original choice for the role of Domino Derval was <a href="/wiki/Julie_Christie" title="Julie Christie">Julie Christie</a> following her performance in <i><a href="/wiki/Billy_Liar_(film)" title="Billy Liar (film)">Billy Liar</a></i> in 1963. Upon meeting her personally, however, he was disappointed and turned his attentions towards <a href="/wiki/Raquel_Welch" title="Raquel Welch">Raquel Welch</a> after seeing her on the cover of the October 1964 issue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_magazine" title="Life magazine"><i>Life</i></a>. Welch, however, was hired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Zanuck" title="Richard Zanuck">Richard Zanuck</a> of <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> to appear in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Voyage" title="Fantastic Voyage">Fantastic Voyage</a></i> the same year instead. <a href="/wiki/Faye_Dunaway" title="Faye Dunaway">Faye Dunaway</a> was also considered for the role and came close to signing for the part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6tt_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mi6tt-14">[14]</a></sup> Saltzman and Broccoli auditioned an extensive list of relatively unknown European actresses and models including former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miss_Italy" title="Miss Italy">Miss Italy</a> <a href="/wiki/Maria_Grazia_Buccella" title="Maria Grazia Buccella">Maria Grazia Buccella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yvonne_Monlaur" title="Yvonne Monlaur">Yvonne Monlaur</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hammer_horror" title="Hammer horror">Hammer horror</a> films and <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Paul" title="Gloria Paul">Gloria Paul</a>. Eventually former <a href="/wiki/Miss_France" title="Miss France">Miss France</a> <a href="/wiki/Claudine_Auger" title="Claudine Auger">Claudine Auger</a> was cast, and the script was rewritten to make her character French rather than Italian, although her lines was redubbed for the final cut. Nevertheless, director Young would cast her once again in his next film, <i><a href="/wiki/Triple_Cross_(1966_film)" title="Triple Cross (1966 film)">Triple Cross</a></i> (1966). One of the actresses that tried for Domino, <a href="/wiki/Luciana_Paluzzi" title="Luciana Paluzzi">Luciana Paluzzi</a>, later accepted the role as the redheaded femme fatale assassin Fiona Kelly who originally was intended by Maibaum to be Irish. The surname was changed to Volpe in co-ordination with Paluzzi's nationality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6tt_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-mi6tt-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Guy_Hamilton" title="Guy Hamilton">Guy Hamilton</a> was invited to direct, but considered himself worn out and "creatively drained" after the production of <i><a href="/wiki/Goldfinger_(film)" title="Goldfinger (film)">Goldfinger</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Terence_Young_(director)" title="Terence Young (director)">Terence Young</a>, director of the first two Bond films, returned to the series. Coincidentally, when Saltzman invited him to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._No_(film)" title="Dr. No (film)">Dr. No</a></i>, Young expressed interest in directing adaptations of <i>Dr. No</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/From_Russia_with_Love_(film)" title="From Russia with Love (film)">From Russia with Love</a></i> and <i>Thunderball</i>. Years later, Young said <i>Thunderball</i> was filmed "at the right time",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TerenceYoung_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-TerenceYoung-15">[15]</a></sup> considering that if it was the first film in the series, the low budget (<i>Dr. No</i> cost only $1 million) would not have yielded good results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TerenceYoung_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-TerenceYoung-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Thunderball</i> was the final James Bond film directed by Young.</p>
<p>Filming commenced on 16 February 1965, with principal photography of the opening scene in Paris. Filming then moved to the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Anet" title="Château d'Anet">Château d'Anet</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Dreux" title="Dreux">Dreux</a>, France for the fight in pre-credit sequence. Much of the film was shot in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>; <i>Thunderball</i> is widely known for its extensive underwater action scenes which are played out through much of the latter half of the film. The rest of the film was shot at <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">Pinewood Studios</a>, Buckinghamshire, <a href="/wiki/Silverstone_Circuit" title="Silverstone Circuit">Silverstone racing circuit</a> for the chase involving Count Lippe, Fiona Volpe and James Bond's Aston Martin DB5 before moving to <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Island" title="Paradise Island">Paradise Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> (where most of the footage was shot), and Miami.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttp_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ttp-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Hartford" title="Huntington Hartford">Huntington Hartford</a> gave permission to shoot footage on his <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Island" title="Paradise Island">Paradise Island</a> and is thanked at the end of the film.</p>

<p>On arriving in Nassau McClory searched for possible locations to shoot many of the key sequences of the film and used the home of a local millionaire couple, the Sullivans, for Largo's estate, <i>Palmyra</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Part of the SPECTRE underwater assault was also shot on the coastal grounds of another millionaire's home on the island. The most difficult sequences to film were the underwater action scenes; the first to be shot underwater was at a depth of 50 feet to shoot the scene where <a href="/wiki/SPECTRE" title="SPECTRE">SPECTRE</a> divers remove the atomic bombs from the sunken Vulcan bomber. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lamont" title="Peter Lamont">Peter Lamont</a> had previously visited a Royal Air Force bomber station carrying a concealed camera which he used to get close-up shots of secret missiles (those appearing in the film were not actually present).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup> Most of the underwater scenes had to be done at lower tides due to the sharks in the Bahamian sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Connery's life was in danger in the sequence with the sharks in Largo's pool. He had been in fear of this risk when he read the script. He insisted that production designer <a href="/wiki/Ken_Adam" title="Ken Adam">Ken Adam</a> build a special Plexiglas partition inside the pool, but it was not a fixed structure and one of the sharks managed to pass through it. Connery had to abandon the pool immediately, seconds away from attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttp_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ttp-16">[16]</a></sup> Ken Adam later told UK daily newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We had to use special effects but, unlike special effects today, they were real. The jet pack we used in <i>Thunderball</i> was real - it was invented for the United States Army. Bloody dangerous, and it only lasted a couple of minutes. The ejector seat in the Aston Martin was real and Emilio Largo's boat, the <i>Disco Volante</i>, was real. You had power boats at that time but there were no good-sized yachts that were able to travel at 40 to 50 knots so it was quite a problem. But by combining a hydrofoil, which we bought in Puerto Rico for $10,000, and a catamaran, it at least looked like a big yacht. We combined the two hulls with a one-inch slip bolt and when they split it worked like a dream. We used lots of sharks for this movie. I'd rented a villa in the Bahamas with a salt-water pool which we filled with sharks and used for underwater filming. The smell was horrendous. This was where Sean Connery came close to being bitten. We had a plexiglass corridor to protect him but I didn't have quite enough plexiglass and one of the sharks got through. He never got out of a pool faster in his life - he was walking on water."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_17_September_2005_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian_17_September_2005-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another dangerous situation occurred when special effects coordinator <a href="/wiki/John_Stears" title="John Stears">John Stears</a> brought in a supposed dead shark carcass to be towed around the pool. The shark, however, was not dead and revived at one point. Due to the dangers on the set, stuntman Bill Cummings demanded an extra fee of £250 to double for Largo's sidekick Quist as he was dropped into the pool of sharks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-mi6-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The climactic underwater battle was shot at Clifton Pier and was choreographed by Hollywood expert <a href="/wiki/Ricou_Browning" title="Ricou Browning">Ricou Browning</a>, who had worked on many films previously such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creature_From_the_Black_Lagoon" title="Creature From the Black Lagoon">Creature From the Black Lagoon</a></i> in 1954. He was responsible for the staging of the cave sequence and the battle scenes beneath the <i>Disco Volante</i> and called in his specialist team of divers who posed as those engaged in the onslaught. <a href="/wiki/Voit" title="Voit">Voit</a> provided much of the underwater gear in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a> and film <a href="/wiki/Tie-in" title="Tie-in">tie-in</a> merchandise. Lamar Boren, an underwater photographer, was brought in to shoot all of the sequences. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> Lieutenant-Colonel <a href="/wiki/Charles_Russhon" title="Charles Russhon">Charles Russhon</a>, who had already helped alliance Eon productions with the local authorities in Turkey for <i>From Russia with Love</i> (1963) and at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Knox" title="Fort Knox">Fort Knox</a> for <i>Goldfinger</i> (1964), stood by and was able to supply the experimental rocket fuel used to destroy the <i>Disco Volante</i>. Russhon, using his position, was also able to gain access to the United States Navy's <a href="/wiki/Fulton_surface-to-air_recovery_system" title="Fulton surface-to-air recovery system">Fulton surface-to-air recovery system</a>, used to lift Bond and Domino from the water at the end of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-mi6-20">[20]</a></sup> Filming ceased in May 1965 and the final scene shot was the physical fight on the bridge of the <i>Disco Volante</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>While in Nassau, during the final shooting days, special effects supervisor John Stears was supplied experimental rocket fuel to use in exploding Largo's yacht, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disco_Volante_(ship)" title="Disco Volante (ship)">Disco Volante</a></i>. Ignoring the true power of the <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> liquid, Stears doused the entire yacht with it, took cover, and then detonated the boat. The resultant massive explosion shattered windows along Bay Street in Nassau roughly 30 miles away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup> Stears went on to win an Academy Award for his work on <i>Thunderball</i>.</p>
<p>As the filming neared its conclusion, Connery had become increasingly agitated with press intrusion and was distracted with difficulties in his marriage of 32 months to actress <a href="/wiki/Diane_Cilento" title="Diane Cilento">Diane Cilento</a>. Connery refused to speak to journalists and photographers who followed him in <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> stating his frustration with the harassment that came with the role; "I find that fame tends to turn one from an actor and a human being into a piece of merchandise, a public institution. Well, I don't intend to undergo that metamorphosis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In the end he gave only a single interview, to <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a>,</i> as filming was wrapped up, and even turned down a substantial fee to appear in a promotional TV special made by Wolper Productions for <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incredible_World_of_James_Bond" title="The Incredible World of James Bond">The Incredible World of James Bond</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-mi6-20">[20]</a></sup> According to editor <a href="/wiki/Peter_R._Hunt" title="Peter R. Hunt">Peter R. Hunt</a>, <i>Thunderball'</i>s release was delayed for three months, from September until December 1965, after he met <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Picker" title="David Picker">David Picker</a> of United Artists, and convinced him it would be impossible to edit the film to a high enough standard without the extra time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_James_Bond_vehicles" title="List of James Bond vehicles">List of James Bond vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_James_Bond_gadgets" title="List of James Bond gadgets">List of James Bond gadgets</a></div>
<p>Thanks to special-effects man <a href="/wiki/John_Stears" title="John Stears">John Stears</a>, <i>Thunderball'</i>s pre-title teaser, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">Aston Martin DB5</a> (introduced in <i>Goldfinger</i>), reappears armed with rear-firing <a href="/wiki/Water_cannon" title="Water cannon">water cannon</a>, seeming noticeably weathered – just dust and dirt, raised moments earlier by Bond's landing with the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Rocket_Belt" title="Bell Rocket Belt">Bell Rocket Belt</a> (developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Aircraft_Corporation" title="Bell Aircraft Corporation">Bell Aircraft Corporation</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Jet_pack" title="Jet pack">rocket belt</a> Bond uses to escape the château actually worked, and was used many times, before and after, for entertainment, most notably at <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_I" title="Super Bowl I">Super Bowl I</a> and at scheduled performances at the 1964–1965 New York World's Fair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rocketbelt_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-rocketbelt-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bond receives a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spear_gun" title="Spear gun">spear gun</a>-armed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diver_Propulsion_Vehicle" title="Diver Propulsion Vehicle">underwater jet pack</a> scuba (allowing the frogman to manoeuvre faster than other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frogmen" title="Frogmen">frogmen</a>). Designed by Jordan Klein, green dye was meant to be used by Bond as a <a href="/wiki/Smoke_screen" title="Smoke screen">smoke screen</a> to escape pursuers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Instead Ricou Browning, the film's underwater director, used it to make Bond's arrival more dramatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ricou_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ricou-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fulton_surface-to-air_recovery_system" title="Fulton surface-to-air recovery system">sky hook</a>, used to rescue Bond at the end of the film, was a rescue system used by the United States military at the time. At <i>Thunderball'</i>s release, there was confusion as to whether a <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> such as the one that appears in the film existed; most Bond gadgets, while implausible, often are based upon real technology. In the real world, a rebreather could not be so small, as it has no room for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_bag" title="Breathing bag">breathing bag</a>, while the alternative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-circuit_scuba" title="Open-circuit scuba">open-circuit</a> scuba releases exhalation bubbles, which the film device does not. It was made with two CO<sub>2</sub> bottles glued together and painted, with a small mouthpiece attached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ricou_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ricou-25">[25]</a></sup> For this reason, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Corps_of_Engineers" title="Royal Corps of Engineers">Royal Corps of Engineers</a> asked Peter Lamont how long a man could use the device underwater, the answer was "As long as you can hold your breath."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maurice_Binder" title="Maurice Binder">Maurice Binder</a> was hired to design the title sequence, and was involved in a dispute with Eon Production to have his name credited in the film. As <i>Thunderball</i> was the first James Bond film shot in <a href="/wiki/Panavision" title="Panavision">Panavision</a>, Binder had to reshoot the iconic gun barrel scene which permitted him to not only incorporate pinhole photographic techniques to shoot inside a genuine gun barrel, but also made Connery appearing in the sequence for the first time a reality, as stunt man <a href="/wiki/Bob_Simmons_(stunt_man)" title="Bob Simmons (stunt man)">Bob Simmons</a> had doubled for him in the three previous films. Binder gained access to the tank at <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">Pinewood</a> which he used to film the silhouetted title girls who appeared naked in the opening sequence, which was the first time actual nudity (although concealed) had ever been seen in a Bond film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi6_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-mi6-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 26 June 2013 <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie's">Christie's</a> auction house sold the <a href="/wiki/Breitling_SA" title="Breitling SA">Breitling SA</a> Top Time watch worn in the film by <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> for over £100,000; given to Bond by Q, it was also a Geiger counter in the plot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breitling_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-breitling-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Thunderball_(soundtrack)" title="Thunderball (soundtrack)">Thunderball (soundtrack)</a></div>
<p><i>Thunderball</i> was the third James Bond score composed by <a href="/wiki/John_Barry_(composer)" title="John Barry (composer)">John Barry</a>, after <i><a href="/wiki/From_Russia_with_Love_(soundtrack)" title="From Russia with Love (soundtrack)">From Russia with Love</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Goldfinger_(soundtrack)" title="Goldfinger (soundtrack)">Goldfinger</a></i>. The original title song was entitled "Mr. Kiss Kiss, Bang Bang", taken from an Italian journalist who in 1962 dubbed agent 007 as Mr. Kiss Kiss, Bang Bang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarnesHearn200352_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnesHearn200352-28">[28]</a></sup> The title theme was written by Barry and <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Bricusse" title="Leslie Bricusse">Leslie Bricusse</a>; the song was originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a>, and later rerecorded by <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>, whose version was not released until the 1990s. The song was removed from the title credits after producers <a href="/wiki/Albert_R._Broccoli" title="Albert R. Broccoli">Albert R. Broccoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Saltzman" title="Harry Saltzman">Harry Saltzman</a> were worried that a theme song to a James Bond film would not work well if the song did not have the title of the film in its lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-making_of-1">[1]</a></sup> Barry then teamed up with lyricist <a href="/wiki/Don_Black_(lyricist)" title="Don Black (lyricist)">Don Black</a> and wrote "Thunderball", which was sung by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a> who, according to Bond production legend, fainted in the recording booth when singing the song's final note. Jones said of it, "I closed my eyes and I held the note for so long when I opened my eyes the room was spinning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Country musician <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a> also submitted a song to Eon productions titled "Thunderball", but it went unused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film premiered on 9 December 1965 in Tokyo and opened on 29 December 1965 in the UK. It was a major success at the box office with record-breaking earnings. <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> reported that <i>Thunderball</i> was the No. 1 money maker of 1966 at the North American box office by a large margin, with a net profit of $26,500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobbett23_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cobbett23-31">[31]</a></sup> The second highest money maker of 1966 was <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Zhivago_(film)" title="Doctor Zhivago (film)">Doctor Zhivago</a></i> at $15,000,000; in third place was <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Afraid_of_Virginia_Woolf%3F_(film)" title="Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? (film)">Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?</a></i> at $10,300,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobbett23_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cobbett23-31">[31]</a></sup> It eventually grossed $63.6 million in the United States, equating to roughly 58.1 million admissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In total, the film has earned $141.2 million worldwide, surpassing the earnings of the three preceding films in the series—easily recouping its $9 million budget—and remained the highest-grossing Bond film until <i><a href="/wiki/Live_and_Let_Die_(film)" title="Live and Let Die (film)">Live and Let Die</a></i> (1973) assumed the record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> After adjusting its earnings to 2011 prices, it has made approximately $1 billion, making it the second most financially successful Bond film after <i><a href="/wiki/Skyfall" title="Skyfall">Skyfall</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2013203_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuinness_World_Records2013203-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Thunderball</i> won an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Academy Award for Best Visual Effects</a> awarded to <a href="/wiki/John_Stears" title="John Stears">John Stears</a> in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-academy_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-academy-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Adam" title="Ken Adam">Ken Adam</a> the production director was also nominated for a <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Production_Design#Best_British_Production_Design_.E2.80.93_Colour" title="BAFTA Award for Best Production Design">Best Production Design</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bafta_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bafta-5">[5]</a></sup> The film won the <a href="/wiki/Goldene_Leinwand" title="Goldene Leinwand">Golden Screen Award</a> in Germany and the Golden Laurel Action Drama award at the 1966 Laurel Awards. The film was also nominated for an Edgar Best Foreign Film award at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe_Awards" title="Edgar Allan Poe Awards">Edgar Allan Poe Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_reviews">Contemporary reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Contemporary reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon its release the film received generally positive reviews. <a href="/wiki/Dilys_Powell" title="Dilys Powell">Dilys Powell</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i> remarked after seeing the film that "The cinema was a duller place before 007."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Robinson_(film_critic_and_author)" title="David Robinson (film critic and author)">David Robinson</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Financial_Times" title="The Financial Times">The Financial Times</a></i> criticised the appearance of Connery and his effectiveness to play Bond in the film remarking: "It's not just that Sean Connery looks a lot more haggard and less heroic than he did two or three years ago; but there is much less effort to establish him as connoisseur playboy. Apart from the off-handed order for Beluga, there is little of that comic display of bon viveur-manship that was one of the charms of Connery's almost-a-gentleman 007."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reflective_reviews">Reflective reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunderball_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reflective reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Danny_Peary" title="Danny Peary">Danny Peary</a>, <i>Thunderball</i> "takes forever to get started and has too many long underwater sequences during which it's impossible to tell what's going on. Nevertheless, it's an enjoyable entry in the Bond series. Sean Connery is particularly appealing as Bond – I think he projects more confidence than in other films in the series. Film has no <i>great</i> scene, but it's entertaining as long as the actors stay above water.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critics such as <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> praised Connery's performance, the <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a> character of Fiona Volpe and the underwater action sequences, remarking that they were well choreographed and clearly shot. He criticised the length of the scenes, however, and believed they were too long and in need of editing, particularly during the film's climax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film received an 86% "fresh" rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2014 <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">Time Out</a></i> polled several film critics, directors, actors and stunt actors to list their top action films;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>Thunderball</i> was listed at number 73.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
