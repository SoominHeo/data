<p>The city of <b>Gainesville</b> is the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hall_County,_Georgia" title="Hall County, Georgia">Hall County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-4">[4]</a></sup> As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>, the city had a population of 33,804.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of its large number of poultry processing plants, it is often called the "<a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">Poultry</a> Capital of the World". Gainesville is the principal city of, and is included in, the Gainesville, Georgia <a href="/wiki/Gainesville,_Georgia_metropolitan_area" title="Gainesville, Georgia metropolitan area">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>, which is included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta</a>-<a href="/wiki/Sandy_Springs,_Georgia" title="Sandy Springs, Georgia">Sandy Springs</a>-Gainesville, Georgia-<a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> (part) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gainesville was established as Mule Camp Springs by European-American settlers in the early 1800s. Less than three years after the organization of <a href="/wiki/Hall_County,_Georgia" title="Hall County, Georgia">Hall County</a> on December 15, 1818, Mule Camp Springs was renamed as Gainesville on April 21, 1821. It was named in honor of General <a href="/wiki/Edmund_P._Gaines" title="Edmund P. Gaines">Edmund P. Gaines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a hero of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> and a noted military surveyor and road-builder. Gainesville was selected by the legislature to be the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a>, and was chartered by the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_General_Assembly" title="Georgia General Assembly">Georgia Legislature</a> on November 30, 1821.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rush</a> that began in nearby <a href="/wiki/Lumpkin_County,_Georgia" title="Lumpkin County, Georgia">Lumpkin County</a> in the 1830s resulted in an increase in the number of settlers and the beginning of a business community. In the middle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nineteenth_century" title="Nineteenth century">nineteenth century</a>, Gainesville had two important events. In 1849, it became established as a resort center, with people attracted to the springs. In 1851, much of the small city was destroyed by fire.</p>
<p>After the American Civil War, Gainesville began to grow from 1870. In 1871 the Airline Railroad, later named the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Southern_Railroad" title="Georgia Southern Railroad">Georgia Southern Railroad</a>, began to stop in Gainesville, increasing its ties to other markets and stimulating business and population. It grew from 1,000 in 1870, to over 5,000 by 1900.</p>
<p>By 1898, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> mills had become the primary driver of the economy, with the railroad integral to delivering raw cotton and carrying away the mills' products. With the revenues generated by the mills, in 1902, Gainesville became the first city south of Baltimore to install street lamps. On March 1, 1905, free mail delivery began in Gainesville and on August 10, 1910 the US Gainesville post office was opened. On December 22, 1915 the city's first <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscraper</a>, the Jackson Building, had its formal opening. In 1919 Southern Bell made improvements to the phone system.</p>
<p>City services began in Gainesville on February 22, 1873 with the election of a City Marshal, followed by solid waste collection in 1874. In 1890, a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_bond" title="Municipal bond">bond issue</a> to fund the waterworks was passed, and the original water distribution system was developed.</p>
<p>In 1943, at the height of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Gainesville contributed to the war effort by leasing the <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> to the US government for $1.00. The military used it as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station" title="Naval Air Station">Naval Air Station</a> for training purposes. In 1947, the airport was returned to the City of Gainesville, improved by the addition of two 4,000 ft landing strips (one of which was later lengthened to 5,500 ft).</p>
<p>After World War II, a businessman named Jesse Jewell started the poultry industry in north Georgia. Chickens have since become the state's largest agricultural crop. This $1 billion a year industry has given Gainesville the title "Poultry Capital of the World."</p>
<p>In 1957, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers constructed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Sidney_Lanier" title="Lake Sidney Lanier">Lake Sidney Lanier</a>, by constructing Buford Dam on the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a>. The river also flows through Alabama and Florida. Named after an American poet, the lake is located along the border with Forsyth County, The manmade lake covers more than 38,000 acres and is a center of outdoor recreation. Not far from the major metropolitan area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, this is the most visited Corps-created lake in the nation. It has an economic impact of more than $2 billion annually.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1996_Atlanta_Olympics" title="1996 Atlanta Olympics">1996 Atlanta Olympics</a>, Gainesville served as the venue for the rowing and kayaking medal competitions, which were staged on Lake Lanier.</p>
<p>Gainesville gained accreditation of its Parks and Recreation Department in 2001. This was the third department in the state to be accredited. The Lakeside Water Treatment Plant opened in 2002. The city has sponsored new social activities, including the Spring Chicken Festival in 2003, the Art in the Square gathering in 2004, and "Dredgefest" in 2008.</p>
<p>2008 also saw the reopening of the Fair Street Neighborhood Center, the reopening of the Linwood Water Reclamation Facility Grand, and the completion of the Longwood Park Fishing Pier.</p>

<p>Gainesville is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gainesville%2C_Georgia&amp;params=34_18_16_N_83_50_2_W_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">34°18′16″N</span> <span class="longitude">83°50′2″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">34.30444°N 83.83389°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">34.30444; -83.83389</span></span></span></a></span> (34.304490, -83.833897).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 29.1 square miles (75 km<sup>2</sup>), of which, 27.1 square miles (70 km<sup>2</sup>) of it is land and 2.0 square miles (5.2 km<sup>2</sup>) of it (6.94%) is water.</p>
<p>Nestled in the <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Mountains" title="Blue Ridge Mountains">Blue Ridge Mountains</a>, parts of Gainesville lie along the shore of one of the nation's most popular inland water destinations, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Lanier" title="Lake Lanier">Lake Lanier</a>. Named after Confederate Veteran, Georgia author and musician <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lanier" title="Sidney Lanier">Sidney Lanier</a>, the lake was created in 1956 when the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Army Corps of Engineers</a> dammed the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Buford,_Georgia" title="Buford, Georgia">Buford, Georgia</a> and flooded an Appalachian mountain valley. Although created primarily for hydroelectricity and flood control, it also serves as a reservoir providing water to the city of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. The lake is also a very popular recreational attraction for all of north Georgia.</p>
<p>Much of Gainesville is heavily wooded, with both <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coniferous" title="Coniferous">coniferous</a> trees.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poultry_farming">Poultry farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Poultry farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The poultry farming industry in Gainesville began to develop after World War II, when Jesse Jewell, a Gainesville feed salesman, began his business. The format he developed was to sell North Georgia farmers baby chicks and feed on credit. When the chicks were grown, Jewell would buy back the adult chickens (broilers) at a price that would cover his costs and guarantee farmers a profit. Once Jewell signed on enough farmers to produce broilers for him, he invested in his own processing plant and hatchery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2013, poultry farming remains a significant economic driver in Gainesville, representing six of its top ten employers (7,600 employees), nearly one-quarter of the total population in the city in 2010 (and a higher proportion of working-age population.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is most well-known business in the area, with statewide revenue exceeding $3 billion. These jobs have attracted numerous Hispanic workers, adding to the diversity of families in the city and county. The proportion of Hispanic and Latino residents is more than 40 percent of the city's population, where the jobs are.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_industry">Other industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest employer in Gainesville is the Northeast Georgia Medical Center, with 5,200 employees. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Wrigley_Company" title="William Wrigley Company">William Wrigley Company</a> has a large manufacturing facility in Gainesville with 700 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> Supercenter in Gainesville employs over 300 people.</p>
<p>The main shopping mall is <a href="/wiki/Lakeshore_Mall_(Georgia)" title="Lakeshore Mall (Georgia)">Lakeshore Mall</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gainesville has a subtropical climate with mild, yet extremely varying<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> winters and very hot summers.</p>
<p>Winters are generally mild, with average temperature highs in the high 40s to mid 50s and lows in the low to mid 30s. However, temperatures can swing up and down in days, often one after another, since <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a> visit frequently. It is not uncommon to see 70s for highs two days and temperatures in the teens for lows the next. Annual snowfall only yields an average of 1 inch, and accumulations for more than a day are rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Gainesville is located in USDA Hardiness Zone 8a, enabling hardy palm trees and cacti to withstand frigid nights. The record low temperature (-14 °F) was recorded in January 1985.</p>
<p>Spring sees highs in the 70s and 80s with lows in the 40s and 50s although March is the wettest month of the year on average.</p>
<p>Summer heat can be intense, but generally highs are in the mid to upper 80s. Gainesville's altitude shaves a few degrees off the summer heat. The area is extremely <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humid</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Heat_index" title="Heat index">heat index</a> can reach the 100s. The record high (107 °F) was recorded in June and July 1952.</p>
<p>Fall has temperatures similar to spring but with much less humidity, especially later in the season. In late October to November the leaves are in the midst of changing colors to brilliant reds, yellows, golden oranges and purples.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_weather">Severe weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Severe weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Gainesville does not sit in <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>, a region of the United States where severe weather is common, <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">Supercell thunderstorms</a> can sweep through any time between March and November, being primarily concentrated in the spring. <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">Tornado watches</a> are frequent in the spring and summer, with a warning appearing at least <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biannually&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biannually (page does not exist)">biannually</a>, occasionally with more than one per year.</p>
<p>Tornado activity in the Gainesville area is above Georgia state average and is 108% greater than the overall U.S. average.</p>
<p>Gainesville was the site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1936_Tupelo-Gainesville_tornado_outbreak" title="1936 Tupelo-Gainesville tornado outbreak">fifth deadliest tornado in U.S. history</a> in 1936,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in which Gainesville was devastated and 203 people were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Gainesville was also the site of another deadly F4 on June 1, 1903, which killed 98 people.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1974_Super_Outbreak" title="1974 Super Outbreak">April 1974</a>, an F4 tornado 22.6 miles away from the Gainesville city center killed six people and injured thirty. In December 1973, a category F3 tornado 2.1 miles away from the city center injured twenty-one people. Both storms caused between $500,000 and $5,000,000 in property damages.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-2">[2]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 33,804 people, 11,273 households, and 7,165 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1.161.6 people per square mile (450.7/km²). There were 12,967 housing units at an average density of 445.6 per square mile (172.9/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 54.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 15.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 3.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 23.4% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 3.2% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 41.6% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 11,273 households out of which 30.3% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 39.3% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 18.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 36.4% were non-families. 28.9% of all households were made up of individuals and 3.64% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.85 and the average family size was 3.55.</p>
<p>Age distribution was 33.9% under the age of 20, 9.5% from 20 to 24, 29.2% from 25 to 44, 16.7% from 45 to 64, and 10.5% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 29.5 years. For every 100 females there were 91.6 males. For every 100 females age 20 and over, there were 84.4 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $38,119, and the median income for a family was $43,734. Males had a median income of $26,377 versus $20,531 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $19,439. About 24.9% of families and 29.1% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 40.7% of those under age 18 and 17.6% of those age 65 or over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In May 2013, the unemployment rate was 6.9%, less than the overall rate in Georgia of 8.3%, the US of 7.6%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the population aged 15 years and over, 31.0% have never been married; 50.0% are now married; 2.4% are separated; 7.7% are widowed; and 9.9% are divorced.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crescent_(Amtrak)" title="Crescent (Amtrak)">Crescent</a></i> connects Gainesville with the cities of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gainesville,_Georgia_(Amtrak_station)" title="Gainesville, Georgia (Amtrak station)">Amtrak station</a> is situated at 116 Industrial Boulevard.</p>
<p>Gainesville also has a bus transit system, the Red Rabbit, that has 130 stops along three routes through Gainesville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Red Rabbit Public Transportation System began its operations in January 2001 with three buses and four mini-buses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GV2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-GV2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gainesville has an airport, the Lee Gilmer Memorial Airport, built in 1940. GVL, with two runways (5,500 ft and 4,001 ft), supports Air Taxi Operations, Itinerant Operations, Local Operations, and Military Operations. Aircraft include 116 single engine aircraft, 21 multi-engine aircraft, 2 jet engine aircraft and 1 helicopter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition, Gainesville has three heliports, Beaver Trail, Lanier Park Hospital and Latham Creek.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gainesville, Georgia is home to the Northeast Georgia Medical Center which houses the Ronnie Greene Heart Center. The city is also served by the Northside Hospital Forsyth in <a href="/wiki/Cumming,_Georgia" title="Cumming, Georgia">Cumming</a>, the BJC Medical Center in <a href="/wiki/Commerce,_Georgia" title="Commerce, Georgia">Commerce</a>, and the Chestatee Regional Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Dahlonega,_Georgia" title="Dahlonega, Georgia">Dahlonega</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of residents 25 and older, 66.2% have a high school degree or higher, 24.2% have a bachelor's degree or higher and 8.9% have a graduate or professional degree.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gainesville_City_School_District">Gainesville City School District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gainesville City School District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gainesville_City_School_District" title="Gainesville City School District">Gainesville City School District</a> holds pre-school to grade twelve, and consists of five elementary schools, a middle school, and a high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The district has 282 full-time teachers and over 4,438 students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Its lone high school, <a href="/wiki/Gainesville_High_School_(Georgia)" title="Gainesville High School (Georgia)">Gainesville High School</a> boasts several notable alumni including, <a href="/wiki/Cris_Carpenter" title="Cris Carpenter">Cris Carpenter</a>, Former professional baseball player (<a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" title="Florida Marlins">Florida Marlins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tasha_Humphrey" title="Tasha Humphrey">Tasha Humphrey</a>, professional basketball player, and <a href="/wiki/Micah_Owings" title="Micah Owings">Micah Owings</a>, Current professional baseball player (<a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a>).</p>

Governor, State of Georgia; James Mills, Georgia State Representative; <a href="/wiki/A.J._Styles" title="A.J. Styles">A.J. Styles</a>, professional wrestler; <a href="/wiki/Mike_Wilson_(offensive_lineman)" title="Mike Wilson (offensive lineman)">Mike "MoonPie" Wilson</a>, former NFL football player; <a href="/wiki/Chester_Willis" title="Chester Willis">Chester Willis</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NFL" title="NFL">NFL</a> football player; <a href="/wiki/Jody_Davis_(baseball)" title="Jody Davis (baseball)">Jody Davis</a>, former catcher for <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> baseball teams; <a href="/wiki/Billy_Greer" title="Billy Greer">Billy Greer</a>, bass guitarist for progressive rock band <a href="/wiki/Kansas_(band)" title="Kansas (band)">Kansas</a>; <a href="/wiki/Corey_Hulsey" title="Corey Hulsey">Corey Hulsey</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NFL" title="NFL">NFL</a> <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Raiders" title="Oakland Raiders">Oakland Raiders</a> football player; <a href="/wiki/Robin_Spriggs" title="Robin Spriggs">Robin Spriggs</a>, author and actor; and <a href="/wiki/Martrez_Milner" title="Martrez Milner">Martrez Milner</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> <a href="/wiki/Tight_end" title="Tight end">tight end</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_education">Private education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Private education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gainesville has three private education facilities, <a href="/wiki/Riverside_Military_Academy" title="Riverside Military Academy">Riverside Military Academy</a> which is a private, college preparatory, boarding and day school for boys in grades 7 through 12; <a href="/wiki/Lakeview_Academy" title="Lakeview Academy">Lakeview Academy</a>, a private, nondenominational, college preparatory school; and Brenau Academy, a female, college preparatory, residential school for grades 9-12, which is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Brenau_University" title="Brenau University">Brenau University</a> system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education">Higher education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Higher education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gainesville has three institutions of higher education: <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Georgia" title="University of North Georgia">University of North Georgia</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Gainesville_State_College" title="Gainesville State College">Gainesville State College</a>), which was established January 8, 2013, as a result of the consolidation of North Georgia College and State University and Gainesville State College; <a href="/wiki/Brenau_University" title="Brenau University">Brenau University</a>, a private, not-for-profit, undergraduate- and graduate-level higher education institution; and the Interactive College of Technology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Library">Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hall County Library, with an operating income of $3,078,611, offers a collection of 292,717 books, 1,355 e-books, 32,858 audio materials, 26,616 video materials, 36 local licensed databases, 146 state licensed databases, 821 print serial subscriptions, and 36 electronic serial subscriptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GV1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-GV1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gainesville,_Georgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Public Defender's Office at Gainesville has been recognized as one of the best in the state of Georgia. Established in 2005, the office provides representation for persons accused of felony offenses in Hall County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Attorneys from the office have been recognized for their community involvement, as well as for their acumen in the courtroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2008, a first-year attorney successfully challenged the Sex Offender Registration Law in the Georgia Supreme Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crime has decreased significantly in the past twelve years in Gainesville, with the rate of crimes per 100,000 population decreasing from a high of 728.6 in 2000, to 307.6 in 2011 (the last year for which data is available).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GV1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-GV1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of full-time law enforcement employees in 2011, including police officers, was 108, with 95 being officers. Gainesville is on par with the state of Georgia in general with 2.77 officers per 1,000 residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GV1_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-GV1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
