<p>The <b>Getty Center</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, is a campus of the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">Getty Museum</a> and other programs of the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Trust" title="J. Paul Getty Trust">Getty Trust</a>. The $1.3 billion Center opened to the public on December 16, 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10Years_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-10Years-2">[2]</a></sup> and is well known for its architecture, gardens, and views overlooking Los Angeles. The Center sits atop a hill connected to a visitors' parking garage at the bottom of the hill by <a href="/wiki/Getty_Center_Monorail" title="Getty Center Monorail">a three-car, cable-pulled</a> <a href="/wiki/Hovertrain" title="Hovertrain">hovertrain</a> <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funicular</a>.</p>
<p>Located in the <a href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Los_Angeles" title="Brentwood, Los Angeles">Brentwood</a> neighborhood of Los Angeles, the Center is one of two locations of the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a> and draws 1.3 million visitors annually. (The other location is the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Villa" title="Getty Villa">Getty Villa</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Palisades,_Los_Angeles" title="Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles">Pacific Palisades</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.) The Center branch of the Museum features pre-20th-century European paintings, drawings, illuminated manuscripts, sculpture, and decorative arts; and 19th- and 20th-century American, Asian, and European photographs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtOnView_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArtOnView-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition, the Museum’s collection at the Center includes outdoor sculpture displayed on terraces and in gardens and the large Central Garden designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Irwin_(artist)" title="Robert Irwin (artist)">Robert Irwin</a>. Among the artworks on display is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vincent_Van_Gogh" title="Vincent Van Gogh">Vincent Van Gogh</a> painting <a href="/wiki/Irises_(painting)" title="Irises (painting)"><i>Irises</i></a>.</p>
<p>Designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Richard_Meier" title="Richard Meier">Richard Meier</a>, the campus also houses the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Research_Institute" title="Getty Research Institute">Getty Research Institute</a> (GRI), the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Conservation_Institute" title="Getty Conservation Institute">Getty Conservation Institute</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Foundation" title="Getty Foundation">Getty Foundation</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Trust" title="J. Paul Getty Trust">J. Paul Getty Trust</a>. The Center's design included special provisions to address concerns regarding earthquakes and fires.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_history">Location and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Getty_USGS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1764" data-file-width="1258" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Getty_USGS.jpg/170px-Getty_USGS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Getty_USGS.jpg/255px-Getty_USGS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Getty_USGS.jpg/340px-Getty_USGS.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Getty_USGS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a> satellite image of the Getty Center. The circular building to the left is the Getty Research Institute. The two buildings at the top are the Getty Trust administrative offices and the rest is the Museum.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Originally, the Getty Museum started in <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty" title="J. Paul Getty">J. Paul Getty</a>'s house located in <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Palisades,_Los_Angeles" title="Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles">Pacific Palisades</a> in 1954. He expanded the house with a museum wing. In the 1970s, Getty built a replica of an Italian villa on his home's property to better house his collection, which opened in 1974. After Getty's death in 1976, the entire property was turned over to the Getty Trust for museum purposes. However, the collection outgrew the site, which has since been renamed the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Villa" title="Getty Villa">Getty Villa</a>, and management sought a location more accessible to Los Angeles. The purchase of the land upon which the Center is located, a campus of 24 acres (9.7 ha) on a 110-acre (45 ha) site in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Mountains" title="Santa Monica Mountains">Santa Monica Mountains</a> above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I-405_(CA)" title="I-405 (CA)">Interstate 405</a>, surrounded by 600 acres (240 ha) kept in a natural state, was announced in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moody_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moody-4">[4]</a></sup> The site cost $25 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The top of the hill is 900 feet (270 m) above I-405, high enough that on a clear day it is possible to see not only the Los Angeles skyline but also the <a href="/wiki/San_Bernardino_Mountains" title="San Bernardino Mountains">San Bernardino Mountains</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Mountains" title="San Gabriel Mountains">San Gabriel Mountains</a> to the east as well as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgenstern_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgenstern-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984, Richard Meier was chosen to be the architect of the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After an extensive conditional-use permit process,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moody_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moody-4">[4]</a></sup> construction by the Hathaway Dinwiddie Construction Company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> began in August 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The construction was significantly delayed, with the planned completion date moved from 1988 to 1995 (as of 1990).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muchnic1990_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muchnic1990-11">[11]</a></sup> By 1995, however, the campus was described as only "more than halfway complete".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moody_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moody-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Center ultimately opened to the public on December 16, 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10Years_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-10Years-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Although the total project cost was estimated to be $350 million as of 1990,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muchnic1990_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Muchnic1990-11">[11]</a></sup> it was later estimated to be $1.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After the Center opened, the villa closed for extensive renovations and reopened on January 28, 2006, to focus on the arts and cultures of ancient Greece, Rome, and Etruria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Currently, the Museum displays collections at both the Getty Center and the Getty Villa in Pacific Palisades.</p>
<p>In 2005, after a series of articles in the <i>Los Angeles Times</i> about the spending practices of the Getty Trust and its then-president Dr. <a href="/wiki/Barry_Munitz" title="Barry Munitz">Barry Munitz</a>, the California Attorney General conducted an investigation of the Getty Trust and found that no laws had been broken. The trust agreed to appoint an outside monitor to review future expenditures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Getty Trust experienced financial difficulties in 2008 and 2009 and cut 205 of 1,487 budgeted staff positions to reduce expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Although the Getty Trust endowment reached $6.4 billion in 2007, it dropped to $4.5 billion in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The endowment rebounded to $6.2 billion by 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Meier" title="Richard Meier">Meier</a> has exploited the two naturally-occurring ridges (which diverge at a 22.5 degree angle) by overlaying two grids along these axes. These grids serve to define the space of the campus while dividing the import of the buildings on it. Along one axis lie the galleries and along the other axis lie the administrative buildings. Meier emphasized the two competing grids by constructing strong view lines through the campus. The main north-south axis starts with the helipad, then includes a narrow walkway between the auditorium and north buildings, continues past the elevator kiosk to the tram station, through the rotunda, past the walls and support columns of the exhibitions pavilion, and finally the ramp besides the west pavilion and the central garden. Its corresponding east-west visual axis starts with the edge of the scholar's wing of the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Research_Institute" title="Getty Research Institute">Getty Research Institute</a> (GRI), the walkway between the central garden and the GRI, the overlook to the azalea pool in the central garden, the walkway between the central garden and the west pavilion, and finally the north wall of the west pavilion and the courtyard between the south and east pavilions.</p>

<p>The main axes of the museum grid that is offset by 22.5 degrees begins with the arrival plaza, carries through the edge of the stairs up to the main entrance, aligns with the columns supporting the rotunda as well as the center point of the rotunda, aligns with travertine benches in the courtyard between the pavilions, includes a narrow walkway between the west and south pavilions, a staircase down to the catus garden and ends in the garden. The corresponding cross axis starts with the center point of the circle forming the GRI library garden, then passing to the center of the entrance rotunda, and aligning with the south wall of the rotunda building. Although all of the Museum is aligned on these alternative axes, portions of the exhibitions pavilion and the east pavilion are aligned on the true north-south axis as a reminder that both grids are present in the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary grid structure is a 30-inch (760 mm) square; most wall and floor elements are 30-inch (760 mm) squares or some derivative thereof. The buildings at the Getty Center are made from <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> with either <a href="/wiki/Travertine" title="Travertine">travertine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> cladding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup> Around 1,200,000 square feet (110,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of travertine was used to build the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the campus, numerous fountains provide white noise as a background. The initial design has remained intact; however benches and fences have been installed around the plaza fountains to discourage visitors from wading into the pools. Some additional revisions have been made in deference to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" title="Americans with Disabilities Act">Americans with Disabilities Act</a>.</p>

<p>The north <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontory</a> is anchored by a circular grass area, which serves as a <a href="/wiki/Heliport" title="Heliport">heliport</a> in case of emergencies, and the south promontory is anchored by a <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulent plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cactus</a> garden. The complex is also encircled by access roads that lead to loading docks and staff parking garages on both the west and east sides of the buildings. The hillside around the complex has been planted with California Live Oak (<a href="/wiki/Quercus_agrifolia" title="Quercus agrifolia">Quercus agrifolia</a>) trees.</p>
<p>The Museum has a seven-story deep underground parking garage with over 1,200 parking spaces. Its roof has an outdoor sculpture garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outdoor_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-outdoor-23">[23]</a></sup> An automated three-car, cable-pulled <a href="/wiki/Hovertrain" title="Hovertrain">hovertrain</a> <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funicular</a>, the "<a href="/wiki/Getty_Center_Monorail" title="Getty Center Monorail">Getty Center Tram</a>", takes passengers between the parking garage at the bottom of the hill and the Museum at the top of the hill.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20031215getty01pano.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A stairway leading down to the Central Garden</div>

<p>Visitors typically arrive at a tram station in the arrival plaza located between the administrative buildings and the Museum entrance. A large set of steps leads to the main doors of the rotunda building. The rotunda building houses information desks, two orientation theatres and Museum shops. It also holds a grand staircase that starts a path toward the paintings located on the second floor of each art pavilion. The rotunda opens to the south to a terrace that links all five of the Museum pavilions. A separate building to the west of the arrival plaza and stairs holds a cafeteria and restaurant. Next to the restaurant is a stone arch, which separates the Museum from the GRI. Stairs from the terrace connecting the GRI and the restaurant lead down to the central garden.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum">Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a>'s estimated 1.3 million visitors annually make it one of the most visited museums in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The collection of the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a> on display at the Getty Center includes "pre-20th-century European paintings, drawings, illuminated manuscripts, sculpture, and decorative arts; and 19th- and 20th-century American and European photographs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtOnView_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArtOnView-3">[3]</a></sup> The paintings include:</p>

<p>The Museum building consists of a three-level base building that is closed to the public and provides staff workspace and storage areas. Five public, two-story towers on the base are called the North, East, South, West and the Exhibitions Pavilions. The Exhibitions Pavilion acts as the temporary residence for traveling art collections and the Foundation's artwork for which the permanent pavilions have no room. The permanent collection is displayed throughout the other four pavilions chronologically: the north houses the oldest art while the west houses the newest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The first-floor galleries in each pavilion house light-sensitive art, such as illuminated manuscripts, furniture, or photography. Computer-controlled skylights on the second-floor galleries allow paintings to be displayed in natural light. The second floors are connected by a series of glass-enclosed bridges and open terraces, both of which offer views of the surrounding hillsides and central plaza. Sculpture is also on display at various points outside the buildings, including on various terraces and balconies. The lower level (the highest of the floors in the base) includes a public cafeteria, the terrace cafe, and the photography galleries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 134,000-square-foot (12,400 m<sup>2</sup>) Central Garden at the Getty Center is the work of artist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Irwin_(artist)" title="Robert Irwin (artist)">Robert Irwin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardens_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gardens-34">[34]</a></sup> Planning for the garden began in 1992, construction started in 1996, and the garden was completed in December 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Irwin was quoted as saying that the Central Garden "is a sculpture in the form of a garden, which aims to be art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Water plays a major role in the garden. A fountain near the restaurant flows toward the garden and appears to fall into a <a href="/wiki/Grotto" title="Grotto">grotto</a> on the north garden wall. The resulting stream then flows down the hillside into the <a href="/wiki/Azalea" title="Azalea">azalea</a> pool. The designers placed rocks and boulders of varying size in the stream bed to vary the sounds from the flowing water. A tree-lined stream descends to a plaza, while the walkway criss-crosses the stream, which continues through the plaza, and goes over a stone waterfall into a round pool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardens_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gardens-34">[34]</a></sup> A maze of azaleas floats in the pool, around which is a series of specialty gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardens_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gardens-34">[34]</a></sup> More than 500 varieties of plant material are used for the Central Garden, but the selection is "always changing, never twice the same".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardens_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gardens-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the original design, an outdoor sculpture garden, called the "Lower Terrace Garden" was added in 2007 on the west side of the central garden just below the scholar's wing of the GRI building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outdoor_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-outdoor-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Getty_Research_Institute_.28GRI.29">Getty Research Institute (GRI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Getty Research Institute (GRI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Getty_Research_Institute" title="Getty Research Institute">Getty Research Institute</a></div>
<p>The Getty Research Institute (GRI) is "dedicated to furthering knowledge and advancing understanding of the visual arts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRI_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRI-38">[38]</a></sup> Among other holdings, GRI's research library contains over 900,000 volumes of books, periodicals, and auction catalogs; special collections; and two million photographs of art and architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> GRI's other activities include exhibitions, publications, and a residential scholars program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRI_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRI-38">[38]</a></sup> At the Getty Center, GRI is located to the west of the Museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup> The round building encircles a landscaped garden and is located to the west of the central garden. The main entrance of GRI is connected by a terrace to the main arrival court of the Museum, with outdoor sculptures placed along the route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outdoor_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-outdoor-23">[23]</a></sup> GRI has one art gallery on its entrance level that is open to the public.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_offices">Other offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The Getty Center, seen from a hill in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bel-Air,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Bel-Air, Los Angeles, California">Bel-Air</a>. East Building, North Building and Auditorium are closest to camera.</div>

<p>Meier also designed three other buildings located next to the north promontory and offset at a 22.5 degree angle from the main axis of the Museum pavilions. The north-most building is an auditorium. Next to it is the North Building, with the East Building sitting between the North Building and the rotunda. The main entrance to the East Building is flanked by two round silos that hold its elevators. A bridge over a sunken courtyard links the main entrance of the East Building to the main walkway that connects the auditorium and North Buildings to the rotunda. These buildings house the Getty Conservation Institute (GCI), the J. Paul Getty Trust and the Getty Foundation. These buildings are generally closed to the public except for special events held in the auditorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup> They are linked to the Museum both by landscaped terraces and by an enclosed glass walkway that leads from the main rotunda.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Getty_Conservation_Institute" title="Getty Conservation Institute">Getty Conservation Institute</a></div>
<p>GCI, which is headquartered at the Getty Center but also has facilities at the Getty Villa, commenced operation in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCI_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCI-40">[40]</a></sup> It "serves the conservation community through scientific research, education and training, model field projects, and the dissemination of the results of both its own work and the work of others in the field" and "adheres to the principles that guide the work of the Getty Trust: service, philanthropy, teaching, and access".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCI_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCI-40">[40]</a></sup> GCI has activities in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_conservation" title="Art conservation">art conservation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architectural_conservation" title="Architectural conservation">architectural conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Getty Foundation awards grants for "the understanding and preservation of the visual arts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In addition, it runs the Getty Leadership Institute for "current and future museum leaders".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Its offices are north of the Museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup> The foundation offices are located in the two administrative buildings that are north of the Museum. The J. Paul Getty Trust, which oversees the Getty Conservation Institute, Getty Foundation, Getty Research Institute, and J. Paul Getty Museum, also has offices there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Architecture_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_for_natural_disasters">Preparation for natural disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Preparation for natural disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg/220px-Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg/330px-Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg/440px-Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Getty_Center_view_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Looking north from main entrance toward arrival plaza.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although the Center's site was thought to have little motion during earthquakes, which are frequent in the Los Angeles area, in 1994, as the Center was being constructed, the <a href="/wiki/1994_Northridge_earthquake" title="1994 Northridge earthquake">Northridge earthquake</a> struck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum-44">[44]</a></sup> It caused "disturbing hairline cracks... in the welds and plated joints of the steel framework."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meier1997_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meier1997-45">[45]</a></sup> As a result, the steelwork through the site was retrofitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meier1997_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meier1997-45">[45]</a></sup> The Center's buildings are thought to be able to survive an earthquake of 7.5 magnitude on the <a href="/wiki/Richter_magnitude_scale" title="Richter magnitude scale">Richter scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fires">Fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Getty_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 16 electrical <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a> at the Center, <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> fluid is used as a coolant "with less risk of ignition" than <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> coolant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The native flammable <a href="/wiki/Chaparral" title="Chaparral">chaparral</a> was removed and fire-resistant <a href="/wiki/Iva_(plant)" title="Iva (plant)">poverty weed</a> was added to the slopes around the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meier1997_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Meier1997-45">[45]</a></sup> Each year, a herd of goats is rented to clear brush on the surrounding hills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the north end of the Center, a tank with 1,000,000 US gal (3,800,000 l) of water, together with a grass-covered helipad, allow helicopters to collect water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The access ramp from the entry plaza to the Museum was constructed to allow a fire truck to pass over it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meier1997_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-Meier1997-45">[45]</a></sup> Inside the Museum, the sprinkler system is designed to balance "between the potential damage of a fire and the risk of water damage to valuable artwork".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
