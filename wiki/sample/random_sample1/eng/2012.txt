<p><b>Genndy Tartakovsky</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> born <b>Gennady Borisovich Tartakovsky</b>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Геннадий Борисович Тартаковский</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup> January 17, 1970) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_American" title="Russian American">Russian American</a> animator, director, writer and producer. He is best known for creating the Cartoon Network animated television series <i><a href="/wiki/Dexter%27s_Laboratory" title="Dexter's Laboratory">Dexter's Laboratory</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Samurai_Jack" title="Samurai Jack">Samurai Jack</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Clone_Wars_(2003_TV_series)" title="Star Wars: Clone Wars (2003 TV series)">Star Wars: Clone Wars</a></i>, and co-creating <i><a href="/wiki/Sym-Bionic_Titan" title="Sym-Bionic Titan">Sym-Bionic Titan</a></i>. In 2011, Tartakovsky joined <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Animation" title="Sony Pictures Animation">Sony Pictures Animation</a>, where he directed his feature film debut, <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Transylvania" title="Hotel Transylvania">Hotel Transylvania</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LosAngelesTimes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LosAngelesTimes-3">[3]</a></sup> and its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Transylvania_2" title="Hotel Transylvania 2">Hotel Transylvania 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although his Russian name <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Геннадий</span> is normally transliterated as Gennady or Gennadiy, he changed its spelling to Genndy after moving to the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genndy_Tartakovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tartakovsky was born January 17, 1970, in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjgt1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-jjgt1-5">[5]</a></sup> His father, Boris, worked as a <a href="/wiki/Dentist" title="Dentist">dentist</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jubera_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jubera-6">[6]</a></sup> for government officials and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Soviet Union national ice hockey team">Soviet Union national ice hockey team</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup> His mother, Miriam, was an assistant principal at a school. He also has a brother, Alexander, who is two years older and currently a computer consultant in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup> Before coming to the United States, his family first moved to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, where he lived next to a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> family. There, Tartakovsky says he was first drawn to art, inspired by a neighbor's daughter. Tartakovsky later commented, "I remember, I was horrible at it. For the life of me, I couldn't draw a circle."</p>
<p>Tartakovsky's family moved to the United States when he was seven<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> due to concerns about the effect of <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitism</a> on their children's lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup> The family originally settled in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feran_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feran-9">[9]</a></sup> and later moved to <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. He was greatly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a> he found there; his first purchase was an issue of the <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Friends" title="Super Friends">Super Friends</a></i>. Tartakovsky began attending Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Field_Elementary_School" title="Eugene Field Elementary School">Eugene Field Elementary School</a> in the third grade. School was hard for him because he felt that everyone recognized him as a foreigner. He went on to attend Chicago's prestigious <a href="/wiki/Lane_Technical_College_Prep_High_School" title="Lane Technical College Prep High School">Lane Technical College Prep High School</a>, and says he never felt he fit in until he was a sophomore there. When he was 16, his father died of a heart attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup> He felt that his father was very strict and was an old-fashioned man, but Genndy's relationship with his father was very special to him. After the death of his father, Genndy and his family moved to government-funded housing, and he began working while still attending high school.</p>
<p>To satisfy his ambitious family, Tartakovsky tried to take an advertising class, because they were encouraging him to be a businessman. However, he signed up late and had little choice over his classes. He was assigned to take an animation class, and this led to his study of film at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_College_Chicago" title="Columbia College Chicago">Columbia College Chicago</a> before moving to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to study animation at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_the_Arts" title="California Institute of the Arts">California Institute of the Arts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feran_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feran-9">[9]</a></sup> (with his friend <a href="/wiki/Rob_Renzetti" title="Rob Renzetti">Rob Renzetti</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup> and there he also met <a href="/wiki/Craig_McCracken" title="Craig McCracken">Craig McCracken</a>. At CalArts, Tartakovsky directed and animated two student films, one of which became the basis for <i><a href="/wiki/Dexter%27s_Laboratory" title="Dexter's Laboratory">Dexter's Laboratory</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feran_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Feran-9">[9]</a></sup> Reportedly, after two years at CalArts, Tartakovsky got a job in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Critic" title="The Critic">The Critic</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jubera_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jubera-6">[6]</a></sup> There, "he learned the trials of TV animation, labor intensive and cranking it out".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jubera_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jubera-6">[6]</a></sup> While he was in Spain, his mother died of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-king-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genndy_Tartakovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Craig McCracken acquired an art director job at <a href="/wiki/Hanna-Barbera" title="Hanna-Barbera">Hanna-Barbera</a> for the show <i><a href="/wiki/2_Stupid_Dogs" title="2 Stupid Dogs">2 Stupid Dogs</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jubera_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jubera-6">[6]</a></sup> and recommended hiring Robert Renzetti and Tartakovsky as well. This was a major turning point in Tartakovsky's career. Hanna-Barbera let Tartakovsky, McCracken, Renzetti and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rudish" title="Paul Rudish">Paul Rudish</a> work in a trailer in the parking lot of the studio, and there Tartakovsky started creating his best-known works. <i>Dexter's Laboratory</i> grew out of a student film with the same title that he produced while at the California Institute of the Arts. Tartakovsky also co-wrote and pencilled the 25th issue of the <i>Dexter's Laboratory</i> comic book series, titled "Stubble Trouble".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Additionally, he helped produce <i><a href="/wiki/The_Powerpuff_Girls" title="The Powerpuff Girls">The Powerpuff Girls</a></i> and has directed many episodes, serving as the animation director for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Powerpuff_Girls_Movie" title="The Powerpuff Girls Movie">The Powerpuff Girls Movie</a></i>. All three projects were nominated repeatedly for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy_Awards" title="Emmy Awards">Emmy Awards</a>, with <i><a href="/wiki/Samurai_Jack" title="Samurai Jack">Samurai Jack</a></i> finally winning "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Animated_Program_(for_Programming_Less_Than_One_Hour)" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program (for Programming Less Than One Hour)">Outstanding Animated Program (For Programming Less Than One Hour)</a>" in 2004 – the same year he would win in the category for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Animated_Program_(for_Programming_One_Hour_or_More)" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program (for Programming One Hour or More)">Outstanding Animated Program (for Programming One Hour or More)</a> for <i>Star Wars: Clone Wars</i>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> creator <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a> hired Tartakovsky to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Clone_Wars_(2003_TV_series)" title="Star Wars: Clone Wars (2003 TV series)">Star Wars: Clone Wars</a></i>, a successful animated microseries taking place between <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_II_%E2%80%93_Attack_of_the_Clones" title="Star Wars: Episode II – Attack of the Clones">Attack of the Clones</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_III_%E2%80%93_Revenge_of_the_Sith" title="Star Wars: Episode III – Revenge of the Sith">Revenge of the Sith</a></i>. The series won three Emmy awards: two for "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Animated_Program_(for_Programming_One_Hour_or_More)" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program (for Programming One Hour or More)">Outstanding Animated Program (for Programming One Hour or More)</a>" in 2004 and 2005, and another for "Outstanding Individual Achievement in Animation" (for background designer Justin Thompson in 2005). Tartakovsky was not involved in the <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Clone_Wars_(2008_TV_series)" title="Star Wars: The Clone Wars (2008 TV series)">follow-up series</a>, and has no plans to work on future <i>Star Wars</i> projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtv_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, Tartakovsky was appointed creative president of <a href="/wiki/The_Orphanage_(company)" title="The Orphanage (company)">The Orphanage</a>'s Orphanage Animation Studios. In 2006, he was chosen as the director for a sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Crystal" title="The Dark Crystal">The Dark Crystal</a></i>, but was eventually replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_and_Peter_Spierig" title="Michael and Peter Spierig">Michael and Peter Spierig</a>. Tartakovsky served as animation director on the 2006 pilot episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Korgoth_of_Barbaria" title="Korgoth of Barbaria">Korgoth of Barbaria</a></i>, which aired on <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a> but was not picked up as a series. He also directed a series of anti-smoking advertisements, one for <a href="/wiki/Nicorette" title="Nicorette">Nicorette</a> in 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and two for <a href="/wiki/Niquitin" title="Niquitin">Niquitin</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He created a short in 2009 entitled <i>Maruined</i> for Cartoon Network's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartoonstitute" title="Cartoonstitute">Cartoonstitute</a></i> program, which has yet to be officially aired or otherwise released.</p>
<p>In 2009, it was announced that Tartakovsky would write and direct a <i><a href="/wiki/Samurai_Jack" title="Samurai Jack">Samurai Jack</a></i> film from <a href="/wiki/Fred_Seibert" title="Fred Seibert">Fred Seibert</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Frederator_Studios" title="Frederator Studios">Frederator Studios</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J._J._Abrams" title="J. J. Abrams">J. J. Abrams</a>' <a href="/wiki/Bad_Robot_Productions" title="Bad Robot Productions">Bad Robot Productions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In June 2012, Tartakovsky said that he had a story to conclude the series, but the project had been shelved after J. J. Abrams moved on to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film)" title="Star Trek (film)">Star Trek</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shock_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shock-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2010, he created storyboards for <a href="/wiki/Jon_Favreau" title="Jon Favreau">Jon Favreau</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_2" title="Iron Man 2">Iron Man 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Tartakovsky's last TV series for Cartoon Network, <i><a href="/wiki/Sym-Bionic_Titan" title="Sym-Bionic Titan">Sym-Bionic Titan</a></i>, aired between 2010 and 2011. He had hoped to expand on the initial 20 episodes but it was not renewed beyond its first season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tag_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-tag-17">[17]</a></sup> On April 7, 2011 an animated prologue by Tartakovsky for the horror movie <i><a href="/wiki/Priest_(2011_film)" title="Priest (2011 film)">Priest</a></i> premiered online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mweb_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-mweb-18">[18]</a></sup> In early 2011, Tartakovsky moved to <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Animation" title="Sony Pictures Animation">Sony Pictures Animation</a>, where he made his feature film directing debut with <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Transylvania" title="Hotel Transylvania">Hotel Transylvania</a></i> (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LosAngelesTimes_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-LosAngelesTimes-3">[3]</a></sup> In July 2012, he signed a long-term deal with Sony to develop and direct his own original projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComingSoonSonyDeal_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComingSoonSonyDeal-19">[19]</a></sup> His first original project is currently titled <i>Can You Imagine?</i> and will be produced by Michelle Murdocca. It's described as a "fantastic journey through one boy's imagination".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cartoon_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-cartoon-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2012, Sony Pictures Animation announced that Tartakovsky was slated to direct a computer-animated <i><a href="/wiki/Popeye#Upcoming_film" title="Popeye">Popeye</a></i> feature film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Popeye_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Popeye-21">[21]</a></sup> On September 18, 2014, Tartakovsky revealed an "animation test" footage, about which he said, "It's just something that kind of represents what we want to do. I couldn't be more excited by how it turned out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In March 2015, Tartakovsky announced that despite the well-received test footage, he was no longer working on the project, and would instead direct <i>Can You Imagine?</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a> announced that Tartakovsky will return for a new season of the animation <i>Samurai Jack</i>, which continues on from the original series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In April 2016, <a href="/wiki/Craig_McCracken" title="Craig McCracken">Craig McCracken</a> mentioned on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> that Tartakovsky no longer works for Sony Pictures Animation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
