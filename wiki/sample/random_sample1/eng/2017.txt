<p>The <b>gelada</b> (<i>Theropithecus gelada</i>), sometimes called the <b>bleeding-heart monkey</b> or the <b>gelada baboon</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_monkey" title="Old World monkey">Old World monkey</a> found only in the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a>, with large populations in the <a href="/wiki/Semien_Mountains" title="Semien Mountains">Semien Mountains</a>. <i><b>Theropithecus</b></i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> root words for "beast-<a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">ape</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Like its close relatives the <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboons</a> (genus <i>Papio</i>), it is largely <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a>, spending much of its time foraging in <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny_and_fossils">Phylogeny and fossils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phylogeny and fossils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_(1835)_Theropithecus_gelada.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="610" data-file-width="865" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png/220px-Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png/330px-Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png/440px-Neue_Wirbelthiere_zu_der_Fauna_von_Abyssinien_geh%C3%B6rig_%281835%29_Theropithecus_gelada.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Since 1979, it has been customary to place the gelada in its own genus (<i>Theropithecus</i>), though some genetic research suggests that this <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkey</a> should be grouped with its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papio" title="Papio">papionine</a> (baboon) kin;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodman-5">[5]</a></sup> other researchers have classified the species even farther distant from <i>Papio</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKennaBell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKennaBell-6">[6]</a></sup> While <i>Theropithecus gelada</i> is the only living species of its genus, separate, larger species are known from the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record: <a href="/wiki/Theropithecus_brumpti" title="Theropithecus brumpti"><i>T. brumpti</i></a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Theropithecus_darti" title="Theropithecus darti">T. darti</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-7">[7]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Theropithecus_oswaldi" title="Theropithecus oswaldi">T. oswaldi</a></i>, formerly classified under genus <i>Simopithecus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Theropithecus</i>, while restricted at present to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, is also known from fossil specimens found in Africa and the Mediterranean into Asia, including South Africa, Malawi, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Tanzania, Uganda, Kenya, Ethiopia, Algeria, Morocco, Spain, and India, more exactly at <a href="/wiki/Mirzapur" title="Mirzapur">Mirzapur</a>, Cueva Victoria, Pirro Nord, Ternifine, <a href="/wiki/Hadar,_Ethiopia" title="Hadar, Ethiopia">Hadar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkana_District" title="Turkana District">Turkana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makapansgat" title="Makapansgat">Makapansgat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swartkrans" title="Swartkrans">Swartkrans</a>.</p>
<p>The two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of gelada are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Northern gelada</b>, <i>Theropithecus gelada gelada</i></li>
<li><b>Eastern gelada</b>, <b>southern gelada</b> or <b>Heuglin's gelada</b>, <i>Theropithecus gelada obscurus</i></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_description">Physical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG/220px-BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG/330px-BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG/440px-BabouinGeladaPortrait.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>The gelada is large and robust. It is covered with buff to dark brown, coarse hair and has a dark face with pale eyelids. Its arms and feet are nearly black. Its short tail ends in a tuft of hair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Napier_1981_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Napier_1981-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup> Adult males have a long, heavy cape of hair on their backs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Napier_1981_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Napier_1981-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup> The gelada has a hairless face with a short muzzle that is closer to a chimpanzee's than a baboon's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup> It can also be physically distinguished from a baboon by the bright patch of skin on its chest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Napier_1981_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Napier_1981-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup> This patch is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hourglass-shaped" title="Hourglass-shaped">hourglass-shaped</a>. On males, it is bright red and surrounded by white hair; on females, it is far less pronounced. However, when in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estrus" title="Estrus">estrus</a>, the female's patch will brighten, and a "necklace" of fluid-filled blisters forms on the patch. This is thought to be analogous to the swollen buttocks common to most baboons experiencing estrus. In addition, females have knobs of skin around their patches. Geladas also have well developed <a href="/wiki/Callosity" title="Callosity">ischial callosities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup> There is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in this species: males average 18.5 kg (40.8 lb) while females are smaller, averaging 11 kg (24.3 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jolly_2007_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jolly_2007-11">[11]</a></sup> The head and body length of this species is 50–75 cm (19.7–29.5 in) for both sexes. Tail length is 30–50 cm (11.8–19.7 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankel-Simons_2007_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ankel-Simons_2007-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gelada has several adaptations for its terrestrial and <a href="/wiki/Graminivore" title="Graminivore">graminivorous</a> (grass-eating) lifestyle. It has small, sturdy fingers adapted for pulling grass and narrow, small incisors adapted for chewing it. The gelada has a unique gait, known as the shuffle gait, that it uses when feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup> It squats bipedally and moves by sliding its feet without changing its posture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup> Because of this gait, the gelada's rump is hidden beneath and so unavailable for display; its bright red chest patch is visible, though.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_ecology">Range and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Range and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Geladas are found only in the high grassland of the deep gorges of the central Ethiopian plateau. They live in elevations 1,800–4,400 m above sea level, using the cliffs for sleeping and montane grasslands for foraging. These grasslands have widely spaced trees and also contain bushes and dense thickets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Napier_1981_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Napier_1981-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iwamoto_1983_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iwamoto_1983-13">[13]</a></sup> The highland areas where they live tend to be cooler and less arid than lowlands areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iwamoto_1983_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iwamoto_1983-13">[13]</a></sup> Thus, the geladas usually do not experience the negative effects that the dry season has on food availability. Nevertheless, in some areas, they do experience frost in the dry season, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hailstorms</a> in the wet season.</p>
<p>Geladas are the only primates that are primarily graminivores and <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazers</a> – grass blades make up to 90% of their diet. They eat both the blades and the seeds of grasses. When both blades and seeds are available, geladas prefer the seeds. They also eat flowers, rhizomes and roots when available,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iwamoto_1983_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Iwamoto_1983-13">[13]</a></sup> using their hands to dig for the latter two. They also consume herbs, small plants, fruits, creepers, bushes and thistles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iwamoto_1983_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Iwamoto_1983-13">[13]</a></sup> Insects can be eaten, but only rarely and only if they can easily be obtained. During the dry season, grasses are eaten less and herbs are preferred. Geladas consume their food more like ungulates than primates, and can chew their food as effectively as <a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">zebra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geladas are primarily <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>. At night, they sleep on the ledges of cliffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crook_1966_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crook_1966-15">[15]</a></sup> At sunrise, they leave the cliffs and travel to the tops of the plateaux to feed and socialize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup> When morning ends, social activities tend to wane and the geladas primarily focus on foraging. They will travel during this time, as well. When evening arrives, geladas exhibit more social activities before descending to the cliffs to sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1977_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1977-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_structure">Social structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gelada_group.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Gelada_group.jpg/220px-Gelada_group.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Gelada_group.jpg/330px-Gelada_group.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Gelada_group.jpg/440px-Gelada_group.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gelada_group.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gelada reproductive unit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Geladas live in a complex multilevel society similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Hamadryas_baboon" title="Hamadryas baboon">hamadryas baboon</a>. The smallest and most basic groups are the reproductive units, which are made up of one to twelve females, their young and one to four males, and the all-male units, which are made up of two to fifteen males. The next level of gelada societies are the bands, which are made up of two to twenty-seven reproductive units and several all-male units. Herds consist of up to 60 reproductive units that are sometimes from different bands and last for short periods of time. Communities are made of one to four bands whose home ranges overlap extensively. A gelada can typically live to around 20 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crook_1966_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crook_1966-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1986_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1986-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zinner_2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zinner_2004-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the reproductive units, the females tend to be closely related and have strong social bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1986_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1986-16">[16]</a></sup> Reproductive units split up if they get too large. While females have strong social bonds in the group, a female will only interact with at most three other members of her unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1986_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1986-16">[16]</a></sup> Grooming and other social interactions among females usually occur between pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup> Females in a reproductive unit exist in a hierarchy, with higher-ranking females have more reproductive success and more offspring than lower-ranking females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1980_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1980-19">[19]</a></sup> Closely related females tend to have a similar hierarchical status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1980_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1980-19">[19]</a></sup> Females generally stay in their natal units for life; cases of females leaving are rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohsawa_1979_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ohsawa_1979-20">[20]</a></sup> Aggression within a reproduction unit, which is rare, is usually just between the females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup> Aggression is more frequent between members of different reproductive units and is usually started by females, but males and females from both sides will join in if the conflict escalates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:D%C5%BEelady.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2190" data-file-width="3456" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/D%C5%BEelady.jpg/220px-D%C5%BEelady.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/D%C5%BEelady.jpg/330px-D%C5%BEelady.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/D%C5%BEelady.jpg/440px-D%C5%BEelady.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:D%C5%BEelady.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Male grooming a female</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Males can remain in a reproductive unit for four to five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1986_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1986-16">[16]</a></sup> While geladas have traditionally been considered to have a male-transfer society, many males appear to be likely to return and breed in their natal bands. Nevertheless, gelada males leave their natal units and try to take over a unit of their own. A male can take over a reproductive unit either through direct aggression and fighting or by joining one as a subordinate and taking some females with him to create a new unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1986_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1986-16">[16]</a></sup> When more than one male is in a unit, only one of them can mate with the females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohsawa_1979_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ohsawa_1979-20">[20]</a></sup> The females in the group together can have power over the dominant male. When a new male tries to take over a unit and overthrow the resident male, the females can choose to support or oppose him. The male maintains his relationship with the females by grooming them rather than forcing his dominance, in contrast to the society of the hamadryas baboon. Females accept a male into the unit by presenting themselves to him. Not all the females may interact with the male. Usually, one may serve as his main partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-male-female_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-male-female-21">[21]</a></sup> The male may sometimes be monopolized by this female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-male-female_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-male-female-21">[21]</a></sup> The male may try to interact with the other females, but they are usually unresponsive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-male-female_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-male-female-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most all-male units consist of several subadults and one young adult, led by one male. A member of an all-male unit may spend two to four years in the group before attempting to join a reproductive unit. All-male groups are generally aggressive towards both reproductive units and other all-male units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup> As in reproductive units, aggression within all-male units is rare. As bands, reproductive units exist in a common home range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1984_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1984-22">[22]</a></sup> Within the band, members are closely related and between the units there is no social hierarchy. Bands usually break apart every eight to nine years as a new band forms in a new home range.</p>
<p>Researchers from the University of the Free State (UFS) in South Africa, while observing gelada monkeys during field studies, discovered that the monkeys were capable of 'cheating' on their partners and covering up their 'infidelity'. A non-dominant male would mate surreptitiously with a female, suppressing their normal mating cries so as not to be overheard. If discovered, the dominant male would attack the miscreants in a clear form of punishment. It is the first time that evidence of the knowledge of cheating and fear of discovery has been recorded among animals in the wild. Dr Aliza le Roux of the Department of Zoology and Entomology at the university believes that dishonesty and punishment are not uniquely human traits, and that the observed evidence of this behaviour among gelada monkeys suggests that the roots of the human system of deceit, crime and punishment lie very deep indeed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Mixed-species association was observed between solitary <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_wolf" title="Ethiopian wolf">Ethiopian wolves</a> and geladas. According to the study's findings, gelada monkeys would not typically move on encountering Ethiopian wolves, even when they were in the middle of the herd—68 percent of encounters resulted in no movement and only 11 percent resulted in a movement of greater than 10 meters. In stark contrast, the geladas always fled great distances to the cliffs for safety whenever they encountered aggressive domestic dogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>When in estrus, the female points her posterior towards a male and raises it, moving her tail to one side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_1975_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein_1975-25">[25]</a></sup> The male then approaches the female and inspects her chest and genital areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_1975_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein_1975-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup> A female will copulate up to five times per day, usually around midday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup> Breeding and reproduction can occur at any time of the year, although some areas have birth peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jolly_2007_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jolly_2007-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_2002_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_2002-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2303" data-file-width="1663" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG/220px-BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG/330px-BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG/440px-BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BabouinGeladaAuReveil.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gelada displaying its teeth and gums with its lip flipped back</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most births occur at night. Newborn infants have red faces and closed eyes, and are covered in black hair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup> On average, newborn infants weigh 464 g.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Females that have just given birth stay on the periphery of the reproductive unit. Other adult females may take an interest in the infants and even kidnap them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup> An infant is carried on its mother’s belly for the first five weeks, and thereafter on her back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrett_1995_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrett_1995-29">[29]</a></sup> Infants can move independently at around five months old. A subordinate male in a reproductive unit may help care for an infant when it is six months old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-26">[26]</a></sup> When herds form, juveniles and infants may gather into play groups of around 10 individuals. When males reach puberty, they gather into unstable groups independent of the reproductive units. Females sexually mature at around three years, but do not give birth for another year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1975_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1975-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunbar_1984_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbar_1984-22">[22]</a></sup> Males reach puberty at around four or five years, but are usually unable to reproduce because of social constraints and have to wait until they are around eight to 10 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jolly_2007_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jolly_2007-11">[11]</a></sup> Average life span in the wild is 19 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication">Communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adult geladas use a diverse repertoire of vocalizations for various purposes, such as: contact, reassurance, appeasement, solicitation, ambivalence, aggression and defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kawai_1979_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kawai_1979-31">[31]</a></sup> The level of complexity of these vocalizations is thought to near that of humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> They sit around and chatter at each other, signifying to those around that they matter, in a way, to the individual "speaking". To some extent, calls are related to the status of an individual. In addition, females have calls signaling their estrus. Geladas communicate through gestures, as well. They display threats by flipping their upper lips back on their nostrils to display their teeth and gums, and by pulling back their scalps to display the pale eyelids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unit2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-unit2-33">[33]</a></sup> A gelada submits by fleeing or presenting itself.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> assessed the gelada as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>, although their population had reduced from an estimated 440,000 in the 1970s to around 200,000 in 2008. It is listed in Appendix II of <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> Major threats to the gelada are a reduction of their range as a result of agricultural expansion, and shooting as crop pests. However, threats that once existed but no longer do are trapping for use as laboratory animals and shooting to obtain their capes to make items of clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> As of 2008, proposals have been made for a new Blue Nile Gorges National Park and Indeltu (Shebelle) Gorges Reserve to protect larger numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
