<p>The <b>Winter Hexagon</b> or <b>Winter Circle/Oval</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy)" title="Asterism (astronomy)">asterism</a> appearing to be in the form of a hexagon with <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rigel" title="Rigel">Rigel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldebaran" title="Aldebaran">Aldebaran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capella" title="Capella">Capella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollux_(star)" title="Pollux (star)">Pollux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procyon" title="Procyon">Procyon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a>. It is mostly upon the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>. On most locations on Earth (except the <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the south of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and further south), this asterism is prominently in the sky from approximately December to March. In the tropics and southern hemisphere, this (then called "summer hexagon") can be extended with the bright star <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a> in the south.</p>
<p>Smaller and more regularly shaped is the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Triangle" title="Winter Triangle">Winter Triangle</a> (also known as the <b>Great Southern Triangle</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), an approximately <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">equilateral triangle</a> that shares two vertices (Sirius and Procyon) with the larger asterism. The third vertex is <a href="/wiki/Betelgeuse" title="Betelgeuse">Betelgeuse</a>, which lies near the center of the hexagon. These three stars are three of the ten <a href="/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars" title="List of brightest stars">brightest objects</a>, as viewed from Earth, outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. Betelgeuse is also particularly easy to locate, being a shoulder of <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>, which assists stargazers in finding the triangle. Once the triangle is located, the larger hexagon may then be found.</p>
<p>Several of the stars in the hexagon may also be found independently of one another by following various lines traced through various stars in <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>.</p>
<p>The stars in the hexagon are parts of six constellations. Counter-clockwise around the hexagon, starting with Rigel, these are <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">Taurus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auriga_(constellation)" title="Auriga (constellation)">Auriga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(constellation)" title="Gemini (constellation)">Gemini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canis_Minor" title="Canis Minor">Canis Minor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canis_Major" title="Canis Major">Canis Major</a>.</p>
