<p><b>.kp</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_code" title="Country code">country code</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a>) of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>. It was created on 24 September 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=.kp&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there are 28 subdomains registered under .kp domains.</p>

<p>Previously, the .kp domain was managed by <a href="/wiki/Korea_Computer_Center" title="Korea Computer Center">KCC Europe</a>. A large number of .kp websites were also hosted by KCC Europe in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. However, as of 2012, management has been transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>-based <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Star_Joint_Venture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Star Joint Venture (page does not exist)">Star Joint Venture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some .kp addresses are used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Korean_Intranet" title="North Korean Intranet">North Korean Intranet</a> only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_in_North_Korea">Internet in North Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.kp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Internet in North Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Internet_in_North_Korea" title="Internet in North Korea">Internet in North Korea</a></div>
<p>Access to the internet in North Korea remains rare and tightly restricted; <a href="/wiki/Reporters_Without_Borders" title="Reporters Without Borders">Reporters Without Borders</a> once described the nation as "the world’s worst Internet black hole"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as the "Internet officially does not exist in the world’s most isolated country, but a handful of privileged people are allowed to go online through the <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">phone system</a> (which is routed through China) or via <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leader_of_North_Korea" title="Leader of North Korea">leader</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-Il" title="Kim Jong-Il">Kim Jong-Il</a>, "I'm an Internet expert too. It's all right to wire the industrial zone only, but there are many problems if other regions of the North are wired." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 October 2010, in conjunction with its 65th anniversary of independence, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPRK" title="DPRK">DPRK</a> made a block of <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> available for use within the country. Hosting of sites and general access was provided by <a href="/wiki/China_Netcom" title="China Netcom">China Netcom</a>. This allowed for Net access to journalists reporting on the anniversary celebrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of February 2013, foreigners can access the internet using a <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a> phone network installed by <a href="/wiki/Optimum_Telecom" title="Optimum Telecom">Orascom</a>, but access to this network for North Korean citizens is limited to phone calls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-20130222_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-20130222-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp-20130226_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-wp-20130226-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_of_North_Korean_websites">Censorship of North Korean websites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.kp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Censorship of North Korean websites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_North_Korean_websites_banned_in_South_Korea" title="List of North Korean websites banned in South Korea">List of North Korean websites banned in South Korea</a></div>
<p>South Korea has banned at least 31 sites considered sympathetic to North Korea through the use of IP blocking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
