<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Grid cell</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>grid cell</b> is a type of neuron in the brains of many species that allows them to understand their position in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fyhn2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fyhn2008-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yartsev2011_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yartsev2011-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Killian2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Killian2012-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedAndKahana2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FriedAndKahana2013-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="641" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png/150px-Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png/225px-Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png/300px-Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uniform_tiling_63-t2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grid cells derive their name from the fact that connecting the centers of their firing fields gives a triangular grid.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Grid cells were discovered in 2005 by <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Moser" title="Edvard Moser">Edvard Moser</a>, <a href="/wiki/May-Britt_Moser" title="May-Britt Moser">May-Britt Moser</a> and their students Torkel Hafting, Marianne Fyhn and Sturla Molden at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kavli_Institute_for_Systems_Neuroscience_and_Centre_for_the_Biology_of_Memory" title="Kavli Institute for Systems Neuroscience and Centre for the Biology of Memory">Centre for the Biology of Memory</a> (CBM) in Norway. They were awarded the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> together with <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Keefe_(neuroscientist)" title="John O'Keefe (neuroscientist)">John O'Keefe</a> for their discoveries of cells that constitute a positioning system in the brain. The arrangement of spatial firing fields all at equal distances from their neighbors led to a hypothesis that these cells encode a cognitive representation of <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> The discovery also suggested a mechanism for dynamic computation of self-position based on continuously updated information about position and direction.</p>
<p>In a typical experimental study, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_unit_recording" title="Single unit recording">electrode</a> capable of recording the activity of an individual <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neuron</a> is implanted in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a> of a rat, in a section called the dorsomedial <a href="/wiki/Entorhinal_cortex" title="Entorhinal cortex">entorhinal cortex</a>, and recordings are made as the rat moves around freely in an open arena. For a grid cell, if a dot is placed at the location of the rat's head every time the neuron emits an <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>, then as illustrated in the adjoining figure, these dots build up over time to form a set of small clusters, and the clusters form the vertices of a grid of equilateral triangles. This regular triangle-pattern is what distinguishes grid cells from other types of cells that show spatial firing. By contrast, if a <a href="/wiki/Place_cell" title="Place cell">place cell</a> from the rat hippocampus is examined in the same way (i.e., by placing a dot at the location of the rat's head whenever the cell emits an action potential), then the dots build up to form small clusters, but frequently there is only one cluster (one "place field") in a given environment, and even when multiple clusters are seen, there is no perceptible regularity in their arrangement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1971, <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Keefe_(neuroscientist)" title="John O'Keefe (neuroscientist)">John O'Keefe</a> and Jonathon Dostrovsky reported the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Place_cell" title="Place cell">place cells</a> in the rat <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> — cells that fire action potentials when an animal passes through a specific small region of space, which is called the <i>place field</i> of the cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This discovery, although controversial at first, led to a series of investigations that culminated in the 1978 publication of a book by O'Keefe and his colleague <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Nadel" title="Lynn Nadel">Lynn Nadel</a> called <i>The Hippocampus as a Cognitive Map</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> — the book argued that the hippocampal neural network instantiates <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_map" title="Cognitive map">cognitive maps</a> as hypothesized by the psychologist <a href="/wiki/Edward_C._Tolman" title="Edward C. Tolman">Edward C. Tolman</a>. This theory aroused a great deal of interest, and motivated hundreds of experimental studies aimed at clarifying the role of the hippocampus in spatial memory and spatial navigation.</p>
<p>Because the entorhinal cortex provides by far the largest input to the hippocampus, it was clearly important to understand the spatial firing properties of entorhinal neurons. The earliest studies, such as Quirk <i>et al.</i> (1992), described neurons in the entorhinal cortex as having relatively large and fuzzy place fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Mosers, however, thought it was possible that a different result would be obtained if recordings were made from a different part of the entorhinal cortex. The entorhinal cortex is a strip of tissue running along the back edge of the rat brain from the ventral to the dorsal sides. Anatomical studies had shown that different sectors of the entorhinal cortex project to different levels of the hippocampus: the dorsal end of the EC projects to the dorsal hippocampus, the ventral end to the ventral hippocampus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This was relevant because several studies had shown that place cells in the dorsal hippocampus have considerably sharper place fields than cells from more ventral levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maurer2005_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maurer2005-11">[11]</a></sup> Every study of entorhinal spatial activity prior to 2004, however, had made use of electrodes implanted near the ventral end of the EC. Accordingly, together with Marianne Fyhn, Sturla Molden and Menno Witter, the Mosers set out to examine spatial firing from the different dorsal-to-ventral levels of the entorhinal cortex. They found that in the dorsal part of medial entorhinal cortex (MEC), cells had sharply defined place fields like in the hippocampus but the cells fired at multiple locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fyhn2004_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fyhn2004-12">[12]</a></sup> The arrangement of the firing fields showed hints of regularity, but the size of the environment was too small for spatial periodicity to be visible in this study.</p>
<p>The next set of experiments, reported in 2005, made use of a larger environment, which led to the recognition that the cells were actually firing in a hexagonal grid pattern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> The study showed that cells at similar dorsal-to-ventral MEC levels had similar grid spacing and grid orientation but the phase of the grid (the offset of the grid vertices relative to the x and y axes) appeared to be randomly distributed between cells. The periodic firing pattern was expressed independently of the configuration of landmarks, in darkness as well as in the presence of visible landmarks and independently of changes in the animal’s speed and direction, leading the authors to suggest that grid cells expressed a path-integration dependent dynamic computation of the animal’s location.</p>
<p>In 2014 John O'Keefe, May-Britt Moser, and Edvard Moser were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> for their discoveries of grid cells.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="560" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG/200px-Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG/300px-Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG/400px-Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Autocorrelationplot_grid_cell.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spatial <a href="/wiki/Autocorrelation" title="Autocorrelation">autocorrelogram</a> of the neuronal activity of the grid cell from the first figure.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Grid cells are neurons that fire when a freely moving animal traverses a set of small regions (firing fields) which are roughly equal in size and arranged in a periodic triangular array that covers the entire available environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> Cells with this firing pattern have been found in all layers of the dorsocaudal medial <a href="/wiki/Entorhinal_cortex" title="Entorhinal cortex">entorhinal cortex</a> (dMEC), but cells in different layers tend to differ in other respects. Layer II contains the largest density of pure grid cells, in the sense that they fire equally regardless of the direction in which an animal traverses a grid location. Grid cells from deeper layers are intermingled with conjunctive cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Head_direction_cells" title="Head direction cells">head direction cells</a> (i.e. in layers III, V and VI there are cells with a grid-like pattern that fire only when the animal is facing a particular direction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sargolini2006_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sargolini2006-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grid cells that lie next to one another (i.e., cells recorded from the same electrode) usually show the same grid spacing and orientation, but their grid vertices are displaced from one another by apparently random offsets. Cells recorded from separate electrodes at a distance from one another, however, frequently show different grid spacings. Cells that are located more ventrally (that is, farther from the dorsal border of the MEC) generally have larger firing fields at each grid vertex, and correspondingly greater spacing between the grid vertices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> The total range of grid spacings is not well established: the initial report described a roughly twofold range of grid spacings (from 39 cm to 73 cm) across the dorsalmost part (upper 25%) of the MEC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> but there are indications of considerably larger grid scales in more ventral zones. Brun <i>et al.</i> (2008) recorded grid cells from multiple levels in rats running along an 18-meter track, and found that the grid spacing expanded from about 25 cm in their dorsalmost sites to about 3 m at the ventralmost sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brun2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brun2008-15">[15]</a></sup> These recordings only extended 3/4 of the way to the ventral tip, so it is possible that even larger grids exist. Interestingly, such multi-scale representations have been shown to be information theoretically desirable .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mathis2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mathis2012-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grid cell activity does not require visual input, since grid patterns remain unchanged when all the lights in an environment are turned off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> When visual cues are present, however, they exert strong control over the alignment of the grids: Rotating a cue card on the wall of a cylinder causes grid patterns to rotate by the same amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> Grid patterns appear on the first entrance of an animal into a novel environment, and usually remain stable thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> When an animal is moved into a completely different environment, grid cells maintain their grid spacing, and the grids of neighboring cells maintain their relative offsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions_with_hippocampal_place_cells">Interactions with hippocampal place cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interactions with hippocampal place cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When a rat is moved to a different environment, the spatial activity patterns of hippocampal place cells usually show <i>"complete remapping"</i> — that is, the pattern of place fields reorganizes in a way that bears no detectable resemblance to the pattern in the original environment (Muller and Kubie, 1987).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> If the features of an environment are altered less radically, however, the place field pattern may show a lesser degree of change, referred to as <i>"rate remapping"</i>, in which many cells alter their firing rates but the majority of cells retain place fields in the same locations as before. Fyhn <i>et al.</i> (2007) examined this phenomenon using simultaneous recordings of hippocampal and entorhinal cells, and found that in situations where the hippocampus shows rate remapping, grid cells show unaltered firing patterns, whereas when the hippocampus shows complete remapping, grid cell firing patterns show unpredictable shifts and rotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fyhn2007_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fyhn2007-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theta_rhythmicity">Theta rhythmicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theta rhythmicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neural activity in nearly every part of the hippocampal system is modulated by the limbic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_rhythm" title="Theta rhythm">theta rhythm</a>, which has a frequency range of about 6–9 Hz in rats. The entorhinal cortex is no exception: like the hippocampus, it receives <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">cholinergic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GABA" title="GABA">GABAergic</a> input from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medial_septum" title="Medial septum">medial septal area</a>, the central controller of theta. Grid cells, like hippocampal place cells, show strong theta modulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup> Grid cells from layer II of the MEC also resemble hippocampal place cells in that they show <a href="/wiki/Place_cell#Phase_precession" title="Place cell">phase precession</a>—that is, their spike activity advances from late to early phases of the theta cycle as an animal passes through a grid vertex. Most grid cells from layer III do not precess, but their spike activity is largely confined to half of the theta cycle. The grid cell phase precession is not derived from the hippocampus, because it continues to appear in animals whose hippocampus has been inactivated by an agonist of GABA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2008_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2008-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_functions">Possible functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_cell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Possible functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many species of mammals can keep track of spatial location even in the absence of visual, auditory, olfactory, or tactile cues, by integrating their movements—the ability to do this is referred to in the literature as <a href="/wiki/Path_integration" title="Path integration">path integration</a>. A number of theoretical models have explored mechanisms by which path integration could be performed by neural networks. In most models, such as those of Samsonovich and McNaughton (1997)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> or Burak and Fiete (2009),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burak2009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burak2009-21">[21]</a></sup> the principal ingredients are (1) an internal representation of position, (2) internal representations of the speed and direction of movement, and (3) a mechanism for shifting the encoded position by the right amount when the animal moves. Because cells in the MEC encode information about position (grid cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafting2005_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Hafting2005-1">[1]</a></sup>) and movement (head direction cells and conjunctive position-by-direction cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sargolini2006_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sargolini2006-14">[14]</a></sup>), this area is currently viewed as the most promising candidate for the place in the brain where path integration occurs. However, the question remains unresolved, as in humans the entorhinal cortex does not appear to be required for path integration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Burak and Fiete (2009) showed that a computational simulation of the grid cell system was capable of performing path integration to a high level of accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burak2009_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burak2009-21">[21]</a></sup> However, more recent theoretical work has suggested that grid cells might perform a more general denoising process not necessarily related to spatial processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hafting et al. (2005) suggested that a place code is computed in the entorhinal cortex and fed into the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>, which may make the associations between place and events that are needed for the formation of memories.</p>
<p>In contrast to a hippocampal <a href="/wiki/Place_cell" title="Place cell">place cell</a>, a grid cell has multiple firing fields, with regular spacing, which tessellate the environment in a hexagonal pattern. The unique properties of grid cells are as follows:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="220" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg/250px-Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg/375px-Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg/500px-Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Equilateral_Triangle_Lattice.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A hexagonal lattice.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ol>
<li>Grid cells have firing fields dispersed over the entire environment (in contrast to place fields which are restricted to certain specific regions of the environment)</li>
<li>The firing fields are organized into a <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_lattice" title="Hexagonal lattice">hexagonal lattice</a></li>
<li>Firing fields are generally equally spaced apart, such that the distance from one firing field to all six adjacent firing fields is approximately the same (though when an environment is resized, the field spacing may shrink or expand differently in different directions; Barry et al. 2007)</li>
<li>Firing fields are equally positioned, such that the six neighboring fields are located at approximately 60 degree increments</li>
</ol>
<p>The grid cells are anchored to external landmarks, but persist in darkness, suggesting that grid cells may be part of a self-motion based map of the spatial environment.</p>
