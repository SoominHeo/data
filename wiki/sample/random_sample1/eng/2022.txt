<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cynicism (contemporary)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"Cynical" redirects here. For the classical philosophy, see <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynicism (philosophy)</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cynicism_(disambiguation)" title="Cynicism (disambiguation)">Cynic</a>.</div>
<p><b>Cynicism</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitude</a> or state of mind characterized by a general distrust of others' motives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A cynic may have a general lack of <a href="/wiki/Faith" title="Faith">faith</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hope" title="Hope">hope</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> species or people motivated by ambition, desire, <a href="/wiki/Greed" title="Greed">greed</a>, gratification, <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialism</a>, goals, and opinions that a cynic perceives as vain, unobtainable, or ultimately meaningless and therefore deserving of ridicule or admonishment. A common misapplication of this attitude involves its attribution to individuals who emote well-thought-out expressions of <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a>. Such miscategorization may occur as the result of either inexperience and/or a belief system in which the innate goodness of man is considered an important tenet or even an irrefutable fact. Thus, contemporary usage incorporates both a form of jaded prudence and (when misapplied) realistic <a href="/wiki/Criticism" title="Criticism">criticism</a> or skepticism. The term originally derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophers" title="Ancient Greek philosophers">ancient Greek philosophers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynics</a>, who rejected all conventions, whether of <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, manners, housing, dress, or decency, instead advocating the pursuit of <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtue</a> in accordance with a simple and idealistic way of life.</p>
<p>By the 19th century, emphasis on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascetic" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> ideals and the critique of current civilization based on how it might fall short of an ideal civilization or <a href="/wiki/Antipositivism" title="Antipositivism">negativistic</a> aspects of <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynic philosophy</a> led the modern understanding of cynicism to mean a disposition of disbelief in the sincerity or goodness of human motives and actions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Modern cynicism is a distrust toward professed <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical</a> and social values, especially when there are high expectations concerning <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>, institutions, and authorities that are unfulfilled. It can manifest itself as a result of frustration, disillusionment, and distrust perceived as owing to organizations, authorities, and other aspects of society.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern cynicism has been defined as an attitude of distrust toward claimed ethical and social values and a rejection of the need to be socially involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is pessimistic in regards to the capacity of human beings to make correct ethical choices, and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonym" title="Antonym">antonym</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naivet%C3%A9" title="Naiveté">naiveté</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encarta_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encarta-3">[3]</a></sup> Modern cynicism is sometimes regarded as a product of <a href="/wiki/Mass_society" title="Mass society">mass society</a>, especially in those circumstances where the individual believes there is a conflict between society's stated motives and goals and actual motives and goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_politics">In politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It has been claimed that "healthy skepticism may have given way to corrosive cynicism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Cynicism regarding government or politics can logically lead to political withdrawal and effective political helplessness. In 2013 conservative politician and political theorist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_J._Bennett" title="William J. Bennett">William J. Bennett</a> warned that America could "crumble from within; that we would become cynical and withdraw."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_effects">Possible effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Possible effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2004 experiment and paper called <i>The Effects of Strategic News on Political Cynicism, Issue Evaluations, and Policy Support: A Two-Wave Experiment</i> found that the way the news media presents the news can cause political cynicism. The experiment also demonstrated; "a negative relation between efficacy and cynicism suggesting that efficacious citizens were less likely to be cynical about politics." It was found that straight dry, "issues-based" news did not cause political cynicism, but that "Strategic News" and "game news" did. The latter two types of news presentation emphasize:</p>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">...the horse race, strategy, and tactics of politics,"..."news coverage of candidate motivations and personalities, focus on disagreement between parties, candidates or voters, and the presence and emphasis on polls in the news,<i>" or "</i>positioning the electorate as spectators and candidates as performers," respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></td>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_cynicism">Social cynicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social cynicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Social cynicism results from excessively high expectations concerning society, institutions and authorities: unfulfilled expectations leads to disillusionment, which releases feelings of disappointment and <a href="/wiki/Betrayal" title="Betrayal">betrayal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In organizations, cynicism manifests itself as a general or specific attitude, characterized by frustration, hopelessness, disillusionment and distrust in regard to economic or governmental organizations, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">managers</a> and/or other aspects of work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_evaluation">Critical evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cynicism can appear more active than <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>. In his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bestselling" title="Bestselling">bestselling</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Critique_of_Cynical_Reason" title="Critique of Cynical Reason">Critique of Cynical Reason</a></i> (1983), <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sloterdijk" title="Peter Sloterdijk">Peter Sloterdijk</a> defined modern cynics as "borderline melancholics, who can keep their symptoms of depression under control and yet retain the ability to work, whatever might happen ... indeed, this is the essential point in modern cynicism: the ability of its bearers to work—in spite of anything that might happen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>One active aspect of cynicism involves the desire to expose hypocrisy and to point out gaps between <a href="/wiki/Ideal_(ethics)" title="Ideal (ethics)">ideals</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Practice_(social_theory)" title="Practice (social theory)">practices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a> allegedly expressed this succinctly: "The power of accurate observation is commonly called cynicism by those who don't have it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cynicism_(contemporary)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> published in <i><a href="/wiki/Neurology_(journal)" title="Neurology (journal)">Neurology</a></i> journal in 2014 found a link between "cynical distrust" (defined as the belief that others are mainly motivated by selfish concerns) and <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>. The survey included 622 people who were tested for dementia for a period of 8 years. In that period, 46 people were diagnosed with dementia. "Once researchers adjusted for other factors that could affect dementia risk, such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol and smoking, people with high levels of cynical distrust were three times more likely to develop dementia than people with low levels of cynicism. Of the 164 people with high levels of cynicism, 14 people developed dementia, compared to nine of the 212 people with low levels of cynicism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
