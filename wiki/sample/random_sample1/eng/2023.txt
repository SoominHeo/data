<p>The <b>large intestine</b>, or the <b>large bowel</b>, is the last part of the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a> and of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>. Water is absorbed here and the remaining waste material is stored as <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> before being removed by <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCILargeIntestineDef_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCILargeIntestineDef-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most sources define the large intestine as the combination of the <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a>, <b>colon</b>, <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kapoor2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kapoor2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayLargeIntestine_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrayLargeIntestine-3">[3]</a></sup> Some other sources exclude the anal canal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In humans, the large intestine begins in the right <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iliac_region" title="Iliac region">iliac region</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a>, just at or below the <a href="/wiki/Waist" title="Waist">waist</a>, where it is joined to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> at the cecum, via the <a href="/wiki/Ileocecal_valve" title="Ileocecal valve">ileocecal valve</a>. It then continues as the colon <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending</a> the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>, across the width of the <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_cavity" title="Abdominal cavity">abdominal cavity</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a>, and then <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> and its endpoint at the <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Overall, in humans, the large intestine is about 1.5 metres (5 ft) long, which is about one-fifth of the whole length of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">Digestion</a></div>

<p>The colon is the last part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a>. It extracts <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> from <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">solid wastes</a> before they are <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">eliminated</a> from the body and is the site in which <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">flora-aided</a> (largely bacterial) fermentation of unabsorbed material occurs. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a>, the colon does not play a major role in absorption of foods and nutrients. About 1.5 litres or 45 ounces of water arrives in the colon each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krogh2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krogh2010-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The length of the adult human male colon is 166cm (range of 80 to 313cm), on average, for females it is 155 cm (range of 80 to 214 cm). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hounnou_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hounnou-10">[10]</a></sup> The average inner circumference of sections of the colon in centimeters (with ranges in parentheses) are cecum 8.7 (8.0-10.5), ascending colon 6.6 (6.0-7.0), transverse colon 5.8 (5.0-6.5), descending/sigmoid colon 6.3 (6.0-6.8) and rectum near rectal/sigmoid junction 5.7 (4.5-7.5).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, the colon consists of five sections: the <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending colon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid colon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCILargeIntestineDef_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCILargeIntestineDef-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sections of the colon are:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending colon</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Colic_flexures" title="Colic flexures">colic flexures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transverse_mesocolon" title="Transverse mesocolon">transverse mesocolon</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid colon</a> – the s-shaped region of the large intestine</li>
</ul>
<p>The parts of the colon are either intraperitoneal or behind it in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroperitoneum" title="Retroperitoneum">retroperitoneum</a>. Retroperitoneal organs in general do not have a complete covering of <a href="/wiki/Peritoneum" title="Peritoneum">peritoneum</a>, so they are fixed in location. Intraperitoneal organs are completely surrounded by peritoneum and are therefore mobile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Of the colon, the ascending colon, descending colon and rectum are retroperitoneal, while the cecum, appendix, transverse colon and sigmoid colon are intraperitoneal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This is important as it affects which organs can be easily accessed during surgery, such as a <a href="/wiki/Laparotomy" title="Laparotomy">laparotomy</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cecum_and_appendix">Cecum and appendix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cecum and appendix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a> is the first section of the colon and involved in the digestion, while the <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a> which develops embryologically from it, is a structure of the colon, not involved in digestion and considered to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Gut-associated_lymphoid_tissue" title="Gut-associated lymphoid tissue">gut-associated lymphoid tissue</a>. The function of the appendix is uncertain, but some sources believe that the appendix has a role in housing a sample of the colon's microflora, and is able to help to repopulate the colon with bacteria if the microflora has been damaged during the course of an immune reaction.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascending_colon">Ascending colon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ascending colon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ascending colon is the first of four sections of the large intestine. It is connected to the small intestine by a section of bowel called the cecum. The ascending colon runs upwards through the abdominal cavity toward the transverse colon for approximately eight inches (20 cm).</p>
<p>One of the main functions of the colon is to remove the water and other key nutrients from waste material and recycle it. As the waste material exits the small intestine through the <a href="/wiki/Ileocecal_valve" title="Ileocecal valve">ileocecal valve</a>, it will move into the cecum and then to the ascending colon where this process of extraction starts. The unwanted waste material is moved upwards toward the transverse colon by the action of <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>. The ascending colon is sometimes attached to the <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a> via <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Gerlach" title="Joseph von Gerlach">Gerlach's valve</a>. The appendix, traditionally seen as a vestigial organ, has been shown to have a high concentration of lymphatic cells. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruminants" title="Ruminants">ruminants</a>, the ascending colon is known as the <b>spiral colon</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transverse_colon">Transverse colon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transverse colon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The transverse colon is the part of the colon from the hepatic flexure to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Splenic_flexure" title="Splenic flexure">splenic flexure</a> (the turn of the colon by the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>). The transverse colon hangs off the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, attached to it by a large fold of <a href="/wiki/Peritoneum" title="Peritoneum">peritoneum</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Greater_omentum" title="Greater omentum">greater omentum</a>. On the posterior side, the transverse colon is connected to the posterior abdominal wall by a <a href="/wiki/Mesentery" title="Mesentery">mesentery</a> known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transverse_mesocolon" title="Transverse mesocolon">transverse mesocolon</a>.</p>
<p>The transverse colon is encased in <a href="/wiki/Peritoneum" title="Peritoneum">peritoneum</a>, and is therefore mobile (unlike the parts of the colon immediately before and after it). <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">Cancers</a> form more frequently further along the large intestine as the contents become more solid (water is removed) in order to form <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="this would imply that the more solid feces are the more probable is cancer growth, we really need a citation for this (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The proximal two-thirds of the transverse colon is perfused by the <a href="/wiki/Middle_colic_artery" title="Middle colic artery">middle colic artery</a>, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery" title="Superior mesenteric artery">superior mesenteric artery</a> (SMA), while the latter third is supplied by branches of the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_artery" title="Inferior mesenteric artery">inferior mesenteric artery</a> (IMA). The "watershed" area between these two blood supplies, which represents the embryologic division between the <a href="/wiki/Midgut" title="Midgut">midgut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindgut" title="Hindgut">hindgut</a>, is an area sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemia</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Descending_colon">Descending colon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Descending colon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The descending colon is the part of the colon from the splenic flexure to the beginning of the sigmoid colon. One function of the descending colon in the digestive system is to store feces that will be emptied into the rectum. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroperitoneal" title="Retroperitoneal">retroperitoneal</a> in two-thirds of humans. In the other third, it has a (usually short) mesentery. The arterial supply comes via the <a href="/wiki/Left_colic_artery" title="Left colic artery">left colic artery</a>. The descending colon is also called the <i>distal gut</i>, as it is further along the gastrointestinal tract than the proximal gut. Gut flora are very dense in this region.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sigmoid_colon">Sigmoid colon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sigmoid colon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <b>sigmoid colon</b> is the part of the large intestine after the descending colon and before the rectum. The name <i>sigmoid</i> means S-shaped (see <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sigmoid" title="wiktionary:sigmoid">sigmoid</a>; cf. <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_sinus" title="Sigmoid sinus">sigmoid sinus</a>). The walls of the sigmoid colon are muscular, and contract to increase the pressure inside the colon, causing the <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">stool</a> to move into the rectum.</p>
<p>The sigmoid colon is supplied with blood from several branches (usually between 2 and 6) of the <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_arteries" title="Sigmoid arteries">sigmoid arteries</a>, a branch of the IMA. The IMA terminates as the <a href="/wiki/Superior_rectal_artery" title="Superior rectal artery">superior rectal artery</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sigmoidoscopy" title="Sigmoidoscopy">Sigmoidoscopy</a> is a common diagnostic technique used to examine the sigmoid colon.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rectum">Rectum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rectum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> is the last section of the large intestine. It holds the formed feces awaiting elimination via defecation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a> – the first part of the large intestine</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taeniae_coli" title="Taeniae coli">Taeniae coli</a> – three bands of smooth muscle</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haustra" title="Haustra">Haustra</a> – bulges caused by contraction of taeniae coli</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiploic_appendages" title="Epiploic appendages">Epiploic appendages</a> – small fat accumulations on the viscera</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Taenia_coli" title="Taenia coli">taenia coli</a> run the length of the large intestine. Because the taenia coli are shorter than the large bowel itself, the colon becomes <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sacculated" title="wikt:sacculated">sacculated</a></i>, forming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haustra" title="Haustra">haustra</a> of the colon which are the shelf-like intraluminal projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_supply">Blood supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Blood supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">Arterial</a> supply to the colon comes from branches of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery" title="Superior mesenteric artery">superior mesenteric artery</a> (SMA) and <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_artery" title="Inferior mesenteric artery">inferior mesenteric artery</a> (IMA). Flow between these two systems communicates via a "marginal artery" that runs parallel to the colon for its entire length. Historically, it has been believed that the arc of Riolan, or the meandering mesenteric artery (of Moskowitz), is a variable vessel connecting the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">proximal</a> SMA to the proximal IMA that can be extremely important if either vessel is occluded. However, recent studies conducted with improved imaging technology have questioned the actual existence of this vessel, with some experts calling for the abolition of the terms from future medical literature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">Venous</a> drainage usually mirrors colonic arterial supply, with the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_vein" title="Inferior mesenteric vein">inferior mesenteric vein</a> draining into the <a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">splenic vein</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">superior mesenteric vein</a> joining the splenic vein to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a> that then enters the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lymphatic_drainage">Lymphatic drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lymphatic drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">Lymphatic drainage</a> from the ascending colon and proximal two-thirds of the <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a> is to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Colic_lymph_node&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Colic lymph node (page does not exist)">colic lymph nodes</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_lymph_node" title="Superior mesenteric lymph node">superior mesenteric lymph nodes</a>, which drain into the <a href="/wiki/Cisterna_chyli" title="Cisterna chyli">cisterna chyli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Little.2C_Brown.2C_and_Company_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Little.2C_Brown.2C_and_Company-18">[18]</a></sup> The lymph from the distal one-third of the <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a>, the sigmoid colon, and the upper <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> drain into the inferior mesenteric and colic lymph nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Little.2C_Brown.2C_and_Company_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Little.2C_Brown.2C_and_Company-18">[18]</a></sup> The lower rectum to the anal canal above the pectinate line drain to the internal <a href="/wiki/Ilium_(bone)" title="Ilium (bone)">iliac</a> nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Aid_for_the_USMLE_Step_1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_Aid_for_the_USMLE_Step_1-19">[19]</a></sup> The anal canal below the pectinate line drains into the superficial <a href="/wiki/Groin" title="Groin">inguinal</a> nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Aid_for_the_USMLE_Step_1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-First_Aid_for_the_USMLE_Step_1-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pectinate_line" title="Pectinate line">pectinate line</a> only roughly marks this transition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">Variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One variation on the normal anatomy of the colon occurs when extra loops form, resulting in a colon that is up to five metres longer than normal. This condition, referred to as <b>redundant colon</b>, typically has no direct major health consequences, though rarely <a href="/wiki/Volvulus" title="Volvulus">volvulus</a> occurs, resulting in obstruction and requiring immediate medical attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A significant indirect health consequence is that use of a standard adult <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonoscope" title="Colonoscope">colonoscope</a> is difficult and in some cases impossible when a redundant colon is present, though specialized variants on the instrument (including the pediatric variant) are useful in overcoming this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Histology">Histology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Histology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonic_crypts">Colonic crypts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Colonic crypts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2550" data-file-width="3300" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg/220px-Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg/330px-Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg/440px-Colonic_crypts_within_four_tissue_sections.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Panels A, B were cut across the long axes of the crypts and panels C, D were cut parallel to the long axes of the crypts. In panel A the bar shows 100 µm and allows an estimate of the frequency of crypts in the colonic epithelium. Panel B includes three crypts in cross-section, each with one segment deﬁcient for CCOI expression and at least one crypt, on the right side, undergoing fission into two crypts. Panel C shows, on the left side, a crypt fissioning into two crypts. Panel D shows typical small clusters of two and three CCOI deficient crypts (the bar shows 50 µm). The images were made from original photomicrographs, but panels A, B and D were also included in an article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-23">[23]</a></sup> and illustrations were published with Creative Commons Attribution-Noncommercial License allowing re-use.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The wall of the large intestine is lined with simple columnar <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_epithelium" title="Intestinal epithelium">epithelium</a> with <a href="/wiki/Invagination" title="Invagination">invaginations</a>. The invaginations are called the <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_gland" title="Intestinal gland">intestinal glands</a> or colonic crypts.</p>
<p>The colon crypts are shaped like microscopic thick walled test tubes with a central hole down the length of the tube (the crypt <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">lumen</a>). Four tissue sections are shown here, two cut across the long axes of the crypts and two cut parallel to the long axes. In these images the cells have been stained by <a href="/wiki/Immunohistochemistry" title="Immunohistochemistry">immunohistochemistry</a> to show a brown-orange color if the cells produce a <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrial</a> protein called <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_c_oxidase_subunit_I" title="Cytochrome c oxidase subunit I">cytochrome c oxidase subunit I</a> (CCOI). The <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a> of the cells (located at the outer edges of the cells lining the walls of the crypts) are stained blue-gray with <a href="/wiki/Haematoxylin" title="Haematoxylin">haematoxylin</a>. As seen in panels C and D, crypts are about 75 to about 110 cells long. Baker et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-24">[24]</a></sup> found that the average crypt circumference is 23 cells. Thus, by the images shown here, there are an average of about 1,725 to 2530 cells per colonic crypt. Nooteboom et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19878146_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19878146-25">[25]</a></sup> measuring the number of cells in a small number of crypts reported a range of 1500 to 4900 cells per colonic crypt. Cells are produced at the crypt base and migrate upward along the crypt axis before being shed into the colonic <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">lumen</a> days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-24">[24]</a></sup> There are 5 to 6 stem cells at the bases of the crypts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>As estimated from the image in panel A, there are about 100 colonic crypts per square millimeter of the colonic epithelium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen-11">[11]</a></sup> Since the average length of the human colon is 160.5 cm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hounnou_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hounnou-10">[10]</a></sup> and the average inner circumference of the colon is 6.2 cm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen-11">[11]</a></sup> the inner surface epithelial area of the human colon has an average area of about 995 sq cm, which includes 9,950,000 (close to 10 million) crypts.</p>
<p>In the four tissue sections shown here, many of the intestinal glands have cells with a <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> mutation in the <i>CCOI</i> gene and appear mostly white, with their main color being the blue-gray staining of the nuclei. As seen in panel B, a portion of the stem cells of three crypts appear to have a mutation in <i>CCOI</i>, so that 40% to 50% of the cells arising from those stem cells form a white segment in the cross cut area.</p>
<p>Overall, the percent of crypts deficient for CCOI is less than 1% before age 40, but then increases linearly with age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-23">[23]</a></sup> Colonic crypts deficient for CCOI in women reaches, on average, 18% in women and 23% in men by 80–84 years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crypts of the colon can reproduce by fission, as seen in panel C, where a crypt is fissioning to form two crypts, and in panel B where at least one crypt appears to be fissioning. Most crypts deficient in CCOI are in clusters of crypts (clones of crypts) with two or more CCOI-deficient crypts adjacent to each other (see panel D).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Large_intestine_histology.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1271" data-file-width="840" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Large_intestine_histology.jpg/220px-Large_intestine_histology.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Large_intestine_histology.jpg/330px-Large_intestine_histology.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Large_intestine_histology.jpg/440px-Large_intestine_histology.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Large_intestine_histology.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Histological section.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It absorbs water and any remaining absorbable nutrients from the food before sending the indigestible matter to the rectum. The colon absorbs vitamins that are created by the colonic bacteria, such as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> (especially important as the daily ingestion of vitamin K is not normally enough to maintain adequate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_coagulation" title="Blood coagulation">blood coagulation</a>), <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B12</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">thiamine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riboflavin" title="Riboflavin">riboflavin</a>. It also compacts feces, and stores fecal matter in the rectum until it can be discharged via the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>. The large intestine also secretes K+ and Cl-. Chloride secretion increases in cystic fibrosis. Recycling of various nutrients takes place in colon. Examples include fermentation of carbohydrates, short chain fatty acids, and urea cycling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a> is attached to the inferior surface of the cecum, and contains a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Mucosa-associated_lymphoid_tissue" title="Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue">mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue</a> which gives the appendix an undetermined role in immunity. However, the appendix is known to be important in fetal life as it contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endocrine" title="Endocrine">endocrine</a> cells that release biogenic amines and peptide hormones important for <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a> during early growth and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAAppendix_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAAppendix-26">[26]</a></sup> The appendix can be removed with no apparent damage or consequence to the patient.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the time the <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a> has reached this tube, most <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> and 90% of the water have been absorbed by the body. At this point some <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> like <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> are left as well as indigestible parts of ingested food (e.g., a large part of ingested <a href="/wiki/Amylose" title="Amylose">amylose</a>, starch which has been shielded from digestion heretofore, and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>, which is largely indigestible <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> in either soluble or insoluble form). As the chyme moves through the large intestine, most of the remaining <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is removed, while the chyme is mixed with <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (known as <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a>), and becomes feces. The <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending colon</a> receives fecal material as a liquid. The muscles of the colon then move the watery waste material forward and slowly absorb all the excess water, causing the stools to gradually solidify as they move along into the <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bacteria break down some of the <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> for their own nourishment and create <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propionate" title="Propionate">propionate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butyrate" title="Butyrate">butyrate</a> as waste products, which in turn are used by the cell lining of the colon for nourishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> No protein is made available. In humans, perhaps 10% of the undigested carbohydrate thus becomes available, though this may vary with diet;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> in other animals, including other apes and primates, who have proportionally larger colons, more is made available, thus permitting a higher portion of plant material in the diet. The large intestine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> produces no digestive <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> — <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_digestion" title="Chemical digestion">chemical digestion</a> is completed in the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> before the chyme reaches the large intestine. The <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> in the colon varies between 5.5 and 7 (slightly <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> to neutral).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standing_gradient_osmosis">Standing gradient osmosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Standing gradient osmosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Water absorption at the colon typically proceeds against a <a href="/wiki/Route_of_administration#Application_location" title="Route of administration">transmucosal</a> <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_pressure" title="Osmotic pressure">osmotic pressure</a> <a href="/wiki/Osmosis#Osmotic_gradient" title="Osmosis">gradient</a>. The <b>standing gradient osmosis</b> is the reabsorption of water against the osmotic gradient in the intestines. This <a href="/wiki/Tonicity" title="Tonicity">hypertonic</a> fluid creates an osmotic pressure that drives water into the lateral intercellular spaces by osmosis via <a href="/wiki/Tight_junction" title="Tight junction">tight junctions</a> and adjacent cells, which then in turn moves across the <a href="/wiki/Basal_lamina" title="Basal lamina">basement membrane</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gut_flora">Gut flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Gut flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">Gut flora</a></div>
<p>The large intestine houses over 700 species of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that perform a variety of functions, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. Species diversity varies by geography and diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The microbes in a human distal gut often number in the vicinity of 100 trillion, and can weigh around 200 grams (0.44 pounds). This mass of mostly symbiotic microbes has recently been called the latest human organ to be "discovered" or in other words, the "forgotten organ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The large intestine absorbs some of the products formed by the bacteria inhabiting this region. Undigested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a> (fiber) are metabolized to short-chain fatty acids by bacteria in the large intestine and absorbed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_diffusion" title="Passive diffusion">passive diffusion</a>. The bicarbonate that the large intestine secretes helps to neutralize the increased acidity resulting from the formation of these fatty acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>These bacteria also produce large amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamins" title="Vitamins">vitamins</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biotin" title="Biotin">biotin</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_vitamin" title="B vitamin">B vitamin</a>), for absorption into the blood. Although this source of vitamins, in general, provides only a small part of the daily requirement, it makes a significant contribution when dietary vitamin intake is low. An individual who depends on absorption of vitamins formed by bacteria in the large intestine may become vitamin-deficient if treated with <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> that inhibit the vitamin producing species of bacteria as well as the intended disease-causing bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other bacterial products include gas (flatus), which is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, with small amounts of the gases <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. Bacterial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> of undigested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a> produces these. Some of the fecal odor is due to <a href="/wiki/Indole" title="Indole">indoles</a>, metabolized from the amino acid tryptophan. The normal flora is also essential in the development of certain tissues, including the cecum and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatics" title="Lymphatics">lymphatics</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>They are also involved in the production of cross-reactive antibodies. These are antibodies produced by the immune system against the normal flora, that are also effective against related pathogens, thereby preventing infection or invasion.</p>
<p>The most prevalent bacteria are the <a href="/wiki/Bacteroides" title="Bacteroides">bacteroides</a>, which have been implicated in the initiation of <a href="/wiki/Colitis" title="Colitis">colitis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bifidobacteria" title="Bifidobacteria">Bifidobacteria</a> are also abundant, and are often described as 'friendly bacteria'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> layer protects the large intestine from attacks from colonic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commensal_bacteria" title="Commensal bacteria">commensal bacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonoscopy">Colonoscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Colonoscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Colonoscopy" title="Colonoscopy">Colonoscopy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Colonoscopy" title="Colonoscopy">Colonoscopy</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Endoscopy" title="Endoscopy">endoscopic</a> examination of the large intestine and the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">distal</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Ileum" title="Ileum">small bowel</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCD_camera" title="CCD camera">CCD camera</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">fiber optic</a> camera on a flexible tube passed through the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>. It can provide a visual diagnosis (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">ulceration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_polyp" title="Colorectal polyp">polyps</a>) and grants the opportunity for <a href="/wiki/Biopsy" title="Biopsy">biopsy</a> or removal of suspected <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer" title="Colorectal cancer">colorectal cancer</a> lesions. Colonoscopy can remove polyps as small as one millimetre or less. Once polyps are removed, they can be studied with the aid of a microscope to determine if they are precancerous or not. It takes 15 years or less for a polyp to turn cancerous.</p>
<p>Colonoscopy is similar to <a href="/wiki/Sigmoidoscopy" title="Sigmoidoscopy">sigmoidoscopy</a>—the difference being related to which parts of the colon each can examine. A colonoscopy allows an examination of the entire colon (1200–1500 mm in length). A sigmoidoscopy allows an examination of the distal portion (about 600 mm) of the colon, which may be sufficient because benefits to cancer survival of colonoscopy have been limited to the detection of lesions in the distal portion of the colon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baxter09_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baxter09-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>A sigmoidoscopy is often used as a screening procedure for a full colonoscopy, often done in conjunction with a fecal occult blood test (FOBT). About 5% of these screened patients are referred to colonoscopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virtual_colonoscopy" title="Virtual colonoscopy">Virtual colonoscopy</a>, which uses 2D and 3D imagery reconstructed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">computed tomography</a> (CT) scans or from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">nuclear magnetic resonance</a> (MR) scans, is also possible, as a totally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-invasive_(medical)" title="Non-invasive (medical)">non-invasive</a> medical test, although it is not standard and still under investigation regarding its diagnostic abilities. Furthermore, virtual colonoscopy does not allow for therapeutic maneuvers such as polyp/tumour removal or biopsy nor visualization of lesions smaller than 5 millimeters. If a growth or polyp is detected using CT colonography, a standard colonoscopy would still need to be performed. Additionally, surgeons have lately been using the term <a href="/wiki/Pouchoscopy" title="Pouchoscopy">pouchoscopy</a> to refer to a colonoscopy of the <a href="/wiki/Ileo-anal_pouch" title="Ileo-anal pouch">ileo-anal pouch</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_intestine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The large intestine is truly distinct only in <a href="/wiki/Tetrapod" title="Tetrapod">tetrapods</a>, in which it is almost always separated from the small intestine by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ileocaecal_valve" title="Ileocaecal valve">ileocaecal valve</a>. In most vertebrates, however, it is a relatively short structure running directly to the anus, although noticeably wider than the small intestine. Although the caecum is present in most <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">amniotes</a>, only in mammals does the remainder of the large intestine develop into a true colon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VertebrateBody_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-VertebrateBody-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some small mammals, the colon is straight, as it is in other tetrapods, but, in the majority of mammalian species, it is divided into ascending and descending portions; a distinct transverse colon is typically present only in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">primates</a>. However, the taeniae coli and accompanying haustra are not found in either <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">carnivorans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>. The rectum of mammals (other than <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">monotremes</a>) is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a> of other vertebrates, and is, therefore, not truly <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> with the "rectum" found in these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VertebrateBody_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-VertebrateBody-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In fish, there is no true large intestine, but simply a short rectum connecting the end of the digestive part of the gut to the cloaca. In <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, this includes a <i>rectal gland</i> that secretes salt to help the animal maintain <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmotic</a> balance with the seawater. The gland somewhat resembles a caecum in structure, but is not a homologous structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VertebrateBody_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-VertebrateBody-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
