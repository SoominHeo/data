<p><b>Mail and plate armour</b> (<b>Plated mail</b>, <b>plated chainmail</b>, <b>splinted mail/chainmail</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">mail</a> with embedded plates. Armour of this type has been used in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Iran" title="Greater Iran">Greater Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_mail_and_plate_armour">Types of mail and plate armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mail_and_plate_armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of mail and plate armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Russia there are three known varieties of mail and plate armour. These were adopted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>, initially as Persian exports<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and have Persian names.</p>
<ul>
<li>Behterets (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Бехтерец</span>), from Persian <i>behter</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> small horizontal plates arranged in vertical rows without gaps, joined by rings, and embedded in mail.</li>
<li>Yushman (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Юшман</span>), from Persian <i>jawshan</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> long horizontal plates embedded in mail and resembling <a href="/wiki/Laminar_armour" title="Laminar armour">laminar armour</a> (e.g. Roman <a href="/wiki/Lorica_segmentata" title="Lorica segmentata">lorica segmentata</a>)</li>
<li>Kalantar (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Калантарь</span>): square plates embedded in mail, very similar to the Japanese karuta <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatami-do" title="Tatami-do">tatami-do</a>. The major difference is that <i>kalantar</i> are not sewn to a cloth backing as Karuta tatami-do are.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to Bobrov<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the first mail and plate armor appeared as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisse" title="Cuisse">cuisses</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and were imported by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horde" title="Golden Horde">Golden Horde</a>. Persian miniatures of the first half of 15th century show different combinations of mail and plate armour with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamellar_armor" title="Lamellar armor">lamellar armor</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigandines" title="Brigandines">brigandines</a> sometimes worn with a single <a href="/wiki/Mirror_armour" title="Mirror armour">round mirror plate</a> as breast re-enforcement. The first representation of mail and plate armour as body protection is shown in Persian miniatures, which show mail and plate armour composed of relatively large plates, worn with <a href="/wiki/Laminar_armour" title="Laminar armour">laminar</a> pauldrons and skirt (formed from long, horizontal plates), re-enforced by a large <a href="/wiki/Mirror_armour" title="Mirror armour">round mirror plate</a>. The first representation of classic mail and plate armour (without lamellar elements) can be seen in Baghdad's miniature which dates from 1465. From the end of the 15th century mail and plate armour began to fully replace <a href="/wiki/Lamellar_armour" title="Lamellar armour">lamellar armours</a>. The main difference between eastern European (Russian and Polish) and Oriental mail and plate armor (according to Bobrov) is that eastern European versions usually do not have sleeves, while Oriental versions have sleeves (the forearms were protected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vambraces" title="Vambraces">vambraces</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In a heavy version these sleeves have embedded plates, and a light version (more widely used) has sleeves entirely made from <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">mail</a>.</p>
<p>In <i>Kitab al-Durra al-Maknuna</i> (<i>The Book of the Hidden Pearl</i>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C4%81bir_ibn_Hayy%C4%81n" title="Jābir ibn Hayyān">Jābir ibn Hayyān</a> describes mail and plate armour for use in <a href="/wiki/Armour" title="Armour">armours</a> (<i>jawasin</i>), <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmets</a> (<i>bid</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shields</a> (<i>daraq</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearl_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearl-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Japan mail and plate armour is called "karuta", small square or rectangular rawhide or metal plates with the gaps between them filled with mail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Korean version of this armour is known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeongbeongap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gyeongbeongap (page does not exist)">gyeongbeongap</a> (경번갑/鏡幡甲). The most famous general who used this type of armor was General Chonji.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
