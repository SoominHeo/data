<p>A <b>misdemeanor</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>misdemeanour</b> (British English) is any "lesser" <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">criminal</a> act in some <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> legal systems. Misdemeanors are generally <a href="/wiki/Punishment" title="Punishment">punished</a> less severely than <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felonies</a>, but theoretically more so than administrative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infraction" title="Infraction">infractions</a> (also known as minor, petty, or <a href="/wiki/Summary_offence" title="Summary offence">summary offences</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_offence" title="Regulatory offence">regulatory offences</a>. Many misdemeanors are punished with monetary fines.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_felonies_and_misdemeanors">Distinction between felonies and misdemeanors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distinction between felonies and misdemeanors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, the federal government generally considers a crime punishable with incarceration for one year or less to be a misdemeanor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> All other crimes are considered felonies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Many states also employ this distinction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A misdemeanor is considered a crime of low seriousness, and a felony one of high seriousness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A principle of the rationale for the degree of punishment meted out is that the punishment should fit the crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One standard for measurement is the degree to which a crime affects others or society. Measurements of the degree of seriousness of a crime have been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSS_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSS-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The distinction between felonies and misdemeanors has been abolished by several <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>). These jurisdictions have generally adopted some other classification: in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth nations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the crimes are divided into <a href="/wiki/Summary_offence" title="Summary offence">summary offences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indictable_offence" title="Indictable offence">indictable offences</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, a former member of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>, also uses these divisions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="When_a_misdemeanor_becomes_a_felony">When a misdemeanor becomes a felony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: When a misdemeanor becomes a felony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States of America, the first time a person commits certain crimes, such as spousal assault, it is a misdemeanor, the second time it becomes a felony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_misdemeanors_and_sentences">Typical misdemeanors and sentences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typical misdemeanors and sentences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graffiti_in_Bucharest,_July_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1482" data-file-width="2218" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg/220px-Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg/330px-Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg/440px-Graffiti_in_Bucharest%2C_July_2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graffiti_in_Bucharest,_July_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In the US, <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> is a common form of misdemeanor <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>, although in many states it is now a felony.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In some jurisdictions, those who are <a href="/wiki/Convict" title="Convict">convicted</a> of a misdemeanor are known as <b>misdemeanants</b> (as contrasted with those convicted of a <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felony</a> who are known as <i>felons</i>). Depending on the jurisdiction, examples of misdemeanors may include: <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">petty theft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_intoxication" title="Public intoxication">public intoxication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">simple assault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disorderly_conduct" title="Disorderly conduct">disorderly conduct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reckless_driving" title="Reckless driving">reckless driving</a>, discharging a firearm within city limits, possession of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> and in some jurisdictions first-time possession of certain other drugs, and other similar crimes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Punishments_for_misdemeanors">Punishments for misdemeanors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Punishments for misdemeanors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, misdemeanors are typically crimes with a maximum punishment of 12 months of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incarceration" title="Incarceration">incarceration</a>, typically in a local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_jail" title="County jail">jail</a> as contrasted with felons, who are typically incarcerated in a <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a>. Jurisdictions such as Massachusetts are a notable exception where the maximum punishment of some misdemeanors is up to 2.5 years. People who are convicted of misdemeanors are often punished with <a href="/wiki/Probation" title="Probation">probation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_service" title="Community service">community service</a>, short <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_jail" title="County jail">jail</a> term, or part-time <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">imprisonment</a>; served on the weekends.</p>
<p>Misdemeanors usually do not result in the loss of civil rights, but may result in loss of privileges, such as professional licenses, public offices, or public employment. Such effects are known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collateral_consequences_of_criminal_charges" title="Collateral consequences of criminal charges">collateral consequences of criminal charges</a>. This is more common when the misdemeanor is related to the privilege in question (such as the loss of a <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxi</a> driver's license after a conviction for <a href="/wiki/Reckless_driving" title="Reckless driving">reckless driving</a>), or when the misdemeanor involves <a href="/wiki/Moral_turpitude" title="Moral turpitude">moral turpitude</a>—and in general is evaluated on a case-by-case basis. One prominent example of this is found in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, which provides that the President may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impeached" title="Impeached">impeached</a> and subsequently removed from office if found guilty by Congress for "high crimes and misdemeanors". The definition of a "high crime" is left to the judgment of Congress.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, misdemeanors generally are sentenced to months<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jail_sentence" title="Jail sentence">jail sentence</a> but with individual crimes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> suspects are sentenced to a harsher sentence. The penalty of <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Fine_(penalty)" title="Fine (penalty)">fine</a> not exceeding S$2,000 or imprisonment not exceeding three years, and also <a href="/wiki/Corporal_punishment" title="Corporal punishment">corporal punishment</a> of not less than three strokes and not more than eight strokes of the cane.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Misdemeanor_classes">Misdemeanor classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Misdemeanor classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1592" data-file-width="1759" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg/220px-Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg/330px-Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg/440px-Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Macro_cannabis_bud.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Possession of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> is an unclassified misdemeanor in parts of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Depending on the jurisdiction, several classes of misdemeanors may exist; the forms of punishment can vary widely between those classes. For example, the federal and <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> governments in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> divide misdemeanors into several classes, with certain classes punishable by jail time and others carrying only a fine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In New York law, a Class A Misdemeanor carries a maximum sentence of one year of imprisonment, while a Class B Misdemeanor "shall not exceed three months".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unclassified_misdemeanors">Unclassified misdemeanors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unclassified misdemeanors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, when a statute does not specify the class, it is referred to as an <i>unclassified misdemeanor</i>.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.legalmatch.com/law-library/article/unclassified-misdemeanor-laws.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> Legislators usually enact such laws when they wish to impose penalties that fall outside the framework specified by each class.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For instance, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> has four classes of misdemeanors, with Class 1 and Class 2 misdemeanors being punishable by twelve-month and six-month jail sentences, respectively, and Class 3 and Class 4 misdemeanors being non-jail offenses payable by fines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leg1.state.va.us_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-leg1.state.va.us-12">[12]</a></sup> First-time <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> <a href="/wiki/Drug_possession" title="Drug possession">possession</a> is an unclassified misdemeanor in Virginia punishable by up to 30 days in jail rather than the normal fines and jail sentences of the four classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leg1.state.va.us_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-leg1.state.va.us-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> New York has three classes of misdemanors: A, B, and Unclassified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Misdemeanor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All distinctions between felony and misdemeanour were abolished by section 1(1) of the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Law_Act_1967" title="Criminal Law Act 1967">Criminal Law Act 1967</a>. Prior to this, a person prosecuted for misdemeanour was called a <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
