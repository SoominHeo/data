<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Master of Business Administration</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"MBA" redirects here. <p>A <b>Master of Business Administration</b> (<b>MBA</b> or <b>M.B.A.</b>) holds a master's degree in <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a> (management). The MBA degree originated in the United States in the early 20th century when the country industrialized and companies sought <a href="/wiki/Scientific_management" title="Scientific management">scientific approaches to management</a>. The core courses in an MBA program cover various areas of business such as accounting, finance, marketing, human resources, and operations in a manner most relevant to management <a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">analysis</a> and strategy. Most programs also include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elective_course" title="Elective course">elective courses</a>.</p>
<p>The MBA is a <a href="/wiki/Terminal_degree" title="Terminal degree">terminal degree</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Professional_school" title="Professional school">professional degree</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_accreditation" title="School accreditation">Accreditation</a> bodies specifically for MBA programs ensure consistency and quality of education. Business schools in many countries offer programs tailored to full-time, part-time, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_title" title="Corporate title">executive</a> (abridged coursework typically occurring on nights and/or weekends), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_learning" title="Distance learning">distance learning</a> students, many with concentrations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a> of business in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> was <a href="/wiki/Wharton_School_of_the_University_of_Pennsylvania" title="Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania">The Wharton School</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> established in 1881 through a donation from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wharton" title="Joseph Wharton">Joseph Wharton</a> <sup><a href="/wiki/Wharton_School_of_the_University_of_Pennsylvania" title="Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania">[4]</a></sup>. In 1900 the <a href="/wiki/Tuck_School_of_Business" title="Tuck School of Business">Tuck School of Business</a> was founded at <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> conferring the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_degree" title="Advanced degree">advanced degree</a> in business, specifically, a Master of Science in Commerce, the predecessor to the MBA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stabile2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stabile2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Business_Administration" title="Harvard Graduate School of Business Administration">Harvard Graduate School of Business Administration</a> established the first MBA program in 1908, with 15 faculty members, 33 regular students, and 47 special students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its first-year curriculum was based on Frederick Winslow Taylor's scientific management. The number of MBA students at Harvard increased quickly, from 80 in 1908, over 300 in 1920, and 1070 in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leach_1993_288_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leach_1993_288-7">[7]</a></sup> At this time, only American universities offered MBAs. Other countries preferred that people learn business on the job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leach_1993_288_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leach_1993_288-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other milestones include:</p>
<ul>
<li>1930: First management and leadership education program for executives and mid-career experienced managers (the <a href="/wiki/Sloan_Fellows" title="Sloan Fellows">Sloan Fellows</a> Program at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>1943: First Executive MBA (EMBA) program for working professionals at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Booth_School_of_Business" title="University of Chicago Booth School of Business">University of Chicago Booth School of Business</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Chicago was also the first business school to establish permanent campuses on three continents in Chicago (USA), Barcelona (Europe) and Singapore (Asia). Most business schools today offer a global component to their executive MBA. Since the program was established, the school has moved its campuses and is now based in Chicago, London and Hong Kong.</li>
<li>1946: First MBA focused on global management at <a href="/wiki/Thunderbird_School_of_Global_Management" title="Thunderbird School of Global Management">Thunderbird School of Global Management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>1950: First MBA outside of the United States, in Canada (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Ivey_School_of_Business" title="Richard Ivey School of Business">Richard Ivey School of Business</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Western_Ontario" title="The University of Western Ontario">The University of Western Ontario</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> followed by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pretoria" title="University of Pretoria">University of Pretoria</a> in South Africa in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>1955: First MBA offered at an Asian school at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Business_Administration_Karachi" title="Institute of Business Administration Karachi">Institute of Business Administration Karachi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Karachi" title="University of Karachi">University of Karachi</a> in Pakistan, in collaboration with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wharton_School" title="Wharton School">Wharton School</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>1957: First MBA offered at a European school (<a href="/wiki/INSEAD" title="INSEAD">INSEAD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>1963: First MBA offered in Korea by Korea University Business School (KUBS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>1986: First MBA program requiring every student to have a laptop computer in the classroom at the Roy E. Crummer Graduate School of Business at <a href="/wiki/Rollins_College" title="Rollins College">Rollins College</a> (Florida).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Beginning with the 1992–1993 academic year, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Business_School" title="Columbia Business School">Columbia Business School</a> required all incoming students to purchase a laptop computer with standard software, becoming the first business school to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>1994: First online executive MBA program at <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_University" title="Athabasca University">Athabasca University</a> (Canada).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The MBA degree has been adopted by universities worldwide in both developed and developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accreditation">Accreditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Accreditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">Business school</a> or MBA program accreditation by external agencies provides students and employers with an independent view of the school or program's quality, as well as whether the curriculum meets specific quality standards. The three major accrediting bodies in the United States are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Association_to_Advance_Collegiate_Schools_of_Business" title="Association to Advance Collegiate Schools of Business">Association to Advance Collegiate Schools of Business</a> (AACSB),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Accreditation_Council_for_Business_Schools_and_Programs" title="Accreditation Council for Business Schools and Programs">Accreditation Council for Business Schools and Programs</a> (ACBSP), and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Assembly_for_Collegiate_Business_Education" title="International Assembly for Collegiate Business Education">International Assembly for Collegiate Business Education</a> (IACBE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>All of these groups also accredit schools outside the US. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACBSP" title="ACBSP">ACBSP</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IACBE" title="IACBE">IACBE</a> are themselves recognized in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Higher_Education_Accreditation" title="Council for Higher Education Accreditation">Council for Higher Education Accreditation</a> (CHEA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> MBA programs with specializations for students pursuing careers in healthcare management also eligible for accreditation by the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_Accreditation_of_Healthcare_Management_Education" title="Commission on the Accreditation of Healthcare Management Education">Commission on the Accreditation of Healthcare Management Education</a> (CAHME).</p>
<p>US MBA programs may also be accredited at the institutional level. Bodies that accredit institutions as a whole include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Middle_States_Association_of_Colleges_and_Schools" title="Middle States Association of Colleges and Schools">Middle States Association of Colleges and Schools</a> (MSA),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_England_Association_of_Schools_and_Colleges" title="New England Association of Schools and Colleges">New England Association of Schools and Colleges</a> (NEASC),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Higher_Learning_Commission" title="Higher Learning Commission">Higher Learning Commission</a> of the <a href="/wiki/North_Central_Association_of_Colleges_and_Schools" title="North Central Association of Colleges and Schools">North Central Association of Colleges and Schools</a> (HLC),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northwest_Commission_on_Colleges_and_Universities" title="Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities">Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities</a> (NWCCU),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Southern_Association_of_Colleges_and_Schools" title="Southern Association of Colleges and Schools">Southern Association of Colleges and Schools</a> (SACS), and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Western_Association_of_Schools_and_Colleges" title="Western Association of Schools and Colleges">Western Association of Schools and Colleges</a> (WASC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Accreditation agencies outside the United States include the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_MBAs" title="Association of MBAs">Association of MBAs</a> (AMBA), a UK-based organization that accredits MBA, DBA and MBM programs worldwide, government accreditation bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/All_India_Council_for_Technical_Education" title="All India Council for Technical Education">All India Council for Technical Education</a> (AICTE), which accredits MBA and Postgraduate Diploma in Management (PGDM) programs across India. Some of the leading bodies in India that certify MBA institutions and their programs are the All India Council for Technical Education (AICTE) and the University Grants Commission (UGC). A distance MBA program needs to be accredited by the Distance Education Council (DEC) in India. The Council on Higher Education (CHE) in South Africa, the <a href="/wiki/European_Foundation_for_Management_Development" title="European Foundation for Management Development">European Foundation for Management Development</a> operates the <a href="/wiki/European_Quality_Improvement_System" title="European Quality Improvement System">European Quality Improvement System</a> (EQUIS) for mostly European, Australian, New Zealand and Asian schools, the <a href="/wiki/Foundation_for_International_Business_Administration_Accreditation" title="Foundation for International Business Administration Accreditation">Foundation for International Business Administration Accreditation</a> (FIBAA), and <a href="/wiki/Central_and_East_European_Management_Development_Association" title="Central and East European Management Development Association">Central and East European Management Development Association</a> (CEEMAN) in Europe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Two-year (Full-Time) MBA</b> programs normally take place over two academic years (i.e. approximately 18 months of term time). For example, in the Northern Hemisphere, they often begin in late August/September of year one and continue until May of year two, with a three- to four-month summer break in between years one and two. Students enter with a reasonable amount of prior real-world work experience and take classes during weekdays like other university students. A typical Full-time, accelerated, part-time or modular MBA requires 60 credits (600 class hours) of graduate work.</p>
<p><b>Accelerated MBA</b> programs are a variation of the two-year programs. They involve a higher course load with more intense class and examination schedules and are usually condensed into one year. They usually have less 'down time' during the program and between semesters. For example, there is no three to four-month summer break, and between semesters there might be seven to ten days off rather than three to five weeks vacation. Accelerated programs typically have a lower cost than full-time two-year programs.</p>
<p><b>Part-Time MBA</b> programs normally hold classes on weekday evenings, after normal working hours, or on weekends. Part-time programs normally last three years or more. The students in these programs typically consist of working professionals, who take a light course load for a longer period of time until the graduation requirements are met.</p>
<p><b>Evening (Second Shift) MBA</b> programs are full-time programs that normally hold classes on weekday evenings, after normal working hours, or on weekends for a duration of two years. The students in these programs typically consist of working professionals, who can not leave their work to pursue a full-time regular shift MBA. Most second shift programs are offered at universities in India.</p>
<p><b>Modular MBA</b> programs are similar to part-time programs, although typically employing a lock-step curriculum with classes packaged together in blocks lasting from one to three weeks.</p>
<p><span id="Executive_MBA_.28EMBA.29"></span><b>Executive MBA (EMBA)</b> programs developed to meet the educational needs of managers and executives, allowing students to earn an MBA (or another business-related graduate degree) in two years or less while working full-time. Participants come from every type and size of organization – profit, nonprofit, government – representing a variety of industries. EMBA students typically have a higher level of work experience, often 10 years or more, compared to other MBA students. In response to the increasing number of EMBA programs offered, The Executive MBA Council was formed in 1981 to advance executive education.</p>
<p><b>Full-time executive MBA</b> programs are a new category of full-time 1 year MBA programs aimed at professionals with approx. 5 years or more. They are primarily offered in countries like India where the 2-year MBA program is targeted at fresh graduates with no experience or minimal experience. These full-time executive MBA programs are similar to 1 year MBA programs offered by schools like Insead and IMD.</p>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_learning" title="Distance learning">Distance learning</a></b> MBA programs hold classes off-campus. These programs can be offered in a number of different formats: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correspondence_course" title="Correspondence course">correspondence courses</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postal_mail" title="Postal mail">postal mail</a> or email, non-interactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_video" title="Broadcast video">broadcast video</a>, pre-recorded video, live <a href="/wiki/Teleconference" title="Teleconference">teleconference</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Videoconference" title="Videoconference">videoconference</a>, offline or online computer courses. Many schools offer these programs.</p>
<p><b>Blended learning</b> programs combine distance learning with face-to-face instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> These programs typically target working professionals who are unable to attend traditional part-time programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>MBA dual degree</b> programs combine an MBA with others (such as an MS, MA, or a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">JD</a>, etc.) to let students cut costs (dual programs usually cost less than pursuing 2 degrees separately), save time on education and to tailor the business education courses to their needs. This is generally achieved by allowing core courses of one program count as electives in the other. Some business schools offer programs in which students can earn both a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a> and an MBA in five years.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Mini-MBA" title="Mini-MBA">Mini-MBA</a></b> is a term used by many non-profit and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/For-profit" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> institutions to describe a <a href="/wiki/Training" title="Training">training</a> regimen focused on the fundamentals of business. In the past, Mini-MBA programs have typically been offered as non-credit bearing courses that require less than 100 hours of total learning. However, due to the criticisms of these certificates, many schools have now shifted their programs to offer courses for full credit so that they may be applied towards a complete traditional MBA degree. This is to allow students to verify business related coursework for employment purposes and still allow the option to complete a full-time MBA degree program at a later period, if they elect to do so.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Admissions_criteria">Admissions criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Admissions criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many programs base their admission decisions on a combination of undergraduate grade point average, academic transcripts, entrance exam scores, a résumé containing significant work experience, essays, letters of recommendation, and personal interviews. Some schools are also interested in extracurricular activities, community service activities or volunteer work, and how the student can improve the school's diversity and contribute to the student body as a whole. The <a href="/wiki/Graduate_Management_Admission_Test" title="Graduate Management Admission Test">Graduate Management Admission Test</a> (GMAT) is the most prominently used entrance exam for admissions into MBA programs. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graduate_Record_Examination" title="Graduate Record Examination">Graduate Record Examination</a> (GRE) is also accepted by almost all MBA programs in order to fulfill any entrance exam requirement they may have.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some schools do not weigh entrance exam scores as heavily as other criteria, and some programs do not require entrance exam scores for admission. In order to achieve a diverse class, business schools also consider the target male-female ratio and local-international student ratios. In rare cases, some MBA degrees do not require students to have an undergraduate degree and will accept significant management experience in lieu of an undergraduate degree. In the UK, for example an HND or even HNC is acceptable in some programs.</p>
<p>Depending on the program, type and length of work experience can be a critical admissions component for many MBA programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Many top-tier programs require five or more years of work experience for admission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, MBA programs are structured around core courses (an essentially standard curriculum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Core_Courses_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Core_Courses-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MFT-MBA_Test_Description_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-MFT-MBA_Test_Description-31">[31]</a></sup>) and elective courses that allow for a subject specialty or concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Thus, in the program's first year (or part), students acquire both a working knowledge of management functions and the analytical skills required for these. After the first year, many full-time students seek <a href="/wiki/Internship" title="Internship">internships</a>. In the second year (part), students pursue elective courses, which may count towards a specialization, dependent on program. (Topics in <a href="/wiki/Business_ethics" title="Business ethics">business ethics</a> may also be included at the generalist or specialist level.) The degree culminates with coursework in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_strategy" title="Business strategy">business strategy</a>, the program capstone. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation">dissertation</a> or major project is usually a degree requirement after the completion of coursework. For Executive MBAs, the curriculum is generally similar, although the perspective will be longer term and strategic as opposed to medium term and tactical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Many MBA programs end with a comprehensive exit examination (see below).</p>
<p>Programs are designed such that students gain exposure to theory and practice alike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Courses include lectures, case studies, and team projects; the mix though, will differ by school<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="#Programs">by format</a>. Theory is covered in the classroom setting by <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">academic faculty</a>, and is reinforced through the <a href="/wiki/Case_method" title="Case method">case method</a>, placing the student in the role of the decision maker. Similar to real world business situations, cases include both constraints and incomplete information. Practical learning (field immersion) often comprises <a href="/wiki/Management_consulting" title="Management consulting">consulting projects</a> with real clients, and is generally undertaken in teams<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (or "syndicates"). The practical elements (as well as the case studies) often involve <a href="/wiki/Clinical_professor" title="Clinical professor">external practitioners</a>—sometimes business executives—supporting the teaching from academic faculty.</p>

<p>As above, courses begin with basic -i.e. underlying - topics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Core_Courses_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Core_Courses-30">[30]</a></sup> and then progress to more advanced functional topics where these are applied. The underlying analytic skills required for management are usually covered initially. The accounting course(s) may treat <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a> separately. <a href="/wiki/Managerial_economics" title="Managerial economics">Managerial economics</a> is a staple in many curricula and mainly focuses on product pricing; more general coverage of micro- and macro-economics is often offered instead. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operations_Research" title="Operations Research">Operations Research</a> and statistics are sometimes combined as "Managerial Decision-Making" or "Quantitative Decision-Making"; organizational behavior and human resource management may similarly be combined. In many programs, applicants with appropriate background may be exempt from various of the analytical courses.</p>
<p>For the functional courses, some programs treat the curriculum in two parts. Here, the first course provides an overview, while the second revisits the subject in depth. Alternatively, the first addresses short-term, tactical problems, while the second addresses long-term, strategic problems (e.g., "Financial Management I" might cover <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_capital_management" title="Working capital management">working capital management</a>, while part II covers <a href="/wiki/Corporate_finance#Investment_and_project_valuation" title="Corporate finance">capital investment decisions</a>). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_systems" title="Information systems">Information systems</a> / <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">technology</a> is often included as a core functional course rather than an elective. Ethics training is often delivered with coursework in corporate social responsibility and corporate governance.</p>
<p>Programs may also include (coursework-based) training in the skills needed at senior levels of management: <a href="/wiki/Soft_skills" title="Soft skills">soft skills</a>, such as (general) leadership and negotiation; hard skills, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spreadsheets" title="Spreadsheets">spreadsheets</a>, project management and foreign languages; <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_thought#Creative_processes" title="Outline of thought">thinking skills</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> and creativity. Training in areas such as <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">multiculturalism</a> and corporate social responsibility is similarly included. Company visits (including overseas travel), and guest lectures or seminars with CEOs and management personalities may also be included. These, with the core subjects, provide the graduate with "breadth", while the specialty courses provide "depth".</p>
<p>For the business strategy component, the degree capstone, the focus is on finding <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a> and the long-term positioning and management of the entity as a whole—i.e., <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration proper</a>—and the key functions are thus synthesized and/or integrated into an overall view. (The strategy course depicts how the various sub-disciplines integrate to tell one continuous story.) Corresponding training in <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurial_leadership" title="Entrepreneurial leadership">business leadership</a> may also be scheduled, while related participation in a <a href="/wiki/Business_simulation" title="Business simulation">business simulation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Business_game" title="Business game">game</a> is a common degree requirement. "Strategy" may be offered as a sequence of courses, beginning in the first part (planning) and culminating in the second (execution), or as a single intensive course, offered during the second part. Some programs offer a specialization in "strategy", others in management consulting which substantially addresses the same issues.</p>
<p>The MBA dissertation (or thesis in some universities) will, in general, comprise the following in some combination:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Literature_review" title="Literature review">discussion of the literature</a>, providing a critical review and structuring of what is known on a given topic, with a view to addressing a specific problem; a <a href="/wiki/Case_study" title="Case study">case study</a> that goes beyond simple description, containing the analysis of hitherto unpublished material; a test of the application or limitations of some known principle or technique in a particular situation, and / or suggested modifications. As an alternative to the dissertation, some programs instead allow for a major project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-degree.net_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-degree.net-37">[37]</a></sup> Here (part-time) students will address a problem current in their organization; particularly in programs with an <a href="/wiki/Action_learning" title="Action learning">action learning</a> orientation, these may be practically oriented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-degree.net_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-degree.net-37">[37]</a></sup> Most MBA programs require additional course work in research methodology, preceding the dissertation or project. Some programs allow that the research component as a whole may be substituted with additional elective coursework.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exit_examination">Exit examination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exit examination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many MBA programs culminate in a <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_examination" title="Comprehensive examination">comprehensive exit examination</a>. The national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standardized_exam" title="Standardized exam">standardized exam</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Major_Field_Test_for_Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Major Field Test for Master of Business Administration">Major Field Test for MBAs</a> (MFT-MBA) has been administered in the MBA programs of over 300 U.S. universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The MFT-MBA aims to assess skills, knowledge, and reasoning ability within the domain of standard MBA curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MFT-MBA_Test_Description_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-MFT-MBA_Test_Description-31">[31]</a></sup> It is administered by <a href="/wiki/Educational_Testing_Service" title="Educational Testing Service">Educational Testing Service</a>. Another prominent option for comprehensive exit exams is the Common Professional Component Comprehensive Exam for MBAs (CPC COMP Exam for MBAs) owned by Peregrine Academic Services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Many programs choose to administer their own in-house exam rather than a standardized test.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Careers">Careers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Careers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An MBA prepares individuals for many types of careers. According to a survey by the Graduate Management Admissions Council, 64% MBA graduates of year 2012 used their MBA to change careers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Some of the more common jobs an MBA prepares one for include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Business analyst or strategist</li>
<li>Business development analyst, associate, or manager</li>
<li>Director (of a department)</li>
<li>Entrepreneur/founder</li>
<li>Financial analyst</li>
<li>Management consultant</li>
<li>Marketing associate, analyst, or manager</li>
<li>Portfolio manager</li>
<li>Project, product, or program manager</li>
<li>Operations analyst, associate, or manager<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_schools_in_Europe" title="List of business schools in Europe">List of business schools in Europe</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1957, INSEAD (French name "Institut Européen d'Administration des Affaires", or European Institute of Business Administration) became the first European university offering the MBA degree,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDHEC" title="EDHEC">EDHEC</a> Business School in 1959 and <a href="/wiki/ICADE" title="ICADE">ICADE</a> in 1960 (who had started offering in 1956 a "Technical Seminary for Business Administration"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/ESADE_Business_School" title="ESADE Business School">ESADE</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/IESE_Business_School" title="IESE Business School">IESE Business School</a> (first two-year program in Europe) in 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Smurfit_Graduate_School_of_Business" title="Michael Smurfit Graduate School of Business">UCD Smurfit Business School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cranfield_School_of_Management" title="Cranfield School of Management">Cranfield School of Management</a> in 1964, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Business_School" title="Manchester Business School">Manchester Business School</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Business_School" title="London Business School">London Business School</a> in 1965, The University of Dublin (<a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotterdam_School_of_Management" title="Rotterdam School of Management">Rotterdam School of Management</a> in 1966, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vlerick_Business_School" title="Vlerick Business School">Vlerick Business School</a> in 1968<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and in 1969 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HEC_School_of_Management" title="HEC School of Management">HEC School of Management</a> (in French, the École des Hautes Études Commerciales) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institut_d%27Etudes_Politiques_de_Paris" title="Institut d'Etudes Politiques de Paris">Institut d'Etudes Politiques de Paris</a>. In 1972, Swiss business school <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMEDE" title="IMEDE">IMEDE</a> (now <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Management_Development" title="International Institute for Management Development">IMD</a>) began offering a full-time MBA program, followed by <a href="/wiki/IE_Business_School" title="IE Business School">IE Business School</a> (in Spanish, Instituto de Empresas) in 1973, and AGH University of Science and Technology in Cracow, Poland in 1974. In 1991, <a href="/wiki/IEDC-Bled_School_of_Management" title="IEDC-Bled School of Management">IEDC-Bled School of Management</a> became the first school in the ex-socialist block of the Central and Eastern to offer an MBA degree.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bologna_Accord">Bologna Accord</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bologna Accord">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Europe, the recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bologna_Accord" title="Bologna Accord">Bologna Accord</a> established uniformity in three levels of higher education: Bachelor (three or four years), Masters (one or two years, in addition to three or four years for a Bachelor), and Doctorate (an additional three or four years after a Master). Students can acquire professional experience after their initial bachelor's degree at any European institution and later complete their masters in any other European institution via the <a href="/wiki/European_Credit_Transfer_and_Accumulation_System" title="European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System">European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accreditation_standards">Accreditation standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Accreditation standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Accreditation standards are not uniform in Europe. Some countries have legal requirements for accreditation (e.g. most German states), in some there is a legal requirement only for universities of a certain type (e.g. Austria), and others have no accreditation law at all. Even where there is no legal requirement, many business schools are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_accreditation" title="School accreditation">accredited</a> by independent bodies voluntarily to ensure quality standards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Austria, MBA programs of <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private universities</a> have to be accredited by the Austrian Accreditation Council (<i>Österreichischer Akkreditierungsrat</i>). State-run universities have no accreditation requirements, however, some of them voluntarily undergo accreditation procedures by independent bodies. There are also MBA programs of non-academic business schools, who are entitled by the Austrian government to offer these programs until the end of 2012 (<i>Lehrgang universitären Charakters</i>). Some non-academic institutions cooperate with state-run universities to ensure legality of their degrees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>January 1999 saw the first meeting of the Association of the Czech MBA Schools (CAMBAS). The Association is housed within the Centre for Doctoral and Managerial Studies of UEP, Prague. All of the founding members of the Association to have their MBA programs accredited by partner institutions in the United Kingdom or United States of America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France_and_French_speaking_countries">France and French speaking countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: France and French speaking countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In France and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">Francophone</a> countries such as Switzerland, Monaco, Belgium, and Canada, the MBA degree programs at the public accredited schools are similar to those offered in the <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">Anglo-Saxon</a> countries. Most French Business Schools are accredited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conf%C3%A9rence_des_grandes_%C3%A9coles_(CGE)" title="Conférence des grandes écoles (CGE)">Conférence des Grandes Écoles</a>, which is an association of higher educational establishments outside the mainstream framework of the public education system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Germany was one of the last western countries to adopt the MBA degree. In 1998, the <i>Hochschulrahmengesetz</i> (Higher Education Framework Act), a German federal law regulating higher education including the types of degrees offered, was modified to permit German universities to offer master's degrees. The traditional German degree in business administration was the <a href="/wiki/Diplom" title="Diplom">Diplom</a> in <i>Betriebswirtschaft</i> (Diplom-Kaufmann; Master's degree equivalent) but since 1999, bachelor's and master's degrees have gradually replaced the traditional degrees due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bologna_process" title="Bologna process">Bologna process</a>. Today most German business schools offer the MBA. Most <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German states</a> require that MBA degrees have to be accredited by one of the six agencies officially recognized by the German <i>Akkreditierungsrat</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> (accreditation council), the German counterpart to the American <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Higher_Education_Accreditation" title="Council for Higher Education Accreditation">CHEA</a>. The busiest of these six agencies (in respect to MBA degrees) is the Foundation for International Business Administration Accreditation (FIBAA). All universities themselves have to be institutionally accredited by the state (<i>staatlich anerkannt</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Italian MBAs programs at public accredited schools are similar to those offered elsewhere in Europe. Italian Business Schools are accredited by EQUIS and by ASFOR.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several MBA programs offered in Poland. Some of these are run as partnerships with American or Canadian Universities. Others rely on their own faculty and enrich their courses by inviting visiting lecturers. Several MBA programs in Poland are also offered in English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several business schools offer highly ranked MBA programs in Portugal. Portuguese M.B.A programs are increasingly internationally oriented, being taught in English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spain has a long history in offering MBA programs with three MBA programs frequently being ranked in the Top 25 worldwide by several international rankings. Spanish MBAs are culturally diverse and taught in English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several schools in Switzerland that offer an MBA as full-time, part-time and executive education programs. Some business schools that offer MBA programs with specializations such as Finance and Healthcare, technology management, and others. As a country with four different national languages (German, French, Italian and Romansh),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Switzerland offers most of its programs in English to attract international students to the country.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recently MBA programs appeared in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> where there are now about twenty schools of business offering a variety of MBA programs. Three of these are subsidiaries of European schools of business, while the remaining institutions are independent. Ukrainian MBA programs are concentrated mainly on particulars of business and management in Ukraine. For example, 2/3 of all case studies are based on real conditions of Ukrainian companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The UK-based Association of MBAs (AMBA) was established in 1967 and is an active advocate for MBA degrees. The association's accreditation service is internationally recognised for all MBA, DBA and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masters_in_Business_and_Management_(MBM)" title="Masters in Business and Management (MBM)">Masters in Business and Management (MBM)</a> programmes. AMBA also offer the only professional membership association for MBA students and graduates. UK MBA programmes typically consist of a set number of taught courses plus a dissertation or project.</p>

<p>The Financial Times in its Executive Education Rankings for 2012 included 5 African business schools.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Business schools administered as colleges within the traditional universities offer a variety of MBA programs. In addition, a few standalone business schools allied with foreign business schools exist.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_schools_in_South_Africa" title="List of business schools in South Africa">List of business schools in South Africa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/League_tables_of_South_African_business_schools" title="League tables of South African business schools">League tables of South African business schools</a></div>
<p>In 2004 South Africa's Council on Higher Education (CHE) completed an extensive re-accreditation of MBA degrees offered in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghana">Ghana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Ghana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Business schools of the traditional universities run a variety of MBA programs. In addition, foreign accredited institutions offer MBA degrees by distance learning in Ghana.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenya">Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Kenya" title="Education in Kenya">Education in Kenya</a></div>
<p>MBA programs are offered in many public and private universities.</p>
<p>Students choose to specialize in one of the following areas: Accounting, Finance, Entrepreneurship, Insurance and Human Resources. The course takes 4 semesters of about 4 months each.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Asia-Pacific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_schools_in_Asia" title="List of business schools in Asia">List of business schools in Asia</a></div>
<p>International MBA programs are acquiring brand value in Asia. For example, while a foreign MBA is still preferred in the Philippines, many students are now studying at one of many "Global MBA" English language programs being offered. English-only MBA programs are also offered in Hong Kong, Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan, and Thailand. For international students who want a different experience, many Asian programs offer scholarships and discounted tuition to encourage an international environment in the classroom.</p>
<p>Rankings have been published for Asia Pacific schools by the magazine <a href="/wiki/Asia_Inc." title="Asia Inc.">Asia Inc.</a> which is a regional business magazine with distribution worldwide. The importance of MBA education in China has risen, too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bangladesh was one of the first countries in Asia to offer MBA degree. There are now more than 50 business schools in Bangladesh offering the MBA, predominantly targeting graduates without any work experience. Most MBAs are two years full-time. There is little use of GMAT. The Business Schools conduct their own admission tests instead. Classes are taught in English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many business schools and colleges in India offering two-year MBA or PGDM programs accredited by AICTE or <a href="/wiki/University_Grants_Commission_(India)" title="University Grants Commission (India)">UGC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institutes_of_Management" title="Indian Institutes of Management">Indian Institutes of Management</a> are among the world's most selective schools according to Bloomberg magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> They offer a post graduate diploma in management rather than a degree because of an archaic Indian law which says that only universities can award degrees. There are 19 IIMS in total, 12 of which were established after the year 2010. The oldest 4 IIMs along with the <a href="/wiki/Indian_School_of_Business" title="Indian School of Business">Indian School of Business</a> constitute the top 5 B-Schools of the country according to the website mbaclub.in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Singapore is South East Asia's leading financial hub. Its competitive educational system starts from primary schools to universities and eventually post-graduate studies such as EMBA programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Japan 2 business schools offer the accredited MBA degree (AACSB, AMBA or EQUIS). The concept of an MBA is still not considered mainstream as traditional companies still perceive that knowledge and learning with respect to business and management can only be effectively gained through experience and not within a classroom. In fact, some companies have been known to place recent MBA recipients in unrelated fields, or try to re-acclimate their Japanese employees who have spent years overseas earning the degree. As a consequence, academic institutions in Japan are attempting to reinvent the perception of the MBA degree, by taking into account the local corporate culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pakistan first offered an MBA program outside the United States in 1955 in collaboration with the University of Pennsylvania. Now in Pakistan, there are 187 Universities/Institutes which are recognized by the Higher Education Commission of Pakistan, offering MBA programs to students and professionals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_schools_in_Australia" title="List of business schools in Australia">List of business schools in Australia</a></div>
<p>In Australia, 42 Australian business schools offer the MBA degree (16 are AACSB, AMBA or EQUIS accredited<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>). Universities differentiate themselves by gaining international accreditation and focusing on national and international rankings. Most MBAs are one to two years full-time. There is little use of GMAT, and instead each educational institution specifies its own requirements, which normally entails several years of management-level work experience as well as proven academic skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graduate_Management_Association_of_Australia" title="Graduate Management Association of Australia">Graduate Management Association of Australia</a> carries out ratings for Australian MBAs and annually publishes <a href="/wiki/Australian_MBA_Star_Ratings" title="Australian MBA Star Ratings">Australian MBA Star Ratings</a>. The Financial Review Boss carries out biennial rankings of Australian MBAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In New Zealand, most universities offer MBA classes, typically through part-time arrangement or evening classes. Only two universities offer full-time programs to international students - University of Otago (Otago MBA) and Auckland University of Technology (AUT). The Otago MBA is the longer established of the two, offering a 240 points program while AUT MBA is a 180-point program.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Korea">South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Korean universities offer full-time and part-time MBA programs that usually consist of a two-year curriculum. The first MBA program was offered in 1963 by Korea University Business School (KUBS). In 2007, the Korean Government established "BK21," a project that supports Korean universities in order to develop their competitiveness in the global MBA market. Korea University Business School topped the evaluation of BK21 professional business graduate schools for six consecutive years. In the meantime, only two universities in Korea ranked in the "2015 Global Top 100 Executive MBA (EMBA) Rankings" conducted by UK Financial Times (Korea University Business School and Yonsei University ranked 27th and 45th worldwide, respectively).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Program_rankings">Program rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Program rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_business_school_rankings" title="List of United States business school rankings">List of United States business school rankings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_MBA_Star_Ratings" title="Australian MBA Star Ratings">Australian MBA Star Ratings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rankings_of_business_schools_in_South_Africa" title="Rankings of business schools in South Africa">Rankings of business schools in South Africa</a></div>
<p>Since 1967, publications have ranked MBA programs using various methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatswrong_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatswrong-62">[62]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Gourman_Report" title="Gourman Report">Gourman Report</a></i> (1967–1997) did not disclose criteria or ranking methods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and these reports were criticized for reporting statistically impossible data, such as no ties among schools, narrow gaps in scores with no variation in gap widths, and ranks of nonexistent departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In 1977 <i>The Carter Report</i> ranked MBA programs based on the number of academic articles published by faculty, the <i>Ladd &amp; Lipset Survey</i> ranked business schools based on faculty surveys, and <i>MBA Magazine</i> ranked schools based on votes cast by business school deans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatswrong_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-whatswrong-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, publications such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_News_%26_World_Report" title="US News &amp; World Report">US News &amp; World Report</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_Week" title="Business Week">Business Week</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> publish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_school_rankings" title="Business school rankings">rankings</a> of MBA programs. Schools' ranks can vary across publications, as the methodologies for rankings differ among publications:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> incorporates responses from deans, program directors, and senior faculty about the academic quality of their programs as well as the opinions of hiring professionals. The ranking is calculated through a weighted formula of quality assessment (40%), placement success (35%), and student selectivity (25%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Business Week'</i>s rankings are based on student surveys, a survey of corporate recruiters, and an intellectual capital rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-BW-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Financial Times</i> uses survey responses from alumni who graduated three years prior to the ranking and information from business schools. Salary and employment statistics are weighted heavily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a>, published in <i>The Economist</i>, surveys both business schools (80%) and students and recent graduates (20%). Ranking criteria include GMAT scores, employment and salary statistics, class options, and student body demographics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>Wall Street Journal</i>, which stopped ranking full-time MBA programs in 2007, based its rankings on skill and behavioral development that may predict career success, such as social skills, teamwork orientation, ethics, and analytic and problem-solving abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_the_Rankings_were_Compiled_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-How_the_Rankings_were_Compiled-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Forbes</i> considers only the return of investment five years after graduation. MBA alumni are asked about their salary, the tuition fees of their MBA program and other direct costs as well as opportunity costs involved. Based on this data, a final "5-year gain" is calculated and determines the MBA ranking position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_.27The_Best_Business_Schools.27_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes_.27The_Best_Business_Schools.27-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Other rankings use nontraditional attributes:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Beyond Grey Pinstripes</i> ranking, published by the <a href="/wiki/Aspen_Institute" title="Aspen Institute">Aspen Institute</a>, is based on the integration of social and environmental stewardship into university curriculum and faculty research. Rankings are calculated on the amount of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> coursework made available to students (20%), amount of student exposure to relevant material (25%), amount of coursework focused on stewardship by for-profit corporations (30%), and relevant faculty research (25%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The 2011 survey and ranking include data from 150 universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Quacquarelli_Symonds" title="Quacquarelli Symonds">Quacquarelli Symonds</a> <i>QS Global 200 Business Schools Report</i> compiles regional rankings of business schools around the world. Ranks are calculated using a two-year moving average of points assigned by employers who hire MBA graduates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>Since 2005, the <i>UT-Dallas Top 100 Business School Research Rankings</i> ranks business schools on the research faculty publish, similar to <i>The Carter Report</i> of the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Does_The_World_Need_Another_B-School_Ranking.3F_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Does_The_World_Need_Another_B-School_Ranking.3F-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The ranking of MBA programs has been discussed in articles and on academic websites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Critics of ranking methodologies maintain that any published rankings should be viewed with caution for the following reasons:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MRN_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-MRN-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Rankings exhibit intentional selection bias as they limit the surveyed population to a small number of MBA programs and ignore the majority of schools, many with excellent offerings.</li>
<li>Ranking methods may be subject to personal biases and statistically flawed methodologies (especially methods relying on subjective interviews of hiring managers, students, and/or faculty).</li>
<li>Rankings use no objective measures of program quality.</li>
<li>The same list of schools appears in each ranking with some variation in ranks, so a school ranked as number 1 in one list may be number 17 in another list.</li>
<li>Rankings tend to concentrate on representing MBA schools themselves, but some schools offer MBA programs of different qualities and yet the ranking will only rely upon information from the full-time program (e.g., a school may use highly reputable faculty to teach a daytime program, but use adjunct faculty in its evening program or have drastically lower admissions criteria for its evening program than for its daytime program).</li>
<li>A high rank in a national publication tends to become a <a href="/wiki/Self-fulfilling_prophecy" title="Self-fulfilling prophecy">self-fulfilling prophecy</a>.</li>
<li>Some leading business schools including Harvard, INSEAD, Wharton and <a href="/wiki/MIT_Sloan_School_of_Management" title="MIT Sloan School of Management">Sloan</a> provide limited cooperation with certain ranking publications due to their perception that rankings are misused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>One study found that objectively ranking MBA programs by a combination of graduates' starting salaries and average student GMAT score can reasonably duplicate the top 20 list of the national publications, and concluded that a truly objective ranking would be individualized to the needs of each prospective student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MRN_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-MRN-76">[76]</a></sup> National publications have recognized the value of rankings against different criteria, and now offer lists ranked different ways: by salary, GMAT score of students, selectivity, and so forth. While useful, these rankings have yet to meet the critique that rankings are not tailored to individual needs, that they use an incomplete population of schools, may fail to distinguish between the different MBA program types offered by each school, or rely on subjective interviews.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Business_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The media raised questions about the value and content of business school programs after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2010">financial crisis of 2007–2010</a>. In general, graduates had reportedly tended to go into finance after receiving their degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrain_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-retrain-78">[78]</a></sup> As financial professionals are widely seen as responsible for the global economic meltdown, anecdotal evidence suggests new graduates are choosing different career paths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deans at top business schools have acknowledged that media and public perception of the MBA degree shifted as a result of the financial crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overhaul_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-overhaul-80">[80]</a></sup> Articles have been written about public perceptions of the crisis, ranging from schools' acknowledgment of issues with the training students receive<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrain_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-retrain-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overhaul_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-overhaul-80">[80]</a></sup> to criticisms of the MBA's role in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
