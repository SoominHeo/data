<p>A <b>lepton</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-integer_spin" title="Half-integer spin">half-integer spin</a> (spin <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>) particle that does not undergo <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Two main classes of leptons exist: <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charged</a> leptons (also known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>-like</i> leptons), and neutral leptons (better known as <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>). Charged leptons can combine with other particles to form various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_particle" title="Composite particle">composite particles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positronium" title="Positronium">positronium</a>, while neutrinos rarely interact with anything, and are consequently rarely observed. The best known of all leptons is the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>.</p>
<p>There are six types of leptons, known as <i><a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">flavours</a></i>, forming three <i><a href="/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics)" title="Generation (particle physics)">generations</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperphysicsLepton_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HyperphysicsLepton-2">[2]</a></sup> The first generation is the <i>electronic leptons</i>, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">electron neutrino</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
e</span></span>); the second is the <i>muonic leptons</i>, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>μ<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Muon_neutrino" title="Muon neutrino">muon neutrino</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
μ</span></span>); and the third is the <i>tauonic leptons</i>, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Tau_(particle)" title="Tau (particle)">tau</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>τ<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span>) and the <a href="/wiki/Tau_neutrino" title="Tau neutrino">tau neutrino</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
τ</span></span>). Electrons have the least mass of all the charged leptons. The heavier muons and taus will rapidly change into electrons and neutrinos through a process of <a href="/wiki/Particle_decay" title="Particle decay">particle decay</a>: the transformation from a higher mass state to a lower mass state. Thus electrons are stable and the most common charged lepton in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, whereas muons and taus can only be produced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_energy_physics" title="High energy physics">high energy</a> collisions (such as those involving <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> and those carried out in <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>).</p>
<p>Leptons have various intrinsic properties, including <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a> however, leptons are not subject to the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, but they are subject to the other three <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a> (excluding neutrinos, which are electrically neutral), and the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>.</p>
<p>For every lepton flavor there is a corresponding type of <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a>, known as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antilepton" title="Antilepton">antilepton</a>, that differs from the lepton only in that some of its properties have <a href="/wiki/Additive_inverse" title="Additive inverse">equal magnitude but opposite sign</a>. However, according to certain theories, neutrinos may be <a href="/wiki/Majorana_fermion" title="Majorana fermion">their own antiparticle</a>, but it is not currently known whether this is the case or not.</p>
<p>The first charged lepton, the electron, was theorized in the mid-19th century by several scientists<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-farrar_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-farrar-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arabatzis_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-arabatzis-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buchwald1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-buchwald1-5">[5]</a></sup> and was discovered in 1897 by <a href="/wiki/J._J._Thomson" title="J. J. Thomson">J. J. Thomson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-thomson-6">[6]</a></sup> The next lepton to be observed was the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a>, discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_D._Anderson" title="Carl D. Anderson">Carl D. Anderson</a> in 1936, which was classified as a <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">meson</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After investigation, it was realized that the muon did not have the expected properties of a meson, but rather behaved like an electron, only with higher mass. It took until 1947 for the concept of "leptons" as a family of particle to be proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAS-8">[8]</a></sup> The first neutrino, the electron neutrino, was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Wolfgang Pauli</a> in 1930 to explain certain characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAS-8">[8]</a></sup> It was first observed in the <a href="/wiki/Cowan%E2%80%93Reines_neutrino_experiment" title="Cowan–Reines neutrino experiment">Cowan–Reines neutrino experiment</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Cowan" title="Clyde Cowan">Clyde Cowan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Reines" title="Frederick Reines">Frederick Reines</a> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-LAS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The muon neutrino was discovered in 1962 by <a href="/wiki/Leon_M._Lederman" title="Leon M. Lederman">Leon M. Lederman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Schwartz" title="Melvin Schwartz">Melvin Schwartz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Steinberger" title="Jack Steinberger">Jack Steinberger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slac.stanford.edu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-slac.stanford.edu-10">[10]</a></sup> and the tau discovered between 1974 and 1977 by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lewis_Perl" title="Martin Lewis Perl">Martin Lewis Perl</a> and his colleagues from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanford_Linear_Accelerator_Center" title="Stanford Linear Accelerator Center">Stanford Linear Accelerator Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tau_neutrino" title="Tau neutrino">tau neutrino</a> remained elusive until July 2000, when the <a href="/wiki/DONUT" title="DONUT">DONUT collaboration</a> from <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a> announced its discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tauonpress_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tauonpress-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tauonpaper_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-tauonpaper-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leptons are an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>. Electrons are one of the components of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Exotic_atom" title="Exotic atom">Exotic atoms</a> with muons and taus instead of electrons can also be synthesized, as well as lepton–antilepton particles such as <a href="/wiki/Positronium" title="Positronium">positronium</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>lepton</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">λεπτός</span> <i>leptós</i>, "fine, small, thin" (<a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">neuter</a> nominative/accusative singular form: λεπτόν <i>leptón</i>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the earliest attested form of the word is the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a> <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐀩𐀡𐀵</span>, <i>re-po-to</i>, written in <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> syllabic script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>Lepton</i> was first used by physicist <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Rosenfeld" title="Léon Rosenfeld">Léon Rosenfeld</a> in 1948:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Following a suggestion of Prof. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._M%C3%B8ller" title="C. Møller">C. Møller</a>, I adopt — as a pendant to "nucleon" — the denomination "lepton" (from λεπτός, small, thin, delicate) to denote a particle of small mass.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The etymology incorrectly implies that all the leptons are of small mass. When Rosenfeld named them, the only known leptons were electrons and muons, whose masses are indeed small compared to nucleons — the mass of an electron (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999511000000000000♠</span>0.511 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass" title="Electronvolt">MeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electron_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Electron-18">[18]</a></sup> and the mass of a muon (with a value of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002105700000000000♠</span>105.7 MeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muon_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muon-19">[19]</a></sup> are fractions of the mass of the "heavy" proton (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002938300000000000♠</span>938.3 MeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proton_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proton-20">[20]</a></sup> However, the mass of the tau (discovered in the mid 1970s) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003177700000000000♠</span>1777 MeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tauon_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tauon-21">[21]</a></sup> is nearly twice that of the proton, and about 3,500 times that of the electron.</p>

<p>The first lepton identified was the electron, discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.J._Thomson" title="J.J. Thomson">J.J. Thomson</a> and his team of British physicists in 1897.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Then in 1930 <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Wolfgang Pauli</a> postulated the <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">electron neutrino</a> to preserve <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">conservation of momentum</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">conservation of angular momentum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Pauli theorized that an undetected particle was carrying away the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> of the initial and observed final particles. The electron neutrino was simply called the neutrino, as it was not yet known that neutrinos came in different flavours (or different "generations").</p>
<p>Nearly 40 years after the discovery of the electron, the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a> was discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_D._Anderson" title="Carl D. Anderson">Carl D. Anderson</a> in 1936. Due to its mass, it was initially categorized as a <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">meson</a> rather than a lepton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It later became clear that the muon was much more similar to the electron than to mesons, as muons do not undergo the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, and thus the muon was reclassified: electrons, muons, and the (electron) neutrino were grouped into a new group of particles – the leptons. In 1962, <a href="/wiki/Leon_M._Lederman" title="Leon M. Lederman">Leon M. Lederman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Schwartz" title="Melvin Schwartz">Melvin Schwartz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Steinberger" title="Jack Steinberger">Jack Steinberger</a> showed that more than one type of neutrino exists by first detecting interactions of the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a> neutrino, which earned them the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">1988 Nobel Prize</a>, although by then the different flavours of neutrino had already been theorized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tau_(particle)" title="Tau (particle)">tau</a> was first detected in a series of experiments between 1974 and 1977 by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lewis_Perl" title="Martin Lewis Perl">Martin Lewis Perl</a> with his colleagues at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLAC" title="SLAC">SLAC</a> <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">LBL group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Like the electron and the muon, it too was expected to have an associated neutrino. The first evidence for tau neutrinos came from the observation of "missing" energy and momentum in tau decay, analogous to the "missing" energy and momentum in beta decay leading to the discovery of the electron neutrino. The first detection of tau neutrino interactions was announced in 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/DONUT" title="DONUT">DONUT</a> collaboration at <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a>, making it the latest particle of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> to have been directly observed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obs_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-obs-28">[28]</a></sup> apart from the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>, which probably has been discovered in 2012.</p>
<p>Although all present data is consistent with three generations of leptons, some particle physicists are searching for a fourth generation. The current lower limit on the mass of such a fourth charged lepton is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002100800000000000♠</span>100.8 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass" title="Electronvolt">GeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> while its associated neutrino would have a mass of at least <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001450000000000000♠</span>45.0 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass" title="Electronvolt">GeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin_and_chirality">Spin and chirality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spin and chirality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Right_left_helicity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="380" height="63" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/200px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/300px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/400px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Right_left_helicity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Left-handed and right-handed helicities</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Leptons are <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>-<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> particles. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin-statistics_theorem" title="Spin-statistics theorem">spin-statistics theorem</a> thus implies that they are <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> and thus that they are subject to the <a href="/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle" title="Pauli exclusion principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>: No two leptons of the same species can be in exactly the same state at the same time. Furthermore, it means that a lepton can have only two possible spin states, namely up or down.</p>
<p>A closely related property is <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(physics)" title="Chirality (physics)">chirality</a>, which in turn is closely related to a more easily visualized property called <a href="/wiki/Helicity_(particle_physics)" title="Helicity (particle physics)">helicity</a>. The helicity of a particle is the direction of its spin relative to its <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>; particles with spin in the same direction as their momentum are called <i>right-handed</i> and otherwise they are called <i>left-handed</i>. When a particle is massless, the direction of its momentum relative to its spin is frame independent, while for massive particles it is possible to 'overtake' the particle by a <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformation" title="Lorentz transformation">Lorentz transformation</a> flipping the helicity. Chirality is a technical property (defined through the transformation behaviour under the <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_group" title="Poincaré group">Poincaré group</a>) that agrees with helicity for (approximately) massless particles and is still well defined for massive particles.</p>
<p>In many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theories" title="Quantum field theories">quantum field theories</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">quantum electrodynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a>, left- and right-handed fermions are identical. However, in the Standard Model, left-handed and right-handed fermions are treated asymmetrically. Only left-handed fermions participate in the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, while there are no right-handed neutrinos. This is an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parity_violation" title="Parity violation">parity violation</a>. In the literature, left-handed fields are often denoted by a capital <i>L</i> subscript (e.g. <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span><sub>L</sub>) and right-handed fields are denoted by a capital <i>R</i> subscript.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetic_interaction">Electromagnetic interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electromagnetic interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="300" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg/220px-Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg/330px-Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg/440px-Lepton-interaction-vertex-eeg.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>One of the most prominent properties of leptons is their <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, <i>Q</i>. The electric charge determines the strength of their electromagnetic interactions. It determines the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> generated by the particle (see <a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb's law">Coulomb's law</a>) and how strongly the particle reacts to an external electric or magnetic field (see <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a>). Each generation contains one lepton with <i>Q</i> = −<i>e</i> (conventionally the charge of a particle is expressed in units of the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary charge</a>) and one lepton with zero electric charge. The lepton with electric charge is commonly simply referred to as a 'charged lepton' while the neutral lepton is called a neutrino. For example, the first generation consists of the electron <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span> with a negative electric charge and the electrically neutral electron neutrino <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
e</span></span>.</p>
<p>In the language of quantum field theory, the electromagnetic interaction of the charged leptons is expressed by the fact that the particles interact with the quantum of the electromagnetic field, the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Feynman_diagram" title="Feynman diagram">Feynman diagram</a> of the electron-photon interaction is shown on the right.</p>

<p>where <i>m</i> is the mass of the lepton and <i>g</i> is the so-called <a href="/wiki/G-factor_(physics)" title="G-factor (physics)">g-factor</a> for the lepton. First order approximation quantum mechanics predicts that the g-factor is 2 for all leptons. However, higher order quantum effects caused by loops in Feynman diagrams introduce corrections to this value. These corrections, referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment" title="Anomalous magnetic dipole moment">anomalous magnetic dipole moment</a>, are very sensitive to the details of a quantum field theory model and thus provide the opportunity for precision tests of the standard model. The theoretical and measured values for the electron anomalous magnetic dipole moment are within agreement within eight significant figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the Standard Model, the left-handed charged lepton and the left-handed neutrino are arranged in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doublet_(physics)" title="Doublet (physics)">doublet</a> <span class="nowrap">(<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
e</span></span><sub>L</sub>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span><sub>L</sub>)</span> that transforms in the <a href="/wiki/Spinor" title="Spinor">spinor</a> representation (<i>T</i> = <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/Weak_isospin" title="Weak isospin">weak isospin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU(2)" title="SU(2)">SU(2)</a> gauge symmetry. This means that these particles are eigenstates of the isospin projection <i>T</i><sub>3</sub> with eigenvalues <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> and −<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> respectively. In the meantime, the right-handed charged lepton transforms as a weak isospin scalar (<i>T</i> = 0) and thus does not participate in the weak interaction, while there is no evidence that a right-handed neutrino exists at all.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a> recombines the gauge fields of the weak isospin SU(2) and the <a href="/wiki/Weak_hypercharge" title="Weak hypercharge">weak hypercharge</a> U(1) symmetries to three massive vector bosons (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">0<br/></span></span>) mediating the weak interaction, and one massless vector boson, the photon, responsible for the electromagnetic interaction. The electric charge <i>Q</i> can be calculated from the isospin projection <i>T</i><sub>3</sub> and weak hypercharge <i>Y</i><sub>W</sub> through the <a href="/wiki/Gell-Mann%E2%80%93Nishijima_formula" title="Gell-Mann–Nishijima formula">Gell-Mann–Nishijima formula</a>,</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Q</i> = <i>T</i><sub>3</sub> + <i>Y</i><sub>W</sub>/2</dd>
</dl>
<p>To recover the observed electric charges for all particles, the left-handed weak isospin doublet <span class="nowrap">(<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>ν<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
e</span></span><sub>L</sub>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span><sub>L</sub>)</span> must thus have <i>Y</i><sub>W</sub> = −1, while the right-handed isospin scalar e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
R</span> must have <i>Y</i><sub>W</sub> = −2. The interaction of the leptons with the massive weak interaction vector bosons is shown in the figure on the left.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass">Mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, each lepton starts out with no intrinsic mass. The charged leptons (i.e. the electron, muon, and tau) obtain an effective mass through interaction with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higgs_field" title="Higgs field">Higgs field</a>, but the neutrinos remain massless. For technical reasons, the masslessness of the neutrinos implies that there is no mixing of the different generations of charged leptons as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CKM_matrix" title="CKM matrix">there is for quarks</a>. This is in close agreement with current experimental observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, it is known from experiments – most prominently from observed <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation" title="Neutrino oscillation">neutrino oscillations</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> – that neutrinos do in fact have some very small mass, probably less than <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000200000000000000♠</span>2 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass" title="Electronvolt">eV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neutrino_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neutrino-34">[34]</a></sup> This implies the existence of physics <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Beyond the Standard Model">beyond the Standard Model</a>. The currently most favoured extension is the so-called <a href="/wiki/Seesaw_mechanism" title="Seesaw mechanism">seesaw mechanism</a>, which would explain both why the left-handed neutrinos are so light compared to the corresponding charged leptons, and why we have not yet seen any right-handed neutrinos.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leptonic_numbers">Leptonic numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Leptonic numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lepton_number" title="Lepton number">Lepton number</a></div>
<p>The members of each generation's <a href="/wiki/Weak_isospin" title="Weak isospin">weak isospin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doublet_(physics)" title="Doublet (physics)">doublet</a> are assigned <a href="/wiki/Lepton_number" title="Lepton number">leptonic numbers</a> that are conserved under the Standard Model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartinShaw_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-MartinShaw-35">[35]</a></sup> Electrons and electron neutrinos have an <i>electronic number</i> of <i>L</i><sub>e</sub> = 1, while muons and muon neutrinos have a <i>muonic number</i> of <i>L</i><sub>μ</sub> = 1, while tau particles and tau neutrinos have a <i>tauonic number</i> of <i>L</i><sub>τ</sub> = 1. The antileptons have their respective generation's leptonic numbers of −1.</p>
<p>Conservation of the leptonic numbers means that the number of leptons of the same type remains the same, when particles interact. Such a violation is considered to be smoking gun evidence for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Beyond the Standard Model">physics beyond the Standard Model</a>. A much stronger conservation law is the conservation of the total number of leptons (<i>L</i>), conserved even in the case of neutrino oscillations, but even it is still violated by a tiny amount by the <a href="/wiki/Chiral_anomaly" title="Chiral anomaly">chiral anomaly</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Universality">Universality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lepton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Universality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
