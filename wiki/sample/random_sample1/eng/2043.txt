<p>The <b>Sutta Pitaka</b> (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">suttapiṭaka</span>; or Suttanta Pitaka; cf Sanskrit <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">सूत्र पिटक</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Sūtra Piṭaka</span>) is the second of the three divisions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tripitaka" title="Tripitaka">Tripitaka</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pali_Canon" title="Pali Canon">Pali Canon</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="Pāli">Pali</a> collection of <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_texts" title="Buddhist texts">Buddhist writings</a> of <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. The Sutta Pitaka contains more than 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Sutra" title="Sutra">suttas</a> (teachings) attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> or his close companions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scriptures tell how the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_councils" title="Buddhist councils">First Council</a> held shortly after the Buddha's death collected together the discipline (vinaya), and the <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a> in five collections. Tradition holds that little was added to the Canon after this. Scholars are more skeptical, but differ in their degrees of skepticism. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gombrich" title="Richard Gombrich">Richard Gombrich</a> thinks most of the first four nikayas (see below) go back to the Buddha, in content but not in form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The late Professor Hirakawa Akira says<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> that the First Council collected only short prose passages or verses expressing important doctrines, and that these were expanded into full length suttas over the next century.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_suttas" title="List of suttas">List of suttas</a></div>
<p>There are five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikaya" title="Nikaya">nikayas</a> (collections) of suttas:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Digha_Nikaya" title="Digha Nikaya">Digha Nikaya</a> (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">dīghanikāya</span>), the "long" discourses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Majjhima_Nikaya" title="Majjhima Nikaya">Majjhima Nikaya</a>, the "middle-length" discourses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samyutta_Nikaya" title="Samyutta Nikaya">Samyutta Nikaya</a> (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">saṃyutta-</span>), the "connected" discourses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anguttara_Nikaya" title="Anguttara Nikaya">Anguttara Nikaya</a> (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">aṅguttara-</span>), the "numerical" discourses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Khuddaka_Nikaya" title="Khuddaka Nikaya">Khuddaka Nikaya</a>, the "minor collection".</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digha_Nikaya">Digha Nikaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Digha Nikaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Digha_Nikaya" title="Digha Nikaya">Digha Nikaya</a></div>
<p>This includes <i>The Greater Discourse on the Foundations of Mindfulness</i>, <i>The Fruits of the Contemplative Life</i>, and <i>The Buddha's Last Days</i>. There are 34 long suttas in this nikaya.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Majjhima_Nikaya">Majjhima Nikaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Majjhima Nikaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Majjhima_Nikaya" title="Majjhima Nikaya">Majjhima Nikaya</a></div>
<p>This includes <i>Shorter Exposition of Kamma</i>, <i>Mindfulness of Breathing</i>, and <i>Mindfulness of the Body</i>. There are 152 medium-length suttas in this nikaya.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Samyutta_Nikaya">Samyutta Nikaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Samyutta Nikaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Samyutta_Nikaya" title="Samyutta Nikaya">Samyutta Nikaya</a></div>
<p>There are, according to one reckoning, 2,889, but according to the commentary 7,762, shorter suttas in this Nikaya.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anguttara_Nikaya">Anguttara Nikaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anguttara Nikaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anguttara_Nikaya" title="Anguttara Nikaya">Anguttara Nikaya</a></div>
<p>These teachings are arranged numerically. It includes, according to the commentary's reckoning, 9,565 short suttas grouped by number from ones to elevens. According to <a href="/wiki/Damien_Keown" title="Damien Keown">Keown</a>, "there is considerable disparity between the Pāli and the Sarvāstivādin versions, with more than two-thirds of the sūtras found in one but not the other compilation, which suggests that much of this portion of the Sūtra Piṭaka was not formed until a fairly late date."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Khuddaka_Nikaya">Khuddaka Nikaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sutta_Pitaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Khuddaka Nikaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Khuddaka_Nikaya" title="Khuddaka Nikaya">Khuddaka Nikaya</a></div>
<p>This is a heterogeneous mix of sermons, doctrines, and poetry attributed to the Buddha and his disciples. The contents vary somewhat between editions. The Thai edition includes 1-15 below, the Sinhalese edition 1-17 and the Burmese edition 1-18.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Niddesa" title="Niddesa">Niddesa</a></li>
