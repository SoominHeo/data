<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Police procedural</h1>

<p>The <b>police procedural</b>, or <b>police crime drama</b>, is a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a> that attempts to convincingly depict the activities of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_force" title="Police force">police force</a> as they investigate <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crimes</a>. Although traditional detective novels usually concentrate on a single crime, police <a href="/wiki/Procedural_drama" title="Procedural drama">procedurals</a> frequently depict investigations into several unrelated crimes in a single story. Traditional mysteries usually adhere to the convention of having the criminal's identity concealed until the <a href="/wiki/Climax_(narrative)" title="Climax (narrative)">climax</a> (the so-called <a href="/wiki/Whodunit" title="Whodunit">whodunit</a>), whereas in police procedurals, the perpetrator's identity is often known to the audience from the outset (the <a href="/wiki/Inverted_detective_story" title="Inverted detective story">inverted detective story</a>). Police procedurals depict a number of police-related topics such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensics" title="Forensics">forensics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsies</a>, the gathering of <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a>, the use of <a href="/wiki/Search_warrant" title="Search warrant">search warrants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interrogation" title="Interrogation">interrogation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The roots of the police procedural have been traced to at least the mid-1880s. <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moonstone" title="The Moonstone">The Moonstone</a></i> (1868), a tale of a <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a> detective investigating the theft of a valuable diamond, has been described as perhaps the earliest clear example of the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Treat" title="Lawrence Treat">Lawrence Treat</a>'s 1945 novel <i>V as in Victim</i> is often cited, by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Boucher" title="Anthony Boucher">Anthony Boucher</a> (mystery critic for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> Book Review</i>) among others, as perhaps the first true police procedural. Another early example is <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Waugh" title="Hillary Waugh">Hillary Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Seen_Wearing_..._(Hillary_Waugh_novel)" title="Last Seen Wearing ... (Hillary Waugh novel)">Last Seen Wearing ...</a></i>, 1952. Even earlier examples from the 20th Century, predating Treat, include the novels <i>Vultures in the Dark</i>, 1925, and <i>The Borrowed Shield</i>, 1925, by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Enright" title="Richard Enright">Richard Enright</a>, retired <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Commissioner" title="New York City Police Commissioner">New York City Police Commissioner</a>, <i>Harness Bull</i>, 1937, and <i>Homicide</i>, 1937, by former Southern California police officer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leslie_T._White&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leslie T. White (page does not exist)">Leslie T. White</a>, <i>P.C. Richardson's First Case</i>, 1933, by Sir <a href="/wiki/Basil_Thomson" title="Basil Thomson">Basil Thomson</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assistant_Commissioner" title="Assistant Commissioner">Assistant Commissioner</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a>, and the short story collection <i>Policeman's Lot</i>, 1933, by former Buckinghamshire High Sheriff and Justice of the Peace <a href="/wiki/Sir_Henry_Aubrey-Fletcher,_6th_Baronet" title="Sir Henry Aubrey-Fletcher, 6th Baronet">Henry Wade</a>.</p>
<p>The procedural became more prominent after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and, while the contributions of <a href="/wiki/Novelist" title="Novelist">novelists</a> like Treat were significant, a large part of the impetus for the post-war development of the procedural as a distinct subgenre of the mystery was due, not to prose fiction, but to the popularity of a number of American films which dramatized and fictionalized actual crimes. Dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Semidocumentary" title="Semidocumentary">semidocumentary</a> films" by movie critics, these motion pictures, often filmed on location, with the cooperation of the law enforcement agencies involved in the actual case, made a point of authentically depicting police work. Examples include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_City" title="The Naked City">The Naked City</a></i> (1948), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Street_with_No_Name" title="The Street with No Name">The Street with No Name</a></i> (1948), <i><a href="/wiki/T-Men" title="T-Men">T-Men</a></i> (1947), <i><a href="/wiki/He_Walked_by_Night" title="He Walked by Night">He Walked by Night</a></i> (1948), and <i><a href="/wiki/Border_Incident" title="Border Incident">Border Incident</a></i> (1949).</p>
<p>Films from other countries soon began following the semidocumentary trend. In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, there was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quai_des_orfevres" title="Quai des orfevres">Quai des orfevres</a></i> (1947), released in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> as <i>Jenny Lamour</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Japan" title="Cinema of Japan">Japanese cinema</a>, there was <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a>'s 1949 film <i><a href="/wiki/Stray_Dog_(film)" title="Stray Dog (film)">Stray Dog</a></i>, a serious police procedural <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a> that was also a precursor to the <a href="/wiki/Buddy_cop_film" title="Buddy cop film">buddy cop film</a> genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>, there were films such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Lamp" title="The Blue Lamp">The Blue Lamp</a></i> (1950) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Arm_(film)" title="The Long Arm (film)">The Long Arm</a></i> (1956) set in London and depicting the Metropolitan Police.</p>
<p>One semidocumentary, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/He_Walked_By_Night" title="He Walked By Night">He Walked By Night</a></i> (1948), released by <a href="/wiki/Eagle-Lion_Films" title="Eagle-Lion Films">Eagle-Lion Films</a>, featured a young radio actor named <a href="/wiki/Jack_Webb" title="Jack Webb">Jack Webb</a> in a supporting role. The success of the film, along with a suggestion from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">LAPD</a> Detective <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">Sergeant</a> Marty Wynn, the film's <a href="/wiki/Technical_advisor" title="Technical advisor">technical advisor</a>, gave Webb an idea for a <a href="/wiki/Radio_drama" title="Radio drama">radio drama</a> that depicted police work in a similarly semidocumentary manner. The resulting series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragnet_(drama)" title="Dragnet (drama)">Dragnet</a></i>, which debuted on radio in 1949 and made the transition to television in 1951, has been called "the most famous procedural of all time" by mystery novelists <a href="/wiki/William_L._DeAndrea" title="William L. DeAndrea">William L. DeAndrea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_V._Forrest" title="Katherine V. Forrest">Katherine V. Forrest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Allan_Collins" title="Max Allan Collins">Max Allan Collins</a>.</p>
<p>The same year that <i>Dragnet</i> debuted on radio, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning playwright <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Kingsley" title="Sidney Kingsley">Sidney Kingsley</a>'s stage play <i><a href="/wiki/Detective_Story_(play)" title="Detective Story (play)">Detective Story</a></i> opened on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a>. This frank, carefully researched dramatization of a typical day in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYPD" title="NYPD">NYPD</a> <a href="/wiki/Police_station" title="Police station">precinct</a> detective squad became another benchmark in the development of the police procedural.</p>
<p>Over the next few years, the number of novelists who picked up on the procedural trend grew to include writers like Ben Benson, who wrote carefully researched novels about the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_State_Police" title="Massachusetts State Police">Massachusetts State Police</a>, retired police officer <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Procter" title="Maurice Procter">Maurice Procter</a>, who wrote a series about North England cop Harry Martineau, and Jonathan Craig, who wrote short stories and novels about <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> police officers. Police novels by writers who would come to virtually define the form, like <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Waugh" title="Hillary Waugh">Hillary Waugh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evan_Hunter" title="Evan Hunter">Ed McBain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Creasey" title="John Creasey">John Creasey</a> started to appear regularly.</p>
<p>In 1956, in his regular <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="New York Times Book Review">New York Times Book Review</a></i> column, mystery critic <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Boucher" title="Anthony Boucher">Anthony Boucher</a>, noting the growing popularity of crime fiction in which the main emphasis was the realistic depiction of police work, suggested that such stories constituted a distinct subgenre of the mystery, and, crediting the success of <i>Dragnet</i> for the rise of this new form, coined the phrase "police procedural" to describe it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_stories">Written stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Written stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ed_McBain">Ed McBain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ed McBain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ed McBain, the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evan_Hunter" title="Evan Hunter">Evan Hunter</a>, wrote dozens of novels in the <i><a href="/wiki/87th_Precinct" title="87th Precinct">87th Precinct</a></i> series beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Cop_Hater" title="Cop Hater">Cop Hater</a>,</i> published in 1956. Hunter continued to write 87th Precinct novels almost until his death in 2005. Although these novels focus primarily on Detective Steve Carella, they encompass the work of many officers working alone and in teams, and Carella is not always present in any individual book.</p>
<p>As if to illustrate the universality of the police procedural, many of McBain's 87th Precinct novels, despite their being set in a slightly fictionalized New York City, have been filmed in settings outside New York, even outside the US. <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a>'s 1963 film, <i><a href="/wiki/High_and_Low_(1963_film)" title="High and Low (1963 film)">High and Low</a></i>, based on McBain's <i>King's Ransom</i> (1959), is set in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. <i>Without Apparent Motive</i> (1972), set on the <a href="/wiki/French_Riviera" title="French Riviera">French Riviera</a>, is based on McBain's <i>Ten Plus One</i> (1963). <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chabrol" title="Claude Chabrol">Claude Chabrol</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Relatives" title="Blood Relatives">Les Liens de Sang</a></i> (1978), based on <i>Blood Relatives</i> (1974), is set in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>. Even <i><a href="/wiki/Fuzz_(film)" title="Fuzz (film)">Fuzz</a></i> (1972), based on the 1968 novel, though set in the US, moves the action to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Creasey.2FJ._J._Marric">John Creasey/J. J. Marric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: John Creasey/J. J. Marric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps ranking just behind McBain in importance to the development of the procedural as a distinct mystery subgenre is <a href="/wiki/John_Creasey" title="John Creasey">John Creasey</a>, a prolific writer of many different kinds of crime fiction, from espionage to criminal protagonist. He was inspired to write a more realistic crime novel when his neighbor, a retired <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a> detective, challenged Creasey to "write about us as we are." The result was <i>Inspector West Takes Charge</i>, 1940, the first of more than forty novels to feature Roger West of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Metropolitan_Police" title="London Metropolitan Police">London Metropolitan Police</a>. The West novels were, for the era, an unusually realistic look at Scotland Yard operations, but the plots were often wildly melodramatic, and, to get around thorny legal problems, Creasey gave West an "amateur detective" friend who was able to perform the extra-procedural acts that West, as a policeman, could not.</p>
<p>In the mid-1950s, inspired by the success of television's <i>Dragnet</i> and a similar British TV series, <i><a href="/wiki/Fabian_of_the_Yard" title="Fabian of the Yard">Fabian of the Yard</a></i>, Creasey decided to try a more down-to-earth series of cop stories. Adopting the pseudonym "J.J. Marric", he wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Gideon%27s_Day" title="Gideon's Day">Gideon's Day</a></i>, 1955, in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gideon" title="George Gideon">George Gideon</a>, a high-ranking detective at Scotland Yard, spends a busy day supervising his subordinates' investigations into several unrelated crimes. This novel was the first in a series of more than twenty books which brought Creasey his best critical notices. One entry, <i>Gideon's Fire</i>, 1961, won an <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Award" title="Edgar Award">Edgar Award</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Mystery_Writers_of_America" title="Mystery Writers of America">Mystery Writers of America</a> for Best Mystery Novel. The Gideon series, more than any other source, helped establish the common procedural plot structure of threading several autonomous story lines through a single novel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sj.C3.B6wall_and_Wahl.C3.B6.C3.B6">Sjöwall and Wahlöö</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sjöwall and Wahlöö">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maj_Sj%C3%B6wall" title="Maj Sjöwall">Maj Sjöwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Per_Wahl%C3%B6%C3%B6" title="Per Wahlöö">Per Wahlöö</a> planned and wrote the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Beck" title="Martin Beck">Martin Beck</a> police procedural series of ten books between the 1960s and 1970s, set in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. The series is particularly renowned for its extensive character development throughout the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Beck himself is gradually promoted from <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">detective</a> in a newly nationalised Swedish police force to <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_staff" title="Chief of staff">Chief</a> <a href="/wiki/Inspector" title="Inspector">Inspector</a> of the National Murder Squad, and the realistic depiction, as well as criticism of the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> at the time whilst the tedium of the police procedural continues in the background, is something still widely used today, with authors such as <a href="/wiki/Jo_Nesb%C3%B8" title="Jo Nesbø">Jo Nesbø</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stieg_Larsson" title="Stieg Larsson">Stieg Larsson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The books gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_noir" title="Scandinavian noir">Swedish noir</a> scene, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Laughing_Policeman_(novel)" title="The Laughing Policeman (novel)">The Laughing Policeman</a></i> earned a "Best Novel" Edgar Award from the Mystery Writers of America in 1971. The books were translated from Swedish into 35 different languages, and have sold roughly ten million copies. Sjöwall and Wahlöö used <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">black humour</a> extensively in the series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and it is widely recognised as one of the finest police procedural series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elizabeth_Linington.2FDell_Shannon.2FLesley_Egan">Elizabeth Linington/Dell Shannon/Lesley Egan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elizabeth Linington/Dell Shannon/Lesley Egan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A prolific author of police procedurals, whose work has fallen out of fashion in the years since her death, is <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Linington" title="Elizabeth Linington">Elizabeth Linington</a> writing under her own name, as well as "Dell Shannon" and "Lesley Egan." Linington reserved her Dell Shannon pseudonym primarily for procedurals featuring LAPD Central <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">Homicide</a> <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> Luis Mendoza (1960–86). Under her own name she wrote about Sergeant Ivor Maddox of LAPD's North Hollywood Station, and as Lesley Egan she wrote about suburban cop Vic Varallo. These novels are often considered severely flawed, partly due to the author's far-right political viewpoint (she was a proud member of the <a href="/wiki/John_Birch_Society" title="John Birch Society">John Birch Society</a>), but primarily because Miss Linington's books, notwithstanding the frequent comments she made about the depth of her research, were all seriously deficient in the single element most identified with the police procedural, technical accuracy. However, they have a certain charm in their depiction of a kinder, gentler California, where the police were always "good guys" who solved all the crimes and respected the citizenry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Georges_Simenon">Georges Simenon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Georges Simenon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inspector_Maigret" title="Inspector Maigret">Inspector Maigret</a></i> novels of <a href="/wiki/Georges_Simenon" title="Georges Simenon">Georges Simenon</a> aren't really procedurals because of their strong focus on the lead character, but the novels have always included subordinate members of his staff as supporting characters. More importantly, Simenon, who had been a journalist covering police investigations before creating Maigret, was giving an accurate depiction, or at least the appearance of an accurate depiction, of law enforcement in Paris. Further, Simenon's influence on later European procedural writers, like <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maj_Sj%C3%B6wall_and_Per_Wahl%C3%B6%C3%B6" title="Maj Sjöwall and Per Wahlöö">Maj Sjöwall and Per Wahlöö</a>, or <a href="/wiki/A._C._Baantjer" title="A. C. Baantjer">Baantjer</a>, is obvious.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joseph_Wambaugh">Joseph Wambaugh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Joseph Wambaugh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though not the first police officer to write procedurals, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wambaugh" title="Joseph Wambaugh">Joseph Wambaugh</a>'s success has caused him to become the exemplar of cops who turn their professional experiences into fiction. The son of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, policeman, Wambaugh joined the Los Angeles Police Department after a stint of military duty. In 1970, his first novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Centurions" title="The New Centurions">The New Centurions</a></i>, was published. This followed three police officers through their training in the Academy, their first few years on the street, culminating in the Watts riots of 1965. It was followed by such novels as <i>The Blue Knight</i>, 1971, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Choirboys_(book)" title="The Choirboys (book)">The Choirboys</a></i>, 1975, <i>Hollywood Station</i>, 2006, and acclaimed non-fiction books like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Onion_Field" title="The Onion Field">The Onion Field</a></i>, 1973, <i>Lines and Shadows</i>, 1984, and <i>Fire Lover</i>, 2002. Wambaugh has said that his main purpose is less to show how cops work on the job, than how the job works on cops.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tony_Hillerman">Tony Hillerman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tony Hillerman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hillerman" title="Tony Hillerman">Tony Hillerman</a>, the author of 17 novels involving Jim Chee and Joe Leaphorn, wrote procedurals in which the procedures were those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_Tribal_Police" title="Navajo Tribal Police">Navajo Tribal Police</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detective_novel_writers">Detective novel writers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Detective novel writers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is difficult to disentangle the early roots of the procedural from its forebear, the traditional detective novel, which often featured a police officer as protagonist. By and large, the better known novelists such as <a href="/wiki/Ngaio_Marsh" title="Ngaio Marsh">Ngaio Marsh</a> produced work that falls more squarely into the province of the traditional or "cozy" detective novel. Nevertheless, some of the work of authors less well known today, like <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Wills_Crofts" title="Freeman Wills Crofts">Freeman Wills Crofts</a>'s novels about Inspector French or some of the work of the prolific team of <a href="/wiki/G._D._H._Cole" title="G. D. H. Cole">G.D.H.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Cole" title="Margaret Cole">Margaret Cole</a>, might be considered as the antecedents of today's police procedural. British mystery novelist and critic <a href="/wiki/Julian_Symons" title="Julian Symons">Julian Symons</a>, in his 1972 history of crime fiction, <i>Bloody Murder</i>, labeled these proto-procedurals "humdrums," because of their emphasis on the plodding nature of the investigators.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="TV_series">TV series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: TV series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>See: <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television program</a></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragnet_(series)#Television" title="Dragnet (series)">Dragnet</a></i> (1951–59, 1967–70, 1989–91 and 2003–04) was a pioneering police procedural that began on radio in 1949 and then on television in 1951. <i>Dragnet</i> established the tone of many police dramas in subsequent decades, and the rigorously authentic depictions of such elements as organizational structure, professional jargon, legal issues, etc., set the standard for technical accuracy that became the most identifiable element of the police procedural in all media. The show was occasionally accused of presenting an overly idealized portrait of law enforcement in which the police (represented by Sgt. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Friday" title="Joe Friday">Joe Friday</a>) were invariably presented as "good guys" and the criminals as "bad guys", with little moral flexibility or complexity between the two. However, many episodes depicted sympathetic perpetrators while others depicted unsympathetic or corrupt cops. Further, though Jack Webb may have seemed to go to extremes to depict the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">Los Angeles Police Department</a> in a favorable light, most depictions of cops at the time of <i>Dragnet'</i>s debut were both unsympathetic and unrealistic. Webb's depiction was meant to offer balance. Also, the show benefited from the unprecedented technical advice, involvement, and support of the LAPD, a first in TV, which may also have been an incentive to depict the Department favorably. After the success of <i>Dragnet</i>, Webb produced other procedural shows like <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_DA%27s_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The DA's Man (page does not exist)">The DA's Man</a></i>, about an undercover investigator for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_County_District_Attorney" title="New York County District Attorney">Manhattan District Attorney</a>'s Office, <i><a href="/wiki/Adam-12" title="Adam-12">Adam-12</a></i>, about a pair of uniformed <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">LAPD</a> officers patrolling their beat in a radio car, and <i><a href="/wiki/O%27Hara,_U.S._Treasury" title="O'Hara, U.S. Treasury">O'Hara, U.S. Treasury</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/David_Janssen" title="David Janssen">David Janssen</a> as a trouble-shooting federal officer.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Untouchables (1959 TV series)">The Untouchables</a></i> (1959–63) fictionalized real-life Federal Agent <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Ness" title="Eliot Ness">Eliot Ness</a>'s ongoing fight with prohibition-era gangs in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> and elsewhere. Originally a two-part presentation on the anthology series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desilu_Playhouse" title="Desilu Playhouse">Desilu Playhouse</a></i>, it made such a splash that a series was launched the following fall. That two-part pilot, later released to theaters under the title <i>The Scarface Mob</i>, stuck comparatively close to the actual events, with Ness, as played by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stack" title="Robert Stack">Robert Stack</a>, recruiting a team of incorruptible investigators to help bring down <a href="/wiki/Al_Capone" title="Al Capone">Al Capone</a>. Later episodes showed Ness and his squad, after Capone, going after just about every big name gangster of the era, and when the writers ran out of real-life figures to pit against Ness, they created new ones. <a href="/wiki/Quinn_Martin" title="Quinn Martin">Quinn Martin</a>, who would become closely associated with police and crime shows like this, produced the series during its first season, leaving to found his own company, QM Productions, which would go one to produce police procedural shows like <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Breed_(TV_series)" title="The New Breed (TV series)">The New Breed</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_F.B.I._(TV_series)" title="The F.B.I. (TV series)">The F.B.I.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dan_August" title="Dan August">Dan August</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Streets_of_San_Francisco" title="The Streets of San Francisco">The Streets of San Francisco</a></i> over the next twenty years. The success of the series led to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>-winning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(1987_film)" title="The Untouchables (1987 film)">motion picture</a> in 1987, and a new TV <a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(1993_TV_series)" title="The Untouchables (1993 TV series)">series</a> that was syndicated to local stations in 1993.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Police_Story_(1973_TV_series)" title="Police Story (1973 TV series)">Police Story</a></i> (1973–78) was an anthology series set in Los Angeles created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LAPD" title="LAPD">LAPD</a> Detective Sergeant Joseph Wambaugh. Hard-hitting and unflinchingly realistic, its anthology format made it possible to look at LAPD police work from many different perspectives, what it was like to be a woman in a male-dominated profession, an honest cop suspected of corruption, a rookie cop, an undercover narc, a veteran facing retirement, or a cop who had to adjust to crippling injuries incurred in the line of duty. Despite its anthology format, there were a number of characters who appeared in more than one episode, including Robbery/Homicide partners Tony Calabrese (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Lo_Bianco" title="Tony Lo Bianco">Tony Lo Bianco</a>) and Bert Jameson (<a href="/wiki/Don_Meredith" title="Don Meredith">Don Meredith</a>), vice cop turned homicide detective Charlie Czonka (<a href="/wiki/James_Farentino" title="James Farentino">James Farentino</a>), and stakeout-surveillance specialist Joe LaFrieda (<a href="/wiki/Vic_Morrow" title="Vic Morrow">Vic Morrow</a>). Several series were spun off from the show, including <i><a href="/wiki/Police_Woman_(TV_series)" title="Police Woman (TV series)">Police Woman</a></i>, <i>Joe Forrester</i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Cassidy:_Man_Under_Cover" title="David Cassidy: Man Under Cover">Man Undercover</a></i>. During its last two seasons, the show appeared as an irregular series of two-hour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_movies" title="TV movies">TV movies</a> rather than a weekly one-hour program. The show was revived for a season in 1988, using old scripts reshot with new casts when a writers' strike made new material inaccessible.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kojak" title="Kojak">Kojak</a></i> (1973–78, 1989–90) created by <a href="/wiki/Abby_Mann" title="Abby Mann">Abby Mann</a>, focused on a veteran <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> detective-lieutenant played by <a href="/wiki/Telly_Savalas" title="Telly Savalas">Telly Savalas</a>. Its exteriors were filmed at New York's Ninth Precinct, the same place where <i><a href="/wiki/NYPD_Blue" title="NYPD Blue">NYPD Blue</a>'</i>s exteriors would be filmed. In 1989 Savalas returned to the role briefly for five two-hour episodes, in which Kojak had been promoted to <a href="/wiki/Inspector" title="Inspector">inspector</a> and placed in charge of the Major Crimes Squad. It rotated with three other detective shows on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>. A 2005 remake for the <a href="/wiki/USA_Network" title="USA Network">USA Network</a> starred <a href="/wiki/Ving_Rhames" title="Ving Rhames">Ving Rhames</a>. Kojak's most memorable character trait was his signature lollipop.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Hill_Street_Blues" title="Hill Street Blues">Hill Street Blues</a></i> (1981–87) featured a number of intertwined storylines in each episode, and pioneered depiction of the conflicts between the work and private lives of officers and detectives on which the police procedural was centered. The show had a deliberate "documentary" style, depicting officers who were flawed and human, and dealt openly with the gray areas of morality between right and wrong. It was set in an unidentified east coast or Midwestern US city. The show was written by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Bochco" title="Steven Bochco">Steven Bochco</a> and Michael Kozoll.</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cagney_and_Lacey" title="Cagney and Lacey">Cagney and Lacey</a></i> (1982–88) revolved around two female NYPD detectives who led very different lives. Christine Cagney, played by <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Gless" title="Sharon Gless">Sharon Gless</a>, was a single-minded, witty, brash career woman. Mary Beth Lacey was a resourceful, sensitive working mom. <a href="/wiki/Loretta_Swit" title="Loretta Swit">Loretta Swit</a> was the original choice for Cagney [she played the role in a TV movie] however she couldn't get out of her contract on <i><a href="/wiki/M*A*S*H_(TV_series)" title="M*A*S*H (TV series)">M*A*S*H</a></i>. During the first season, <a href="/wiki/Meg_Foster" title="Meg Foster">Meg Foster</a> played the part of Cagney, while <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Daly" title="Tyne Daly">Tyne Daly</a> played Lacey, the role she'd originated in the pilot. <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> canceled the series claiming low ratings. It was brought back due both to a letter-writing campaign which drew millions of letters nationwide and because the ratings went <i>up</i> during summer reruns. A <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> magazine read "Welcome Back". Daly continued as Lacey, but Foster was replaced with Gless, who would become the actress most identified with the part. It had 36 nominations and 14 wins during its run. Four TV movies were broadcast after the series ended.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Miami_Vice" title="Miami Vice">Miami Vice</a></i> (1984–90) and <i><a href="/wiki/21_Jump_Street" title="21 Jump Street">21 Jump Street</a></i> (1987–91) showed the MTV style of Police procedurals.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order_(franchise)" title="Law &amp; Order (franchise)"><i>Law &amp; Order</i> franchise</a>, which started with the long-running series <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i> (1990–2010), focuses on the two 'halves' of a criminal proceeding in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> criminal justice system: the investigation of the crime by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department" title="New York City Police Department">New York City Police Department</a> homicide detectives and the subsequent prosecution of the criminals by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_County_District_Attorney" title="New York County District Attorney">New York County District Attorney</a>'s office. <li><i><a href="/wiki/Homicide:_Life_on_the_Street" title="Homicide: Life on the Street">Homicide: Life on the Street</a></i> (1993–99; TV movie in 2000), a police procedural focusing on the homicide unit of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> city police department. Critically praised<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li><i><a href="/wiki/NYPD_Blue" title="NYPD Blue">NYPD Blue</a></i> (1993–2005) explored the internal and external struggles of the assorted investigators of the fictional 15th Precinct of Manhattan. The show gained notoriety for profanity and nudity never previously broadcast on American network television. <i>NYPD Blue</i> was created by genre veteran <a href="/wiki/Steven_Bochco" title="Steven Bochco">Steven Bochco</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Milch" title="David Milch">David Milch</a>. The cast of <i>NYPD Blue</i> included actor Dennis Franz, who previously played Detective Buntz on <i>Hill Street Blues</i>, as well as on a spin-off series, <i>Beverly Hills Buntz</i>. Another cast member, David Caruso, would later play Lt. Horatio Caine on <i>CSI: Miami</i>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/CSI_(franchise)" title="CSI (franchise)">CSI franchise</a> which started with <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_Crime_Scene_Investigation" title="CSI: Crime Scene Investigation">CSI: Crime Scene Investigation</a></i> (2000–2015) and eventually spawned three spin-offs focused on solving ordinary crimes using forensics, <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_Miami" title="CSI: Miami">CSI: Miami</a></i> (2002-2012) and <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_NY" title="CSI: NY">CSI: NY</a></i> (2004-2013). <li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">The Shield</a></i> (2002–08) is about an experimental division of the Los Angeles Police Department set up in the fictional Farmington district ("the Farm") of Los Angeles, using a converted church ("the Barn") as their police station, and featuring a group of detectives called "The Strike Team", who will do anything to bring justice to the streets. Michael Chiklis (Chiklis previously played the title character in the TV series <i>The Commish</i>) has top billing with his portrayal of Strike Team leader Detective Victor "Vic" Mackey. The show has an ensemble cast that will normally run a number of separate story lines through each episode. It was on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FX_network" title="FX network">FX network</a> and was known for its portrayal of police brutality and its realism. The show inspired other shows similar to <i>The Shield</i> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Blue_(TV_series)" title="Dark Blue (TV series)">Dark Blue</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Southland_(TV_series)" title="Southland (TV series)">Southland</a></i>. <i>The Shield</i> was created by writer/producer <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Ryan" title="Shawn Ryan">Shawn Ryan</a>.</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wire_(TV_series)" title="The Wire (TV series)">The Wire</a></i> (2002–08), a television drama series set and produced in and around Baltimore, Maryland, created and primarily written by author and former police reporter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Simon_(writer)" title="David Simon (writer)">David Simon</a>. Simon has stated that he originally set out to create a police drama loosely based on the experiences of his writing partner <a href="/wiki/Ed_Burns" title="Ed Burns">Ed Burns</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">homicide</a> <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">detective</a>. Burns, when working on protracted investigations of violent drug dealers using <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a> technology, had often been frustrated by the bureaucracy of the Baltimore police department; Simon saw similarities with his own ordeals as a police reporter for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Baltimore_Sun" title="The Baltimore Sun">The Baltimore Sun</a></i>. The show is realistic in depicting the processes of both police work and criminal activity, with many of the plot points based on the experiences of Simon and Burns. Despite only receiving modest ratings and never winning major television awards, <i>The Wire</i> has been described by many critics as the greatest television series ever made and one of the most accomplished works of fiction of the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metascore1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-metascore1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>

NCIS deals with the caseload of the Major Case Response Team, while <i>NCIS: Los Angeles</i> deals with special undercover assignments and <i>NCIS New Orleans</i> focuses on a small group of agents who handle cases from the Mississippi River to the Texas Panhandle.</li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Fabian_of_the_Yard" title="Fabian of the Yard">Fabian of the Yard</a></i>, (1954–55) – possibly the first police drama to be made for British TV, this series, based on the memoirs of real-life Scotland Yard detective Robert Fabian, had a lot in common with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragnet_(series)" title="Dragnet (series)">Dragnet</a></i>. Just as <i>Dragnet</i> had been the first network drama series with continuing characters to be shot on film, so <i>Fabian of the Yard</i> was one of the first British series to be filmed. Both shows featured voice-over narration by the main character; both fictionalized stories derived from real-life cases; and both ended with an epilogue that revealed the ultimate fate of the criminals. On <i>Fabian</i>, this took the form of a medium-shot of Bruce Seton, who played Fabian in the series, seated at a desk. The shot slowly dissolved into one of the real-life Fabian in the same pose at the same desk. At that point, the actual Fabian stood up and told the audience what happened to the criminal he'd caught in the real-life case that had just been dramatized.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dixon_of_Dock_Green" title="Dixon of Dock Green">Dixon of Dock Green</a></i>, (1955–76) – <a href="/wiki/Jack_Warner_(actor)" title="Jack Warner (actor)">Jack Warner</a> reprised the role of <a href="/wiki/Constable" title="Constable">Constable</a> George Dixon, the uniformed beat cop he had played in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Lamp" title="The Blue Lamp">The Blue Lamp</a></i>, despite the fact that the Dixon character had been tragically murdered in that film. During the course of this somewhat gentle series, Warner's character became, for many, the living embodiment of what every British "bobby" was supposed to be. As the series progressed, Dixon went through several promotions, eventually winding up as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Station_Sergeant" title="Station Sergeant">Station Sergeant</a> at his local division. By the final season, with Warner now over 80, Dixon retired and the focus shifted to the younger officers he'd trained up over the years.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/No_Hiding_Place" title="No Hiding Place">No Hiding Place</a></i>, (1957–67) – Produced with the cooperation of Scotland Yard, this long-running series featured Raymond Francis as high-ranking Met detective Tom Lockhart. During its run, the series went through several title changes. When it began in 1957, it was known as <i>Murder Bag</i>, referring to the <a href="/wiki/Murder_bag" title="Murder bag">bag of investigative tools</a> that <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_(police)" title="Superintendent (police)">Superintendent</a> Lockhart carried with him whenever he was called to a case. In 1959, with Lockhart promoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Superintendent" title="Chief Superintendent">Chief Superintendent</a>, it became <i>Crime Sheet</i>. Later in 1959, the series was given its final and best-remembered title, <i>No Hiding Place</i>, which lasted until the series ended in 1967.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Z-Cars" title="Z-Cars">Z-Cars</a></i>, (1962–78) – a police drama about two teams of uniformed constables (<a href="/wiki/Brian_Blessed" title="Brian Blessed">Brian Blessed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brady_(actor)" title="Joseph Brady (actor)">Joseph Brady</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Ellis_(actor)" title="James Ellis (actor)">James Ellis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Kemp" title="Jeremy Kemp">Jeremy Kemp</a>) assigned to "Crime Patrol" duties in a pair of powerful <a href="/wiki/Ford_Zephyr" title="Ford Zephyr">Ford Zephyrs</a>, under the supervision of Detective Sergeant John Watt (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Windsor" title="Frank Windsor">Frank Windsor</a>) and Detective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Inspector" title="Chief Inspector">Chief Inspector</a> Charlie Barlow (<a href="/wiki/Stratford_Johns" title="Stratford Johns">Stratford Johns</a>). A franker, and often less flattering portrait of police work than audience were used to seeing on <i>Dixon of Dock Green</i>, the show was an immediate hit, its popularity generating spin-offs like <i><a href="/wiki/Softly,_Softly_(TV_series)" title="Softly, Softly (TV series)">Softly, Softly</a></i> (1966–76), <i><a href="/wiki/Barlow_at_Large" title="Barlow at Large">Barlow at Large</a></i> (1971–75), and <i><a href="/wiki/Second_Verdict" title="Second Verdict">Second Verdict</a></i> (1976).</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_strips_and_books">Comic strips and books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Police_procedural&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Comic strips and books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strip</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dick_Tracy" title="Dick Tracy">Dick Tracy</a></i> is often pointed to as an early procedural. Indeed, in his introduction to a 1970 collection of <i>Tracy</i> strips entitled <i>The Celebrated Cases of Dick Tracy</i>, no less an authority than <a href="/wiki/Ellery_Queen" title="Ellery Queen">Ellery Queen</a> suggested that Tracy, predating Webb, Treat, Creasey, and McBain, was the first "truly" procedural policeman in any fictional medium.</p>
<p>Certainly <i>Tracy</i> creator <a href="/wiki/Chester_Gould" title="Chester Gould">Chester Gould</a> seemed to be trying reflect the real world. Tracy himself, conceived by Gould as a "modern-day <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a>", was partly modeled on real-life law enforcer Eliot Ness, and his first, and most frequently recurring, antagonist, the <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_%22Big_Boy%22_Caprice" title='Alphonse "Big Boy" Caprice'>Big Boy</a>, was based on Ness's real-life nemesis Al Capone. Other members of Tracy's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recurring_characters_in_Dick_Tracy" title="Recurring characters in Dick Tracy">Rogues Gallery</a>, like Boris Arson, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flattop_Jones" title="Flattop Jones">Flattop Jones</a>, and Maw Famon, were inspired, respectively, by <a href="/wiki/John_Dillinger" title="John Dillinger">John Dillinger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_%22Pretty_Boy%22_Floyd" title='Charles "Pretty Boy" Floyd'>Charles "Pretty Boy" Floyd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ma_Barker" title="Ma Barker">Kate "Ma" Barker</a>.</p>
<p>More to the point, Gould was making a genuine attempt to portray police work realistically. Once <i>Tracy</i> was sold to the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> syndicate, Gould enrolled in a criminology class at <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>, met with members of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Police_Department" title="Chicago Police Department">Chicago Police Department</a>, and did research at the Department's crime lab, to make his depiction of law enforcement more authentic. Ultimately, he hired retired <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> policeman Al Valanis, a pioneering forensic sketch artist, as both an artistic assistant and police technical advisor.</p>
<p>Later stories, in which Gould veered into <a href="/wiki/Space_opera" title="Space opera">space opera</a> and extraterrestrial contacts, mitigated somewhat against the strip's being recognized for its early use of realistic police procedure, but any examination of the <i>Tracy</i> strip from its beginnings in 1931 through the 1950s makes Gould's status as a pioneer in the police procedural subgenre clear.</p>
<p>The success of <i>Tracy</i> led to many more police strips. While some, like Norman Marsh's <i><a href="/wiki/Dan_Dunn" title="Dan Dunn">Dan Dunn</a></i> were unabashedly slavish imitations of <i>Tracy</i>, others, like <a href="/wiki/Dashiell_Hammett" title="Dashiell Hammett">Dashiell Hammett</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Raymond" title="Alex Raymond">Alex Raymond</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Agent_X-9" title="Secret Agent X-9">Secret Agent X-9</a></i>, took a more original approach. Still others, like Eddie Sullivan's and Charlie Schmidt's <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Patrol" title="Radio Patrol">Radio Patrol</a></i> and Will Gould's <i>Red Barry</i>, steered a middle course. One of the best post-<i>Tracy</i> procedural comics was <i><a href="/wiki/Kerry_Drake" title="Kerry Drake">Kerry Drake</a></i>, written and created by Allen Saunders and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Andriola" title="Alfred Andriola">Alfred Andriola</a>. It diverged from the metropolitan settings used in <i>Tracy</i> to tell the story of the titular Chief Investigator for the District Attorney of a small-town jurisdiction. Later, following a personal tragedy, he leaves the DA's Office and joins his small city's police force in order to fight crime closer to the grass roots level. As both a DA's man and a city cop, he fights a string of flamboyant, Gould-ian criminals like "Stitches", "Bottleneck", and "Bulldozer."</p>
<p>Other syndicated police strips include <a href="/wiki/Zane_Grey" title="Zane Grey">Zane Grey</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Royal_Mounted" title="King of the Royal Mounted">King of the Royal Mounted</a></i>, depicting police work in the contemporary Canadian Northwest, Lank Leonard's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mickey_Finn_(comics)" title="Mickey Finn (comics)">Mickey Finn</a></i>, which emphasized the home life of a hard-working cop, and <i>Dragnet</i>, which adapted stories from the pioneering radio-TV series into comics. Early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_books" title="Comic books">comic books</a> with police themes tended to be reprints of syndicated newspaper strips like <i>Tracy</i> and <i>Drake</i>. Others adapted police stories from other mediums, like the radio-inspired anthology comic <i><a href="/wiki/Gang_Busters" title="Gang Busters">Gang Busters</a></i>, Dell's <i>87th Precinct</i> issues, which adapted McBain's novels, or <i>The Untouchables</i>, which adapted the fictionalized TV adventures of real-life policeman Eliot Ness.</p>
<p>More recently, there have been attempts to depict police work with the kind of hard-edged realism seen in the novels of writers like Wambaugh, such as <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel's</a> four-issue mini-series <i>Cops: The Job</i>, in which a rookie police officer learns to cope with the physical, emotional, and mental stresses of law enforcement during her first patrol assignment. With <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superheroes</a> having long dominated the comic book market, there have been some recent attempts to integrate elements of the police procedural into the universe of costumed crime-fighters. <i><a href="/wiki/Gotham_Central" title="Gotham Central">Gotham Central</a></i>, for example, depicts a group of police detectives operating in <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a>, and suggested that the caped crimefighter is disliked by many Gotham detectives for treading on their toes. Meanwhile, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolis_SCU&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Metropolis SCU (page does not exist)">Metropolis SCU</a></i> tells the story of the Special Crimes Unit, an elite squad of cops in the police force serving <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(comics)" title="Metropolis (comics)">Metropolis</a>.</p>
<p>The use of police procedural elements in superhero comics can partly be attributed to the success of <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Busiek" title="Kurt Busiek">Kurt Busiek</a>'s groundbreaking 1994 series <i><a href="/wiki/Marvels" title="Marvels">Marvels</a></i>, and his subsequent <i><a href="/wiki/Astro_City" title="Astro City">Astro City</a></i> work, both of which examine the typical superhero universe from the viewpoint of the common man who witnesses the great dramas from afar, participating in them tangentially at best.</p>
<p>In the wake of Busiek's success, many other writers mimicked his approach, with mixed results – the narrative possibilities of someone who does not get involved in drama are limited. In 2000, however, <a href="/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a> published the first issue of <a href="/wiki/Brian_Michael_Bendis" title="Brian Michael Bendis">Brian Michael Bendis</a>'s comic <i><a href="/wiki/Powers_(comics)" title="Powers (comics)">Powers</a></i>, which followed the lives of homicide detectives as they investigated superhero-related cases. Bendis's success has led both Marvel Comics and <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> to begin their own superhero-themed police procedurals (<i><a href="/wiki/District_X" title="District X">District X</a></i> and the aforementioned <i>Gotham Central</i>), which focus on how the job of a police officer is affected by such tropes as secret identities, superhuman abilities, costumes, and the near-constant presence of <a href="/wiki/Vigilante" title="Vigilante">vigilantes</a>.</p>
<p>While the detectives in <i>Powers</i> were "normal" (unpowered) humans dealing with super-powered crime, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Ha" title="Gene Ha">Gene Ha</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Top_10_(comics)" title="Top 10 (comics)">Top 10</a></i> mini-series, published by <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Best_Comics" title="America's Best Comics">America's Best Comics</a> in 2000–01, centered around the super-powered police force in a setting where powers are omnipresent. The comic detailed the lives and work of the police force of Neopolis, a city in which everyone, from the police and criminals to civilians, children and even pets, has super-powers, colourful costumes and secret identities.</p>
