<p>An <b>experience point</b> (often abbreviated to <b>Exp</b> or <b>XP</b>) is a unit of measurement used in many <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a> (RPGs) and <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> to quantify a <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a>'s progression through the game. Experience points are generally awarded for the completion of quests, overcoming obstacles and opponents, and for successful <a href="/wiki/Role-playing" title="Role-playing">role-playing</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In many RPGs, characters start as fairly weak and untrained. When a sufficient amount of experience is obtained, the character "levels up", achieving the next stage of character development. Such an event usually increases the character's statistics, such as maximum <a href="/wiki/Health_(gaming)" title="Health (gaming)">health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magic_(gaming)" title="Magic (gaming)">magic</a> and strength, and may permit the character to acquire new abilities or improve existing ones. Leveling up may also give the character access to more areas or items.</p>
<p>In some role-playing games, particularly those derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>, experience points are used to improve characters in discrete <a href="#Level-based_progression">experience levels</a>; in other games, such as <i><a href="/wiki/GURPS" title="GURPS">GURPS</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Darkness" title="World of Darkness">World of Darkness</a></i> games, experience points are spent on specific abilities or attributes chosen by the player.</p>
<p>In most games, as the difficulty of the challenge increases, the experience rewarded for overcoming it also increases. As players gain more experience points, the amount of experience needed to gain new abilities typically increases. Alternatively, games keep the amount of experience points per level constant, but progressively lower the experience gained for the same tasks as the character's level increases. Thus, as the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a> strengthens from gaining experience, they are encouraged to accept tasks that are commensurate with their improved abilities in order to advance.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Level-based_progression">Level-based progression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Level-based progression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Level_(video_gaming)" title="Level (video gaming)">Level (video gaming)</a>.</div>
<p>In games derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> (<i>D&amp;D</i>), an accumulation of a sufficient number of experience points (XP) increases a character's "level", a number that represents a character's overall skill and experience. To "level" or "level up" means to gain enough XP to reach the next level. By gaining a level, a character's abilities or stats will increase, making the character stronger and able to accomplish more difficult tasks, including safely battling stronger enemies, gaining access to more powerful abilities (such as spells or combat techniques), and to make, fix or disable more complex mechanical devices, or resolve increasingly difficult social challenges.</p>
<p>Typically levels are associated with a <a href="/wiki/Character_class" title="Character class">character class</a>, and many systems will allow combinations of classes, allowing a player to customize how their character develops.</p>
<p>Some systems that use a level-based experience system also incorporate the ability to purchase specific traits with a set amount of experience; for example, <i>D&amp;D</i> 3rd Edition bases the creation of <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magical</a> items around a system of experience expenditure (known as <i>burning xp</i>) and also uses a system of feat selection which closely matches the advantages of systems such as <a href="/wiki/GURPS" title="GURPS">GURPS</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Hero_System" title="Hero System">Hero System</a>. The d20 system also introduced the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prestige_class" title="Prestige class">prestige classes</a> which bundle sets of mechanics, character development and requirements into a package which can be "leveled" like an ordinary class.</p>
<p>Some games have a level cap, or a limit of levels available. For example, in the online game <i><a href="/wiki/RuneScape" title="RuneScape">RuneScape</a></i>, no player can currently get higher than level 99 which needs a combined 13,034,431 experience points to gain, nor can any one skill gain more than 200 million experience. Some games have a dynamic level cap, where the level cap is dependent upon the levels of the average player (so it gradually increases).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activity-based_progression">Activity-based progression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Activity-based progression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some systems, such as the classic tabletop role-playing games <i><a href="/wiki/Traveller_(role-playing_game)" title="Traveller (role-playing game)">Traveller</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Cthulhu_(role-playing_game)" title="Call of Cthulhu (role-playing game)">Call of Cthulhu</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Basic_Role-Playing" title="Basic Role-Playing">Basic Role-Playing</a></i>, and the role-playing video games <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_II" title="Final Fantasy II">Final Fantasy II</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elder_Scrolls" title="The Elder Scrolls">The Elder Scrolls</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/SaGa_(series)" title="SaGa (series)">SaGa</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPGFan-Romancing-Saga_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RPGFan-Romancing-Saga-2">[2]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Grandia_(series)" title="Grandia (series)">Grandia</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> series progression is based on increasing individual <a href="/wiki/Statistic_(role-playing_games)" title="Statistic (role-playing games)">statistics</a> (skills, rank and other features) of the character, and is not driven by the acquisition of (general) experience points. The skills and attributes are made to grow through exercised use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free-form_advancement">Free-form advancement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Free-form advancement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Character_point" title="Character point">Character point</a></div>
<p>Free-form advancement is used by many role-playing systems including <i><a href="/wiki/GURPS" title="GURPS">GURPS</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Hero_System" title="Hero System">Hero</a> or the <a href="/wiki/World_of_Darkness" title="World of Darkness">World of Darkness</a> series. It allows the player to select which skills to advance by allocating "points". Each character attribute is assigned a price to improve, so for example it might cost a character 2 points to raise an <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a> skill one notch, 10 points to raise overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dexterity" title="Dexterity">dexterity</a> by one, or it might cost 20 points to learn a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spell_(gaming)" title="Spell (gaming)">magic spell</a>.</p>
<p>Players are typically free to spend points however they choose, which greatly increases the control that a player has over the character's development, but also usually causes players to find that complexity increases as well. Some games therefore simplify character creation and advancement by suggesting <i>packages</i> or <i>templates</i> of pre-selected ability sets, so for example a player could have their character become an "investigator" by purchasing a package deal which includes many skills and abilities, rather than buying them each separately.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cash-in_advancement">Cash-in advancement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cash-in advancement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Cash-in Experience advancement system uses experience points to "purchase" such character advancements as Class Levels, Skill Points, new skills, feats or increasing saving throw bonuses or base attribute points each of which has a set cost in experience points with set limits on the maximum bonuses that can be purchased at a given time usually once per game session. Once experience points are used thus they are "spent" and are erased from the character record or marked as spent and cannot be used again. <a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_XIII" title="Final Fantasy XIII">Final Fantasy XIII</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minecraft" title="Minecraft">Minecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dice_%26_Glory" title="Dice &amp; Glory">Dice &amp; Glory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Warhammer_Fantasy_Roleplay" title="Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay">Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay</a> are examples of games that use a cash-in advancement system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_systems">Hybrid systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hybrid systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some games use advancement systems which combine elements from two or more of the above types. For example, in the third edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>, whenever a level is gained in a character class, it provides a number of skill points (the exact number is calculated based on the class and the character's intelligence statistic), which can be spent to raise various skills. Character level (generally the sum of a character's total levels in all classes) is used to calculate how high skills can be raised, when an ability score can be raised and when a character can gain new feats (a class of special abilities which include special attacks, proficiencies in various weapons and bonuses on the dice rolls used to determine the outcome of various actions) and how many experience points are needed to advance in level. In <a href="/wiki/Ragnarok_Online" title="Ragnarok Online">Ragnarok Online</a>, experience points are divided into two categories: base experience and job experience. Gaining base experience increases a character's base level, which is used to calculate a character's maximum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hit_point" title="Hit point">HP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_point" title="Magic point">SP</a>, increasing base level also provides points which can be spent to increase stats such as strength, agility and intelligence. Gaining job experience increases a character's job level, each job level provides a skill point which can be spent in the job's skill tree to gain a new ability, such as a spell, special attack or passive bonus, or improve an existing ability.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since many early <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> are derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-4">[4]</a></sup> most use a level-based experience system.</p>
<p>In many games, characters must obtain a minimum level to perform certain actions, such as wielding a particular weapon, entering a restricted area, or earning the respect of a <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player character</a>. Some games use a system of "character levels", where higher-level characters hold an absolute advantage over those of lower level. In these games, statistical character management is usually kept to a minimum. Other games use a system of "skill levels" to measure advantages in terms of specific aptitudes, such as weapon handling, spell-casting proficiency, and stealthiness. These games allow the players to customize their characters to a greater extent.</p>
<p>Some games, particularly among <a href="/wiki/MUD" title="MUD">MUDs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massively_multi-player_online_role-playing_game" title="Massively multi-player online role-playing game">MMORPGs</a>, place a limit on the experience a character gains from a single encounter or challenge, to reduce the effectiveness of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerleveling" title="Powerleveling">power-leveling</a>.</p>
<p>Remorting is another technique that, while encouraging power-leveling, alleviates its ill effects by giving the player a sense of achievement as it maintains balance with other characters of lower level within the game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perks">Perks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Perks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skill_tree" title="Skill tree">Skill tree</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power-up" title="Power-up">Power-up</a></div>
<p>"Perks" are special bonuses that <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> players can add to their characters to give special abilities. The term refers to the general usage of "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perk" title="wiktionary:perk">perk</a>" as an abbreviation of "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perquisite" title="wiktionary:perquisite">perquisite</a>". Perks are a variation of the <a href="/wiki/Power-up" title="Power-up">power-up</a> mechanic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_cod4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-1up_cod4-5">[5]</a></sup> but are permanent rather than temporary and are progressively unlocked through experience points.</p>
<p>The concept of permanent power-ups that are progressively unlocked dates back to the early <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">NES</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action RPGs</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Deadly_Towers" title="Deadly Towers">Deadly Towers</a></i> (1986) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rygar_(Nintendo_Entertainment_System)" title="Rygar (Nintendo Entertainment System)">Rygar</a></i> (1987), which blurred the line between the power-ups used in <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">action-adventures</a> and the experience points used in <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The first video game to use the term "perks" to refer to such a mechanic was possibly the 1997 role-playing video game <i><a href="/wiki/Fallout_(video_game)" title="Fallout (video game)">Fallout</a></i>. Perks have been used in various other video games in recent times, including <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooters</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty_4:_Modern_Warfare" title="Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare">Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare</a></i> (2007),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_cod4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-1up_cod4-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2" title="Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2">Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</a></i> (2009), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Killing_Floor_(2009_video_game)" title="Killing Floor (2009 video game)">Killing Floor</a></i> (2009), as well as <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action games</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_Online" title="Metal Gear Online">Metal Gear Online</a></i> (2008).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remorting">Remorting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Remorting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Remorting" (also known as "rebirth", "ascending/ascension", "reincarnating", or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_game_plus" title="New game plus">new game plus</a>") is a game mechanic in some role-playing games whereby, once the player character reaches a specified level limit, the player can elect to start over with a new version of his or her character. The bonuses that are given are dependent on several factors, which generally involve the stats of the character before the reincarnation occurs. The remorting character generally loses all levels, but gains an advantage that was previously unavailable, usually access to different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(fantasy)" title="Race (fantasy)">races</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avatar_(virtual_reality)" title="Avatar (virtual reality)">avatars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Character_class" title="Character class">classes</a>, skills, or otherwise inaccessible play areas within the game. A symbol often identifies a remorted character.</p>
<p>The term "remort" comes from <a href="/wiki/MUD" title="MUD">MUDs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in some of which players may become <a href="/wiki/Immortal_(MUD)" title="Immortal (MUD)">immortal</a> characters—administrative staff—simply by advancing to the maximum level. These users are generally expected to distance themselves from gameplay, and interaction with players may be severely limited. When an immortal chooses to vacate his or her position to resume playing the game—usually from level one just as with any new character—he or she is said to have remorted, "becoming mortal again".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ywwr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ywwr-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A MUD called <i>Arcane Nites</i>, formerly <i>Nitemare</i>, claims to have created the first remort system and coined the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arcanenites_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-arcanenites-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grinding">Grinding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grinding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grinding_(video_gaming)" title="Grinding (video gaming)">Grinding (video gaming)</a></div>
<p>Grinding refers to the process of repeating one specific activity over and over. This is done, for example, by repeatedly participating in challenges, quests, tasks and events which reward experience points for performing repetitive, often menial challenges. This definition can also be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_games#Multiplayer" title="Computer games">multi-player games</a>, but it is typically displaced by a much more charged meaning. A term intended to describe this style of play without pejorative connotation is <a href="/wiki/Optimization_(role-playing_games)" title="Optimization (role-playing games)">optimization</a>, also known as "XP farming".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power-leveling"><span id="Power-Leveling"></span> Power-leveling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Power-leveling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Power-leveling is using the help of another, stronger player to level a character more quickly than is possible alone.</p>
<p>One kind of power-leveling refers to an experienced character <a href="/wiki/Twinking#RPGs" title="Twinking">assisting a character of much lower power</a> (who is often controlled by the experienced character's controller as an assistant character or improved future main character in training) in defeating enemies that would normally be too powerful for the inexperienced character but are easily and quickly killed by the more powerful character or, in games in which experience points or "credits" are distributed in proportion to group members' levels, weakened almost to the point of death by the more powerful character, who is not grouped with the inexperienced character. In the latter case, once an enemy is weakened to the point at which the lower-level character can safely finish the kill (usually with the more powerful character's use of healing spells/effects as a backup), the more powerful character uses a spell or effect to stop battle or, if the lower-level character's skills permit, allows itself to be "rescued" by the lower-level character, who then finishes the kill and gets all of the associated experience points. Defeating high-level enemies rewards the lower level character with more experience points than it could otherwise achieve.</p>
<p>A second kind of power-leveling refers to a player paying a company or individual to play and level-up their character. The customer provides the company with the username and password for their account, and the company assigns an employee to play the character for the customer until a desired level is reached. This is against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terms_of_Service" title="Terms of Service">Terms of Service</a> of many games and, if caught, may result in the character being banned. There are also risks involved, as an unscrupulous service may "steal" the character, for later resale to another customer.</p>
<p>Power-leveling increased in <i><a href="/wiki/EverQuest" title="EverQuest">EverQuest</a></i> as it became more common to sell characters through the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Techniques of <a href="/wiki/Kill_stealing" title="Kill stealing">kill stealing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Powergaming" title="Powergaming">power-gaming</a> would make this pursuit considerably more attractive.</p>
<p>To combat power-leveling and leeching, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_designer" title="Game designer">game designers</a> have devised better means of rewarding a player based on their actual contribution to the completion of the task. Another method used is to cap how much experience a character can gain at any single moment. For example, the game might not allow a character to gain more than 20% of the experience they need to level up by defeating an enemy. This is controversial in that it also punishes players who are skilled enough to face challenges more difficult than regular players or that band together with other players to face more difficult challenges. Another anti-power-leveling method, popularized through widespread adoption of the CircleMUD code base, is to distribute experience points from an enemy across a party <i><a href="/wiki/Pro_rata" title="Pro rata">pro rata</a></i> by level, such that each party member gains a fraction of the enemy's experience points corresponding to the fraction of a party's total level ownership possessed by that character. For example, after any given battle, a level-30 character in a party would earn twice as much experience as would a level-15 character. Power-levelers sometimes circumvent this provision by what could be called "passive power-leveling", where a high level character who has access to healing abilities does not formally join the lower-level character's party, instead <i>a</i>) healing and/or "powering up" the lower-level character, <i>b</i>) targeting the enemy with spells or effects that do not involve joining the battle, and/or <i>c</i>) fighting alongside the lower-level character until the enemy is nearly defeated and then pausing the battle (<i>e.g.</i>, through use of "calm" or a similar command) and allowing the lower-level character to resume, finish, and claim all the experience points for itself. Finally, power-levelling may be rendered more difficult by having very large jumps between experience points required for each subsequent level of experience. It is common practice to have experience needed increasing in a non-linear way relative to experience levels to push players to the next town or land, but it can also reduce the opportunities for power-levelling as a player would be forced to find a different power-levelling technique for every couple of experience levels and move and do that technique.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leeching">Leeching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Leeching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some online games, it is possible to join a group and gain experience, loot or other rewards, while providing little or no contribution to the group. This type of behaviour is referred to as leeching, particularly when it is done without the permission of other group members. In games which allow players to gain rewards by <a href="/wiki/Kill_stealing" title="Kill stealing">kill stealing</a>, this is also considered a form of leeching. This is extremely common in games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Defenders" title="Dungeon Defenders">Dungeon Defenders</a></i>, in which all players receive the same rewards regardless of their contributions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botting">Botting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Experience_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Botting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_game_bot" title="Computer game bot">computer game bot</a></div>
<p>Some players of online games use automated programs known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_game_bot" title="Computer game bot">bots</a> to grind or leech for them in order to progress with minimal effort. This practice often violates the terms of service. Bots are also commonly used in commercial operations in order to powerlevel a character, either to increase the sale value of the account, or to allow the character to be used for commercial <a href="/wiki/Gold_farming" title="Gold farming">gold farming</a>.</p>
