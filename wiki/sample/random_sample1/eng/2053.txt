<p>A <b>phylogenetic tree</b> or <b>evolutionary tree</b> is a branching <a href="/wiki/Diagram" title="Diagram">diagram</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Tree_(graph_theory)" title="Tree (graph theory)">tree</a>" showing the inferred <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> relationships among various biological <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> or other entities—their <b>phylogeny</b>—based upon similarities and differences in their physical or genetic characteristics. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> joined together in the tree are implied to have descended from a <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common ancestor</a>. Phylogenetic trees are central to the field of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetics</a>.</p>
<p>In a <em>rooted</em> phylogenetic tree, each node with descendants represents the inferred <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> of the descendants, and the edge lengths in some trees may be interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> estimates. Each node is called a taxonomic unit. Internal nodes are generally called hypothetical taxonomic units, as they cannot be directly observed. Trees are useful in fields of biology such as <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_comparative_methods" title="Phylogenetic comparative methods">phylogenetic comparative methods</a>.</p>
<p><i>Unrooted</i> trees illustrate only the relatedness of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_nodes" title="Leaf nodes">leaf nodes</a> and do not require the ancestral root to be known or inferred.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_of_life_(science)" title="Tree of life (science)">tree of life</a>" arose from ancient notions of a ladder-like progression from lower to higher forms of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> (such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Chain_of_Being" title="Great Chain of Being">Great Chain of Being</a>). Early representations of "branching" phylogenetic trees include a "paleontological chart" showing the geological relationships among plants and animals in the book <i>Elementary Geology</i>, by Edward Hitchcock (first edition: 1840).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> (1859) also produced one of the first illustrations and crucially popularized the notion of an <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">evolutionary "tree"</a> in his seminal book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Origin_of_Species" title="The Origin of Species">The Origin of Species</a></i>. Over a century later, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biologists</a> still use <a href="/wiki/Tree_structure" title="Tree structure">tree diagrams</a> to depict <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> because such diagrams effectively convey the concept that <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> occurs through the <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptive</a> and semi<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random" title="Random">random</a> splitting of lineages. Over time, species classification has become less static and more dynamic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rooted_tree">Rooted tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rooted tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A rooted phylogenetic tree (see two graphics at top) is a <a href="/wiki/Directed_graph" title="Directed graph">directed</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree_(data_structure)" title="Tree (data structure)">tree</a> with a unique node corresponding to the (usually <a href="/wiki/Imputation_(statistics)" title="Imputation (statistics)">imputed</a>) most recent common ancestor of all the entities at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_node" title="Leaf node">leaves</a> of the tree. The most common method for rooting trees is the use of an uncontroversial <a href="/wiki/Outgroup_(cladistics)" title="Outgroup (cladistics)">outgroup</a>—close enough to allow inference from trait data or molecular sequencing, but far enough to be a clear outgroup.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrooted_tree">Unrooted tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unrooted tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Unrooted trees illustrate the relatedness of the leaf nodes without making assumptions about ancestry. They do not require the ancestral root to be known or inferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Unrooted trees can always be generated from rooted ones by simply omitting the root. By contrast, inferring the root of an unrooted tree requires some means of identifying ancestry. This is normally done by including an outgroup in the input data so that the root is necessarily between the outgroup and the rest of the taxa in the tree, or by introducing additional assumptions about the relative rates of evolution on each branch, such as an application of the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock" title="Molecular clock">molecular clock</a> <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maher_2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maher_2002-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bifurcating_tree">Bifurcating tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bifurcating tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both rooted and unrooted phylogenetic trees can be either <a href="/wiki/Bifurcation_theory" title="Bifurcation theory">bifurcating</a> or multifurcating, and either labeled or unlabeled. A rooted bifurcating tree has exactly two descendants arising from each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_node" title="Interior node">interior node</a> (that is, it forms a <a href="/wiki/Binary_tree" title="Binary tree">binary tree</a>), and an unrooted bifurcating tree takes the form of an <a href="/wiki/Unrooted_binary_tree" title="Unrooted binary tree">unrooted binary tree</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_tree" title="Free tree">free tree</a> with exactly three neighbors at each internal node. In contrast, a rooted multifurcating tree may have more than two children at some nodes and an unrooted multifurcating tree may have more than three neighbors at some nodes. A labeled tree has specific values assigned to its leaves, while an unlabeled tree, sometimes called a tree shape, defines a topology only. The number of possible trees for a given number of leaf nodes depends on the specific type of tree, but there are always more multifurcating than bifurcating trees, more labeled than unlabeled trees, and more rooted than unrooted trees. The last distinction is the most biologically relevant; it arises because there are many places on an unrooted tree to put the root. For labeled bifurcating trees, there are:</p>

<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Dendrogram" title="Dendrogram">dendrogram</a> is a broad term for the diagrammatic representation of a phylogenetic tree.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a> is a phylogenetic tree formed using <a href="/wiki/Cladistics" title="Cladistics">cladistic</a> methods. This type of tree only represents a branching pattern; i.e., its branch spans do not represent time or relative amount of character change.</li>
<li>A phylogram is a phylogenetic tree that has branch spans proportional to the amount of character change.</li>
<li>A chronogram is a phylogenetic tree that explicitly represents evolutionary time through its branch spans.</li>
<li>A spindle diagram (often called a Romerogram after the American palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Romer" title="Alfred Romer">Alfred Romer</a>) is the representation of the evolution and abundance of the various taxa through time.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Dahlgren" title="Rolf Dahlgren">Dahlgrenogram</a> is a diagram representing a cross section of a phylogenetic tree</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_network" title="Phylogenetic network">phylogenetic network</a> is not strictly speaking a tree, but rather a more general <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">graph</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Directed_acyclic_graph" title="Directed acyclic graph">directed acyclic graph</a> in the case of rooted networks. They are used to overcome some of the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree#Limitations" title="Phylogenetic tree">limitations</a> inherent to trees.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phylogenetic_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computational_phylogenetics" title="Computational phylogenetics">Computational phylogenetics</a></div>
<p>Phylogenetic trees composed with a nontrivial number of input sequences are constructed using <a href="/wiki/Computational_phylogenetics" title="Computational phylogenetics">computational phylogenetics</a> methods. Distance-matrix methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighbor-joining" title="Neighbor-joining">neighbor-joining</a> or <a href="/wiki/UPGMA" title="UPGMA">UPGMA</a>, which calculate <a href="/wiki/Genetic_distance" title="Genetic distance">genetic distance</a> from <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sequence_alignment" title="Multiple sequence alignment">multiple sequence alignments</a>, are simplest to implement, but do not invoke an evolutionary model. Many sequence alignment methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ClustalW" title="ClustalW">ClustalW</a> also create trees by using the simpler algorithms (i.e. those based on distance) of tree construction. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_parsimony" title="Maximum parsimony">Maximum parsimony</a> is another simple method of estimating phylogenetic trees, but implies an implicit model of evolution (i.e. parsimony). More advanced methods use the <a href="/wiki/Optimality_criterion" title="Optimality criterion">optimality criterion</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_likelihood" title="Maximum likelihood">maximum likelihood</a>, often within a <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_inference" title="Bayesian inference">Bayesian Framework</a>, and apply an explicit model of evolution to phylogenetic tree estimation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felsenstein_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Felsenstein-4">[4]</a></sup> Identifying the optimal tree using many of these techniques is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NP-hard" title="NP-hard">NP-hard</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felsenstein_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Felsenstein-4">[4]</a></sup> so <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">heuristic</a> search and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimization_(mathematics)" title="Optimization (mathematics)">optimization</a> methods are used in combination with tree-scoring functions to identify a reasonably good tree that fits the data.</p>
<p>Tree-building methods can be assessed on the basis of several criteria:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>efficiency (how long does it take to compute the answer, how much memory does it need?)</li>
<li>power (does it make good use of the data, or is information being wasted?)</li>
<li>consistency (will it converge on the same answer repeatedly, if each time given different data for the same model problem?)</li>
<li>robustness (does it cope well with violations of the assumptions of the underlying model?)</li>
<li>falsifiability (does it alert us when it is not good to use, i.e. when assumptions are violated?)</li>
</ul>
<p>Tree-building techniques have also gained the attention of mathematicians. Trees can also be built using <a href="/wiki/T-theory" title="T-theory">T-theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although phylogenetic trees produced on the basis of sequenced <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomic</a> data in different species can provide evolutionary insight, they have important limitations. Most importantly, they do not necessarily accurately represent the evolutionary history of the included taxa. In fact, they are literally scientific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotheses" title="Hypotheses">hypotheses</a>, subject to <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">falsification</a> by further study (e.g., gathering of additional data, analyzing the existing data with improved methods). The data on which they are based is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_noise" title="Signal noise">noisy</a>; the analysis can be confounded by <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">genetic recombination</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arenas_2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arenas_2010-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woese_2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woese_2002-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> between species that were not nearest neighbors on the tree before hybridisation takes place, <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Conserved_sequence" title="Conserved sequence">conserved sequences</a>.</p>
<p>Also, there are problems in basing the analysis on a single type of character, such as a single <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> or only on morphological analysis, because such trees constructed from another unrelated data source often differ from the first, and therefore great care is needed in inferring phylogenetic relationships among species. This is most true of genetic material that is subject to lateral gene transfer and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombination</a>, where different <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> blocks can have different histories. In general, the output tree of a phylogenetic analysis is an estimate of the <i>character'</i>s phylogeny (i.e. a gene tree) and not the phylogeny of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> (i.e. species tree) from which these characters were sampled, though ideally, both should be very close. For this reason, serious phylogenetic studies generally use a combination of genes that come from different genomic sources (e.g., from mitochondrial or plastid vs. nuclear genomes), or genes that would be expected to evolve under different selective regimes, so that homoplasy (false <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homology</a>) would be unlikely to result from natural selection.</p>
<p>When extinct species are included in a tree, they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_node" title="Leaf node">terminal nodes</a>, as it is unlikely that they are direct ancestors of any extant species. Skepticism might be applied when extinct species are included in trees that are wholly or partly based on DNA sequence data, because little useful "<a href="/wiki/Ancient_DNA" title="Ancient DNA">ancient DNA</a>" is preserved for longer than 100,000 years, and except in the most unusual circumstances no DNA sequences long enough for use in phylogenetic analyses have yet been recovered from material over 1 million years old.</p>
<p>The range of useful DNA materials has expanded with advances in extraction and sequencing technologies. Development of technologies able to infer sequences from smaller fragments, or from spatial patterns of DNA degradation products, would further expand the range of DNA considered useful.</p>
<p>In some organisms, <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiont" title="Endosymbiont">endosymbionts</a> have an independent genetic history from the host.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_network" title="Phylogenetic network">Phylogenetic networks</a> are used when bifurcating trees are not suitable, due to these complications which suggest a more <a href="/wiki/Reticulate" title="Reticulate">reticulate</a> evolutionary history of the organisms sampled.</p>
