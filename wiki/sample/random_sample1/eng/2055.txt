<p><br/>
<b>Late Hōjō clan</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">後北条氏</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Go-Hōjō-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was one of the most powerful warrior clans in Japan in the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a> and held domains primarily in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D" title="Kantō">Kantō</a> region.</p>
<p>The history of the family is written in the <i>Hojo Godaiki</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sansom2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sansom2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The clan is traditionally reckoned to be started by Ise Shinkurō, who came from a branch of the prestigious Ise clan, a family in the direct employment of the Ashikaga Shoguns. During the succession crisis in the 15th century, Shinkuro became associated with the Imagawa clan via the marriage of his sister to the Imagawa head, who led an army to Kyoto. Through this relationship Shinkuro quickly established a base of power in Kanto.</p>
<p>His son wanted his lineage to have a more illustrious name, and chose <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_clan" title="Hōjō clan">Hōjō</a>, after the line of regents of the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura shogunate</a>, to which his wife also belonged. So he became <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Ujitsuna" title="Hōjō Ujitsuna">Hōjō Ujitsuna</a>, and his father, Ise Shinkurō, was posthumously renamed <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_S%C5%8Dun" title="Hōjō Sōun">Hōjō Sōun</a>.</p>
<p>The Late Hōjō, sometimes known as the <b>Odawara Hōjō</b> after their home castle of <a href="/wiki/Odawara_Castle" title="Odawara Castle">Odawara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sagami_Province" title="Sagami Province">Sagami Province</a>, were not related to the earlier Hōjō clan. Their power rivaled that of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_clan" title="Tokugawa clan">Tokugawa clan</a>, but eventually <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a> eradicated the power of the Hōjō in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Odawara_(1590)" title="Siege of Odawara (1590)">Siege of Odawara (1590)</a>, banishing <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Ujinao" title="Hōjō Ujinao">Hōjō Ujinao</a> and his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toku_Hime_(1565%E2%80%931615)" title="Toku Hime (1565–1615)">Toku Hime</a> (a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">Tokugawa Ieyasu</a>) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Koya" title="Mount Koya">Mount Kōya</a>, where Ujinao died in 1591.</p>

<p>The heads of the Late Hōjō clan were</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_S%C5%8Dun" title="Hōjō Sōun">Hōjō Sōun</a> (1432–1519)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Ujimasa" title="Hōjō Ujimasa">Hōjō Ujimasa</a> (1538–1590), son of Ujiyasu</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Ujinao" title="Hōjō Ujinao">Hōjō Ujinao</a> (1562–1591), son of Ujimasa</li>
</ul>
