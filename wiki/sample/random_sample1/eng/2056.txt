<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">High-intensity discharge lamp</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>High-intensity discharge lamps</b> (<b>HID lamps</b>) are a type of <a href="/wiki/Electric_light" title="Electric light">electrical</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas-discharge_lamp" title="Gas-discharge lamp">gas-discharge lamp</a> which produces light by means of an electric arc between <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> housed inside a translucent or transparent <a href="/wiki/Fused_quartz" title="Fused quartz">fused quartz</a> or fused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> arc tube. This tube is filled with both <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> salts. The gas facilitates the arc's initial strike. Once the arc is started, it heats and evaporates the metal salts forming <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>, which greatly increases the intensity of light produced by the arc and reduces its power consumption. High-intensity discharge lamps are a type of <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lamp</a>.</p>
<p>Brand new high-intensity discharge lamps make more <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">visible light per unit of electric power consumed</a> than <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent</a> lamps, since a greater proportion of their radiation is visible light in contrast to infrared. However, the lumen output of HID lighting can deteriorate by up to 70% over 10,000 burning hours.</p>
<p>Many modern vehicles use HID bulbs for the main lighting systems, some applications are now moving from HID bulbs to LED and laser technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, this HID technology is not new and was first demonstrated by Francis Hauksbee in 1705.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-intensity_discharge_lamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="448" data-file-width="640" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg/220px-High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg/330px-High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg/440px-High_pressure_sodium_lamp.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Various types of chemistry are used in the arc tubes of HID lamps, depending on the desired characteristics of light intensity, <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature#Correlated_color_temperature" title="Color temperature">correlated color temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Color_rendering_index" title="Color rendering index">color rendering index</a> (CRI), energy efficiency, and lifespan. Varieties of HID lamp include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercury-vapor_lamp" title="Mercury-vapor lamp">Mercury-vapor lamps</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metal-halide_lamp" title="Metal-halide lamp">Metal-halide</a> (MH) lamps</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ceramic_discharge_metal-halide_lamp" title="Ceramic discharge metal-halide lamp">Ceramic MH</a> lamps</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sodium-vapor_lamp" title="Sodium-vapor lamp">Sodium-vapor lamps</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Xenon_arc_lamp" title="Xenon arc lamp">Xenon short-arc lamps</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The light-producing element of these lamp types is a well-stabilized <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">arc</a> discharge contained within a refractory envelope <b>arc tube</b> with wall loading in excess of 3 W/cm² (19.4 W/in²).</p>
<p>Mercury-vapor lamps were the first commercially available HID lamps. Originally they produced a bluish-green light, but more recent versions can produce light with a less pronounced color tint. However, mercury-vapor lamps are falling out of favor and being replaced by sodium-vapor and metal-halide lamps.</p>
<p>Metal-halide and ceramic metal-halide lamps can be made to give off neutral white light useful for applications where normal color appearance is critical, such as TV and movie production, indoor or nighttime sports games, automotive headlamps, and aquarium lighting.</p>
<p>Low-pressure sodium-vapor lamps are extremely efficient. They produce a deep yellow-orange light and have an effective <a href="/wiki/Color_rendering_index" title="Color rendering index">CRI</a> of nearly zero; items viewed under their light appear <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochromatic</a>. This makes them particularly effective as photographic <a href="/wiki/Safelight" title="Safelight">safelights</a>. High-pressure sodium lamps tend to produce a much whiter light, but still with a characteristic orange-pink cast. New color-corrected versions producing a whiter light are now available, but some efficiency is sacrificed for the improved color.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1350" data-file-width="2100" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg/220px-Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg/330px-Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg/440px-Nk70lup_35t6_100_70enp_BW.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Like fluorescent lamps, HID lamps require a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballast_(electrical)" title="Ballast (electrical)">ballast</a> to start and maintain their arcs. The method used to initially strike the arc varies: mercury-vapor lamps and some metal-halide lamps are usually started using a third electrode near one of the main electrodes, while other lamp styles are usually started using pulses of high voltage.</p>
<p>Replacements for the toxic mercury in the HID lamps have been investigated and are a matter of ongoing research. Experiments show promising results and widespread future applications are expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioactive_substances">Radioactive substances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-intensity_discharge_lamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radioactive substances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some HID lamps make use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> substances such as <a href="/wiki/Krypton-85" title="Krypton-85">krypton-85</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEMA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEMA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LampType_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LampType-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ion_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ion-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transport_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Transport-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPA-7">[7]</a></sup> These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopes" title="Isotopes">isotopes</a> help start the lamps and improve lamp operating characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEMA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEMA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ion_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ion-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Krypton-85 is a gas and is found mixed in with the <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, which is in the arc tube of the lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPA-7">[7]</a></sup> The thorium, which is a solid, is used in the electrodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-HPA-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>These isotopes produce <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>. It is because of their particular ionization properties that they are used in lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ion_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ion-5">[5]</a></sup> They produce <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beta_particle" title="Beta particle">beta</a> radiation, which causes high ionization inside the lamp without being able to escape from the lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ion_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ion-5">[5]</a></sup> The amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a> produced by the isotopes that can escape from the lamp is negligible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ion_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ion-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-intensity_discharge_lamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HID lamps are typically used when high levels of light over large areas are required, and when energy efficiency and/or light intensity are desired. These areas include <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gymnasiums</a>, large public areas, <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouses</a>, movie theaters, football stadiums,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> outdoor activity areas, roadways, parking lots, and pathways. More recently, HID lamps have been used in small retail and even residential environments because of advances in reduced lumen bulbs. Ultra-high performance (UHP) HID lamps are used in LCD or DLP projection TV sets or projection displays as well.</p>
<p>HID lamps have made indoor gardening practical, particularly for plants that require high levels of direct sunlight in their natural habitat; HID lamps, specifically metal-halide and high-pressure sodium, are a common light source for indoor gardens. They are also used to reproduce tropical intensity sunlight for indoor <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>.</p>
<p>Most HID lamps produce significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_radiation" title="UV radiation">UV radiation</a> and require UV-blocking filters to prevent UV-induced degradation of lamp fixture components and fading of dyed items illuminated by the lamp. Exposure to HID lamps operating with faulty or absent UV-blocking filters causes injury to humans and animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_eye" title="Arc eye">arc eye</a>. Many HID lamps are designed to quickly extinguish if their outer UV-shielding glass envelope is broken.</p>
<p>Beginning in the early 1990s, HID lamps have seen applications in automotive <a href="/wiki/Headlamp#HID" title="Headlamp">headlamps</a>. Xenon, or high-intensity discharge (HID), lighting provides brighter headlights and increases visibility of many peripheral objects (i.e., street signs and pedestrians) left in the shadows by standard halogen lighting.</p>
<p>HID lamps are used in high-performance <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_lighting" title="Bicycle lighting">bicycle headlamps</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">flashlights</a> and other portable lights, because they produce a great amount of light per unit of power. As the HID lights use less than half the power of an equivalent tungsten-halogen light, a significantly smaller and lighter-weight power supply can be used.</p>
<p>HID lamps have also become common on many aircraft as replacements for traditional landing and taxi lights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>HID lamps are also used in lamps for <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a>. The higher <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">efficacy</a> of HID lamps compared to halogen units means longer burn times for a given battery size and light output.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_life">End of life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-intensity_discharge_lamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: End of life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Factors of wear come mostly from on/off cycles versus the total on time. The highest wear occurs when the HID burner is ignited while still hot and before the metallic salts have recrystallized.</p>
<p>At the end of life, many types of high-intensity discharge lamps exhibit a phenomenon known as <i>cycling</i>. These lamps can be started at a relatively low <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a>. As they heat up during operation, however, the internal gas pressure within the arc tube rises and a higher voltage is required to maintain the <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">arc discharge</a>. As a lamp gets older, the voltage necessary to maintain the arc eventually rises to exceed the voltage provided by the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">electrical ballast</a>. As the lamp heats to this point, the arc fails and the lamp goes out. Eventually, with the arc extinguished, the lamp cools down again, the gas pressure in the arc tube is reduced, and the ballast can once again cause the arc to strike. The effect of this is that the lamp glows for a while and then goes out, repeatedly.</p>
<p>Another phenomenon associated with HID lamp wear and aging is discoloration of the emitted light beam ("fading"). Commonly, a shift towards blue and/or violet can be observed. This shift is slight at first and is more generally a sign of the lamps being "broken in" whilst still being in good overall working order, but towards the end of its life, the HID lamp is often perceived as <i>only</i> producing blue and violet light. Based on <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck's law">Planck's law</a>, this is a direct result of the increased voltage and higher temperature necessary to maintain the arc.</p>
<p>More sophisticated ballast designs detect cycling and give up attempting to start the lamp after a few cycles. If power is removed and reapplied, the ballast will make a new series of startup attempts.</p>
<p>Sometimes the quartz tube containing mercury can explode in a UHP lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When that happens, up to 50 mg of mercury vapor is released into the atmosphere. This quantity of mercury is potentially toxic, but the main hazard from broken lamps is glass cuts, and occasional exposure to broken lamps is not expected to have adverse effects. Philips recommends the use of a mercury vacuum cleaner, ventilation or respiratory protection, eye protection, and protective clothing when dealing with broken lamps. Mercury lamps also require special waste disposal, depending on location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
