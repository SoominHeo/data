<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Kokin Wakashū</i></h1>

<p>The <i><b>Kokin Wakashū</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">古今和歌集</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "Collection of Japanese Poems of Ancient and Modern Times")</span>, commonly abbreviated as <i><b>Kokinshū</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">古今集</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, is an early anthology of the <i><a href="/wiki/Waka_(poetry)" title="Waka (poetry)">waka</a></i> form of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_poetry" title="Japanese poetry">Japanese poetry</a>, dating from the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>. It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_anthology" title="Imperial anthology">Imperial anthology</a>, conceived of by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Uda" title="Emperor Uda">Emperor Uda</a> (r. 887–897) and published by order of his son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Daigo" title="Emperor Daigo">Emperor Daigo</a> (r. 897–930), in about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/905_in_poetry" title="905 in poetry">905</a>. Its finished form dates to c. 920, though according to several historical accounts the last poem was added to the collection in 914. The compilers of the anthology were four court poets, led by <a href="/wiki/Ki_no_Tsurayuki" title="Ki no Tsurayuki">Ki no Tsurayuki</a> and also including <a href="/wiki/Ki_no_Tomonori" title="Ki no Tomonori">Ki no Tomonori</a> (who died before its completion), <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cshik%C5%8Dchi_no_Mitsune" title="Ōshikōchi no Mitsune">Ōshikōchi no Mitsune</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mibu_no_Tadamine" title="Mibu no Tadamine">Mibu no Tadamine</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kokin_Wakash%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Kokinshū</i> is the first of the <i><a href="/wiki/Nij%C5%ABichidaish%C5%AB" title="Nijūichidaishū">Nijūichidaishū</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">二十一代集</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, the 21 collections of Japanese poetry compiled at Imperial request. It was the most influential realization of the ideas of poetry at the time, dictating the form and format of Japanese poetry until the late nineteenth century; it was the first anthology to divide itself into seasonal and love poems. The primacy of poems about the seasons pioneered by the <i>Kokinshū</i> continues even today in the <i><a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a></i> tradition.</p>
<p>The Japanese preface by <a href="/wiki/Ki_no_Tsurayuki" title="Ki no Tsurayuki">Ki no Tsurayuki</a> is also the beginning of Japanese criticism as distinct from the far more prevalent Chinese poetics in the literary circles of its day. (The anthology also included a <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a> preface authored by Ki no Yoshimochi.) The idea of including old as well as new poems was another important innovation, one which was widely adopted in later works, both in prose and verse. The poems of the <i>Kokinshū</i> were ordered temporally; the love poems, for instance, though written by many different poets across large spans of time, are ordered in such a way that the reader may understand them to depict the progression and fluctuations of a courtly love-affair. This association of one poem to the next marks this anthology as the ancestor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Renga" title="Renga">renga</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Haikai" title="Haikai">haikai</a></i> traditions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kokin_Wakash%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exact number of poems in the collection varies depending on the textual tradition. The online edition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-online-1">[1]</a></sup> which follows the Date Family text based on a manuscript prepared by <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Teika" title="Fujiwara no Teika">Fujiwara no Teika</a>, contains 1,111 poems. The collection is divided into twenty parts, reflecting older models such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB" title="Man'yōshū">Man'yōshū</a></i> and various Chinese anthologies. The organization of topics is however different from all earlier models, and was followed by all later official collections, although some collections like the <i><a href="/wiki/Kin%27y%C5%8D_Wakash%C5%AB" title="Kin'yō Wakashū">Kin'yō Wakashū</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shika_Wakash%C5%AB" title="Shika Wakashū">Shika Wakashū</a></i> scaled the model down to ten parts.</p>

<p>The compilers included the name of the author of each poem, and the topic <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">題</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>dai</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> or inspiration of the poem, if known. Major poets of the <i>Kokinshū</i> include <a href="/wiki/Ariwara_no_Narihira" title="Ariwara no Narihira">Ariwara no Narihira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ono_no_Komachi" title="Ono no Komachi">Ono no Komachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henj%C5%8D" title="Henjō">Henjō</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Okikaze" title="Fujiwara no Okikaze">Fujiwara no Okikaze</a>, apart from the compilers themselves. Inclusion in any imperial collection, and particularly the <i>Kokinshū</i>, was a great honour.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscripts">Manuscripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kokin_Wakash%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manuscripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 20, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Konan_Women%27s_University" title="Konan Women's University">Kōnan Women's University</a> announced the discovery of a complete manuscript dating to c. 1220–1240. It is the oldest manuscript to contain both the kanji and kana prefaces. It is split into two volumes, 15.9 cm tall by 14.6 cm wide, totaling 429 pages containing all 1111 poems. It is thought to be a copy of a manuscript made by <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Teika" title="Fujiwara no Teika">Fujiwara no Teika</a>, but the identity of the copier is unknown. The manuscript was purchased from a used book store in 1982 for 4,280,000 yen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
