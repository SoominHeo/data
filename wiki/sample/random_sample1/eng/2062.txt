<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Caruncle (bird anatomy)</h1>

<p>A <b>caruncle</b> is defined as 'a small, fleshy excrescence that is a normal part of an animal's anatomy'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Within this definition, caruncles in birds include <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattles</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewlaps" title="Dewlaps">dewlaps</a>), <a href="/wiki/Snood_(anatomy)" title="Snood (anatomy)">snoods</a> and earlobes. The term caruncle is derived from Latin 'caruncula', the diminutive of 'car≈ç', flesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caruncle_(bird_anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Caruncles are carnosities, often of bright colors such as red, blue, yellow or white. They can be present on the head, neck, throat, cheeks or around the eyes of some birds. They may be present as <a href="/wiki/Comb_(anatomy)" title="Comb (anatomy)">combs or crests</a> and other structures near the beak, or, hanging from the throat or neck. Caruncles may be featherless, or, have small scattered feathers. In some species, they may form pendulous structures of <a href="/wiki/Erectile_tissue" title="Erectile tissue">erectile tissue</a>, such as the "<a href="/wiki/Snood_(anatomy)" title="Snood (anatomy)">snood</a>" of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_turkey" title="Domestic turkey">domestic turkey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Word_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Word-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bull_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bull-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caruncles are sometimes <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sex_characteristic" title="Secondary sex characteristic">secondary sexual characteristics</a>, having a more intense color or even a different color, developing as the male reaches sexual maturity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caruncle_(bird_anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Caruncles are also ornamental elements used by males to attract females to breeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burke-6">[6]</a></sup> Having large caruncles or colorful bright ones indicates high levels of testosterone, that they are well-fed birds able to elude other predators thus showing the good quality of their genes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-7">[7]</a></sup> It has been proposed that these organs are also associated with genes which encode resistance to disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-7">[7]</a></sup> It is believed that for birds living in tropical regions, the caruncles also play a role in thermoregulation, making the blood cool faster when flowing through them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sora_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sora-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkeys">Turkeys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caruncle_(bird_anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Turkeys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1282" data-file-width="978" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg/220px-Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg/330px-Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg/440px-Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goulds_Turkey_with_Snood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Wild_turkey#Gould.27s_wild_turkey_.28M._g._mexicana.29_.28Gould.2C_1856.29" title="Wild turkey">Gould's wild turkey</a> with non-erected snood and wattle.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkey_(bird)" title="Turkey (bird)">turkeys</a>, the term usually refers to small, bulbous, fleshy protuberances found on the head, neck and throat, with larger structures particularly at the bottom of the throat. The wattle is a flap of skin hanging under the chin connecting the throat and head and the snood is a highly erectile appendage emanating from the forehead. Both sexes of turkey possess caruncles, although they are more pronounced in the male. Usually they are pale, but when the male becomes excited or during courtship, the caruncles, wattle and snood all engorge with blood, become bright red or blue, and enlarge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>beard</i> (a tuft of modified brush-like feathers) also becomes erect.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscovy_ducks">Muscovy ducks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caruncle_(bird_anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Muscovy ducks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Muscovy_duck" title="Muscovy duck">Muscovy ducks</a>, caruncles refer to the red fleshy mask that surrounds the head of adult birds, particularly prominent in adult drakes (males).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
