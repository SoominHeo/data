<p>The <b>.300 Winchester Magnum</b> (also known as <b>.300 Win Mag</b> or <b>300WM</b>) (7.62×67mm) is a popular, <a href="/wiki/Belted_magnum" title="Belted magnum">belted</a>, bottlenecked magnum rifle <a href="/wiki/Cartridge_(firearms)" title="Cartridge (firearms)">cartridge</a> that was introduced by Winchester Repeating Arms Company in 1963 as a member of the family of <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Magnum" title="Winchester Magnum">Winchester Magnum</a> cartridges. The .300 Winchester Magnum is a magnum cartridge designed to fit in a standard length action. It is based on the .375 H&amp;H Magnum, which has been blown out, shortened, and necked down to accept a .30 caliber (7.62 mm) bullet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The .300 Winchester is extremely versatile and has been adopted by a wide range of users including hunters, target shooters, military units, and law enforcement departments. Hunters found the cartridge to be an effective all-around choice with bullet options ranging from the flatter shooting 165 grain to the harder hitting 200+ grain selections available from the factory. The .300 Win Mag remains the most popular .30 caliber magnum with American hunters, despite being surpassed in performance by the more powerful <a href="/wiki/.300_Weatherby_Magnum" title=".300 Weatherby Magnum">.300</a> and <a href="/wiki/.30-378_Weatherby_Magnum" title=".30-378 Weatherby Magnum">.30-378 Weatherby Magnums</a> and the newer <a href="/wiki/.300_Remington_Ultra_Magnum" title=".300 Remington Ultra Magnum">.300 Remington Ultra Magnum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haviland_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-haviland-4">[4]</a></sup> It is a popular selection for hunting <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>, and bighorn sheep as it can deliver better long range performance with better bullet weight than most other .30 caliber cartridges. Military and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_agency" title="Law enforcement agency">law enforcement</a> departments adopted the cartridge for long range sniping and marksmanship. As a testament to its accuracy, since its introduction it has gone on to win several 1,000-yard (910 m) competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hermansen300Mag_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hermansen300Mag-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridge_history">Cartridge history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.300_Winchester_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cartridge history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the design of the .300 Winchester Magnum there were several cartridges that provided what could be best described as a magnum level of energy. The heritage of .30 caliber (7.62 mm) magnums can be traced back to the .30 Newton in 1913 and to the <a href="/wiki/.300_H%26H_Magnum" title=".300 H&amp;H Magnum">.300 H&amp;H Magnum</a> in 1925. Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/.270_Weatherby_Magnum" title=".270 Weatherby Magnum">.270 Weatherby Magnum</a> in 1943, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Weatherby" title="Roy Weatherby">Roy Weatherby</a> introduced a line of cartridges based on a standard length (2.5 in [63.50 mm]) magnum case. This was accomplished by taking the .30 Super Belted Rimless H&amp;H case and having it blown out (reducing the taper) and shortened so that it could be cycled through a standard length bolt-action rifle. Then in 1944 he designed the .300 Weatherby Magnum, which essentially was an improved version of the .30 Super Belted Rimless H&amp;H, a close variant of the .300 H&amp;H Magnum.</p>
<p>The Weatherby’s standard length magnum case was soon noticed. In 1958 Winchester introduced three cartridges – the <a href="/wiki/.264_Winchester_Magnum" title=".264 Winchester Magnum">.264 Winchester Magnum</a>, .338 Winchester Magnum and the <a href="/wiki/.458_Winchester_Magnum" title=".458 Winchester Magnum">.458 Winchester Magnum</a>, all based on the shortened and blown out .375 H&amp;H Magnum case. The popular .30 caliber’s omission from that lineup was not missed. Wildcatters soon produced the .30-338 Winchester and <a href="/wiki/Norma_Precision" title="Norma Precision">Norma Projektilfabrik</a>, who were by now manufacturing ammunition for Weatherby, took the standard length basic Weatherby brass and necked it down to .30 caliber (7.62 mm) and called it the <a href="/wiki/.308_Norma_Magnum" title=".308 Norma Magnum">.308 Norma Magnum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CartridgesoftheWorld11_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CartridgesoftheWorld11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The .300 Winchester Magnum was introduced in 1963 by Winchester for use in the <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Model_70" title="Winchester Model 70">Model 70</a> rifle. The introduction of the .300 Winchester Magnum was not unforeseen; rather, its introduction was anticlimactic. Winchester developed the .300 Winchester Magnum by taking the .338 Winchester Magnum, which was introduced in 1958, and moved the shoulder forward by 0.156 inches (4.0 mm) and lengthening it by 0.120 inches (3.0 mm). This caused the cartridge to have a neck shorter than the diameter of the bullet. There has been some speculation that if the cartridge was released earlier, the dimensions of the cartridge would have matched the .30-338 Winchester wildcat cartridge. Since its introduction the cartridge has remained extremely popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hornady7_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hornady7-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The .300 Winchester Magnum's high availability in popular rifles such as Winchester's Model 70 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remington_700" title="Remington 700">Remington Model 700</a> made the cartridge a popular choice among the shooting public. Although the .300 H&amp;H Magnum, .30-338 Winchester Magnum and the .308 Norma Magnum had a head start on the .300 Winchester Magnum these cartridges soon faded into obsolescence. Only the .300 Winchester Magnum was to survive as a readily available cartridge. Other cartridges, while available, are more expensive.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_specifications">Design and specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.300_Winchester_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design and specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .300 Winchester uses the same case head design of the .375 H&amp;H Magnum, its parent cartridge. The taper of the cartridge was reduced to provide the cartridge with more volume so as to increase its potential powder capacity. The lengthening of the case and the move of the shoulder forward over the .338 Winchester Magnum allowed for the reaming of the .308 Norma Magnum or .30-338 Winchester chamber to dimensions of the .300 Winchester Magnum. The down side was a neck which was shorter than the caliber of the bullet it fired, which meant that the bullet had to be seated more deeply in the case.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridge_dimensions">Cartridge dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.300_Winchester_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cartridge dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ammunition standards organizations <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAAMI" title="SAAMI">SAAMI</a> (Sporting Arms and Ammunition Manufacturers' Institute) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.I.P." title="C.I.P.">C.I.P.</a> (Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives) have provided specifications for the .300 Winchester Magnum cartridge. Almost no divergence between C.I.P. and SAAMI dimensional values exist for this cartridge.<br/>
The case volume will vary from manufacturer to manufacturer to such a degree the reloading software suite <a href="/wiki/QuickLOAD" title="QuickLOAD">QuickLOAD</a> provides five differing cartridge case capacities, that for reloading purposes significantly differ between manufacturers. The SAAMI specification .300 Winchester Magnum case capacity is 91.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">grains</a> grains of H<sub>2</sub>O (5.93 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a>).<br/>
For four ammunition manufacturers the cartridge case capacities are provided as:<br/>
Remington 88.0 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">grains</a> grains of H<sub>2</sub>O (5.70 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a>)<br/>
Federal 92.0 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">grains</a> grains of H<sub>2</sub>O (5.96 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a>)<br/>
Winchester 93.8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">grains</a> grains of H<sub>2</sub>O (6.08 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a>)<br/>
Norma 95.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">grains</a> grains of H<sub>2</sub>O (6.19 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">ml</a>)</p>

<p><b>.300 Winchester Magnum SAAMI cartridge dimensions</b>. All sizes in inches (in) and millimeters (mm). The projectile diameter should be 0.309 in (7.85 mm) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>SAAMI recommends a bore diameter of 0.300 inches (7.6 mm) and a groove diameter of 0.308 inches (7.8 mm). SAAMI recommended a six-groove barrel with each groove being 0.110 inches (2.8 mm) wide. Recommended twist ratio is 1:10 (254 mm).<br/>
C.I.P. defines the common <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> twist rate for this cartridge as 254 mm (1 in 10 in), 6 grooves, Ø lands = 7.62 mm (0.300 in), Ø grooves = 7.82 mm (0.308 in), land width = 2.79 mm (0.110 in), and the <a href="/wiki/Primer_(firearm)" title="Primer (firearm)">primer type</a> is large rifle magnum.</p>
<p>The SAAMI Maximum Average Pressure (MAP) for this cartridge is 64,000 psi (441.3 MPa) piezo pressure (54,000 <a href="/wiki/Copper_units_of_pressure" title="Copper units of pressure">CUP</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><br/>
According to the official C.I.P. (Commission Internationale Permanente pour l'Epreuve des Armes à Feu Portatives) rulings the .300 Winchester Magnum can handle up to 430.00 MPa (62,366 psi) P<sub>max</sub> piezo pressure. In C.I.P. regulated countries every rifle cartridge combo has to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(firearms)" title="Proof (firearms)">proofed</a> at 125% of this maximum C.I.P. pressure to certify for sale to consumers. This means that .300 Winchester Magnum chambered arms in C.I.P. regulated countries are currently (2013) proof tested at 494.50 MPa (71,721 psi) PE piezo pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.300_Winchester_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Winchester’s factory ammunition for the .300 Winchester Magnum is capable of 3,260 feet per second (990 m/s) with the 150-<a href="/wiki/Grain_(unit)" title="Grain (unit)">grain</a> (9.7 g) bullet and 3,000 ft/s (910 m/s) with the 180-grain (12 g) bullet, however old Western "Power Point" <a href="/wiki/Soft-point_bullet" title="Soft-point bullet">soft point</a> can reach past 3,400 feet per second (1036 m/s) with 150 grain projectiles. The maximum point blank range for the 150 gr (9.7 g) bullet is 318 yards (291 m) yards when zeroed at 270 yards (250 m). The maximum point blank range for the 180 gr (12 g) bullet is 300 yards when zeroed at 254 yards (232 m). The ability to zero the .300 Winchester Magnum and shoot without hold over to 300 yards (270 m) makes the cartridge one of the flatter shooting cartridges.</p>

<p>The .30 caliber is the most popular caliber in the United States<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. So it is not surprising that the widest range of bullets available is in the .30 caliber. The most useful bullet weights for the .300 Winchester Magnum are those weighing between 150–200 grains (9.7–13.0 g). However, bullets weighing between 110–250 gr (7.1–16.2 g) are available to the reloader for the .300 Winchester Magnum.</p>
<p>Compared with the 30-06 Springfield the .300 Winchester Magnum provides a nearly 300 ft/s (91 m/s) increase in velocity. This translates to about 20% greater energy advantage over the 30-06 Springfield cartridge. Due to the short neck, heavier bullets particularly those weighing greater than 200 grains (13 g) and mono-metal bullets such as the Barnes X bullets will need to be seated more deeply into the cartridge. As the bullet will take up volume which could have been taken by the propellant velocity advantages diminish as the weight of the bullet increases.</p>
<p>The .300 Winchester Magnum is known for its accuracy and has been used for 1,000-yard (910 m) and 1,000-metre (1,100 yd) competitions. While in hunting situations such accuracy is unnecessary, such accuracy does aid in the extending the range of the cartridge. Taken together with its performance it remains one of the most useful and popular cartridges today.</p>
<p>Although cartridges such as the <a href="/wiki/.30-378_Weatherby_Magnum" title=".30-378 Weatherby Magnum">.30-378 Weatherby Magnum</a>, <a href="/wiki/.300_Remington_Ultra_Magnum" title=".300 Remington Ultra Magnum">.300 Remington Ultra Magnum</a> and the .300 Weatherby Magnum all exceed performance of the .300 Winchester Magnum none of these cartridges can be chambered in a standard length action. Few .30 caliber (7.62 mm) standard length cartridges can match the performance and versatility of the .300 Winchester Magnum.</p>
<p>The down side to this performance is recoil. The amount of recoil the cartridge generates is a step up from the non-magnum .30 caliber (7.62 mm) cartridges. Its recoil is about 30% greater than that of the .30-06 Springfield,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which is known as a 'stout' cartridge. This would put the .300 Winchester Magnum at the upper limit of what most shooters can shoot comfortably for extended shooting sessions. As a rough comparison, the recoil of the .300 Winchester Magnum is roughly comparable to a 12 gauge shotgun shooting 1 oz. slugs. This greater recoil can make the .300 Winchester Magnum, despite its inherent accuracy advantages, a harder cartridge to shoot accurately, when compared to non-magnum .30 caliber cartridges such as the .30-06 Springfield or the .308 Winchester. On the other hand, recoil is subjective (some are more sensitive to it than others) and one can get used to it with practice. Also, many rifles available today now have effective recoil attenuating features built into them, such as muzzle compensators and energy absorbing stocks and butt-pads, that can significantly lessen recoil as it is felt by the shooter.</p>

<p>The .300 Winchester Magnum has more than enough power to hunt all species of ungulate. It is particularly useful when hunting the members of the ungulate family such as elk and moose and is a popular cartridge among hunters for these class 3 game species. Elk can weigh as much as 1,000 pounds (450 kg) and moose 1,400 pounds (640 kg). Bullet weights of 165–200 grains (10.7–13.0 g) are the preferred choices for these game species. Controlled expansion bullets such as the Nosler Partition or Barnes X are preferred rather than more lightly constructed bullets for these larger species of ungulate. Bullets weighing 150–165 gr (9.7–10.7 g) are adequate for smaller deer such as the mule deer and white tail deer.</p>
<p>With its velocity, low bullet drop and ability to retain usable energy at an extended range, the .300 Winchester Magnum comes into its element when hunting sheep. Be it for chamois in the Alps, bighorn in the Rockies, argali in the Pamirs or mouflon in the Caucasus the cartridge is an excellent choice for the sheep hunter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>.300 Winchester Magnum makes a good rifle for dangerous class 3 game such as bear. Both the black<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCOC2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCOC2010-13">[13]</a></sup> and grizzly bears<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> are hunted using the cartridge. The grizzly bear can grow to be twice as large as the black bear and are among the largest predators. The Alaskan brown bear and the polar bear are the largest carnivores. Many consider the .300 Winchester Magnum to be on the lighter side of what is required for the largest bears but hunters have had success with the cartridge against these large bruins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Due to its ability to shoot flat and carry its energy efficiently the cartridge is one of the more favored rounds for African plains game. With bullets of good sectional density and ballistic coefficients the cartridge possesses the long range performance necessary to take large game species at extended ranges. The .300 Winchester Magnum can be used to hunt everything from the dik-dik to the <a href="/wiki/Giant_eland" title="Giant eland">giant eland</a>. It is an excellent cartridge choice for all plains game under 1,500 pounds (680 kg).</p>
<p>The .300 Win Mag sees use in long-range <a href="/wiki/Benchrest_shooting" title="Benchrest shooting">benchrest shooting</a> competitions and has been adopted by law enforcement marksmen and by a few specific branches of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Military" title="U.S. Military">U.S. Military</a> for use by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snipers" title="Snipers">snipers</a>. Maximum effective range is generally accepted to be 1,210 yards (1,110 m) with ammunition incorporating low-drag projectiles. Sub 1 minute-of-angle (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_angle" title="Minute of angle">MOA</a>) accuracy out to 1,000 yards (910 m) is not unusual in precision-built rifles firing match-grade ammunition.</p>
<p>Recoil from the .300 Win Mag is noticeably higher than the well-known and popular <a href="/wiki/.30-06_Springfield" title=".30-06 Springfield">.30-06 Springfield</a>. Subsequently, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remington_Arms_Company" title="Remington Arms Company">Remington</a> has made low-recoil rounds called "Managed-Recoil" available for the .300 Win Mag, which recoil less while providing performance similar to the .30-06 Springfield.</p>

<p>The U.S. government purchased <b>MK 248 MOD 1 .300 Winchester Magnum</b> match-grade ammunition in 2009 for use in adapted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XM2010" title="XM2010">M24 Sniper Weapon Systems</a> and other .300 Winchester Magnum sniper rifles like the U.S. Navy Mk.13s. This ammunition was developed as a .300 Winchester Magnum Match Product Improvement (PIP) and uses the 220 gr (14.26 g) Sierra MatchKing Hollow Point Boat Tail (HPBT) <a href="/wiki/Very-low-drag_bullet" title="Very-low-drag bullet">very-low-drag bullet</a> fired at a nominal muzzle velocity of 2,850 ft/s plus or minus 50 ft/s (869 m/s ± 15.2 m/s). According to the U.S. Navy this ammunition should increase the maximum effective range of .300 Winchester Magnum sniper rifle systems to 1,500 yards (1,370 m), decrease wind deflection on bullets in flight and use Hodgdon H1000, a reduced muzzle flash propellant that remains temperature stable across an operational temperature range of −25 °F to +165 °F (−32 °C to 74 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The 3.55 in (90.2 mm) long MK 248 MOD 1 or alike ammunition is not offered commercially, since it exceeds SAAMI standards for overall length and maximum chamber pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to JBM Ballistics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbmballistics.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-jbmballistics.com-20">[20]</a></sup> using the G7 ballistic coefficient provided by Bryan Litz, and a Weapon Employment Zone (WEZ) analysis of the XM2010 rifle with various .300 Winchester Magnum ammunition types by Bryan Litz, the MK 248 MOD 1 .300 Winchester Magnum cartridge, when fired at its nominal muzzle velocity of 869 m/s (2,850 ft/s), should have 1,286 to 1,289 m (1,406 to 1,410 yd) supersonic range under <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> conditions at sea level (<a href="/wiki/Density_of_air" title="Density of air">air density</a> ρ = 1.225 kg/m<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2014, the U.S. Department of Defense annual testing report found that the older A191 or MK 248 Mod 0 .300 Winchester Magnum service round loaded with aerodynamically less efficient 190 gr (12.32 g) Sierra MatchKing Hollow Point Boat Tail (HPBT) bullets fired from the XM2010 demonstrated adequate performance and lethality. Live fire tests were conducted in March 2013 against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistics_gelatin" title="Ballistics gelatin">ballistics gelatin</a>, light material barriers, and other targets to determine the projectile’s ability to perforate targets. This was the first time the Pentagon’s Director, Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&amp;E) tested the round, which can hit targets out to 1,200 m (1,312 yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several companies, among them HS precision, Kimber and Remington manufacture rifles chambered for the .300 Winchester Magnum specifically targeted at law enforcement agencies. The Chattanooga Police Department<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and Minot Police Department S.W.A.T units<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and the L.A. County Sheriffs Department's Special Enforcement Bureau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> which have adopted the .300 Winchester Magnum in some capacity. Due to the power and performance of the .300 Winchester Magnum cartridge the cartridge is more likely to be employed by specialist units within a police department rather than as a general service weapon issued to law enforcement agents.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.300_Winchester_Magnum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The .300 Winchester Magnum was designed with a neck which is shorter than the diameter of its bullet. If Winchester had released the cartridge prior to 1960 the cartridge would have been similar to the .30-338 Winchester wildcat cartridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-300MagNorma_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-300MagNorma-26">[26]</a></sup> However, by the time Winchester got around to designing their own .300 the .308 Norma Magnum and the .30-338 were already on the scene. To help differentiate it from the other .300 magnums and to allow for the chambers of the standard length .300 magnums to be rebored to the .300 Winchester Magnum chamber dimensions, Winchester moved the shoulder forward and lengthened the cartridge slightly. This created the much criticized short neck of the .300 Winchester Magnum.</p>
<p>The short neck was thought to hinder accuracy because it would prevent the alignment of cartridge to the bore but this is rarely an issue either today or when the cartridge was designed. The fact that the cartridge has gone to win many 1,000-yard (910 m) matches puts this criticism to rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hermansen300Mag_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hermansen300Mag-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
