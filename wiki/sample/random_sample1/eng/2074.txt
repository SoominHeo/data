<p><b>Kaoliang</b>, <b>Gaoliangjiu</b> or <b>sorghum wine</b> is a strong distilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquor" title="Liquor">liquor</a> of Chinese origin made from fermented <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>. It is a type of unflavoured <a href="/wiki/Baijiu" title="Baijiu">baijiu</a>. The liquor originates from Dazhigu (大直沽, located east of <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a>), first appearing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>. It is now primarily made and sold in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and also popular in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, where it is called <i>goryangju</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a>: 고량주; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a>: 高粱酒) or bbaegal (which is originated from Chinese character 白干).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kaoliang is an important product of the islands <a href="/wiki/Kinmen" title="Kinmen">Kinmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matsu_Islands" title="Matsu Islands">Matsu</a> which are part of Taiwan. <i>Kaoliang</i> ranges between 38 and 63 percent alcohol by volume.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_brands_from_Taiwan">Famous brands from Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaoliang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Famous brands from Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kaoliang.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2343" data-file-width="717" height="327" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Kaoliang.jpg/100px-Kaoliang.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Kaoliang.jpg/150px-Kaoliang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/Kaoliang.jpg/200px-Kaoliang.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kaoliang.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kinmen 58% Kaoliang</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Kinmen Kaoliang Liquor (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">金門高粱酒</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Jīnmén Gāoliáng Jiǔ</span></i>) is one of the most popular brands of kaoliang in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a>. As its name indicates, it is produced on the island of Kinmen. The mainstays of the range are the standard 58 percent and 38 percent alcohol bottlings.</p>
<p>Yusan Kaoliang Chiew (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">玉山高粱酒</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Yùshān Gāoliáng Jiǔ</span></i>) is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Tobacco_and_Liquor_Corporation" title="Taiwan Tobacco and Liquor Corporation">Taiwan Tobacco and Liquor Corporation</a>. It is named after the highest mountain in Taiwan, <a href="/wiki/Yushan_(mountain)" title="Yushan (mountain)">Yushan</a>. One of the most notable products in the range is an "X.O." kaoliang aged for five years in tanks before bottling.</p>
<p>Tunnel 88 Kaoliang Liquor (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">八八坑道高粱酒</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Bā Bā Kēngdào Gāoliáng Jiǔ</span></i>) is produced by the Matsu Distillery on the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nankan,_Lienchiang" title="Nankan, Lienchiang">Nankan</a>, part of the Matsu archipelago. The name is derived from the name of an abandoned military tunnel which the distillery took over as storage space for their kaoliang and aged rice wine. All of the distillery's aged kaoliangs are stored in the tunnel for at least five years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaoliang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The 1987 <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Yimou" title="Zhang Yimou">Zhang Yimou</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Sorghum_(film)" title="Red Sorghum (film)">Red Sorghum</a></i> (紅高粱; Pinyin: Hóng Gāoliáng) is set in a rural <i>kaoliang</i> distillery in the <a href="/wiki/Shandong" title="Shandong">Shandong</a> province of China.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_character" title="Fictional character">fictional character</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Kao" title="Li Kao">Li Kao</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hughart" title="Barry Hughart">Barry Hughart</a>'s award-winning novels <i><a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_Birds" title="Bridge of Birds">Bridge of Birds</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_the_Stone_(Barry_Hughart)" title="The Story of the Stone (Barry Hughart)">The Story of the Stone</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eight_Skilled_Gentlemen" title="Eight Skilled Gentlemen">Eight Skilled Gentlemen</a></i>, is named after <i>kaoliang</i>; his mother died in <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a> while requesting a last drink, and the abbot attending the birth mistook the syllables "Kao...li...kao..." as her intended name for the child. In <i>Bridge of Birds</i>, while telling the story of his birth, Li Kao describes kaoliang as "the finest <a href="/wiki/Paint_thinner" title="Paint thinner">paint thinner</a> and worst <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> ever invented."</li>
<li>Kaoliang is an important catalyst of the action in the story "New Year's Eve" in Taiwanese writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bai_Xianyong" title="Bai Xianyong">Bai Xianyong</a>'s short story collection <i><a href="/wiki/Taipei_People" title="Taipei People">Taipei People</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
