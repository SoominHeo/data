<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gorenstein ring</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">commutative algebra</a>, a <b>Gorenstein local ring</b> is a commutative <a href="/wiki/Noetherian_ring" title="Noetherian ring">Noetherian</a> <a href="/wiki/Local_ring" title="Local ring">local ring</a> <i>R</i> with finite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injective_dimension" title="Injective dimension">injective dimension</a> as an <i>R</i>-module. There are many equivalent conditions, some of them listed below, often saying that a Gorenstein ring is self-dual in some sense.</p>
<p>Gorenstein rings were introduced by Grothendieck in his 1961 seminar (published in (<a href="#CITEREFHartshorne1967">Hartshorne 1967</a>)). The name comes from a duality property of singular plane curves studied by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gorenstein" title="Daniel Gorenstein">Gorenstein</a> (<a href="#CITEREFGorenstein1952">1952</a>) (who was fond of claiming that he did not understand the definition of a Gorenstein ring). The zero-dimensional case had been studied by <a href="#CITEREFMacaulay1934">Macaulay (1934)</a>. <a href="#CITEREFSerre1961">Serre (1961)</a> and <a href="#CITEREFBass1963">Bass (1963)</a> publicized the concept of Gorenstein rings.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frobenius_ring" title="Frobenius ring">Frobenius rings</a> are noncommutative analogs of zero-dimensional Gorenstein rings. <a href="/wiki/Gorenstein_scheme" title="Gorenstein scheme">Gorenstein schemes</a> are the geometric version of Gorenstein rings.</p>
<p>For Noetherian local rings, there is the following chain of inclusions.</p>
<dl>
<dd><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universally_catenary_ring" title="Universally catenary ring">Universally catenary rings</a></b> ⊃ <b><a href="/wiki/Cohen%E2%80%93Macaulay_ring" title="Cohen–Macaulay ring">Cohen–Macaulay rings</a></b> ⊃ <b><strong class="selflink">Gorenstein rings</strong></b> ⊃ <b><a href="/wiki/Complete_intersection_ring" title="Complete intersection ring">complete intersection rings</a></b> ⊃ <b><a href="/wiki/Regular_local_ring" title="Regular local ring">regular local rings</a></b></dd>
</dl>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorenstein_ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>Gorenstein ring</b> is a commutative Noetherian ring such that each <a href="/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring" title="Localization of a ring">localization</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Prime_ideal" title="Prime ideal">prime ideal</a> is a Gorenstein local ring, as defined above. A Gorenstein ring is in particular <a href="/wiki/Cohen%E2%80%93Macaulay_ring" title="Cohen–Macaulay ring">Cohen–Macaulay</a>.</p>
<p>One elementary characterization is: a Noetherian local ring <i>R</i> of <a href="/wiki/Krull_dimension" title="Krull dimension">dimension</a> zero (equivalently, with <i>R</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_length" title="Finite length">finite length</a> as an <i>R</i>-module) is Gorenstein if and only if Hom<sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) has dimension 1 as a <i>k</i>-vector space, where <i>k</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Residue_field" title="Residue field">residue field</a> of <i>R</i>. Equivalently, <i>R</i> has simple <a href="/wiki/Socle_(mathematics)#Socle_of_a_module" title="Socle (mathematics)">socle</a> as an <i>R</i>-module.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> More generally, a Noetherian local ring <i>R</i> is Gorenstein if and only if there is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_sequence_(algebra)" title="Regular sequence (algebra)">regular sequence</a> <i>a</i><sub>1</sub>,...,<i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> in the maximal ideal of <i>R</i> such that the quotient ring <i>R</i>/( <i>a</i><sub>1</sub>,...,<i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>) is Gorenstein of dimension zero.</p>
<p>For example, if <i>R</i> is a commutative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graded_algebra" title="Graded algebra">graded algebra</a> over a field <i>k</i> such that <i>R</i> has finite dimension as a <i>k</i>-vector space, <i>R</i> = <i>k</i> ⊕ <i>R</i><sub>1</sub> ⊕ ... ⊕ <i>R</i><sub><i>m</i></sub>, then <i>R</i> is Gorenstein if and only if it satisfies <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality" title="Poincaré duality">Poincaré duality</a>, meaning that the top graded piece <i>R</i><sub><i>m</i></sub> has dimension 1 and the product <i>R</i><sub><i>a</i></sub> × <i>R</i><sub><i>m</i>−<i>a</i></sub> → <i>R</i><sub><i>m</i></sub> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_pairing" title="Perfect pairing">perfect pairing</a> for every <i>a</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another interpretation of the Gorenstein property as a type of duality, for not necessarily graded rings, is: for a field <i>F</i>, a commutative <i>F</i>-algebra <i>R</i> of finite dimension as an <i>F</i>-vector space (hence of dimension zero as a ring) is Gorenstein if and only if there is an <i>F</i>-linear map <i>e</i>: <i>R</i> → <i>F</i> such that the symmetric bilinear form (<i>x</i>, <i>y</i>) := <i>e</i>(<i>xy</i>) on <i>R</i> (as an <i>F</i>-vector space) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nondegenerate_bilinear_form" title="Nondegenerate bilinear form">nondegenerate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a commutative Noetherian local ring (<i>R</i>, <i>m</i>, <i>k</i>) of Krull dimension <i>n</i>, the following are equivalent:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>R</i> has finite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injective_dimension" title="Injective dimension">injective dimension</a> as an <i>R</i>-module;</li>
<li><i>R</i> has injective dimension <i>n</i> as an <i>R</i>-module;</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ext_group" title="Ext group">Ext group</a> Ext<sup><i>i</i></sup><sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) is zero for <i>i</i> ≠ <i>n</i> and Ext<sup><i>n</i></sup><sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) is isomorphic to <i>k</i>;</li>
<li>Ext<sup><i>i</i></sup><sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) = 0 for some <i>i</i> &gt; <i>n</i>;</li>
<li>Ext<sup><i>i</i></sup><sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) = 0 for all <i>i</i> &lt; <i>n</i> and Ext<sup><i>n</i></sup><sub><i>R</i></sub>(<i>k</i>, <i>R</i>) is isomorphic to <i>k</i>;</li>
<li><i>R</i> is an <i>n</i>-dimensional Gorenstein ring.</li>
</ul>
<p>A (not necessarily commutative) ring <i>R</i> is called Gorenstein if <i>R</i> has finite injective dimension both as a left <i>R</i>-module and as a right <i>R</i>-module. If <i>R</i> is a local ring, <i>R</i> is said to be a local Gorenstein ring.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorenstein_ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>A Noetherian local ring is Gorenstein if and only if its <a href="/wiki/Completion_(algebra)" title="Completion (algebra)">completion</a> is Gorenstein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_module" title="Canonical module">canonical module</a> of a Gorenstein local ring <i>R</i> is isomorphic to <i>R</i>. In geometric terms, it follows that the standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dualizing_complex" title="Dualizing complex">dualizing complex</a> of a Gorenstein scheme <i>X</i> over a field is simply a <a href="/wiki/Invertible_sheaf" title="Invertible sheaf">line bundle</a> (viewed as a complex in degree −dim(<i>X</i>)); this line bundle is called the <a href="/wiki/Canonical_bundle" title="Canonical bundle">canonical bundle</a> of <i>X</i>. Using the canonical bundle, <a href="/wiki/Serre_duality" title="Serre duality">Serre duality</a> takes the same form for Gorenstein schemes as in the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_scheme" title="Smooth scheme">smooth</a> case.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>In the context of graded rings <i>R</i>, the canonical module of a Gorenstein ring <i>R</i> is isomorphic to <i>R</i> with some degree shift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
