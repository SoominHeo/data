<p><b>Île de Gorée</b> (i.e. "Gorée Island"; (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ildəɡoʁe]</a></span>, from Dutch <i>Goede Reede</i> (good harbor)) is one of the 19 <i>communes d'arrondissement</i> (i.e. districts) of the city of <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>. It is an 18.2-hectare (45-acre) island located 2 kilometres (1.1 nmi; 1.2 mi) at sea from the main harbor of Dakar (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;params=14_40_0_N_17_24_0_W_scale:40000"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">14°40′0″N</span> <span class="longitude">17°24′0″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">14.66667°N 17.40000°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">14.66667; -17.40000</span></span></span></a></span>).</p>
<p>Its population as of the 2013 census was 1,680 inhabitants, giving a density of 5,802 inhabitants per square kilometre (15,030/sq mi), which is only half the average density of the city of Dakar. Gorée is both the smallest and the least populated of the 19 <i>communes d'arrondissement</i> of Dakar.</p>
<p>Gorée is famous as a destination for people interested in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>. Other important centres for the slave trade from Senegal were further north, at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Louis,_Senegal" title="Saint-Louis, Senegal">Saint-Louis, Senegal</a>, or to the south in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">Gambia</a>, at the mouths of major rivers for trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h-net.org_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-h-net.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Les_Guides_Bleus_p._123_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Les_Guides_Bleus_p._123-2">[2]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_slave_trade">History and slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Gorée is a small island 900 metres (3,000 ft) in length and 350 metres (1,150 ft) in width sheltered by the <a href="/wiki/Cap-Vert" title="Cap-Vert">Cap-Vert</a> Peninsula. Now part of the city of Dakar, it was a minor port and site of European settlement along the coast. Being almost devoid of drinking water, the island was not settled before the arrival of Europeans. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> were the first to establish a presence on Gorée <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1450</span>, where they built a small stone chapel and used land as a cemetery.</p>
<p>Gorée is known as the location of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Slaves" title="House of Slaves">House of Slaves</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Maison des esclaves</i></span>), built by an Afro-French <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis" title="Métis">Métis</a> family about 1780–1784. The House of Slaves is one of the oldest houses on the island. It is now used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tourist" title="Tourist">tourist</a> destination to show the horrors of the slave trade throughout the Atlantic world.</p>
<p>After the decline of the slave trade from Senegal in the 1770s and 1780s, the town became an important port for the shipment of peanuts, peanut oil, gum arabic, ivory, and other products of the "legitimate" trade. It was probably in relation to this trade that the so-called <i>Maison des Esclaves</i> was built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h-net.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-h-net.org-1">[1]</a></sup> As discussed by historian <a href="/wiki/Ana_Lucia_Araujo" title="Ana Lucia Araujo">Ana Lucia Araujo</a>, the building started gaining reputation as a slave depot mainly because of the work of its curator <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Ndiaye" title="fr:Joseph Ndiaye">Boubacar Joseph Ndiaye</a>, who was able to move the audiences who visited the house with his performance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many public personalities visit the House of Slaves, which plays the role of a site of memory of slavery. In June 2013, President of the United States Barack Obama visited the House of Slaves.</p>
<p>The island of Gorée was one of the first places in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> to be settled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">Europeans</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Portuguese</a> settled on the island in 1444. It was captured by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Netherlands" title="United Netherlands">United Netherlands</a> in 1588, then the Portuguese again, and again the Dutch. They named it after the Dutch island of <a href="/wiki/Goeree-Overflakkee" title="Goeree-Overflakkee">Goeree</a>, before the British took it over under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Holmes_(admiral)" title="Robert Holmes (admiral)">Robert Holmes</a> in 1664.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_occupation">French occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: French occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">French</a> gained control in 1677, the island remained continuously French until 1960. There were brief periods of British occupation during the various wars fought by France and Britain. In 1960 Senegal was granted independence. The island was notably taken and occupied by the British between 1758 and 1763 following the <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Gor%C3%A9e" title="Capture of Gorée">Capture of Gorée</a> and wider <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Senegal" title="Capture of Senegal">Capture of Senegal</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> before being returned to France at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1763)" title="Treaty of Paris (1763)">Treaty of Paris (1763)</a>. For a brief time between 1779 and 1783, Gorée was again under British control, until ceded again to France in 1783 at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Treaty of Paris (1783)">Treaty of Paris (1783)</a>. During that time, the infamous <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wall_(colonial_administrator)" title="Joseph Wall (colonial administrator)">Joseph Wall</a> was Lieutenant Governor there, who had a man unlawfully flogged to death in 1782.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gorée was principally a trading post, administratively attached to <a href="/wiki/Saint-Louis,_Senegal" title="Saint-Louis, Senegal">Saint-Louis</a>, capital of the Colony of Senegal. Apart from slaves, beeswax, hides and grain were also traded. The population of the island fluctuated according to circumstances, from a few hundred free Africans and Creoles to about 1,500. There would have been few European residents at any one time.</p>
<p>In the 18th and 19th century, Gorée was home to a Franco-African Creole, or <i><a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis" title="Métis">Métis</a></i>, community of merchants with links to similar communities in Saint-Louis and the Gambia, and across the Atlantic to France's colonies in the Americas. Métis women, called <i><a href="/wiki/Signare" title="Signare">signares</a></i> from the Portuguese <i>senhora</i> descendants of African women and European traders, were especially important to the city’s business life. The signares owned ships and property and commanded male clerks. They were also famous for cultivating fashion and entertainment. One such <i>signare</i>, Anne Rossignol, lived in Saint-Domingue (the modern <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>) in the 1780s before the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="969" data-file-width="1300" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg/220px-L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg/330px-L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg/440px-L%27Isle_de_Gore_Schley_1772.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In February 1794 during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, France was the first nation in the world to abolish slavery. The slave trade from Senegal stopped. In May 1802, however, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> reestablished slavery after intense lobbying by sugar <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a> owners of the Caribbean <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement" title="Département">départements</a></i> of France. The wife of Napoleon, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9phine_de_Beauharnais" title="Joséphine de Beauharnais">Joséphine de Beauharnais</a>, daughter of a rich planter from <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, supported their position.</p>
<p>In March 1815, during his political comeback known as the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Days" title="Hundred Days">Hundred Days</a>, Napoleon definitively abolished the slave trade to build relations with Great Britain. (Scotland had never recognized slavery and England finally abolished the slave trade in 1807.) This time, abolition continued.</p>
<p>As the trade in slaves declined in the late eighteenth century, Gorée converted to legitimate commerce. The tiny city and port were ill situated for the shipment of industrial quantities of peanuts, which began arriving in bulk from the mainland. Consequently, its merchants established a presence directly on the mainland, first in <a href="/wiki/Rufisque" title="Rufisque">Rufisque</a> (1840) and then in Dakar (1857). Many of the established families started to leave the island.</p>
<p>Civic franchise for the citizens of Gorée was institutionalized in 1872, when it became a French “commune” with an elected mayor and a municipal council. <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Diagne" title="Blaise Diagne">Blaise Diagne</a>, the first African deputy elected to the French National Assembly (served 1914 to 1934), was born on Gorée. From a peak of about 4,500 in 1845, the population fell to 1,500 in 1904. In 1940 Gorée was annexed to the municipality of Dakar.</p>
<p>From 1913 to 1938, Gorée was home to the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_normale_sup%C3%A9rieure_William_Ponty" title="École normale supérieure William Ponty">École normale supérieure William Ponty</a>, a government teachers' college run by the French Colonial Government. Many of the school's graduates would one day lead the struggle for independence from France.</p>
<p>Gorée is connected to the mainland by regular 30-minute ferry service, for pedestrians only; there are no cars on the island. Senegal’s premier tourist site, the island was listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1978. It now serves mostly as a <a href="/wiki/Memorial" title="Memorial">memorial</a> to the slave trade. Many of the historic commercial and residential buildings have been turned into restaurants and hotels to support the tourist traffic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Goree.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="400" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Map_of_Goree.svg/220px-Map_of_Goree.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Map_of_Goree.svg/330px-Map_of_Goree.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Map_of_Goree.svg/440px-Map_of_Goree.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Goree.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Gorée</div>

<p>With the foundation of Dakar in 1857, Gorée gradually lost its importance. In 1872, the French colonial authorities created the two communes of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Louis,_Senegal" title="Saint-Louis, Senegal">Saint-Louis</a> and Gorée, the first western-style municipalities in West Africa, with the same status as any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commune_in_France" title="Commune in France">commune in France</a>. Dakar, on the mainland, was part of the commune of Gorée, whose administration was located on the island. However, as early as 1887, Dakar was detached from the commune of Gorée and was turned into a commune in its own right. Thus, the commune of Gorée became limited to its tiny island.</p>
<p>In 1891, Gorée still had 2,100 inhabitants, while Dakar only had 8,737 inhabitants. However, by 1926 the population of Gorée had declined to only 700 inhabitants, while the population of Dakar had increased to 33,679 inhabitants. Thus, in 1929 the commune of Gorée was merged with Dakar. The commune of Gorée disappeared, and Gorée was now only a small island of the commune of Dakar.</p>
<p>In 1996, a massive reform of the administrative and political divisions of Senegal was voted by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Senegal" title="Parliament of Senegal">Parliament of Senegal</a>. The commune of Dakar, deemed too large and too populated to be properly managed by a central municipality, was divided into 19 <i>communes d'arrondissement</i> to which extensive powers were given. The commune of Dakar was maintained above these 19 <i>communes d'arrondissement</i>. It coordinates the activities of the <i>communes d'arrondissement</i>, much as <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> coordinates the activities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_borough" title="London borough">London boroughs</a>.</p>
<p>Thus, in 1996 the commune of Gorée was resurrected, although it is now only a <i>commune d'arrondissement</i> (but in fact with powers quite similar to a commune). The new <i>commune d'arrondissement</i> of Gorée, which is officially known in French as the <i>Commune d'Arrondissement de l'île de Gorée</i>, took possession of the old <i>mairie</i> (town hall) in the center of the island. This had been used as the <i>mairie</i> of the former commune of Gorée between 1872 and 1929.</p>
<p>The <i>commune d'arrondissement</i> of Gorée is ruled by a municipal council (<i>conseil municipal</i>) democratically elected every 5 years, and by a mayor elected by members of the municipal council.</p>
<p>The current mayor of Gorée is <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Senghor" title="Augustin Senghor">Augustin Senghor</a>, elected in 2002.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology_of_Gor.C3.A9e_Island">Archaeology of Gorée Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Archaeology of Gorée Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The island is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>, since September 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheikh_Anta_Diop.2C94-1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheikh_Anta_Diop.2C94-1-6">[6]</a></sup> Most of the main buildings in Gorée were constructed during the second half of the eighteenth century. The main buildings are the Slave house, 1786; William Ponty School, 1770; Musée de la mer (Maritime museum), 1835; Fort d'Estrées, originally called the northern battery, which now contains the Historical Museum of Senegal, built between 1852–65; palais du Government (Government Palace), 1864, occupied by the first governor general of Senegal from 1902-07.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheikh_Anta_Diop.2C94-2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheikh_Anta_Diop.2C94-2-7">[7]</a></sup> The Gorée Castle and the seventeenth-century Gorée Police Station, formerly a dispensary, believed to be the site of the first chapel built by the Portuguese in the fifteenth century, and the beach are also of interest to tourists.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_(1837.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg/220px-Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg/330px-Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg/440px-Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_%281837.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_-_Tam-tam_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e_(1837.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>This historical site is a rare example of a European colony where we see free and enslaved Africans (making up half of Gorée's population), Europeans and Afro-Europeans living alongside each other, even as the island was a prominent center in the Atlantic slave trade. Archaeology on Gorée Island leads to many contradictory and contrasting conclusions. On one end of the spectrum, enslaved peoples on Gorée were treated poorly, like animals, on the other there is evidence for enslaved peoples being welcomed as part of families. The <i>signares</i> (free African or Afro-European women) were recorded preferring to eat on the floor with a spoon and communal bowl, as their domestic slaves, but European men kept tradition and used a table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Archaeological research on Gorée has been undertaken by Dr. Ibrahima Thiaw (Associate Professor of Archaeology at the <a href="/wiki/Institut_Fondamental_d%27Afrique_Noire" title="Institut Fondamental d'Afrique Noire">Institut Fondamental d'Afrique Noire</a> (IFAN); and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_Cheikh_Anta_Diop" title="University Cheikh Anta Diop">University Cheikh Anta Diop</a> of Dakar, Senegal); Dr. Susan Keech McIntosh (Professor of Archaeology, <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a>, Houston, Texas); and Raina Croff (PhD candidate at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, New Haven, Connecticut). Dr. Shawn Murray (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>) also contributed to archaeological research at Gorée through a study of local and introduced trees and shrubs, which aids in identifying the ancient plant remains found in the excavations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Excavations at Gorée have also uncovered numerous European imports: bricks, nails, bottles from alcoholic beverages such as wine, beer and other liquor, beads, ceramics and gunflints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gor.C3.A9e_Archaeological_Project">The Gorée Archaeological Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Gorée Archaeological Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Gorée Archaeological Project, or GAP, started its undertakings (survey, testing, mapping, and excavations) in 2001. The project, extending over a period of several years, aimed to collect artifacts pertaining to the historical time periods of the pre- and post- European settlement, as well as identify the use of the different quarters on the island using the material culture excavated from those areas. In his preliminary results, Ibrahima Thiaw also discusses the difficulties of excavation on a primarily tourist island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2839" data-file-width="2134" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg/220px-La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg/330px-La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg/440px-La_Signare_de_Gor%C3%A9e_avec_ses_esclaves-Mus%C3%A9e_de_la_Compagnie_des_Indes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Portuguese Major Captain Lançarote and his crew were the first to make Afro-European relations with Gorée Island in 1445. After sighting Gorée approximately three kilometres (1.9 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a>) off the shore from modern day <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar, Senegal</a>, Lançarote and his officers sent ashore a few officers to leave peace offerings to the natives of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> They deposited on Gorée soil a cake, a mirror and a piece of paper with a cross drawn on it, all of which were intended to be symbols for peaceful actions. However, the Africans did not respond in the desired way and tore up the paper and smashed the cake and the mirror, thus setting the tone for future relations between the Portuguese and Africans of Gorée Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of the early 18th century, Gorée settlements were segregated into quarters: the Bambara quarter (slaves), "<a href="/wiki/Gourmette" title="Gourmette">gourmettes</a>" (Christianized Africans), and a quarter for the residents of Gorée, including free Africans. By the later half of the 18th century, the segregation was between <a href="/wiki/Signare" title="Signare">signares</a> and their families and the rest of the island as well as the previous instated quarters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-European_Settlement">Pre-European Settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pre-European Settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to preliminary results by Ibrahim Thiaw, the levels between the pre and post European contact deposits were characterized by an obvious infestation of termite nests. One interpretation of this fact is the possibility that these termite nests were cause of the abandonment of Gorée before Dutch arrival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deposits of the pre-European time period are dense with pottery decorated with twine and fish vertebrae motifs and could be found in the domestic settlement context, under or at the same levels of floors and fireplaces. The pottery near the settlements suggests that these settlements were semi-permanent or permanent. Fishing tools and equipment were rare although deposits were dense with fish remains. There was also no sign of iron or its usage before the eighteenth century.</p>
<p>Due to a plethora of features containing ritual pots found in the core of the pre European settlement, Thiaw has concluded that the island may have been primarily used for ritual activity and practices. Nevertheless, abandonment is archaeologically evident by the middle of the fifteenth century, possibly due to a mighty termite invasion. There is no archaeological or physical evidence of a struggle or conflict between the eventual Europeans or any other cultural group. Thiaw's hypothesis suggest the possibility that when the Portuguese used the island to bury their dead, the island, in the eyes of the natives, became haunted or was negatively impacted by the mysterious powers of the spirits of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Settlement">European Settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: European Settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some speculation of how Gorée came to be under European or Dutch control. There is some textual evidence that states that the Dutch purchased the island from the chief of Dakar or from local fisherman on the island. While there is little archaeological evidence of this transaction, all European deposits are relatively abundant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> On Gorée there are four distinct deposits found through excavation and testing.The first kinds of deposition are located on the northwestern and western part of the island, and were typically three meters of domestic debris and shell midden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> Surrounding the area that was once Fort Nassau, these depositions were determined in correlation with Fort Nassau activity, which was seen to be relatively unfluctuating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A rare deposition was found near the Castel at G18, the sole site excavated in the area. Depositions in this area were typically shallow and right on top of a limestone bedrock. However, this one site produced three burials, all of which were dug into the limestone bedrock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>G13, a site located on the eastern side of the island has produced cultural debris from one of its trash pits. This debris includes nails, European late pearlware/ early whiteware with similar patterns dating from 1810 to 1849, sardine cans, and window glass, among other artifacts. Located near the military barracks from a military occupation in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the analysis of these ceramics suggests that many of them were replacements coinciding with the occupation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> Deposits like this were not common around the island.</p>

<p>As with many archaeological sites around the globe, modern influences and activities affect the sites and lead to disturbances in the archaeological record or unintentional site destruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> The European government imposed strict rules regarding the use of space and overall settlement development on the island. Archaeology shows this development in the soil; the constructions, levelling, reconstructions, some of which can be linked to a change in the European ruler at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> However, this evidence of development too show results of the consequences from contemporary activity, thus it is an intricate puzzle to determine complex social identities and groups, such as slave or free or African or Afro-European.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> An overall deduction can be made however: Atlantic trade significantly impacts the lives of those on Gorée, seen in the influx of ideas, complex identities and settlement structure.</p>
<p>Atlantic trade also influenced the physiological aspects of Gorean society. Archaeology has uncovered a plethora of evidence for massive imports of alcoholic beverages on the island. The massive import of alcoholic beverages naturally suggests a high level of consumption which its effects have been recorded as drunken conflicts, commonly between the military inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> The previously mentioned Dr. Ibrahima Thiaw is also the author of <i>Digging on Contested Grounds: Archaeology and the Commemoration of Slavery on Gorée Island</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> In this article, Thiaw discusses the difference between the historical accounts full of slavery and shackles and the lack of archaeological evidence to support those accounts. Raina Croff, one of Thiaw's colleagues, states that she personally has never found any evidence of slavery on Gorée Island, however she also includes that archaeological evidence such as shackles and chains would not be found on an island, because there is no need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maison_des_Esclaves">Maison des Esclaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maison des Esclaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:183px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_(2).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3968" data-file-width="2976" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG/181px-Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG/271px-Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG/361px-Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_%282%29.JPG 2x" width="181"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gor%C3%A9e_-_Maison_des_esclaves_(2).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Door of No Return at Maison des Esclaves on Gorée Island</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Maison des Esclaves, or the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Slaves" title="House of Slaves">Slave House</a>, was built in 1780-1784 by Nicolas Pépin. Although it is the home of the infamous “Door of No Return”, which is said to be the last place exported slaves touched African soil for the rest of their lives, there is little evidence at Maison des Esclaves to suggest a “large-scale trans-Atlantic slave trade” economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to census records obtained from the 18th century, the majority of enslaved population fell under the category of domestic slaves, rather than slaves to be exported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h-net.org_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-h-net.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Pépin and his heiress may have had domestic slaves, but again there is little archaeological evidence that they were involved in any slave exportation business. Despite this lack of evidence, Maison des Esclaves has become a pilgrimage site to commemorate forcible removal of Africans from their homeland, also known as the <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora" title="African diaspora">African diaspora</a>. This contrasts with the role of the site of Rue des Dungeons on Gorée. At Rue des Dungeons, as the name suggests, there is a presence of dungeons, which can clearly be associated with the confinement of the slaves to be exported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bambara_Quartier">Bambara Quartier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bambara Quartier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On the southcentral end of Gorée, in the Bambara quarter, although less abundant in artifacts, the deposits from this area differ in sediment inclusions from the rest of the island. Inclusions such as limestone, red bricks, shell, or stones in these two to three meter depositions are no older than the eighteenth century and shows frequent building up and tearing down processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> This could be correlated to the extensive settlement of this area maybe by domestic slaves beginning in the eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> Quartier Bambara was a segregated settlement, which suggests domestic slavery rather than exportation. The maps of this settlement has segregated boundary lines that eventually, by the mid-eighteenth century, were shown to be reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> Found in the center of the island, Bambara was inhabited by the Bambara people. The <a href="/wiki/Bambara_people" title="Bambara people">Bambara people</a> had an unfavorable stereotype; found in the mainland of Senegal and Mali, the Bambara were known for being excellent slaves. Brought to Gorée by the French, the Bambara people were set to build roads, forts and houses.</p>
<p>These buildings (Maison des Esclaves, Quartier Bambara, and Rue des Dungeons), made of stone or brick, contrasted with the structures built by the Africans made of straw and mud. This contrast aided in the segregation and status separation between the Africans and the European inhabitants and followed the common association that masonry was a European influence. However, the construction of these architectural buildings were most likely built by the slaves, and without floor plans, as indicated by the haphazard city layout and irregular angles in the rooms. Settlement analysis demonstrates the possibility that with time, the masters' and the enslaved peoples' statuses evened out enough to work and live side by side on the island by the second half of the eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gor%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gorée Island was the Pit Stop for Leg 4 of <a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Race_6#Leg_4_.28Sweden_.E2.86.92_Senegal.29" title="The Amazing Race 6">The Amazing Race 6</a>, and the Slave House itself was visited during Leg 5.</p>
<p>Gorée Island has been featured in many songs, due to its history related to the slave trade.</p>
<p>The following songs have significant references to Gorée Island:</p>
<ul>
<li>Steel Pulse - "Door Of No Return"</li>
<li>Doug E. Fresh - "Africa"</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Akon" title="Akon">Akon</a> - "Senegal"</li>
<li>Burning Spear - "One Africa"</li>
<li>Alpha Blondy - "Goree (Senegal)"</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuru_Kane" title="Nuru Kane">Nuru Kane</a> - "Goree"</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gilberto_Gil" title="Gilberto Gil">Gilberto Gil</a> - "La Lune de Goree", composed by <a href="/wiki/Gilberto_Gil" title="Gilberto Gil">Gilberto Gil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Carlos_Capinam" title="José Carlos Capinam">José Carlos Capinam</a></li>
<li>The father of French rapper <a href="/wiki/Booba" title="Booba">Booba</a> (born Elie Yaffa) is from Gorée. In his song "Garde la pêche" he mentions the island, saying "Gorée c'est ma terre" (Gorée is my land/hometown). Also, in his song "0.9," he says "A dix ans j'ai vu Gorée, depuis mes larmes sont eternelles" (When I was 10 I saw Gorée, since then my tears have been eternal."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marcus_Miller" title="Marcus Miller">Marcus Miller</a> - "Gorée (Go-ray)"</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2007 the Swiss director <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Yves_Borgeaud" title="Pierre-Yves Borgeaud">Pierre-Yves Borgeaud</a> made a documentary called <i><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retour_%C3%A0_Gor%C3%A9e" title="fr:Retour à Gorée">Retour à Gorée</a></i> (Return to Gorée).</p>
