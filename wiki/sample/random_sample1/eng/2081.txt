<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Gorgythion</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: Γοργυθίων, gen.: Γοργυθίωνος) was one of the sons of <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">King Priam</a> of <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a> and appears as a minor character in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>. His mother was Castianeira of <b>Aisyme</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parentage">Parentage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorgythion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Parentage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Near the end of the <i>Iliad</i>, Priam himself tells <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a>: "I begat the bravest sons in wide Troy, of whom I say that none are left. Fifty there were to me, when the sons of the Greeks arrived; nineteen indeed from one womb, but the others women bore to me in my palaces. And of the greater number fierce Mars indeed has relaxed the knees under them..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Gorgythion is referred to at his death as "...the brave son of Priam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup> Of Gorgythion's mother Castianeira, Homer says (in <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(novelist)" title="Samuel Butler (novelist)">Samuel Butler</a>'s translation) "His mother, fair Castianeira, lovely as a goddess, had been married from Aesyme."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i> says that Priam had nine sons and four daughters by <a href="/wiki/Hecuba" title="Hecuba">Hecuba</a> (the sons being <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris_(mythology)" title="Paris (mythology)">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deiphobus" title="Deiphobus">Deiphobus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helenus" title="Helenus">Helenus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pammon" title="Pammon">Pammon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polites_(Prince_of_Troy)" title="Polites (Prince of Troy)">Polites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antiphus" title="Antiphus">Antiphus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hipponous" title="Hipponous">Hipponous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polydorus" title="Polydorus">Polydorus</a>, and the daughters <a href="/wiki/Creusa" title="Creusa">Creusa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laodice_(mythology)" title="Laodice (mythology)">Laodice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyxena" title="Polyxena">Polyxena</a>, and the prophetess <a href="/wiki/Cassandra" title="Cassandra">Cassandra</a>), and he names thirty-eight sons by other women, including <a href="/wiki/Troilus" title="Troilus">Troilus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippothous" title="Hippothous">Hippothous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kebriones" title="Kebriones">Kebriones</a>, and Gorgythion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ap_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ap-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i>Fabulae</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Hyginus" title="Gaius Julius Hyginus">Gaius Julius Hyginus</a>, fable 90 consists wholly of a list of "Sons and daughters of Priam to the number of fifty-five", in which Gorgythion is included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_description">Name and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorgythion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <i>Iliad</i>, Gorgythion is described as beautiful, and his <a href="/wiki/Epithets_in_Homer" title="Epithets in Homer">epithet</a> is <i>the blameless</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jane_Ellen_Harrison" title="Jane Ellen Harrison">Jane Ellen Harrison</a> pointed out<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> that "blameless" (άμύμων) was an epithet of the heroized dead, who were venerated and appeased at shrines. Zeus even applies the epithet to <a href="/wiki/Aegisthus" title="Aegisthus">Aegisthus</a>, the usurper, Harrison observes.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The epithet άμύμων in Homer is applied to individual heroes, to a hero's tomb [<i>Odyssey</i> xxiv.80], to magical, half-mythical peoples like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phaeacia" title="Phaeacia">Phaeacians</a> and Aethiopians [<i>Iliad</i> x.423] who to the popular imagination are half canonized, to the magic island [<i>Odyssey</i> xii.261] of the god <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">Helios</a>, to the imaginary half-magical Good Old King [<i>Odyssey</i> xix.109]. It is used also of the 'convoy' [<i>Iliad</i> vi.171] sent by the gods, which of course is magical in character; it is never, I believe, an epithet of the Olympians themselves. There is about the word a touch of what is magical and <a href="/wiki/Daemon_(classical_mythology)" title="Daemon (classical mythology)">demonic</a> rather than actually divine."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In applying "blameless" to Gorgythion, then, the poet may have been reflecting a tradition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_(religion)" title="Cult (religion)">cult</a> among his descendents, that was known to Homer or in the Homeric tradition. John Pairman Brown has suggested that Gorgythion's name "surely echoes the Gergithes; the 'Gergithes remnants of the Teucrians' are projected back into the heroic age as individual antagonists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, the Gergithes were "the remnants of the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teucrian" title="Teucrian">Teucrians</a>" (that is, of the ancient Trojans).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorgythion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gorgythion is killed by an arrow of <a href="/wiki/Teucer" title="Teucer">Teucer</a>'s at lines 303-305 of Book VIII of the <i>Iliad</i>, although Teucer's target is Gorgythion's brother <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>. Teucer aims two arrows at Hector, but kills first Gorgythion and next Hector's friend Archeptolemus, which serves to increase the impression of Hector's elusiveness and strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Gorgythion dies, Homer says<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup> -</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">And as a poppy, which in the garden is weighed down with fruit and vernal showers, droops its head to one side, so did his head incline aside, depressed by the helmet.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Susanne Lindgren Wofford comments on this simile "But the poppy is not wilted or dead, just top-heavy; in any case, a poppy will return every spring to bow its head, but Gorgythion's death is final; it is a unique event that does not participate in any natural cycles of renewal or return... to make death seem beautiful is to transform it into something different."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>'s looser but more poetic translation (1715–1720), the death scene reads<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> -</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
<div class="poem">
<p>The weapon flies<br/>
At Hector’s breast, and sings along the skies:<br/>
He miss’d the mark; but pierced Gorgythio’s heart,<br/>
And drench’d in royal blood the thirsty dart.<br/>
(Fair Castianira, nymph of form divine,<br/>
This offspring added to king Priam’s line.)<br/>
As full-blown poppies, overcharged with rain,<br/>
Decline the head, and drooping kiss the plain;<br/>
So sinks the youth: his beauteous head, depress’d<br/>
Beneath his helmet, drops upon his breast.</p>
</div>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>This translation of the <i>Iliad</i> was called by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Samuel Johnson</a> "a performance which no age or nation could hope to equal", while <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bentley" title="Richard Bentley">Richard Bentley</a> wrote: "It is a pretty poem, Mr Pope, but you must not call it Homer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 4th century AD, a Roman called Q. Septimius published <i>Dictys Cretensis Ephemeridos belli Trojani</i>, purporting to be a translation by Lucius Septimius of a chronicle of the Trojan War by <a href="/wiki/Dictys_Cretensis" title="Dictys Cretensis">Dictys of Crete</a>, the companion of <a href="/wiki/Idomeneus" title="Idomeneus">Idomeneus</a> during the Trojan War. In Book 3, <a href="/wiki/Patroclus" title="Patroclus">Patroclus</a>, and not Teucer, is said to have killed Gorgythion:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Patroclus, however, had seen the enemy coming. Protected by his armor and holding a spear he had snatched from the ground, he resisted more boldly. He slew Gorgythion and drove off Deiphobus, Gorgythion’s brother, wounding him in the leg with his spear.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_name">Other uses of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorgythion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other uses of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The name Gorgythion was given to a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrginae" title="Pyrginae">Pyrginae</a>, North American <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spread-winged_Skippers" title="Spread-winged Skippers">Spread-winged Skippers</a>, in Frederick Du Cane Godman and O. Salvin's <i>Biologia Centrali Americana</i> (1896).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/48373_Gorgythion" title="48373 Gorgythion">48373 Gorgythion</a> is an asteroid of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, discovered on October 16, 1977, by C.
