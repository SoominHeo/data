<p>The <b>Gordian Knot</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> <a href="/wiki/Gordium" title="Gordium">Gordium</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>. It is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> for an intractable problem (disentangling an "impossible" knot) solved easily by loophole or "thinking outside the box" ("cutting the Gordian knot"):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>Turn him to any cause of policy,<br/>
The Gordian Knot of it he will unloose,<br/>
Familiar as his garter</p>
</div>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Henry_V_(play)" title="Henry V (play)">Henry V</a></i>, Act 1 Scene 1. 45–47</cite></div>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend">Legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gordian_Knot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At one time the <a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygians</a> were without a <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">king</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">oracle</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telmissus" title="Telmissus">Telmissus</a> (the ancient capital of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>) decreed that the next man to enter the city driving an <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">ox</a>-<a href="/wiki/Cart" title="Cart">cart</a> should become their king. A <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasant</a> farmer named <a href="/wiki/Gordias" title="Gordias">Gordias</a> drove into town on an ox-cart. His position had also been predicted earlier by an <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> landing on his cart, a sign to him from the gods, and, on entering the city, Gordias was declared king. Out of gratitude, his son <a href="/wiki/Midas" title="Midas">Midas</a> dedicated the ox-cart<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> to the Phrygian god <a href="/wiki/Sabazios" title="Sabazios">Sabazios</a> (whom the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> identified with <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>) and tied it to a post with an intricate <a href="/wiki/Knot" title="Knot">knot</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Cornel" title="European Cornel">cornel</a> (<i>Cornus mas</i>) bark. The ox-cart<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> still stood in the <a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palace</a> of the former kings of Phrygia at <a href="/wiki/Gordium" title="Gordium">Gordium</a> in the fourth century BCE when Alexander arrived, at which point Phrygia had been reduced to a <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrapy</a>, or province, of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a>.</p>
<p>Several themes of myth converged on the chariot, as <a href="/wiki/Robin_Lane_Fox" title="Robin Lane Fox">Robin Lane Fox</a> remarks:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Midas was connected in legend with Alexander's native <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedonia</a>, where the lowland "Gardens of Midas" still bore his name, and the Phrygian tribes were rightly remembered as having once dwelt in Macedonia. So, in 333 BCE, while wintering at Gordium, Alexander the Great attempted to untie the knot. When he could not find the end to the knot to unbind it, he sliced it in half with a stroke of his sword, producing the required ends (the so-called "Alexandrian solution").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, another solution is presented by Aristobulus, which indicates "he unfastened it quite easily by removing the pin which secured the yoke to the pole of the chariot, then pulling out the yoke itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> That night there was a violent thunderstorm. Alexander's prophet <a href="/wiki/Aristander" title="Aristander">Aristander</a> took this as a sign that Zeus was pleased and would grant Alexander many victories. Once Alexander had sliced the knot with a sword-stroke, his biographers claimed in retrospect<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> that an oracle further prophesied that the one to untie the knot would become the king of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexander is a figure of outstanding celebrity and the dramatic episode with the Gordian Knot remains widely known. Literary sources are Alexander's propagandist <a href="/wiki/Arrian" title="Arrian">Arrian</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabasis_Alexandri" title="Anabasis Alexandri">Anabasis Alexandri</a></i> 2.3) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintus_Curtius" title="Quintus Curtius">Quintus Curtius</a> (3.1.14), <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a>'s epitome of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompeius_Trogus" title="Pompeius Trogus">Pompeius Trogus</a> (11.7.3), and <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus" title="Claudius Aelianus">Aelian</a>'s <i>De Natura Animalium</i> 13.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While sources from antiquity agree that Alexander was confronted with the challenge of the knot, the means by which he solved the problem are disputed. Both <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arrian" title="Arrian">Arrian</a> relate that according to <a href="/wiki/Aristobulus_of_Cassandreia" title="Aristobulus of Cassandreia">Aristobulus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Alexander pulled the knot out of its pole pin, exposing the two ends of the cord and allowing him to untie the knot without having to cut through it. Some classical scholars regard this as more plausible than the popular account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexander later went on to conquer Asia as far as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a> thus, for Callisthenes, fulfilling the prophecy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gordian_Knot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The knot may have been a religious knot-cipher guarded by Gordian/Midas's priests and priestesses. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">Robert Graves</a> suggested that it may have symbolized the ineffable name of <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> that, knotted like a cipher, would have been passed on through generations of priests and revealed only to the kings of Phrygia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a>, true <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myth</a> has few completely arbitrary elements. This myth taken as a whole seems designed to confer legitimacy to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynastic" title="Dynastic">dynastic</a> change in this central <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolian</a> kingdom: thus Alexander's "brutal cutting of the knot... ended an ancient dispensation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The ox-cart suggests a longer voyage, rather than a local journey, perhaps linking Gordias/Midas with an attested origin-myth in Macedon, of which Alexander is most likely to have been aware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Based on the myth, the new dynasty was not immemorially ancient, but had widely remembered origins in a local, but non-priestly "outsider" class, represented by Greek reports equally as an <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponymous</a> peasant "Gordias"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or the locally attested, authentically Phrygian "Midas"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in his ox-cart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek myths</a> legitimize dynasties by right of conquest (compare <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a>), but the legitimizing <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">oracle</a> stressed in this myth suggests that the previous dynasty was a race of priest-kings allied to the unidentified oracle deity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_phrase">Use of the phrase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gordian_Knot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use of the phrase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The Gordian knot is alluded to in the motto of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanto_monta" title="Tanto monta">Tanto monta</a></i> ("It amounts to the same, (cutting as untying)")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proverbios_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proverbios-17">[17]</a></sup> and in the yoke representing Isabella<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in the emblem of the <a href="/wiki/Yoke_and_arrows" title="Yoke and arrows">yoke and arrows</a>.</li>
<li>Brian Coless has suggested that <a href="/wiki/Donald_Wiseman" title="Donald Wiseman">Donald Wiseman</a> "cut the Gordian knot" of "the intractable problem of identifying King <a href="/wiki/Darius_the_Mede" title="Darius the Mede">Darius the Mede</a>" in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Daniel" title="Book of Daniel">Book of Daniel</a>, by identifying Darius with <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Honoré de Balzac</a> makes a reference to the Gordian Knot in his novel, <a href="/wiki/Splendeurs_et_mis%C3%A8res_des_courtisanes" title="Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes">Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes</a>, in which he likens it to the relationship Lucien de Rubempré makes with <a href="/wiki/Vautrin" title="Vautrin">Vautrin</a> and French society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/W._G._Sebald" title="W. G. Sebald">W. G. Sebald</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Rings_of_Saturn" title="The Rings of Saturn">The Rings of Saturn</a> recounts the episode of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a> who was shot or shot himself in the chest allowing him to "Cut the Gordian Knot" of, in Sebald's telling, a stormy love affair. (Conrad did attempt suicide at age 20, in a severe depression most probably precipitated by his financial situation.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>* <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Upjohn" title="Lord Upjohn">Lord Upjohn</a>, speaking of the allocation of beneficial interests between the parties under a <a href="/wiki/Constructive_trust" title="Constructive trust">constructive trust</a> in <i>National Provincial Bank Ltd v Ainsworth</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> said that the parties' affairs are sometimes so inextricably intermixed that "an equitable knife must be used to sever the Gordian Knot".</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a> argues in his essay On Nature Itself that refusing to acknowledge an active force in things and instead "simply to absorb this force into a command of God’s - a command given just once in the past, having no effect on things and leaving no traces of itself in them - is so far from making the matter easier to grasp that it is more like abandoning the role of the philosopher altogether and cutting the Gordian knot with a sword."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Henry Purcell</a>, an English composer, wrote a piece of music entitled "The Gordion Knot Unty'd", <a href="/wiki/Catalogues_of_classical_compositions#List_of_catalogues" title="Catalogues of classical compositions">Z.</a> 597<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Spurgeon" title="Charles Spurgeon">Charles Spurgeon</a>, preaching at the Metropolitan Tabernacle in London, England, made mention of the "many gordian knots which wicked men may cut, and which righteous men may try to unravel, but which God alone can untie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albert_Camus" title="Albert Camus">Albert Camus</a>, in his lecture at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Uppsala" title="University of Uppsala">University of Uppsala</a> on December 14, 1957, used the Gordian knot as a metaphor for the <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> falling apart at the sword of rampant <a href="/wiki/Realpolitik" title="Realpolitik">politics of power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nihilism" title="Nihilism">nihilism</a> of the 20th century. He called for the newborn artists, the "anti-Alexanders", to heal the wound and repair the knot: "Yes, the rebirth is in the hands of all of us. It is up to us if the West is to bring forth any anti-Alexanders to tie together the Gordian Knot of civilization cut by the sword. For this purpose, we must assume all the risks and labors of freedom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>, "In Sein und Zeit Heidegger seems to have profited by study of his predecessors and to have been deeply impressed with this twofold necessity: (1) the relation between "human-realities" must be relation of being; (2) this relation must cause "human-realities" to depend on one another in their essential being. At least his theory fulfills these two requirements. In his abrupt, rather barbaric fashion of cutting Gordian knots rather than trying to untie them, he gives in answer to the question posited a pure and simple <i>definition</i>." (from "Being and Nothingness", Wash. Sq. Press, 1956, p330)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>The graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Watchmen" title="Watchmen">Watchmen</a></i> features a fictional Gordian Knot Lock Company, with a <a href="/wiki/Running_gag" title="Running gag">running gag</a> of the locks being easy to break with a single kick. The name of the company also alludes to its true owner, Ozymandias, who at one point styled himself as a new Alexander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>The card game, <a href="/wiki/Android:_Netrunner" title="Android: Netrunner">Android: Netrunner</a>, which simulates computer hacking, features a program card called "Gordian Blade". The card's flavor text says that "it can cut through the thickest knots of data".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>A gold brooch in the shape of a Gordian Knot features in the origin of the Ilvermorny School of Witchcraft and Wizardry, an American school that features in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.K._Rowling" title="J.K. Rowling">J.K. Rowling</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pottermore" title="Pottermore">Pottermore</a> writings. The brooch belonged to the mother of Isolt Sayre, who founded the Ilvermorny School of Witchcraft and Wizardry in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. An image of the knot is set into the middle of the stone floor of the school's entrance hall, and all Ilvermorny student robes are fastened by a gold Gordian Knot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the Phineas and Ferb episode Knot my problem, Phineas, Ferb, and their friends recreate the Gordian Knot with large strands of licorice, and Candace eats it after being shot by Dr. Doofenshmirtz's All-You-Can-Eat Inator.</li>
<li>In Submachine 8 - The Plan, someone references the Gordian Knot by saying, "It's not a toy. It's a knot. You have to untie it. Unless you're Alexander."</li>
</ul>
