<p><b>Gorki Leninskiye</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Го́рки Ле́нинские</span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Types_of_inhabited_localities_in_Russia" title="Types of inhabited localities in Russia">urban locality</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Urban-type_settlement" title="Urban-type settlement">work settlement</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Leninsky_District,_Moscow_Oblast" title="Leninsky District, Moscow Oblast">Leninsky District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Oblast" title="Moscow Oblast">Moscow Oblast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, located 10 kilometers (6.2 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> city limits and the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Ring_Road" title="Moscow Ring Road">Moscow Ring Road</a>. Population: <span class="nowrap">3,586 (<a href="/wiki/Russian_Census_(2010)" title="Russian Census (2010)">2010 Census</a>)</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010Census_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010Census-1">[1]</a></sup> <span class="nowrap">1,729 (<a href="/wiki/Russian_Census_(2002)" title="Russian Census (2002)">2002 Census</a>)</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopCensus_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopCensus-2">[2]</a></sup> <span class="nowrap">1,711 (<a href="/wiki/Soviet_Census_(1989)" title="Soviet Census (1989)">1989 Census</a>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census1989_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census1989-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The estate of Gorki belonged to various Muscovite noblemen from the 18th century. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zinaida_Morozova&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zinaida Morozova (page does not exist)">Zinaida Morozova</a>, the widow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savva_Timofeyevich_Morozov" title="Savva Timofeyevich Morozov">Savva Morozov</a>, purchased it in 1909, the year before she married General Anatoly Reinbot (later Anatoly Rezvoy), the Moscow head of police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She engaged the most fashionable Russian architect, <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Schechtel" title="Fyodor Schechtel">Fyodor Schechtel</a>, to remodel the mansion in the then current <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical</a> style, complete with a six-column Ionic portico.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lenin.27s_dacha">Lenin's dacha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorki_Leninskiye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lenin's dacha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the Soviet government moved to Moscow in 1918, it nationalized the luxurious estate and converted it into <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a>. In September 1918, the Soviet leader recuperated there following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fanni_Kaplan" title="Fanni Kaplan">an assassination attempt</a>. He spent an increasing amount of time there as his health declined over the following years. On May 15, 1923, Lenin followed medical advice and left the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Kremlin" title="Moscow Kremlin">Moscow Kremlin</a> for Gorki. He lived there in semi-retirement until his death on January 21, 1924.</p>
<p>After Lenin's death, Gorki was renamed "Gorki Leninskiye"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (meaning "Lenin's Gorki"). The house became a museum holding many of Lenin's possessions. Also located on the estate are a large museum built in 1987 concerning Lenin's life there, containing such artifacts as his <a href="/wiki/Lenin%27s_Testament" title="Lenin's Testament">Last Testament</a> (as transcribed by <a href="/wiki/Nadezhda_Krupskaya" title="Nadezhda Krupskaya">Nadezhda Krupskaya</a>), other documents, photos, books, Lenin's personal car (a <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Silver_Ghost" title="Rolls-Royce Silver Ghost">Rolls-Royce Silver Ghost</a>), his wheelchairs, and his apartment and office from the Kremlin, reconstructed in a separate building. A monument representing "The Death of the Leader" was unveiled in the 18th-century park in 1958.</p>
