<p>Traditionally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_classical_music" title="Western classical music">Western music</a>, a <b>musical tone</b> is a steady <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">periodic</a> sound. A musical tone is characterized by its <a href="/wiki/Duration_(music)" title="Duration (music)">duration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">intensity</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a> (or quality).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Musical_note" title="Musical note">notes</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> can be more complex than musical tones, as they may include aperiodic aspects, such as attack <a href="/wiki/Transient_(acoustics)" title="Transient (acoustics)">transients</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attack-Decay-Sustain-Release" title="Attack-Decay-Sustain-Release">envelope modulation</a>.</p>
<p>A simple tone, or <a href="#Pure_tone">pure tone</a>, has a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoidal</a> <a href="/wiki/Waveform" title="Waveform">waveform</a>. A complex tone is any musical tone that is not sinusoidal, but is periodic, such that it can be described as a sum of simple tones with harmonically related frequencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_tone">Pure tone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Musical_tone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pure tone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>

<p>A <b>pure tone</b> is a tone with a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave#Occurrences" title="Sine wave">sinusoidal</a> <a href="/wiki/Waveform" title="Waveform">waveform</a>, e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">sine</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosine" title="Cosine">cosine</a> wave. This means that regardless of other characteristic properties such as amplitude or phase, the wave consists of a single frequency. Sine and cosine waves are the most <a href="/wiki/Basis_function#Fourier_basis" title="Basis function">basic</a> building blocks of more complex waves, and as additional frequencies (i.e. additional sine and cosine waves having different frequencies) are <a href="/wiki/Superposition_principle" title="Superposition principle">combined</a>, the waveform transforms from a sinusoidal into a more complex shape.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine wave</a> is characterized by its <i>frequency</i>, the number of cycles per second—or its <i><a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a></i>, the distance the waveform travels through its medium within a period—and the <i><a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a></i>, the size of each cycle. A pure tone has the unique property that its waveshape and sound are changed only in amplitude and phase by linear acoustic systems.</p>
<p>A pure sine wave is an artificial sound. <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">Hermann von Helmholtz</a> is credited as the first creator of a sine wave, using the 'Helmholtz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siren_(noisemaker)" title="Siren (noisemaker)">siren</a>', a mechanical device that sends compressed air through holes in a rotating plate. This is presumably the closest thing to a sine wave that was heard before the invention of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_oscillator" title="Electronic oscillator">electronic oscillators</a>.</p>
<p>Sine waves are generally uncomfortable to the ear, and may cause <a href="/wiki/Noise-induced_hearing_loss" title="Noise-induced hearing loss">noise-induced hearing loss</a> at lower volumes than other noises.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Sound_localization" title="Sound localization">Sound localization</a> is often more difficult with sine waves than with other sounds; they seem to ‘fill the room’.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourier_theorem">Fourier theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Musical_tone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fourier theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_theorem" title="Fourier theorem">Fourier theorem</a> states that any periodic waveform can be approximated as closely as desired as the sum of a series of sine waves with frequencies in a <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_series_(mathematics)" title="Harmonic series (mathematics)">harmonic series</a> and at specific <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> relationships to each other.</p>
<p>The lowest of these frequencies (the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental frequency</a>), which is also the inverse of the period of the waveform, determines the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> of the tone, which is perceived by the human hearing. In music, <a href="/wiki/Musical_note" title="Musical note">notes</a> are assigned to tones with different fundamental frequencies, in order to describe the pitch of played tones.</p>
