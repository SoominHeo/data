<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wicker</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Wicker</b> is a material made of plant stalks, branches or shoots formed by a kind of <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> into a rigid material, most often used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baskets" title="Baskets">baskets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">furniture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> Wicker is traditionally made of material of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> origin, but <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> fibers are now also used. Wicker is light yet sturdy, making it suitable for furniture that will be moved often like <a href="/wiki/Porch" title="Porch">porch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patio" title="Patio">patio</a> furniture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfresia1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-alfresia1-2">[2]</a></sup> A variety of plants are used such as reeds, grasses (including <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>), creepers (including <a href="/wiki/Rattan" title="Rattan">rattan</a>), and thin tree branches (especially <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a>). <b>Rushwork</b> and wickerwork are terms used in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arts_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-arts-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wicker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wicker has been documented as far back as <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, made from indigenous "reed and swamp grasses."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> Middle-class families could only afford a few pieces, such as small tables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, archaeologists working on the tombs of the wealthy <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaohs</a> (rulers) have uncovered a wider variety of wicker items,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-saunders-6">[6]</a></sup> including "chests, baskets, wig boxes, and chairs.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> Wicker even found use in the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> on the battlefield, in <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The popularity of wicker passed from Egypt and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>. Wicker baskets were used to carry items in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Furniture was manufactured out of wicker in the Roman style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> It has been proposed that the extensive use of wicker in the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> (1200 BC – 400 AD in Europe) may have influenced the development of the woven patterns used in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By the 16th and 17th centuries, wicker was "quite common"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> in European countries like <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and England.</p>
<p>Wicker received a boost during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Exploration" title="Age of Exploration">Age of Exploration</a>, when international sea traders returned from <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a> with a species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palm_tree" title="Palm tree">palm</a> called <a href="/wiki/Rattan" title="Rattan">rattan</a>. Rattan is stronger than traditional European wicker materials,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-saunders-6">[6]</a></sup> although the rattan stem can be separated so the softer inner core can be used for wicker.</p>
<p>The 19th century brought immense popularity for wicker in Europe, England, and North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> It was used outdoors as well as indoors. People in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" title="Victorian Era">Victorian Era</a> believed it to be more sanitary than upholstered furniture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> It was inexpensive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aiasf_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-aiasf-9">[9]</a></sup> resisted harsh weather and was adaptable to many styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aiasf_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-aiasf-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_Wakefield" title="Cyrus Wakefield">Cyrus Wakefield</a> began constructing rattan furniture in the 1850s. He first used rattan that had been offloaded from ships, where it was used as ballast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saunders_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-saunders-6">[6]</a></sup> but as his designs became well-known, he began importing the material himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> Wakefield's company became one of the leading industries in wicker furniture;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> it later merged with the Heywood Chair Manufacturing Company (a wooden chair company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> that had invented a mechanical process for weaving wicker seats<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup>) to form the <a href="/wiki/Heywood-Wakefield_Company" title="Heywood-Wakefield Company">Heywood-Wakefield</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gardner,_Massachusetts" title="Gardner, Massachusetts">Gardner, Massachusetts</a>, one of the oldest and most prominent North American wicker manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhistory_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-randomhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent times, its aesthetic was influenced heavily by the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a> at the turn of the 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Wicker is still a popular material. Antique wicker products are highly sought after by collectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Reproductions of furniture and accent pieces are also sold for indoor and outdoor use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfresia3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-alfresia3-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> (In North America today, "rattan" and "wicker" are frequently used interchangeably.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Wickerwork is an important industry in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, employing hundreds of skilled workers to create goods for export to western Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture">Manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wicker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wicker can be made from natural or synthetic materials, or a combination. In furniture, such as benches, chairs, stools and other seating devices, a frame is typically made of stiffer materials, after which more pliant material is woven into the frame to fill it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In a smaller piece such as a basket, a strengthening frame is not needed so the entire piece is woven from the wicker material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Natural wicker is well known for its strength and durability, and for the high level of beauty and comfort that an expert craftsman can produce. Materials used can be any part of a plant, such as the cores of <a href="/wiki/Cane" title="Cane">cane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rattan" title="Rattan">rattan</a> stalks, or whole thicknesses of plants, as with <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> switches. Other popular materials include <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>. Natural wicker requires maintenance to keep it in good shape.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Synthetic types include paper-wrapped high tensile wire (using the <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Loom" title="Lloyd Loom">Lloyd Loom</a> process patented in the early 20th century), and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Resin#Synthetic_resins" title="Resin">resin</a>. The synthetic wickers are often preferred for outdoor use ("all-weather wicker"). The frame material used in these more recent versions includes aluminum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfresia2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-alfresia2-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wicker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word wicker is from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>wiker</i>, of <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">Scandinavian</a> origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
