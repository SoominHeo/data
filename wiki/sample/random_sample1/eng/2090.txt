<p><i><b>Le Père Goriot</b></i> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[lə pɛʁ ɡɔʁjo]</a></span>, <i>Old Goriot</i> or <i>Father Goriot</i>) is an 1835 novel by French novelist and playwright <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Honoré de Balzac</a> (1799–1850), included in the <i>Scènes de la vie privée</i> section of his <a href="/wiki/Novel_sequence" title="Novel sequence">novel sequence</a> <i><a href="/wiki/La_Com%C3%A9die_humaine" title="La Comédie humaine">La Comédie humaine</a></i>. Set in Paris in 1819, it follows the intertwined lives of three characters: the elderly doting Goriot; a mysterious criminal-in-hiding named <a href="/wiki/Vautrin" title="Vautrin">Vautrin</a>; and a naive law student named <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_de_Rastignac" title="Eugène de Rastignac">Eugène de Rastignac</a>.</p>
<p>Originally published in <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">serial</a> form during the winter of 1834/35, <i>Le Père Goriot</i> is widely considered Balzac's most important novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finest_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-finest-1">[1]</a></sup> It marks the first serious use by the author of characters who had appeared in other books, a technique that distinguishes Balzac's fiction. The novel is also noted as an example of his <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realist</a> style, using minute details to create character and <a href="/wiki/Subtext" title="Subtext">subtext</a>.</p>
<p>The novel takes place during the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">Bourbon Restoration</a>, which brought profound changes to French society; the struggle by individuals to secure a higher social status is a major theme in the book. The city of Paris also impresses itself on the characters – especially young Rastignac, who grew up in the provinces of southern France. Balzac analyzes, through Goriot and others, the nature of family and marriage, providing a pessimistic view of these institutions.</p>
<p>The novel was released to mixed reviews. Some critics praised the author for his complex characters and attention to detail; others condemned him for his many depictions of corruption and greed. A favorite of Balzac's, the book quickly won widespread popularity and has often been adapted for film and the stage. It gave rise to the French expression "<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikt:Rastignac" title="fr:Wikt:Rastignac">Rastignac</a>", a social climber willing to use any means to better his situation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Le Père Goriot</i> begins in June 1819, following <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s defeat at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Waterloo</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> had been <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">restored</a> to the throne of France. Tension was mounting between the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a>, which had returned with <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII_of_France" title="Louis XVIII of France">King Louis XVIII</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During this era, France saw a tightening of social structures, with a lower class steeped in overwhelming poverty. By one estimate, almost three-quarters of Parisians did not make the 500–600 <a href="/wiki/Franc" title="Franc">francs</a> a year required for a minimal standard of living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kanes-3">[3]</a></sup> At the same time, this upheaval made possible a social mobility unthinkable during the <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a> of previous centuries. Individuals willing to adapt themselves to the rules of this new society could sometimes ascend into its upper echelons from modest backgrounds, much to the distaste of the established wealthy class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_background">Literary background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Balzac began writing <i>Le Père Goriot</i> in 1834, he had written several dozen books, including a stream of <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonymously</a> published <a href="/wiki/Potboiler" title="Potboiler">potboiler</a> novels. In 1829 he published <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Chouans" title="Les Chouans">Les Chouans</a></i>, the first novel to which he signed his own name; this was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Louis_Lambert" title="Louis Lambert">Louis Lambert</a></i> (1832), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Colonel_Chabert_(novel)" title="Le Colonel Chabert (novel)">Le Colonel Chabert</a></i> (1832), and <i><a href="/wiki/La_Peau_de_chagrin" title="La Peau de chagrin">La Peau de chagrin</a></i> (1831).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Around this time, Balzac began organizing his work into a <a href="/wiki/Novel_sequence" title="Novel sequence">sequence of novels</a> that he eventually called <i><a href="/wiki/La_Com%C3%A9die_humaine" title="La Comédie humaine">La Comédie humaine</a></i>, divided into sections representing various aspects of life in France during the early 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vidocq.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
French criminal <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Fran%C3%A7ois_Vidocq" title="Eugène François Vidocq">Eugène François Vidocq</a> was the basis for the character Vautrin in <i>Le Père Goriot</i>.</div>
</div>

<p>One of these aspects which fascinated Balzac was the life of crime. In the winter of 1828–29, a French <a href="/wiki/Confidence_trick" title="Confidence trick">grifter</a>-turned-policeman named <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Fran%C3%A7ois_Vidocq" title="Eugène François Vidocq">Eugène François Vidocq</a> published a pair of sensationalized memoirs recounting his criminal exploits. Balzac met Vidocq in April 1834, and used him as a model for a character named <a href="/wiki/Vautrin" title="Vautrin">Vautrin</a> he was planning for an upcoming novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_and_publication">Writing and publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Writing and publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the summer of 1834 Balzac began to work on a tragic story about a father who is rejected by his daughters. His journal records several undated lines about the plot: "Subject of Old Goriot – A good man – middle-class lodging-house – 600 fr. income – having stripped himself bare for his daughters who both have 50,000 fr. income – dying like a dog."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He wrote the first draft of <i>Le Père Goriot</i> in forty autumn days; it was published as a <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">serial</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Revue_de_Paris" title="Revue de Paris">Revue de Paris</a></i> between December and February. It was released as a novel in March 1835 by the publishing house of Werdet, who also published the second edition in May. A much-revised third edition was published in 1839 by Charpentier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As was his custom, Balzac made copious notes and changes on proofs he received from publishers, so that the later editions of his novels were often significantly different from the earliest. In the case of <i>Le Père Goriot</i>, he changed a number of the characters into persons from other novels he had written, and added new paragraphs filled with detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The character <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_de_Rastignac" title="Eugène de Rastignac">Eugène de Rastignac</a> had appeared as an old man in Balzac's earlier philosophical fantasy novel <i><a href="/wiki/La_Peau_de_chagrin" title="La Peau de chagrin">La Peau de chagrin</a></i>. While writing the first draft of <i>Le Père Goriot</i>, Balzac named the character "Massiac", but he decided to use the same character from <i>La Peau de chagrin</i>. Other characters were changed in a similar fashion. It was his first structured use of recurring characters, a practice whose depth and rigor came to characterize his novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1843 Balzac placed <i>Le Père Goriot</i> in the section of <i>La Comédie humaine</i> entitled "Scènes de la vie parisienne" ("Scenes of life in Paris"). Quickly thereafter, he reclassified it – due to its intense focus on the private lives of its characters – as one of the "Scènes de la vie privée" ("Scenes of private life").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These categories and the novels in them were his attempt to create a body of work "depicting all society, sketching it in the immensity of its turmoil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balzac_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balzac-13">[13]</a></sup> Although he had prepared only a small predecessor for <i>La Comédie humaine</i>, entitled <i>Études de Mœurs</i>, at this time, Balzac carefully considered each work's place in the project and frequently rearranged its structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel opens with an extended description of the Maison Vauquer, a <a href="/wiki/Boarding_house" title="Boarding house">boarding house</a> in Paris' <i>rue Neuve-Sainte-Geneviève</i> covered with vines, owned by the widow Madame Vauquer. The residents include the law student Eugène de Rastignac, a mysterious agitator named Vautrin, and an elderly retired <a href="/wiki/Vermicelli" title="Vermicelli">vermicelli</a>-maker named Jean-Joachim Goriot. The old man is ridiculed frequently by the other boarders, who soon learn that he has bankrupted himself to support his two well-married daughters.</p>
<p>Rastignac, who moved to Paris from the south of France, becomes attracted to the upper class. He has difficulty fitting in, but is tutored by his cousin, Madame de Beauséant, in the ways of high society. Rastignac endears himself to one of Goriot's daughters, Delphine, after extracting money from his own already-poor family. Vautrin, meanwhile, tries to convince Rastignac to pursue an unmarried woman named Victorine, whose family fortune is blocked only by her brother. He offers to clear the way for Rastignac by having the brother killed in a <a href="/wiki/Duel" title="Duel">duel</a>.</p>
<p>Rastignac refuses to go along with the plot, balking at the idea of having someone killed to acquire their wealth, but he takes note of Vautrin's machinations. This is a lesson in the harsh realities of high society. Before long, the boarders learn that police are seeking Vautrin, revealed to be a master criminal nicknamed <i>Trompe-la-Mort</i> ("Cheater of Death"). Vautrin arranges for a friend to kill Victorine's brother, in the meantime, and is captured by the police.</p>
<p>Goriot, supportive of Rastignac's interest in his daughter and furious with her husband's tyrannical control over her, finds himself unable to help. When his other daughter, Anastasie, informs him that she has been selling off her husband's family jewelry to pay her lover's debts, the old man is overcome with grief at his own impotence and suffers a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>.</p>
<p>Delphine does not visit Goriot as he lies on his deathbed, and Anastasie arrives too late, only once he has lost consciousness. Before dying, Goriot rages about their disrespect toward him. His funeral is attended only by Rastignac, a servant named Christophe, and two <a href="/wiki/Professional_mourning" title="Professional mourning">paid mourners</a>. Goriot's daughters, rather than being present at the funeral, send their empty coaches, each bearing their families' respective coat of arms. After the short ceremony, Rastignac turns to face Paris as the lights of evening begin to appear. He sets out to dine with Delphine de Nucingen and declares to the city: "À nous deux, maintenant!" ("It's between you and me now!")</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Balzac's style in <i>Le Père Goriot</i> is influenced by the American novelist <a href="/wiki/James_Fenimore_Cooper" title="James Fenimore Cooper">James Fenimore Cooper</a> and Scottish writer <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>. In Cooper's representations of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>, Balzac saw a human barbarism that survived through attempts at civilization. In a preface to the second edition in 1835, Balzac wrote that the title character Goriot – who made his fortune selling <a href="/wiki/Vermicelli" title="Vermicelli">vermicelli</a> during a time of widespread hunger – was an "<a href="/wiki/Illinois_Confederation" title="Illinois Confederation">Illinois</a> of the flour trade" and a "<a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Huron</a> of the grain market".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kan4-5_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-kan4-5-15">[15]</a></sup> Vautrin refers to Paris as "a forest of the New World where twenty varieties of savage tribes clash" – another sign of Cooper's influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scott was also a profound influence on Balzac, particularly in his use of real historical events as the backdrop for his novels. Although history is not central to <i>Le Père Goriot</i>, the post-Napoleonic era serves as an important setting, and Balzac's use of meticulous detail reflects the influence of Scott.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kan4-5_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-kan4-5-15">[15]</a></sup> In his 1842 introduction to <i>La Comédie humaine</i>, Balzac praises Scott as a "modern troubadour" who "vivified [literature] with the spirit of the past".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balzac_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balzac-13">[13]</a></sup> At the same time, Balzac accused the Scottish writer of romanticizing history, and tried to distinguish his own work with a more balanced view of human nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kan4-5_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-kan4-5-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although the novel is often referred to as "a mystery",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> it is not an example of <a href="/wiki/Whodunit" title="Whodunit">whodunit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a>. Instead, the central puzzles are the origins of suffering and the motivations of unusual behavior. Characters appear in fragments, with brief scenes providing small clues about their identity. Vautrin, for example, slips in and out of the story – offering advice to Rastignac, ridiculing Goriot, bribing the housekeeper Christophe to let him in after hours – before he is revealed as a master criminal. This pattern of people moving in and out of view mirrors Balzac's use of characters throughout <i>La Comédie humaine</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Le Père Goriot</i> is also recognized as a <i><a href="/wiki/Bildungsroman" title="Bildungsroman">bildungsroman</a></i>, wherein a naive young person matures while learning the ways of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Rastignac is tutored by Vautrin, Madame de Beauséant, Goriot, and others about the truth of Parisian society and the coldly dispassionate and brutally realistic strategies required for social success. As an <a href="/wiki/Everyman" title="Everyman">everyman</a>, he is initially repulsed by the gruesome realities beneath society's gilded surfaces; eventually, however, he embraces them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Setting aside his original goal of mastering the law, he pursues money and women as instruments for social climbing. In some ways this mirrors Balzac's own social education, reflecting the distaste he acquired for the law after studying it for three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurring_characters">Recurring characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recurring characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Le Père Goriot</i>, especially in its revised form, marks an important early instance of Balzac's trademark use of recurring characters: persons from earlier novels appear in later works, usually during significantly different times of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Pleased with the effect he achieved with the return of Rastignac, Balzac included 23 characters in the first edition of <i>Le Père Goriot</i> that would recur in later works; during his revisions for later editions the number increased to 48.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Although Balzac had used this technique before, the characters had always reappeared in minor roles, as nearly identical versions of the same people. Rastignac's appearance shows, for the first time in Balzac's fiction, a novel-length <a href="/wiki/Backstory" title="Backstory">backstory</a> that illuminates and develops a returning character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Balzac experimented with this method throughout the thirty years he worked on <i>La Comédie humaine</i>. It enabled a depth of <a href="/wiki/Characterization" title="Characterization">characterization</a> that went beyond simple narration or dialogue. "When the characters reappear", notes the critic <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Rogers" title="Samuel Rogers">Samuel Rogers</a>, "they do not step out of nowhere; they emerge from the privacy of their own lives which, for an interval, we have not been allowed to see."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Although the complexity of these characters' lives inevitably led Balzac to make errors of chronology and consistency, the mistakes are considered minor in the overall scope of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Readers are more often troubled by the sheer number of people in Balzac's world, and feel deprived of important context for the characters. Detective novelist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> said that he never tried to read Balzac, because he "did not know where to begin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>This pattern of character reuse had repercussions for the plot of <i>Le Père Goriot</i>. Baron de Nucingen's reappearance in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Maison_Nucingen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Maison Nucingen (page does not exist)">La Maison Nucingen</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Maison_Nucingen" title="fr:La Maison Nucingen">fr</a>)</span></i> (1837) reveals that his wife's love affair with Rastignac was planned and coordinated by the baron himself. This new detail sheds considerable light on the actions of all three characters within the pages of <i>Le Père Goriot</i>, complementing the evolution of their stories in the later novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Realism">Realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Balzac uses meticulous, abundant detail to describe the Maison Vauquer, its inhabitants, and the world around them; this technique gave rise to his title as the father of the <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">realist novel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The details focus mostly on the penury of the residents of the Maison Vauquer. Much less intricate are the descriptions of wealthier homes; Madame de Beauséant's rooms are given scant attention, and the Nucingen family lives in a house sketched in the briefest detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the start of the novel, Balzac declares (in English): "All is true".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Although the characters and situations are fictions, the details employed – and their reflection of the realities of life in Paris at the time – faithfully render the world of the Maison Vauquer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The rue Neuve-Sainte-Geneviève (where the house is located) presents "a grim look about the houses, a suggestion of a jail about those high garden walls".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The interiors of the house are painstakingly described, from the shabby sitting room ("Nothing can be more depressing") to the coverings on the walls depicting a feast ("papers that a little suburban tavern would have disdained") – an ironic decoration in a house known for its wretched food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Balzac owed the former detail to the expertise of his friend Hyacinthe de Latouche, who was trained in the practice of hanging wallpaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The house is even defined by its repulsive smell, unique to the poor boardinghouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_stratification">Social stratification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Social stratification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the main themes in <i>Le Père Goriot</i> is the quest to understand and ascend <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">society's strata</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_1814" title="Charter of 1814">Charter of 1814</a> granted by King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII_of_France" title="Louis XVIII of France">Louis XVIII</a> had established a "legal country" which allowed only a small group of the nation's most wealthy men to vote. Thus, Rastignac's drive to achieve social status is evidence not only of his personal ambition but also of his desire to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Body_politic" title="Body politic">body politic</a>. As with Scott's characters, Rastignac epitomizes, in his words and actions, the <i><a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">Zeitgeist</a></i> in which he lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanes_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kanes-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through his characters and narration, Balzac lays bare the <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a> of this society. In one particularly blunt speech, Madame de Beauséant tells Rastignac:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The more cold-blooded your calculations, the further you will go. Strike ruthlessly; you will be feared. Men and women for you must be nothing more than post-horses; take a fresh relay, and leave the last to drop by the roadside; in this way you will reach the goal of your ambition. You will be nothing here, you see, unless a woman interests herself in you; and she must be young and wealthy, and a woman of the world. Yet, if you have a heart, lock it carefully away like a treasure; do not let any one suspect it, or you will be lost; you would cease to be the executioner, you would take the victim's place. And if ever you should love, never let your secret escape you!<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This attitude is further explored by Vautrin, who tells Rastignac: "The secret of a great success for which you are at a loss to account is a crime that has never been discovered, because it was properly executed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This sentence has been frequently – and somewhat inaccurately – paraphrased as: "Behind every great fortune is a great crime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_Paris">Influence of Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influence of Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The novel's representations of social stratification are specific to Paris, perhaps the most densely populated city in Europe at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Traveling only a few blocks – as Rastignac does continually – takes the reader into vastly different worlds, distinguished by their architecture and reflecting the class of their inhabitants. Paris in the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">post-Napoleonic era</a> was split into distinct neighborhoods. Three of these are featured prominently in <i>Le Père Goriot</i>: the aristocratic area around the <a href="/wiki/Boulevard_Saint-Germain" title="Boulevard Saint-Germain">Boulevard Saint-Germain</a>, the newly upscale quarter of the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_la_Chauss%C3%A9e-d%27Antin" title="Rue de la Chaussée-d'Antin">rue de la Chaussée-d'Antin</a>, and the run-down area on the eastern slope of the <a href="/wiki/Montagne_Sainte-Genevi%C3%A8ve" title="Montagne Sainte-Geneviève">Montagne Sainte-Geneviève</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>These <i>quartiers</i> of the city serve as microcosms which Rastignac seeks to master; Vautrin, meanwhile, operates in stealth, moving among them undetected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Rastignac, as the naive young man from the country, seeks in these worlds a new home. Paris offers him a chance to abandon his far-away family and remake himself in the city's ruthless image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> His urban exodus is like that of many people who moved into the French capital, doubling its population between 1800 and 1830. The texture of the novel is thus inextricably linked to the city in which it is set; "Paris", explains critic <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brooks_(writer)" title="Peter Brooks (writer)">Peter Brooks</a>, "is the looming presence that gives the novel its particular tone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption">Corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rastignac, Vautrin, and Goriot represent individuals corrupted by their desires. In his thirst for advancement, Rastignac has been compared to <a href="/wiki/Faust" title="Faust">Faust</a>, with Vautrin as <a href="/wiki/Mephistopheles" title="Mephistopheles">Mephistopheles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Critic Pierre Barbéris calls Vautrin's lecture to Rastignac "one of the great moments of the <i>Comédie humaine</i>, and no doubt of all world literature".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> France's social upheaval provides Vautrin with a playground for an ideology based solely on personal advancement; he encourages Rastignac to follow suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Still, it is the larger social structure that finally overwhelms Rastignac's soul – Vautrin merely explains the methods and causes. Although he rejects Vautrin's offer of murder, Rastignac succumbs to the principles of brutality upon which high society is built. By the end of the novel, he tells Bianchon: "I'm in Hell, and I have no choice but to stay there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Rastignac desires wealth and social status, Goriot longs only for the love of his daughters: a longing that borders on <a href="/wiki/Idolatry" title="Idolatry">idolatry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Because he represents bourgeois wealth acquired through trade – and not aristocratic <a href="/wiki/Primitive_accumulation_of_capital" title="Primitive accumulation of capital">primitive accumulation</a> – his daughters are happy to take his money, but will see him only in private. Even as he is dying in extreme poverty, at the end of the book, he sells his few remaining possessions to provide for his daughters so that they might look splendid at a ball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_relations">Family relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Family relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The relations between family members follow two patterns: the bonds of marriage serve mostly as <a href="/wiki/Machiavellianism" title="Machiavellianism">Machiavellian</a> means to financial ends, while the obligations of the older generation to the young take the form of sacrifice and deprivation. Delphine is trapped in a loveless marriage to Baron de Nucingen, a money-savvy banker. He is aware of her extramarital affairs, and uses them as a means to extort money from her. Anastasie, meanwhile, is married to the comte de Restaud, who cares less about the illegitimate children she has than the jewels she sells to provide for her lover – who is conning her in a scheme that Rastignac has heard was popular in Paris. This depiction of marriage as a tool of power reflects the harsh reality of the unstable social structures of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<p>Parents, meanwhile, give endlessly to their children; Goriot sacrifices everything for his daughters. Balzac refers to him in the novel as the "Christ of paternity" for his constant suffering on behalf of his children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> That they abandon him, lost in their pursuit of social status, only adds to his misery. The end of the book contrasts Goriot's deathbed moments with a festive ball hosted by Madame de Beauséant – attended by his daughters, as well as Rastignac – suggesting a fundamental schism between society and the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The betrayal of Goriot's daughters is often compared to that of the characters in Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Balzac was even accused of plagiarism when the novel was first published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kan13_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kan13-56">[56]</a></sup> Discussing these similarities, critic <a href="/wiki/George_Saintsbury" title="George Saintsbury">George Saintsbury</a> claims that Goriot's daughters are "as surely murderesses of their father as [Lear's daughters] Goneril and Regan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> As Herbert J. Hunt points out in <i>Balzac's Comédie humaine</i>, however, Goriot's tale is in some ways more tragic, since "he has a Regan and a Goneril, but no Cordelia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The narrative of Goriot's painful relations with his children has also been interpreted as a tragicomic parable of Louis XVI's decline. At a crucial moment of filial sentiment in Balzac's novel, Vautrin breaks in singing "O Richard, O mon roi"—the royalist anthem that precipitated the October Days of 1789 and the eventual downfall of Louis XVI—a connection that would have been powerful to Balzac's readers in the 1830s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> An ill-founded faith in paternal legitimacy follows both Goriot and Louis XVI into the grave.</p>
<p>Rastignac's family, off-stage, also sacrifices extensively for him. Convinced that he cannot achieve a decent status in Paris without a considerable display of wealth, he writes to his family and asks them to send him money: "Sell some of your old jewelry, my kind mother; I will give you other jewels very soon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> They do send him the money he requests, and – although it is not described directly in the novel – endure significant hardship for themselves as a result. His family, absent while he is in Paris, becomes even more distant despite this sacrifice. Although Goriot and Vautrin offer themselves as father figures to him, by the end of the novel they are gone and he is alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_legacy">Reception and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P%C3%A8re_Goriot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reception and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Le Père Goriot</i> is widely considered Balzac's essential novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finest_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-finest-1">[1]</a></sup> Its influence on <a href="/wiki/French_literature" title="French literature">French literature</a> has been considerable, as shown by novelist <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9licien_Marceau" title="Félicien Marceau">Félicien Marceau</a>'s remark: "We are all children of <i>Le Père Goriot</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Brooks refers to its "perfection of form, its economy of means and ends".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-broix_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-broix-63">[63]</a></sup> Martin Kanes, meanwhile, in his book <i>Le Pére Goriot: Anatomy of a Troubled World</i>, calls it "the keystone of the <i>Comédie humaine</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It is the central text of Anthony Pugh's voluminous study <i>Balzac's Recurring Characters</i>, and entire chapters have been written about the detail of the Maison Vauquer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Because it has become such an important novel for the study of French literature, <i>Le Père Goriot</i> has been translated many times into many languages. Thus, says Balzac biographer <a href="/wiki/Graham_Robb" title="Graham Robb">Graham Robb</a>, "<i>Goriot</i> is one of the novels of <i>La Comédie humaine</i> that can safely be read in English for what it is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initial reviews of the book were mixed. Some reviewers accused Balzac of <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a> or of overwhelming the reader with detail and painting a simplistic picture of Parisian high society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kan13_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kan13-56">[56]</a></sup> Others attacked the questionable morals of the characters, implying that Balzac was guilty of legitimizing their opinions. He was condemned for not including more individuals of honorable intent in the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Balzac responded with disdain; in the second preface of 1835, he wrote with regard to Goriot: "Poor man! His daughters refused to recognize him because he had lost his fortune; now the critics have rejected him with the excuse that he was immoral."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many critics of the time, though, were positive: a review in <i>Le Journal des femmes</i> proclaimed that Balzac's eye "penetrates everywhere, like a cunning serpent, to probe women's most intimate secrets".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QKan15_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-QKan15-69">[69]</a></sup> Another review, in <i>La Revue du théâtre</i>, praised his "admirable technique of details".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QKan15_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-QKan15-69">[69]</a></sup> The many reviews, positive and negative, were evidence of the book's popularity and success. One publisher's critique dismissed Balzac as a "boudoir writer", although it predicted for him "a brief career, but a glorious and enviable one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QKan15_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-QKan15-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Balzac himself was extremely proud of the work, declaring even before the final installment was published: "<i>Le Père Goriot</i> is a raging success; my fiercest enemies have had to bend the knee. I have triumphed over everything, over friends as well as the envious."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> As was his custom, he revised the novel between editions; compared to other novels, however, <i>Le Père Goriot</i> remained largely unchanged from its initial version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kan13_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kan13-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the years following its release, the novel was often adapted for the stage. Two theatrical productions in 1835 – several months after the book's publication – sustained its popularity and increased the public's regard for Balzac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In the 20th century, a number of film versions were produced, including adaptations directed by <a href="/wiki/Travers_Vale" title="Travers Vale">Travers Vale</a> (1915), <a href="/wiki/Jacques_de_Baroncelli" title="Jacques de Baroncelli">Jacques de Baroncelli</a> (1922), and <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Russell" title="Paddy Russell">Paddy Russell</a> (1968). The name of Rastignac, meanwhile, has become an iconic <a href="/wiki/Sobriquet" title="Sobriquet">sobriquet</a> in the French language; a "<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikt:Rastignac" title="fr:Wikt:Rastignac">Rastignac</a>" is synonymous with a person willing to climb the social ladder at any cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-broix_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-broix-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another well known line of this book by Balzac is when <a href="/wiki/Vautrin" title="Vautrin">Vautrin</a> tells Eugene, "In that case I will make you an offer that no one would decline."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> This has been reworked by <a href="/wiki/Mario_Puzo" title="Mario Puzo">Mario Puzo</a> in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather" title="The Godfather">The Godfather</a></i> (1969) and <a href="/wiki/The_Godfather" title="The Godfather">its film adaptation</a> (1972); "I'm gonna make him an offer he can't refuse". It was ranked as the second most significant cinematic quote in <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movie_Quotes" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Movie Quotes">AFI's 100 Years...100 Movie Quotes</a> (2005) by the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>.</p>
