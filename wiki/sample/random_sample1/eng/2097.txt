<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Squeegee</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>squeegee</b> or <b>squilgee</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> with a flat, smooth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> blade, used to remove or control the flow of liquid on a flat surface. It is used for cleaning and in printing.</p>
<p>The earliest written references to squeegees date from the mid-19th century and concern deck-cleaning tools, some with leather rather than rubber blades. The name "squeegee" may come from the word "squeege", meaning press or squeeze, which was first recorded in 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The closely related "squeedging" was reportedly first used in 1782, in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Covent Garden Theatre</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> during the performing of the comedy "Which is the man?" by <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Cowley" title="Hannah Cowley">Hannah Cowley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Window_cleaning">Window cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Window cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rakel_fcm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2008" data-file-width="1518" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Rakel_fcm.jpg/220px-Rakel_fcm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Rakel_fcm.jpg/330px-Rakel_fcm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Rakel_fcm.jpg/440px-Rakel_fcm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rakel_fcm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A squeegee</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The best-known of these tools is probably the hand-held <b>window squeegee</b>, used to remove the cleaning fluid or water from a <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> surface. A <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soapy</a> solution acts as a lubricant and breaks up the dirt, then the squeegee is used to draw the now water-borne dirt off the glass leaving a clean surface. Some squeegees are backed with a sponge which can soak up soapy water from a bucket for application to a dirty window.</p>
<p>Squeegees were in use for cleaning windows by 1918 when an American book on navy jargon explained that a deck-cleaning tool called a squeegee was "used in civil life to clean windows".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This is the earliest written reference to a window cleaning squeegee given by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a>. (For earlier uses see "floor cleaning" section below.)</p>
<p>With the development of the <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscraper</a> in the 20th century, a more efficient tool for the cleaning of window exteriors was needed. Professional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Window_washer" title="Window washer">window washers</a> began using the <b>Chicago squeegee</b>, a bulky tool with two heavy pink rubber blades. Changing the blades required the loosening of twelve separate screws. The modern single-blade window cleaning squeegee was patented by Ettore Steccone in 1936; who dubbed it the "New Deal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meet-Ettore_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meet-Ettore-6">[6]</a></sup> It was made of lightweight brass with a very flexible and sharp rubber blade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Steccone began a manufacturing process and sold the product in his garage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meet-Ettore_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meet-Ettore-6">[6]</a></sup> The Ettore Products Co. is still the leader in the squeegee market today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Squeegee kits can include a telescoping pole to extend the washer's reach.</p>
<p>Simple squeegees are made in various shapes for household use, including the cleaning of shower doors, bathroom tile, and garage floors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "swivel method", or "fan method" as it is referred to by professionals, uses a series of strokes combined with turns that hold the water away from the leading edge of the squeegee; when the turn is completed in the opposing direction, there is no water and no dirt left isolated. However straight strokes, either horizontally or vertically are normally much more efficient than “fanning”. If a few spots are missed, a <a href="/wiki/Chamois_leather" title="Chamois leather">chamois leather</a> cloth works better for touch up than a towel of cloth or paper.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This is caused by the squeegee being angled incorrectly forcing the water under the rubber blade out into the dry area of the glass or from solution being pushed up into the top edge of the window. The squeegee should instead be tilted in the direction that the blade is moving across the glass to force to water into the wet area of the glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another method used by window cleaners is to tap the blade on an already wet area of the glass to remove any excess water on the rubber blade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Alternatively the rubber blade can be dried with a towel, although this method is slower and not practical when using extension poles.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a>, the world's fastest window cleaner is Terry Burrows of <a href="/wiki/South_Ockendon" title="South Ockendon">South Ockendon</a>, Essex, England, who cleaned three standard 45-inch × 45-inch office windows set in a frame in 9.24 seconds at the <a href="/wiki/National_Exhibition_Centre" title="National Exhibition Centre">National Exhibition Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> in March 2005. He used an 11.8-inch squeegee and 2.4 gallons of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Floor_cleaning">Floor cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Floor cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>floor squeegee</b> is similar to the window squeegee but has a long handle like a <a href="/wiki/Broom" title="Broom">push broom</a>, used to <a href="/wiki/Floor_cleaning" title="Floor cleaning">clean floors</a> after they have been sprayed with water or soap, to push the water into drains.</p>
<p>This is often used in places that need the floors cleaned regularly, such as army barracks or the meat departments in supermarkets. <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">Hospitals</a> sometimes use the floor squeegee to clean up any spills that occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operating_theatre" title="Operating theatre">operating rooms</a> or regular patient rooms as the design of the squeegee lends itself towards a more sanitary clean up.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decks_of_ships">Decks of ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decks of ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest quotations mentioning squeegees in the Oxford English Dictionary refer to their use in cleaning decks on board ship: in 1844 a "squee gee" in an American book,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in 1851 a "<a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leathern</a> squilgee" in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moby_Dick" title="Moby Dick">Moby Dick</a>, and in 1867 in a British book by Admiral <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Smyth" title="William Henry Smyth">William Henry Smyth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following are considered correct English terminologies, according to the [Canadian] Naval Terminology Standardization Committee:</p>
<ul>
<li>squeegee;</li>
<li>deck squeegee;</li>
<li>squilgee; and</li>
<li>squillagee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Street_cleaning">Street cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Street cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="627" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png/220px-Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png/330px-Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png/440px-Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rotating_squeegee_street_washing_machine.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kindling Street Washing Machine, also known as the Kindling Squeegee, c1911</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Squeegees on broom handles were used for street cleaning in the later nineteenth century. This was the case in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> by 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the early twentieth century some cities in Europe and North America used horse-drawn machinery with rotating rubber squeegee blades on rollers behind a water tank connected to sprinklers. In 1911 this was described as "a German invention which has been for some years in successful operation in leading German cities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grig_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-grig-16">[16]</a></sup> A US version of the rotating squeegee machine, known as the Kindling Squeegee or Kindling Street Washing Machine, was in use by the time of that description.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grig_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-grig-16">[16]</a></sup> It was manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> by Louis Kindling who had migrated from Germany to Wisconsin in 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> By 1915 some streets in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_DC" title="Washington DC">Washington DC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> were being cleaned by this kind of machine, while London still depended on men with hand brooms and squeegees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1919 Kindling got a US <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for a design with “new and useful Improvements in Squeegee-Rollers”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> following another "improvements" patent for squeegee street cleaning machines filed in 1915 by the inventor Samuel Whinery (resident of <a href="/wiki/East_Orange,_New_Jersey" title="East Orange, New Jersey">East Orange, New Jersey</a>) and published in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1914, William H. Connell (Chief, Bureau of Highways and Street Cleaning in Philadelphia) explained that the street cleaning was done in batteries of "two and three squeegee machines preceded by sprinklers" reportedly about 200 yards (180 m) ahead. The American Highway Engineers' Handbook of 1919 reveals that this method was used in order for the water:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[...] to loosen up the dirt on the pavement without giving it time to evaporate. [...] The idea of sprinkling is to soften the surface and enable the squeegee to cleanse the streets of all slime as well as the coarser materials. The squeegees are followed by two men, whom immediately sweep up the windrows of dirt into piles, and a sufficient number of carts follow to remove the dirt from the streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The need for supporting labour and foot was seen as a disadvantage. Further more, the squeegee machines were pulled by horses, which would defecate on the streets which were attempted to be cleansed. Therefor, they were gradually replaced by mechanical street cleaning devices, which were introduced as early as 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_and_photography">Printing and photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Printing and photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Screen_printing" title="Screen printing">screen printing</a>, a squeegee is used to spread ink evenly across the back of a <a href="/wiki/Stencil" title="Stencil">stencil</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkscreen" title="Silkscreen">silkscreen</a>, making a clean image on the printed surface. Screen-printing squeegees usually have much thicker and less flexible blades than the window cleaning variety.</p>
<p>A squeegee is also used in <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> printing on fabric to dry the <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">photographic paper</a> after it is washed, preventing wrinkles or water spots.</p>
<p>The earliest reference to a squeegee used for drying in photography is an 1878 description<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> by chemist and photographer <a href="/wiki/William_de_Wiveleslie_Abney" title="William de Wiveleslie Abney">William Abney</a> of squeezing excess water away. His squeegee had no handle, and was "a flat bar of wood, into which is let a piece of <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">india- rubber</a> about 1/2 centimetre thick and 2 centimetres broad." The user should note that "the india-rubber of the squeegee must be brought to bear with considerable pressure on to the surface of the paper, and the strokes made with it should commence from the centre and finish towards the ends."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ice on <a href="/wiki/Ice_rink" title="Ice rink">skating rinks</a> is resurfaced using a squeegee and other tools. Nowadays, they are all integrated in an <a href="/wiki/Ice_resurfacer" title="Ice resurfacer">ice resurfacer</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tennis_court" title="Tennis court">Tennis courts</a> sometimes have squeegees to help keep them dry and control the flow of water.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squeegee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stiff-bladed squeegees are used in addition to <a href="/wiki/Trowel" title="Trowel">margin trowels</a> and grout floats to apply <a href="/wiki/Grout" title="Grout">grout</a> or adhesive when applying ceramic <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiles</a> to a surface.</p>
<p>Small, hand-held plastic and rubber wedges with an edge formed as a blade are used in signwriting for the application of vinyl sheeting to decrease the possibility of air pockets. Signwriters' squeegees come in different models, some of which do not have handles, but are approximately the size and shape of a credit card.</p>
<p>Automobile squeegees are used in some universities to clean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalkboard" title="Chalkboard">chalkboards</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001, window washer Jan Demczur used a squeegee to free himself and five others from an elevator shaft in the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a> in New York City. The squeegee is now currently on display at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smithsonian" title="Smithsonian">Smithsonian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
