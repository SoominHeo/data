<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Guided_bus#Rubber-tyred_.22trams.22_.28trolleybuses.29" title="Guided bus">Translohr and Bombardier Guided Light Transit</a></div>
<p>A <b>rubber-tyred metro</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">rapid transit</a> system that uses a mix of <a href="/wiki/Road_transport" title="Road transport">road</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> technology. The vehicles have <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheels</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyre_(wheel)" title="Tyre (wheel)">tires</a> which run on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_pad" title="Rolling pad">rolling pads</a> inside <a href="/wiki/Guide_bar" title="Guide bar">guide bars</a> for traction, as well as traditional railway steel wheels with deep <a href="/wiki/Flange" title="Flange">flanges</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" title="Rail tracks">steel tracks</a> for guidance through <a href="/wiki/Railroad_switch" title="Railroad switch">conventional switches</a> as well as guidance in case a tyre fails. Most rubber-tyred trains are purpose-built and designed for the system on which they operate. <a href="/wiki/Guided_bus" title="Guided bus">Guided buses</a> are sometimes referred to as '<a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">trams</a> on tyres', and compared to rubber-tyred metros.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The first idea for rubber-tyred railway vehicles was the work of Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Robert_William_Thomson" title="Robert William Thomson">Robert William Thomson</a>, the original inventor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic_tyre" title="Pneumatic tyre">pneumatic tyre</a>. In his patent of 1846<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> he describes his 'Aerial Wheels' as being equally suitable for, "the ground or rail or track on which they run".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tompkins.2C_Aerial_Wheel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tompkins.2C_Aerial_Wheel-3">[3]</a></sup> The patent also included a drawing of such a railway, with the weight carried by pneumatic main wheels running on a flat board track and guidance provided by small horizontal steel wheels running on the sides of a central vertical guide rail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tompkins.2C_Aerial_Wheel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tompkins.2C_Aerial_Wheel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> German occupation of Paris, the Metro system was used to capacity, with relatively little maintenance performed. At the end of the war, the system was so worn out that thought was given as to how to renovate it. Rubber-tyred metro technology was first applied to the <a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro" title="Paris Métro">Paris Métro</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">Michelin</a>, who provided the tyres and guidance system, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a>, who provided the vehicles. Starting in 1951, an experimental vehicle, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MP_51" title="MP 51">MP 51</a>, operated on a test track between Porte des Lilas and Pré Saint Gervais, a section of line not open to the public.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro_Line_11" title="Paris Métro Line 11">Line 11</a> <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2telet_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Châtelet (Paris Métro)">Châtelet</a> - <a href="/wiki/Mairie_des_Lilas_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Mairie des Lilas (Paris Métro)">Mairie des Lilas</a> was the first line to be converted, in 1956, chosen because of its <a href="/wiki/Grade_(slope)" title="Grade (slope)">steep grades</a>. This was followed by Line 1 <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Vincennes_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Château de Vincennes (Paris Métro)">Château de Vincennes</a> - <a href="/wiki/Pont_de_Neuilly_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Pont de Neuilly (Paris Métro)">Pont de Neuilly</a> in 1964, and Line 4 <a href="/wiki/Porte_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Porte d'Orléans (Paris Métro)">Porte d'Orléans</a> - <a href="/wiki/Porte_de_Clignancourt_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Porte de Clignancourt (Paris Métro)">Porte de Clignancourt</a> in 1967, converted because they had the heaviest traffic load of all Paris Métro lines. Finally, Line 6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle_-_%C3%89toile_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro_and_RER)" title="Charles de Gaulle - Étoile (Paris Métro and RER)">Charles de Gaulle - Étoile</a> - <a href="/wiki/Nation_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro_and_RER)" title="Nation (Paris Métro and RER)">Nation</a> was converted in 1974 to cut down <a href="/wiki/Train_noise" title="Train noise">train noise</a> on its many elevated sections. Because of the high cost of converting existing rail-based lines, this is no longer done in Paris, nor elsewhere; now rubber-tyred metros are used in new systems or lines only, including the new <a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro_Line_14" title="Paris Métro Line 14">Paris Métro Line 14</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Metro" title="Montreal Metro">first completely rubber-tyred metro system</a> was built in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, in 1966. <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Metro" title="Santiago Metro">Santiago Metro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City_Metro" title="Mexico City Metro">Mexico City Metro</a> are based on <a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro" title="Paris Métro">Paris Métro</a> rubber-tyred trains. A few more recent rubber-tyred systems have used automated, driverless trains; one of the first such systems, developed by <a href="/wiki/Matra" title="Matra">Matra</a>, opened in 1983 in <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a>, and others have since been built in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Metro_Line_14" title="Paris Metro Line 14">Paris Metro Line 14</a> was automated from its beginning (1998), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Metro_Line_1" title="Paris Metro Line 1">Line 1</a> was converted to automatic in 2007-2011. The first automated rubber-tyred system opened in <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, in February 1981. It is the Portliner linking Sanomiya railway station with Port Island.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg/220px-ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg/330px-ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg/440px-ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ST_SN5000_20061102_001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
5000 series <a href="/wiki/Guide_rail#Railway_guide_rail" title="Guide rail">central rail-guided</a> rubber-tyred rolling stock operated by Sapporo City Transportation Bureau, Japan, and built by <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki_Heavy_Industries_Rolling_Stock_Company" title="Kawasaki Heavy Industries Rolling Stock Company">Kawasaki Heavy Industries Rolling Stock Company</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The vehicle is in the form of <a href="/wiki/Electric_multiple_unit" title="Electric multiple unit">electric multiple unit</a>, with power supplied by both, of the <a href="/wiki/Guide_bar" title="Guide bar">guide bars</a>, which thus also serves as the third rail (the current is not picked up through the horizontal wheels, but through a separate lateral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_shoe" title="Contact shoe">pickup shoe</a>). The return current passes through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_shoe" title="Contact shoe">return shoe</a> to the top of one, or both of the <a href="/wiki/Rail_profile" title="Rail profile">rails</a>, or to the other guide bar, depending on the type of system.</p>
<p>The type of guideway used on a system varies between networks. Two parallel <a href="/wiki/Roll_way" title="Roll way">roll ways</a>, each the width of a tyre, are used, either of <a href="/wiki/Precast_concrete" title="Precast concrete">concrete</a> (<a href="/wiki/Montreal_Metro" title="Montreal Metro">Montreal Metro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lille_Metro" title="Lille Metro">Lille Metro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toulouse_Metro" title="Toulouse Metro">Toulouse Metro</a>, most part of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Metro" title="Santiago Metro">Santiago Metro</a>), <a href="/wiki/Concrete_slab" title="Concrete slab">concrete slab</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busan_Subway_Line_4" title="Busan Subway Line 4">Busan Subway Line 4</a>), <a href="/wiki/I-beam" title="I-beam">H-Shape</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_rolling" title="Hot rolling">hot rolled steel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro" title="Paris Métro">Paris Métro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City_Metro" title="Mexico City Metro">Mexico City Metro</a>, the non-underground section of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Metro" title="Santiago Metro">Santiago Metro</a>), or flat <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Municipal_Subway" title="Sapporo Municipal Subway">Sapporo Municipal Subway</a>). As on a railway, the driver does not have to steer, because the system relies on a redundant system of railway steel wheels with <a href="/wiki/Flange" title="Flange">flanges</a> on steel <a href="/wiki/Track_(rail_transport)" title="Track (rail transport)">rail tracks</a>. The Sapporo system is an exception as it uses a central <a href="/wiki/Guide_rail#Railway_guide_rail" title="Guide rail">guide rail</a> only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9hicule_Automatique_L%C3%A9ger" title="Véhicule Automatique Léger">VAL</a> system used in Lille and Toulouse has conventional track between the guide bars.</p>
<p>On some systems (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Metro" title="Paris Metro">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Metro" title="Montreal Metro">Montreal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City_Metro" title="Mexico City Metro">Mexico City</a>), there is a <span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span>) <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Track_(rail_transport)" title="Track (rail transport)">railway track</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rollway" title="Rollway">rollways</a> and the vehicles also have <a href="/wiki/Wheelset_(rail_transport)#Special_wheelsets" title="Wheelset (rail transport)">railway wheels</a> with larger (taller) than normal <a href="/wiki/Flange" title="Flange">flanges</a>, but these are normally at some distance above the rails and are used only in the case of a flat tyre and at <a href="/wiki/Railroad_switch" title="Railroad switch">switches/points</a> and <a href="/wiki/Level_junction" title="Level junction">crossings</a>. In Paris these rails were also used to enable mixed traffic with rubber-tyred and steel-wheeled trains using the same track, particularly during conversion from normal railway track. Other systems (e.g. Lille and Toulouse) have other sorts of flat tyre compensation and switching methods.</p>
<p>Rubber-tyres have higher <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">rolling resistance</a> when compared to traditional steel wheels, which leads to some advantages and disadvantages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg/220px-Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg/330px-Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg/440px-Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ligne-D--detail-de-l-essieu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>MPL-85</b> rolling stock in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyon_m%C3%A9tro" title="Lyon métro">Lyon métro</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Compared to steel wheel on steel rail, the advantages of rubber-tyred metro systems are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Smoother rides (with little jostling around).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Faster <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> and higher <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>, along with the ability to climb or descend steeper slopes (~<a href="/wiki/Grade_(slope)" title="Grade (slope)">gradient</a> 13%) than would be feasible with conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" title="Rail tracks">rail tracks</a>, which would likely need a <a href="/wiki/Rack_railway" title="Rack railway">rack</a> instead.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_a1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber-tyred_metro#endnote_a1">[1]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>For example, the rubber-tyred Line 2 of the <a href="/wiki/Lausanne_Metro" title="Lausanne Metro">Lausanne Metro</a> has grades of up to 12%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Shorter braking distances, allowing trains to be <a href="/wiki/Railway_signal" title="Railway signal">signalled</a> closer together.</li>
<li>Quieter rides in open air (both inside and outside the train).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Greatly reduced rail wear with resulting reduced maintenance costs of those parts.</li>
</ul>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metro_Mexico_City.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Metro_Mexico_City.jpg/220px-Metro_Mexico_City.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Metro_Mexico_City.jpg/330px-Metro_Mexico_City.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Metro_Mexico_City.jpg/440px-Metro_Mexico_City.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metro_Mexico_City.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>NM-73</b> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexico_City_metro" title="Mexico City metro">Mexico City metro</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The higher friction and increased rolling resistance cause disadvantages (compared to steel wheel on steel rail):</p>
<ul>
<li>Higher energy consumption.</li>
<li>Possibility of tyre blow-outs - not possible in railway wheels.</li>
<li>Hotter operation.</li>
<li>Weather variance. <i>(Applicable only to above-ground installations)</i>
<ul>
<li>Loss of the <a href="/wiki/Traction_(engineering)" title="Traction (engineering)">traction</a>-advantage in inclement weather (snow and ice).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_b1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber-tyred_metro#endnote_b1">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Heavier as steel rails remain for switching purposes, to provide electricity or <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">grounding</a> to the trains and as a safety backup.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_b2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber-tyred_metro#endnote_b2">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">Tyre</a>'s which frequently need to be replaced; contrary to rails using steel wheels, which need to be replaced less often. <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_b3"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber-tyred_metro#endnote_b3">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Creation of air pollution; tyres break down during use and turn into particulate matter, which can be dangerous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BTC_4005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/BTC_4005.jpg/220px-BTC_4005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/BTC_4005.jpg/330px-BTC_4005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/BTC_4005.jpg/440px-BTC_4005.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BTC_4005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busan_Subway_Line_4" title="Busan Subway Line 4">Busan Subway Line 4</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ol>
<li><small><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_a1"><b><a href="#ref_a1">^</a></b></span> Rubber-tyred wheels have better adhesion than traditional rail wheels. However, modern steel-on-steel rolling stock using distributed-traction with a high-proportion of powered axles, have narrowed the gap to the performance found in rubber-tyred rolling stock.</small></li>
<li><small><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_b1"><b><a href="#ref_b1">^</a></b></span> In order to reduce weather disruption, the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Metro" title="Montreal Metro">Montreal Metro</a> runs completely underground. On <a href="/wiki/Paris_M%C3%A9tro_Line_6" title="Paris Métro Line 6">Paris Métro Line 6</a>, upgrades of tyres (as used with cars) and special ribbed tracks have been tried out. The southernmost section of the <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Municipal_Subway" title="Sapporo Municipal Subway">Sapporo Municipal Subway</a> <a href="/wiki/Namboku_Line_(Sapporo)" title="Namboku Line (Sapporo)">Namboku Line</a> is also elevated, but is covered by an aluminum shelter to reduce weather disruption.</small></li>
<li><small><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_b2"><b><a href="#ref_b2">^</a></b></span> In effect, there are two systems running in parallel so it is more expensive to build, install and maintain.</small></li>
<li><small><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_b3"><b><a href="#ref_b3">^</a></b></span> Since rubber tyres have higher wear rates, they need more frequent replacement. Although steel wheels set is more expensive than tyres, the frequency of their respective replacements makes rubber tyres the more expensive option. Rubber tyres for guidance are needed.</small></li>
</ol>
<p>Although it is a more complex technology, most rubber-tyred metro systems use quite simple techniques, in contrary to <a href="/wiki/Guided_bus" title="Guided bus">guided buses</a>. Heat dissipation is an issue as eventually all traction energy consumed by the train — except the electric energy regenerated back into the substation during <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_brake#Dynamic_and_regenerative_electrical_brakes" title="Regenerative brake">electrodynamic braking</a> — will end up in losses (mostly heat). In frequently operated tunnels (typical metro operation) the extra heat from rubber tyres is a widespread problem, necessitating ventilation of the tunnels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_technologies">Similar technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-tyred_metro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Similar technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Automated_guideway_transit" title="Automated guideway transit">Automated driverless systems</a> are not exclusively rubber-tyred; many have since been built using conventional rail technology, such as London's <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Light_Railway" title="Docklands Light Railway">Docklands Light Railway</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_metro" title="Copenhagen metro">Copenhagen metro</a> and Vancouver's <a href="/wiki/SkyTrain_(Vancouver)" title="SkyTrain (Vancouver)">SkyTrain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Resort_Line" title="Disneyland Resort Line">Disneyland Resort Line</a> which uses converted rolling stocks from non-driverless trains, as well as <a href="/wiki/AirTrain_JFK" title="AirTrain JFK">AirTrain JFK</a> which links <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">JFK Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> with local subway and commuter trains. Most <a href="/wiki/Monorail" title="Monorail">monorail</a> manufacturers prefer rubber tyres.</p>
