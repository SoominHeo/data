<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Go Mi-young</h1>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This is a <a href="/wiki/Korean_name" title="Korean name">Korean name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Korean_family_names" title="List of Korean family names">family name</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(Korean_surname)" title="Go (Korean surname)"><i>Go</i></a>.</div>
<p><b>Go Mi-Young</b>, (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: 고미영, <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: 高美英, March 3, 1967 – July 11, 2009) was a <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> female mountaineer.</p>
<p>Together with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jae-Soo_Kim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jae-Soo Kim (page does not exist)">Jae-Soo Kim</a>, she became one of the first climbers to summit three 8,000-metre peaks in a single season when they climbed <a href="/wiki/Makalu" title="Makalu">Makalu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kangchenjunga" title="Kangchenjunga">Kangchenjunga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dhaulagiri" title="Dhaulagiri">Dhaulagiri</a> in six weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 11, 2009, after reaching the top of <a href="/wiki/Nanga_Parbat" title="Nanga Parbat">Nanga Parbat</a>, she fell off a cliff on the descent in bad weather and was later found dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was one of the leaders in the quest to become the first woman to scale the world's 14 highest peaks (<i>the <a href="/wiki/Eight-thousander" title="Eight-thousander">eight-thousanders</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
