<p><b>Thurman Lowell Tucker</b> (September 26, 1917 – May 7, 1993) was an American <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional baseball</a> player. A <a href="/wiki/Center_fielder" title="Center fielder">center fielder</a>, Tucker played in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> for nine seasons in the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a>. In 701 career games, Tucker recorded a <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a> of .255 and accumulated 24 <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a>, nine <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a>, and 179 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runs_batted_in" title="Runs batted in">runs batted in</a> (RBI). He was nicknamed "Joe E." Tucker because of his resemblance to comedian <a href="/wiki/Joe_E._Brown" title="Joe E. Brown">Joe E. Brown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-digest-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Born and raised in Texas, Tucker first played professionally with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siloam_Springs_Travelers" title="Siloam Springs Travelers">Siloam Springs Travelers</a>. After gradually progressing through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">minor league baseball</a>, he signed with the Chicago White Sox before the 1941 season. His major league debut came the following year and he spent two years as the White Sox's starting center fielder until he enlisted in the armed forces during World War II. Upon his return, Tucker played two more seasons for the White Sox. Subsequently, he was traded to the Cleveland Indians, for whom he played four years, and continued to play minor league baseball throughout the 1950s. After his retirement, he became a major league scout and insurance agent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thurman_Tucker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thurman Tucker was born on September 26, 1917, and raised in <a href="/wiki/Gordon,_Texas" title="Gordon, Texas">Gordon, Texas</a>. In high school, he was a three-sport athlete, playing baseball (where he was a <a href="/wiki/Second_baseman" title="Second baseman">second baseman</a>), <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>. After graduating in 1935, he played semi-professional baseball and enrolled in a baseball school located in <a href="/wiki/Hot_Springs,_Arkansas" title="Hot Springs, Arkansas">Hot Springs, Arkansas</a>. In 1936, at age 18, Tucker signed as a professional with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fayetteville_Bears" title="Fayetteville Bears">Fayetteville Bears</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas%E2%80%93Missouri_League" title="Arkansas–Missouri League">Arkansas–Missouri League</a>, but left the team after only two weeks, without playing a game. Soon after, he was signed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siloam_Springs_Travelers" title="Siloam Springs Travelers">Siloam Springs Travelers</a> of the same league, where he began his professional career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_league_career">Minor league career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thurman_Tucker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Minor league career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1936, his first season with the Travelers, Tucker changed fielding positions and became an outfielder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-2">[2]</a></sup> In 117 games, he had a .319 <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a> and 25 <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">doubles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> The following year, he was to play for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Dorado_Lions" title="El Dorado Lions">El Dorado Lions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cotton_States_League" title="Cotton States League">Cotton States League</a>, but a back injury caused him to miss nearly the entire season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-2">[2]</a></sup> After recovering from the injury, Tucker continued to progress through the minors, spending 1938 with two separate clubs; he played 55 games for the <a href="/wiki/Abbeville_A%27s" title="Abbeville A's">Abbeville A's</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Evangeline_Baseball_League" title="Evangeline Baseball League">Evangeline Baseball League</a> and 50 games for the <a href="/wiki/Greenville_Bucks" title="Greenville Bucks">Greenville Bucks</a> of the Cotton States League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> He remained in the Cotton States League for 1939, playing for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarksdale_Red_Sox" title="Clarksdale Red Sox">Clarksdale Red Sox</a>, at the time a minor league affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>. In 136 games for Clarksdale, Tucker had a .298 batting average and 10 <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> During his first few years in the minor leagues, one manager asked him to take up <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clowning" title="Clowning">clowning</a> due to his resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Joe_E._Brown" title="Joe E. Brown">Joe E. Brown</a> and his nickname as a result, Joe E. Tucker objected due to his serious nature and the idea was later dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tucker's breakthrough minor league year came in 1940, his second with Clarksdale. By the end of June, he was leading the Cotton State League with a batting average of .374.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After playing in 97 games, finishing with an average of .390, Tucker was promoted and played in 40 games for the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_Indians" title="Oklahoma City Indians">Oklahoma City Indians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> At the end of the season, Chicago White Sox <a href="/wiki/Farm_team" title="Farm team">farm</a> manager Billy Webb was impressed enough to purchase Tucker's contract from Oklahoma City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> At the beginning of the 1941 season, Tucker failed to win the final outfield spot on the White Sox roster from <a href="/wiki/Dave_Short" title="Dave Short">Dave Short</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and consequently spent 1941 at Oklahoma City, where he was coached by <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Hornsby" title="Rogers Hornsby">Rogers Hornsby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 141 games for the Indians, Tucker had a batting average of .246 and 12 triples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>At spring training for the 1942 season, Tucker competed against <a href="/wiki/Dave_Philley" title="Dave Philley">Dave Philley</a> for the final outfield spot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> White Sox management liked Tucker's defensive abilities, leading to them adding Tucker to their 1942 major league roster; Tucker made his major league debut on April 14, 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> After playing two games for the White Sox, Tucker was sent down to the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth_Cats_(Texas_League)" title="Fort Worth Cats (Texas League)">Fort Worth Cats</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_League" title="Texas League">Texas League</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> where he spent most of the season and hit .313 in 144 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> When the minor league season ended, Tucker returned to the major league squad, and finished the year having played seven games for the White Sox.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicago_White_Sox">Chicago White Sox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thurman_Tucker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chicago White Sox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of the Chicago White Sox outfielders at the beginning of the 1943 season, only <a href="/wiki/Wally_Moses" title="Wally Moses">Wally Moses</a> was assured of a place on the team. During spring training, manager <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dykes" title="Jimmy Dykes">Jimmy Dykes</a> was impressed by Tucker, who was competing for the starting center fielder position alongside <a href="/wiki/Moose_Solters" title="Moose Solters">Moose Solters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Consequently, after spring training Tucker became the starting center fielder for 1943, and the team's <a href="/wiki/Leadoff_hitter" title="Leadoff hitter">leadoff hitter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Partway through the season, Tucker's performance caught the eye of American League President <a href="/wiki/Will_Harridge" title="Will Harridge">Will Harridge</a>, who noted him as someone the public came out to watch in the absence of stars serving in World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Among Tucker's achievements during the year were a <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">walk-off home run</a> on July 26 to win a game against the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> 2–1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Tucker finished the season with a .235 batting average, six triples, and 79 <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walks</a> in 135 games. He also stole 29 bases, the third best total in the AL, and was caught stealing 17 times, which was second in the league.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tucker passed a physical examination for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> before the 1944 season began. Although expected to be called up to serve in the war that year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> he was able to play the entire season for the White Sox. Tucker hit very well during the first month of 1944; he had a .403 batting average on May 16, which led the American League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> His hitting and fielding abilities impressed critics: sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Fred_Lieb" title="Fred Lieb">Fred Lieb</a> noted him as a breakout performer that year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and manager Jimmy Dykes called Tucker the finest defensive outfielder in the American League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Tucker and <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Walker" title="Dixie Walker">Dixie Walker</a> led their respective leagues in batting average throughout June; at the end of the month, Tucker had an average of .369 in the American League while Walker had an average of .377 in the National League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Owing to his achievements, Tucker was added to the 1944 <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star</a> roster for the only time in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He was the leadoff hitter in the <a href="/wiki/1944_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1944 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">1944 Major League Baseball All-Star Game</a>, but went hitless in four at-bats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tucker's form faded after the All-Star Game; in early July, he had a hitless streak of 28 at-bats, causing his batting average to shrink from .375 to .327, resulting in losing his status as league leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> When his average fell to .320 after recording one base hit in 35 at-bats, he was removed from the starting lineup for a weekend matchup against the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> in an attempt to halt his decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Tucker returned to the starting lineup shortly after being removed, and finished the season with a batting average of .287 and six triples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> At the end of July that season, both Tucker and <a href="/wiki/George_Case" title="George Case">George Case</a> participated in a 75-yard dash as part of the White Sox's annual benefit for the war effort; Tucker lost the race to Case by a yard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> After the season ended, Tucker formally joined the Navy, and spent the 1945 season serving in the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the players returned to their teams at the end of the war, Tucker was slated to be the starting center fielder for the 1946 season, working alongside Wally Moses and <a href="/wiki/Taffy_Wright" title="Taffy Wright">Taffy Wright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Unlike the previous season, he struggled with the bat at first, and was relegated to his original status on the White Sox roster as a good fielder but a poor hitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At the end of June, Tucker had a batting average of .229, nearly 150 points lower than his average at the same point in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> His form recovered in the second half of the season and he finished the year with a batting average of .288 and 20 doubles, both career highs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/1947_Chicago_White_Sox_season" title="1947 Chicago White Sox season">1947 Chicago White Sox season</a> began, Tucker remained in his center field position after hitting .400 in the last month of the 1946 season, while rookie Dave Philley played in left field and Taffy Wright and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kennedy" title="Bob Kennedy">Bob Kennedy</a> <a href="/wiki/Platoon_system" title="Platoon system">platooned</a> in right field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, he started the year by missing some playing time due to a stomach ailment, and did not play regularly for the White Sox until the middle of May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> After returning to regular play, Tucker alternated playing time with Philley, and he finished the season with a .236 batting average in 89 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleveland_Indians">Cleveland Indians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thurman_Tucker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cleveland Indians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 27, 1948, Tucker was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Weigel" title="Ralph Weigel">Ralph Weigel</a>; the Indians front office regarded Tucker as "the finest defensive player in baseball."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He started off the year as the starting center fielder for the Indians, and in one early matchup against the Detroit Tigers, was the only player not to record a hit in an 8–2 victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He missed three weeks of playing time in June after breaking a finger when he was hit by a pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Upon returning to the lineup, Tucker spent most of the second half of the season as a <a href="/wiki/Fourth_outfielder" title="Fourth outfielder">fourth outfielder</a>, splitting time with <a href="/wiki/Allie_Clark" title="Allie Clark">Allie Clark</a> and playing in the outfield alongside <a href="/wiki/Larry_Doby" title="Larry Doby">Larry Doby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dale_Mitchell_(baseball)" title="Dale Mitchell (baseball)">Dale Mitchell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He finished the season with a .260 batting average and 52 runs in 83 games, and ended the season with a perfect <a href="/wiki/Fielding_percentage" title="Fielding percentage">fielding percentage</a> of 1.000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> Tucker participated in game six of the <a href="/wiki/1948_World_Series" title="1948 World Series">1948 World Series</a>, scoring a run in the sixth inning on a <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walk</a> en route to a 4–3 win for the Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Indians planned to use Tucker as their fourth outfielder for the 1949 season when he served mostly as backup to Doby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> He was relegated mostly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinch_hitting" title="Pinch hitting">pinch hitting</a> duties, and finished the season with a .244 batting average in 20 games and under 200 at-bats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> Due to his hitting struggles, Indians manager <a href="/wiki/Lou_Boudreau" title="Lou Boudreau">Lou Boudreau</a> tried converting Tucker to a <a href="/wiki/Switch_hitter" title="Switch hitter">switch hitter</a> to start off the 1950 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Tucker was again set to be a backup outfielder in 1950. His performances during the year included hitting a home run, the only one he hit that season, in an 8–5 victory over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>; the teams raised $60,000 in that game to benefit <a href="/wiki/Sandlot_ball" title="Sandlot ball">sandlot</a> teams in Cleveland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Tucker finished the season with a .178 batting average in 54 games, the lowest mark of his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tucker and Allie Clark both attempted to make the Indians roster to begin the 1951 season, as the additions of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Simpson" title="Harry Simpson">Harry Simpson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnie_Mi%C3%B1oso" title="Minnie Miñoso">Minnie Miñoso</a> made it likely that one or both of them would be traded or released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Tucker played only one game for the Indians, on April 29 when he recorded a strikeout in his lone at-bat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup> In early May, the Indians sent him to their <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_(baseball)" title="Triple-A (baseball)">Triple-A minor league affiliate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres_(PCL)" title="San Diego Padres (PCL)">San Diego Padres</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League" title="Pacific Coast League">Pacific Coast League</a>; his last major league game was April 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thurman_Tucker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After being sent to the Padres, Tucker completed the 1951 season with them. In 88 games, Tucker had two triples and a .222 batting average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> In the offseason, Tucker operated his own taxicab in Texas, and he contemplated retirement from baseball during a contract dispute in February 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> He eventually played 47 games for the now-unaffiliated Padres, hitting .225 in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> In mid-June, the Padres sold his contract to the Oklahoma City Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Tucker played in 72 games for the Indians that season, hitting .263. He retired from baseball before the 1953 season, and did not play with any professional team during that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tucker returned to baseball in 1954 to play for the <a href="/wiki/Lubbock_Hubbers" title="Lubbock Hubbers">Lubbock Hubbers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/West_Texas%E2%80%93New_Mexico_League" title="West Texas–New Mexico League">West Texas–New Mexico League</a>. He played part-time for the team, serving as a replacement when players needed time off, whether through injury or to spend time with their families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He hit .360 in 25 games for the Hubbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> The following year, he served as player-manager for the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carlsbad_Potashers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carlsbad Potashers (page does not exist)">Carlsbad Potashers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Longhorn_League" title="Longhorn League">Longhorn League</a>. Tucker hit .275 in 114 games for the Potashers, including 25 doubles and eight home runs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> He continued as player-manager for the Potashers in 1956, but the management considered firing him during a 14-game losing streak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> He finished the year with a .306 batting average in 128 games. The following season, he was the player-manager of the Hobbs Sports, but only played in 16 games for them, hitting .273.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brm_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-brm-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1958, he ended his playing career, and became the general manager of the Hobbs team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>After retiring, Tucker became an insurance agent and lived in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>. He married and had four children; his son Ronald served in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-digest-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1962, he also became one of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>' first <a href="/wiki/Scout_(sport)" title="Scout (sport)">scouts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Tucker died on May 7, 1993, in Oklahoma City and is buried at Gordon Cemetery in his hometown of Gordon, Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-br-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
