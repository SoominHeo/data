<p>The <b>Gobi Desert</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Àà/ primary stress follows">Àà</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">…°</span><span title="/o ä/ long 'o' in 'code'">o ä</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>: <span lang="mn" xml:lang="mn">–ì–æ–≤—å</span>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Mongolian transliteration">govƒ≠</span></i>, <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Mongolian" title="Help:IPA for Mongolian">/…¢…îw ≤/</a>, "semidesert"; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">ÊààÂ£Å</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Gƒìb√¨</span></i>, <a href="/wiki/Xiao%27erjing" title="Xiao'erjing">Xiao'erjing</a>: ŸÇŸíÿ®Ÿê, <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Mandarin" title="Help:IPA for Mandarin">/k…§À•piÀ•À©/</a>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> region in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It covers parts of <a href="/wiki/North_China" title="North China">northern</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwestern_China" title="Northwestern China">northwestern China</a>, and of southern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. The desert basins of the Gobi are bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> and the grasslands and <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a> of Mongolia on the north, by the <a href="/wiki/Taklamakan_Desert" title="Taklamakan Desert">Taklamakan Desert</a> to the west, by the <a href="/wiki/Hexi_Corridor" title="Hexi Corridor">Hexi Corridor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> to the southwest, and by the <a href="/wiki/North_China_Plain" title="North China Plain">North China Plain</a> to the southeast. The Gobi is most notable in history as part of the great <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol Empire</a>, and as the location of several important cities along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>.</p>
<p>The Gobi is a <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> desert, formed by the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan mountain range</a> blocking rain-carrying clouds from the Indian Ocean reaching the Gobi territory.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>
<p>The Gobi measures over 1,600¬†km (1,000¬†mi) from southwest to northeast and 800¬†km (500¬†mi) from north to south. The desert is widest in the west, along the line joining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Bosten" title="Lake Bosten">Lake Bosten</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lop_Nor" title="Lop Nor">Lop Nor</a> (87¬∞‚Äì89¬∞ east). It occupies an arc of land 1,295,000¬†km<sup>2</sup> (500,000¬†sq¬†mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">[1]</a></sup> in area as of 2007; it is the fifth-largest desert in the world and Asia's largest. Much of the Gobi is not sandy but has exposed bare rock.</p>
<p>In its broadest definition, the Gobi includes the long stretch of desert and semi-desert area extending from the foot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamirs" title="Pamirs">Pamirs</a>, 77¬∞ east, to the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khingan" title="Greater Khingan">Greater Khingan</a> Mountains, 116¬∞-118¬∞ east, on the border of <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>; and from the foothills of the Altay, <a href="/wiki/Sayan_Mountains" title="Sayan Mountains">Sayan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yablonoi_Mountains" title="Yablonoi Mountains">Yablonoi</a> mountain ranges on the north to the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altyn-Tagh" title="Altyn-Tagh">Altyn-Tagh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian</a> mountain ranges, which form the northern edges of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a>, on the south.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>A relatively large area on the east side of the Greater Khingan range, between the upper waters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songhua" title="Songhua">Songhua</a> (Sungari) and the upper waters of the Liao-ho, is reckoned to belong to the Gobi by conventional usage. Some <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologists</a> prefer to regard the western area of the Gobi region (as defined above): the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">basin of the Tarim</a> in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> and the desert basin of Lop Nor and Hami (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kumul_(city)" title="Kumul (city)">Kumul</a>), as forming a separate and independent desert, called the <a href="/wiki/Taklamakan_Desert" title="Taklamakan Desert">Taklamakan Desert</a>.</p>
<p>Archeologists and paleontologists have done excavations in the <a href="/wiki/Nemegt_Basin" title="Nemegt Basin">Nemegt Basin</a> in the northwestern part of the Gobi Desert (in Mongolia), which is noted for its fossil treasures, including <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_mammal" title="Prehistoric mammal">early mammals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinosaur_eggs" title="Dinosaur eggs">dinosaur eggs</a>, and prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone implements</a>, some 100,000 years old.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The Gobi is a cold desert, with frost and occasionally snow occurring on its dunes. Besides being quite far north, it is also located on a plateau roughly 910‚Äì1,520 metres (2,990‚Äì4,990¬†ft) above sea level, which contributes to its low temperatures. An average of approximately 194 millimetres (7.6¬†in) of rain falls annually in the Gobi. Additional moisture reaches parts of the Gobi in winter as snow is blown by the wind from the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> Steppes. These winds cause the Gobi to reach extremes of temperature ranging from ‚àí40¬†¬∞C or ‚àí40¬†¬∞F in winter to 45¬†¬∞C or 113¬†¬∞F in summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcpe_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcpe-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of the Gobi is one of great extremes, combined with rapid changes of temperature of as much as 35¬†¬∞C (63¬†¬∞F). These can occur not only seasonally but within 24 hours.</p>

<p>In southern Mongolia, the temperature has been recorded as low as ‚àí32.8¬†¬∞C (‚àí27.0¬†¬∞F). In contrast, in <a href="/wiki/Alxa_League" title="Alxa League">Alxa</a>, Inner Mongolia, it rises as high as 37¬†¬∞C (99¬†¬∞F) in July.</p>
<p>Average winter minimums are a frigid ‚àí25¬†¬∞C (‚àí13¬†¬∞F) while summertime temperatures are warm to hot, with highs that range up to 50¬†¬∞C (122¬†¬∞F). Most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> falls during the summer.</p>
<p>Although the southeast <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a> reach the southeast parts of the Gobi, the area throughout this region is generally characterized by extreme dryness, especially during the winter, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_anticyclone" title="Siberian anticyclone">Siberian anticyclone</a> is at its strongest. Hence, the icy <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">sandstorms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">snowstorms</a> of spring and early summer plus early January (winter).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation.2C_ecology.2C_and_economy">Conservation, ecology, and economy</span></h2>
<p>The Gobi Desert is the source of many important fossil finds, including the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinosaur_eggs" title="Dinosaur eggs">dinosaur eggs</a>.</p>
<p>Despite the harsh conditions, these deserts and the surrounding regions sustain many animals, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-tailed_gazelle" title="Black-tailed gazelle">black-tailed gazelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marbled_polecat" title="Marbled polecat">marbled polecats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_Bactrian_camel" title="Wild Bactrian camel">wild Bactrian camels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_wild_ass" title="Mongolian wild ass">Mongolian wild ass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">sandplovers</a>. They are occasionally visited by <a href="/wiki/Snow_leopard" title="Snow leopard">snow leopards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bears</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>. Drought-adapted shrubs in the desert included <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_sparrow%27s_saltwort&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gray sparrow's saltwort (page does not exist)">gray sparrow's saltwort</a> (<i>Salsola passerina</i>), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_sagebrush&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gray sagebrush (page does not exist)">gray sagebrush</a>, and low grasses such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Needle_grass" title="Needle grass">needle grass</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bridlegrass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bridlegrass (page does not exist)">bridlegrass</a>. Several large nature reserves have been established in the Gobi, including <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Gurvansaikhan_National_Park" title="Gobi Gurvansaikhan National Park">Gobi Gurvansaikhan National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Gobi_A_Strictly_Protected_Area" title="Great Gobi A Strictly Protected Area">Great Gobi A</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Gobi_B_Strictly_Protected_Area" title="Great Gobi B Strictly Protected Area">Great Gobi B Strictly Protected Area</a>.</p>
<p>The area is vulnerable to trampling by <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and off-road vehicles (effects from human intervention are greater in the eastern Gobi Desert, where rainfall is heavier and may sustain livestock). In Mongolia, grasslands have been degraded by goats, which are raised by nomadic herders as source of <a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">cashmere wool</a>. The economic trends of livestock privatization and the collapse of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_economy" title="Urban economy">urban economy</a> have caused people to return to subsistence <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_lifestyle" title="Rural lifestyle">rural lifestyles</a>, away from <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a>.</p>
<p>Large copper and gold deposits located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oyuu_Tolgoi" title="Oyuu Tolgoi">Oyuu Tolgoi</a>, about 80 kilometres from the Chinese border into Mongolia, are being investigated for development as mining operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Oyu Tolgoi copper and gold mine, under construction by Rio Tinto in the South Gobi Desert, is expected to begin operation in early 2013, and is the biggest economic undertaking in the country's history. Rio Tinto estimates that taxes, royalties and dividends generated by the Oyu Tolgoi project is expected to add a third to the country's gross domestic product by 2020. Rio Tinto forecasts average annual production of 450,000 tonnes of copper and 330,000 ounces of gold, and with 1.4 billion tonnes of reserves and a resource of 3.1 billion tonnes, the mine is expected to last for more than 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mine has been and remains controversial. There is significant opposition in Mongolia's parliament to the terms under which the mine will proceed, and some are calling for the terms to be renegotiated. Specifically, the contention revolves primarily around the question of whether negotiations were fair (Rio Tinto is far better resourced) and whether Rio Tinto will pay adequate taxes on the revenues it derives from the mine (an agreement was reached whereby the operation will be exempt from <a href="/wiki/Windfall_tax_(Mongolia)" title="Windfall tax (Mongolia)">windfall tax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Desertification">Desertification</span></h2>
<p>The Gobi Desert is expanding at an alarming rate, in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>. The expansion is particularly rapid on the southern edge into China, which has seen 3,600¬†km<sup>2</sup> (1,390¬†sq¬†mi) of grassland overtaken every year by the Gobi Desert. Dust storms, which used to occur regularly in China, have increased in frequency in the past 20 years, mainly due to desertification. They have caused further damage to China's agriculture economy.</p>
<p>The expansion of the Gobi is attributed mostly to human activities, notably <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a>, and depletion of water resources. China has tried various plans to slow the expansion of the desert, which have met with some small degree of success, but no major effects. The most recent plan involves the planting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Wall_of_China" title="Green Wall of China">Green Wall of China</a>, a huge ring of newly planted forests; the government hopes the forests will help stabilize the soil, retain moisture, and act as a buffer against further desertification.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecoregions_of_the_Gobi">Ecoregions of the Gobi</span></h2>
<p>The Gobi, broadly defined, can be divided into five distinct dry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecoregions" title="Ecoregions">ecoregions</a>, based on variations in climate and topography.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Eastern Gobi desert steppe</b>, the easternmost of the Gobi ecoregions, covering an area of 281,800¬†km<sup>2</sup> (108,804¬†sq¬†mi). It extends from the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolian</a> <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">Plateau</a> in China northward into Mongolia. It includes the <a href="/wiki/Yin_Mountains" title="Yin Mountains">Yin Mountains</a> and many low-lying areas with salt pans and small ponds. It is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian-Manchurian_grassland" title="Mongolian-Manchurian grassland">Mongolian-Manchurian grassland</a> to the north, the Yellow River Plain to the southeast, and the Alashan Plateau semi-desert to the southeast and east.</li>
<li><b>Alashan Plateau semi-desert</b>, lies west and southwest of the Eastern Gobi desert steppe. It consists of the desert basins and low mountains lying between the Gobi Altai range on the north, the <a href="/wiki/Helan_Mountains" title="Helan Mountains">Helan Mountains</a> to the southeast, and the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a> and northeastern portion of the Tibetan Plateau on the southwest.</li>
<li><b>Gobi Lakes Valley desert steppe</b>, ecoregion lies north of Alashan Plateau semi-desert, between the Gobi Altai range to the south and the <a href="/wiki/Khangai_Mountains" title="Khangai Mountains">Khangai Mountains</a> to the north.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dzungarian_Basin" title="Dzungarian Basin">Dzungarian Basin</a> semi-desert</b>, includes the desert basin lying between the Altai mountains on the north and the <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> range on the south. It includes the northern portion of China's Xinjiang province and extends into the southeastern corner of Mongolia. The Alashan Plateau semi-desert lies to the east, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emin_Valley_steppe" title="Emin Valley steppe">Emin Valley steppe</a> to the west, on the China-<a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> border.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tian_Shan_range" title="Tian Shan range">Tian Shan range</a></b>, separates the Dzungarian Basin semi-desert from the <a href="/wiki/Taklamakan_Desert" title="Taklamakan Desert">Taklamakan Desert</a>, which is a low, sandy desert basin surrounded by the high mountain ranges of the Tibetan Plateau to the south and the <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamirs</a> to the west. The Taklamakan Desert ecoregion includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_of_Lop" title="Desert of Lop">Desert of Lop</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>The surface is extremely diversified, although there are no great differences in vertical elevation. Between <a href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">Ulaanbaatar</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gobi_Desert&amp;params=48_00_N_107_00_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">48¬∞00‚Ä≤N</span> <span class="longitude">107¬∞00‚Ä≤E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">Ôªø / Ôªø</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">48.000¬∞N 107.000¬∞E</span><span style="display:none">Ôªø / <span class="geo">48.000; 107.000</span></span></span></a></span>) and the small lake of Iren-dubasu-nor (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gobi_Desert&amp;params=43_45_N_111_50_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">43¬∞45‚Ä≤N</span> <span class="longitude">111¬∞50‚Ä≤E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">Ôªø / Ôªø</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">43.750¬∞N 111.833¬∞E</span><span style="display:none">Ôªø / <span class="geo">43.750; 111.833</span></span></span></a></span>), the surface is greatly eroded. Broad flat depressions and basins are separated by groups of flat-topped mountains of relatively low elevation 150 to 180¬†m (490 to 590¬†ft)), through which archaic rocks crop out as crags and isolated rugged masses. The floors of the depressions lie mostly between 900 to 1,000¬†m (3,000 to 3,300¬†ft) above sea-level. Farther south, between Iren-dutiasu-nor and the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>, comes a region of broad tablelands alternating with flat plains, the latter ranging at altitudes of 1000‚Äì1100¬†m and the former at 1,070 to 1,200¬†m (3,510 to 3,940¬†ft). The slopes of the plateaus are more or less steep, and are sometimes penetrated by "bays" of the lowlands.</p>
<p>As the border-range of the Hyangan is approached, the country steadily rises up to 1,370¬†m (4,490¬†ft) and then to 1,630¬†m (5,350¬†ft). Here small lakes frequently fill the depressions, though the water in them is generally salt or brackish. Both here and for 320¬†km (199¬†mi) south of Ulaanbaatar, streams are frequent and grass grows more or less abundantly. Through all the central parts, until the bordering mountains are reached, trees and shrubs are utterly absent. Clay and sand are the predominant formations; the watercourses, especially in the north, being frequently excavated 2 to 3¬†m (6¬†ft 7¬†in to 9¬†ft 10¬†in) deep. In many places in the flat, dry valleys or depressions farther south, beds of <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a>, 5 to 6¬†m (16 to 20¬†ft) thick, are exposed. West of the route from Ulaanbaatar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalgan" title="Kalgan">Kalgan</a>, the country presents approximately the same general features, except that the mountains are not so irregularly scattered in groups but have more strongly defined strikes, mostly east to west, west-north-west to east-south-east, and west-south-west to east-north-east.</p>
<p>The altitudes are higher, those of the lowlands ranging from 1,000 to 1,700¬†m (3,300 to 5,600¬†ft), and those of the ranges from 200 to 500¬†m (660 to 1,640¬†ft) higher, though in a few cases they reach altitudes of 2,400¬†m (7,900¬†ft). The elevations do not form continuous chains, but make up a congeries of short ridges and groups rising from a common base and intersected by a labyrinth of ravines, gullies, glens and basins. But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plateaus" title="Plateaus">tablelands</a>, built up of the horizontal red deposits of the Han-gai (<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Obruchev" title="Vladimir Obruchev">Obruchev</a>'s Gobi formation) which are characteristic of the southern parts of eastern Mongolia, are absent here or occur only in one locality, near the Shara-muren river. They are greatly intersected by gullies or dry watercourses. Water is scarce, with no streams, no lakes, no wells, and precipitation falls seldom. The prevailing winds blow from the west and northwest, and the pall of dust overhangs the country as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takla_Makan" title="Takla Makan">Takla Makan</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_of_Lop" title="Desert of Lop">desert of Lop</a>. Characteristic of the flora are wild garlic, <i>Kalidium gracile</i>, <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_(genus)" title="Artemisia (genus)">wormwood</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxaul" title="Saxaul">saxaul</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nitraria" title="Nitraria">Nitraria</a> schoberi</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Caragana" title="Caragana">Caragana</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephedra_(genus)" title="Ephedra (genus)">Ephedra</a>, saltwort and the <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grass</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lasiagrostis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lasiagrostis (page does not exist)">Lasiagrostis</a> splendens</i>. The taana wild onion <i><a href="/wiki/Allium" title="Allium">Allium</a> polyrrhizum</i> is the main browse eaten by many herd animals, and Mongolians claim that this is essential to produce the correct, slightly hazelnut-like flavour of camel <a href="/wiki/Kumis" title="Kumis">airag</a> (fermented milk).</p>
<p>This great desert country of Gobi is crossed by several trade routes, some of which have been in use for thousands of years. Among the most important are those from <a href="/wiki/Zhangjiakou" title="Zhangjiakou">Kalgan</a> (at the Great Wall) to Ulaanbaatar (960¬†km (597¬†mi)); from <a href="/wiki/Jiuquan" title="Jiuquan">Jiuquan</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>) to Hami 670¬†km (416¬†mi); from Hami to Beijing (2,000¬†km (1,243¬†mi)); from <a href="/wiki/Hohhot" title="Hohhot">Hohhot</a> to Hami and Barkul; and from <a href="/wiki/Lanzhou" title="Lanzhou">Lanzhou</a> (in Gansu) to Hami.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alashan_Plateau_semi-desert">Alashan Plateau semi-desert</span></h3>
<p>The southwestern portion of the Gobi, known also as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hsi-tau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hsi-tau (page does not exist)">Hsi-tau</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Gobi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Little Gobi (page does not exist)">Little Gobi</a>, fills the space between the great north loop of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> on the east, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ejin_River" title="Ejin River">Ejin River</a> on the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a> and narrow rocky chain of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Longshou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Longshou (page does not exist)">Longshou</a>, 3,200 to 3,500¬†m (10,500 to 11,500¬†ft) in altitude, on the southwest. The <a href="/wiki/Ordos_Desert" title="Ordos Desert">Ordos Desert</a>, which covers the northeastern portion of the Ordos Plateau, in the great north loop of the Yellow River, is part of this ecoregion. It belongs to the middle basin of the three great depressions into which Potanin divides the Gobi as a whole.</p>
<p>"Topographically," says <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Przhevalsky" title="Nikolai Przhevalsky">Nikolai Przhevalsky</a>, "it is a perfectly level plain, which in all probability once formed the bed of a huge lake or inland sea." He concludes this based on the level area of the region as a whole, the hard saldgine clay and the sand-strewn surface and, lastly, the salt lakes which occupy its lowest parts. For hundreds of kilometers, nothing can be seen but bare sands; in some places they continue so far without a break that the Mongols call them <i>Tengger</i> (i.e. sky). These vast expanses are absolutely waterless, nor do any oases relieve the unbroken stretches of yellow sand, which alternate with equally vast areas of saline clay or, nearer the foot of the mountains, with barren shingle. Although on the whole a level country with a general altitude of 1,000 to 1,500¬†m (3,300 to 4,900¬†ft), this section, like most other parts of the Gobi, is crowned by a chequered network of hills and broken ranges going up 300¬†m higher. The vegetation is confined to a few varieties of bushes and a dozen kinds of grasses and herbs, the most conspicuous being saxaul (<i>Haloxylon ammondendron</i>) and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agriophyllum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agriophyllum (page does not exist)">Agriophyllum</a> gobicum</i>. The others include prickly <a href="/wiki/Convolvulus" title="Convolvulus">convolvulus</a>, field wormwood (<i>Artemisia campestris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Inula" title="Inula">Inula</a> ammophila</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sophora" title="Sophora">Sophora</a> flavescens</i>, <i>Convolvulus ammanii</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Peganum" title="Peganum">Peganum</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Astragalus" title="Astragalus">Astragalus</a></i>, but all dwarfed, deformed and starved. The fauna consists of little but antelope, wolf, fox, hare, hedgehog, marten, numerous lizards and a few birds, e.g. the sandgrouse, lark, stonechat, sparrow, crane, <a href="/wiki/Henderson%27s_ground_jay" title="Henderson's ground jay">Henderson's ground jay</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Podoces" title="Podoces">Podoces</a> hendersoni</i>), <a href="/wiki/Horned_lark" title="Horned lark">horned lark</a> (<i>Eremophila alpestris</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Crested_lark" title="Crested lark">crested lark</a> (<i>Galerida cristata</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dzungarian_Basin_semi-desert">Dzungarian Basin semi-desert</span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The structure here is that of the mighty <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">T'ien Shan</a>, or Heavenly Mountains, running from west to east. It divides the northern one-third of Sinkiang from the southern two-thirds. On the northern side, rivers formed from the snow and glaciers of the high mountains break through barren foothill ranges and flow out into an immense, hollow plain. Here the rivers begin to straggle and fan out, and form great marshes with dense reed-beds. Westerners call this terrain the Dzungarian desert. The Chinese also call it a desert, but the Mongols call it a 'gobi'‚Äîthat is, a land of thin herbage, more suitable for camels than for cows, but capable also, if herds are kept small and moved frequently, of sustaining horses, sheep, and goats. The herbage comprises a high proportion of woody, fragrant plants. Gobi mutton is the most aromatic in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Yulduz valley or valley of the Haidag-gol (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gobi_Desert&amp;params=43_N_83_E_region:CN-65"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">43¬∞N</span> <span class="longitude">83¬∞E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">Ôªø / Ôªø</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">43¬∞N 83¬∞E</span><span style="display:none">Ôªø / <span class="geo">43; 83</span></span></span></a></span>‚Äì<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gobi_Desert&amp;params=43_N_86_E_region:CN-65"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">43¬∞N</span> <span class="longitude">86¬∞E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">Ôªø / Ôªø</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">43¬∞N 86¬∞E</span><span style="display:none">Ôªø / <span class="geo">43; 86</span></span></span></a></span>) is a mini desert enclosed by two prominent members of the Shanashen Trahen Osh mountain range, namely the chucis and the kracenard pine rallies, running perpendicular and far from one another. As they proceed south, they transcend and transpose, sweeping back on east and west respectively, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Bosten" title="Lake Bosten">Lake Bosten</a> in between. These two ranges mark the northern and the southern edges respectively of a great swelling, which extends eastward for nearly twenty degrees of longitude. On its northern side, the Chol-tagh descends steeply, and its foot is fringed by a string of deep depressions, ranging from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lukchun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lukchun (page does not exist)">Lukchun</a> (130¬†m (427¬†ft) below sea level) to <a href="/wiki/Hami_City" title="Hami City">Hami</a> (850¬†m (2,789¬†ft) above sea-level). To the south of the Kuruk-tagh lie the desert of <a href="/wiki/Lop_Nur" title="Lop Nur">Lop Nur</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kum-tagh" title="Kum-tagh">Kum-tagh</a> desert, and the valley of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bulunzir-gol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bulunzir-gol (page does not exist)">Bulunzir-gol</a>. To this great swelling, which arches up between the two border-ranges of the Chol-tagh and Kuruk-tagh, the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> give the name of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ghashuun-Gobi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ghashuun-Gobi (page does not exist)">Ghashuun-Gobi</a></i> or "Salt Desert". It is some 130 to 160¬†km (81 to 99¬†mi) across from north to south, and is traversed by a number of minor parallel ranges, ridges and chains of hills. Down its middle runs a broad stony valley, 40 to 80¬†km (25 to 50¬†mi) wide, at an elevation of 900 to 1,370¬†m (2,950 to 4,490¬†ft). The Chol-tagh, which reaches an average altitude of 1,800¬†m (5,900¬†ft), is absolutely sterile, and its northern foot rests upon a narrow belt of barren sand, which leads down to the depressions mentioned above.</p>
<p>The Kuruk-tagh is the greatly disintegrated, denuded and wasted relic of a mountain range which used to be of incomparably greater magnitude. In the west, between Lake Bosten and the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_River" title="Tarim River">Tarim</a>, it consists of two, possibly of three, principal ranges, which, although broken in continuity, run generally parallel to one another, and embrace between them numerous minor chains of heights. These minor ranges, together with the principal ranges, divide the region into a series of long; narrow valleys, mostly parallel to one another and to the enclosing mountain chains, which descend like terraced steps, on the one side towards the depression of Lukchun and on the other towards the desert of Lop. In many cases these latitudinal valleys are barred transversely by ridges or spurs, generally elevations en masse of the bottom of the valley. Where such elevations exist, there is generally found, on the east side of the transverse ridge, a cauldron-shaped depression, which some time or other has been the bottom of a former lake, but is now nearly a dry salt-basin. The surface configuration is in fact markedly similar to that which occurs in the inter-mount latitudinal valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrography</a> of the Ghashiun-Gobi and the Kuruk-tagh is determined by these chequered arrangements of the latitudinal valleys. Most of the principal streams, instead of flowing straight down these valleys, cross them diagonally and only turn west after they have cut their way through one or more of the transverse barrier ranges. To the highest range on the great swelling <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Grumm-Grzhimaylo" title="Grigory Grumm-Grzhimaylo">Grumm-Grzhimailo</a> gives the name of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tuge-tau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tuge-tau (page does not exist)">Tuge-tau</a>, its altitude being 2,700¬†m (8,858¬†ft) above the level of the sea and some 1,200¬†m (3,937¬†ft) above the crown of the swelling itself. This range he considers to belong to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Choltagh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Choltagh (page does not exist)">Choltagh</a> system, whereas <a href="/wiki/Sven_Hedin" title="Sven Hedin">Sven Hedin</a> would assign it to the Kuruk-tagh. This last, which is pretty certainly identical with the range of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kharateken-ula&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kharateken-ula (page does not exist)">Kharateken-ula</a> (also known as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kyzyl-sanghir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyzyl-sanghir (page does not exist)">Kyzyl-sanghir</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sinir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sinir (page does not exist)">Sinir</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Singher_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Singher Mountains (page does not exist)">Singher Mountains</a>), that overlooks the southern shore of the Lake Bosten, though parted from it by the drift-sand desert of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ak-bel-kum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ak-bel-kum (page does not exist)">Ak-bel-kum</a> (White Pass Sands), has at first a west-northwest to east-southeast strike, but it gradually curves round like a scimitar towards the east-northeast and at the same time gradually decreases in elevation. In 91¬∞ east, while the principal range of the Kuruk-tagh system wheels to the east-northeast, four of its subsidiary ranges terminate, or rather die away somewhat suddenly, on the brink of a long narrow depression (in which Sven Hedin sees a northeast bay of the former great Central Asian lake of Lop-nor), having over against them the √©cheloned terminals of similar subordinate ranges of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pe-shan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pe-shan (page does not exist)">Pe-shan</a> (Boy-san) system (see below). The Kuruk-tagh is throughout a relatively low, but almost completely barren range, being entirely destitute of animal life, save for hares, antelopes and wild camels, which frequent its few small, widely scattered oases. The vegetation, which is confined to these same relatively favoured spots, is of the scantiest and is mainly confined to bushes of <a href="/wiki/Haloxylon_ammodendron" title="Haloxylon ammodendron">saxaul</a> <i>(Haloxylon)</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Anabasis_(plant)" title="Anabasis (plant)">anabasis</a></i>, reeds (kamish), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamarisk" title="Tamarisk">tamarisks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ephedra" title="Ephedra">Ephedra</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_exploration_up_to_1911">European exploration up to 1911</span></h2>
<p>The Gobi had a long history of human habitation, mostly by nomadic peoples. By the early 20th century the region was under the nominal control of Manchu-China, and inhabited mostly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongols</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uyghur_people" title="Uyghur people">Uyghurs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhs" title="Kazakhs">Kazakhs</a>.
