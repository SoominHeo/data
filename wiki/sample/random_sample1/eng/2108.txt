<p><i><b>Kojiki</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">古事記</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "Records of Ancient Matters" or "An Account of Ancient Matters")</span>, also known as <i><b>Furukotofumi</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the oldest extant chronicle in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, dating from the early 8th century (711–712) and composed by <a href="/wiki/%C5%8C_no_Yasumaro" title="Ō no Yasumaro">Ō no Yasumaro</a> at the request of <a href="/wiki/Empress_Genmei" title="Empress Genmei">Empress Genmei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>Kojiki</i> is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myths</a> concerning the origin of the four home islands of Japan, and the <a href="/wiki/Kami" title="Kami">Kami</a>. Along with the <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>, the myths contained in the <i>Kojiki</i> are part of the inspiration behind many practices. Later, the myths were re-appropriated for <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> practices including the <a href="/wiki/Misogi" title="Misogi">misogi</a> purification ritual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-e-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-d-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kokugo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-kokugo-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kojiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Kojiki</i> contains various songs/poems. While the historical records and myths are written in a form of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> with a heavy mixture of Japanese elements, the songs are written with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a> that are only used to convey sounds. This special use of Chinese characters is called <i><a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana" title="Man'yōgana">Man'yōgana</a></i>, a knowledge of which is critical to understanding these songs, which are written in <a href="/wiki/Old_Japanese" title="Old Japanese">Old Japanese</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sections">Sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kojiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Kojiki</i> is divided into three parts: the <i>Kamitsumaki</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">上巻</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"first volume"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, the <i>Nakatsumaki</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">中巻</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "middle volume")</span> and the <i>Shimotsumaki</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">下巻</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "lower volume")</span>.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Kamitsumaki</i>, also known as the <i>Kamiyo no Maki</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">神代巻</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "Volume of the Age of the Gods")</span>, includes the <a href="/wiki/Preface" title="Preface">preface</a> of the <i>Kojiki</i>, and is focused on the deities of creation and the births of various deities of the <i>kamiyo</i> period, or <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Gods" title="Age of the Gods">Age of the Gods</a>. The <i>Kamitsumaki</i> also outlines the myths concerning the foundation of Japan. It describes how <a href="/wiki/Ninigi-no-Mikoto" title="Ninigi-no-Mikoto">Ninigi-no-Mikoto</a>, grandson of <a href="/wiki/Amaterasu" title="Amaterasu">Amaterasu</a> and great-grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jimmu" title="Emperor Jimmu">Emperor Jimmu</a>, descended from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takamagahara" title="Takamagahara">heaven</a> to Takachihonomine in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a> and became the <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">progenitor</a> of the Japanese imperial line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-e-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kokugo_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-kokugo-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>Nakatsumaki</i> begins with the story of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jimmu" title="Emperor Jimmu">Emperor Jimmu</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor</a>, and his conquest of Japan, and ends with the 15th Emperor, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_%C5%8Cjin" title="Emperor Ōjin">Emperor Ōjin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Suizei" title="Emperor Suizei">second</a> through <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Kaika" title="Emperor Kaika">ninth</a> emperors' reigns are recorded in a minimum of detail, with only their names, the names of their various descendants, and the place-names of their palaces and tombs listed, and no mention of their achievements. Many of the stories in this volume are mythological, and the allegedly historical information in them is highly suspect. Recent studies support the view that these emperors were invented to push Jimmu's reign further back to the year 660 BC.</li>
<li>The <i>Shimotsumaki</i> covers the 16th to 33rd emperors and, unlike previous volumes, has very limited references to the interactions with deities. These interactions are very prominent in the first and second volumes. Information about the 24th to the 33rd emperors are largely missing, as well.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscripts">Manuscripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kojiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Manuscripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two major branches of <i>Kojiki</i> manuscripts: Ise and Urabe. The extant Urabe branch consists of 36 existing manuscripts all based on the 1522 copies by Urabe Kanenaga. The Ise branch may be subdivided into the Shinpukuji-bon <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">真福寺本</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> manuscript of 1371–1372 and the Dōka-bon <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">道果本</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> manuscripts. The Dōka sub-branch consists of:</p>
<ul>
<li>the Dōka-bon <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">道果本</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> manuscript of 1381; only the first half of the first volume remains</li>
<li>the Dōshō-bon <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">道祥本</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> manuscript of 1424; only the first volume remains, and there are many defects</li>
<li>the Shun'yu-bon <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">春瑜本</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> manuscript of 1426; one volume</li>
</ul>
<p>The Shinpukuji-bon manuscript (1371–1372) is the oldest existing manuscript. While divided into the Ise branch, it is actually a mixture of the two branches. The monk Ken'yu based his copy on Ōnakatomi Sadayo's copy. In 1266, Sadayo copied volumes one and three, but did not have access to the second volume. Finally, in 1282, he obtained access to the second volume through a Urabe-branch manuscript that he used to transcribe.</p>
