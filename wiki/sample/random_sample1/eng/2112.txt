<p><b>Altitude sickness</b>—also known as <b>acute mountain sickness</b> (<b>AMS</b>), is a pathological <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_high_altitude_on_humans" title="Effects of high altitude on humans">effect of high altitude on humans</a>, caused by acute exposure to low <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas#Partial_pressure_of_oxygen" title="Breathing gas">partial pressure of oxygen</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude" title="High altitude">high altitude</a>.</p>
<p>Although minor symptoms such as breathlessness may occur at altitudes of 1,500 metres (5,000 ft), AMS commonly occurs above 2,400 metres (8,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borden_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borden-2">[2]</a></sup> It presents as a collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonspecific_symptoms" title="Nonspecific symptoms">nonspecific symptoms</a>, acquired at high altitude or in low air pressure, resembling a case of "flu, carbon monoxide poisoning, or a hangover".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is hard to determine who will be affected by altitude sickness, as there are no specific factors that correlate with a susceptibility to altitude sickness. However, most people can ascend to 2,400 metres (8,000 ft) without difficulty.</p>
<p>Acute mountain sickness can progress to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_pulmonary_edema" title="High altitude pulmonary edema">high altitude pulmonary edema</a> (HAPE) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_cerebral_edema" title="High altitude cerebral edema">high altitude cerebral edema</a> (HACE), which are potentially fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borden_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Borden-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chronic_mountain_sickness" title="Chronic mountain sickness">Chronic mountain sickness</a>, also known as <i>Monge's disease</i>, is a different condition that only occurs after very prolonged exposure to high altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face,_before_and_after;_Annapurna_Base_Camp,_2015.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="3264" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG/290px-Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG/435px-Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG/580px-Peripheral_edema_of_a_woman%27s_face%2C_before_and_after%3B_Annapurna_Base_Camp%2C_2015.JPG 2x" width="290"/></a>

<p>People have different susceptibilities to altitude sickness; for some otherwise healthy people, acute altitude sickness can begin to appear at around 2,000 metres (6,600 ft) above sea level, such as at many mountain ski resorts, equivalent to a pressure of 80 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kilopascals</a> (0.79 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This is the most frequent type of altitude sickness encountered. Symptoms often manifest themselves six to ten hours after ascent and generally subside in one to two days, but they occasionally develop into the more serious conditions. Symptoms include headache, fatigue, stomach illness, dizziness, and sleep disturbance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-4">[4]</a></sup> Exertion aggravates the symptoms.</p>
<p>Those individuals with the lowest initial partial pressure of end-tidal pCO<sub>2</sub> (the lowest concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at the end of the respiratory cycle, a measure of a higher alveolar ventilation) and corresponding high oxygen saturation levels tend to have a lower incidence of acute mountain sickness than those with high end-tidal pCO<sub>2</sub> and low oxygen saturation levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_symptoms">Primary symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Primary symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">Headaches</a> are the primary symptom used to diagnose altitude sickness, although a headache is also a symptom of <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>. A headache occurring at an altitude above 2,400 metres (7,900 ft) – a pressure of 76 kilopascals (0.75 atm) – combined with any one or more of the following symptoms, may indicate altitude sickness:</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>Gastrointestinal disorder:</td>
<td>Loss of appetite, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, or vomiting, <a href="/wiki/High-altitude_flatus_expulsion" title="High-altitude flatus expulsion">excessive flatulation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auerbach_2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Auerbach_2007-8">[8]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Nervous system disorder:</td>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatigue_(physical)" title="Fatigue (physical)">Fatigue</a> or weakness, headache with or without <a href="/wiki/Dizziness" title="Dizziness">dizziness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lightheadedness" title="Lightheadedness">lightheadedness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Locomotory system disorder:</td>
<td><a href="/wiki/Peripheral_edema" title="Peripheral edema">Peripheral edema</a> (swelling of hands, feet, and face)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Respiratory system disorder:</td>
<td>Nose bleeding, shortness of breath upon exertion</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Cardiovascular system disorder:</td>
<td>Persistent rapid pulse</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Others:</td>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraesthesia" title="Paraesthesia">Pins and needles</a>, general <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">malaise</a></td>
</tr>
</table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_symptoms">Severe symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Severe symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Symptoms that may indicate life-threatening altitude sickness include:</p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">Pulmonary edema</a> (fluid in the lungs)</dt>
<dd>Symptoms similar to <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a></dd>
<dd>Persistent dry cough</dd>
<dd>Fever</dd>
<dd>Shortness of breath even when resting</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cerebral_edema" title="Cerebral edema">Cerebral edema</a> (swelling of the brain)</dt>
<dd>Headache that does not respond to analgesics</dd>
<dd>Unsteady gait</dd>
<dd>Gradual loss of consciousness</dd>
<dd>Increased nausea and vomiting</dd>
<dd>Retinal hemorrhage</dd>
</dl>
<p>The most serious symptoms of altitude sickness arise from <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a> (fluid accumulation in the tissues of the body). At very high altitude, humans can get either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_pulmonary_edema" title="High altitude pulmonary edema">high altitude pulmonary edema</a> (HAPE), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_cerebral_edema" title="High altitude cerebral edema">high altitude cerebral edema</a> (HACE). The physiological cause of altitude-induced edema is not conclusively established. It is currently believed, however, that HACE is caused by local vasodilation of cerebral blood vessels in response to <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>, resulting in greater blood flow and, consequently, greater capillary pressures. On the other hand, HAPE may be due to general vasoconstriction in the pulmonary circulation (normally a response to regional ventilation-perfusion mismatches) which, with constant or increased cardiac output, also leads to increases in capillary pressures. For those suffering HACE, <a href="/wiki/Dexamethasone" title="Dexamethasone">dexamethasone</a> may provide temporary relief from symptoms in order to keep descending under their own power.</p>
<p>HAPE can progress rapidly and is often fatal. Symptoms include fatigue, severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a> at rest, and cough that is initially dry but may progress to produce pink, frothy <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a>. Descent to lower altitudes alleviates the symptoms of HAPE.</p>
<p>HACE is a life-threatening condition that can lead to coma or death. Symptoms include headache, fatigue, visual impairment, bladder dysfunction, bowel dysfunction, loss of coordination, paralysis on one side of the body, and confusion. Descent to lower altitudes may save those afflicted with HACE.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1615" data-file-width="2971" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg/220px-Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg/330px-Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg/440px-Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Climbers on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> often experience altitude sickness.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Altitude sickness can first occur at 1,500 metres, with the effects becoming severe at extreme altitudes (greater than 5,500 metres). Only brief trips above 6,000 metres are possible and supplemental oxygen is needed to avert sickness.</p>
<p>As altitude increases, the available amount of oxygen to sustain mental and physical alertness decreases with the overall air pressure, though the relative percentage of oxygen in air, at about 21%, remains practically unchanged up to 21,000 metres (70,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_mean_square_speed" title="Root mean square speed">RMS velocities</a> of diatomic nitrogen and oxygen are very similar and thus no change occurs in the ratio of oxygen to nitrogen until stratospheric heights.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">Dehydration</a> due to the higher rate of water vapor lost from the lungs at higher altitudes may contribute to the symptoms of altitude sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rate of ascent, altitude attained, amount of physical activity at high altitude, as well as individual susceptibility, are contributing factors to the onset and severity of high-altitude illness.</p>
<p>Altitude sickness usually occurs following a rapid ascent and can usually be prevented by ascending slowly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-4">[4]</a></sup> In most of these cases, the symptoms are temporary and usually abate as altitude acclimatization occurs. However, in extreme cases, altitude sickness can be fatal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_altitude">High altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: High altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Breathing#Air_pressure" title="Breathing">Breathing § Air pressure</a></div>
<p>At high altitude, 1,500 to 3,500 metres (4,900 to 11,500 ft), the onset of physiological effects of diminished inspiratory oxygen pressure (PiO<sub>2</sub>) includes decreased exercise performance and increased ventilation (lower arterial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_pressure_of_carbon_dioxide" title="Partial pressure of carbon dioxide">partial pressure of carbon dioxide</a>- PCO<sub>2</sub>). While arterial oxygen transport may be only slightly impaired the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_(medicine)" title="Oxygen saturation (medicine)">arterial oxygen saturation</a>, SaO<sub>2</sub>, generally stays above 90%. Altitude sickness is common between 2,400 and 4,000m because of the large number of people who ascend rapidly to these altitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auerbach_2007_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Auerbach_2007-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very_high_altitude">Very high altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Very high altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At very high altitude, 3,500 to 5,500 metres (11,500 to 18,000 ft), maximum SaO<sub>2</sub> falls below 90% as the arterial PO<sub>2</sub> falls below 60mmHg. Extreme <a href="/wiki/Hypoxemia" title="Hypoxemia">hypoxemia</a> may occur during exercise, during sleep, and in the presence of high altitude pulmonary edema or other acute lung conditions. Severe altitude illness occurs most commonly in this range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auerbach_2007_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Auerbach_2007-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_altitude">Extreme altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Extreme altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Above 5,500 metres (18,000 ft), marked hypoxemia, <a href="/wiki/Hypocapnia" title="Hypocapnia">hypocapnia</a>, and alkalosis are characteristic of extreme altitudes. Progressive deterioration of physiologic function eventually outstrips acclimatization. As a result, no permanent human habitation occurs above 6,000 metres (20,000 ft). A period of acclimatization is necessary when ascending to extreme altitude; abrupt ascent without supplemental oxygen for other than brief exposures invites severe altitude sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auerbach_2007_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Auerbach_2007-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Breathing#Air_pressure" title="Breathing">physiology of altitude sickness</a> is based on the following equation:</p>
<p><span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub><i>gas</i></sub>=<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">A</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">T</span></span><i>D</i><sub><i>k</i></sub>(<i>P</i><sub><i>1</i></sub>−<i>P</i><sub><i>2</i></sub>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Where <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub><i>gas</i></sub></span> is the diffusion rate, A is the area of the lung, T is the thickness of the lung membranes, <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i><sub><i>k</i></sub>(<i>P</i><sub><i>1</i></sub>−<i>P</i><sub><i>2</i></sub>)</span> is the difference in partial pressure of gases - but most importantly <span class="texhtml"><i>CO</i><sub><i>2</i></sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>O</i><sub><i>2</i></sub></span>-where in high altitudes the partial pressure differences for <span class="texhtml"><i>O</i><sub><i>2</i></sub></span> are low and the differences in partial pressures for <span class="texhtml"><i>CO</i><sub><i>2</i></sub></span> are high. Thus <span class="texhtml"><i>CO</i><sub><i>2</i></sub></span> will have a high diffusion out and O2 will have a low diffusion through the alveolar membranes and into the blood.</p>
<p>The body's response to high altitude includes the following:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>↑ Erythropoietin → ↑ hematocrit and hemoglobin</li>
<li>↑ 2,3-BPG (allows ↑ release of O2 and a right shift on the Hb-O2 disassociation curve)</li>
<li>↑ kidney excretion of bicarbonate (use of acetazolamide can augment for treatment)</li>
<li>Chronic hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (can cause right ventricular hypertrophy)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ascending slowly is the best way to avoid altitude sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-4">[4]</a></sup> Avoiding strenuous activity such as skiing, hiking, etc. in the first 24 hours at high altitude reduces the symptoms of AMS. Alcohol and sleeping pills are respiratory depressants, and thus slow down the acclimatization process and should be avoided. Alcohol also tends to cause dehydration and exacerbates AMS. Thus, avoiding alcohol consumption in the first 24–48 hours at a higher altitude is optimal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-acclimatization">Pre-acclimatization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pre-acclimatization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pre-acclimatization is when the body develops tolerance to low oxygen concentrations before ascending to an altitude. It significantly reduces risk because less time has to be spent at altitude to acclimatize in the traditional way. Additionally, because less time has to be spent on the mountain, less food and supplies have to be taken up. Several commercial systems exist that use <a href="/wiki/Altitude_tent" title="Altitude tent">altitude tents</a>, so called because they mimic altitude by reducing the percentage of oxygen in the air while keeping air pressure constant to the surroundings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altitude_acclimatization">Altitude acclimatization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Altitude acclimatization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Altitude acclimatization is the process of adjusting to decreasing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> levels at higher elevations, in order to avoid altitude sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acclimatization_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Acclimatization-12">[12]</a></sup> Once above approximately 3,000 metres (10,000 ft) – a pressure of 70 kilopascals (0.69 atm) – most climbers and high-altitude trekkers take the "climb-high, sleep-low" approach. For high-altitude climbers, a typical acclimatization regimen might be to stay a few days at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_camp" title="Base camp">base camp</a>, climb up to a higher camp (slowly), and then return to base camp. A subsequent climb to the higher camp then includes an overnight stay. This process is then repeated a few times, each time extending the time spent at higher altitudes to let the body adjust to the oxygen level there, a process that involves the production of additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Once the climber has acclimatized to a given altitude, the process is repeated with camps placed at progressively higher elevations. The rule of thumb is to ascend no more than 300 m (1,000 ft) per day to sleep. That is, one can climb from 3,000 m (9,800 ft) (70 kPa or 0.69 atm) to 4,500 m (15,000 ft) (58 kPa or 0.57 atm) in one day, but one should then descend back to 3,300 m (10,800 ft) (67.5 kPa or 0.666 atm) to sleep. This process cannot safely be rushed, and this is why climbers need to spend days (or even weeks at times) acclimatizing before attempting to climb a high peak. Simulated altitude equipment that produces hypoxic (reduced oxygen) air can be used to acclimate to high altitude, reducing the total time required on the mountain itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Altitude acclimatization is necessary for some people who move rapidly from lower altitudes to intermediate altitudes (e.g., by aircraft and ground transportation over a few hours), such as from sea level to 8,000 feet (2,400 m) as in many Colorado, USA mountain resorts. Stopping at an intermediate altitude overnight can alleviate or eliminate occurrences of AMS.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medications">Medications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Medications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The drug <a href="/wiki/Acetazolamide" title="Acetazolamide">acetazolamide</a> (trade name Diamox) may help some people making a rapid ascent to sleeping altitude above 2,700 metres (9,000 ft), and it may also be effective if started early in the course of AMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Acetazolamide can be taken before symptoms appear as a preventive measure at a dose of 125 mg twice daily. The <a href="/wiki/Everest_Base_Camp" title="Everest Base Camp">Everest Base Camp</a> Medical Centre cautions against its routine use as a substitute for a reasonable ascent schedule, except where rapid ascent is forced by flying into high altitude locations or due to terrain considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaseCampMD_prophylaxis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaseCampMD_prophylaxis-14">[14]</a></sup> The Centre suggests a dosage of 125 mg twice daily for prophylaxis, starting from 24 hours before ascending until a few days at the highest altitude or on descending;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaseCampMD_prophylaxis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-BaseCampMD_prophylaxis-14">[14]</a></sup> with 250 mg twice daily recommended for treatment of AMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC) suggest the same dose for prevention of 125 mg acetazolamide every 12 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_Yellow_Book_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_Yellow_Book-16">[16]</a></sup> Acetazolamide, a mild diuretic, works by stimulating the kidneys to secrete more bicarbonate in the urine, thereby acidifying the blood. This change in pH stimulates the respiratory center to increase the depth and frequency of respiration, thus speeding the natural acclimatization process. An undesirable side-effect of acetazolamide is a reduction in aerobic endurance performance. Other minor side effects include a tingle-sensation in hands and feet. Although a sulfonamide, acetazolamide is a non-antibiotic and has not been shown to cause life-threatening allergic cross-reactivity in those with a self-reported sulfa allergy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Dosage of 1000 mg/day will produce a 25% decrease in performance, on top of the reduction due to high-altitude exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The CDC advises that <a href="/wiki/Dexamethasone" title="Dexamethasone">Dexamethasone</a> be reserved for treatment of severe AMS and HACE during descents, and notes that <a href="/wiki/Nifedipine" title="Nifedipine">Nifedipine</a> may prevent HAPE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_Yellow_Book_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_Yellow_Book-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A single <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trial</a> found that <a href="/wiki/Sumatriptan" title="Sumatriptan">sumatriptan</a> may help prevent altitude sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17557349_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17557349-21">[21]</a></sup> Despite their popularity, <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> treatments have not been found to be effective medications for prevention of AMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19273551_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19273551-22">[22]</a></sup> Interest in phosphodiesterase inhibitors such as <a href="/wiki/Sildenafil" title="Sildenafil">sildenafil</a> has been limited by the possibility that these drugs might worsen the headache of mountain sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17408118_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17408118-23">[23]</a></sup> A promising possible preventive for altitude sickness is myo-inositol trispyrophosphate (ITPP), which increases the amount of oxygen released by hemoglobin.</p>
<p>Prior to the onset of altitude sickness, <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">ibuprofen</a> is a suggested non-steroidal anti-inflammatory and painkiller that can help alleviate both the headache and nausea associated with AMS. It has not been studied for the prevention of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_edema" title="Cerebral edema">cerebral edema</a> (swelling of the brain) associated with extreme symptoms of AMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>For centuries, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aymaras" title="Aymaras">Aymaras</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Altiplano" title="Altiplano">Altiplano</a>, have chewed <a href="/wiki/Coca" title="Coca">coca</a> leaves to try to alleviate the symptoms of mild altitude sickness. In Chinese and Tibetan traditional medicine, an extract of the root tissue of <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodiola_rosea" title="Rhodiola rosea">Radix rhodiola</a></i> is often taken in order to prevent the same symptoms, though neither of these therapies has been proven effective in clinical study.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_enrichment">Oxygen enrichment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Oxygen enrichment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In high-altitude conditions, oxygen enrichment can counteract the hypoxia related effects of altitude sickness. A small amount of supplemental oxygen reduces the equivalent altitude in climate-controlled rooms. At 3,400 metres (11,200 ft) (67 kPa or 0.66 atm), raising the oxygen concentration level by 5% via an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_concentrator" title="Oxygen concentrator">oxygen concentrator</a> and an existing ventilation system provides an effective altitude of 3,000 m (10,000 ft) (70 kPa or 0.69 atm), which is more tolerable for those unaccustomed to high altitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7777705_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7777705-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oxygen from gas bottles or liquid containers can be applied directly via a nasal cannula or mask. Oxygen concentrators based upon <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorption</a> (PSA), VSA, or vacuum-pressure swing adsorption (VPSA) can be used to generate the oxygen if electricity is available. Stationary oxygen concentrators typically use PSA technology, which has performance degradations at the lower barometric pressures at high altitudes. One way to compensate for the performance degradation is to utilize a concentrator with more flow capacity. There are also portable oxygen concentrators that can be used on vehicular DC power or on internal batteries, and at least one system commercially available measures and compensates for the altitude effect on its performance up to 4,000 m (13,000 ft). The application of high-purity oxygen from one of these methods increases the partial pressure of oxygen by raising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIO2" title="FIO2">FiO<sub>2</sub></a> (fraction of inspired oxygen).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods">Other methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increased water intake may also help in acclimatization<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dannen_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-dannen-26">[26]</a></sup> to replace the fluids lost through heavier breathing in the thin, dry air found at altitude, although consuming excessive quantities ("over-hydration") has no benefits and may cause dangerous <a href="/wiki/Hyponatremia" title="Hyponatremia">hyponatremia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude_sickness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The only reliable treatment and, in many cases the only option available, is to descend. Attempts to treat or stabilize the patient <i>in situ</i> (at altitude) are dangerous unless highly controlled and with good medical facilities. However, the following treatments have been used when the patient's location and circumstances permit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Oxygen may be used for mild to moderate AMS below 3,700 metres (12,000 ft) and is commonly provided by physicians at mountain resorts. Symptoms abate in 12 to 36 hours without the need to descend.</li>
<li>For more serious cases of AMS, or where rapid descent is impractical, a <a href="/wiki/Gamow_bag" title="Gamow bag">Gamow bag</a>, a portable plastic <a href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_medicine" title="Hyperbaric medicine">hyperbaric chamber</a> inflated with a foot pump, can be used to reduce the effective altitude by as much as 1,500 m (5,000 ft). A Gamow bag is generally used only as an aid to evacuate severe AMS patients, not to treat them at altitude.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acetazolamide" title="Acetazolamide">Acetazolamide</a> 250 mg twice daily dosing assists in AMS treatment by quickening altitude acclimatization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid5916650_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid5916650-27">[27]</a></sup> A study by the Denali Medical Research Project concluded: "In established cases of acute mountain sickness, treatment with acetazolamide relieves symptoms, improves arterial oxygenation, and prevents further impairment of pulmonary gas exchange."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1739236_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1739236-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>The folk remedy for altitude sickness in <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> is a tea made from the coca plant. See <a href="/wiki/Coca_tea" title="Coca tea">mate de coca</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steroids" title="Steroids">Steroids</a> can be used to treat the symptoms of pulmonary or cerebral edema, but do not treat the underlying AMS.</li>
<li>Two studies in 2012 showed that Ibuprofen 600 milligrams three times daily was effective at decreasing the severity and incidence of AMS; it was not clear if HAPE or HACE was affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipman_2012_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lipman_2012-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gertsch_2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gertsch_2012-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
