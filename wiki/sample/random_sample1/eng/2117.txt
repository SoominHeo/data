<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fast Ethernet</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a>, <b>Fast Ethernet</b> is a collective term for a number of <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> standards that carry traffic at the nominal rate of 100 Mbit/s (the original Ethernet speed was 10 Mbit/s). Of the Fast Ethernet standards, <b>100BASE-TX</b> is by far the most common.</p>
<p>Fast Ethernet was introduced in 1995 as the <b>IEEE 802.3u</b> standard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and remained the fastest version of Ethernet for three years before the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">Gigabit Ethernet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The acronym <i>GE/FE</i> is sometimes used for devices supporting both standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_design">General design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fast Ethernet is an extension of the 10-megabit <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> standard. It runs on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTP_cable" title="UTP cable">UTP</a> data or <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber_cable" title="Optical fiber cable">optical fiber cable</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_topology" title="Star topology">star wired bus topology</a>, similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T">10BASE-T</a> where all cables are attached to a hub. Fast Ethernet devices are generally backward compatible with existing 10BASE-T systems, enabling plug-and-play upgrades from 10BASE-T. Fast Ethernet is sometimes referred to as <b>100BASE-X</b>, where "X" is a placeholder for the FX and TX variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The standard specifies the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSMA/CD" title="CSMA/CD">CSMA/CD</a> for media access control. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-duplex" title="Full-duplex">full-duplex</a> mode is also specified and in practice all modern networks use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethernet_switch" title="Ethernet switch">Ethernet switches</a> and operate in full-duplex mode.</p>
<p>The "100" in the media type designation refers to the transmission speed of 100 Mbit/s, while the "BASE" refers to <a href="/wiki/Baseband" title="Baseband">baseband</a> signalling. The letter following the dash ("T" or "F") refers to the physical medium that carries the signal (twisted pair or fiber, respectively), while the last character ("X", "4", etc.) refers to the used <a href="/wiki/Line_code" title="Line code">encoding method</a>.</p>
<p>A Fast Ethernet adapter can be logically divided into a <a href="/wiki/Media_access_control" title="Media access control">media access controller</a> (MAC), which deals with the higher-level issues of medium availability, and a Physical Layer Interface (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHY" title="PHY">PHY</a>). The MAC may be linked to the PHY by a four-bit 25 MHz synchronous parallel interface known as a <a href="/wiki/Media-independent_interface" title="Media-independent interface">media-independent interface</a> (MII), or by a two-bit 50 MHz variant called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduced_media_independent_interface" title="Reduced media independent interface">reduced media independent interface</a> (RMII). In rare cases the MII may be an external connection but is usually a connection between ICs in a network adapter or even within a single IC. The specs are written based on the assumption that the interface between MAC and PHY will be a MII but they do not require it. Repeaters (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_hub" title="Network hub">hubs</a>) may use the MII to connect to multiple PHYs for their different interfaces.</p>
<p>The MII fixes the theoretical maximum data bit rate for all versions of Fast Ethernet to 100 Mbit/s. The <a href="/wiki/Data_signaling_rate" title="Data signaling rate">data signaling rate</a> actually observed on real networks is less than the theoretical maximum, due to the necessary header and trailer (addressing and error-detection bits) on every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_(telecommunications)" title="Frame (telecommunications)">frame</a>, the occasional "lost frame" due to noise, and time waiting after each sent frame for other devices on the network to finish transmitting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copper">Copper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Copper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><b>100BASE-T</b> is any of several Fast Ethernet standards for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twisted_pair_cable" title="Twisted pair cable">twisted pair cables</a>, including: 100BASE-TX (100 Mbit/s over two-pair <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">Cat5</a> or better cable), 100BASE-T4 (100 Mbit/s over four-pair <a href="/wiki/Category_3_cable" title="Category 3 cable">Cat3</a> or better cable, defunct), 100BASE-T2 (100 Mbit/s over two-pair Cat3 or better cable, also defunct). The segment length for a 100BASE-T cable is limited to 100 metres (328 ft) (as with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T">10BASE-T</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">gigabit Ethernet</a>). All are or were standards under <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3" title="IEEE 802.3">IEEE 802.3</a> (approved 1995). Almost all 100BASE-T installations are 100BASE-TX.</p>
<p>In the early days of Fast Ethernet, much vendor advertising centered on claims by competing standards that said vendors' standards will work better with existing cables than other standards. In practice, it was quickly discovered that few existing networks actually met the assumed standards, because 10 Megabit Ethernet was very tolerant of minor deviations from specified electrical characteristics and few installers ever bothered to make exact measurements of cable and connection quality; if Ethernet worked over a cable, no matter how well it worked, it was deemed acceptable. Thus most networks had to be rewired for 100 Megabit speed whether or not there had supposedly been CAT3 or CAT5 cable runs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><b>100BASE-TX</b> is the predominant form of Fast Ethernet, and runs over two wire-pairs inside a <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">category 5</a> or above cable. Like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T">10BASE-T</a>, the active pairs in a standard connection are terminated on pins 1, 2, 3 and 6. Since a typical category 5 cable contains 4 pairs, it can support two 100BASE-TX links with a wiring adaptor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Cabling is conventional wired to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TIA/EIA-568-B" title="TIA/EIA-568-B">TIA/EIA-568-B</a>'s termination standards, T568A or T568B. This places the active pairs on the orange and green pairs (<a href="/wiki/Canon_(basic_principle)" title="Canon (basic principle)">canonical</a> second and third pairs).</p>
<p>Each <a href="/wiki/Network_segment" title="Network segment">network segment</a> can have a maximum cabling distance of 100 metres (328 ft). In its typical configuration, 100BASE-TX uses one pair of twisted wires in each direction, providing 100 Mbit/s of throughput in each direction (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-duplex" title="Full-duplex">full-duplex</a>). See <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3" title="IEEE 802.3">IEEE 802.3</a> for more details.</p>
<p>The configuration of 100BASE-TX networks is very similar to 10BASE-T. When used to build a <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a>, the devices on the network (computers, printers etc.) are typically connected to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_hub" title="Network hub">hub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">switch</a>, creating a <a href="/wiki/Star_network" title="Star network">star network</a>. Alternatively it is possible to connect two devices directly using a <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_crossover_cable" title="Ethernet crossover cable">crossover cable</a>.</p>
<p>With 100BASE-TX hardware, the raw bits (4 bits wide clocked at 25 MHz at the MII) go through <a href="/wiki/4B5B" title="4B5B">4B5B</a> binary encoding to generate a series of 0 and 1 symbols clocked at 125 MHz <a href="/wiki/Symbol_rate" title="Symbol rate">symbol rate</a>. The 4B5B encoding provides DC equalization and spectrum shaping (see the standard for details). Just as in the 100BASE-FX case, the bits are then transferred to the physical medium attachment layer using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NRZI" title="NRZI">NRZI</a> encoding. However, 100BASE-TX introduces an additional, medium dependent sublayer, which employs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MLT-3" title="MLT-3">MLT-3</a> as a final encoding of the data stream before transmission, resulting in a maximum "fundamental frequency" of 31.25 MHz. The procedure is borrowed from the ANSI X3.263 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDDI" title="FDDI">FDDI</a> specifications, with minor discrepancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mlt3_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mlt3-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-T4">100BASE-T4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 100BASE-T4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>100BASE-T4</b> was an early implementation of Fast Ethernet. It requires four twisted copper pairs, but those pairs were only required to be category 3 rather than the category 5 required by TX. One pair is reserved for transmit, one for receive, and the remaining two will switch direction as negotiated. A very unusual <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=8B6T&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="8B6T (page does not exist)">8B6T</a> code is used to convert 8 data bits into 6 base-3 digits (the signal shaping is possible as there are nearly three times as many 6-digit base-3 numbers as there are 8-digit base-2 numbers). The two resulting 3-digit base-3 symbols are sent in parallel over 3 pairs using 3-level <a href="/wiki/Pulse-amplitude_modulation" title="Pulse-amplitude modulation">pulse-amplitude modulation</a> (PAM-3). The fact that 3 pairs are used to transmit in each direction makes 100BASE-T4 inherently half-duplex. This standard can be implemented with CAT 3, 4, 5 UTP cables, or STP if needed against interference. Maximum distance is limited to 100 meters. 100BASE-T4 was not widely adopted but the technology developed for it is used in 1000BASE-T.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spurgeon_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spurgeon-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <b>100BASE-T2</b>, standardized in IEEE 802.3y, the data is transmitted over two copper pairs, 4 bits per symbol, but these pairs are only required to be category 3 rather than the category 5 required by TX. It uses these two pairs for simultaneously transmitting and receiving on both pairs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breyer_and_Riley_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breyer_and_Riley-8">[8]</a></sup> thus allowing full-duplex operation. First, a 4-bit symbol is expanded into two 3-bit symbols through a non-trivial scrambling procedure based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_feedback_shift_register" title="Linear feedback shift register">linear feedback shift register</a>; see the standard for details. This is needed to flatten the bandwidth and emission spectrum of the signal, as well as to match transmission line properties. The mapping of the original bits to the symbol codes is not constant in time and has a fairly large period (appearing as a pseudo-random sequence). The final mapping from symbols to <a href="/wiki/Pulse-amplitude_modulation" title="Pulse-amplitude modulation">PAM-5</a> line modulation levels obeys the table on the right. 100BASE-T2 was not widely adopted but the technology developed for it is used in 1000BASE-T.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spurgeon_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spurgeon-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-T1">100BASE-T1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 100BASE-T1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <b>100BASE-T1</b>, standardized in IEEE 802.3bw-2015 Clause 96, the data is transmitted over a single copper pair, 3 bits per symbol (PAM3). It supports only full-duplex, transmitting in both directions simultaneously. The twisted-pair cable is required to support 66 MHz, with a maximum length of 15 m. No specific connector is defined. The standard is intended for automotive applications or when Fast Ethernet is to be integrated into another application. It has been developed as <a href="/wiki/BroadR-Reach" title="BroadR-Reach">BroadR-Reach</a> before IEEE standardization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BaseVG">100BaseVG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 100BaseVG">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proposed and marketed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard" title="Hewlett Packard">Hewlett Packard</a>, <a href="/wiki/100BaseVG" title="100BaseVG">100BaseVG</a> was an alternative design using category 3 cabling and a token concept instead of CSMA/CD. It was slated for standardization as IEEE 802.12 but it quickly vanished when switched 100BASE-TX became popular.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiber_optics">Fiber optics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fiber optics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-FX">100BASE-FX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 100BASE-FX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>100BASE-FX</b> is a version of Fast Ethernet over <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a>. It uses a 1300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a> near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> (NIR) light <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> transmitted via two strands of optical fiber, one for receive (RX) and the other for transmit (TX). Maximum length is 412 metres (1,350 ft)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-duplex" title="Half-duplex">half-duplex</a> connections (to ensure <a href="/wiki/Collision_(telecommunications)" title="Collision (telecommunications)">collisions</a> are detected), and 2 kilometres (6,600 ft) for full-duplex over <a href="/wiki/Multi-mode_optical_fiber" title="Multi-mode optical fiber">multi-mode optical fiber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> 100BASE-FX uses the same 4B5B encoding and NRZI line code that 100BASE-TX does. 100BASE-FX should use SC, ST, LC, MTRJ or MIC <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber_connector" title="Optical fiber connector">connectors</a> with SC being the preferred option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 100BASE-FX Physical Medium Dependent (PMD) sublayer is defined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDDI" title="FDDI">FDDI</a>'s PMD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> so 100BASE-FX is not compatible with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-FL" title="10BASE-FL">10BASE-FL</a>, the 10 MBit/s version over optical fiber.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-SX">100BASE-SX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 100BASE-SX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>100BASE-SX</b> is a version of Fast Ethernet over optical fiber standardized in TIA/EIA-785-1-2002. It uses two strands of multi-mode optical fiber for receive and transmit. It is a lower cost alternative to using 100BASE-FX, because it uses short wavelength optics which are significantly less expensive than the long wavelength optics used in 100BASE-FX. Depending on fiber quality, 100BASE-SX has a minimum reach of 300 metres (980 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>100BASE-SX uses the same wavelength as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10BASE-FL" title="10BASE-FL">10BASE-FL</a>, the 10 Mbit/s version over optical fiber. Unlike 100BASE-FX, this allows 100BASE-SX to be backwards-compatible with 10BASE-FL.</p>
<p>Because of the shorter wavelength used (850 nm) and the shorter distance it can support, 100BASE-SX uses less expensive optical components (LEDs instead of lasers) which makes it an attractive option for those upgrading from 10BASE-FL and those who do not require long distances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-BX10">100BASE-BX10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 100BASE-BX10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>100BASE-BX10</b> is a version of Fast Ethernet over a single strand of optical fiber (unlike 100BASE-FX, which uses a pair of fibers). Single-mode fiber is used, along with a special optics which splits the signal into transmit and receive wavelengths; the two wavelengths used for transmit and receive are 1310 nm and 1550 nm. The transceivers on each side of the fiber are not equal, as the one transmitting "downstream" (from the center of the network to the outside) uses the 1550 nm wavelength, and the one transmitting "upstream" uses the 1310 nm wavelength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-802.3-2012-s5-c58_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-802.3-2012-s5-c58-14">[14]</a></sup> Transceivers for longer distances of 20 or 40 km are also available.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100BASE-LX10">100BASE-LX10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fast_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 100BASE-LX10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>100BASE-LX10</b> is a version of Fast Ethernet over two <a href="/wiki/Single-mode_optical_fiber" title="Single-mode optical fiber">single-mode optical fibers</a>. It has a nominal reach of 10 km and a nominal <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of 1310 nm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-802.3-2012-s5-c58_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-802.3-2012-s5-c58-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
