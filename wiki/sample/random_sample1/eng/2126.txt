<p><b>Palaeognathae</b>, or <b>paleognaths</b>, is one of the two living <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> – the other being <a href="/wiki/Neognathae" title="Neognathae">Neognathae</a>. Together, these two clades form the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neornithes" title="Neornithes">Neornithes</a>. Palaeognathae contains five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> branches of flightless lineages (plus two extinct <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a>), termed <a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratites</a>, and one flying lineage, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropic" title="Neotropic">Neotropic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tinamou" title="Tinamou">tinamous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wetmore1960_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wetmore1960-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker2014-3">[3]</a></sup> There are 47 species of tinamous, 5 of <a href="/wiki/Kiwi" title="Kiwi">kiwis</a> (<i>Apteryx</i>), 3 of <a href="/wiki/Cassowary" title="Cassowary">cassowaries</a> (<i>Casuarius</i>), 1 of <a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">emus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Dromaius" title="Dromaius">Dromaius</a></i>) (another became extinct in historic times), 2 of <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(bird)" title="Rhea (bird)">rheas</a> and 2 of <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clements_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-4">[4]</a></sup> Recent research has indicated that paleognaths are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> but the traditional taxonomic split between flightless and flighted forms is incorrect; tinamous are within the <a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratite</a> radiation, meaning flightlessness arose independently multiple times via <a href="/wiki/Parallel_evolution" title="Parallel evolution">parallel evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell2014-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are three extinct groups, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithornithiformes" title="Lithornithiformes">Lithornithiformes</a>, the Dinornithiformes (<a href="/wiki/Moa" title="Moa">moas</a>) and the Aepyornithiformes (<a href="/wiki/Elephant_bird" title="Elephant bird">elephant birds</a>), that are undisputed members of Palaeognathae. There are other extinct birds which have been allied with the Palaeognathae by at least one author, but their affinities are a matter of dispute.</p>
<p>The word <i>Paleognath</i> is derived from the ancient Greek for "old jaws" in reference to the skeletal anatomy of the <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">palate</a>, which is described as more primitive and reptilian than that in other birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houde1988_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houde1988-6">[6]</a></sup> Paleognathous birds retain some <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> morphological characters but are by no means living fossils as their genomes continued to evolve at the DNA level under selective pressure at rates comparable to the Neognathae branch of living birds, though there is some controversy about the precise relationship between them and the other birds. There are also several other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_controversies" title="Scientific controversies">scientific controversies</a> about their evolution (see below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonardetal2005_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonardetal2005-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_paleognaths" title="List of paleognaths">List of paleognaths</a></div>
<p>No unambiguously paleognathous fossil birds are known until the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> (though birds occasionally interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Lithornithidae" title="Lithornithidae">lithornithids</a> occur in <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> <a href="/wiki/Appalachia_(Mesozoic)" title="Appalachia (Mesozoic)">appalachian</a> sites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palaeogene_Fossil_Birds_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeogene_Fossil_Birds-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escholarship.org_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-escholarship.org-9">[9]</a></sup> ), but there have been many reports of putative paleognathes, and it has long been inferred that they may have evolved in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>. Given the northern hemisphere location of the morphologically most <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> fossil forms (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lithornis" title="Lithornis">Lithornis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudocrypturus" title="Pseudocrypturus">Pseudocrypturus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Paracathartes" title="Paracathartes">Paracathartes</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Palaeotis" title="Palaeotis">Palaeotis</a></i>), a <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasian</a> origin for the group can be inferred. The present almost entirely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondwanan" title="Gondwanan">Gondwanan</a> distribution would then have resulted from multiple colonisations of the southern landmasses by flying forms that subsequently evolved flightlessness, and in many cases, gigantism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yonezawa2017_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yonezawa2017-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

</div>
<p>One study of molecular and paleontological data found that modern bird orders, including the paleognathous ones, began diverging from one another in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper.26Penny1997_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper.26Penny1997-11">[11]</a></sup> Benton (2005) summarized this and other molecular studies as implying that paleognaths should have arisen 110 to 120 million years ago in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a>. He points out, however, that there is no fossil record until 70 million years ago, leaving a 45 million year gap. He asks whether the paleognath fossils will be found one day, or whether the estimated rates of molecular evolution are too slow, and that bird evolution actually accelerated during an adaptive radiation after the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary">Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary</a> (K–Pg boundary).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benton2005_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benton2005-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other authors questioned the <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a> of the Palaeognathae on various grounds, suggesting that they could be a hodgepodge of unrelated birds that have come to be grouped together because they are coincidentally flightless. Unrelated birds might have developed ratite-like anatomies multiple times around the world through <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>. McDowell (1948) asserted that the similarities in the palate anatomy of paleognathes might actually be <a href="/wiki/Neoteny" title="Neoteny">neoteny</a>, or retained embryonic features. He noted that there were other feature of the skull, such as the retention of sutures into adulthood, that were like those of juvenile birds. Thus, perhaps the characteristic palate was actually a frozen stage that many carinate bird embryos passed through during development. The retention of early developmental stages, then, may have been a mechanism by which various birds became flightless and came to look similar to one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowell1948_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDowell1948-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hope (2002) reviewed all known bird fossils from the Mesozoic looking for evidence of the origin of the evolutionary radiation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neornithes" title="Neornithes">Neornithes</a>. That radiation would also signal that the paleognaths had already diverged. She notes five <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a> taxa that have been assigned to the Palaeognathae. She finds that none of them can be clearly assigned as such. However, she does find evidence that the Neognathae and, therefore, also the Palaeognathae had diverged no later than the Early <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">Campanian</a> age of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hope2002_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hope2002-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Vegavis" title="Vegavis">Vegavis</a></i> is a fossil bird from the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> period of <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> Antarctica. <i>Vegavis</i> is most closely related to true ducks. Because virtually all phylogenetic analyses predict that ducks diverged after paleognathes, this is evidence that paleognathes had already arisen well before that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkeetal2005_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarkeetal2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>An exceptionally preserved specimen of the extinct flying paleognathe <i><a href="/wiki/Lithornis" title="Lithornis">Lithornis</a></i> was published by Leonard et al. in 2005. It is an articulated and nearly complete fossil from the early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> of Denmark, and thought to have the best preserved lithornithiform skull ever found. The authors concluded that <i>Lithornis</i> was a close sister taxon to tinamous, rather than ostriches, and that the lithorniforms + tinamous were the most basal paleognaths. They concluded that all ratites, therefore, were monophyletic, descending from one common ancestor that became flightless. They also interpret the paleognath-like <i><a href="/wiki/Limenavis" title="Limenavis">Limenavis</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, as possible evidence of a Cretaceous and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> origin for paleognathes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonardetal2005_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leonardetal2005-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>An ambitious genomic analysis of the living birds was performed in 2007, and it contradicted Leonard et al. (2005). It found that tinamous are not primitive within the paleognathes, but among the most advanced. This requires multiple events of flightlessness within the paleognathes and partially refutes the Gondwana vicariance hypothesis (see below). The study looked at DNA sequences from 19 loci in 169 species. It recovered evidence that the paleognathes are one natural group (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a>), and that their divergence from other birds is the oldest divergence of any extant bird groups. It also placed the tinamous within the ratites, more derived than ostriches, or rheas and as a sister group to emus and kiwis, and this makes ratites <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hackettetal2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hackettetal2008-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A related study addressed the issue of paleognath phylogeny exclusively. It used molecular analysis and looked at twenty unlinked nuclear genes. This study concluded that there were at least three events of flightlessness that produced the different ratite orders, that the similarities between the ratite orders are partly due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>, and that the Palaeognathae are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a>, but the ratites are not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harshmanetal2008_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harshmanetal2008-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 2010, DNA analysis studies have shown that tinamous are the <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> to extinct <a href="/wiki/Moa" title="Moa">moa</a> of New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baker2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell2014_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips2010_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips2010-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allentoft2012_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allentoft2012-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_classifications">History of classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the history of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> there have been many competing taxonomies of the birds now included in the Palaeognathae. The topic has been studied by Dubois (1891), Sharpe (1891), Shufeldt (1904), Sibley and Ahlquist (1972, 1981) and Cracraft (1981).</p>
<p>Merrem (1813) is often credited with classifying the paleognathes together, and he coined the taxon "Ratitae" (see above). However, Linnaeus (1758) placed cassowaries, emus, ostriches, and rheas together in <i>Struthio</i>. Lesson (1831) added the kiwis to the Ratitae. Parker (1864) reported the similarities of the palates of the tinamous and ratites, but Huxley (1867) is more widely credited with this insight. Huxley still placed the tinamous with the Carinatae of Merrem because of their keeled <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sterna</a>, and thought that they were most closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">Galliformes</a>.</p>
<p>Pycraft (1900) presented a major advance when he coined the term Palaeognathae. He rejected the Ratitae-Carinatae classification that separated tinamous and ratites. He reasoned that a keelless, or "ratite", sternum could easily evolve in unrelated birds that independently became flightless. He also recognized that the ratites were secondarily flightless. His subdivisions were based on the characters of the palatal skeleton and other organ systems. He established seven roughly modern orders of living and fossil paleognaths (Casuarii, Struthiones, Rheae, Dinornithes, Aepyornithes, Apteryges, and Crypturi – the latter his term for tinamous, after the Tinamou genus <i><a href="/wiki/Crypturellus" title="Crypturellus">Crypturellus</a></i>).</p>
<p>The Palaeognathae are usually considered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superorder" title="Superorder">superorder</a>, but authors have treated them as a taxon as high as <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">subclass</a> (Stresemann 1927–1934) or as low as an <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> (Cracraft 1981).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paleognathes are named for a characteristic, complex architecture of the bones in the bony palate. Cracraft (1974) defined it with five characters.</p>
<ol>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Vomer" title="Vomer">vomer</a> is large and articulates with the <a href="/wiki/Premaxilla" title="Premaxilla">premaxillae</a> and maxillopalatines anteriorly. Posteriorly the vomer fuses to the ventral surface of the <a href="/wiki/Pterygoid_bone" title="Pterygoid bone">pterygoid</a>, and the palatines fuse to the ventral surface of this pterygovomer articulation.</li>
<li>The pterygoid prevents the palatine from articulating medially with the basisphenoid.</li>
<li>The palatine and pterygoid fuse into a rigid joint.</li>
<li>The articulation on the pterygoid for the basipterygoid process of the basicranium is located near the articulation between the pterygoid and quadrate.</li>
<li>The pterygoid–quadrate articulation is complex and includes the orbital process of the quadrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cracraft1974_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-cracraft1974-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Paleognathes share similar pelvis anatomy. There is a large, open <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilio%E2%80%93ischiatic_fenestra" title="Ilio–ischiatic fenestra">ilio–ischiatic fenestra</a> in the pelvis. The pubis and <a href="/wiki/Ischium" title="Ischium">ischium</a> are likely to be longer than the <a href="/wiki/Ilium_(bone)" title="Ilium (bone)">ilium</a>, protruding out beneath the tail. The <a href="/wiki/Acetabulum" title="Acetabulum">postacetabular</a> portion of the pelvis is longer than the preacetabular portion.</p>
<p>Paleognathes share a pattern of grooves in the horny covering of the bill. This covering is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhamphotheca" title="Rhamphotheca">rhamphotheca</a>. The paleognath pattern has one central strip of horn, with long, triangular, strips to either side.</p>
<p>In Paleognathes, the male incubates the eggs. The male may include in his nest the eggs of one female or more than one. He may also have eggs deposited in his nest by females that did not breed with him, in cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nest_parasite" title="Nest parasite">nest parasitism</a>. Only in ostriches and the <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_kiwi" title="Great spotted kiwi">great spotted kiwi</a> does the female also assist in incubating the eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tinamou" title="Tinamou">tinamous</a> of Central and South America are primarily terrestrial, though they fly weakly. Tinamous have very short tail feathers, giving them an almost tailess aspect. In general, they resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galliform" title="Galliform">galliform</a> birds like quails and grouse.</p>
<p>Tinamous have a very long, keeled, breastbone with an unusual three-pronged shape. This bone, the <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a>, has a central blade (the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carina_sterni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carina sterni (page does not exist)">Carina sterni</a>), with two long, slender <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lateral_trabeculae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lateral trabeculae (page does not exist)">lateral trabeculae</a>, which curve to either side and nearly touch the keel posteriorly. These trabeculae may also be thought of as the rims of two large <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/foramen" title="wikt:foramen">foramina</a> that incise the posterior edge of the sternum, and extend almost its whole length. Tinamous have a proper semicircular <a href="/wiki/Furcula" title="Furcula">furcula</a>, with no trace of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocleidium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hypocleidium (page does not exist)">hypocleidium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eyton1867_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eyton1867-22">[22]</a></sup> There is an acute angle between the <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coracoid" title="Coracoid">coracoid</a>, as in all flying birds. The pelvis has an open <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilio%E2%80%93ischiatic_fenestra" title="Ilio–ischiatic fenestra">ilio–ischiatic fenestra</a> that incises the posterior edge between the <a href="/wiki/Ilium_(bone)" title="Ilium (bone)">ilium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ischium" title="Ischium">ischium</a>, as in all paleognathes. Tinamous have no true <a href="/wiki/Pygostyle" title="Pygostyle">pygostyle</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_vertebrae" title="Caudal vertebrae">caudal vertebrae</a> remain unfused, as in ratites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feduccia1996_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feduccia1996-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tinamou feathers look like those of volant birds in that they have a rachis and two vanes. The structure of tinamou feathers is unique, however, in that they have barbs that remain joined at their tips. Thus the parallel barbs are separated only by slits between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies2002_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2002-24">[24]</a></sup> Tinamous have <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial glands</a>.</p>

<p>Ratite birds are strictly flightless and their anatomy reflects specializations for terrestrial life. The term "<a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratite</a>" is from the Latin word for raft, <i>ratis</i>, because they possess a flat breastbone, or <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a>, shaped like a raft. This characteristic sternum differs from that in flighted birds, where the pectoral musculature is disproportionately large to provide the power for wingbeats and the sternum develops a prominent keel, or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carina_sterni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carina sterni (page does not exist)">carina sterni</a> to anchor these muscles. The <a href="/wiki/Clavicle" title="Clavicle">clavicles</a> do not fuse into a furcula. Instead, if present at all, each is splint-like and lies along the medial border of the <a href="/wiki/Coracoid" title="Coracoid">coracoid</a>, attached there by a coraco–clavicular ligament. There is an obtuse angle between the <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coracoid" title="Coracoid">coracoid</a>, and the two bones fuse together to form a <a href="/wiki/Scapulocoracoid" title="Scapulocoracoid">scapulocoracoid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feduccia1996_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feduccia1996-23">[23]</a></sup> Ratites have reduced and simplified wing structures and strong legs. Except in some rhea wing feathers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barb_(feather)" title="Barb (feather)">barb</a> filaments that make up the <a href="/wiki/Pennaceous_feather" title="Pennaceous feather">vanes</a> of the feathers do not lock tightly together, giving the plumage a shaggier look and making it unnecessary to oil their feathers. Adult ratites have no preen gland (<a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial gland</a>) that contains preening oil.</p>
<p>Paleognaths as a whole tend to have proportionally small brains, and are among the living birds with the most limited cognitive abilities. Kiwis are exceptional, however, and have large brains comparable to those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parrots" title="Parrots">parrots</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songbirds" title="Songbirds">songbirds</a>, though evidence for similar levels of behaviour complexity are currently lacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sizes">Sizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Living members of Palaeognathae range from 6 inches (15 cm) to 9 feet (2.7 m) and weight can be from .09 to 345 pounds (0.0–156.5 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> Ostriches are the largest struthioniforms (members of the Struthioniformes order), with long legs and neck. They range in height from 5.7 to 9 feet (1.7–2.7 m) and weigh from 139 to 345 pounds (63–156 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> They have loose-feathered wings. Males have black and white feathers while the female has grayish brown feathers. They are unique among birds in that they retain only the third and fourth toe on each foot. Ostrich wings have claws, or <a href="/wiki/Ungual" title="Ungual">unguals</a>, on the first and second fingers (and, in some individuals, also on the third). Ostriches differ from other paleognathes in that they have a reduced vomer bone of the skull.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Emus are 6 to 7.5 feet (1.8–2.3 m) in height and weigh 75 to 110 pounds (34–50 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> They have short wings and the adults have brown feathers.</p>
<p>Rheas are 3 to 4.6 feet (91–140 cm) and weigh 33 to 88 pounds (15–40 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> Their feathers are gray or spotted brown and white. They have large wings but no tail feathers. They have no clavicles.</p>
<p>Cassowaries are 3.5 to 5.6 feet (1.1–1.7 m) in height and weigh 30 to 130 pounds (14–59 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> They have rudimentary wings with black feathers and six stiff, porcupine-like, quills in the place of their primary and secondary feathers.</p>
<p>Kiwis are the smallest of ratites, ranging in height from 14 to 22 inches (36–56 cm) and weight 2.6 to 8.6 pounds (1.2–3.9 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup> They have shaggy brown feathers.</p>
<p>Tinamous range in size from 8 to 21 inches (20–53 cm) and weigh 1.4 to 5 pounds (640–2,270 g).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grzimelek_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimelek-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotion">Locomotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locomotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the larger ratite birds have extremely long legs and the largest living bird, the <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a>, can run at speeds over 35 mph (60 km/h). Emus have long, strong legs and can run up to 30 mph (48 km/h). Cassowaries and rheas show a similar likeness in agility and some extinct forms may have reached speeds of 45 mph (75 km/h).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeography">Biogeography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biogeography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, the ratites are largely restricted to the Southern Hemisphere, though across the Cenozoic they were also present in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>, these southern continents were connected, forming a single continent called <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>. Gondwana is the crucial territory in a major scientific question about the evolution of Palaeognathae, and thus about the evolution of all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neornithes" title="Neornithes">Neornithes</a>.</p>
<p>Did the paleognathes evolve once, from one ancestor, on Gondwana during the Cretaceous, and then ride on the daughter landmasses that became today's southern continents, or did they evolve after the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a> from multiple flying ancestors on multiple continents around the world? The former is often called the Gondwana vicariance hypothesis. It is supported most strongly by molecular clock studies, but it is weakened by the lack of any Cretaceous or southern fossil paleognaths, as well as the early radiation of paleognaths in laurasian landmasses. The latter is called the Tertiary radiation hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tertiary_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-tertiary-27">[a]</a></sup> This hypothesis is supported by molecular phylogeny studies and matches the fossil record, but it is weakened by morphological phylogenetic studies. Both hypotheses have been supported and challenged by many studies by many authors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houde1988_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Houde1988-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A recent study of both genetic and morphological divergence concludes that the group had a <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasian</a> origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yonezawa2017_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yonezawa2017-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gondwana_vicariance_hypothesis">Gondwana vicariance hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gondwana vicariance hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cracraft (2001) gave a comprehensive review to the data and strongly supported the Gondwana vicariance hypothesis with phylogenetic evidence and historical biogeography. He cites molecular clock studies that show a basal divergence date for neornithes being around 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago">Mya</a>. He credits the authors of the molecular clock studies with the observation that the lack of southern paleognath fossils may correspond to the relatively scarce southern <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> deposits, and the relative lack of paleontological field work in the southern hemisphere. Moreover, Cracraft synthesizes the morphological and molecular studies, noting conflicts between the two, and finds that the bulk of the evidence favors paleognath <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a>. He also notes that not only the ratites, but other basal groups of neognathous birds, show trans-Antarctic distribution, as we would expect if the paleognaths and neognaths had diverged in Gondwana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cracraft2001_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cracraft2001-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geological analyses have suggested that New Zealand may have been entirely <a href="/wiki/Zealandia_(continent)" title="Zealandia (continent)">under water</a> as recently as 28 Mya, making it impossible for flightless birds to have survived.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The discovery of a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphenodon" title="Sphenodon">Sphenodon</a></i> fossil dating to the Early <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> 19-16 Mya raises question as to whether the island mass was completely submerged. This finding offers further evidence that ancient <i>Sphenodon</i> species lived on some portion of the land mass since it separated from <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> approximately 82 Mya. Evidence of a sea level rise submerging much of New Zealand is generally accepted, but the question is how much was submerged? A <i>Sphenodon</i> species surviving on a remnant part of the island suggests that larger species may have survived as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, this idea is often reliant on willing ignorance and dismissal of early <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasian</a> taxa. The earliest reccorded paleognaths are flying, presumably plesiomorphic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithornithid" title="Lithornithid">lithornithids</a>, found quite possibly as early as the Late Cretaceous in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palaeogene_Fossil_Birds_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeogene_Fossil_Birds-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escholarship.org_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-escholarship.org-9">[9]</a></sup> while some of the earliest flightless ratites occur in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[29]</a></sup> The vicariance hypothesis relies on the assumption southern landmasses were more relevant to ratite evolution than the northern ones, often to irrational extremes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tertiary_radiation_hypothesis.5Ba.5D">Tertiary radiation hypothesis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tertiary_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-tertiary-27">[a]</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tertiary radiation hypothesis[a]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Feduccia (1995) emphasized the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">extinction event</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cretaceous-Paleogene_boundary" title="Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary">Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary</a> as the probable engine of diversification in the Neornithes, picturing only one or very few lineages of birds surviving the end of the Cretaceous. He also noted that birds around the world had developed ratite-like anatomies when they became flightless, and saw the affinities of modern ratites, especially kiwis, as ambiguous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feduccia1995_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feduccia1995-32">[31]</a></sup> In this emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>, rather than <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period, as the time of basal divergences between neornithines, he follows Olson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olson1989_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olson1989-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Houde demonstrated that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithornithiformes" title="Lithornithiformes">Lithornithiformes</a>, a group of flying birds that were common in the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> of the northern hemisphere, were also paleognaths. He argues that the lithornithiform bird <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleotis" title="Paleotis">Paleotis</a></i>, known from fossils in Denmark (northern hemisphere), shared unique anatomical features of the skull that make it a member of the same order as the ostriches. He also argued that the kiwis should not have reached New Zealand, which moved away from the mainland in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a>, if their ancestor was flightless; this claim at least has been vindicated by the discovery of the possibly volant <i><a href="/wiki/Proapteryx" title="Proapteryx">Proapteryx</a></i>. He therefore deduced that lithornithiform ancestors could have reached the southern continents some 30 to 40 million years ago, and evolved flightless forms which are today's ratites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houde1986_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houde1986-34">[33]</a></sup> This hypothesis is contradicted by some later molecular studies (Cooper 1997), but supported by others (Harshman et al. 2008) (see below).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palaeognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The human lineage evolved in Africa in <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatry</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostriches</a>. After <i><a href="/wiki/Homo" title="Homo">Homo</a></i> appeared and left Africa for other continents, they continued to encounter ostriches in <a href="/wiki/Arabian_ostrich" title="Arabian ostrich">Arabia</a> and much of southern and central <a href="/wiki/Asian_ostrich" title="Asian ostrich">Asia</a>. No contact was made with other palaeognath genera until the Aborigines populated New Guinea and Australia. Subsequently, Paleo-Indians encountered tinamous and rheas in Central and South America, Austronesian settlers encountered and exterminated the elephant birds of Madagascar, and the Maori did likewise to the moa of New Zealand. The giant ratites of Madagascar and New Zealand had evolved with little or no exposure to mammalian predators, and were unable to cope with predation by humans; many other oceanic species met the same fate (as apparently had the Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dromornithid" title="Dromornithid">dromornithids</a> earlier). Worldwide, most giant birds became extinct by the end of the 18th century and most surviving species are now <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a>.</p>
<p>Today, ratites such as the <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a> are farmed and sometimes even kept as pets. Ratites play a large role in human culture; they are farmed, eaten, raced, protected, and kept in zoos.</p>
