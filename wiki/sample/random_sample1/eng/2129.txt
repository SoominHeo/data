<p><span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Celestial_coordinate_system" title="Celestial coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <img alt="Sky map" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="128" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Celestia.png/20px-Celestia.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Celestia.png/30px-Celestia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Celestia.png/40px-Celestia.png 2x" title="Sky map" width="20"/> <a class="external text" href="http://www.wikisky.org/?ra=17.975950833333&amp;de=66.6332&amp;zoom=5&amp;show_grid=1&amp;show_constellation_lines=1&amp;show_constellation_boundaries=1&amp;show_const_names=1&amp;show_galaxies=1&amp;img_source=IMG_all" rel="nofollow">17<sup>h</sup> 58<sup>m</sup> 33.423<sup>s</sup>, +66° 37′ 59.52″</a></span></p>
<p>The <b>Cat's Eye Nebula</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/New_General_Catalogue" title="New General Catalogue">NGC</a> 6543</b>, is a relatively bright <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> in the northern constellation of <a href="/wiki/Draco_(constellation)" title="Draco (constellation)">Draco</a>, discovered by <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> on February 15, 1786. It was the first planetary nebula whose <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectrum</a> was investigated by the English <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Huggins" title="William Huggins">William Huggins</a>, demonstrating that planetary nebulae were gaseous and not stellar in nature. Structurally, the object has had high-resolution images by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> revealing knots, jets, bubbles and complex arcs, being illuminated by the central hot <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula nucleus</a> (PNN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaw_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-shaw-5">[3]</a></sup> It is a well-studied object that has been observed from <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> to <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_Spectrum" title="Electromagnetic Spectrum">wavelengths</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_information">General information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NGC 6543 is a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern</a> <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> deep-sky object and is near the apparent position of the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_pole" title="Ecliptic pole">North Ecliptic Pole</a>. It has the combined <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> of 8.1, with high <a href="/wiki/Surface_brightness" title="Surface brightness">surface brightness</a>. Its small bright inner nebula subtends an average of 16.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcsec</a>, with the outer prominent condensations about 25 arcsec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reedetal1999-3_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reedetal1999-3-6">[4]</a></sup> Deep images reveal an extended <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halo</a> about 300 arcsec or 5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminutes" title="Arcminutes">arcmin</a> across,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick2001-1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balick2001-1-7">[5]</a></sup> that was once ejected by the central <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">progenitor</a> star during its <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> phase.</p>
<p>Observations show the bright nebulosity has temperatures between <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003700000000000000♠</span>7000</span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003900000000000000♠</span>9000 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span>, whose densities average of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003500000000000000♠</span>5000</span> particles per cubic centimetre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson1-8">[6]</a></sup> Its outer halo has the higher temperature around <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004150000000000000♠</span>15<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> K</span>, but is of much lower density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson2-9">[7]</a></sup> Velocity of the fast <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a> is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006190000000000000♠</span>1900 km/s</span>, where <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic analysis</a> shows the current rate of mass loss averages <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6993320000000000000♠</span>3.2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−7</sup></span> solar masses per year, equivalent to twenty trillion <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> per second (20 Eg/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson1-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1280" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg/220px-Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg/330px-Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg/440px-Cat%27s_Eye_Halo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>Surface temperature for the central PNN is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004800000000000000♠</span>80<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> K</span>, being <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004100000000000000♠</span>10<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> times as luminous as the sun. <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">Stellar classification</a> is O7 + <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" title="Wolf-Rayet star">[WR]</a>–type star<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson1-8">[6]</a></sup> Calculations suggest the PNN is over one <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a>, from a theoretical initial 5 solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> The central Wolf-Rayet star has a radius of 0.65 <i>R</i><sub>☉</sub> (452,000 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> The Cat's Eye Nebula, given in some sources, lies about three thousand light-years from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cat's Eye was the first planetary nebula to be observed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscope</a> by <a href="/wiki/William_Huggins" title="William Huggins">William Huggins</a> on August 29, 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-13">[11]</a></sup> Huggins' observations revealed that the nebula's spectrum was non-continuous and made of a few bright emission lines, first indication that planetary nebulae consist of tenuous ionised gas. Spectroscopic observations at these wavelengths are used in abundance determinations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson3-14">[12]</a></sup> while images at these wavelengths have been used to reveal the intricate structure of the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick1987-1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balick1987-1-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_observations">Infrared observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infrared observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Observations of NGC 6543 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">far-infrared</a> wavelengths (about 60 μm) reveal the presence of <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_cloud" title="Interstellar cloud">stellar dust</a> at low temperatures. The dust is believed to have formed during the last phases of the progenitor star's life. It absorbs light from the central star and re-radiates it at <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> wavelengths. The spectrum of the infrared dust emission implies that the dust temperature is about 85 K, while the mass of the dust is estimated at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996640000000000000♠</span>6.4<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−4</sup></span> solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klaas_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klaas-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Infrared emission also reveals the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_(physics)" title="Ion (physics)">un-ionised</a> material such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. In many planetary nebulae, molecular emission is greatest at larger distances from the star, where more material is un-ionised, but molecular hydrogen emission in NGC 6543 seems to be bright at the inner edge of its outer halo. This may be due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_waves" title="Shock waves">shock waves</a> exciting the H<sub>2</sub> as ejecta moving at different speeds collide. The overall appearance of the Cat's Eye Nebula in infrared (wavelengths 2–8 μm) is similar in visible light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hora_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hora-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_and_ultraviolet_observations">Optical and ultraviolet observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Optical and ultraviolet observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hubble Space Telescope image produced here is in false colour, designed to highlight regions of high and low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_(physics)" title="Ion (physics)">ionisation</a>. Three images were taken, in filters isolating the light emitted by singly ionised <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> at 656.3 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>, singly ionised <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> at 658.4 nm and doubly ionised <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> at 500.7 nm. The images were combined as red, green and blue channels respectively, although their true colours are red, red and green. The image reveals two "caps" of less ionised material at the edge of the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson4-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray_observations">X-ray observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: X-ray observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="350" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg/220px-Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg/330px-Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula.X-ray_image.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
X-ray image of Nebula.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2001, observations at <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> wavelengths by the <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra X-ray Observatory</a> revealed the presence of extremely hot gas within NGC 6543 with the temperature of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006170000000000000♠</span>1.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>6</sup> K</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chu_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chu-19">[17]</a></sup> It is thought that the very hot gas results from the violent interaction of a fast stellar wind with material previously ejected. This interaction has hollowed out the inner bubble of the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick1987-1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balick1987-1-15">[13]</a></sup> Chandra observations have also revealed a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/point_source" title="wikt:point source">point source</a> at the position of the central star. The spectrum of this source extends to the hard part of the X-ray spectrum, to 0.5–<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6984160217648700000♠</span>1.0 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">keV</a></span>. A star with the <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photospheric</a> temperature of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005100000000000000♠</span>100<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> K</span> would not be expected to emit strongly in hard X-rays, and so their presence is something of a mystery. It may suggest the presence of a high temperature <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerrero_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guerrero-20">[18]</a></sup> The hard X-ray data remain intriguing more than ten years later: the Cat's Eye was included in a 2012 Chandra survey of 21 central stars of planetary nebulae (CSPNe) in the solar neighborhood, which found: "All but one of the X-ray point sources detected at CSPNe display X-ray spectra that are harder than expected from hot (~<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005100000000000000♠</span>100<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> K</span>) central star photospheres, possibly indicating a high frequency of binary companions to CSPNe. Other potential explanations include self-shocking winds or PN mass fallback." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kastner_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kastner-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance">Distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Planetary nebulae distances like NGC 6543 are generally very inaccurate and not well known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reedetal1999-1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reedetal1999-1-22">[20]</a></sup> Some recent Hubble Space Telescope observations of NGC 6543 taken several years apart determine its distance from the angular expansion rate of 3.457 milliarcseconds per year. Assuming a line of sight expansion velocity of 16.4 km·s<sup>−1</sup>, this implies that NGC 6543's distance is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100100000000000♠</span>1001<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>269</span> <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7019300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>19</sup> m</span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003330000000000000♠</span>3300</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-years" title="Light-years">light-years</a>) away from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reedetal1999-2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reedetal1999-2-23">[21]</a></sup> Several other distance references, like what is quoted in <a href="/wiki/SIMBAD" title="SIMBAD">SIMBAD</a> in 2014 Stanghellini, L., et al. (2008) suggest the distance is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003162300000000000♠</span>1623</span> parsecs (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003530000000000000♠</span>5300</span> light-years).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age">Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The angular expansion of the nebula can also be used to estimate its age. If it has been expanding at a constant rate of 10 milliarcseconds a year, then it would take <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010315576000000000♠</span>1000<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>260 years</span> to reach a diameter of 20 arcseconds. This may be an upper limit to the age, because ejected material will be slowed when it encounters material ejected from the star at earlier stages of its evolution, and the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reedetal1999-2_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reedetal1999-2-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Catseye-big.jpg"><img alt="Blue-green diffuse disk with complex circular structure in its center. The disk is crossed by s-shaped brown curve." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1417" data-file-width="1417" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Catseye-big.jpg/220px-Catseye-big.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Catseye-big.jpg/330px-Catseye-big.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Catseye-big.jpg/440px-Catseye-big.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catseye-big.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Image of NGC 6543 processed to reveal the concentric rings surrounding the inner core. Also visible are the linear structures, possibly caused by precessing jets from a binary central star system.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like most astronomical objects, NGC 6543 consists mostly of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, with heavier elements present in small quantities. The exact composition may be determined by spectroscopic studies. Abundances are generally expressed relative to hydrogen, the most abundant element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson2-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different studies generally find varying values for elemental abundances. This is often because <a href="/wiki/Spectrograph" title="Spectrograph">spectrographs</a> attached to telescopes do not collect all the light from objects being observed, instead gathering light from a slit or small <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>. Therefore, different observations may sample different parts of the nebula.</p>
<p>However, results for NGC 6543 broadly agree that, relative to hydrogen, the helium abundance is about 0.12, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> abundances are both about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−4</sup></span>, and the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> abundance is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996700000000000000♠</span>7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−4</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson3-14">[12]</a></sup> These are fairly typical abundances for planetary nebulae, with the carbon, nitrogen and oxygen abundances all larger than the values found for the sun, due to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a> enriching the star's atmosphere in heavy elements before it is ejected as a planetary nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deep spectroscopic analysis of NGC 6543 may indicate that the nebula contains a small amount of material which is highly enriched in heavy elements; this is discussed below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesson3_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson3-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinematics_and_morphology">Kinematics and morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kinematics and morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cat's Eye Nebula is structurally a very complex nebula, and the mechanism or mechanisms that have given rise to its complicated morphology are not well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick1987-1_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Balick1987-1-15">[13]</a></sup> The central bright part of the nebular consists of the inner elongated bubble (inner ellipse) filled with hot gas. It in turn is nested into a pair of larger spherical bubbles conjoined together along their waist. The waist is observed as the second larger ellipse lying perpendicular to the bubble with hot gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reedetal1999-4_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reedetal1999-4-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The structure of the bright portion of the nebula is primarily caused by the interaction of a fast <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a> being emitted by the central PNN with the visible material ejected during the formation of the nebula. This interaction causes the emission of X-rays discussed above. The stellar wind, blowing with the velocity as high as <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006190000000000000♠</span>1900 km/s</span>, has 'hollowed out' the inner bubble of the nebula, and appears to have burst the bubble at both ends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick1987-1_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Balick1987-1-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also suspected that the central WR:+O7 spectral class PNN star, HD 1064963 / BD +66 1066 / PPM 20679 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simbad_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-simbad-1">[1]</a></sup> of the nebula may be generated by a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simbad_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-simbad-1">[1]</a></sup> The existence of an <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a> caused by mass transfer between the two components of the system may give rise to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_jet" title="Polar jet">polar jets</a>, which would interact with previously ejected material. Over time, the direction of the polar jets would vary due to <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outside the bright inner portion of the nebula, there are a series of concentric rings, thought to have been ejected before the formation of the planetary nebula, while the star was on the <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hertzsprung-Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung-Russell diagram">Hertzsprung-Russell diagram</a>. These rings are very evenly spaced, suggesting that the mechanism responsible for their formation ejected them at very regular intervals and at very similar speeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick2001-1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balick2001-1-7">[5]</a></sup> The total mass of the rings is about 0.1 solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick2001-2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balick2001-2-27">[25]</a></sup> The pulsations that formed the rings probably started 15,000 years ago and ceased about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100000000000000♠</span>1000</span> years ago, when the formation of the bright central part began (see above).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick2001-3_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balick2001-3-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further out, a large faint halo extends to large distances from the star. The halo again predates the formation of the main nebula. The mass of the halo is estimated as 0.26–0.92 solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balick2001-2_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balick2001-2-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
