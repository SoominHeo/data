<p>A <b>cat organ</b> or <b>cat piano</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Katzenorgel</i> or <i>Katzenklavier</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>orgue à chats</i> or <i>piano à chats/pianno chats</i></span>) is a conjectural <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a> which consists of a line of <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> fixed in place with their tails stretched out underneath a <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboard</a> so that they <a href="/wiki/Cat_communication" title="Cat communication">cry out</a> in pain when a key is pressed. The cats would be arranged according to the natural <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">tone</a> of their voices.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat_organ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no official record of a cat organ actually being built; rather it is described in literature as a bizarre concept. "The details of the cat organ present it clearly as an instrument cat lovers might wish was a fictional horror."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betancourt_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Betancourt-1">[1]</a></sup> The instrument is used in stories which criticize the cruelty of royalty while the <a href="/wiki/Piganino" title="Piganino">piganino</a>, a similar instrument using pigs, has been used to criticize the poor.</p>
<p>This instrument was described by the French writer <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Weckerlin" title="Jean-Baptiste Weckerlin">Jean-Baptiste Weckerlin</a> in his book <i>Musiciana, extraits d’ouvrages rare ou bizarre</i> (Musiciana, descriptions of rare or bizarre inventions):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When the King of Spain <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Felipe II</a> was in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> in 1549 visiting his father the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Charles_V" title="Emperor Charles V">Emperor Charles V</a>, each saw the other rejoicing at the sight of a completely singular procession. At the head marched an enormous bull whose horns were burning, between which there was also a small devil. Behind the bull a young boy sewn into a bear skin rode on a horse whose ears and tail were cut off. Then came the archangel Saint Michael in bright clothing, and carrying a balance in his hand.</p>
<p>The most curious was on a chariot that carried the most singular music that can be imagined. It held a bear that played the organ; instead of pipes, there were sixteen cat heads each with its body confined; the tails were sticking out and were held to be played as the strings on a piano, if a key was pressed on the keyboard, the corresponding tail would be pulled hard, and it would produce each time a lamentable <a href="/wiki/Meow" title="Meow">meow</a>. The historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Christoval_Calvete&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Christoval Calvete (page does not exist)">Juan Christoval Calvete</a>, noted the cats were arranged properly to produce a succession of notes from the octave... (<a href="/wiki/Chromatic_scale" title="Chromatic scale">chromatically</a>, I think).</p>
<p>This abominable orchestra arranged itself inside a theatre where monkeys, wolves, deer and other animals danced to the sounds of this infernal music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The instrument was described by German physician <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christian_Reil" title="Johann Christian Reil">Johann Christian Reil</a> (1759–1813) for the purpose of treating patients who had lost the ability to focus their attention. Reil believed that if they were forced to see and listen to this instrument, it would inevitably capture their attention and they would be cured: "A fugue played on this instrument--when the ill person is so placed that he cannot miss the expression on their faces and the play of these animals--must bring Lot's wife herself from her fixed state into conscious awareness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The instrument was first described by <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">Athanasius Kircher</a> in his 1650 work <i>Musurgia Universalis</i>, though the lack of an image may have left doubt in the minds of some writers. (<i>The New York Times</i>, for example, has carried an article claiming he described the instrument,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1-5">[5]</a></sup> and another stating he did not describe it in <i>Musurgia Universalis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-6">[6]</a></sup>). His description appears in Book 6, Part 4, Chapter 1, under the heading "Corollaria," (emphasis added):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Constructum non ita pridem ad melancholiam magni cuiusdam Pincipis depellendam abinsigni ingeniosoque Histrione tale quodpiam instrumentum. Feles vivas accepit omnes differentis magnitudinis, quas cistae cuidam huic negotio dedita opera fabricatae ita inclusit ut caudae perforamina extentae, certis quibusdam cana, libus insererentur affixae, hisce subdidit palmulas subtilissimis ac uleis loco malleolorum instructas; <b>Feles vero iuxta differentem magnitudinem tonatim ita disposuit</b>; ut singulae palmulae singulis responderent felium caudis, <b>instrumentumque ad relaxationem Principis praeparatum oportuno loco condidit, quod deinde pulsatum eam harmonia<i>m</i> redditit, quale<i>m</i> Feliu<i>m</i> voces reddere possunt</b>. Nam palmulae digitis Organoe di depressae aculeis suis du<i>m</i> caudas pu<i>n</i>gunt cattoru<i>m</i> hi in rabiem acti miserabili voce, nunc gravem, modò acutam intonantes, eam ex felium vocibus compositam reddiderunt harmoniam, quae <i>et</i> moveret homines ad risum, <i>et</i> vel sorices ipsos ad saltum concitare posset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The citation is noted by Kircher's student <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Schott" title="Gaspar Schott">Gaspar Schott</a> in <i>Magia Naturalis naturae et artis,</i> Part 2, Book 6, Pragmatia 2, titled "Felium Musicam exhibere [Cat Concert]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_citations_and_reconstructions">Modern citations and reconstructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cat_organ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern citations and reconstructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Betancourt" title="Michael Betancourt">Michael Betancourt</a> compares the <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> of cats' meows used in <a href="/wiki/Jingle_Cats" title="Jingle Cats">Jingle Cats</a>' albums, <i>Meowy Christmas</i> (1993) and <i>Here Comes Santa Claws</i> (1994), to the cat organ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betancourt_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Betancourt-1">[1]</a></sup> in that both require cats, but diminish each performer's importance.</p>
<p>Kircher notes that the instrument can be used to reduce the melancholy of princes by moving them to laughter, almost exactly the situation that occurred in 2010 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a> was greatly amused by a performance of the tune "<a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow" title="Over the Rainbow">Over the Rainbow</a>" on an instrument recreated using <a href="/wiki/Squeaky_toy" title="Squeaky toy">squeaky toy</a> cats by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Dagg" title="Henry Dagg">Henry Dagg</a> for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_party" title="Garden party">garden party</a> held at <a href="/wiki/Clarence_House" title="Clarence House">Clarence House</a> supporting Charles's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Start_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Start Initiative (page does not exist)">Start</a> initiative for sustainable living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/The_People%27s_Republic_of_Animation" title="The People's Republic of Animation">The People's Republic of Animation</a>, a professional animation studio, released an animation titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cat_Piano" title="The Cat Piano">The Cat Piano</a></i>. This work tells the tale of a city of cats whose musicians are kidnapped by a human in order to make a cat piano. This short film has received several awards, as well as nominations for awards. The Academy announced that it was shortlisted for an Oscar for Best Animated Short. However, it was not nominated.</p>

Live cats of all sizes where placed in a box built for this purpose, containing sharp points near the tails so that they struck cats of different sizes, producing different pitches when different keys where pressed.
