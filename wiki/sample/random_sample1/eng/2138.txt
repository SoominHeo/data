<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Proper morphism</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>, a <b>proper morphism</b> between <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics)" title="Scheme (mathematics)">schemes</a> is an analog of a <a href="/wiki/Proper_map" title="Proper map">proper map</a> between <a href="/wiki/Complex_analytic_space" title="Complex analytic space">complex analytic spaces</a>.</p>
<p>Some authors call a proper <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_variety" title="Algebraic variety">variety</a> over a <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a> <i>k</i> a <a href="/wiki/Complete_variety" title="Complete variety">complete variety</a>. For example, every <a href="/wiki/Projective_variety" title="Projective variety">projective variety</a> over a field <i>k</i> is proper over <i>k</i>. A scheme <i>X</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphism_of_finite_type" title="Morphism of finite type">finite type</a> over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_numbers" title="Complex numbers">complex numbers</a> (for example, a variety) is proper over <b>C</b> if and only if the space <i>X</i>(<b>C</b>) of complex points with the classical (Euclidean) topology is <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hausdorff_space" title="Hausdorff space">Hausdorff</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Closed_immersion" title="Closed immersion">closed immersion</a> is proper. A morphism is <a href="/wiki/Finite_morphism" title="Finite morphism">finite</a> if and only if it is proper and <a href="/wiki/Quasi-finite_morphism" title="Quasi-finite morphism">quasi-finite</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proper_morphism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Morphism_of_schemes" title="Morphism of schemes">morphism</a> <i>f</i>: <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> of schemes is called <b>universally closed</b> if for every scheme <i>Z</i> with a morphism <i>Z</i> → <i>Y</i>, the projection from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_product" title="Fiber product">fiber product</a></p>

<p>is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_map" title="Closed map">closed map</a> of the underlying <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a>. A morphism of schemes is called <b>proper</b> if it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separated_morphism" title="Separated morphism">separated</a>, of finite type, and universally closed ([EGA] II, 5.4.1 <a class="external autonumber" href="http://modular.fas.harvard.edu/scans/papers/grothendieck/PMIHES_1961__8__5_0.pdf" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>). One also says that <i>X</i> is proper over <i>Y</i>. In particular, a variety <i>X</i> over a field <i>k</i> is said to be proper over <i>k</i> if the morphism <i>X</i> → Spec(<i>k</i>) is proper.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proper_morphism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For any natural number <i>n</i>, <a href="/wiki/Projective_space" title="Projective space">projective space</a> <b>P</b><sup><i>n</i></sup> over a <a href="/wiki/Commutative_ring" title="Commutative ring">commutative ring</a> <i>R</i> is proper over <i>R</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projective_morphism" title="Projective morphism">Projective morphisms</a> are proper, but not all proper morphisms are projective. For example, there is a <a href="/wiki/Smooth_scheme" title="Smooth scheme">smooth</a> proper complex variety of dimension 3 which is not projective over <b>C</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Affine_variety" title="Affine variety">Affine varieties</a> of positive dimension over a field <i>k</i> are never proper over <i>k</i>. More generally, a proper affine morphism of schemes must be finite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> For example, it is not hard to see that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affine_line" title="Affine line">affine line</a> <i>A</i><sup>1</sup> over a field <i>k</i> is not proper over <i>k</i>, because the morphism <i>A</i><sup>1</sup> → Spec(<i>k</i>) is not universally closed. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_characterizations_of_proper_morphisms">Properties and characterizations of proper morphisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proper_morphism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties and characterizations of proper morphisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the following, let <i>f</i>: <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> be a morphism of schemes.</p>
<ul>
<li>The composition of two proper morphisms is proper.</li>
<li>Any <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck%27s_relative_point_of_view" title="Grothendieck's relative point of view">base change</a> of a proper morphism <i>f</i>: <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> is proper. That is, if <i>g</i>: Z → <i>Y</i> is any morphism of schemes, then the resulting morphism <i>X</i> ×<sub><i>Y</i></sub> <i>Z</i> → <i>Z</i> is proper.</li>
<li>Properness is a <a href="/wiki/Local_property" title="Local property">local property</a> on the base (in the Zariski topology). That is, if <i>Y</i> is covered by some open subschemes <i>Y<sub>i</sub></i> and the restriction of <i>f</i> to all <i>f<sup>−1</sup>(Y<sub>i</sub>)</i> is proper, then so is <i>f</i>.</li>
<li>More strongly, properness is local on the base in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fpqc_topology" title="Fpqc topology">fpqc topology</a>. For example, if <i>X</i> is a scheme over a field <i>k</i> and <i>E</i> is a field extension of <i>k</i>, then <i>X</i> is proper over <i>k</i> if and only if the base change <i>X</i><sub><i>E</i></sub> is proper over <i>E</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Closed_immersion" title="Closed immersion">Closed immersions</a> are proper.</li>
<li>More generally, finite morphisms are proper. This is a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Going_up_and_going_down" title="Going up and going down">going up</a> theorem.</li>
<li>By <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Deligne" title="Pierre Deligne">Deligne</a>, a morphism of schemes is finite if and only if it is proper and quasi-finite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This had been shown by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck" title="Alexander Grothendieck">Grothendieck</a> if the morphism <i>f</i>: <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> is <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_algebraic_geometry#finite_presentation" title="Glossary of algebraic geometry">locally of finite presentation</a>, which follows from the other assumptions if <i>Y</i> is <a href="/wiki/Noetherian_scheme" title="Noetherian scheme">noetherian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>For <i>X</i> proper over a scheme <i>S</i>, and <i>Y</i> separated over <i>S</i>, the image of any morphism <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> over <i>S</i> is a closed subset of <i>Y</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This is analogous to the theorem in topology that the image of a continuous map from a compact space to a Hausdorff space is a closed subset.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Stein_factorization" title="Stein factorization">Stein factorization</a> theorem states that any proper morphism to a locally noetherian scheme can be factored as <i>X</i> → <i>Z</i> → <i>Y</i>, where <i>X</i> → <i>Z</i> is proper, surjective, and has geometrically connected fibers, and <i>Z</i> → <i>Y</i> is finite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chow%27s_lemma" title="Chow's lemma">Chow's lemma</a> says that proper morphisms are closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projective_morphism" title="Projective morphism">projective morphisms</a>. One version is: if <i>X</i> is proper over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasi-compact" title="Quasi-compact">quasi-compact</a> scheme <i>Y</i> and <i>X</i> has only finitely many irreducible components (which is automatic for <i>Y</i> noetherian), then there is a projective surjective morphism <i>g</i>: <i>W</i> → <i>X</i> such that <i>W</i> is projective over <i>Y</i>. Moreover, one can arrange that <i>g</i> is an isomorphism over a dense open subset <i>U</i> of <i>X</i>, and that <i>g</i><sup>−1</sup>(<i>U</i>) is dense in <i>W</i>. One can also arrange that <i>W</i> is integral if <i>X</i> is integral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nagata%27s_compactification_theorem" title="Nagata's compactification theorem">Nagata's compactification theorem</a>, as generalized by Deligne, says that a separated morphism of finite type between quasi-compact and <a href="/wiki/Quasi-separated_morphism" title="Quasi-separated morphism">quasi-separated</a> schemes factors as an open immersion followed by a proper morphism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Proper morphisms between locally noetherian schemes preserve coherent sheaves, in the sense that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_direct_image" title="Higher direct image">higher direct images</a> <i>R<sup>i</sup>f</i><sub>∗</sub>(<i>F</i>) (in particular the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_image" title="Direct image">direct image</a> <i>f</i><sub>∗</sub>(<i>F</i>)) of a <a href="/wiki/Coherent_sheaf" title="Coherent sheaf">coherent sheaf</a> <i>F</i> are coherent (EGA III, 3.2.1). (Analogously, for a proper map between complex analytic spaces, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Grauert" title="Hans Grauert">Grauert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Remmert" title="Reinhold Remmert">Remmert</a> showed that the higher direct images preserve coherent analytic sheaves.) As a very special case: the ring of regular functions on a proper scheme <i>X</i> over a field <i>k</i> has finite dimension as a <i>k</i>-vector space. By contrast, the ring of regular functions on the affine line over <i>k</i> is the polynomial ring <i>k</i>[<i>x</i>], which does not have finite dimension as a <i>k</i>-vector space.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Valuative_criterion_of_properness">Valuative criterion of properness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proper_morphism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Valuative criterion of properness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>There is a very intuitive criterion for properness which goes back to <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chevalley" title="Claude Chevalley">Chevalley</a>. It is commonly called the <b>valuative criterion of properness</b>. Let <i>f</i>: <i>X</i> → <i>Y</i> be a morphism of finite type of <a href="/wiki/Noetherian_scheme" title="Noetherian scheme">noetherian schemes</a>. Then <i>f</i> is proper if and only if for all <a href="/wiki/Discrete_valuation_ring" title="Discrete valuation ring">discrete valuation rings</a> <i>R</i> with <a href="/wiki/Field_of_fractions" title="Field of fractions">fraction field</a> <i>K</i> and for any <i>K</i>-valued point <i>x</i> ∈ <i>X</i>(<i>K</i>) that maps to a point <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>) that is defined over <i>R</i>, there is a unique lift of <i>x</i> to <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

(EGA II, 7.3.8). Noting that <i>Spec K</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Generic_point" title="Generic point">generic point</a> of <i>Spec R</i> and discrete valuation rings are precisely the <a href="/wiki/Regular_ring" title="Regular ring">regular</a> <a href="/wiki/Local_ring" title="Local ring">local</a> one-dimensional rings, one may rephrase the criterion: given a regular curve on <i>Y</i> (corresponding to the morphism <i>s</i>: Spec <i>R</i> → <i>Y</i>) and given a lift of the generic point of this curve to <i>X</i>, <i>f</i> is proper if and only if there is exactly one way to complete the curve.</p>

<p>For example, given the valuative criterion, it becomes easy to check that projective space <b>P</b><sup><i>n</i></sup> is proper over a field (or even over <b>Z</b>).
