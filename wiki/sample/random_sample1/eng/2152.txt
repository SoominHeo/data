<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Solid rocket booster</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">Solid rocket</a> <a href="/wiki/Booster_(rocketry)" title="Booster (rocketry)">boosters</a></b> (<b>SRBs</b>) are large solid propellant motors used to provide <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> in spacecraft launches from initial launch through the first ascent stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many launch vehicles, including the <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">Ariane 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, use SRBs to give launch vehicles much of the thrust required to escape Earth's gravitational pull. The NASA Space Shuttle used two <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Solid_Rocket_Booster" title="Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster">Space Shuttle SRBs</a>, which were the largest solid propellant motors ever built and the first designed for recovery and reuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The propellant for each solid rocket motor on the Space Shuttle weighed approximately 500,000 kilograms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science_ksc_nasa_gov-srb_html_srb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-science_ksc_nasa_gov-srb_html_srb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-fuel_rocket" title="Liquid-fuel rocket">liquid propellant rockets</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_rocket" title="Solid rocket">solid-propellant</a> SRBs are capable of providing large amounts of thrust with a relatively simple design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They provide greater thrust without significant refrigeration and insulation requirements. Adding detachable SRBs to a vehicle also powered by liquid-propelled rockets (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staging_(rocketry)" title="Staging (rocketry)">staging</a>) reduces the amount of liquid propellant needed and lowers the launch vehicle mass. Solid boosters are usually cheaper to design, test, and produce in the long run compared to the equivalent liquid propellant boosters. Reusability of components across multiple flights, as in the Shuttle assembly, also decreases hardware costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, SRB costs on a per-flight basis tend to be equivalent to those of their liquid counterparts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One example of increased performance provided by SRBs is the <a href="/wiki/Ariane_4" title="Ariane 4">Ariane 4</a> rocket. The basic 40 model with no additional boosters is capable of lifting a 2,175 kilogram payload to <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">Geostationary transfer orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The 44P model with 4 solid boosters has a payload of 3,465 kg to the same orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solid propellant boosters are not controllable and must generally burn until exhaustion after ignition, unlike liquid propellant or <a href="/wiki/Cold_gas_thruster" title="Cold gas thruster">cold-gas</a> propulsion systems. However, launch abort systems and <a href="/wiki/Range_safety" title="Range safety">range safety</a> destruct systems can attempt to cut off propellant flow by using <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">shaped charges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Estimates for SRB failure rates range from 1 in 1,000 to 1 in 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> SRB assemblies can fail suddenly and catastrophically. Nozzle blocking or deformation can lead to overpressure or a reduction in thrust, while defects in the booster's casing or stage couplings can cause the assembly to break apart by increasing aerodynamic stresses. Additional failure modes include bore choking and combustion instability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Failure of an <a href="/wiki/O-ring" title="O-ring">O-ring</a> seal on the Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i>'s right solid rocket booster led to its <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster">disintegration</a> shortly after liftoff.</p>
<p>Solid rocket motors also present a significant handling risk on the ground, as a fully fueled booster carries a risk of accidental detonation. Such an accident occurred on August 22, 2003, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_rocket_explosion" title="Brazilian rocket explosion">killing 21 technicians</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centro_de_Lan%C3%A7amento_de_Alc%C3%A2ntara" title="Centro de Lançamento de Alcântara">VLS rocket launch pad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Liquid rocket boosters generally cannot be moved after preparation is completed.</p>
