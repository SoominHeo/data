<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Solid nitrogen</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Solid nitrogen</b> is the solid form of the element <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. It is an important component of the surfaces of <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and outer moons of the Solar System such as <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triton_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Triton-2">[2]</a></sup> Under low or moderate pressure solid nitrogen contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen" title="Dinitrogen">dinitrogen</a> molecules held together by <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-yamas-3">[3]</a></sup> Non-molecular forms of solid nitrogen produced by extreme pressures, have a higher energy density than any other non-nuclear material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solid nitrogen was first made in 1884, by first <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquefying hydrogen</a> with evaporating <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a>, and then allowing the liquid hydrogen to freeze the nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Karol_Olszewski" title="Karol Olszewski">Karol Olszewski</a> achieved a world record lowest temperature by evaporating vapour from solid nitrogen getting down to 48 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Solid nitrogen is normally made in a laboratory by evaporating liquid nitrogen in a vacuum. The solid produced is porous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulk_properties">Bulk properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bulk properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid nitrogen has several properties relevant to its formation of rocks in the outer Solar System. Even at the low temperatures of solid nitrogen it is fairly volatile and can <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublime</a> to form an atmosphere, or condense back into nitrogen frost. At 58 K the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultimate_compressive_strength" title="Ultimate compressive strength">ultimate compressive strength</a> is 0.24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPa" title="MPa">MPa</a>. Strength increases as temperature lowers becoming 0.54 MPa at 40.6 K. Elastic modulus varies from 161 to 225 MPa over the same range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Compared to other materials, solid nitrogen loses <a href="/wiki/Cohesion_(chemistry)" title="Cohesion (chemistry)">cohesion</a> at low pressures and flows in the form of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> when amassed. Yet its density is higher than that of water ice, so the forces of <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> will naturally transport blocks of water ice towards the surface. This effect has been most clearly observed on Pluto (by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i> space probe in 2015), where water ice makes up a major part of the surface layers as icebergs on top of nitrogen ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pluto_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pluto-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solid nitrogen mixes with solid <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solid methane (page does not exist)">solid methane</a> on the surface of Pluto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pluto_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pluto-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of solid nitrogen is 0.7 W m<sup>−1</sup> K<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Thermal conductivity varies with temperature and the relation is given by k=180.2×T<sup>0.1041</sup> watts per kelvin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trowb_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trowb-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat" title="Specific heat">Specific heat</a> is given by 926.91×e<sup>0.0093T.</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trowb_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trowb-11">[11]</a></sup> Its appearance at 50 K (<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvins</a>) is transparent, while at 20 K it is white.</p>
<p>Nitrogen frost has a density of 0.25 g cm<sup>−3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sat004_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sat004-12">[12]</a></sup> As a bulk material the crystals are pressed together and density is near that of water. It is temperature dependent and given by ρ = 0.0134T<sup>2</sup> − 0.6981T + 1038.1 kgm<sup>−3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trowb_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Trowb-11">[11]</a></sup> The volume coefficient of expansion is given by 2×10<sup>−6</sup>T<sup>2</sup> − 0.0002T + 0.006 K<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trowb_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Trowb-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_of_refraction" title="Index of refraction">index of refraction</a> at 6328 Å is 1.25 and hardly varies with temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sat004_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sat004-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in solid nitrogen is 1452 m s<sup>−1</sup> at 20 K and 1222 m s<sup>−1</sup> at 44 K. The longitudinal velocity ranges from 1850 m s<sup>−1</sup> at 5 K to 1700 m s<sup>−1</sup> at 35 K. With temperature rise the nitrogen changes phase and the longitudinal velocity drops rapidly over a small temperature range to below 1600 m s<sup>−1</sup> and then it slowly drops to 1400 m s<sup>−1</sup> near the melting point. The transverse velocity is much lower ranging from 900 m s<sup>−1</sup> to 800 m s<sup>−1</sup> over the same temperature range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamas_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-yamas-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bulk_modulus" title="Bulk modulus">bulk modulus</a> of s-N<sub>2</sub> is 2.16 GPa at 20 K, and 1.47 GPa at 44 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamas_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-yamas-3">[3]</a></sup> At temperatures below 30 K solid nitrogen will undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle_failure" title="Brittle failure">brittle failure</a>, particularly if strain is applied quickly. Above this temperature the failure mode is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile_failure" title="Ductile failure">ductile failure</a>. Dropping 10 K makes the solid nitrogen 10 times as stiff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamas_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-yamas-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Melting">Melting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Melting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid nitrogen melts at a higher temperature with increasing ambient pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The slope of the melting point line of the phase diagram is 190 K GPa<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> At 2.8 GPa nitrogen melts at 308 K, at 4 GPa it melts at 368 K, and at 7 GPa it melts at 484 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The melting point increases all the way to 1920 K at a pressure of 50 GPa. Above this pressure the melting point decreases. This is due to a change in the liquid, which becomes denser than the solid at that pressure. The liquid is predicted to become a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>. The melting point drops to 1400 K at 71 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muk007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muk007-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solid nitrogen is slightly soluble in <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>. At 15K there are somewhere between 10<sup>10</sup> and 10<sup>11</sup> molecules of nitrogen per cm<sup>3</sup> of liquid hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> At the boiling point of hydrogen the amount in solution is 10<sup>−8</sup> molar fraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup> At 32.5 K the molar concentration of dissolved N<sub>2</sub> in close to critical H<sub>2</sub> is 7.0×10<sup>−6</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sublimation">Sublimation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sublimation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the pressure is below the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a>, solid nitrogen directly <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimes</a> to gas. The triple point is at 63.14±0.06 K and 0.1255±0.0005 bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fray_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fray-17">[17]</a></sup> The vapour pressure has been measured from 20 K up to the triple point. For α-nitrogen (below 35 K) the logarithm of the pressure is given by 12.40 −807.4 × T<sup>−1</sup> −3926 T<sup>−2</sup> +6.297×10<sup>+ 4</sup>T<sup>−3</sup> −4.633× 10 <sup>+5</sup>T<sup>−4</sup> 1.325× 10<sup>+ 6</sup>T<sup>−5</sup>. For β-nitrogen it is given by 8.514 −458.4T<sup>−1</sup> −19870 T<sup>−2</sup> 4.800 × 10<sup>+ 5</sup>T<sup>−3</sup> −4.524 × 10<sup>+6</sup>T<sup>−4</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fray_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-fray-17">[17]</a></sup> Where the solid is not pure nitrogen, the vapour pressure can be estimated using <a href="/wiki/Raoult%27s_law" title="Raoult's law">Raoult's law</a> in which the pressure is reduced by the molar concentration. This calculation is relevant for the atmosphere of outer solar system bodies, where there could be a 1% contamination with carbon monoxide and methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fray_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-fray-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_structure">Crystal structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crystal structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B2">β</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: β">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several known solid forms of molecular dinitrogen. At ambient pressures there are two solid forms. β-N<sub>2</sub> is a hexagonal close packed structure which exists from 35.6 K up to 63.1 5K at which point it melts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> At 45 K the unit cell has a=4.050 Å and c=6.604 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> At 4125 atmospheres pressure, and 49K the unit cell sizes have shrunk to a=3.861 Å c=6.265 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup> If the pressure is increased the c/a ratio stays the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the β phase, the molecule centres are hexagonal close packed. This means that the c/a ratio is ≈ 1.633 = √8/3. The nitrogen molecules are randomly tipped at an angle of 55° from the c-axis. There is a strong quadrapole-quadrapole interaction between the molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B1">α</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: α">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another phase termed α-N<sub>2</sub> exists below 35.6 K at low pressure and has a cubic structure. The space group is <i>Pa</i>3. At 21 K the unit cell dimension is 5.667 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> Under 3785 bars this reduces to 5.433 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup> At low temperatures the α-phase can be compressed to 3500 atmospheres before it changes (to γ), and as the temperature rises above 20 K, this pressure rises to about 4500 atmospheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nitrogen molecules are located on the body diagonals of the unit cell cube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B3">γ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: γ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tetragonal γ form exists at low temperatures below 44.5 K between about 0.3 GPa and 3 GPa pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The triple point for α/β/γ<sub>2</sub> is at 0.47 GPa and 44.5 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The space group of the γ phase is <i>P</i>4<sub>2</sub>/<i>mnm</i> and its unit cell has lattice constants a=3.957 Å, c=5.109 Å at 20 K and 4000 bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The <sup>15</sup>N isotope converts at a pressure 400 atmospheres lower to the γ form than natural nitrogen, at 20 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the γ form, nitrogen molecules appear to have the shape of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">prolate spheroid</a>, 4.34 Å in the long dimension, and 3.39 Å in the short diameter. The boundary of the molecule appears at an <a href="/wiki/Electron_density" title="Electron density">electron density</a> of 0.0135 eÅ<sup>−3</sup>. The molecules line up in rows end to end diagonally on the ab plane. These rows stack side by side with molecules offset by half their length to form layers in the (001) plane, perpendicular to the c-axis. Layers stack on top of each other each rotated by 90° compared to the plane below. Coordinates of atoms in the unit cell are given by (x,x,0),(-x,-x,0),(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>+x,<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>-x,<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>),(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>-x,<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>+x,<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>) with x=r/a√8 and r=interatomic distance in nitrogen molecule, = 1.10 Å. (unit cell dimension as above a=3.957 Å). The molecules can vibrate up to 10° on the ab plane, and up to 15° in the direction of the c axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schu_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-schu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B4">δ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: δ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>δ-N<sub>2</sub> has a triple point with β and γ Nitrogen at 2.3 GPa and 150 K. δ-N<sub>2</sub> has a cubic structure with space group <i>pm</i>3<i>m</i>. The lattice constant is 1.164 at 300 K but 4.9 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> At room temperature and high pressure δ-nitrogen is ordered in its molecular orientation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vos92_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-vos92-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Above the pressure of 2 GPa there is the lower temperature rhombohedral phase ε-N<sub>2</sub> and above 80 K cubic δ-N<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup> The triple point of δ-N<sub>2</sub>, β-N<sub>2</sub> and liquid is somewhere between 8 and 10 GPa and 555 and 578 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkov04_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkov04-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B5">ε</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: ε">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ε-N<sub>2</sub> is rhombohedral with space group R<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span><i>c</i> is a high pressure form of dinitrogen, stable at 13 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotakoski08_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kotakoski08-20">[20]</a></sup> Cell dimensions are a=8.02 Å, b=8.02 Å, c=11.104 Å, α=β=90°, γ=120°, volume 618.5 Å<sup>3</sup>, Z=24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> ε-nitrogen has disordered orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vos92_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-vos92-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the phase diagram ε-N<sub>2</sub> appears at pressures above 2 GPa at temperatures below 50 K. Below this the γ form is stable. When heated ε-N<sub>2</sub> transforms to δ-N<sub>2</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B6">ζ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: ζ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Above 69 GPa ε-N<sub>2</sub> transforms to an orthorhombic phase designated by ζ-N<sub>2</sub> with a 6% reduction in volume. The space group of ζ-N<sub>2</sub> is <i>P</i>222<sub>1</sub>. The lattice constants are a=4.159 Å, b=2.765 Å, c=5.039 Å with eight atoms per unit cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup> At 80 GPa the distance between nitrogen atoms in the molecules is 0.982 Å, but the closest distance to other nitrogen atoms is 1.93 Å. As the pressure increases to 138 GPa the bond in the molecules actually lengthens to 1.002 Å while intermolecular distances shorten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B8">θ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: θ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A ζ-N<sub>2</sub> phase compressed to 95 GPa and then heated to over 600 K produces a new structure called θ nitrogen which has a uniform translucent appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gon004_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gon004-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B9">ι</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ι">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When ε-N<sub>2</sub> is heated to 750 K at a pressure between 65 and 70 GPa a high temperature phase ι-N<sub>2</sub> forms. This can also be formed from θ nitrogen by decompression and heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gon004_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gon004-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.BC">μ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: μ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the ζ-N<sub>2</sub> phase is compressed at room temperature over 150 GPa an amorphous form is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup> This is designated as the μ-phase. It is a narrow gap semiconductor. The μ-phase has been brought to atmospheric pressure by first cooling it to 100 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plas15_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plas15-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cubic_gauche">Cubic gauche</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cubic gauche">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under pressures higher than 110 GPa and temperatures around 2000 K nitrogen forms a network solid, bound by single <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> in what is called a cubic-gauche structure, abbreviated as cg-N. This substance is very stiff with a <a href="/wiki/Bulk_modulus" title="Bulk modulus">bulk modulus</a> around 298 GPa, similar to diamond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeh005_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boeh005-25">[25]</a></sup> It is very high energy and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> when pressure is released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The cubic-gauche form has space group <i>I</i>2<sub>1</sub>3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotakoski08_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kotakoski08-20">[20]</a></sup> The unit cell edge is 3.805 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotakoski08_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kotakoski08-20">[20]</a></sup> There are eight nitrogen atoms per unit cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotakoski08_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kotakoski08-20">[20]</a></sup> The bond angles are very close to tetrahedral. The structure contains rings of nitrogen atoms that are fused together. The position of the lone pairs of electrons is ranged so that their overlap is minimised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plas15_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Plas15-24">[24]</a></sup> The difference in bond energy varies from 0.83 eV per atom in nitrogen gas to 4.94 eV per atom, so having a difference in energy of over 4 eV per atom. This cubic-gauche nitrogen is the highest energy non-nuclear material and is being investigated for use in explosives and rocket fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup> Its energy density is 33 kJ g<sup>−1</sup> which is over three times the energy density of <a href="/wiki/HMX" title="HMX">HMX</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> cg-N has all bonds the same length<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erem004_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-erem004-4">[4]</a></sup> of 1.346 Å at 115 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeh005_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boeh005-25">[25]</a></sup> The cubic-gauche structure for nitrogen was predicted by Christian Mailhiot, Lin Yang and A. K. McMahan in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mail_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-mail-28">[28]</a></sup> Their prediction was for a bond lengths of 1.40 Å, bond angles of 114.0° and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihedral_angles" title="Dihedral angles">dihedral angless</a> of −106.8°. The term <i>gauche</i> refers to the odd dihedral angles, if it was 0° it would be called <i><a href="/wiki/Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism" title="Cis–trans isomerism">cis</a></i>, and if 180° it would be called <i>trans</i>. The dihedral angle Φ is related to the bond angle θ by sec(Φ) = sec(θ) − 1. The coordinate of one atom in the unit cell at x,x,x also determines the bond angle by cos(θ) = x(x-1/4)/(x<sup>2+(x-1/4)<sup>2</sup>).</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mail_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-mail-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poly-N">Poly-N</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Poly-N">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another network solid nitrogen called poly-N and abbreviated <i>p</i>N was predicted in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotakoski08_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kotakoski08-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>p</i>N has space group <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> and cell dimensions a = 5.49 Å, β = 87.68°. Other higher pressure polymeric forms are predicted in theory, and a metallic form is expected if the pressure is high enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layered_polymetric">Layered polymetric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Layered polymetric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The LP-N phase is an actually produced, layered polymetric phase stable between 120 and 180 GPa. It has a density of 4.85 g cm<sup>−3</sup> at the low pressure end of its range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The structure contains layers of fused rings of seven nitrogen atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plas15_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Plas15-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yet other phases of solid dinitrogen are termed ζ'-N<sub>2</sub> and κ-N<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plas15_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Plas15-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_substances">Related substances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Related substances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under pressure nitrogen can form <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_compound" title="Van der Waals compound">van der Waals compounds</a> with other molecules. It can form an orthorhombic phase with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> above 5 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldous_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-aldous-31">[31]</a></sup> With helium He(N<sub>2</sub>)<sub>11</sub> is formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vos92_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-vos92-19">[19]</a></sup> N<sub>2</sub> crystallizes with water in <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_clathrate" title="Nitrogen clathrate">nitrogen clathrate</a> and in a mixture with oxygen O<sub>2</sub> and water in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Air_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Air clathrate (page does not exist)">air clathrate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Choukroun_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Choukroun-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helium">Helium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Helium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solid nitrogen can dissolve 2 mole % He under pressure in its disordered phases such as the γ-phase. Under higher pressure 9 mol% He can react with ε-nitrogen to form a hexagonal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birefringent" title="Birefringent">birefringent</a> crystalline van der Waals compound. The unit cell contains 22 nitrogen atoms and 2 helium atoms. It has a volume of 580 Å<sup>3</sup> for a pressure of 11 GPa decreasing to 515 Å<sup>3</sup> at 14 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vos92_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-vos92-19">[19]</a></sup> It resembles the ε-phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> At 14.5 GPa and 295 K the unit cell has space group <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>m</i> and a=7.936 Å c=9.360 Å. At 28 GPa a transition happens in which the orientation of N<sub>2</sub> molecules becomes more ordered. When the pressure on He(N<sub>2</sub>)<sub>11</sub> exceeds 135 GPa the substance changes from clear to black, and takes on an amorphous form similar to η-N<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane">Methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solid nitrogen can crystallise with some solid methane included. At 55 K the molar percentage can range up to 16.35% CH<sub>4</sub>, and at 40 K only 5%. In the complementary situation, solid methane can include some nitrogen in its crystals, up to 17.31% nitrogen. As the temperature drops, less methane can dissolve in solid nitrogen, and in α-N<sub>2</sub> there is a major drop in methane solubility. These mixtures are prevalent in outer Solar System objects such as Pluto that have both nitrogen and methane on their surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proto015_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-proto015-35">[35]</a></sup> At room temperature there is a clathrate of methane and nitrogen in 1:1 ratio formed at pressures over 5.6 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_monoxide">Carbon monoxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Carbon monoxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The carbon monoxide molecule (CO) is very similar to dinitrogen in size, and it can mix in all proportions with solid nitrogen without changing crystal structure. Carbon monoxide is also found on the surfaces of Pluto and Triton at levels below 1%. Variations in the infrared linewidth of carbon monoxide absorption can reveal the concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_gases">Noble gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Noble gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neon or xenon atoms can also be included in solid nitrogen in the β and δ phases. Inclusion of neon pushes the β−δ phase boundary to higher pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kooi99_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-kooi99-38">[38]</a></sup> Argon is also very miscible in solid nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kooi99_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-kooi99-38">[38]</a></sup> For compositions of argon and nitrogen with 60% to 70% nitrogen, the hexagonal form remains stable to 0 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A van der Waals compound of xenon and nitrogen exists above 5.3 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kooi99_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-kooi99-38">[38]</a></sup> A van der Waals compounds of neon and nitrogen was shown using Raman spectroscopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kooi99_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-kooi99-38">[38]</a></sup> The compound has formula (N<sub>2</sub>)<sub>6</sub>Ne<sub>7</sub>. It has a hexagonal structure, with a=14.400 c=8.0940 at a pressure of 8 GPa. A van der Waals compound with argon is not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With dideuterium, a clathrate (N<sub>2</sub>)<sub>12</sub>D<sub>2</sub> exits around 70 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen">Oxygen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Oxygen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solid nitrogen can take up to a one fifth substitution by oxygen O<sub>2</sub> and still keep the same crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siha_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-siha-42">[42]</a></sup> δ-N<sub>2</sub> can be substituted by up to 95% O<sub>2</sub> and retain the same structure. Solid O<sub>2</sub> can only have a solid solution of 5% or less of N<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siha_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-siha-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_treatment">Radiation treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Radiation treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When solid nitrogen is irradiated by high speed protons or electrons, several reactive radicals are formed, including atomic nitrogen (N), nitrogen cations (N<sup>+</sup>), dinitrogen cation (N<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>), trinitrogen radicals (N<sub>3</sub> and N<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a> (N<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid nitrogen is used in a slush mixture with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> in order to cool faster than with liquid nitrogen alone, useful for applications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sperm_cryopreservation" title="Sperm cryopreservation">sperm cryopreservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The semi-solid mixture can also be called <b>slush nitrogen</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> or SN2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solid nitrogen is used as a matrix on which to store and study reactive chemical species, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals">free radicals</a> or isolated atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> One use is to study <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_complex" title="Dinitrogen complex">dinitrogen complexes</a> of metals in isolation from other molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_nitrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

Much of the surface of <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a> is covered in the hexagonal form of solid nitrogen (the β crystal phase), which can be seen as a bluish green band around the equator in this photomosaic.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Solid nitrogen forms a large part of the surface of Pluto and the Neptunian moon <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>. On Pluto it was directly observed for the first time in July 2015 by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i> space probe and on Triton it was directly observed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> space probe in August 1989.</p>
<p>On Triton, solid nitrogen takes the form of frost crystals and a transparent sheet layer of <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealed</a> nitrogen ice, often referred to as a "glaze".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triton_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Triton-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">Geysers</a> of nitrogen gas were observed by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> to spew from the subpolar regions around Triton's southern polar ice cap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> A possible explanation of this observed phenomenon is that the sun shines through the transparent layer of nitrogen ice, heating the layers beneath. Nitrogen sublimes and eventually erupts through holes in the upper layer, carrying dust along with it and creating dark streaks.</p>
