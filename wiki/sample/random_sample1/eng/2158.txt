<p>A <i><b>kotatsu</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">炬燵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a low, wooden table frame covered by a <i><a href="/wiki/Futon" title="Futon">futon</a></i>, or heavy blanket, upon which a table top sits. Underneath is a heat source, formerly a charcoal <a href="/wiki/Brazier" title="Brazier">brazier</a> but now electric, often built into the table itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kotatsu are used almost exclusively in Japan, although <a href="#Other_countries">similar devices</a> are used elsewhere: for example the Spanish brasero.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kotatsu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of the <i>kotatsu</i> begins in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muromachi_era" title="Muromachi era">Muromachi era</a> during the fourteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its origins begin with the Japanese cooking <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">hearth</a>, known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Irori" title="Irori">irori</a></i>. Charcoal was the primary method of cooking and heating in the traditional Japanese household and was used to heat the <i>irori</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup> By the fourteenth century in Japan, a seating platform was introduced to the <i>irori</i> and its cooking function became separated from its seating function. On top of the wooden platform a quilt was placed, known as an <i>oki</i> that trapped and localized the heat of the charcoal burner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This early ancestor to the modern <i>kotatsu</i> was called a <i>hori-gotatsu</i>. The word <i>hori-gotatsu</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">掘り炬燵</span></span>) is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">掘り</span></span> (hori) meaning ditch, digging, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">炬</span></span> (ko) meaning torch or fire, and <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">燵</span></span> (tatsu) meaning foot warmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horigotatsu_kanji_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-horigotatsu_kanji-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formation of the <i>hori-gotatsu</i> was slightly changed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edo_Period" title="Edo Period">Edo Period</a> during the seventeenth century. These changes consisted of the floor around the <i>irori</i> being dug-out into the ground in a square shape. The wooden platform was placed around this, making a hearth. Then the blanket was placed on top of the platform again, where one could sit with legs underneath to stay warm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The moveable <i>kotatsu</i> was created later, originating from the concept of <i>hori-gotatsu</i>. This <i>kotatsu</i> came about with the popular use of <i><a href="/wiki/Tatami" title="Tatami">tatami</a></i> matting in Japanese homes. Instead of placing the charcoals in the <i>irori</i>, they were placed in an earthen pot which was placed on the <i>tatami</i> making the <i>kotatsu</i> transportable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup> This more modern style <i>kotatsu</i> is known as the <i>oki-gotatsu</i>. The word <i>oki-gotatsu</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">置き炬燵</span></span>) is derived from the kanji <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">置き</span></span> (oki) meaning placement, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">炬</span></span> meaning torch or fire, and <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">燵</span></span> meaning foot warmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okigotatsu_Kanji_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Okigotatsu_Kanji-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the middle of the twentieth century charcoal was replaced with electricity as a heating source. Instead of having the moveable earthen pot of charcoals beneath the <i>kotatsu</i>, it was possible to attach an electric heating fixture directly to the frame of the <i>kotatsu</i>. Thus, the <i>kotatsu</i> became completely mobile with electricity and became a common feature of Japanese homes during winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kotatsu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two kinds of <i>kotatsu</i> used in Japan today, differing in the configuration and the type of heating:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Electric</b>: The modern style of <i>kotatsu</i> (<i><b>oki-gotatsu</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">置き炬燵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>) consists of a table with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_heater" title="Electric heater">electric heater</a> attached to the underside of the table. This evolved from a clay pot with hot coals placed under a table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpn-miyabi_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-jpn-miyabi-2">[2]</a></sup> The kotatsu usually is set on a thin futon, like a throw rug. A second, thicker futon is placed over the <i>kotatsu</i> table, above which the tabletop is placed. The electric heater attached to the underside of the table heats the space under the comforter.</li>
<li><b>Charcoal</b>: The more traditional type is a table placed over a recessed floor (<i><b>hori-gotatsu</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">掘り炬燵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>). The pit is cut into the floor and is about 40 centimeters deep. A <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> heater is placed somewhere in the pit's floor, walls, or, as in the modern-style <i>kotatsu</i>, attached to the table-frame. There are pit-type kotatsu with an electric heater too.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kotatsu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the twenty-first century, the <i>kotatsu</i> typically consists of the electric heater attached to the frame, which is no longer limited to wood, but may be made of plastic or other materials. Generally, a blanket (or <i>shitagake</i>) is draped over the frame and heater and under the table-top. This first blanket is covered by a second heavier blanket, known as a <i>kotatsu-gake</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">火燵掛布</span></span>). <i>Kotatsu-gake</i> often are decorative and may be designed to match home décor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A person sits on the floor or on <i><a href="/wiki/Zabuton" title="Zabuton">zabuton</a></i> cushions with their legs under the table and the blanket draped over the lower body. The <i>kotatsu</i> was designed when people most commonly wore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Japanese_clothing" title="List of Japanese clothing">traditional Japanese style clothes</a>, where the heat would enter through the bottom of the robes and rise to exit around the neck, thus heating the entire body.</p>
<p>Most Japanese housing is not insulated to the same degree as a western domicile and do not have <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating</a>, thus relying primarily on space heating. Heating is expensive because of the lack of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a> and the draftiness of housing. A <i>kotatsu</i> is a relatively inexpensive way to stay warm in the winter, as the futons trap the warm air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Families may choose to concentrate their activity in this one area of the house in order to save on energy costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the summer, the blanket may be removed, and the <i>kotatsu</i> used as a normal table.</p>
<p>It is possible to sleep under a <i>kotatsu</i>, although unless one is quite short, one's body will not be completely covered. This generally is considered acceptable for naps, but not for overnight sleeping for many reasons: one's body is not completely covered, yielding uneven heating; the table is low, so one may touch heating elements accidentally when moving while asleep, risking burns. Traditionally, children are told that they will catch a cold if they sleep under a <i>kotatsu</i>. Pets such as cats frequently sleep under <i>kotatsu</i>, however, and are small enough to fit completely underneath—comparable to cats who sleep on floor heating vents in western countries (Japanese homes do not generally have floor heating vents).</p>
<p>During the winter months in Japan, the <i>kotatsu</i> often is the center of domestic life. In the evening family members gather around the <i>kotatsu</i> to enjoy food, television, games, and conversation while keeping the lower half of their bodies warm. It has been said that, "once under the <i>kotatsu</i>, all of your worries slip away as a familiar warmth takes over and you become completely relaxed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kotatsu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are similar systems in countries around the world: economical and often sociable ways to keep warm while sitting still. In Spain and Portugal, the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mesa_camilla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mesa camilla (page does not exist)">mesa camilla</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/mesa_camilla" title="es:mesa camilla">es</a>)</span></i> is a small round table with a <i><a href="/wiki/Brasero_(heater)" title="Brasero (heater)">brasero</a></i> heater placed underneath. The Netherlands used to use a <a href="/wiki/Foot_stove" title="Foot stove">foot stove</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a> built 'Japanese footwarmers' in the trenches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
For centuries, a very similar item called a <i>sandali</i> has been used widely in Tajikistan and Afghanistan. They are used even today in many traditional houses, as a warm family eating place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A similar product called a <i><a href="/wiki/Korsi" title="Korsi">korsi</a></i> is used in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.</p>
<p>In China and Korea, <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_heating" title="Underfloor heating">underfloor heating</a> traditionally is used. The devices used in a similar fashion are, respectively, a <a href="/wiki/Kang_bed-stove" title="Kang bed-stove">Kang bed-stove</a> and an <i><a href="/wiki/Ondol" title="Ondol">ondol</a></i>.</p>
