<p><b>Ferdinand Gotthold Max Eisenstein</b> (16 April 1823 – 11 October 1852) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>. He specialized in <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">analysis</a>, and proved several results that eluded even <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Gauss</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois" title="Évariste Galois">Galois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niels_Henrik_Abel" title="Niels Henrik Abel">Abel</a> before him, Eisenstein died before the age of 30. He was born and died in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gotthold_Eisenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His parents, Johan Konstantin Eisenstein and Helene Pollack, were of Jewish descent and converted to Protestantism prior to his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> From an early age, he demonstrated talent in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>. As a young child he learned to play <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, and he continued to play and compose for piano throughout his life.</p>
<p>He suffered various health problems throughout his life, including <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a> as an infant, a disease that took the lives of all five of his brothers and sisters. In 1837, at the age of 14, he enrolled at Friedrich Wilhelm <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasium</a>, and soon thereafter at Friedrich Werder Gymnasium in Berlin. His teachers recognized his talents in mathematics, but by 15 years of age he had already learned all the material taught at the school. He then began to study <a href="/wiki/Differential_calculus" title="Differential calculus">differential calculus</a> from the works of <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph-Louis Lagrange">Joseph-Louis Lagrange</a>.</p>
<p>At 17, still a student, Eisenstein began to attend classes given by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gustav_Lejeune_Dirichlet" title="Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet">Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet</a> and others at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>. In 1842, before taking his final exams, he traveled with his mother to England, to search for his father. In 1843 he met <a href="/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton" title="William Rowan Hamilton">William Rowan Hamilton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, who gave him a copy of his book on <a href="/wiki/Niels_Henrik_Abel" title="Niels Henrik Abel">Niels Henrik Abel</a>'s proof of the impossibility of solving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abel-Ruffini_theorem#Proof" title="Abel-Ruffini theorem">fifth degree polynomials</a>, a work that would stimulate Eisenstein's interest in mathematical research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Five_remarkable_years">Five remarkable years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gotthold_Eisenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Five remarkable years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1843 Eisenstein returned to Berlin, where he passed his graduation exams and enrolled in the University the following autumn. In January 1844 he had already presented his first work to the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Berlin Academy</a>, on cubic forms in two variables. The same year he met for the first time with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>, who would later become Eisenstein's patron. Humboldt managed to find grants from the King, the government of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, and the Berlin academy to compensate for Eisenstein's extreme <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The monies, always late and grudgingly given, were earned in full measure by Eisenstein: in 1844 alone he published over 23 papers and two problems in <a href="/wiki/Crelle%27s_Journal" title="Crelle's Journal">Crelle's Journal</a>, including two proofs of the law of <a href="/wiki/Quadratic_reciprocity" title="Quadratic reciprocity">quadratic reciprocity</a>, and the analogous laws of <a href="/wiki/Cubic_reciprocity" title="Cubic reciprocity">cubic reciprocity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quartic_reciprocity" title="Quartic reciprocity">quartic reciprocity</a>.</p>
<p>In June 1844 Eisenstein visited <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> in <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a>. In 1845, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Eduard_Kummer" title="Ernst Eduard Kummer">Kummer</a> saw to it that he received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_doctorate" title="Honorary doctorate">honorary doctorate</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Breslau" title="University of Breslau">University of Breslau</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jakob_Jacobi" title="Carl Gustav Jakob Jacobi">Jacobi</a> also encouraged the distinction, but later relations between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jakob_Jacobi" title="Carl Gustav Jakob Jacobi">Jacobi</a> and Eisenstein were always rocky, due primarily to a disagreement over the order of discoveries made in 1846. In 1847 Eisenstein <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habilitated" title="Habilitated">habilitated</a> at the University of Berlin, and he began to teach there. <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann" title="Bernhard Riemann">Bernhard Riemann</a> attended his classes on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptic_functions" title="Elliptic functions">elliptic functions</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imprisonment_and_death">Imprisonment and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gotthold_Eisenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Imprisonment and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1848 Eisenstein was imprisoned briefly by the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> army for his revolutionary activities in Berlin. Eisenstein always had republican sympathies, and while he did not actively participate in the revolution of 1848, he was arrested on 19 March of that year. Although he was released just one day later, the harsh treatment he suffered damaged his already delicate health. But his association with the Republican cause led to his official stipends being revoked, despite Humboldt's tenaciously coming to his defense.</p>
<p>Despite his health, Eisenstein continued writing papers on quadratic partitions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_numbers" title="Prime numbers">prime numbers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Reciprocity_law" title="Reciprocity law">reciprocity laws</a>. In 1851, at the instigation of Gauss, he was elected to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Academy_of_G%C3%B6ttingen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Academy of Göttingen (page does not exist)">Academy of Göttingen</a>; one year later, this time at the recommendation of Dirichlet, he was also elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Academy_of_Sciences_Berlin" title="German Academy of Sciences Berlin">Academy of Berlin</a>.</p>
<p>He died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> at the age of 29. Humboldt, then 83, accompanied his remains to the cemetery. He had recently obtained, too late, as it turned out, the funding necessary to send Eisenstein on holiday to <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purported_Gauss_quote">Purported Gauss quote</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gotthold_Eisenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Purported Gauss quote">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:50%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Seit 1847 war er Privatdocent an der Universität zu Breslau, seit dem 24. April 1852 ordentliches Mitglied der dortigen Akademie der Wissenschaften, als welcher er am 1. Juli seine Antrittsrede hielt, ein Vierteljahr später starb der geniale Mathematiker, den ein Gauß so sehr seiner Freundschaft gewürdigt hatte, daß er eine Sammlung Eisenstein’scher Aufsätze, welche 1848, also noch während des Lebens des Verfassers, in Berlin erschien, mit einer Vorrede einleitete, und sich gesprächsweise einmal äußerte, <b>es habe nur drei epochebildende Mathematiker gegeben: Archimed, Newton, Eisenstein.</b></p>
<p>Gauss ... in conversation once remarked that, <b>there had been only three epoch-making mathematicians: Archimedes, Newton, and Eisenstein.</b></p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Moritz Cantor (<a href="#CITEREFCantor1877">1877</a>) This is the origin of the quote sometimes attributed to Gauss about Eisenstein. (Bold added.)</cite></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eric_Temple_Bell" title="Eric Temple Bell">E. T. Bell</a> in his 1937 book <a href="/wiki/Men_of_Mathematics" title="Men of Mathematics">Men of Mathematics</a> (page 237) claims that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Gauss</a> said "There have been but three epoch-making mathematicians, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>, and Eisenstein", and this has been widely quoted in writings about Eisenstein. This is not a quote by Gauss, but is (a translation of) the end of a sentence from the biography of Eisenstein by <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Cantor" title="Moritz Cantor">Moritz Cantor</a> (<a href="#CITEREFCantor1877">1877</a>), one of Gauss's last students and a historian of mathematics, who was summarizing his recollection of a remark made by Gauss about Eisenstein in a conversation many years earlier.</p>
<p>Although it is doubtful that Gauss really put Eisenstein in the same league as Newton, his writings show that Gauss thought very highly of Eisenstein. For example, a letter from Gauss to Humboldt, dated the 14th of April in 1846, says that Eisenstein's talent is one that nature bestows only a few times a century ("welche die Natur in jedem Jahrhundert nur wenigen erteilt").</p>
