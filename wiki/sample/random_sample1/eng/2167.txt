<p>The <b>Kofun period</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">古墳時代</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kofun jidai</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is an era in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan" title="History of Japan">history of Japan</a> from around 250 to 538 AD. It follows the <a href="/wiki/Yayoi_period" title="Yayoi period">Yayoi period</a>. The word <i><a href="/wiki/Kofun" title="Kofun">kofun</a></i> is Japanese for the type of <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">burial mounds</a> dating from this era. The Kofun and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">Asuka periods</a> are sometimes referred to collectively as the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_period" title="Yamato period">Yamato period</a>. The Kofun period is the earliest era of <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a> in Japan; as the chronology of its historical sources tends to be very distorted, studies of this period require deliberate criticism and the aid of archaeology.</p>
<p>The Kofun period is divided from the <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">Asuka period</a> by its cultural differences. The Kofun period is characterized by a <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> culture which existed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> prior to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. Politically, the leader of a powerful clan won control over much of west <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a> and the northern half of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a> and eventually established the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_House_of_Japan" title="Imperial House of Japan">Imperial House of Japan</a>. Kofun burial mounds on <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima" title="Tanegashima">Tanegashima</a> and two very old Shinto shrines on <a href="/wiki/Yakushima" title="Yakushima">Yakushima</a> suggest that these islands were the southern boundaries of the Yamato state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while its northernmost extent was as far north as Tainai in the modern <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata Prefecture</a>, where mounds have been excavated associated with a person with close links to the Yamato kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kofun_tombs">Kofun tombs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kofun tombs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kofun" title="Kofun">Kofun</a></div>

<p><i>Kofun</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a> <i>kú</i> 古 "ancient" + <i>bjun</i> 墳 "burial mound") are defined as the burial mounds built for the people of the ruling class during the 3rd to 7th centuries in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keally_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-keally-3">[3]</a></sup> and the Kofun period takes its name from these distinctive earthen mounds. The mounds contained large stone burial chambers. Some are surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moats</a>.</p>
<p>Kofun come in many shapes, with round and square being the most common. A distinct style is the keyhole-shaped kofun, with its square front and round back. Kofun range in size from several meters to over 400 meters in length and unglazed pottery figures called <a href="/wiki/Haniwa" title="Haniwa">Haniwa</a> were often buried under the circumference of the kofun.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KofunCuirass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="927" data-file-width="605" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/KofunCuirass.jpg/200px-KofunCuirass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/KofunCuirass.jpg/300px-KofunCuirass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/KofunCuirass.jpg/400px-KofunCuirass.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KofunCuirass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Iron helmet and armour with gilt bronze decoration, Kofun period, 5th century. <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_National_Museum" title="Tokyo National Museum">Tokyo National Museum</a>.</div>

<p>The oldest Japanese kofun is said to be Hokenoyama Kofun located in <a href="/wiki/Sakurai,_Nara" title="Sakurai, Nara">Sakurai, Nara</a>, which dates to the late 3rd century. In the Makimuku district of Sakurai, later keyhole kofuns (Hashihaka Kofun, Shibuya Mukaiyama Kofun) were built around the early 4th century. The trend of the keyhole kofun first spread from <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Province" title="Yamato Province">Yamato</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kawachi_Province" title="Kawachi Province">Kawachi</a> (where gigantic kofun such as Daisenryō Kofun exist), and then throughout the country (except for <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tōhoku region</a>) in the 5th century. Keyhole kofun disappeared later in the 6th century, probably because of the drastic reformation which took place in the Yamato court; <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i> records the introduction of Buddhism at this time. The last two great kofun are the Imashirozuka kofun (length: 190m) of Osaka, which is believed by current scholars to be the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Keitai" title="Emperor Keitai">Emperor Keitai</a>, and the Iwatoyama kofun (length: 135m) of Fukuoka which was recorded in <i>Fudoki of Chikugo</i> to be the tomb of Iwai, the political archrival of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Keitai" title="Emperor Keitai">Keitai</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Yamato_court">Yamato court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Yamato court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the controversy over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamataikoku" title="Yamataikoku">Yamataikoku</a> the actual start of Yamato rule is disputed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, it is usually believed to have been created around 250 CE. Regardless, it is generally agreed that Yamato rulers possessed keyhole kofun culture and held hegemony in Yamato up to the 4th century. The regional autonomy of local powers remained throughout the period, particularly in places such as Kibi (current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okayama_prefecture" title="Okayama prefecture">Okayama prefecture</a>), Izumo (current <a href="/wiki/Shimane_Prefecture" title="Shimane Prefecture">Shimane Prefecture</a>), Koshi (current <a href="/wiki/Fukui_Prefecture" title="Fukui Prefecture">Fukui</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata Prefecture</a>), Kenu (northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D" title="Kantō">Kantō</a>), Chikushi (northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a>), and Hi (central Kyūshū); it was only in the 6th century that the Yamato clans began to assert dominance over the entire southern half of Japan. Yamato's relationships with China are likely to have begun in the late 4th century, according to the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Song" title="Book of Song">Book of Song</a>.</p>
<p>The Yamato <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polity</a>, which emerged by the late 5th century, was distinguished by powerful <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clans</a> (豪族: <i><a href="/wiki/G%C5%8Dzoku" title="Gōzoku">Gōzoku</a></i>). Each clan was headed by a <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarch</a> (氏上: <i>Uji-no-kami</i>) who performed sacred rites to the clan's <i><a href="/wiki/Kami" title="Kami">kami</a></i> to ensure the long-term welfare of the clan. Clan members were the aristocracy, and the kingly line that controlled the Yamato court was at its pinnacle. Powerful clan leaders were awarded <a href="/wiki/Kabane" title="Kabane">kabane</a>, a title that denoted a political rank. This title was inherited, and used instead of the family name.</p>
<p>The Kofun period of Japanese culture is also sometimes called the Yamato period by some Western scholars, since this local chieftainship rose to become the Imperial dynasty at the end of the Kofun period. Yamato and its dynasty however were just one rival polity among others throughout the Kofun era. Japanese archaeologists emphasise instead the fact that, in the first half of the Kofun period, other regional chieftainships, such as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kibi" title="Kingdom of Kibi">Kibi</a> were in close contention for dominance or importance. The Tsukuriyama Kofun of Kibi is the fourth largest kofun in Japan.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KofunSwordHilts.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1442" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/KofunSwordHilts.jpg/220px-KofunSwordHilts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/KofunSwordHilts.jpg/330px-KofunSwordHilts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/KofunSwordHilts.jpg/440px-KofunSwordHilts.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KofunSwordHilts.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gilded sword hilt pommels, Kofun period, 6th century, Japan</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yamato_period" title="Yamato period">Yamato court</a> ultimately exercised power over clans in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>, bestowing titles, some hereditary, on clan chieftains. The Yamato name became synonymous with all of Japan as the Yamato rulers suppressed the clans and acquired agricultural lands. Based on <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> models (including the adoption of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_written_language" title="Chinese written language">Chinese written language</a>), they started to develop a central administration and an imperial court attended by subordinate clan chieftains but with no permanent capital. The famous powerful clans were the <a href="/wiki/Soga_clan" title="Soga clan">Soga</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E8%91%9B%E5%9F%8E%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:葛城氏">Katsuraki</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B9%B3%E7%BE%A4%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:平群氏">Heguri</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B7%A8%E5%8B%A2%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:巨勢氏">Koze</a> clans in the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Province" title="Yamato Province">Yamato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bizen_Province" title="Bizen Province">Bizen Province</a>, and the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%90%89%E5%82%99%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:吉備氏">Kibi</a> clans in the <a href="/wiki/Izumo_Province" title="Izumo Province">Izumo Province</a>. The <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_clan" title="Ōtomo clan">Ōtomo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mononobe_clan" title="Mononobe clan">Mononobe clans</a> were the military leaders, and the <a href="/wiki/Nakatomi_clan" title="Nakatomi clan">Nakatomi</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%BF%8C%E9%83%A8%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:忌部氏">Inbe</a> clans handled rituals. The <a href="/wiki/Soga_clan" title="Soga clan">Soga clan</a> provided the highest minister in the government, while the Ōtomo and Mononobe clans provided the second highest ministers. The heads of provinces were called Kuni-no-miyatsuko. The crafts were organized into guilds.</p>

<p>In addition to archaeological findings indicating a local monarchy in the <a href="/wiki/Kibi_Province" title="Kibi Province">Kibi Province</a> as an important rival, the legend of the 4th century Prince <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Takeru" title="Yamato Takeru">Yamato Takeru</a> alludes to the borders of the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Province" title="Yamato Province">Yamato</a> and battlegrounds in the area. A frontier was obviously somewhere close to the later <a href="/wiki/Izumo_Province" title="Izumo Province">Izumo Province</a> (the eastern part of today's <a href="/wiki/Shimane_Prefecture" title="Shimane Prefecture">Shimane Prefecture</a>). Another frontier, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a>, was apparently somewhere north of today's <a href="/wiki/Kumamoto_Prefecture" title="Kumamoto Prefecture">Kumamoto Prefecture</a>. The legend specifically states that there was an eastern land in Honshū "whose people disobeyed the imperial court", against whom Yamato Takeru was sent to fight. It's not clear whether the rivalling country was located rather close to the Yamato nucleus area itself, or relatively far away. The modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kai_province" title="Kai province">Kai province</a> is mentioned as one of the locations where prince Yamato Takeru sojourned in his said military expedition.</p>
<p>The northern frontier of this age was also explained in <i><a href="/wiki/Kojiki" title="Kojiki">Kojiki</a></i> as the legend of Shido Shogun's (四道将軍: Shoguns to four ways) expedition. Out of four shoguns, Ōbiko set northward to Koshi and his son Take Nunakawawake set to eastern states. The father moved east from northern Koshi while the son moved north on his way, and they finally met at Aizu (current western <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Prefecture" title="Fukushima Prefecture">Fukushima</a>). Although the legend itself is not likely to be a historical fact, Aizu is rather close to southern Tōhoku, where the north end of keyhole kofun culture as of the late 4th century is located.</p>

<p>During the Kofun period, a highly aristocratic society with <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militaristic</a> rulers developed.</p>
<p>The Kofun period was a critical stage in Japan's evolution toward a more cohesive and recognized state. This society was most developed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinai_Region" title="Kinai Region">Kinai Region</a> and the easternmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Seto_Inland_Sea" title="Seto Inland Sea">Inland Sea</a>. Japan's rulers of the time even petitioned the Chinese court for confirmation of royal titles.</p>
<p>While the rulers' titles are diplomatically <i>King</i>, they locally titled themselves as <i>Ōkimi</i> (Great King) during this period. Inscriptions in two swords, <a href="/wiki/Inariyama_Sword" title="Inariyama Sword">Inariyama Sword</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eta_Funayama_Sword" title="Eta Funayama Sword">Eta Funayama Sword</a> had records of <i>Amenoshita Shiroshimesu</i> (治天下; "ruling of Heaven and Earth") and <i>Ōkimi</i> (大王) in common, to be a ruler that the bearers of these swords were subjected to. It reveals that rulers of this age also grasped religious authorities to justify their thrones through heavenly dignities. The title of <i>Amenoshita Shiroshimesu Okimi</i> was used up to 7th century, until being replaced by <i>Tenno</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clans_of_the_Yamato_Court">Clans of the Yamato Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clans of the Yamato Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the clans and local chieftains that made up the Yamato polity claimed descent from the imperial family or other <a href="/wiki/Kami" title="Kami">tribal Gods</a>. The archeological evidence for such clans is found in the Inariyama sword, on which the bearer recorded the names of his ancestors to claim its origin to <i>Ōbiko</i> (大彦) who was recorded in <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i> as a son of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dgen" title="Emperor Kōgen">Emperor Kōgen</a>. On the other hand, there are also a number of clans claiming origins in China or the Korean peninsula.</p>
<p>In the 5th century, the <i>Kazuraki clan</i> (葛城氏), descending from the legendary grandson of Emperor Kōgen, was the most prominent power in the court and intermarried with the imperial family. After Kazuraki declined in the late 5th century, the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_clan" title="Ōtomo clan">Ōtomo clan</a> temporarily took its place. When Emperor Buretsu died with no apparent heir, it was <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_no_Kanamura" title="Ōtomo no Kanamura">Ōtomo no Kanamura</a> who recommended <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Keitai" title="Emperor Keitai">Emperor Keitai</a>, a very distant imperial relative who resided in <a href="/wiki/Koshi_Province" title="Koshi Province">Koshi Province</a>, as the new monarch. However, Kanamura resigned due to the failure of his diplomatic policies, and the court was eventually controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Mononobe_clan" title="Mononobe clan">Mononobe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soga_clan" title="Soga clan">Soga clans</a> at the beginning of the Asuka period.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toraijin">Toraijin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Toraijin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B8%A1%E6%9D%A5%E4%BA%BA" title="wiktionary:渡来人">Toraijin</a></i> refers to people who came to Japan from abroad in a broad sense, but it also refers to people who became naturalized citizens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Japan" title="Ancient Japan">ancient Japan</a> from the Chinese continent via the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a> or the Korean Peninsula in ancient times in a limited sense. They introduced many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese culture</a> to Japan. Valuing their knowledge and culture, the Yamato government gave preferential treatment to toraijin. The elements of Chinese culture introduced to the Yamato Imperial Court are very important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup> According to the book <i><a href="/wiki/Shinsen_Sh%C5%8Djiroku" title="Shinsen Shōjiroku">Shinsen Shōjiroku</a></i> compiled in 815, a total 154 out of 1,182 clans in the <a href="/wiki/Kinai" title="Kinai">Kinai</a> area on Honshū were regarded as people with foreign genealogy. The book specifically mentions 163 were from China, 104 such families from <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a> (<i>Paekche</i> in the older romanization), 41 from <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, 6 from <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>, and 3 from <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saeki_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-5">[5]</a></sup> They might be families that moved to Japan between the years A.D. 356-645.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_migration">Chinese migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chinese migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many important figures were also immigrants from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Chinese immigrants also had considerable influence according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Shinsen_Sh%C5%8Djiroku" title="Shinsen Shōjiroku">Shinsen Shōjiroku</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saeki_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-5">[5]</a></sup> which was used as a directory of aristocrats. Yamato Imperial Court had officially edited the directory in 815, and 163 Chinese clans were registered.</p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hata_tribe" title="Hata tribe">Hata clan</a>, which was composed of descendants of <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> arrived at Yamato in 403 (the fourteenth year of Ōjin) leading the people of 120 provinces. According to the <i>Shinsen Shōjiroku</i>, the Hata clan were dispersed in various provinces during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Nintoku" title="Emperor Nintoku">Emperor Nintoku</a> and were made to undertake sericulture and the manufacturing of silk for the court. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finance_ministry" title="Finance ministry">finance ministry</a> was set up in Yamato Court, Hata no Otsuchichi became <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tomo_no_miyatsuko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tomo no miyatsuko (page does not exist)">Tomo no miyatsuko</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%BC%B4%E9%80%A0" title="ja:伴造">ja</a>)</span> (the chief of various departments of the Yamato Court) and was appointed <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Treasury" title="Ministry of the Treasury">Okura no jo (Ministry of the Treasury)</a>, and the heads of family seem to have served as financial officials of the Yamato Court.</p>
<p>In 409 (the twentieth year of Ōjin), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Achi-no-Omi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Achi-no-Omi (page does not exist)">Achi-no-Omi</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%98%BF%E7%9F%A5%E4%BD%BF%E4%B8%BB" title="ja:阿知使主">ja</a>)</span>, the ancestor of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-Aya_clan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yamato-Aya clan (page does not exist)">Yamato-Aya clan</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9D%B1%E6%BC%A2%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:東漢氏">ja</a>)</span>, which was also composed of Chinese immigrants, arrived with people from 17 districts. According to the <i>Shinsen Shōjiroku</i>, Achi obtained the permission to establish the Province of Imaki. The Kawachi-no-Fumi clan, descendants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaozu_of_Han" title="Gaozu of Han">Gaozu of Han</a>, introduced aspects of Chinese writing to the Yamato court.</p>
<p>The <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%AB%98%E5%90%91%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:高向氏">Takamuko clan</a> is a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Cao" title="Cao Cao">Cao Cao</a>. <a href="/wiki/Takamuko_no_Kuromaro" title="Takamuko no Kuromaro">Takamuko no Kuromaro</a> was a center member of <a href="/wiki/Taika_Reform" title="Taika Reform">Taika Reform</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_migration">Korean migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Korean migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the many Korean immigrants who settled in Japan beginning in the 4th century, some came to be the progenitors of Japanese clans. According to <i>Kojiki</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>, the oldest record of a <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> immigrant is <a href="/wiki/Amenohiboko" title="Amenohiboko">Amenohiboko</a>, a legendary prince of <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> who settled to Japan at the era of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Suinin" title="Emperor Suinin">Emperor Suinin</a>, perhaps around the 3rd or 4th century.</p>
<p>Baekje and Silla sent their princes as hostages to the Yamato court in exchange for military support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For example, King <a href="/wiki/Muryeong_of_Baekje" title="Muryeong of Baekje">Muryeong of Baekje</a> was born in <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chikuzen_Province" title="Chikuzen Province">筑紫</a>) of Japan as a child of hostage in 462,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and left a son in Japan who settled there and became an ancestor of the Yamato no Fubito <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">和史</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"Scribes of Yamato"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> of minor <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Shoku_Nihongi" title="Shoku Nihongi">Shoku Nihongi</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">続日本紀</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, <a href="/wiki/Takano_no_Niigasa" title="Takano no Niigasa">Takano no Niigasa</a>, background of the naturalized clansmen <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato_no_Fumito&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yamato no Fumito (page does not exist)">Yamato no Fumito</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">和史</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, was a 10th-generation descendant of King Muryeong of Baekje who was chosen as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a> for <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dnin" title="Emperor Kōnin">Emperor Kōnin</a> and subsequently became the mother of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Kanmu" title="Emperor Kanmu">Emperor Kanmu</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kofun_Culture">Kofun Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Kofun Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese language</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_people" title="Korean people">Korean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> wrote accounts of history mostly in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>, making original pronunciations difficult to trace.</p>
<p>While writing was largely unknown to the indigenous Japanese of this period, the literary skills of foreigners seem to have become increasingly appreciated by the Japanese elite in many regions. The <a href="/wiki/Inariyama_Sword" title="Inariyama Sword">Inariyama Sword</a>, tentatively dated 471 or 531, contains Chinese-character inscriptions in styles used in China at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeley_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seeley-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haniwa">Haniwa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Haniwa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> wore armour, carried <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">swords</a> and other weapons, and used advanced military methods like those of northeast Asia. Evidence of these advances is seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Haniwa" title="Haniwa">haniwa</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">埴輪</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "clay ring")</span>, the clay offerings placed in a ring on and around the tomb mounds of the ruling elite. The most important of these <i>haniwa</i> were found in southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>—especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kinai region</a> around <a href="/wiki/Nara_Prefecture" title="Nara Prefecture">Nara Prefecture</a>—and northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a>. <i>Haniwa</i> grave offerings were made in numerous forms, such as horses, chickens, birds, fans, fish, houses, weapons, shields, sunshades, pillows, and male and female humans. Another funerary piece, the <i><a href="/wiki/Magatama" title="Magatama">magatama</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">勾玉</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "curved jewel")</span>, became one of the symbols of the power of the imperial house.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_material_culture_to_Japan">Introduction of material culture to Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Introduction of material culture to Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of the <a href="/wiki/Material_culture" title="Material culture">material culture</a> of the Kofun period demonstrates that Japan at this time was in close political and economic contact with continental Asia, especially with the southern dynasties of China, via the countries of the Korean peninsula. Indeed, bronze mirrors cast from the same mould have been found on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Tsushima_Strait" title="Tsushima Strait">Tsushima Strait</a>. <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">Irrigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">sericulture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> were also brought to Japan by Chinese immigrants who are mentioned in the ancient Japanese histories. For instance, the Hata clan of Chinese origin <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">秦</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, read "Qín" in Chinese)</span> introduced sericulture and certain kinds of weaving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shogakukan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shogakukan-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Towards_Asuka_period">Towards Asuka period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Towards Asuka period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kofun period gave way to the <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">Asuka period</a> in mid-6th century AD with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. The religion was officially introduced the year 538, and this year is traditionally taken as the start of the new period. The Asuka period also coincided with the reunification of China under the <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">Sui dynasty</a> later in this century. Japan became deeply influenced by Chinese culture, adding a broader cultural context to the religious distinction between the Kofun and Asuka periods.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_records">Japanese records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Japanese records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>, Silla was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Jing%C5%AB" title="Empress Jingū">Japanese Empress-consort Jingū</a> in the third century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> According to <i>Nihon Shoki</i>, the prince of <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> came to Japan to serve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Emperor" title="Japanese Emperor">Japanese Emperor</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and he lived in <a href="/wiki/Tajima_Province" title="Tajima Province">Tajima Province</a>. He was called Amenohiboko. His descendant is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tajima_mori&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tajima mori (page does not exist)">Tajima mori</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> According to <i>Kojiki</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <i>Nihon Shoki</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> during <a href="/wiki/Emperor_%C5%8Cjin" title="Emperor Ōjin">Emperor Ōjin</a>'s reign, <a href="/wiki/Geunchogo_of_Baekje" title="Geunchogo of Baekje">Geunchogo of Baekje</a> presented stallions and broodmares with <a href="/wiki/Horse_trainer" title="Horse trainer">horse trainers</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_emperor" title="Japanese emperor">Japanese emperor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KUrano_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-KUrano-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan%E2%80%93Korea_relations" title="History of Japan–Korea relations">History of Japan–Korea relations</a></div>
<p>Japan and South Korea have revisited previous interpretations of the history of this period, voiding many erroneous quotes and interpretations after several studies over the past decade. The first joint history project between Japan and South Korea was halted in 2005 due to disagreements between the two countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-english.hani.co.kr_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-english.hani.co.kr-29">[29]</a></sup> but later resumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreajoongangdaily.joinsmsn.com_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-koreajoongangdaily.joinsmsn.com-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The archaeological record, and ancient <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">Chinese sources</a>, indicate that the various tribes and chiefdoms of Japan did not begin to coalesce into states until 300 AD, when large tombs began to appear. Some describe the "mysterious century" as a time of internecine warfare as various chiefdoms competed for hegemony on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farris_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farris-31">[31]</a></sup> Even more complicating is the <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i> referencing the Japanese king who is Korean rulers sovereign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coval_1984_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coval_1984-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Due to this conflicting information, nothing can be concluded for the book of Song or <i>Nihon Shoki</i>.</p>
<p>According to the history records in Japan (<i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>) and Korea (<i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i>), Korean princes were sent to Japan as <a href="/wiki/Hostage#Historical_hostage_practices" title="Hostage">hostages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">[33]</a></sup> Due to the confusion on the exact nature of this relationship of whether Japanese is the founder of Silla<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> or hostages and the fact that the Nihon Shoki is a compilation of myths make it difficult to evaluate. In Japan the hostage interpretation is dominant. Other historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> like the ones who collaborated in the works for "Paekche of Korea and the origin of Yamato Japan" and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_W_Best&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jonathan W Best (page does not exist)">Jonathan W Best</a> who helped translate what was left of the Baekjae annals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> have noted Silla King served as horse keeper for the Japanese Emperor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and were the vanguard of the Japanese Navy during the war with Koguryeo as evidence of them being diplomats with some kind of familial tie to the Japanese imperial family and not hostages. In addition, the translation of these documents are difficult because in the past the term "Wa" was derogatory meaning "midget pirate" or "dwarf pirate" in Chinese. It is difficult to assess what is truly being stated; this could have been a derogatory statement between 2 warring nations.</p>
<p>Part of the reason the Joint history project finds these Japanese views questionable: There is no evidence of Japan ever having been sophisticated enough to control any part of Korea during the time of Jingu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenneth_B._Lee_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenneth_B._Lee-38">[38]</a></sup> However, there is archaeological evidence of Koreans going to Japan during this time, According to the book "<i>Korea and Japan in East Asian History</i>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-39">[39]</a></sup> and "<i>Paekche of Korea and the Origin of Yamato Japan</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> such findings as horse sculptures, <a href="/wiki/Shinju-kyo" title="Shinju-kyo">Shinju-kyo</a>, painting and iron-ware made in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei China</a> which do not match the dates of Emperor Ojin's reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The question that always comes up within the Korean community is, 'Why would a Japanese culture that doesn't have Korean ceramic ability or horses yet have horse sculptures in their tombs?'. Also significant is <a href="/wiki/Emperor_%C5%8Cjin" title="Emperor Ōjin">Emperor Ōjin</a>'s reign is off by more than a century with <a href="/wiki/Geunchogo_of_Baekje" title="Geunchogo of Baekje">Geunchogo of Baekje</a> in other words these people did not exist in the same time period. According to the book "<i>Paekchae of Korea and the origin of Yamato Japan</i>”, "The prince of <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> was the ancestor to the Japanese Emperor. The translation of "Nihon Shoki Vol.6" was added and Amenohiboko is described in <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i> as a maternal predecessor of Tajima-no-morosuku <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">但馬諸助</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> whose controversial legend says that she defeated Silla in the 5th century. This is highly inconsistent, as Jingū is said to have lived in the 2nd and 3rd centuries, and she is supposed to have died in 269 AD which would make her 300 to 400 years old. This conflicting information makes it difficult to understand these records.</p>
<p>According to the book <i>Paekchae of Korea and the origin of Yamato Japan</i>, "The prince of Silla was the ancestor to the Japanese Emperor." The translation of "Nihon Shoki Vol.6" was added and Amenohiboko is described in <i>Nihon Shoki</i> as a maternal predecessor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jing%C5%AB_of_Japan" title="Jingū of Japan">Empress Jingū</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, whose controversial legend says that she defeated Silla in the 5th Century. This is highly inconsistent, as Jingū is said to have lived in the 2nd and 3rd centuries, and she is supposed to have died in 269 AD which would make her 300 to 400 years old. This conflicting information makes it difficult to understand these records. "天日槍對曰　僕新羅國主之子也　然聞日本國有聖皇　則以己國授弟知古而化歸之" Amehiboko was said to have stated 2 possible translations depending on how you punctuate the sentence and how you evaluate the syntax. 1. "I am a prince in Korea. I heard that there was saint's king in Japan. To become a vassal of the king in Japan, I transferred the country to younger brother." Again in the past the <i>original text</i> and not modern Japanese or Korean translations, lacking past tense/present tense and words like transferring or transferred or transfer would be impossible to interpret. This same sentence can be 2. "I am a prince in Korea. I heard that there was a saint's King in Japan to become a vassal. The king in Japan I transfer the country to my younger brother." It is impossible to tell whether the sentence is stating that a Korean prince loves his younger brother, calling him a saint and ordaining his younger brother to be his vassal (and to rule as the King of Japan), or simply the other way around.</p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Song" title="Book of Song">Book of Song</a></i>, a Chinese emperor appointed <a href="/wiki/Five_kings_of_Wa" title="Five kings of Wa">five kings of Wa</a> to the position of ruler of Silla in 421,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> but what is confusing is that Japan wielded influence over the southern part of the Korean peninsula through the remote region according to the <i>Nihon Shoki</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In addition, the book of Song and the book of Sui can not be possible because many of the states considered to be Japan's vassal such as Chinhan and Mahan did not exist in the same time period as the vassal king of Yamato. In addition, the <i>Book of Song</i> was incomplete with missing volumes and filled in centuries later in a biased manner for political reasons.　Also, Silla did not have official contact with the Song/Sui until the 6th century making this 4th to 5th century statement impossible. "As Egami (1964) notes, it may look very strange that the names of six or seven states listed in the self-claimed titles included Chin-han and Ma-han which had preceded, respectively, the states of Silla and Paekche. Perhaps the King of Wa had included the names of six or seven south Korean states in his title merely to boast of the extent of his rule. But Wa Kings could not have included the names of nonexistent states." Other historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> also dispute Japan's theory, claiming there is no evidence of Japanese rule in Gaya or any other part of Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-45">[45]</a></sup> Another problem with the book of Song and book of Sui is that many of the volumes of the books were missing and re-written later in a biased manner. It is difficult to make any sense of what the relationship was like in the past.</p>
<p>Japan of the Kofun period was very receptive to the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese culture</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_culture" title="Korean culture">Korean culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imamura_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imamura-46">[46]</a></sup> Chinese and Korean immigrants played an important role in introducing elements of both to early Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">[33]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The special burial customs of the Goguryeo culture had an important influence on other cultures in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Decorated tombs and painted tumuli which date from the fifth century and later found in Japan are generally accepted as Northeast of China and Northern part of Korean peninsula exports to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Takamatsuzuka_Tomb" title="Takamatsuzuka Tomb">Takamatsuzuka Tomb</a> even has paintings of a woman dressed in distinctive clothes, similar to wall paintings from Goguryeo and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astrology" title="Chinese astrology">Chinese astrology</a> was being introduced during this time.</p>
<p>According to the <i>Book of Song</i>, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liu_Song_Dynasty" title="Liu Song Dynasty">Liu Song Dynasty</a>, the Emperor of China bestowed military sovereignty over Silla, <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Imna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinhan" title="Chinhan">Chinhan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mahan_confederacy" title="Mahan confederacy">Mahan</a> on <a href="/wiki/Five_kings_of_Wa" title="Five kings of Wa">King Sai of Wa</a>. However, this theory is widely rejected even in Japan as there is no evidence of Japanese rule in Gaya or any other part of Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-45">[45]</a></sup> After the death of King Kō of Wa, his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Five_kings_of_Wa" title="Five kings of Wa">Bu</a> acceded to the throne; King Bu requested to have Baekje added to the list of protectorates included in the official title bestowed upon the King of Wa by mandate of the Emperor of China, but his title was only renewed as "Supervisor of All Military Affairs of the Six Countries of Wa, Silla, Imna, Gara, Chinhan, and Mahan, Great General Who Keeps Peace in the East, King of the Country of Wa."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-song_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-song-16">[16]</a></sup> This entire statement is impossible because Chinhan and Mahan did not exist in the same time period as Silla, Baekje when the vassal Kings of Yamato were supposed to rule. As Egami wrote in 1964 "But Wa Kings could not have included the names of nonexistent states." In addition, Silla did not have official contact with the Song/Sui until the 6th century making this 4th to 5th century claim not possible. Due to the lack of evidence, and the confusion of whether the Wa were the descendants of Koreans, again no certain information is discernible.</p>
<p>Chinese chronicles note that horses were absent from the islands of Japan; they are first noted in the chronicles during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintoku" title="Nintoku">Nintoku</a>, most likely imported by Chinese and Korean immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coval_1984_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coval_1984-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-33">[33]</a></sup> According to some accounts, the horse was one of the treasures presented when the king of Silla surrendered to <a href="/wiki/Empress_Jing%C5%AB" title="Empress Jingū">Empress Jingū</a> in the <i>Nihon Shoki</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakamoto_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sakamoto-52">[52]</a></sup> Other accounts contend that there is no evidence of this from Silla, and the king who supposedly surrendered dates to the 5th century, thus making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Jingu" title="Empress Jingu">Empress Jingu</a> 200 years old. The <i>Nihon Shoki</i> states that the father of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Jingu" title="Empress Jingu">Empress Jingu</a> was <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Kaika" title="Emperor Kaika">Emperor Kaika</a>'s grandchild, and her mother was from the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E8%91%9B%E5%9F%8E%E6%B0%8F" title="ja:葛城氏">Katuragi clan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In addition, the <i>Nihon Shoki</i> states that a Korean from Silla, Amenohiboko, was an ancestor of Jingu so both the <i>Nihon Shoki</i> and the Chinese chronicles relating to Japan are difficult to interpret. In addition, there is no evidence of Japanese war with Korea or any Japanese presence in Korea at this time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-45">[45]</a></sup> and the Japanese did not have actual knowledge about horses until well after this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-K.C5.8Dz.C5.8D-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restricted_access_to_Gosashi_tomb">Restricted access to Gosashi tomb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofun_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Restricted access to Gosashi tomb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976 Japan stopped all foreign archaeologists from studying the Gosashi tomb, which is supposedly the resting place of Emperor Jingu. In 2008, Japan allowed controlled, limited access to foreign archaeologists, but the international community still has many unanswered questions. <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic News</a> reported that Japan "<i>the agency has kept access to the tombs restricted, prompting rumors that officials fear excavation would reveal bloodline links between the "pure" imperial family and Korea—or that some tombs hold no royal remains at all.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.nationalgeographic.com_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.nationalgeographic.com-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Yamato court had ties to the <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya confederacy</a>, called <i>Mimana</i> in Japanese. This region of the Korean peninsula has burial mounds similar to kofun. This has caused scholars to begin examining the shared relationship between the Yamato and Baekje during the 3rd and the 7th centuries AD, including the method of tomb construction. While a variety of theories exist, most have come to the conclusion that there was sharing of culture and construction methods both directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yoshii_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-yoshii-55">[55]</a></sup> For example, earrings discovered in Silla and Kaya tombs are very similar to Japanese earrings dated to the Kofun period, "The ultimate source of such elaborate techniques as granulation is probably the Greek and Etruscan goldsmiths of western Asia and Europe, whose skills were transmitted to northern China and later to Korea. The resemblance of <i>earrings</i> found in Japan in the Kofun period (ca. 3rd century—538 A.D.) to those from Silla and Kaya tombs suggests that such articles are imported from Korea."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metmoa_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-metmoa-56">[56]</a></sup> Spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_of_China" title="Culture of China">Chinese civilisation</a>, Han styles of tomb construction were gradually adopted in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three kingdoms of Korea">three kingdoms of Korea</a>, mainly from the 4th century onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The tombs in the southern part of Korea and Japan appear to have a relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yoshii_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-yoshii-55">[55]</a></sup> However, all the kofun-style tombs discovered in Korea have been dated as younger than those found in Japan. Leading Japanese scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to insist that those found in Korea were either built by Japanese immigrants or influenced by culture brought by them, but the advanced artifacts found in Korean's huge tombs are Japanese Haniwa,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hani_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-hani-58">[58]</a></sup> and Japan's tombs are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol_people" title="Mongol people">Mongol people</a> who came from Korea like the pottery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> horse sculptures and earrings,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.nationalgeographic.com_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.nationalgeographic.com-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Important note regarding these records is Japan and South Korea had revisited many of these interpretations in history and came to void many erroneous quotes and interpretations after a three-year study. Both countries agreed in a joint Japan and South Korea history project that Japan's interpretation of the 4th century was incorrect but the Japanese government did not agree with the historians of both countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreajoongangdaily.joinsmsn.com_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-koreajoongangdaily.joinsmsn.com-30">[30]</a></sup> "After conducting research for three years since 2002, scholars of the two countries announced their first report on three categories - the ancient, medieval, and modern times. At that time, Seoul demanded that the research institute’s findings be reflected in the textbooks of the two nations, but Japan rejected this request"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-english.hani.co.kr_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-english.hani.co.kr-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
