<p><b>Kunqu</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">崑曲</span>), also known as <b>Kunju</b> (崑劇), <b>Kun opera</b> or <b>Kunqu Opera</b>, is one of the oldest extant forms of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_opera" title="Chinese opera">Chinese opera</a>. It evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Kunshan" title="Kunshan">Kunshan</a> melody, and dominated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_theatre" title="Chinese theatre">Chinese theatre</a> from the 16th to the 18th centuries. The style originated in the <a href="/wiki/Wu_(region)" title="Wu (region)">Wu cultural area</a>. It is listed as one of the <a href="/wiki/Masterpieces_of_the_Oral_and_Intangible_Heritage_of_Humanity" title="Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity">Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> since 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kunqu singing techniques are said to have been developed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> by Wei Liangfu in the port of Taicang, but linked to the songs of nearby <a href="/wiki/Kunshan" title="Kunshan">Kunshan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Kunqu performance is closely inter-related with the performance of many other styles of Chinese musical theatre, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jingju" title="Jingju">Jingju</a> (Peking Opera), which contains much Kunqu repertoire. The emergence of <i>chuanqi</i> plays, commonly sung to Kunqu, is said to have ushered in a "second Golden Era of Chinese drama". Kunqu troupes experienced a commercial decline in the late 19th century. However, in the early 20th century, Kunqu was re-established by philanthropists as a theatrical genre that was subsequently subsidised by the Communist state. Like all traditional forms, Kunqu suffered setbacks both during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> and again under the influx of Western culture during the Reform and Opening Up policies, only to experience an even greater revival in the new millennium. Today, Kunqu is performed professionally in seven Mainland Chinese major cities: <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Northern Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Northern Kunqu Theatre</a>), <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shanghai_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shanghai Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Shanghai Kunqu Theatre</a>), <a href="/wiki/Suzhou" title="Suzhou">Suzhou</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suzhou_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suzhou Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Suzhou Kunqu Theatre</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jiangsu_Province_Kunqu_Theatre" title="Jiangsu Province Kunqu Theatre">Jiangsu Province Kunqu Theatre</a>), <a href="/wiki/Chenzhou" title="Chenzhou">Chenzhou</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hunan_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hunan Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Hunan Kunqu Theatre</a>), <a href="/wiki/Yongjia_County" title="Yongjia County">Yongjia County</a>/<a href="/wiki/Wenzhou" title="Wenzhou">Wenzhou</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yongjia_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yongjia Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Yongjia Kunqu Theatre</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hangzhou" title="Hangzhou">Hangzhou</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhejiang_Province_Kunqu_Theatre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhejiang Province Kunqu Theatre (page does not exist)">Zhejiang Province Kunqu Theatre</a>), as well as in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>. Non-professional opera societies are active in many other cities in China and abroad, and opera companies occasionally tour.</p>
<p>There are many plays that continue to be famous today, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peony_Pavilion" title="The Peony Pavilion">The Peony Pavilion</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peach_Blossom_Fan" title="The Peach Blossom Fan">The Peach Blossom Fan</a></i>, which were originally written for the Kunqu stage. In addition, many classical Chinese novels and stories, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Romance of the Three Kingdoms">Romance of the Three Kingdoms</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Water_Margin" title="Water Margin">Water Margin</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_West" title="Journey to the West">Journey to the West</a></i> were adapted very early into dramatic pieces.</p>
<p>Its melody or tune is one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Great_Characteristic_Melodies" title="Four Great Characteristic Melodies">Four Great Characteristic Melodies</a> in Chinese opera.</p>
