<p><b>Calvary</b>, also <b>Gagulta</b> (<a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="arc" xml:lang="arc">ܓܓܘܠܬܐ</span>‎‎), was, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a>, a site immediately outside <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_in_Christianity" title="Jerusalem in Christianity">Jerusalem's</a> walls where <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Jesus was crucified</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Golgotha(s)</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Γολγοθᾶς</span>) is the Greek transcription in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a> term <i><a href="/wiki/Language_of_Jesus" title="Language of Jesus">Gagultâ</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> translates the term to mean <i>place of [the] skull</i>, which in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> is Κρανίου Τόπος (<i>Kraníou Tópos</i>), and in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> is <i>Calvariæ Locus</i>, from which the English word <i>Calvary</i> is derived.</p>

<p>Golgotha is referred to in early writings as a hill resembling a skullcap located very near to a gate into Jerusalem: "A spot there is called Golgotha, – of old the fathers' earlier tongue thus called its name, 'The skull-pan of a head'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 6th century it has been referred to as the location of a mountain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup> and as a small hill since 333.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup> The Gospels describe it as a place near enough to the city that those coming in and out could read the inscription 'Jesus of Nazareth – King of the Jews'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a> was written, the translators used an anglicized version – <i>Calvary</i> – of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> gloss from the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> (<i>Calvariæ</i>), to refer to Golgotha in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a>, rather than translate it; subsequent uses of <i>Calvary</i> stem from this single translation decision. The location itself is mentioned in all four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical_Canon" title="Biblical Canon">canonical Gospels</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a>: And they brought him to the place called Gol'gotha (which means the place of a skull).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a>: And when they came to a place called Gol'gotha (which means the place of a skull).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a>: And when they came to the place which is called The Skull, there they crucified him, and the criminals, one on the right and one on the left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">John</a>: So they took Jesus, and he went out, bearing his own cross, to the place called the place of a skull, which is called in Hebrew Gol'gotha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douay-Rheims_Version" title="Douay-Rheims Version">Douay-Rheims Version</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Bible#Catholic_English_versions" title="Catholic Bible">Roman Catholic English translation</a> of the Bible), Luke 23:33 reads:</p>
<p>"And when they were come to the place which is called Calvary, they crucified him there; and the robbers, one on the right hand, and the other on the left."</p>
<p>The “place of a skull” (Aramaic: <i>gagûltâ</i> ܓܓܘܠܬܐ) etymology is based on the Hebrew verbal root <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Cardo, Shofar, David, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">גלל</span>‎ g-l-l, from which the Hebrew word for skull, <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Cardo, Shofar, David, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">גֻּלְגֹּלֶת</span>‎ (<i>gulgōleṯ</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is derived. A number of alternative explanations have been given for the name. It has been suggested that the Aramaic name is actually <i>Gol Go<b>a</b>tha</i>, meaning <i>mount of execution</i>, possibly the same location as the <i>Goatha</i> mentioned in a <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jeremiah" title="Book of Jeremiah">Book of Jeremiah</a> passage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> describing the geography of Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup> An alternative explanation is that the location was a place of public execution, and the name refers to abandoned skulls that would be found there,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup> or that the location was near a cemetery, and the name refers to the bones buried there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some Christian and Jewish traditions, the name <i>Golgotha</i> refers to the location of the skull of <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup> A common version states that <a href="/wiki/Shem" title="Shem">Shem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melchizedek" title="Melchizedek">Melchizedek</a> traveled to the resting place of <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Noah's Ark</a>, retrieved the body of Adam from it, and were led by Angels to Golgotha – described as a skull-shaped hill at the centre of the Earth, where also the serpent's head had been crushed following the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_man" title="Fall of man">fall of man</a>. This tradition appears in numerous older sources, including <a href="/wiki/Clementine_literature" title="Clementine literature">the <i>Kitab al-Magall</i></a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_Adam_and_Eve_with_Satan" title="Conflict of Adam and Eve with Satan">Conflict of Adam and Eve with Satan</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Treasures" title="Cave of Treasures">Cave of Treasures</a></i>, and the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Eutychius_of_Alexandria" title="Patriarch Eutychius of Alexandria">Patriarch Eutychius of Alexandria</a>. It is also suggested that the location's landscape resembled the shape of a skull, and gained its name for that reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-ce-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_location">Traditional location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditional location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The traditional location of Golgotha derives from its identification by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_of_Constantinople" title="Helena of Constantinople">Helena</a>, the mother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a>, in 325. A few yards nearby, Helena also identified the location of the tomb of Jesus and claimed to have discovered the <a href="/wiki/True_Cross" title="True Cross">True Cross</a>; her son, Constantine, then built the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Church of the Holy Sepulchre">Church of the Holy Sepulchre</a> around the whole site. In 333, the <a href="/wiki/Itinerarium_Burdigalense" title="Itinerarium Burdigalense">Pilgrim of Bordeaux</a>, entering from the east described the result:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>On the left hand is the little hill of Golgotha where the Lord was crucified. About a stone's throw from thence is a vault [crypta] wherein his body was laid, and rose again on the third day. There, at present, by the command of the Emperor Constantine, has been built a <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>; that is to say, a church of wondrous beauty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In Nazénie Garibian de Vartavan's doctoral thesis, now published as <i>La Jérusalem Nouvelle et les premiers sanctuaires chrétiens de l’Arménie. Méthode pour l’étude de l’église comme temple de Dieu,</i> she concluded, through multiple arguments (mainly theological and archaeological), that the true site of Golgotha was precisely at the vertical of the now buried Constantinian basilica's altar and away from where the traditional rock of Golgotha is situated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garibian_de_Vartavan_2008_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garibian_de_Vartavan_2008-12">[12]</a></sup> The plans published in the book indicate the location of the Golgotha within a precision of less than two meters, below the circular passage situated a metre away from where the blood stained shirt of Christ was traditionally recovered and immediately before the stairs leading down to "St. Helena's Chapel" (the above-mentioned mother of Emperor Constantine), alternatively called "St. Vartan's Chapel".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_to_Aphrodite">Temple to Aphrodite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Temple to Aphrodite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roman_Jerusalem.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="353" data-file-width="308" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Roman_Jerusalem.PNG/220px-Roman_Jerusalem.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/Roman_Jerusalem.PNG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roman_Jerusalem.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jerusalem after being rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>. Two main east-west roads were built rather than one</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to Helena's identification, the site had been a <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a> to <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>. Constantine's construction took over most of the site of the earlier temple enclosure, and the <i>Rotunda</i> and <a href="/wiki/Cloister" title="Cloister">cloister</a> (which was replaced after the 12th century by the present <i><a href="/wiki/Katholikon" title="Katholikon">Catholicon</a></i> and <i>Calvary chapel</i>) roughly overlap with the temple building itself; the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> church which Constantine built over the remainder of the enclosure was destroyed at the turn of the 11th century, and has not been replaced. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_Tradition" title="Sacred Tradition">Christian tradition</a> claims that the location had originally been a Christian place of veneration, but that Hadrian had deliberately buried these Christian sites and built his own temple on top, on account of his alleged hatred for Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is certainly evidence that circa 160 CE, at least as early as 30 years after <a href="/wiki/Aelia_Capitolina" title="Aelia Capitolina">Hadrian's temple</a> had been built, Christians associated it with the site of <i>Golgotha</i>; <a href="/wiki/Melito_of_Sardis" title="Melito of Sardis">Melito of Sardis</a>, an influential mid-2nd century bishop in the region, described the location as "in the middle of the street, in the middle of the city",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which matches the position of Hadrian's temple within the mid-2nd century city.</p>
<p>A typical Roman city was built according to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippodamus" title="Hippodamus">Hippodamian</a> <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">grid plan</a>; a North-South <a href="/wiki/Arterial_road" title="Arterial road">arterial road</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cardo" title="Cardo">Cardo</a> (which is now the Suq Khan-ez-Zeit), and an East-West arterial road, the <a href="/wiki/Decumanus_Maximus" title="Decumanus Maximus">Decumanus Maximus</a> (which is now the <a href="/wiki/Via_Dolorosa" title="Via Dolorosa">Via Dolorosa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warwick_Ball_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warwick_Ball-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Forum_(Roman)" title="Forum (Roman)">forum</a> would traditionally be located on the intersection of the two roads, with the main temples adjacent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warwick_Ball_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Warwick_Ball-15">[15]</a></sup> However, due to the obstruction posed by the Temple Mount, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Legio_X_Fretensis" title="Legio X Fretensis">Tenth Legion</a> encampment on the Western Hill, Hadrian's city had two <i>Cardo</i>, two <i>Decamanus Maximus</i>, two forums,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warwick_Ball_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Warwick_Ball-15">[15]</a></sup> and several temples. The Western Forum (now <a href="/wiki/Muristan" title="Muristan">Muristan</a>) is located on the crossroads of the West Cardo and what is now El-Bazar/David Street, with the Temple of Aphrodite adjacent, on the intersection of the Western Cardo and the Via Dolorossa. The Northern Forum is located north of the Temple Mount, on the junction of the Via Dolorossa and the Eastern Cardo, adjacent to the Temple of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_Capitolinus" title="Jupiter Capitolinus">Jupiter Capitolinus</a> intentionally built atop the Temple Mount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clermont-Ganneau_I_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clermont-Ganneau_I-16">[16]</a></sup> Another popular holy site that Hadrian converted to a pagan temple was the <a href="/wiki/Pool_of_Bethesda" title="Pool of Bethesda">Pool of Bethesda</a>, possibly referenced to in the fifth chapter of the Gospel of John,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MurphyOConnor_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-MurphyOConnor-18">[18]</a></sup> on which was built the Temple of <a href="/wiki/Asclepius" title="Asclepius">Asclepius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serapis" title="Serapis">Serapis</a>. While the positioning of the Temple of Aphrodite may be, in light of the common <a href="/wiki/Colonia_(Roman)" title="Colonia (Roman)">Colonia</a> layout, entirely unintentional, Hadrian is known to have concurrently built pagan temples on top of other holy sites in Jerusalem as part of an overall <a href="/wiki/Romanization_(cultural)" title="Romanization (cultural)">Romanization</a> policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sch.C3.A4fer2003_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sch.C3.A4fer2003-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erp-places_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-erp-places-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Archaeological excavations under the Church of the Holy Sepulchre have revealed Christian pilgrims' graffiti, dating from the period that the Temple of Aphrodite was still present, of a ship, a common early Christian symbol<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and the etching "DOMINVS IVIMVS", meaning "Lord, we went",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clermont-Ganneau_II_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clermont-Ganneau_II-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> lending possible support to the statement by Melito of Sardis asserting that early Christians identified Golgotha as being in the middle of Hadrian's city rather than outside.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_in_relation_to_city_walls">Location in relation to city walls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Location in relation to city walls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The New Testament describes the crucifixion site, <i>Golgotha</i>, as being "near the city" (John 19:20), and "outside the city wall" (Heb. 13:12). The traditionally identified location is in the heart of Hadrian's city, well within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerusalem%27s_Old_City_Walls" title="Jerusalem's Old City Walls">Jerusalem's Old City Walls</a>; there has therefore been some questioning of the legitimacy of the traditional identification on these grounds. Some defenders of this tradition have responded by citing Jewish history of the wall, that the city had been much narrower in Jesus' time, with the site then having been outside the walls; since <a href="/wiki/Herod_Agrippa" title="Herod Agrippa">Herod Agrippa</a> (41–44) is recorded by history as extending the city to the north (beyond the present northern walls), the required repositioning of the western wall is traditionally attributed to him as well. In 2003, Professor Sir Henry Chadwick (former Dean of <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Oxford" title="Christ Church, Oxford">Christ Church, Oxford</a>) argued that when Hadrian's builders replanned the old city, they "incidentally confirm[ed] the bringing of Golgotha inside a new town wall."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chadwick_2003_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chadwick_2003-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Protestant advocates of an alternative site claim that a wall would imply the existence of a defensive ditch outside it, so an earlier wall couldn't be immediately adjacent to the <i>Golgotha</i> site, which combined the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">Temple Mount</a> would make the city inside the wall quite thin; essentially for the traditional site to have been outside the wall, the city would have had to be limited to the lower parts of the <a href="/wiki/Tyropoeon_Valley" title="Tyropoeon Valley">Tyropoeon Valley</a>, rather than including the defensively advantageous western hill. Since these geographic considerations imply that not including the hill within the walls would be willfully making the city prone to attack from it, some scholars, including the late 19th century surveyors of the <a href="/wiki/Palestine_Exploration_Fund" title="Palestine Exploration Fund">Palestine Exploration Fund</a>, consider it unlikely that a wall would ever have been built which would cut the hill off from the city in the valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> However, in 2007 <a href="/wiki/Dan_Bahat" title="Dan Bahat">Dan Bahat</a>, the former City Archaeologist of Jerusalem and Professor of Land of Israel Studies at <a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_University" title="Bar-Ilan University">Bar-Ilan University</a>, stated that "Six graves from the first century were found on the area of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. That means, this place [was] outside of the city, without any doubt…",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZDF_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZDF-31">[31]</a></sup> casting doubt on the "Strategic Weakness" and "Defensive Ditch" hypotheses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rockface">Rockface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rockface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2731" data-file-width="2684" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg/220px-Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg/330px-Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg/440px-Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Golgotha_Stone_Chapel_of_Adam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Natural stone of Golgotha in the Chapel of Adam below site</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During 1973–1978 restoration works and excavations inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre and under the nearby <a href="/wiki/Muristan" title="Muristan">Muristan</a>, it was found that the area was originally a quarry, from which white Meleke <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> was struck;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesemann170_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hesemann170-32">[32]</a></sup> surviving parts of the quarry to the north-east of the chapel of St. Helena are now accessible from within the chapel (by permission). Inside the church is a rock, about 7 m long by 3 m wide by 4.8 m high,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesemann170_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hesemann170-32">[32]</a></sup> that is traditionally believed to be all that now remains visible of <i>Golgotha</i>; the design of the church means that the <i>Calvary Chapel</i> contains the upper foot or so of the rock, while the remainder is in the chapel beneath it (known as the <i>tomb of Adam</i>). <a href="/wiki/Virgilio_Canio_Corbo" title="Virgilio Canio Corbo">Virgilio Corbo</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> priest and archaeologist, present at the excavations, suggested that from the city the little hill (which still exists) could have looked like a skull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HesemannHill_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-HesemannHill-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a 1986 repair to the floor of the <i>Calvary Chapel</i> by the art historian George Lavas and architect Theo Mitropoulos, a round slot of 11.5 cm (4.5 in) diameter was discovered in the rock, partly open on one side (Lavas attributes the open side to accidental damage during his repairs);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lavas_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lavas-34">[34]</a></sup> although the dating of the slot is uncertain, and could date to Hadrian's temple of Aphrodite, Lavas suggested that it could have been the site of the crucifixion, as it would be strong enough to hold in place a wooden trunk of up to 2.5 metres (8 ft 2 in) in height (among other things).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The same restoration work also revealed a crack running across the surface of the rock, which continues down to the <i>Chapel of Adam</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lavas_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lavas-34">[34]</a></sup> the crack is thought by archaeologists to have been a result of the quarry workmen encountering a flaw in the rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on the late 20th century excavations of the site, there have been a number of attempted reconstructions of the profile of the cliff face. These often attempt to show the site as it would have appeared to Constantine. However, as the ground level in Roman times was about 4–5 feet (1.2–1.5 m) lower and the site housed Hadrian's temple to Aphrodite, much of the surrounding rocky slope must have been removed long before Constantine built the church on the site. The height of the <i>Golgotha</i> rock itself would have caused it to jut through the platform level of the Aphrodite temple, where it would be clearly visible. The reason for Hadrian not cutting the rock down is uncertain, but Virgilio Corbo suggested that a statue, probably of Aphrodite, was placed on it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> a suggestion also made by <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a>. Some archaeologists have been suggested that prior to Hadrian's use, the rock outcrop had been a <i>nefesh</i> - a Jewish funeral monument, equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilgrimages_to_Constantine.27s_Church">Pilgrimages to Constantine's Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pilgrimages to Constantine's Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="678" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg/220px-Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg/330px-Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg/440px-Jesus_in_Golgotha_by_Theophanes_the_Cretan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Itinerarium_Burdigalense" title="Itinerarium Burdigalense">Itinerarium Burdigalense</a> speaks of Golgotha in 333: "... On the left hand is the little hill of Golgotha where the Lord was crucified. About a stone's throw from thence is a vault (crypta) wherein His body was laid, and rose again on the third day. There, at present, by the command of the Emperor Constantine, has been built a basilica, that is to say, a church of wondrous beauty,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Jerusalem" title="Cyril of Jerusalem">Cyril of Jerusalem</a>, a distinguished theologian of the early Church, and eyewitness to the early days of Constantine's edifice, speaks of Golgotha in eight separate passages, sometimes as near to the church in which he and his listeners were assembled:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> "Golgotha, the holy hill standing above us here, bears witness to our sight: the Holy Sepulchre bears witness, and the stone which lies there to this day."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> And just in such a way the <a href="/wiki/Egeria_(pilgrim)" title="Egeria (pilgrim)">pilgrim Egeria</a> often reported in 383: "… the church, built by Constantine, which is situated in Golgotha …",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and also bishop <a href="/wiki/Eucherius_of_Lyon" title="Eucherius of Lyon">Eucherius of Lyon</a> wrote to the island presbyter Faustus in 440: "Golgotha is in the middle between the Anastasis and the Martyrium, the place of the Lord's passion, in which still appears that rock which once endured the very cross on which the Lord was.",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and Breviarius de Hierosolyma reports in 530: "From there (the middle of the basilica), you enter into Golgotha, where there is a large court. Here the Lord was crucified. All around that hill, there are silver screens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> (See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius</a> in 338<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_locations">Alternative locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calvary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alternative locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golgotha_photo.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Golgotha_photo.JPG/250px-Golgotha_photo.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Golgotha_photo.JPG/375px-Golgotha_photo.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Golgotha_photo.JPG/500px-Golgotha_photo.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Golgotha_photo.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rocky escarpment resembling a skull, located northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Church of the Holy Sepulchre">Church of the Holy Sepulchre</a>, near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_Tomb" title="Garden Tomb">Garden Tomb</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The traditional location of Calvary is not universally accepted. In 1842, heavily relying on the research of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Robinson_(scholar)" title="Edward Robinson (scholar)">Edward Robinson</a>, a German theologian and biblical scholar from Dresden named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Thenius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Otto Thenius (page does not exist)">Otto Thenius</a> was the first to publish a proposal that the rocky knoll north of Damascus Gate was the biblical <i>Golgotha</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golgotha_and_The_Holy_Sepulchre_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golgotha_and_The_Holy_Sepulchre-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 1882–83, Major-General <a href="/wiki/Charles_George_Gordon" title="Charles George Gordon">Charles George Gordon</a> endorsed this view, and subsequently the site has sometimes been known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon%27s_Calvary" title="Gordon's Calvary">Gordon's Calvary</a>. The location, usually referred to today as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skull_Hill" title="Skull Hill">Skull Hill</a></i>, is beneath a cliff which contains two large sunken holes, which Gordon regarded as resembling the eyes of a skull; he and a few others before him believed that the skull-like appearance would have caused the location to be known as Golgotha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nearby is an ancient rock-cut tomb known today as <a href="/wiki/The_Garden_Tomb" title="The Garden Tomb">the Garden Tomb</a> which Gordon proposed as the tomb of Jesus. The Garden Tomb contains several ancient burial places, although the archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Barkay" title="Gabriel Barkay">Gabriel Barkay</a> has proposed that the tomb dates to the 7th century BCE and that the site may have been abandoned by the 1st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> comments that Golgotha was in his day (the 4th century) pointed out <i>north of Mount Zion</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> While "Mount Zion" was used previously in reference to the Temple Mount itself, <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a>, the first-century CE historian who knew the city as it was before the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(AD_70)" title="Siege of Jerusalem (AD 70)">Roman destruction of Jerusalem</a>, identified Mount Zion as being the Western Hill (the current Mount Zion),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> which is south of both the Garden Tomb and the Holy Sepulchre.</p>
<p>Another alternate location has been proposed by Rodger Dusatko, a missionary in Germany. He claims that the location of Golgotha is just outside the <a href="/wiki/Lions%27_Gate" title="Lions' Gate">Lions' Gate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skulltotemple.jpg" title="The hill having the appearance of a cranium (the skull-pan of a head, approx. 200 meters northeast of where the curtain at the temple entrance once stood"><img alt="The hill having the appearance of a cranium (the skull-pan of a head, approx. 200 meters northeast of where the curtain at the temple entrance once stood" data-file-height="178" data-file-width="740" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/58/Skulltotemple.jpg" width="800"/></a></p>
<p>All four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a> use the Greek word 'Kranion' to describe the place where Jesus was crucified. Unlike Skufion (skull), Kranion (in English - cranium) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> is the upper part of the skull excluding the face bones.</p>
<p>The Gospels <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a> testify that as Jesus died the curtain of the temple ripped. The earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> Gospels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Diatessaron" title="Diatessaron">Diatessaron</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tatian" title="Tatian">Tatian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Fathers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> testify that it was the curtain at the entrance of the temple which ripped, and not the inner veil before the <a href="/wiki/Holy_of_Holies" title="Holy of Holies">Holy of Holies</a>.</p>
<p>And the curtain of the entrance of The Temple was ripped in two from the top unto the bottom. Mk 15,38 (Syriac Peshitta)</p>
<p>Since the temple faced east,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> the curtain would have been in direct view of those gathered on this mount at the northeast corner of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">Temple Mount</a>, just outside the city wall. And to testify that the curtain ripped at the very moment in which Jesus died,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> there must have been eyewitnesses.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a> refers to Golgotha as being very near the city, so near that all who passed by could read the inscription<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Jn+19%3A20&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[19:20]</a></sup>. Considering also the prophecy in Psalms 69:12<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ps+69%3A12&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[69:12]</a></sup>, his place of crucifixion would have been near enough to the gate that Jesus could hear what the people were saying about him there. And just as <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> comments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius%27_Onomasticon" title="Eusebius' Onomasticon">Onomasticon</a> concerning Golgotha as being a hill just outside Jerusalem, north of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion" title="Mount Zion">Mount Zion</a>, this hill fits his description.</p>

<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Calvary_(sanctuary)" title="Calvary (sanctuary)">Calvary</a></i> may mean also a set of religious edifices imitating <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, often constructed on hills. It functions as a sanctuary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_of_Christ" title="Passion of Christ">Passion of Christ</a> where <a href="/wiki/Mystery_play" title="Mystery play">Mystery plays</a> are held before <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, often with miraculous images of <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Sorrows" title="Our Lady of Sorrows">Our Lady of Sorrows</a> and sometimes the relics of <a href="/wiki/True_Cross" title="True Cross">True Cross</a>. This kind of shrine was especially popular in the age of <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> was under Turkish rule and it was difficult to pilgrimage to the Calvary in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The most popular is probably <a href="/wiki/Kalwaria_Zebrzydowska" title="Kalwaria Zebrzydowska">Kalwaria Zebrzydowska</a> near <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, but there are many more such complexes, often very large.</li>
<li>The name <i><a href="/wiki/Calvary_(sculpture)" title="Calvary (sculpture)">Calvary</a></i> often refers to sculptures or pictures representing the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">crucifixion</a> of Jesus, or a small <a href="/wiki/Wayside_shrine" title="Wayside shrine">wayside shrine</a> incorporating such a picture. It also can be used to describe larger, more monumentlike constructions, essentially artificial hills often built by devotees, especially a tradition in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> in France of large stone monuments.</li>
<li>Churches in various Christian denominations have been named Calvary. The name is also sometimes given to cemeteries, especially those associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>.</li>
<li>Two Catholic religious orders have been dedicated to Mount Calvary. Several places worldwide have been named after it; including the town <a href="/wiki/Kalvarija,_Lithuania" title="Kalvarija, Lithuania">Kalvarija</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and towns <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B3ra_Kalwaria" title="Góra Kalwaria">Góra Kalwaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalwaria_Zebrzydowska" title="Kalwaria Zebrzydowska">Kalwaria Zebrzydowska</a> in Poland.</li>
<li>In some regions in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, the words Kalvarija, Kalgorij, and Kalgarij are dialect names for <i>Put Križa</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Cross" title="Stations of the Cross">Stations of the Cross</a>.</li>
<li>In the 18th and early 19th centuries at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge universities</a> the rooms of the heads of colleges and halls were nicknamed <i>golgotha</i>. Apart from the pun on <i>the place of skulls</i> (i.e. heads), this was also due to the punishments that students received in these rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the Eastern Orthodox Church, a <i>Golgotha</i> is a representation (<a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a>) of the crucified Jesus, often with the <a href="/wiki/Theotokos" title="Theotokos">Theotokos</a> (Mother of God) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_the_Beloved_Disciple" title="John the Beloved Disciple">John the Beloved Disciple</a> standing to either side. This is used during Holy Week, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a> of Great Friday (Good Friday). During the rest of the year, and in the Slavic tradition, it may stand in the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> of the church, off to one side, and will be the place where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pannikhida" title="Pannikhida">Pannikhidas</a> (memorial services) will be chanted. In the Greek tradition, it remains in the sanctuary behind the holy altar.</li>
</ul>
