<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jewish_folklore" title="Jewish folklore">Jewish folklore</a>, a <b>golem</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/əm/ 'm' in 'rhythm'">əm</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">GOH</span></span></b>-ləm</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he">גולם</span>‎‎) is an animated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> being that is magically created entirely from inanimate matter (specifically clay or mud). The word was used to mean an amorphous, unformed material in <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most famous golem narrative involves <a href="/wiki/Judah_Loew_ben_Bezalel" title="Judah Loew ben Bezalel">Judah Loew ben Bezalel</a>, the late-16th-century <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. There are many tales differing on how the golem was brought to life and afterward controlled.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word <i>golem</i> occurs once in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> in <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt26d9.htm#16" rel="nofollow">Psalm 139:16</a>, which uses the word <span lang="he">גלמי</span> (<i>galmi</i>; my golem),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introduction_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Introduction-2">[2]</a></sup> that means "my light form", "raw" material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">[3]</a></sup> connoting the unfinished human being before God's eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introduction_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Introduction-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a> uses the term for an uncultivated person: "Seven characteristics are in an uncultivated person, and seven in a learned one," (שבעה דברים בגולם) (<a href="/wiki/Pirkei_Avot" title="Pirkei Avot">Pirkei Avot</a> 5:6 in the Hebrew text; English translations vary). In <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern Hebrew</a>, <i>golem</i> is used to mean "dumb" or "helpless". Similarly, it is often used today as a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> for a brainless lunk or entity who serves man under controlled conditions but is hostile to him under others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> "Golem" passed into <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a> as <i>goylem</i> to mean someone who is clumsy or slow.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_stories">Earliest stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Earliest stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The oldest stories of golems date to early Judaism. In the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> (Tractate <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_(tractate)" title="Sanhedrin (tractate)">Sanhedrin</a> 38b), <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a> was initially created as a golem (גולם) when his dust was "kneaded into a shapeless husk". Like Adam, all golems are <a href="/wiki/Creation_of_man_from_clay" title="Creation of man from clay">created from mud</a> by those close to divinity, but no anthropogenic golem is fully human. Early on, the main disability of the golem was its inability to speak. <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_(tractate)" title="Sanhedrin (tractate)">Sanhedrin</a> 65b describes <a href="/wiki/Rava_(amora)" title="Rava (amora)">Rava</a> creating a man (<i>gavra</i>). He sent the man to <a href="/wiki/Rav_Zeira" title="Rav Zeira">Rav Zeira</a>. Rav Zeira spoke to him, but he did not answer. Rav Zeira said, "You were created by the sages; return to your dust".</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, passages from the <i><a href="/wiki/Sefer_Yetzirah" title="Sefer Yetzirah">Sefer Yetzirah</a></i> (<i>Book of Creation</i>) were studied as a means to create and animate a golem, although there is little in the writings of Jewish mysticism that supports this belief. It was believed that golems could be activated by an <a href="/wiki/Ecstasy_(emotion)" title="Ecstasy (emotion)">ecstatic</a> experience induced by the ritualistic use of various letters of the Hebrew Alphabet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup> forming a "<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shemhamphorasch" title="Shemhamphorasch">shem</a></i>" (any one of the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_Judaism" title="Names of God in Judaism">Names of God</a>), wherein the <i>shem</i> was written on a piece of paper and inserted in the mouth or in the forehead of the golem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A golem is inscribed with Hebrew words in some tales (for example, some versions of Chełm and Prague, as well as in Polish tales and versions of <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>), such as the word <i>emet</i> (אמת, "truth" in Hebrew) written on its forehead. The golem could then be deactivated by removing the aleph (א) in <i>emet</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> thus changing the inscription from "truth" to "death" (<i>met</i> מת, meaning "dead"). Other versions add that an entity of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> would be brought to life by placing into his mouth a <i>shem</i> with a magic formula, and it could later be immobilized by pulling out the <i>shem</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legend_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legend-6">[6]</a></sup> or by reversing the creative combinations. Rabbi Jacob ben Shalom arrived at <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> from Germany in 1325 and remarked that the law of destruction is the reversal of the law of creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Delmedigo" title="Joseph Delmedigo">Joseph Delmedigo</a> informs us in 1625 that "many legends of this sort are current, particularly in Germany".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magic_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-magic-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest known written account of how to create a golem can be found in <i>Sodei Razayya</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleazar_ben_Judah" title="Eleazar ben Judah">Eleazar ben Judah</a> of <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a> (1165–1230).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Golem_of_Che.C5.82m">The Golem of Chełm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Golem of Chełm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Ba%27al_Shem_of_Chelm" title="Elijah Ba'al Shem of Chelm">Elijah Ba'al Shem of Chelm</a></div>
<p>The oldest description of the creation of a golem by a historical figure is included in a tradition connected to <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Ba%27al_Shem_of_Chelm" title="Elijah Ba'al Shem of Chelm">Rabbi Eliyahu</a> of <a href="/wiki/Che%C5%82m" title="Chełm">Chełm</a> (1550–1583).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introduction_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Introduction-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magic_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-magic-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelbin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gelbin-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalist</a>, writing in about 1630–1650, reported the creation of a golem by Rabbi Eliyahu thus: "And I have heard, in a certain and explicit way, from several respectable persons that one man [living] close to our time, whose name is R. Eliyahu, the master of the name, who made a creature out of matter [Heb. <i>Golem</i>] and form [Heb. <i>tzurah</i>] and it performed hard work for him, for a long period, and the name of <i>emet</i> was hanging upon his neck, until he finally removed it for a certain reason, the name from his neck and it turned to dust."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup> A similar account was reported by a Christian author, Christoph Arnold, in 1674.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Emden" title="Jacob Emden">Jacob Emden</a> (d. 1776) elaborated on the story in a book published in 1748: "As an aside, I'll mention here what I heard from my father's holy mouth regarding the Golem created by his ancestor, the Gaon R. Eliyahu Ba'al Shem of blessed memory. When the Gaon saw that the Golem was growing larger and larger, he feared that the Golem would destroy the universe. He then removed the Holy Name that was embedded on his forehead, thus causing him to disintegrate and return to dust. Nonetheless, while he was engaged in extracting the Holy Name from him, the Golem injured him, scarring him on the face."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Polish Kabbalist, "the legend was known to several persons, thus allowing us to speculate that the legend had indeed circulated for some time before it was committed to writing and, consequently, we may assume that its origins are to be traced to the generation immediately following the death of R. Eliyahu, if not earlier."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_classic_narrative:_The_Golem_of_Prague">The classic narrative: The Golem of Prague</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The classic narrative: The Golem of Prague">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The most famous golem narrative involves <a href="/wiki/Judah_Loew_ben_Bezalel" title="Judah Loew ben Bezalel">Judah Loew ben Bezalel</a>, the late 16th century rabbi of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, also known as the Maharal, who reportedly created a golem to defend the Prague <a href="/wiki/Ghetto" title="Ghetto">ghetto</a> from <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitic</a> attacks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pogrom" title="Pogrom">pogroms</a>. Depending on the version of the legend, the Jews in Prague were to be either expelled or killed under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf II, the Holy Roman Emperor</a>. To protect the Jewish community, the rabbi constructed the Golem out of clay from the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Vltava" title="Vltava">Vltava</a> river, and brought it to life through rituals and Hebrew incantations. The Golem was called Josef and was known as Yossele. It was said that he could make himself invisible and summon spirits from the dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup> The only care required of the Golem was that he couldn't be active on the day of Sabbath (Saturday).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legend_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Legend-6">[6]</a></sup> Rabbi Loew deactivated the Golem on Friday evenings by removing the <i>shem</i> before the Sabbath began,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legend_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Legend-6">[6]</a></sup> so as to let it rest on Sabbath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup> One Friday evening Rabbi Loew forgot to remove the <i>shem</i>, and feared that the Golem would desecrate the Sabbath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup> A different story tells of a golem that fell in love, and when rejected, became the violent monster seen in most accounts. Some versions have the golem eventually going on a murderous rampage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rabbi then managed to pull the <i>shem</i> from his mouth and immobilize him<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legend_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Legend-6">[6]</a></sup> in front of the synagogue, whereupon the golem fell in pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup> The Golem's body was stored in the attic <i><a href="/wiki/Genizah" title="Genizah">genizah</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_New_Synagogue" title="Old New Synagogue">Old New Synagogue</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup> where it would be restored to life again if needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legend_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Legend-6">[6]</a></sup> According to legend, the body of Rabbi Loew's Golem still lies in the synagogue's attic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup> When the attic was renovated in 1883, no evidence of the Golem was found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup> Some versions of the tale state that the Golem was stolen from the <i>genizah</i> and entombed in a graveyard in <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDi%C5%BEkov" title="Žižkov">Prague's Žižkov district</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDi%C5%BEkov_Television_Tower" title="Žižkov Television Tower">Žižkov Television Tower</a> now stands. A recent legend tells of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> agent ascending to the synagogue attic during World War II and trying to stab the Golem, but he died instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A film crew who visited and filmed the attic in 1984 found no evidence either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup> The attic is not open to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some strictly orthodox Jews believe that the Maharal did actually create a golem. Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Mendel_Schneerson" title="Menachem Mendel Schneerson">Menachem Mendel Schneerson</a> (the last <a href="/wiki/Rebbe" title="Rebbe">Rebbe</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lubavitch" title="Lubavitch">Lubavitch</a>) wrote that his father-in-law Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Yitzchak_Schneersohn" title="Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn">Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn</a> was asked about his experiences visiting the attic of the <a href="/wiki/Old_New_Synagogue" title="Old New Synagogue">Old New Synagogue</a>, and he said that he was unwilling to speak about it. Yosef Yitzchak wrote in his memoirs that he visited the <a href="/wiki/Old_New_Synagogue" title="Old New Synagogue">Old New Synagogue</a>'s attic, and his father was very grave when he descended back to the ground floor, saying that he had recited psalms for his safety while he visited the attic. Shnayer L. Leiman writes in an article that Yosef Yitzchak's daughter, Chana Gurary (<a href="/wiki/Barry_Gurary" title="Barry Gurary">Barry Gurary</a>'s mother), related to Rabbi Berel Junik that her father, had seen "[the] form of a man wrapped up and covered. The body was lying on its side," and that he said that he was "very frightened by this sight. I looked around at some of the shemus (discarded ritual objects) that were there and left frightened by what I had seen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Rabbi Chaim Noach Levin also wrote in his notes on <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Megillas_Yuchsin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Megillas Yuchsin (page does not exist)">Megillas Yuchsin</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> that he heard directly from Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Saul_Nathansohn" title="Joseph Saul Nathansohn">Yosef Shaul Halevi</a>, the head of the Rabbinical court of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lemberg" title="Lemberg">Lemberg</a>, that he wanted to go see the remains of the Golem but the sexton of the Alt-Neu Shul said that Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yechezkel_Landau" title="Yechezkel Landau">Yechezkel Landau</a> had advised against going up to the attic after he himself had gone up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The evidence for this belief has been analyzed from an orthodox Jewish perspective by Shnayer Z. Leiman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tradition_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-tradition-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_the_Prague_narrative">Sources of the Prague narrative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sources of the Prague narrative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The general view of historians and critics is that the story of the Golem of Prague was a German literary invention of the early 19th century. According to Robert Zucker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> "the golem legend about R. Chełm moved to Prague and became related with" Rabbi Loew of Prague about mid-18th century. According to John Neubauer, the first writers on the Prague Golem were:</p>
<ul>
<li>1837: <a href="/wiki/Berthold_Auerbach" title="Berthold Auerbach">Berthold Auerbach</a>, <i>Spinoza</i></li>
<li>1841: Gustav Philippson, <i>Der Golam, eine Legende</i></li>
<li>1841: Franz Klutschak, <i>Der Golam des Rabbi Löw</i></li>
<li>1842: Adam Tendlau <i>Der Golem des Hoch-Rabbi-Löw</i></li>
<li>1847: Leopold Weisel, <i>Der Golem</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neubauer_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-neubauer-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There is also a published account from 1838, written by the German Czech journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Franz_Klutschack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Franz Klutschack (page does not exist)">Franz Klutschack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Cathy Gelbin finds an earlier source in Philippson's <i>The Golem and the Adulteress</i>, published in the Jewish magazine <i>Shulamit</i> in 1834, which describes how the Maharal sent a golem to find the reason for an epidemic among the Jews of Prague,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelbin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gelbin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> although doubts have been expressed as to whether this date is correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The earliest known source for the story thus far is the 1834 book <i>Der Jüdische Gil Blas</i> by Josef Seligman Kohn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The story was repeated in <i>Gallerie der Sippurim</i> (1847), an influential collection of Jewish tales published by <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Pascheles" title="Wolf Pascheles">Wolf Pascheles</a> of Prague.</p>
<p>All these early accounts of the Golem of Prague are in German by Jewish writers. It has been suggested that they emerged as part of a Jewish <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> movement parallel with the contemporary German folklore movement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelbin_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-gelbin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-golems_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-golems-27">[27]</a></sup> and that they may have been based on Jewish oral tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-golems_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-golems-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origins of the story have been obscured by attempts to exaggerate its age and to pretend that it dates from the time of the Maharal. It has been said that Rabbi Yudel Rosenberg (1859–1935)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Tar%C5%82%C3%B3w" title="Tarłów">Tarłów</a> (before moving to Canada where he became one of its most prominent rabbis) originated the idea that the narrative dates from the time of the Maharal. Rosenberg published <i>Nifl'os Maharal</i> (<i>Wonders of Maharal</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Piotrk%C3%B3w_Trybunalski" title="Piotrków Trybunalski">Piotrków</a>, 1909)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which purported to be an eyewitness account by the Maharal's son-in-law, who had helped to create the Golem. Rosenberg claimed that the book was based upon a manuscript that he found in the main library in Metz. <i>Wonders of Maharal</i> "is generally recognized in academic circles to be a literary hoax".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idel_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-idel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tradition_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-tradition-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gershom_Sholem" title="Gershom Sholem">Gershom Sholem</a> observed that the manuscript "contains not ancient legends but modern fiction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Rosenberg's claim was further disseminated in Chayim Bloch's (1881–1973) <i>The Golem, legends of the Ghetto of Prague</i> (English edition 1925).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Encyclopedia" title="Jewish Encyclopedia">Jewish Encyclopedia</a> of 1906 cites the historical work <i>Zemach David</i> by <a href="/wiki/David_Gans" title="David Gans">David Gans</a>, a disciple of the Maharal, published in 1592.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewishencyclopedia_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-jewishencyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In it, Gans writes of an audience between the Maharal and Rudolph II: "Our lord the emperor ... Rudolph ... sent for and called upon our master Rabbi Low ben Bezalel and received him with a welcome and merry expression, and spoke to him face to face, as one would to a friend. The nature and quality of their words are mysterious, sealed and hidden."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> But it has been said of this passage, "Even when [the Maharal is] eulogized, whether in David Gans' <i>Zemach David</i> or on his epitaph …, not a word is said about the creation of a golem. No Hebrew work published in the 16th, 17th, and 18th centuries (even in Prague) is aware that the Maharal created a golem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neubauer_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-neubauer-21">[21]</a></sup> Furthermore, the Maharal himself did not refer to the Golem in his writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup> Rabbi Yedidiah Tiah Weil (1721–1805), a Prague resident, who described the creation of golems, including those created by Rabbis Avigdor Kara of Prague (died 1439) and Eliyahu of Chelm, did not mention the Maharal, and Rabbi Meir Perels' biography of the Maharal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-megilasYuchsin_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-megilasYuchsin-34">[34]</a></sup> published in 1718 does not mention a golem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gelbin_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-gelbin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leiman_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-leiman-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Golem_of_Vilna">The Golem of Vilna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Golem of Vilna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a similar tradition relating to the <a href="/wiki/Vilna_Gaon" title="Vilna Gaon">Vilna Gaon</a> or "the saintly genius from Vilnius" (1720–1797). Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaim_Volozhin" title="Chaim Volozhin">Chaim Volozhin</a> (Lithuania 1749–1821) reported in an introduction to <i>Siphra Dzeniouta</i> (1818)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> that he once presented to his teacher, the Vilna Gaon, ten different versions of a certain passage in the <i>Sefer Yetzira</i> and asked the Gaon to determine the correct text. The Gaon immediately identified one version as the accurate rendition of the passage. The amazed student then commented to his teacher that, with such clarity, he should easily be able to create a live human. The Gaon affirmed Rabbi Chaim's assertion, and said that he once began to create a person when he was a child, under the age of 13, but during the process he received a sign from Heaven ordering him to desist because of his tender age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> (See also discussion in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Ludwig_Held&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hans Ludwig Held (page does not exist)">Hans Ludwig Held</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Ludwig_Held" title="de:Hans Ludwig Held">de</a>)</span>, <i>Das Gespenst des Golem, eine Studie aus d. hebräischen Mystik mit einem Exkurs über das Wesen des Doppelgängers</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> München 1927.) The Vilna Gaon wrote an extensive commentary on the <i>Sefer Yetzira</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Kol_HaTor" title="Kol HaTor">Kol HaTor</a></i>, in which it is said that he had tried to create a Golem to fight the power of evil at the Gates of Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> As far as we know, the Vilna Gaon was the only rabbi who had actually claimed that he tried to create a Golem; all such stories about other rabbis were told after their time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hubris_theme">Hubris theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hubris theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The existence of a golem is sometimes a mixed blessing. Golems are not intelligent, and if commanded to perform a task, they will perform the instructions literally. In many depictions Golems are inherently perfectly obedient. In its earliest known modern form, the Golem of Chełm became enormous and uncooperative. In one version of this story, the rabbi had to resort to trickery to deactivate it, whereupon it crumbled upon its creator and crushed him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introduction_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Introduction-2">[2]</a></sup> There is a similar <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a> theme in <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sorcerer%27s_Apprentice" title="The Sorcerer's Apprentice">The Sorcerer's Apprentice</a></i>, and some other golem-derived stories in popular culture, for example: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i>. The theme also manifests itself in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.U.R._(Rossum%27s_Universal_Robots)" title="R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots)">R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots)</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Karel_%C4%8Capek" title="Karel Čapek">Karel Čapek</a>'s 1921 play which coined the term <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robot</a>; the play was written in Prague, and while Čapek denied that he modeled the robot after the Golem, there are many similarities in the plot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_of_the_Czech_Republic">Culture of the Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture of the Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Golem is a popular figure in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>. There are several restaurants and other businesses whose names make reference to the creature, a Czech <a href="/wiki/Strongman_(strength_athlete)" title="Strongman (strength athlete)">strongman</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Richter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="René Richter (page does not exist)">René Richter</a>) goes by the nickname "Golem",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlife_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-newlife-12">[12]</a></sup> and a Czech <a href="/wiki/Monster_truck" title="Monster truck">monster truck</a> outfit calls itself the "Golem Team".</p>
<p>Abraham Akkerman preceded his article on human automatism in the contemporary city with a short satirical poem on a pair of golems turning human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clay_Boy_variation">Clay Boy variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Clay Boy variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Yiddish and Slavic folktale is the Clay Boy, which combines elements of the Golem and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gingerbread_Man" title="The Gingerbread Man">The Gingerbread Man</a></i>, in which a lonely couple make a child out of clay, with disastrous or comical consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In one common Russian version, an older couple whose children have left home make a boy out of clay, and dry him by their hearth. The Clay Boy comes to life; at first the couple are delighted and treat him like a real child, but the Clay Boy does not stop growing, and eats all their food, then all their livestock, and then the Clay Boy eats his parents. The Clay Boy rampages through the village until he is smashed by a quick-thinking <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Golem_in_the_20th_and_21st_centuries">Golem in the 20th and 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Golem in the 20th and 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Mainstream European society adopted the golem in the early 20th century. Most notably, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Meyrink" title="Gustav Meyrink">Gustav Meyrink</a>'s 1914 novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Golem_(Meyrink)" title="The Golem (Meyrink)">Der Golem</a></i> is loosely inspired by the tales of the golem created by <a href="/wiki/Judah_Loew_ben_Bezalel" title="Judah Loew ben Bezalel">Rabbi Loew</a>. Another famous treatment from the same era is <a href="/wiki/H._Leivick" title="H. Leivick">H. Leivick</a>'s 1921 Yiddish-language "dramatic poem in eight sections", <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golem_(Leivick)" title="The Golem (Leivick)">The Golem</a></i>. In 1923, Romanian composer Nicolae Bretan wrote the one-act opera <i>The Golem,</i> first performed the following year in Cluj and later revived in Denver, Colorado, US in 1990. Nobel prize winner <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Bashevis_Singer" title="Isaac Bashevis Singer">Isaac Bashevis Singer</a> also wrote a version of the legend,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Elie_Wiesel" title="Elie Wiesel">Elie Wiesel</a> wrote a children's book on the legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974 <a href="/wiki/CBS_Radio_Mystery_Theater" title="CBS Radio Mystery Theater">CBS Radio Mystery Theater</a> aired an episode entitled <i>The Golem</i>, which takes place during the Holocaust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> published three <i>Strange Tales</i> comic books that included a golem character, and later series included variations of the golem idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marge_Piercy" title="Marge Piercy">Marge Piercy</a>'s 1991 <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke_Award" title="Arthur C. Clarke Award">Arthur C. Clarke Award</a>-winning novel <a href="/wiki/He,_She_and_It" title="He, She and It">He, She and It</a> features a substantial subplot that retells the story of Rabbi Loew and his golem. The novels of <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a> in the fictional setting of <a href="/wiki/Discworld" title="Discworld">Discworld</a> also include several golems as characters. They are introduced in the 19th Discworld novel, "Feet of Clay" (1996).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Ozick" title="Cynthia Ozick">Cynthia Ozick</a>'s 1997 novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Puttermesser_Papers" title="The Puttermesser Papers">The Puttermesser Papers</a>,</i> a modern Jewish woman, Ruth Puttermesser, creates a female golem out of the dirt in her flowerpots to serve as the daughter she never had. The golem helps Puttermesser become elected Mayor of New York before it begins to run out of control.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pete_Hamill" title="Pete Hamill">Pete Hamill</a>'s 1997 novel <i>Snow In August</i> includes a story of a rabbi from Prague who has a golem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1997 <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i> episode, "<a href="/wiki/Kaddish_(The_X-Files)" title="Kaddish (The X-Files)">Kaddish</a>", features a golem as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Monster-of-the-Week_characters_in_The_X-Files" title="List of Monster-of-the-Week characters in The X-Files">Monster of the Week</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Chabon" title="Michael Chabon">Michael Chabon</a>'s 2000 novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Adventures_of_Kavalier_%26_Clay" title="The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier &amp; Clay">The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier &amp; Clay</a></i>, features one of the protagonists, escape artist Josef Kavalier, smuggling himself out of Prague along with the golem. Petrie describes the theme of escape in the novel, culminating in Kavalier's own drawing of a modern <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> centered on a golem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/James_Sturm" title="James Sturm">James Sturm</a>'s 2001 graphic novel <i>The Golem's Mighty Swing</i>, a Jewish baseball team in the 1920s creates a golem to help them win their games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Michael_Scott_(Irish_author)" title="Michael Scott (Irish author)">Michael Scott</a> novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Alchemyst" title="The Alchemyst">The Alchemyst</a></i>, the immortal Dr. John Dee attacked Nicholas Flamel with two golems, which, along with being made of mud, each had a pair of shiny stone "eyes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Stroud" title="Jonathan Stroud">Jonathan Stroud</a>'s children's fantasy book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golem%27s_Eye" title="The Golem's Eye">The Golem's Eye</a></i> centers on a golem created by magicians in an alternate London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The story depicts the golem as being impervious to magical attacks. The golem is finally destroyed by removing the creation parchment from its mouth.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Byron_Sherwin" title="Byron Sherwin">Byron L. Sherwin's</a> 2006 novel <i>The Cubs and the Kabbalist</i>, rabbis create a golem named Sandy Greenberg to help baseball's Chicago Cubs win the World Series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, medieval mystery author <a href="/wiki/Jeri_Westerson" title="Jeri Westerson">Jeri Westerson</a>, depicted her version of a golem terrifying the streets of fourteenth century London in the third book of her Crispin Guest series, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Demon%27s_Parchment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Demon's Parchment (page does not exist)">The Demon's Parchment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daniel Handler's 2002 novel Watch Your Mouth explores the possibility that a modern-day wife and mom is creating a golem in the basement of her family home. Publishers Weekly wrote of the book: "After the opera-melodrama's weird but tantalizing climax, involving death and the golem myth, the novel actually recovers its narrative balance as the psychologically scarred Joseph turns to New Age recovery paperbacks, which replace opera as Handler's satiric model."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inspired by Gustav Meyrink's novel was a classic set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expressionism_(film)" title="Expressionism (film)">expressionistic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silent_movies" title="Silent movies">silent movies</a> (1915–1920), <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wegener" title="Paul Wegener">Paul Wegener</a>'s <i>Golem</i> series, of which <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golem:_How_He_Came_into_the_World" title="The Golem: How He Came into the World">The Golem: How He Came into the World</a></i> (also released as <i>The Golem</i>, 1920, US 1921: the only surviving film of the trilogy) is especially famous. In the first film the golem is revived in modern times before falling from a tower and breaking apart. Also notable is <a href="/wiki/Julien_Duvivier" title="Julien Duvivier">Julien Duvivier</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Golem" title="Le Golem">Le Golem</a></i> (1936), a French/Czechoslovakian sequel to the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wegener" title="Paul Wegener">Wegener</a> film.</p>
<p>Golems appear in the fantasy role-playing game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dungeons_and_Dragons" title="Dungeons and Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> (first published in the 1970s), and the influence of <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> has led to the inclusion of golems in other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a> and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role-playing_game_(pen_and_paper)" title="Role-playing game (pen and paper)">tabletop role-playing games</a>.</p>
<p>The 2016 <i><a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex:_Mankind_Divided" title="Deus Ex: Mankind Divided">Deus Ex: Mankind Divided</a></i> is set in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and features a section of the city which is nicknames "Golem City", being a ghetto for mechanically augmented people. The augmented were placed in isolation after an event in which many of them suffered a breakdown and turned on "normal" humans; this most likely led to the place's nickname, as the "golem that turned on its master".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
