<p><b>Goliath</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" style="white-space:nowrap;" xml:lang="he">גָּלְיָת</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern</a></small> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="he transliteration">Golyat</span></i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Tiberian_vocalization" title="Tiberian vocalization">Tiberian</a></small> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Hebrew transliteration">Golyāṯ</span></i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: جالوت, <i>Ǧālūt</i> <small>(Qur'anic term)</small>, جليات <i>Ǧulyāt</i> <small>(Christian term)</small>) of <a href="/wiki/Gath_(city)" title="Gath (city)">Gath</a> (one of five city states of the Philistines) is a Bible character described as a <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giant</a> <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistine</a> warrior defeated in one version by the young <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>, the future king of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a>, according to a Biblical story told in the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">Books of Samuel</a> (1 Samuel 17), and in 2 Samuel 21:19 by "<a href="/wiki/Elhanan_son_of_Jair" title="Elhanan son of Jair">Elhanan the son of Jaare-oregim</a>, the Bethlehemite".</p>
<p>Post-Classical Jewish traditions stressed Goliath's status as the representative of <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">paganism</a>, in contrast to David, the champion of the <a href="/wiki/God_in_Judaism" title="God in Judaism">God of Israel</a>. Christian tradition gave him a distinctively Christian perspective, seeing in David's battle with Goliath the victory of God's king over the enemies of God's helpless people as a prefiguring of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>' victory over sin on the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">cross</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Church</a>'s victory over <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phrase "David and Goliath" has taken on a more secular meaning, denoting an <a href="/wiki/Underdog_(term)" title="Underdog (term)">underdog</a> situation, a contest where a smaller, weaker opponent faces a much bigger, stronger adversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Goliath_narrative_in_1_Samuel_17">The Goliath narrative in 1 Samuel 17</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Goliath narrative in 1 Samuel 17">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One account of the battle is between David and Goliath, as told in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Samuel" title="1 Samuel">1 Samuel</a>, chapter 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saul" title="Saul">Saul</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a> are facing the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Elah" title="Valley of Elah">Valley of Elah</a>. Another account is found in 2 Samuel 21:19, which tells how Goliath the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gittite" title="Gittite">Gittite</a> was killed by "<a href="/wiki/Elhanan_son_of_Jair" title="Elhanan son of Jair">Elhanan the son of Jaare-oregim</a>, the Bethlehemite."</p>
<p>In the David and Goliath version, twice a day for 40 days, Goliath, the champion of the Philistines, comes out between the lines and challenges the Israelites to send out a champion of their own to decide the outcome in <a href="/wiki/Single_combat#Single_combat" title="Single combat">single combat</a>, but Saul and all the Israelites are afraid. <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>, bringing food for his elder brothers, hears that Goliath had defied the armies of God and of the reward from Saul to the one that defeats him, and accepts the challenge. Saul reluctantly agrees and offers his armor, which David declines, taking only his staff, <a href="/wiki/Sling_(weapon)" title="Sling (weapon)">sling</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">קָלַ֗ע</span>‎‎ <i>qāla‘</i>) and five stones from a brook.</p>
<p>David and Goliath confront each other, Goliath with his armor and javelin, David with his staff and sling. "The Philistine cursed David by his gods", but David replies: "This day the L<span class="smallcaps" style="font-size:83%">ORD</span> will deliver you into my hand, and I will strike you down; and I will give the dead bodies of the host of the Philistines this day to the birds of the air and to the wild beasts of the earth; that all the earth may know that there is a God in Israel, and that all this assembly may know that God saves not with sword and spear; for the battle is God's, and he will give you into our hand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>David hurls a stone from his sling with all his might and hits Goliath in the center of his forehead, Goliath falls on his face to the ground, and David cuts off his head. The Philistines flee and are pursued by the Israelites "as far as Gath and the gates of <a href="/wiki/Ekron" title="Ekron">Ekron</a>". David puts the armor of Goliath in his own tent and takes the head to Jerusalem, and Saul sends <a href="/wiki/Abner" title="Abner">Abner</a> to bring the boy to him. The king asks whose son he is, and David answers, "I am the son of your servant <a href="/wiki/Jesse" title="Jesse">Jesse</a> the <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehemite</a>."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_of_the_Book_of_Samuel_and_the_Goliath_narrative">Composition of the Book of Samuel and the Goliath narrative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition of the Book of Samuel and the Goliath narrative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Samuel" title="Book of Samuel">Book of Samuel</a>, together with the books of Joshua, Judges and Kings, make up a unified history of Israel stretching from the entry into Canaan to the early Babylonian exile of the 6th century BCE, which biblical scholars call the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomistic_history" title="Deuteronomistic history">Deuteronomistic history</a>. The first edition of the history was probably written at the court of Judah's King <a href="/wiki/Josiah" title="Josiah">Josiah</a> (late 7th century) and a revised second edition during the exile (6th century), with further revisions in the post-exilic period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien20002_and_fn6_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien20002_and_fn6-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerson201010.E2.80.9311_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerson201010.E2.80.9311-6">[6]</a></sup> The Goliath story contains the traces of this in its many contradictions and illogicalities - to take a few examples, Saul finds it necessary to send for David when as the king's shield-bearer he should already be beside his royal master, and he has to ask who David is, which sits strangely with David's status at his court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien2000262_fn62_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien2000262_fn62-7">[7]</a></sup> These signs indicate that the Goliath story is made up of base-narrative with numerous additions made probably after the exile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien2000259-260_fn58_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbellO.27Brien2000259-260_fn58-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Original story</i></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The Israelites and Philistines face each other; Goliath makes his challenge to single combat;</li>
<li>David volunteers to fight Goliath;</li>
<li>David defeats Goliath, the Philistines flee the battlefield.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><i>Additions</i></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>David is sent by his father to bring food to his brothers, hears the challenge, and expresses his desire to accept;</li>
<li>Details of the account of the battle;</li>
<li>Saul asks who David is, and he is introduced to the king through Abner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson201510-11_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson201510-11-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Two_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Two-10">[Note 1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Textual_considerations">Textual considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Textual considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goliath.27s_height">Goliath's height</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Goliath's height">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Goliath's stature as described in various ancient manuscripts varies: the oldest manuscripts—the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a> text of Samuel, the 1st century historian <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a>, and the 4th century Septuagint manuscripts—all give his height as "four <a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">cubits</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Span_(unit)" title="Span (unit)">span</a>" (6 feet 9 inches or 2.06 metres) whereas the <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">Masoretic Text</a> gives this as "six cubits and a span" (9 feet 9 inches or 2.97 metres; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">שֵׁ֥שׁ אַמֹּ֖ות וָזָֽרֶת</span>‎‎ <i>šêš ’ammōṯ wā-zāreṯ</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2005_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2005-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goliath.27s_injury_and_fall">Goliath's injury and fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Goliath's injury and fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The biblical account describes Goliath as falling on his face after he is struck by a stone that sank into his forehead. British rabbi <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Magonet" title="Jonathan Magonet">Jonathan Magonet</a> has discussed some of the textual difficulties this raises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> In the first place, he notes that archaeological information suggests that Philistine helmets generally had a forehead covering, in some cases extending down to the nose. Why (he asks) should David aim at such an impenetrable spot (and how did it hit with such force to penetrate thick bone)? And why should Goliath fall forward when struck by something heavy enough to stop him, rather than backwards? An answer to both questions, Magonet suggests, lies in the Hebrew word <i>meitzach</i> (מֵ֫צַח <i>mêṣaḥ</i>), normally translated forehead. A word almost identical with it appears earlier in the passage—the word <i>mitzchat</i> (מִצְחַת <i>miṣḥaṯ</i>), translated as "greaves"—the flexible leg-armour that protected Goliath's lower leg (see I Samuel 17: 6). It is possible, grammatically in the passage, for the same word to be used in verse 49, a reconstruction of which, replacing מֵ֫צַח with מִצְחַת, would imply that the stone sank down behind Goliath's leg-armour (as his leg was bent), making it impossible for him to straighten his leg, and causing him to stumble and fall. Then David removed the head of Goliath to show all that the giant was killed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elhanan_and_Goliath">Elhanan and Goliath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Elhanan and Goliath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Goliath makes another appearance in 2 Samuel 21:19, which tells how Goliath the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gittite" title="Gittite">Gittite</a> was killed by "<a href="/wiki/Elhanan_son_of_Jair" title="Elhanan son of Jair">Elhanan the son of Jaare-oregim</a>, the Bethlehemite." The fourth-century BC <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Chronicles" title="Books of Chronicles">1 Chronicles</a> explains the second Goliath by saying that Elhanan "slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath", possibly constructing the name Lahmi from the last portion of the word "Bethlehemite" ("<i>beit-ha’lahmi</i>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version" title="Authorized King James Version">King James Bible</a> translators adopted this into their translation of 2 Samuel 21:18–19, although the Hebrew text at this point makes no mention of the word "brother". "Most likely, storytellers displaced the deed from the otherwise obscure Elhanan onto the more famous character, David."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goliath_and_the_Philistines">Goliath and the Philistines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Goliath and the Philistines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tell_es-Safi" title="Tell es-Safi">Tell es-Safi</a>, the biblical <a href="/wiki/Gath_(city)" title="Gath (city)">Gath</a> and traditional home of Goliath, has been the subject of extensive excavations by Israel's <a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_University" title="Bar-Ilan University">Bar-Ilan University</a>. The archaeologists have established that this was one of the largest of the Philistine cities until destroyed in the ninth century BC, an event from which it never recovered. A <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">potsherd</a> discovered at the site, and reliably dated to the tenth to mid-ninth centuries BC, is inscribed with the two names "alwt" and "wlt". While the names are not directly connected with the biblical Goliath, they are etymologically related and demonstrate that the name fits with the context of late-tenth/early-ninth-century BC Philistine culture. The name "Goliath" itself is non-Semitic and has been linked with the <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Alyattes_I" title="Alyattes I">Alyattes</a>, which also fits the Philistine context of the biblical Goliath story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> A similar name, Uliat, is also attested in <a href="/wiki/Carian_language" title="Carian language">Carian</a> inscriptions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aren_Maeir" title="Aren Maeir">Aren Maeir</a>, director of the excavation, comments: "Here we have very nice evidence [that] the name Goliath appearing in the Bible in the context of the story of David and Goliath … is not some later literary creation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goliath_and_Saul">Goliath and Saul</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Goliath and Saul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1412" data-file-width="2048" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg/220px-Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg/330px-Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg/440px-Rembrandt_Harmensz._van_Rijn_029.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The underlying purpose of the story of Goliath is to show that Saul is not fit to be king (and that David is). Saul was chosen to lead the Israelites against their enemies, but when faced with Goliath he refuses to do so; Goliath is a giant, and Saul is a very tall man. His exact height is not given, but he was a head taller than anyone else in all Israel (1 Samuel 9:2), which implies he was over 6 feet (1.8 m) tall and the obvious challenger for Goliath. Also, Saul's armour and weaponry are apparently no worse than Goliath's (and David, of course, refuses Saul's armour in any case). "David declares that when a lion or bear came and attacked his father's sheep, he battled against it and killed it, [but Saul] has been cowering in fear instead of rising up and attacking the threat to his sheep (i.e. Israel)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2005_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2005-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goliath_and_the_Greeks">Goliath and the Greeks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Goliath and the Greeks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The armor described in 1 Samuel 17 appears typical of Greek armor of the sixth century BC rather than of <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a>' armor of the tenth century. Narrative formulae such as the settlement of battle by <a href="/wiki/Single_combat" title="Single combat">single combat</a> between champions has been thought characteristic of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric_epics" title="Homeric epics">Homeric epics</a> (the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>), rather than of the ancient Near East. The designation of Goliath as a איש הביניים, "man of the in-between" (a longstanding difficulty in translating 1 Samuel 17) appears to be a borrowing from Greek "man of the <i>metaikhmion</i> (μεταίχμιον)", i.e. the space between two opposite army camps where champion combat would take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A story very similar to that of David and Goliath appears in the <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a>, written circa 760–710 BC, where the young <a href="/wiki/Nestor_(mythology)" title="Nestor (mythology)">Nestor</a> fights and conquers the giant Ereuthalion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FinkelsteinSilberman2007_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FinkelsteinSilberman2007-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Each giant wields a distinctive weapon—an iron club in Ereuthalion's case, a massive bronze spear in Goliath's; each giant, clad in armor, comes out of the enemy's massed array to challenge all the warriors in the opposing army; in each case the seasoned warriors are afraid, and the challenge is taken up by a stripling, the youngest in his family (Nestor is the twelfth son of <a href="/wiki/Neleus" title="Neleus">Neleus</a>, David the seventh or eighth son of <a href="/wiki/Jesse" title="Jesse">Jesse</a>). In each case an older and more experienced father figure (Nestor's own father, David's patron Saul) tells the boy that he is too young and inexperienced, but in each case the young hero receives divine aid and the giant is left sprawling on the ground. Nestor, fighting on foot, then takes the chariot of his enemy, while David, on foot, takes the sword of Goliath. The enemy army then flees, the victors pursue and slaughter them and return with their bodies, and the boy-hero is acclaimed by the people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_traditions">Later traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Later traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish">Jewish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jewish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_Talmud" title="Babylonian Talmud">Babylonian Talmud</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sotah_(Talmud)" title="Sotah (Talmud)">Sotah</a> 42b) Goliath was a son of <a href="/wiki/Orpah" title="Orpah">Orpah</a>, the sister-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Ruth_(biblical_figure)" title="Ruth (biblical figure)">Ruth</a>, David's own great grandmother (Ruth → Obed → Jesse → David). <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Rabbah" title="Ruth Rabbah">Ruth Rabbah</a>, a haggadic and homiletic interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ruth" title="Book of Ruth">Book of Ruth</a>, makes the blood-relationship even closer, considering Orpah and Ruth to have been full sisters. Orpah was said to have made a pretense of accompanying Ruth but after forty paces left her. Thereafter she led a dissolute life. According to the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Talmud" title="Jerusalem Talmud">Jerusalem Talmud</a> Goliath was born by <a href="/wiki/Polyspermy" title="Polyspermy">polyspermy</a>, and had about one hundred fathers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Talmud stresses the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thrasonical" title="wikt:thrasonical">thrasonical</a> Goliath's ungodliness: his taunts before the Israelites included the boast that it was he who had captured the <a href="/wiki/Ark_of_the_Covenant" title="Ark of the Covenant">Ark of the Covenant</a> and brought it to the temple of <a href="/wiki/Dagon" title="Dagon">Dagon</a>; and his challenges to combat were made at morning and evening in order to disturb the Israelites in their prayers. His armour weighed 60 tons, according to rabbi <a href="/wiki/Hanina" title="Hanina">Hanina</a>; 120, according to rabbi <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abba_bar_Kahana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abba bar Kahana (page does not exist)">Abba bar Kahana</a>; and his sword, which became the sword of David, had marvellous powers. On his death it was found that his heart carried the image of Dagon, who thereby also came to a shameful downfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Philo" title="Pseudo-Philo">Pseudo-Philo</a>, believed to have been composed between 135 BC and 70 AD, David picks up seven stones and writes on them the names of his fathers, his own name, and the name of God, one name per stone; then, speaking to Goliath, he says "Hear this word before you die: were not the two woman from whom you and I were born, sisters? And your mother was Orpah and my mother Ruth ..." After David strikes Goliath with the stone he runs to Goliath before he dies and Goliath says "Hurry and kill me and rejoice." and David replies "Before you die, open your eyes and see your slayer." Goliath sees an angel and tells David that it is not he who has killed him but the angel. Pseudo-Philo then goes on to say that the angel of the Lord changes David's appearance so that no one recognizes him, and thus Saul asks who he is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Goliath appears in chapter 2 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> (2: 247–252), in the narrative of David and <a href="/wiki/Saul" title="Saul">Saul</a>'s battle against the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-26">[25]</a></sup> Called "Jalut" in Arabic, Goliath's mention in the Qur'an is concise, though it remains a parallel to the account in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>. Muslim scholars have tried to trace Goliath's origins, most commonly with the <a href="/wiki/Amalek" title="Amalek">Amalekites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-27">[26]</a></sup> Goliath, in early scholarly tradition, became a kind of byword or collective name for the oppressors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israelite" title="Israelite">Israelite</a> nation before <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-26">[25]</a></sup> Muslim tradition sees the battle with the Philistines as a prefiguration of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Badr" title="Battle of Badr">battle of Badr</a>, and sees Goliath as parallel to the enemies that the Prophet faced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-27">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>American actor <a href="/wiki/Ted_Cassidy" title="Ted Cassidy">Ted Cassidy</a> portrayed Goliath in the TV series <i>Greatest Heroes of the Bible</i> in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Italian actor <a href="/wiki/George_Eastman_(actor)" title="George Eastman (actor)">Luigi Montefiori</a> portrayed this nine-foot-tall giant in <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount</a>'s 1985 live-action movie <i><a href="/wiki/King_David_(film)" title="King David (film)">King David</a></i> as part of a flashback.</p>
<p>The PBS series <i><a href="/wiki/Wishbone_(TV_series)" title="Wishbone (TV series)">Wishbone</a></i> featured Goliath in its first-season episode "Little Big Dog".</p>
<p>Big Idea's popular <i><a href="/wiki/VeggieTales" title="VeggieTales">VeggieTales</a></i> episode was called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dave_and_the_Giant_Pickle" title="Dave and the Giant Pickle">Dave and the Giant Pickle</a>", where <a href="/wiki/Phil_Vischer" title="Phil Vischer">Phil Vischer</a> voiced Goliath.</p>
<p>In 1972, <a href="/wiki/Toho" title="Toho">Toho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsuburaya_Productions" title="Tsuburaya Productions">Tsuburaya Productions</a> collaborated on a movie called <i><a href="/wiki/Daigoro_vs._Goliath" title="Daigoro vs. Goliath">Daigoro vs. Goliath</a></i>, which follows the story relatively closely but recasts the main characters as <a href="/wiki/Kaiju" title="Kaiju">Kaiju</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005, Lightstone Studios released a direct-to-DVD movie musical titled "One Smooth Stone", which was later changed to "David and Goliath". It is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liken_the_scriptures" title="Liken the scriptures">Liken the Scriptures</a> (now just Liken) series of movie musicals on DVD based on scripture stories. <a href="/wiki/Thurl_Bailey" title="Thurl Bailey">Thurl Bailey</a>, a former NBA basketball player, was cast to play the part of Goliath in this film.</p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> aired <a href="/wiki/Kings_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Kings (U.S. TV series)">Kings</a> which has a narrative loosely based on the Biblical story of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_David" title="King David">King David</a>, but set in a kingdom that culturally and technologically resembles the present-day United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> The part of Goliath is portrayed by a tank, which David destroys with a shoulder fired rocket launcher.</p>
<p>Goliath was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Conan_Stevens" title="Conan Stevens">Conan Stevens</a> in the 2013 TV miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bible_(TV_miniseries)" title="The Bible (TV miniseries)">The Bible</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_Goliath_film_series_.281960.E2.80.931964.29">Italian Goliath film series (1960–1964)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Italian Goliath film series (1960–1964)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Italians used Goliath as an action superhero in a series of Biblical adventure films (peplums) in the early 1960s. He possessed amazing strength, and the films were similar in theme to their Hercules and <a href="/wiki/Maciste" title="Maciste">Maciste</a> movies. After the classic <i><a href="/wiki/Hercules_(1958_film)" title="Hercules (1958 film)">Hercules</a></i> (1958) became a blockbuster sensation in the film industry, a 1959 Steve Reeves film <i>Terrore dei Barbari</i> (<i>Terror of the Barbarians</i>) was retitled <i><a href="/wiki/Goliath_and_the_Barbarians" title="Goliath and the Barbarians">Goliath and the Barbarians</a></i> in the United States, (after <a href="/wiki/Joseph_E._Levine" title="Joseph E. Levine">Joseph E. Levine</a> claimed the sole right to the name of <i>Hercules</i>); the film was so successful at the box office, it inspired Italian filmmakers to do a series of four more films featuring a beefcake hero named Goliath, although the films were not really related to each other. (The 1960 Italian film <i>David and Goliath</i> starring <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> was not one of these, since that movie was a straightforward adaptation of the Biblical story).</p>
<p>The four titles in the Italian <i>Goliath</i> series were as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Goliath contro i giganti</i>/<i>Goliath Against the Giants</i> (1960) starring <a href="/wiki/Brad_Harris" title="Brad Harris">Brad Harris</a></li>
<li><i>Goliath e la schiava ribelle</i>/<i>Goliath and the Rebel Slave</i> (a.k.a. <i>The Tyrant of Lydia vs. The Son of Hercules</i>) (1963) starring <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Scott" title="Gordon Scott">Gordon Scott</a></li>
<li><i>Golia e il cavaliere mascherato</i>/<i>Goliath and the Masked Rider</i> (a.k.a. <i>Hercules and the Masked Rider</i>) (1964) starring Alan Steel</li>
<li><i>Golia alla conquista di Bagdad</i>/<i>Goliath at the Conquest of Baghdad</i>(a.k.a. <i>Goliath at the Conquest of Damascus</i>, 1964) starring <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lupus" title="Peter Lupus">Peter Lupus</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The name Goliath was later inserted into the film titles of three other Italian muscle man movies that were retitled for distribution in the United States in an attempt to cash in on the Goliath craze, but these films were not originally made as Goliath movies in Italy.</p>
<p>Both <i>Goliath and the Vampires</i> (1961) and <i>Goliath and the Sins of Babylon</i> (1963) actually featured the famed superhero Maciste in the original Italian versions, but American distributors didn't feel the name Maciste had any meaning to American audiences. <i>Goliath and the Dragon</i> (1960) was originally an Italian Hercules movie called <i>The Revenge of Hercules</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage_of_.22David_and_Goliath.22">Modern usage of "David and Goliath"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goliath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title='Edit section: Modern usage of "David and Goliath"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent usage, the phrase "David and Goliath" has taken on a secular meaning, denoting an <a href="/wiki/Underdog_(term)" title="Underdog (term)">underdog</a> situation, a contest where a smaller, weaker opponent faces a much bigger, stronger adversary; if successful, the underdog may win in an unusual or surprising way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macmillan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-macmillan-30">[29]</a></sup> It is arguably the most famous underdog story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">Theology</a> professor Leonard Greenspoon, in his essay, "David vs. Goliath in the Sports Pages", explains that "most writers use the story for its underdog overtones (the little guy wins) ... Less likely to show up in newsprint is the contrast that was most important to the biblical authors: David's victory shows the power of his God, while Goliath's defeat reveals the weakness of the Philistine deities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phrase is widely used in news media, to succinctly characterize underdog situations in every conceivable context, without religious overtones. Recent headlines include: sports ("Haye relishes underdog role in 'David and Goliath' fight with Nikolai Valuev"—<i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>); business ("On Internet, David-and-Goliath Battle Over Instant Messages"—<i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup>); science ("David and Goliath: How a tiny spider catches much larger prey"—<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ScienceDaily" title="ScienceDaily">ScienceDaily</a></i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-35">[34]</a></sup> politics ("Dissent in Cuba: David and Goliath"—<i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup>); social justice ("David-and-Goliath Saga Brings Cable to Skid Row"—<i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>).</p>
