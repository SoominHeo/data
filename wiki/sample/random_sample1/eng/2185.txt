<p><b>Aggregate</b> is the component of a <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> that resists compressive stress and provides bulk to the composite material. For efficient filling, aggregate should be much smaller than the finished item, but have a wide variety of sizes. For example, the particles of stone used to make <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> typically include both <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_fiber_composites">Comparison to fiber composites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aggregate_(composite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Comparison to fiber composites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Aggregate composites</i> tend to be much easier to fabricate, and much more predictable in their finished properties, than <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_reinforced_composite" title="Fiber reinforced composite">fiber composites</a></i>. Fiber orientation and continuity can have an overwhelming effect, but can be difficult to control and assess. Fabrication aside, aggregate materials themselves also tend to be less expensive; the most common aggregates mentioned above are found in nature and can often be used with only minimal processing.</p>
<p>Not all composite materials include aggregate. Aggregate particles tend to have about the same dimensions in every direction (that is, an <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aspect_ratio" title="wiktionary:aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a> of about one), so that aggregate composites do not display the level of synergy that fiber composites often do. A strong aggregate held together by a weak <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(geology)" title="Matrix (geology)">matrix</a> will be weak in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tension_(mechanics)" title="Tension (mechanics)">tension</a>, whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibers" title="Fibers">fibers</a> can be less sensitive to matrix properties, especially if they are properly oriented and run the entire length of the part (i.e., a <i>continuous filament</i>).</p>
<p>Most composites are filled with particles whose aspect ratio lies somewhere between oriented filaments and spherical aggregates. A good compromise is <i>chopped fiber</i>, where the performance of filament or cloth is traded off in favor of more aggregate-like processing techniques. <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">Ellipsoid</a> and plate-shaped aggregates are also used.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_properties">Aggregate properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aggregate_(composite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aggregate properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most cases, the ideal finished piece would be 100% aggregate. A given application's most desirable quality (be it high strength, low cost, high dielectric constant, or low density) is usually most prominent in the aggregate itself; all the aggregate lacks is the ability to flow on a small scale, and form attachments between particles. The matrix is specifically chosen to serve this role, but its abilities should not be abused.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_size">Aggregate size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aggregate_(composite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aggregate size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Experiments and mathematical models show that more of a given volume can be filled with hard spheres if it is first filled with large spheres, then the spaces between (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interstice" title="wiktionary:interstice">interstices</a>) are filled with smaller spheres, and the new interstices filled with still smaller spheres as many times as possible. For this reason, control of <i>particle size distribution</i> can be quite important in the choice of aggregate; appropriate simulations or experiments are necessary to determine the optimal proportions of different-sized particles.</p>
<p>The upper limit to particle size depends on the amount of flow required before the composite sets (the gravel in paving concrete can be fairly coarse, but fine sand must be used for <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tile</a> <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>), whereas the lower limit is due to the thickness of matrix material at which its properties change (clay is not included in concrete because it would "absorb" the matrix, preventing a strong bond to other aggregate particles). Particle size distribution is also the subject of much study in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">powder metallurgy</a>.</p>
<p>Some exceptions to this rule include:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toughened_composites">Toughened composites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aggregate_(composite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toughened composites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">Toughness</a> is a compromise between the (often contradictory) requirements of <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plasticity</a>. In many cases, the aggregate will have one of these properties, and will benefit if the matrix can add what it lacks. Perhaps the most accessible examples of this are composites with an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> matrix and <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> aggregate, such as <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">asphalt concrete</a> ("tarmac") and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Filled_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Filled plastic (page does not exist)">filled plastic</a> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">Nylon</a> mixed with powdered <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>), although most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_matrix_composites" title="Metal matrix composites">metal matrix composites</a> also benefit from this effect. In this case, the correct balance of hard and soft components is necessary or the material will become either too weak or too brittle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanocomposites">Nanocomposites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aggregate_(composite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nanocomposites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many materials properties change radically at small length scales (see <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>). In the case where this change is desirable, a certain range of aggregate size is necessary to ensure good performance. This naturally sets a lower limit to the amount of matrix material used.</p>
<p>Unless some practical method is implemented to orient the particles in micro- or nano-composites, their small size and (usually) high strength relative to the particle-matrix bond allows any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> object made from them to be treated as an aggregate composite in many respects.</p>
<p>While bulk synthesis of such nanoparticles as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotubes</a> is currently too expensive for widespread use, some less extreme nanostructured materials can be synthesized by traditional methods, including <a href="/wiki/Electrospinning" title="Electrospinning">electrospinning</a> and spray <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a>. One important aggregate made by spray pyrolysis is <a href="/wiki/Glass_microsphere" title="Glass microsphere">glass microspheres</a>. Often called <i>microballoons</i>, they consist of a hollow shell several tens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a> thick and approximately one <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometer</a> in diameter. Casting them in a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> matrix yields <a href="/wiki/Glass_microsphere" title="Glass microsphere">syntactic foam</a>, with extremely high compressive strength for its low density.</p>
<p>Many traditional nanocomposites escape the problem of aggregate synthesis in one of two ways:</p>
<p><b>Natural aggregates</b>: By far the most widely used aggregates for nano-composites are naturally occurring. Usually these are ceramic materials whose <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> structure is extremely directional, allowing it to be easily separated into flakes or fibers. The nanotechnology touted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" title="General Motors Corporation">General Motors</a> for automotive use is in the former category: a fine-grained <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> with a laminar structure suspended in a <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olefin" title="Olefin">olefin</a> (a class which includes many common plastics like <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>). The latter category includes fibrous <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> composites (popular in the mid-20th century), often with matrix materials such as <a href="/wiki/Linoleum" title="Linoleum">linoleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>.</p>
<p><b>In-situ aggregate formation</b>: Many micro-composites form their aggregate particles by a process of self-assembly. For example, in high impact <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">immiscible</a> phases of <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> (including brittle polystyrene and rubbery <a href="/wiki/Polybutadiene" title="Polybutadiene">polybutadiene</a>) are mixed together. Special molecules (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graft_copolymer" title="Graft copolymer">graft copolymers</a>) include separate portions which are soluble in each phase, and so are only stable at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interface_(chemistry)" title="Interface (chemistry)">interface</a> between them, in the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergent</a>. Since the number of this type of molecule determines the interfacial area, and since spheres naturally form to minimize <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>, synthetic chemists can control the size of polybutadiene droplets in the molten mix, which harden to form rubbery aggregates in a hard matrix. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dispersion_strengthening&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dispersion strengthening (page does not exist)">Dispersion strengthening</a> is a similar example from the field of <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Glass-ceramic" title="Glass-ceramic">glass-ceramics</a>, the aggregate is often chosen to have a negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a>, and the proportion of aggregate to matrix adjusted so that the overall expansion is very near zero. Aggregate size can be reduced so that the material is transparent to <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light.</p>
