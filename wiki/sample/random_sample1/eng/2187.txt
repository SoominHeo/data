<p><b>Juncaceae</b> is a family of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a>, commonly known as <b>rush family</b>. It consists of eight <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> and about 464 known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz-Byng2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz-Byng2016-2">[2]</a></sup> of slow-growing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhizomatous" title="Rhizomatous">rhizomatous</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Monocotyledon" title="Monocotyledon">monocotyledonous</a> plants that may superficially resemble <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a>. They often grow on infertile soils in a wide range of moisture conditions. The best-known and largest genus is <i><a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">Juncus</a></i>. Most of the <i>Juncus</i> species grow exclusively in wetland habitats. A few rushes are <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annuals</a>, but most are <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennials</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juncaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> and well-developed in a basal aggregation on an erect stem. They are alternate and tristichous (i.e., with three rows of leaves up the stem, each row of leaves arising one-third of the way around the stem from the previous leaf). Only in the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distichia_(genus)" title="Distichia (genus)">Distichia</a></i> are the leaves distichous. The rushes of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">Juncus</a></i> have flat, hairless leaves or cylindrical leaves. The leaves of the wood-rushes of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Luzula" title="Luzula">Luzula</a></i> are always flat and bear long white hairs.</p>
<p>The plants are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditic" title="Hermaphroditic">hermaphroditic</a> or, rarely, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioecious" title="Dioecious">dioecious</a>. The small <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are arranged in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflorescences" title="Inflorescences">inflorescences</a> of loose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyme_(botany)" title="Cyme (botany)">cymes</a>, but also in rather dense heads or <a href="/wiki/Corymb" title="Corymb">corymbs</a> at the top of the stem or at its side. This family typically has reduced <a href="/wiki/Perianth" title="Perianth">perianth</a> segments called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tepals" title="Tepals">tepals</a>. These are usually arranged in two <a href="/wiki/Whorl_(botany)" title="Whorl (botany)">whorls</a>, each containing three thin, papery tepals. They are not bright or flashy in appearance, and their color can vary from greenish to whitish, brown, purple, black, or hyaline. The three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpel" title="Carpel">stigmas</a> are in the center of the flowers. As is characteristic of monocots, all of the flower parts appear in multiples of three.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is usually a nonfleshy, three-sectioned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dehiscent" title="Dehiscent">dehiscent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" title="Capsule (fruit)">capsule</a> containing many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seeds" title="Seeds">seeds</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_uses">Domestic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juncaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Domestic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dried <a href="/wiki/Pith" title="Pith">pith</a> of plants of this family was used to make a type of <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candle</a> known as a <a href="/wiki/Rushlight" title="Rushlight">rushlight</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_rush" title="Soft rush">soft rush</a> (<i>Juncus effusus</i>) is called <i>igusa</i> in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> and is used to weave the soft surface cover of <a href="/wiki/Tatami" title="Tatami">tatami</a> mats.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">medieval Europe</a>, loose fresh rushes would be strewn on earthen floors in dwellings for cleanliness and insulation. Particularly favored for such a purpose was <i><a href="/wiki/Acorus_calamus" title="Acorus calamus">Acorus calamus</a></i> (sweet flag), but despite its alternate vernacular name "sweet rush", it is a plant from a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocot" title="Monocot">monocot</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acorales" title="Acorales">Acorales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
