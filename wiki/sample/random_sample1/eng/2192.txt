<p><i><b>Commonweal</b></i> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberal</a> journal of opinion, edited and managed by lay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Catholics</a>, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/The_Interchurch_Center" title="The Interchurch Center">The Interchurch Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. It is the oldest independent Roman Catholic journal of opinion in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commonweal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Founded in 1924 by Michael Williams (1877–1950) and the Calvert Associates, <i>Commonweal</i> is the oldest independent Roman Catholic journal of opinion in the United States. The magazine was originally modeled on <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">The New Republic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i> but “expressive of the Catholic note” in covering literature, the arts, religion, society, and politics.</p>
<p>Since its founding the magazine has been <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberal</a> in temperament.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has, however, taken controversial positions, going back to its neutral stance on the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> in 1938, when circulation plummeted by 20 percent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The editors condemned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">firebombing of Dresden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki</a>, both during World War II. In the decades that followed, the magazine criticized American racism, the <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">anti-Semitism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Coughlin" title="Charles Coughlin">Father Charles Coughlin</a>, and the tactics of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy" title="Joseph McCarthy">Senator Joseph McCarthy</a>; supported resistance to U.S. involvement in Vietnam; and took issue with the 1968 papal encyclical <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanae_Vitae" title="Humanae Vitae">Humanae vitae</a></i> but also the 1973 Supreme Court decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_v._Wade" title="Roe v. Wade">Roe v. Wade</a></i>.</p>
<p><i>Commonweal</i> has published the writing of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mauriac" title="François Mauriac">François Mauriac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bernanos" title="Georges Bernanos">Georges Bernanos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">Hannah Arendt</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Belloc" title="Hilaire Belloc">Hilaire Belloc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Maritain" title="Jacques Maritain">Jacques Maritain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Day" title="Dorothy Day">Dorothy Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Mounier" title="Emmanuel Mounier">Emmanuel Mounier</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conor_Cruise_O%E2%80%99Brien" title="Conor Cruise O’Brien">Conor Cruise O’Brien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Merton" title="Thomas Merton">Thomas Merton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Sheed" title="Wilfrid Sheed">Wilfrid Sheed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ramsey_(ethicist)" title="Paul Ramsey (ethicist)">Paul Ramsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bernardin" title="Joseph Bernardin">Joseph Bernardin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abigail_McCarthy" title="Abigail McCarthy">Abigail McCarthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lasch" title="Christopher Lasch">Christopher Lasch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kerr" title="Walter Kerr">Walter Kerr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marilynne_Robinson" title="Marilynne Robinson">Marilynne Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Timothy_Johnson" title="Luke Timothy Johnson">Luke Timothy Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Eagleton" title="Terry Eagleton">Terry Eagleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Johnson_(theologian)" title="Elizabeth Johnson (theologian)">Elizabeth Johnson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bacevich" title="Andrew Bacevich">Andrew Bacevich</a>. It has printed the short fiction of <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._F._Powers" title="J. F. Powers">J. F. Powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alice_McDermott" title="Alice McDermott">Alice McDermott</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Sayers" title="Valerie Sayers">Valerie Sayers</a>; the poetry of <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowell" title="Robert Lowell">Robert Lowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roethke" title="Theodore Roethke">Theodore Roethke</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Les_Murray_(poet)" title="Les Murray (poet)">Les Murray</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Berryman" title="John Berryman">John Berryman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marie_Ponsot" title="Marie Ponsot">Marie Ponsot</a>; and the artwork of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charlot" title="Jean Charlot">Jean Charlot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rita_Corbin" title="Rita Corbin">Rita Corbin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Eichenberg" title="Fritz Eichenberg">Fritz Eichenberg</a>, and Emil Antonucci.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commonweal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The journal, tagged as "A Review of Religion, Politics, and Culture." is run as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Not-for-profit" title="Not-for-profit">not-for-profit</a> enterprise and managed by an eighteen-member board of directors. The word "commonweal" is a reference to an important term in the political philosophy of St. Thomas Aquinas, who argued that legitimate leaders must prioritize the "common good" of the "commonweal" in making political decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Commonweal</i> publishes editorials, columns, essays, and poetry, along with film, book, and theater reviews. Twenty issues of <i>Commonweal</i> are released each year, with a circulation of approximately 20,000. In 1951, <i>Commonweal</i> was hit by financial troubles and almost shut down because of a loss in subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viewpoint">Viewpoint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commonweal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Viewpoint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although <i>Commonweal</i> maintains a relatively strong focus on issues of specific interest to liberal Catholics, this focus is not exclusionary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A broad range of issues—religious, political, social, and cultural—are examined independent of any relationship to Catholicism and the Church. <i>Commonweal</i> has attracted contributors from all points of the mainstream political spectrum in the United States.</p>
<p><i>Commonweal</i> published several articles in support of censured theologian <a href="/wiki/Roger_Haight" title="Roger Haight">Roger Haight</a> in 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Previous editors of <i>Commonweal</i> have been Michael Williams (1924-38); Edward S. Skillin (1938-67); James O’Gara (1967-84); <a href="/wiki/Peter_Steinfels" title="Peter Steinfels">Peter Steinfels</a> (1984-88); and Margaret O’Brien Steinfels (1988-2002).</p>
