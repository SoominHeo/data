<p>A <b>percentage point</b> or <b>percent point</b> (<b>pp</b>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_(measurement)" title="Unit (measurement)">unit</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Difference_(mathematics)" title="Difference (mathematics)">arithmetic difference</a> of two <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percentages</a>. For example, going from 40% to 44% is a 4 percentage point increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the literature, the percentage point unit is usually either written out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or abbreviated as <i>pp</i> or <i>p.p.</i>. Consider the following hypothetical example: In 1980, 40 percent of the population smoked, and in 1990 only 30 percent smoked. One can thus say that from 1980 to 1990, the prevalence of smoking decreased by 10 <i>percentage points</i> although smoking did not decrease by 10 percent (actually it decreased by <i>25 percent</i>) – percentages indicate ratios, not differences.</p>
<p>Percentage point differences are one way to express a risk or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilities" title="Probabilities">probability</a>. Consider a drug that cures a given disease in 70 percent of all cases, while without the drug, the disease heals spontaneously in only 50 percent of cases. The drug reduces absolute risk by 20 percentage points. Alternatives may be more meaningful to consumers of statistics, such as the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">reciprocal</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Number_needed_to_treat" title="Number needed to treat">number needed to treat</a> (NNT). In this case, the reciprocal transform of the percentage point difference would be 1/(20%) = 1/0.20 = 5. Thus if 5 patients are treated with the drug, one could expect to heal one more case of the disease than would have occurred in the absence of the drug.</p>
<p>For measurements with percentage as unit, like growth, <a href="/wiki/Yield_(finance)" title="Yield (finance)">yield</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ejection_fraction" title="Ejection fraction">ejection fraction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> will have percentage points as unit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Mistakenly using percentage as the unit for the standard deviation is confusing since percentage is also used as a unit for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_standard_deviation" title="Relative standard deviation">relative standard deviation</a>, i.e. the standard deviation divided by the average value (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_Variation" title="Coefficient of Variation">Coefficient of Variation</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
