<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Common Fisheries Policy</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Common Fisheries Policy</b> (<b>CFP</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> policy of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU). It sets quotas for which <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">member states</a> are allowed to catch each type of fish, as well as encouraging the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a> by various market interventions. In 2004 it had a budget of €931 million, approximately 0.75% of the <a href="/wiki/Budget_of_the_European_Union" title="Budget of the European Union">EU budget</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The common fisheries policy has been criticised by fishermen who say it is threatening their livelihoods, although fishing stocks were in decline long before the policy came into being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When it came into force, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> formally enshrined fisheries <i>conservation</i> policy as one of the handful of 'exclusive competences' reserved for the European Union, to be decided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qualified_Majority_Voting" title="Qualified Majority Voting">Qualified Majority Voting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, general fisheries policy remains a "shared competence" of the Union and its member states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus decisions will still be made primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Council of the European Union">Council of the European Union</a>, as is the case now.</p>
<p>The common fisheries policy was created to manage fish stock for the European Union as a whole. Article 38 of the 1957 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Rome" title="Treaty of Rome">Treaty of Rome</a>, which created the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities" title="European Communities">European Communities</a> (now <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>), stated that there should be a common policy for fisheries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_fishing">Importance of fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Importance of fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fishing is a relatively unimportant economic activity within the EU. It contributes generally less than 1% to gross national product. In 2007 the fisheries sector employed 141,110 fishermen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2007, 6.4 million tonnes of fish were caught by EU countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The EU fleet has 97,000 vessels of varying sizes. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">Fish farming</a> produced a further 1 million tonnes of fish and shellfish and employed another 85,000 people. The shortfall between fish catches and demand varies, but there is an EU trade deficit in processed fish products of €3 billion.</p>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fisheryexpenditure2004.png"><img alt="Fisheryexpenditure2004.png" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="594" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fisheryexpenditure2004.png/250px-Fisheryexpenditure2004.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fisheryexpenditure2004.png/375px-Fisheryexpenditure2004.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fisheryexpenditure2004.png/500px-Fisheryexpenditure2004.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fraserburgh" title="Fraserburgh">Fraserburgh</a>, Scotland, the fishing industry creates 40% of employment and a similar figure in <a href="/wiki/Peterhead" title="Peterhead">Peterhead</a>. They are the EU's largest fishing ports and home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> vessel fleet. It is often in areas where other employment opportunities are limited. For this reason, community funds have been made available to fishing as a means of encouraging regional development.</p>
<p>The market for fish and fish products has changed in recent years. Supermarkets are now the main buyers of fish and expect steady supplies. Fresh fish sales have fallen, but demand for processed fish and prepared meals has grown. Despite this, employment in <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">fish processing</a> has been falling, with 60% of fish consumed in the EU coming from elsewhere. This is partly due to improvements in the ability to transport fresh fish internationally. Competitiveness of the EU fishing industry has been affected by overcapacity and shortages of fish to catch.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aquaculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fish farming is the fastest growing area of world food production. In 1995 it produced 1/3 in value of world production of fish and shellfish. Main species in the EU are trout, salmon, mussels and oysters, but interest has been shown in sea bass, sea bream and turbot. Community support began in 1971 for inland fish farming, but was extended to other areas in the late 1970s. EU support covers similar areas to other land installations, but with additional concerns of technical and environmental problems caused by introducing major fish concentrations where farms are built. The industry suffers problems due to fluctuating demand for farmed fish.</p>

<p>The CFP currently has four components:</p>
<ul>
<li>Regulation of production, quality, grading, packaging and labelling</li>
<li>Encouraging producer organisations intended to protect fishermen from sudden market changes</li>
<li>Setting minimum fish prices and financing buying up of unsold fish</li>
<li>Set rules for trade with non-EU countries</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_allowable_catch_.28TAC.29">Total allowable catch (TAC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Total allowable catch (TAC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CFP sets quotas for how much of each species can be caught in a certain <a class="external text" href="http://geo.ices.dk/viewer.php?add_layers=ices_ref:ices_areas" rel="nofollow">ICES Statistical Area</a> or groups of areas on a yearly or two-yearly basis. Each country is given a quota based upon the total available (Total Allowable Catch, TAC) and their traditional share (percentage). TACs are fixed annually by the Council of Ministers. They consider proposals drawn up by the European Commission, which consults its own scientific advisers (Scientific, Technical and Economic Committee of Fisheries, STECF). STECF generally provides its advice to the European Commission taking account of the work conducted by the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_the_Exploration_of_the_Sea" title="International Council for the Exploration of the Sea">International Council for the Exploration of the Sea</a> (ICES). The Council of Ministers furthermore (when relevant) takes account of the views of non-EU fishing nations and the advice coming directly from ICES, which is independent of EU institutions. After quotas are fixed by the Council of Ministers, each EU member state is responsible for policing its own quota. Countries distribute their quotas among fishermen using different systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing_controls">Fishing controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fishing controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Basic Regulation sets the common principles for the EU management, under which each Member State can use different management approaches as licences, limited entry or individual fishing quota. Catches and landings must be recorded. Regulations cover the kind of fishing gear that may be used. Areas may be closed from fishing to allow stocks to recover.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Minimum_landing_size" title="Minimum landing size">minimum size</a> for catch led to fishermen dumping dead fish that were too small to land legally, so a minimum mesh size was introduced, which let small fish escape to replenish stocks. Choice of mesh is complicated, because mature fish of different species are naturally different sizes and require different nets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_policy_and_onshore_fishing_industry">Structural policy and onshore fishing industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structural policy and onshore fishing industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1977 an aid programme was introduced to improve the fish processing industries. This includes such things as fish filleting, salting, drying, smoking, cooking, freezing and canning. It was intended to indirectly assist the catching industry. There has been an attempt to introduce new technologies to the sector, improve hygiene conditions, and also fund conversions of fish processing factories to other uses.</p>
<p>Each country is given a target for the size of its fleet. Funding is available to assist modernisation of boats and installations, but also to buy-out fishermen to reduce the fleet size. Money is available for advertising campaigns to encourage consumption of fish species that are not over-fished, or are unfamiliar to the public. Also, grants are available to assist the industry in improving product quality and managing quotas.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>There are now more than 160 producer organisations (PO) in the EU. These are voluntary organisations set up by fishermen or fish farmers to assist in selling their product. Their members must include a minimum percentage of vessels in that sector, not discriminate in terms of nationality or location of their members within the EU, and must comply with other EU regulations. Organisations are required to develop plans to adjust fish catches to market demand. They may require non-members fishing in the same areas to follow the same restrictions as members.</p>
<p>They are empowered to take produce out of the market if prices fall below levels set by the council of ministers and receive compensation from the community. Levels of compensation are set such that price falls as the amount of fish involved increases. Fish stocks may be stored and later returned to the market, or sold for animal feed. Buying-up of stocks must only be to cover occasional surpluses.</p>
<p>Tuna fishermen have a scheme where surplus stock is not bought up, but fishermen receive direct compensation if their income falls.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_relations">International relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: International relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fishing rights to fisheries outside the EU were lost when international boundaries were expanded in 1976. The EU has negotiated agreements to recover some of these fishing grounds in return for alternative trading rights with the EU. External trade is now affected by the <a href="/wiki/General_Agreement_on_Tariffs_and_Trade" title="General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade">General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade</a> (GATT), regulated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organisation" title="World Trade Organisation">World Trade Organisation</a> (WTO).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_co-operation">Areas of co-operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Areas of co-operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Responsibility for fisheries in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_sea" title="Baltic sea">Baltic sea</a> was shared with the International Baltic Sea Fishery Commission (IBFC), to which the EU belonged until 1 January 2006. The Commission ceased to exist on 1 January 2007. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most Mediterranean fishing is confined to a 12-mile (22-km) strip considered territorial waters. The EU belongs to the General Fisheries Commission for the Mediterranean (GFCM) and the International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tuna, which also makes recommendations for Mediterranean tuna. In 1994 conservation regulations were introduced banning certain fishing methods. In 1997 targets were set for tuna catches.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compliance">Compliance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Compliance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Enforcement is the responsibility of member states, but there is a community level inspection service to ensure that member states enforce the rules within their own country. Member states are also under an obligation to ensure that their vessels observe EU agreements when operating outside the EU. The regulations are also intended to harmonise penalties for breaking the regulations in different countries.</p>
<p>Enforcement involves managing quotas and implementing technical measures to preserve fish stocks. Inspectors may check fishing gear and inspect the register of fish caught. The type of fish caught is checked and compared to quotas of total permitted catch for a vessel. Checks may be made in port or at sea, and using aerial photography.</p>
<p>Inspectors may also check fish processing factories to ensure that all fish is documented and can be traced to its source. EU inspectors check that hygiene and processing regulations in any country exporting to the EU are satisfactory and of an equal standard to controls within the EU.</p>
<p>Non-compliance remains a significant problem. In a number of EU fisheries, illegal fishing accounts for one-third to one-half of all catches.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_provision">Funding provision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Funding provision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fishing was initially funded under the European Agriculture Guidance and Guarantee Fund (EAGGF). In 1993 a separate fund was established (FIFG), the Financial Instrument for Fisheries. From 1994 to 1999 the budget for FIFG totalled 700 million ECU. Any grant from FIFG must be accompanied by a minimum contribution from the national government. A grant to business must include a proportionate contribution from the business itself. Different rates of aid are applied to different regions.</p>
<p>From 2007 to 2013, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_Funds_and_Cohesion_Funds" title="Structural Funds and Cohesion Funds">European Fisheries Fund</a> (EFF) will provide approximately 4.3 billion Euro to the European fishing sector. The adoption of the EFF was not uncontested, in particular by environmental groups, as it includes the possibility to fund vessel modernisation and other measures, which might increase pressure on already <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overfished" title="Overfished">overfished</a> stocks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing_and_the_environment">Fishing and the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fishing and the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1997 <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> states and EU representatives agreed a joint approach to identifying risks to the marine environment. A precautionary approach was adopted to seek to prevent pollution before damage was caused to the environment. Studies are being undertaken to monitor stocks of all fish, not just commercially important species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Common Fisheries Policy has been argued by certain commentators to have had disastrous consequences for the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This view is contradicted by historical evidence revealing that fishing stocks have been in chronic decline over the last century as a result of intensive <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">trawl fishing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup> According to scientific research published in 2010, the depletion of fishing stocks is a consequence of mismanagement long before the Common Fisheries Policy came into being, a statement illustrated by the fact that British catches have declined by 94% over the last 118 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Nonetheless, the Common Fisheries Policy has continued the trend of ineffective fisheries management in European waters. Indeed, the Common Fisheries Policy has done little if anything to reverse the decline of European fish stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>EU quotas can mean that fish are thrown overboard after being caught; yet as they are dead, this does not alleviate the problem as it was intended to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Fisheries policy has been a major reason for countries with substantial fish resources, coupled with small home markets, like Norway<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and Iceland, the Danish dependencies <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and some more dependencies, to stay outside the European Union.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiarity">Subsidiarity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Subsidiarity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A common criticism of the CFP is its <a href="/wiki/Centralisation" title="Centralisation">centralised</a>, top-down approach to management; although <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">Member States</a> are responsible for the policy's implementation and enforcement, the EU adopts sole competence in the creation of proposals and the making of decisions. The Commission is not exclusively responsible for the setting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Allowable_Catch" title="Total Allowable Catch">total allowable catches</a>. These are proposed by the commission but ultimately determined by the Council of Ministers. Allocation of national catch quotas to Member States is on a pre-determined basis - the so-called relative stability - giving each member state pre-determined percentages of the available fishing opportunities. Although Member States hold some responsibilities, such as the distribution of quotas, it is argued that the EU retains too much authority over fisheries management. Furthermore, critics maintain that the organisation is ill-suited to the task of fisheries management as it lacks sufficient understanding of fisheries, and is too far removed from the realities of the industry to set accurate TACs and quotas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command-and-control" title="Command-and-control">command-and-control</a> method characterised by the CFP is no longer deemed an effective form of fisheries management, and advocates of CFP reform consider a shift from traditional government to participatory third-order <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a>, incorporating the fisheries industry and Member States, to be vital to the success of the policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Consequently, it is suggested that the management of the CFP could be improved through the application of the theory of <a href="/wiki/Subsidiarity" title="Subsidiarity">subsidiarity</a>—the principle that political decisions should be handled at the lowest, least-centralised competent level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The subsidiarity principle was introduced into EU policies as part of the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Maastricht_Treaty" title="Maastricht Treaty">Maastricht Treaty</a>; however, it does not apply to areas such as the CFP over which the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">Community</a> retains exclusive competence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A partial <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a> of authority, for example involving Member States in the decision-making process and delegating the day-to-day management of fisheries to industry-based organisations, could potentially facilitate the inclusion of industry concerns into the CFP, involving those directly affected by the policy in management decisions and creating to a CFP which encourages compliance and collaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The call for application of the subsidiarity principle to the CFP lies within the argument for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decentralisation" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a>. De-centralisation featured prominently in discussions related to the 2002 CFP reform, but the reform itself actually increased centralisation within the CFP, removing the right of Member States to block quota proposals and increasing the EU's role in enforcement. This increasing monopoly and disregard for the wishes of the fisheries industry led to alienation of <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a> and resulted in reduced compliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The failure of this increasingly centralised reform has proved to de-centralisation advocates that stakeholder participation in the governance process is crucial to the future success of fisheries governance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, some critics argue that applying the subsidiarity principle to the CFP may not improve the policy's effectiveness, as it may lead to what de Vivero <i>et al.</i> term the ‘participation paradox’—the theory that the greater the number of actors involved in the decision-making process, the less significant the contribution made by each actor, and the smaller the participatory role played in the policy process. Greater devolution within CFP decision-making may therefore silence the voice of the fisheries industry as it competes with other state, private and civil actors to whom authority is also granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Thus, although the subsidiarity principle can facilitate the government-to-governance transition advocated by many in relation to reform of the CFP, the participatory role of key stakeholders affected by the policy must be maximised to ensure the development of an effective and equitable Common Fisheries Policy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970">1970</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 1970">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The first rules were created in 1970. The original six Common Market members realised that four countries applying to join the Common Market at that time (Britain, Ireland, Denmark including Greenland, and Norway) would control the richest fishing grounds in the world. The original six therefore drew up Council Regulation 2141/70 giving all Members equal access to all fishing waters, even though the Treaty of Rome gave no authority to do this. This was adopted on the morning of 30 June 1970, a few hours before the applications to join were officially received. This ensured that the regulations became part of the acquis communautaire before the new members joined, obliging them to accept the regulation. In its accession negotiations, the UK at first refused to accept the rules but by the end of 1971 the UK gave way and signed the Accession Treaty on 22 January 1972, thereby bringing into the CFP joint management an estimated four fifths of all the fish off Western Europe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Norway decided not to join. Greenland left the EC in 1985, after having gained partial independence from Denmark in 1979.</p>
<p>When the fisheries policy was originally set up the intention was to create a free trade area in fish and fish products with common rules. It was agreed that fishermen from any state should have access to all waters. An exception was made for the coastal strip, which was reserved for local fishermen who had traditionally fished those areas. A policy was created to assist modernisation of fishing vessels and on-shore installations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1976">1976</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 1976">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976 The EU extended its fishing waters from 12 miles to 200 miles (22.2 km to 370.4 km) from the coast, in line with other international changes. This required additional controls and the CFP as such was created in 1983. This now had four areas of activity: conservation of stocks, vessels and installations, market controls, and external agreements with other nations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992">1992</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 1992">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was determined that there had been over-investment in vessels, over-fishing and that numbers of fish landed were decreasing. The review identified a need to improve compliance with the regulations. This led to a tightening of regulations and better monitoring of individual vessels. A second review was planned for 2002.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995">1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although fishing could be managed by reducing the fleet size, available fish vary from year to year too much to make this sensible. So a permit system was introduced stating where and when boats are allowed to fish. Scientific studies were commissioned to better-determine available stocks and guide allocation of permits.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009">2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, the EU Commission launched a wide-ranging debate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> on the way that EU fisheries are managed. It received input from EU citizens, organisations and EU-countries and published a report on the consultation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Fisheries_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2013 the European Parliament voted for reform of the Common Fisheries Policy, including measures to protect endangered stocks, and the ending of discards. The new CFP came into effect from 1 January 2014, though more talks with EU governments are involved. In presenting the reform package, the German Social Democrat MEP Ulrike Rodust stated: "As of 2015 the principle of <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">maximum sustainable yield</a> shall apply... Our objective is that depleted fish stocks recover by 2020. Not only nature will benefit, but also fishermen: bigger stocks produce higher yields."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The 2013 reform led to a greater role for the European Parliament, involving the convening of a trilateral dialogue (or ‘trilogue’) between the European Council, European Commission and the Parliament, to work towards general agreement on reforming the CFP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
