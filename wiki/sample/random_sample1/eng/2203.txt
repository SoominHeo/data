<p><i><b>Dinotopia</b></i> is a four-hour (three-episode) TV miniseries co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Television" title="Walt Disney Television">Walt Disney Television</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonar_Entertainment" title="Sonar Entertainment">Hallmark Entertainment</a>. It is based on the fictional world of <a href="/wiki/Dinotopia" title="Dinotopia">Dinotopia</a>, a utopia in which sentient dinosaurs and humans coexist, created by American author <a href="/wiki/James_Gurney" title="James Gurney">James Gurney</a>. The miniseries uses plot details from Gurney's first two Dinotopia books, <i>Dinotopia</i> and <i>Dinotopia: The World Beneath</i>, although it takes place in a time farther into the future. The main characters are two American teenage boys from a contemporary time frame (unlike the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> castaways in the books—the film thus loses some of the classicism of the original books).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The boys crash their father's plane into the sea and get stranded on Dinotopia, where they must adjust to a new society. The story in the film contains references to many of the characters in the book series, with some of their descendents occupying key roles in the plot. The original score was composed by <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Jones_(composer)" title="Trevor Jones (composer)">Trevor Jones</a>.</p>
<p>The miniseries premiered as an episode of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_World_of_Disney" title="The Wonderful World of Disney">The Wonderful World of Disney</a></i> anthology on May 12, 2002 on <a href="/wiki/Disney_Channel" title="Disney Channel">Disney Channel</a>. It received an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Special_Visual_Effects" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Special Visual Effects">Outstanding Special Visual Effects for a Miniseries, Movie or a Special</a>. It was soon followed by a television series which only lasted for one season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinotopia_(miniseries)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A pair of teenage half-brothers, Karl and David Scott, are on a flight with their father on his private <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">plane</a> when Karl takes over the controls, allowing his father to fall asleep. After flying into a storm, Karl struggles, causing Frank to retake control before it crashes into the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>. Frank saves his sons, but had trouble escaping the crashed plane as his seatbelt failed to release. Karl and David wash up on the shore of a large uncharted island. Looking for help, Karl and David meet a man named Cyrus Crabb who leads them to Waterfall City, the capital of Dinotopia, a hidden civilization where human beings and dinosaurs peacefully coexist. In fact, some of the dinosaurs, notably a neurotic, but friendly Troodon named Zippo, possess human intelligence and speak perfect English. Among the many rules in Dinotopia is the edict that, once an outsider arrived, he or she was never allowed to leave. As Karl and David prepare to take their places in their new surroundings, both boys develop a strong bond with young Marion, who is on the verge of becoming a "matriarch" of the Dinotopian society. Unfortunately, the incursion of vicious carnivores, not to mention an unanticipated human villain, threaten to destroy Dinotopia and everyone living there.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinotopia_(miniseries)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plans for a Dinotopia film were previously rejected due to the expense of creating a world populated by both humans and dinosaurs. Both <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a> at one time made the attempt, but both studios abandoned the idea, Disney opting to make their own homegrown film, <i><a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_(film)" title="Dinosaur (film)">Dinosaur</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/2000_in_film" title="2000 in film">2000</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RHI_Entertainment" title="RHI Entertainment">Hallmark Entertainment</a> chief Robert Halmi Sr. then purchased the rights after having seen <a href="/wiki/James_Gurney" title="James Gurney">Gurney’s</a> book.</p>
<p>Halmi was willing to spend $80 million on the film, despite the performance of his previous fantasy miniseries, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tenth_Kingdom" title="The Tenth Kingdom">The Tenth Kingdom</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> ABC had so much confidence in the miniseries that they began shooting episodes of the spin-off television series before the miniseries had aired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than 75% of the scenes in the miniseries required visual effects, many of which required interaction between the live-action human actors and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animatronic" title="Animatronic">animatronic</a> or computer-animated dinosaurs. The computer-animated dinosaurs were created by a <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>-based company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Framestore_CFC" title="Framestore CFC">Framestore CFC</a>, who also did the <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> work for the <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs" title="Walking with Dinosaurs">Walking with Dinosaurs</a></i> series. The series also used other <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">visual effects</a> techniques such as digital set extensions. Many of the sets were only partially built, the rest being done digitally, in order to create the enormous buildings used by both dinosaurs and humans in the film. Even so, the actual set for Waterfall City, Dinotopia’s capital, took up five-and-a-half acres of the back lot of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">Pinewood Studios</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson%27s_Creature_Shop" title="Jim Henson's Creature Shop">Jim Henson's Creature Shop</a> provided the animatronic dinosaurs.</p>
<p>Although Dinotopia started out as a TV miniseries, later all the parts were combined and put on DVD as one film, except in the UK, where the mini-series was put onto discs as separate episodes.</p>
