<p><b>Imperial Noble Consort Gongshun</b> (1787 – 23 April 1860) was a consort of the <a href="/wiki/Jiaqing_Emperor" title="Jiaqing Emperor">Jiaqing Emperor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Noble_Consort_Gongshun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gongshun was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">Manchu</a> <a href="/wiki/Niohuru" title="Niohuru">Niohuru</a> clan. Her personal name is unknown. Her father was Shanqing (善慶), who served as a <i>zhushi</i> (主事) under the Qing government. She entered the <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_City" title="Forbidden City">Forbidden City</a> in the early reign of the <a href="/wiki/Jiaqing_Emperor" title="Jiaqing Emperor">Jiaqing Emperor</a> and received the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranks_of_imperial_consorts_in_China#Qing" title="Ranks of imperial consorts in China">Noble Lady</a> under the title "Noble Lady Ru" (如貴人). In 1805, she gave birth to the <a href="/wiki/Jiaqing_Emperor#Daughters" title="Jiaqing Emperor">Jiaqing Emperor's eighth daughter</a> and was promoted to "Imperial Concubine Ru" (如嬪) three months later. In 1810, she was promoted to "Consort Ru" (如妃). A year later, she gave birth to the Jiaqing Emperor's ninth daughter, Gurun Princess Huimin (慧愍固倫公主). In 1814, she bore the Jiaqing Emperor's fifth son, Mianyu (綿愉).</p>
<p>In 1820, the Jiaqing Emperor died and was succeeded by his second son Mianning, who was enthroned as the <a href="/wiki/Daoguang_Emperor" title="Daoguang Emperor">Daoguang Emperor</a>. In the same year, the Daoguang Emperor granted Gongshun the title "Dowager Noble Consort Ru" (皇考如貴妃) and let her reside in Shou'an Palace (壽安宮). In 1846, she was honoured as "Dowager Imperial Noble Consort Ru" (皇考如皇貴妃). In 1850, the Daoguang Emperor died and was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Xianfeng_Emperor" title="Xianfeng Emperor">Xianfeng Emperor</a>, who granted Lady Niohuru the title "Grand Dowager Imperial Noble Consort Ru" (皇祖如皇貴太妃).</p>
<p>Gongshun died in 1860 and was granted the posthumous title "Imperial Noble Consort Gongshun" (恭順皇貴妃). She was interred in the Changling Mausoleum in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Qing_tombs" title="Western Qing tombs">Western Qing tombs</a>.</p>

</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearances_in_media">Appearances in media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Noble_Consort_Gongshun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearances in media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gongshun is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Sheren_Tang" title="Sheren Tang">Sheren Tang</a> in the 2004 Hong Kong television series <i><a href="/wiki/War_and_Beauty" title="War and Beauty">War and Beauty</a></i>. In the television drama, Gongshun (referred to as Consort Ru) is portrayed as a bullying and manipulative woman who engages in a power struggle against the empress. It is later revealed that it was a facade because Consort Ru admits that she had behaved as such in order to survive in the imperial harem. Tang's portrayal of Consort Ru won widespread acclaim by media critics and audiences, raising her popularity.</p>
