<p>The process of <b>expropriation</b> "occurs when a public agency (for example, the provincial government and its agencies, regional districts, municipalities, school boards, post-secondary institutions and utilities) takes private property for a purpose deemed to be in the public interest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">eminent domain</a>, expropriation may also refer to the taking of private property by a <i>private</i> entity authorized by a government to take property in certain situations.</p>
<p>The term appears as "expropriation of expropriators (<a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling classes</a>)" in <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist theory</a>, and also as the slogan "Loot the looters!" ("грабь награбленное"), which was very popular during the Russian <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term is also used to describe <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalization</a> campaigns by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_states" title="Communist states">communist states</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dekulakization" title="Dekulakization">dekulakization</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collectivization_in_the_USSR" title="Collectivization in the USSR">collectivization in the USSR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pipes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pipes-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>One example of expropriation occurred between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in 1938 when the Mexican president <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1zaro_C%C3%A1rdenas" title="Lázaro Cárdenas">Lázaro Cárdenas</a> signed an order that expropriated almost all of the foreign oil companies operating in Mexico. This initially turned out to have great negative consequences on the Mexican economy when their oil exports were boycotted by major oil companies, decreasing exports dramatically,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but later on the economic benefits of this move became apparent, with the new national oil company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEMEX" title="PEMEX">PEMEX</a> being an important contributor to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_Miracle" title="Mexican Miracle">Mexican Miracle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and other countries soon followed with oil nationalisation carried out in much of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and the developing world.</p>
<p>Due to political risks that are involved when countries engage in international business it is important to understand the expropriation risks and laws within each of the countries that business is conducted in order to understand your risks as an investor in that country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
