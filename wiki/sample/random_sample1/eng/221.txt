<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Considered harmful</h1>

<p><b>Considered harmful</b> is a part of a <a href="/wiki/Phrasal_template" title="Phrasal template">phrasal template</a> used in the titles of at least 65 critical essays in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and related disciplines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was popularized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edsger_Dijkstra" title="Edsger Dijkstra">Edsger Dijkstra</a>'s letter <i>Go To Statement Considered Harmful</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dijkstra1968_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dijkstra1968-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ewd215_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ewd215-3">[3]</a></sup> published in the March 1968 <i>Communications of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a></i> (CACM), in which he criticized the excessive use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOTO" title="GOTO">GOTO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statement_(programming)" title="Statement (programming)">statement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> of the day and advocated <a href="/wiki/Structured_programming" title="Structured programming">structured programming</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The original title of the letter, as submitted to CACM, was <i>A Case Against the Goto Statement</i>, but CACM editor <a href="/wiki/Niklaus_Wirth" title="Niklaus Wirth">Niklaus Wirth</a> changed the title to <i>Go To Statement Considered Harmful</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Regarding this new title, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Knuth" title="Donald Knuth">Donald Knuth</a> quipped that "<a href="/wiki/Eiichi_Goto" title="Eiichi Goto">Dr. Goto</a> cheerfully complained that he was always being eliminated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Frank Rubin published a criticism of Dijkstra's letter in the March 1987 CACM where it appeared under the title <i>'GOTO Considered Harmful' Considered Harmful</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin87goto_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubin87goto-7">[7]</a></sup> The May 1987 CACM printed further replies, both for and against, under the title <i>'"GOTO Considered Harmful" Considered Harmful' Considered Harmful?</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acm_may87_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-acm_may87-8">[8]</a></sup> Dijkstra's own response to this controversy was titled <i>On a Somewhat Disappointing Correspondence</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ewd1009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ewd1009-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Considered harmful</i> was already a journalistic cliché used in headlines, well before the Dijkstra article, as in, for example, the headline over a letter published in 1949 in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>: "Rent Control Controversy / Enacting Now of Hasty Legislation Considered Harmful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-liberman-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some variants with replacement <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> (<i>considered silly</i>, etc.) have been noted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(programmer_subculture)" title="Hacker (programmer subculture)">hacker</a> <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esr-jargon_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-esr-jargon-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yegge-singleton-stupid_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-yegge-singleton-stupid-12">[12]</a></sup> Many variants deal with computer-related issues, such as:</p>
