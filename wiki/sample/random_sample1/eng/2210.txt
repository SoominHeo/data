<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Codependency</b> is a type of dysfunctional helping relationship where one person supports or <a href="/wiki/Enabling" title="Enabling">enables</a> another person's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_addiction" title="Drug addiction">drug addiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambling_addiction" title="Gambling addiction">gambling addiction</a>, poor <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>, immaturity, irresponsibility, or under-achievement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup> Among the core characteristics of codependency, the most common theme is an excessive reliance on other people for approval and a sense of identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup> Given its grassroots origin, the precise definition of codependency varies based on the source but can be generally characterized as a subclinical and situational or episodic behavior similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Dependent_personality_disorder" title="Dependent personality disorder">dependent personality disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup> In its broadest definition, a codependent is someone who cannot function from their innate self and whose thinking and behavior is instead organized around another person, or even a process, or substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dummies_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dummies-2">[2]</a></sup> In this context, people who are addicted to a substance, like drugs, or a process, like gambling or sex, can also be considered codependent. In its most narrow definition, it requires one person to be physically or psychologically addicted, such as to <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>, and the second person to be psychologically dependent on that behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary.reference.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.reference.com-3">[3]</a></sup> Some users of the codependency concept use the word as an alternative to using the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_families" title="Dysfunctional families">dysfunctional families</a>, without statements that classify it as a disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archive_copy.2C_July_24.2C_2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archive_copy.2C_July_24.2C_2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Disability_studies" title="Disability studies">disability studies</a> specialist <a href="/wiki/Lennard_J._Davis" title="Lennard J. Davis">Lennard J. Davis</a>, historically, the concept of co-dependence "comes directly out of <a href="/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous" title="Alcoholics Anonymous">Alcoholics Anonymous</a>, part of a dawning realization that the problem was not solely the addict, but also the family and friends who constitute a network for the alcoholic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis08_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis08-5">[5]</a></sup> "While not an actual diagnosis, the term “codependent” was first used to describe how family members of individuals with substance abuse issues might actually interfere with recovery by overhelping."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was subsequently broadened to cover the way "that the codependent person is fixated on another person for approval, sustenance, and so on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis08_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davis08-5">[5]</a></sup> The concept of codependency overlaps with, but developed in the mainstream independently from, the older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoanalytic" title="Psychoanalytic">psychoanalytic</a> concept of the passive dependent personality which is attaching oneself to a stronger personality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> "Dependency" is well-established in psychological literature. Whereas early on <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalytic_theory" title="Psychoanalytic theory">psychoanalytic theory</a> emphasized the oral character and structural basis of dependency, <a href="/wiki/Social_learning_theory" title="Social learning theory">social learning theory</a> considered a tendency to be acquired by learning and experience, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attachment_Theory" title="Attachment Theory">ethological attachment theory</a> posited that attachment or affectional bonding is the basis for dependency. All three theories have contributed to the concept of dependent personality disorder as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders</a> (DSM) of the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">American Psychiatric Association</a>. The definition and criteria have changed in the different versions of the DSM. In DSM-I, passive dependency personality was characterized by helplessness, <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a>, and indecisiveness, and was considered a subtype of passive aggressive personality. By DSM-IV, there were nine criteria with an essential feature of a pervasive or lifetime pattern of dependent and submissive behavior. The DSM-IV definition emphasized the excessive need to be taken care of, leading to submissive and clinging behavior and fear of separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sperry_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sperry-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The codependency movement may have its roots in the theories of German psychoanalyst <a href="/wiki/Karen_Horney" title="Karen Horney">Karen Horney</a>. In 1941, she proposed that some people adopt what she termed a "Moving Toward" personality style to overcome their basic anxiety. Essentially, these people move toward others by gaining their approval and affection, and unconsciously control them through their dependent style. They are unselfish, virtuous, martyr-like, faithful, and turn the other cheek despite personal humiliation. Approval from others is more important than respecting themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Anon" title="Al-Anon">Al-Anon</a> was formed in 1951, 16 years after Alcoholics Anonymous was founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA-10">[10]</a></sup> Al-Anon holds the view that alcoholism is a family illness<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Anon_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Anon-11">[11]</a></sup> and is one of the earliest recognitions of codependency.</p>
<p>The expansion of the meaning of codependency happened very publicly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Janet_G._Woititz" title="Janet G. Woititz">Janet G. Woititz's</a> <i>Adult Children of Alcoholics</i> had come out in 1983 and sold two million copies while being on the <i>New York Times</i> bestseller list for forty-eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robin_Norwood" title="Robin Norwood">Robin Norwood</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Women_Who_Love_Too_Much" title="Women Who Love Too Much">Women Who Love Too Much</a></i>, 1985, sold two and a half million copies and spawned Twelve Step groups across the country for women "addicted" to men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Melody_Beattie" title="Melody Beattie">Melody Beattie</a> popularized the concept of codependency in 1986 with the book <i><a href="/wiki/Codependent_No_More" title="Codependent No More">Codependent No More</a></i> which sold eight million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Timmen_L._Cermak" title="Timmen L. Cermak">Timmen Cermak, M.D.</a> wrote <i>Diagnosing and Treating Co-Dependence: A Guide for Professionals</i>. In the book and an article published in the Journal of Psychoactive Drugs (Volume 18, Issue 1, 1986), Cermak argued (unsuccessfully) for the inclusion of codependency as a separate <a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">personality disorder</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders</a> (DSM-III-R; American Psychiatric Association, 1987).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Cermak's book paved the way for a Twelve-step take-off program, called <a href="/wiki/Co-Dependents_Anonymous" title="Co-Dependents Anonymous">Co-Dependents Anonymous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_1999_30_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irving_1999_30-15">[15]</a></sup> The first Co-Dependents Anonymous meeting was held October 22, 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_1999_30_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irving_1999_30-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms_and_behaviors">Symptoms and behaviors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Symptoms and behaviors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Given its grassroots origin, the precise definition of codependency varies based on the source but can be generally characterized as a subclinical and situational or episodic behavior similar to that of dependent personality disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup> In its broadest definition, a codependent is someone who cannot function from their innate self and whose thinking and behavior is instead organized around another person, or even a process, or substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dummies_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dummies-2">[2]</a></sup> In this context, people who are addicted to a substance, like drugs, or a process, like gambling or sex, can also be considered codependent. In its most narrow definition, it requires one person to be physically or psychologically addicted, such as to <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>, and the second person to be psychologically dependent on that behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary.reference.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.reference.com-3">[3]</a></sup> Some users of the codependency concept use the word as an alternative to using the concept of dysfunctional families, without statements that classify it as a disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archive_copy.2C_July_24.2C_2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Archive_copy.2C_July_24.2C_2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Codependency does not refer to all caring behavior or feelings, but only those that are excessive to an unhealthy degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> One of the distinctions is that healthy empathy and caregiving is motivated by conscious choice; whereas for codependents, their actions are compulsive, and they usually aren't able to weigh in the consequences of them or their own needs that they're sacrificing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some scholars and treatment providers feel that codependency is an overresponsibility and that overresponsibility needs to be understood as a positive impulse gone awry. Responsibility for relationships with others needs to coexist with responsibility to self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Codependency has been referred to as the disease of a lost self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dummies_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dummies-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Codependent relationships are marked by intimacy problems, dependency, control (including caretaking) denial, dysfunctional communication and <a href="/wiki/Personal_boundaries" title="Personal boundaries">boundaries</a>, and high reactivity. Often, there is imbalance, so one person is abusive or in control or supports or enables another person's addiction, poor mental health, immaturity, irresponsibility, or under-achievement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lancer_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lancer-19">[19]</a></sup> Some codependents often find themselves in relationships where their primary role is that of rescuer, supporter, and confidante. These helper types are often dependent on the other person's poor functioning to satisfy their own emotional needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup> Many codependents place a lower priority on their own needs, while being excessively preoccupied with the needs of others. Codependency can occur in any type of relationship, including family, work, friendship, and also romantic, peer or community relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-patterns-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commonly cited symptoms of codependency are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPDFamily_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-BPDFamily-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>intense and unstable interpersonal relationships</li>
<li>inability to tolerate being alone, accompanied by frantic efforts to avoid being alone</li>
<li>chronic feelings of <a href="/wiki/Boredom" title="Boredom">boredom</a> and emptiness</li>
<li>subordinating one's own needs to those of the person with whom one is involved</li>
<li>overwhelming desire for acceptance and affection</li>
<li><a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Perfectionism" title="Perfectionism">perfectionism</a></li>
<li>over-controlling</li>
<li>external referencing</li>
<li>dishonesty and denial</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Psychological_manipulation" title="Psychological manipulation">manipulation</a></li>
<li>lack of <a href="/wiki/Trust_(emotion)" title="Trust (emotion)">trust</a></li>
<li>low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-worth" title="Self-worth">self-worth</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patterns_and_characteristics">Patterns and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patterns and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Codependency can occur in any type of relationship, including family, work, friendship, and also romantic, peer or community relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-patterns-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enmeshed_romantic_relationship">Enmeshed romantic relationship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enmeshed romantic relationship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a codependent relationship, the codependent's sense of purpose is based on making extreme sacrifices to satisfy their partner's needs. Codependent relationships signify a degree of unhealthy "clinginess", where one person does not have <a href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" title="Self-sufficiency">self-sufficiency</a> or <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>. One or both parties depend on their loved one for fulfillment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webmd_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-webmd-22">[22]</a></sup> There is almost always an unconscious reason for continuing to put another person's life ahead of your own, and often it is because of the mistaken notion that self-worth comes from other people.</p>
<p>Particularly problematic pairings include:</p>
<dl>
<dd><b><a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">Personality disorder</a> and codependent pairing</b>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">Borderline personality disorder</a></b>. There is a tendency for loved ones of people with borderline personality disorder (BPD) to slip into "caretaker" roles, giving priority and focus to problems in the life of the person with BPD rather than to issues in their own lives. Too often in these kinds of relationships, the codependent will gain a sense of worth by being "the sane one" or "the responsible one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danielle_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Danielle-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Narcissistic_personality_disorder" title="Narcissistic personality disorder">Narcissistic personality disorder</a></b>. Codependents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narcissists" title="Narcissists">narcissists</a> are sometimes called co-narcissists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-co-24">[24]</a></sup> Narcissists, with their ability to get others to "buy into their vision" and help them make it a reality, seek and attract partners who will put others' needs before their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Codependents can provide the narcissist with an obedient and attentive audience — the perfect backdrop for a self-absorbed narcissist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Among the reciprocally interlocking interactions of the pair are the narcissist's overpowering need to feel important and special and the co-dependent person's strong need to help others feel that way.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dysfunctional_family">Dysfunctional family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dysfunctional family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_family" title="Dysfunctional family">dysfunctional family</a> the child learns to become attuned to the parent's needs and feelings instead of the other way around.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lancer_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-lancer-19">[19]</a></sup> Parenting is a role that requires a certain amount of self-sacrifice and giving a child's needs a high priority. A parent can, nevertheless, be codependent towards their own children if the caretaking or parental sacrifice reaches unhealthy or destructive levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-patterns-20">[20]</a></sup> Generally, a parent who takes care of their own needs (emotional and physical) in a healthy way will be a better caretaker, whereas a codependent parent may be less effective, or may even do harm to a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-patterns-20">[20]</a></sup> Another way to look at it is that the needs of an infant are necessary but temporary, whereas the needs of the codependent are constant. Children of codependent parents who ignore or negate their children's feelings may become codependent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_and_prognosis">Recovery and prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recovery and prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Not all <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> professionals agree about standard methods of treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Caring for an individual with a physical addiction is not necessarily treating a pathology. The caregiver may only require <a href="/wiki/Assertiveness" title="Assertiveness">assertiveness</a> skills and the ability to place responsibility for the addiction on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-affleck_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-affleck-29">[29]</a></sup> There are various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recovery_model" title="Recovery model">recovery</a> paths for individuals who struggle with codependency. For example, some may choose cognitive-behavioral <a href="/wiki/Psychotherapy" title="Psychotherapy">psychotherapy</a>, sometimes accompanied by chemical therapy for accompanying depression. There also exist support groups for codependency, such as <a href="/wiki/Co-Dependents_Anonymous" title="Co-Dependents Anonymous">Co-Dependents Anonymous</a> (CoDA), <a href="/wiki/Al-Anon/Alateen" title="Al-Anon/Alateen">Al-Anon/Alateen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nar-Anon" title="Nar-Anon">Nar-Anon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adult_Children_of_Alcoholics" title="Adult Children of Alcoholics">Adult Children of Alcoholics</a> (ACoA), which are based on the <a href="/wiki/Twelve-step_program" title="Twelve-step program">twelve-step program</a> model of <a href="/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous" title="Alcoholics Anonymous">Alcoholics Anonymous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celebrate_Recovery" title="Celebrate Recovery">Celebrate Recovery</a> a Christian, Bible-based group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLLET1990_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-COLLET1990-30">[30]</a></sup> Many self-help guides have been written on the subject of codependency.</p>
<p>Sometimes an individual can, in attempts to recover from codependency, go from being overly passive or overly giving to being overly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggressive" title="Aggressive">aggressive</a> or excessively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selfish" title="Selfish">selfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> Many therapists maintain that finding a balance through healthy <a href="/wiki/Assertiveness" title="Assertiveness">assertiveness</a> (which leaves room for being a caring person and also engaging in healthy caring behavior) is true recovery from codependency and that becoming extremely selfish, a <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bully</a>, or an otherwise conflict-addicted person is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-affleck_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-affleck-29">[29]</a></sup> Developing a permanent stance of being a victim (having a <a href="/wiki/Victim_mentality" title="Victim mentality">victim mentality</a>) would also not constitute true recovery from codependency and could be another example of going from one extreme to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> A victim mentality could also be seen as a part of one's original state of codependency (lack of empowerment causing one to feel like the "subject" of events rather than being an empowered actor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> Someone truly recovered from codependency would feel empowered and like an author of their life and actions rather than being at the mercy of outside forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> A victim mentality may also occur in combination with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive%E2%80%93aggressive" title="Passive–aggressive">passive–aggressive</a> control issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup> From the perspective of moving beyond victim-hood, the capacity to forgive and let go (with exception of cases of very severe abuse) could also be signs of real recovery from codependency, but the willingness to endure further abuse would not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alcoholism_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholism-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unresolved patterns of codependency can lead to more serious problems like alcoholism, drug addiction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eating_disorders" title="Eating disorders">eating disorders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_addiction" title="Sex addiction">sex addiction</a>, psychosomatic illnesses, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-destructive" title="Self-destructive">self-destructive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Self-defeating_personality_disorder" title="Self-defeating personality disorder">self-defeating behaviors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-cd-31">[31]</a></sup> People with codependency are also more likely to attract further abuse from aggressive individuals, more likely to stay in stressful jobs or relationships, less likely to seek medical attention when needed and are also less likely to get promotions and tend to earn less money than those without codependency patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-cd-31">[31]</a></sup> For some people, the social insecurity caused by codependency can progress into full-blown <a href="/wiki/Social_anxiety" title="Social anxiety">social anxiety</a> disorders like <a href="/wiki/Specific_social_phobia" title="Specific social phobia">social phobia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avoidant_personality_disorder" title="Avoidant personality disorder">avoidant personality disorder</a> or painful <a href="/wiki/Shyness" title="Shyness">shyness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-cd-31">[31]</a></sup> Other stress-related disorders like <a href="/wiki/Panic_disorder" title="Panic disorder">panic disorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PTSD" title="PTSD">PTSD</a> may also be present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cd_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-cd-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_proposal_for_inclusion_in_DSM">Failed proposal for inclusion in DSM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Failed proposal for inclusion in DSM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timmen_L._Cermak,_M.D." title="Timmen L. Cermak, M.D.">Timmen Cermak, M.D.</a>, proposed that co‐dependency be listed as a <a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">personality disorder</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders</a> (DSM-III-R; American Psychiatric Association, 1987). Cermak reasoned that when specific personality traits become excessive and maladaptive and caused significant impairment in functioning or caused significant distress, it warrants a personality disorder diagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cermak_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-cermak-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cermak proposed the following criteria for this disorder:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cermak_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-cermak-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Continued investment of <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a> in the ability to control both oneself and others in the face of serious adverse consequences.</li>
<li>Assumption of responsibility for meeting others' needs to the exclusion of acknowledging one's own.</li>
<li>Anxiety and boundary distortions around intimacy and separation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Enmeshment" title="Enmeshment">Enmeshment</a> in relationships with personality disordered, chemically dependent, other co ‐ dependent, and/or impulse ‐ disordered individuals.</li>
<li>Three or more of the following:
<ol>
<li>Excessive reliance on <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a></li>
<li>Constriction of emotions (with or without dramatic outbursts)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">Depression</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypervigilance" title="Hypervigilance">Hypervigilance</a></li>
<li>Compulsions</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">Anxiety</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">Substance abuse</a></li>
<li>Has been (or is) the victim of recurrent physical or sexual abuse</li>
<li>Stress ‐ related medical illnesses</li>
<li>Has remained in a primary relationship with an active substance abuser for at least two years without seeking outside help.</li>
</ol>
</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Codependency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some scholars believe that codependency is not a negative trait, and does not need to be treated, as it is more likely a healthy personality trait taken to excess. Codependency in nonclinical populations has some links with favourable characteristics of family functioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stan_Katz" title="Stan Katz">Stan Katz</a> states that codependence is over-diagnosed, and that many people who could be helped with shorter-term treatments instead become dependent on long-term self-help programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The language of, symptoms of, and treatment for codependence derive from the <a href="/wiki/Medical_model" title="Medical model">medical model</a> suggesting a <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> process underlies the behavior. However, there is no evidence that codependence is caused by a disease process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
