<p>The <b>Confucius Peace Prize</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">孔子和平奖</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">孔子和平獎</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Kǒngzǐ Hépíngjiǎng</span></i>) is a Chinese alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> established in 2010 by the Association of Chinese Indigenous Arts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auto1H-1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Auto1H-1-2">[2]</a></sup> in the People's Republic of China (PRC), in response to a proposal by business person Liu Zhiqin on November 17, 2010. The chairman of the committee said that the award existed to "promote world peace from an Eastern perspective", and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> peace specifically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-1">[1]</a></sup> The winner purportedly receives a cash prize of ¥100,000 RMB (US$15,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20101208huffingtonpost_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-20101208huffingtonpost-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite an attempt by China's Ministry of Culture to ban the prize<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TelegraphCancel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-TelegraphCancel-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zb_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-zb-6">[6]</a></sup> in September 2011, the original organizers re-established in Hong Kong as the "China International Peace Research Center", awarding the prize to <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> in November 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCMcancel_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCMcancel-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shanghaiist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shanghaiist-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-9">[9]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Longping" title="Yuan Longping">Yuan Longping</a> in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raymondli_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-raymondli-10">[10]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> in 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr370086460_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr370086460-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mugabe" title="Robert Mugabe">Robert Mugabe</a> in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qz530500_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-qz530500-12">[12]</a></sup> The Chinese government has denied having any connections with the prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_first_award">Origins and first award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and first award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Confucius Peace Prize originated as a response to the announcement that Chinese dissident <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xiaobo" title="Liu Xiaobo">Liu Xiaobo</a> had won the <a href="/wiki/2010_Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="2010 Nobel Peace Prize">2010 Nobel Peace Prize</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-confucius_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-confucius-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20141211nytimes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-20141211nytimes-15">[15]</a></sup> the awarding of the prize to Liu was viewed negatively in China, with some in the government arguing that Liu did not promote "international friendship, disarmament, and peace meetings", the stated goals of the Nobel Peace Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Age:_China_furious_at_Nobel.27s_.27violation.27_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Age:_China_furious_at_Nobel.27s_.27violation.27-16">[16]</a></sup> Competing groups staked claims to its founding, and some have links to the Ministry of Culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20141211nytimes_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-20141211nytimes-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, Liu Zhiqin, a Chinese banker, was the first to propose the prize, in a commentary in the <i><a href="/wiki/Global_Times" title="Global Times">Global Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-17">[17]</a></sup> Liu's commentary stated, "The Nobel Peace Prize Committee won Liu Xiaobo while losing the trust of 1.3 billion Chinese people. They support a criminal while creating 1.3 billion 'dissidents' that are dissatisfied with the Nobel Committee, which is definitely a bad decision. ... China's civil society should consider setting up a 'Confucius Peace Prize', launching the evaluation and selection and finding the real Peace Prize winners from all over the world. This is the best opportunity for the Chinese to declare China's view in peace and human rights to the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Association of Chinese Indigenous Arts, which is registered with the Chinese Cultural Ministry, awarded the first prize to Taiwanese politician <a href="/wiki/Lien_Chan" title="Lien Chan">Lien Chan</a> in December 2010, for his contribution to developing positive ties <a href="/wiki/Cross-Strait_relations" title="Cross-Strait relations">between Taiwan and mainland China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Lien never claimed the prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-confucius_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-confucius-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-20">[20]</a></sup> China's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_(China)" title="Ministry of Culture (China)">Minister of Culture</a> talked to the <i><a href="/wiki/United_Daily_News" title="United Daily News">United Daily News</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a> and stated they had never heard of this prize for Lien Chan until there was newspaper coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mingpao_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mingpao-21">[21]</a></sup> The Hong Kong newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Ming_Pao" title="Ming Pao">Ming Pao</a></i> reported that the letter issued by the committee to Lien Chan did not have the Ministry of Culture's official seal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mingpao_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-mingpao-21">[21]</a></sup> The award, consisting of a small sculpture and a bundle of banknotes, was collected by a young girl in front of an audience of some 100 journalists. In the jostling, one group tried to create the "Confucius World Peace Prize" to rival the "Confucius Peace Prize".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20141211nytimes_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-20141211nytimes-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purported_cancellation_and_second_award">Purported cancellation and second award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purported cancellation and second award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Culture of the People's Republic of China">Ministry of Culture</a> stated that it would be disbanding the organizers of Confucius Peace Prize and cancelling the prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCMcancel_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CCMcancel-7">[7]</a></sup> The Ministry stated in a news conference on September 17 that there was improper uses of the Ministry's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-9">[9]</a></sup> The Association of Chinese Indigenous Arts was quick to blame a "rogue department" for the debacle surrounding the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TelegraphCancel_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-TelegraphCancel-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the cancellation, on November 15, 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> reported that the original organizers had formed a new committee, the China International Peace Studies Center, in Hong Kong, where they awarded the second Confucius Peace Prize to Russian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>. Other contenders were <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Zuma" title="Jacob Zuma">Jacob Zuma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Longping" title="Yuan Longping">Yuan Longping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyaincain_Norbu" title="Gyaincain Norbu">Gyaincain Norbu</a> (one of the <a href="/wiki/Panchen_Lama" title="Panchen Lama">Panchen Lamas</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soong_Chu-yu" title="Soong Chu-yu">Soong Chu-yu</a>. Putin won for his opposition to <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> involvement in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_Civil_War" title="2011 Libyan Civil War">Libyan Civil War</a> as well as his decision to go to war in <a href="/wiki/Second_Chechen_War" title="Second Chechen War">Chechnya in 1999</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc2011_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2011-22">[22]</a></sup> According to the committee, Putin's "Iron hand and toughness revealed in this war impressed the Russians a lot, and he was regarded to be capable of bringing safety and stability to Russia." Putin was also praised for fulfilling his childhood dream of joining the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>. The 2011 award, a gilded statuette of Confucius was given out on December 9 along with a certificate, although the committee did not mention a cash prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-9">[9]</a></sup> The award ceremony featured a speech by <a href="/wiki/Kong_Qingdong" title="Kong Qingdong">Kong Qingdong</a>. Kong claimed that the award accurately reflects <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a>'s original vision of peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20111209voanews_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-20111209voanews-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_award_and_recipients">The award and recipients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The award and recipients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prize initially carried a financial reward of CNY100,000, and has been awarded at the same time as the Nobel Peace Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20141211nytimes_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-20141211nytimes-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1095244_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1095244-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2012, the China International Peace Studies Centre boasted that the new reward of $1.5 million – mostly derived from sales of works of the committee chairman's, Qiao Damo – was "more than the US$1.2 million that the recipient of this year's Nobel Peace Prize, will receive".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1095244_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1095244-24">[24]</a></sup> However, <i>The Guardian</i> reported in 2015 that the cash prize was worth CNY500,000 (£51,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20151022guardian_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-20151022guardian-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012">2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The shortlist for the 2012 award consisted of previous nominees <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, agricultural scientist <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Longping" title="Yuan Longping">Yuan Longping</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyancain_Norbu" title="Gyancain Norbu">Gyancain Norbu</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a>, Thai prime minister <a href="/wiki/Yingluck_Shinawatra" title="Yingluck Shinawatra">Yingluck Shinawatra</a>, Chinese philosopher <a href="/wiki/Tang_Yijie" title="Tang Yijie">Tang Yijie</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wang_Dingguo" title="Wang Dingguo">Wang Dingguo</a>, the last surviving female participant in Mao Zedong's <a href="/wiki/Long_March" title="Long March">Long March</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120901time_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-20120901time-26">[26]</a></sup> Due to a tie in the voting, the committee announced that Annan and Yuan would share the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raymondli_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-raymondli-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1095244_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1095244-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zen master <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yi_Cheng&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yi Cheng (page does not exist)">Yi Cheng</a> was awarded the 2013 prize for his work as chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_Association_of_China" title="Buddhist Association of China">Buddhist Association of China</a> and his contribution for Chinese Buddhism. As an addition to the Peace Prize in 2013 the first <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Art_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Confucius Art Prize (page does not exist)">Confucius Art Prize</a> was awarded. A gold medal was for calligrapher <a href="/wiki/Ouyang_Zhongshi" title="Ouyang Zhongshi">Ouyang Zhongshi</a> and silver medals for Yang Lin and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hou_Mingming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hou Mingming (page does not exist)">Hou Mingming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014">2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Global Times</i> reported that nominees included former Cuban president, <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, South Korean President <a href="/wiki/Park_Geun-hye" title="Park Geun-hye">Park Geun-hye</a> and the UN Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr370086460_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr370086460-11">[11]</a></sup> The 2014 recipient of the prize was Castro, who when leader of Cuba, "never used any violence or force when faced with problems and conflicts in international relations, especially in Cuba's relationship with the United States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NR2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NR2-29">[29]</a></sup> Castro's prize was received by a Cuban student in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr370086460_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-npr370086460-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015">2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nominated for the award in 2015 were the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Taoist_Association" title="Chinese Taoist Association">Chinese Taoist Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a>, former Japanese prime ministers <a href="/wiki/Yasuo_Fukuda" title="Yasuo Fukuda">Yasuo Fukuda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tomiichi_Murayama" title="Tomiichi Murayama">Tomiichi Murayama</a>, Kazakhstan president <a href="/wiki/Nursultan_Nazarbayev" title="Nursultan Nazarbayev">Nursultan Nazarbayev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, Chinese-American politician <a href="/wiki/Anna_Chennault" title="Anna Chennault">Anna Chennault</a>, South Korean president <a href="/wiki/Park_Geun-hye" title="Park Geun-hye">Park Geun-hye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hsing_Yun" title="Hsing Yun">Hsing Yun</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fo_Guang_Shan" title="Fo Guang Shan">Fo Guang Shan</a> Buddhist movement in Taiwan, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mugabe" title="Robert Mugabe">Robert Mugabe</a> of Zimbabwe. Mugabe was eventually declared the winner for "working tirelessly to build the political and economic stability of his country, bringing peace to the people of Zimbabwe, strongly supporting pan-Africanism and African independence, and making unparalleled contributions for the renaissance of African civilisation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20151002shanghaiist_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-20151002shanghaiist-30">[30]</a></sup> One of the committee's 76 members disclosed that only 36 voted for Mugabe over concerns for his record, saying that "Mugabe has been in power for such a long time that he could be easily be labelled a dictator, tyrant or despot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20151022guardian_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-20151022guardian-25">[25]</a></sup> Mugabe, who made a speech to the United Nations that day in which he declared that "we are not gays", did not collect his prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20151002shanghaiist_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-20151002shanghaiist-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The award drew out critics of Mugabe and his human rights record. An unofficial spokesman said that the Chinese government had notified Zimbabwe that it was not associated with the conferring organisation. Mugabe repudiated the award, a spokesman saying that he "receives many prizes without even knowing, approving or receiving them. What is dramatic in this case is that oppositional interests drew a revolver at the mentioning of an honour or prize to Robert Mugabe. That is what Hitler did when someone mentioned ‘culture’ to him".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The prize for 2016 was awarded posthumously to three Chinese peacekeepers who were killed while serving on <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_peacekeeping" title="United Nations peacekeeping">United Nations peacekeeping</a> missions in Mali and South Sudan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Shen Liangliang was killed while serving on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Multidimensional_Integrated_Stabilization_Mission_in_Mali" title="United Nations Multidimensional Integrated Stabilization Mission in Mali">mission in Mali</a> on May 31, 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> while Yang Shupeng and Li Lei were killed while serving on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_in_South_Sudan" title="United Nations Mission in South Sudan">mission in South Sudan</a> on July 10, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_to_Confucius_Peace_Prize">Response to Confucius Peace Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confucius_Peace_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Response to Confucius Peace Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chinese sources have not treated the prize seriously. The Ministry of Culture of the People's Republic of China denied any cooperation with the Association of Chinese Indigenous Arts to present the prize,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup> stating that prior to foreign press reports, the Ministry "had never even heard of the name". The ministry added that "the government is very careful" and "if the government genuinely intended to establish a 'Confucius Peace prize', it would not have been implemented so sloppily."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup> Mainland media did not widely report about the prize in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup> with journalists claiming that they had come across neither the organization nor the prize, believing it to be "闹剧" (a farce).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Netizens in Mainland China responded to the Prize with widespread humour as well as derision, referring to the prize organizers as "bullheaded dreaming idiots", "jokes", and "embarrassments to Chinese",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auto1H-2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Auto1H-2-35">[35]</a></sup> as well as suspicion that the prize was a practical joke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-e-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Confucius Peace Prize's first winner, Lien Chan, claimed he had not officially heard that he had won; an aide said that they had only received "secondhand information from journalists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-17">[17]</a></sup> This contradicted a statement in the <i><a href="/wiki/Global_Times" title="Global Times">Global Times</a></i> by the chairman of the Confucius Peace Prize Committee, Tan Liuchang, that Lien had been contacted by the committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-global-36">[36]</a></sup> Tsai Chi-chang, a spokesperson for the opposition <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Progressive_Party" title="Democratic Progressive Party">Democratic Progressive Party</a> in Taiwan, said the award should not be taken seriously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-times-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Officials from the Taiwanese government are reported to have found the award of the Confucius Peace Prize to Lien Chan "amusing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-confucius_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-confucius-14">[14]</a></sup> When asked by a reporter about the prize, a spokesman for Vladimir Putin said that he had heard about the award through the press, but that "we do not know much about the prize."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Western media, however, have reacted to the prize as genuine. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> compared the substitute award to the reaction by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/German_National_Prize_for_Art_and_Science" title="German National Prize for Art and Science">German National Prize for Art and Science</a> after <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Ossietzky" title="Carl von Ossietzky">Carl von Ossietzky</a> was prohibited from accepting his Nobel Prize in 1935, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> preventing <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a> from accepting his Nobel Peace Prize in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asia_view_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asia_view-37">[37]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i> likewise compared the prize to Hitler's and Stalin's responses to the Nobel Peace Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NR1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NR1-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate</a></i> has described the prize as "[s]omething of a <a href="/wiki/Internet_troll" title="Internet troll">trolling</a> exercise".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Global Times</i> reported that the five 2011 nominees were selected based on their being the winners of an online poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-global-36">[36]</a></sup> However, when asked about the details of the online vote, the organizer said they failed to carry it out because of "technical problems", The <i>South China Morning Post</i> reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20101209scmp_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-20101209scmp-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
