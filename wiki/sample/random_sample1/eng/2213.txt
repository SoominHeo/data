<p><b>Levitation</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>levitas</i> "lightness")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the process by which an object is held aloft, without mechanical support, in a stable position.</p>
<p>Levitation is accomplished by providing an upward <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> that counteracts the pull of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravity</a> (in relation to gravity on earth), plus a smaller stabilizing force that pushes the object toward a home position whenever it is a small distance away from that home position. The force can be a fundamental force such as magnetic or electrostatic, or it can be a reactive force such as optical, buoyant, aerodynamic, or hydrodynamic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Levitation excludes <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">floating</a> at the surface of a liquid because the liquid provides direct mechanical support. Levitation excludes hovering flight by insects, hummingbirds, helicopters, rockets, and balloons because the object provides its own counter-gravity force.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">Physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Levitation (on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> or any planetoid) requires an upward force that cancels out the <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> of the object, so that the object does not fall (accelerate downward) or rise (accelerate upward). For positional stability, any small displacement of the levitating object must result in a small change in force in the opposite direction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The small changes in force can be accomplished by gradient field(s) or by active regulation. If the object is disturbed, it might oscillate around its final position, but its motion eventually decreases to zero due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">damping</a> effects. (In a turbulent flow, the object might oscillate indefinitely.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Levitation techniques are useful tools in physics research. For example, levitation methods are useful for high-temperature melt property studies because they eliminate the problem of reaction with containers and allow deep undercooling of melts. The containerless conditions may be obtained by opposing gravity with a levitation force instead of allowing an entire experiment to freefall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordine2000_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nordine2000-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_levitation">Magnetic levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Magnetic levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_levitation" title="Magnetic levitation">Magnetic levitation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="3375" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg/220px-Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg/330px-Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg/440px-Stickstoff_gek%C3%BChlter_Supraleiter_schwebt_%C3%BCber_Dauermagneten_2009-06-21.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Magnetic levitation is the most commonly seen and used form of levitation.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">Diamagnetic</a> materials are commonly used for demonstration purposes. In this case the returning force appears from the interaction with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screening_current" title="Screening current">screening currents</a>. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> sample, which can be considered either as a perfect diamagnet or an <a href="/wiki/Ideally_hard_superconductor" title="Ideally hard superconductor">ideally hard superconductor</a>, easily levitates in an ambient external magnetic field. The superconductor is first heated strongly, then cooled with liquid nitrogen to levitate on top of a diamagnet. In a very strong magnetic field by means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic_levitation" title="Diamagnetic levitation">diamagnetic levitation</a>, even small live animals have been levitated.</p>
<p>It is possible to levitate pyrolytic graphite by placing thin squares of it above four cube magnets with the north poles forming one diagonal and south poles forming the other diagonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_maglev_train_coming_out,_Pudong_International_Airport,_Shanghai.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1259" data-file-width="2338" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg/220px-A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg/330px-A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg/440px-A_maglev_train_coming_out%2C_Pudong_International_Airport%2C_Shanghai.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A magnetically levitated (maglev) train departing <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Pudong_International_Airport" title="Shanghai Pudong International Airport">Shanghai Pudong International Airport</a> on the first commercial high-speed maglev line in the world.</div>

<p>Magnetic levitation is in development for use for transportation systems. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Maglev" title="Maglev">Maglev</a> includes trains that are levitated by a large number of magnets. Due to the lack of friction on the guide rails, they are faster, quieter, and smoother than wheeled mass transit systems.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_suspension" title="Electrodynamic suspension">Electrodynamic suspension</a> uses AC magnetic fields.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrostatic_levitation">Electrostatic levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electrostatic levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_levitation" title="Electrostatic levitation">Electrostatic levitation</a></div>
<p>In electrostatic levitation an <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> is used to counteract gravitational force.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamic_levitation">Aerodynamic levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aerodynamic levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_levitation" title="Aerodynamic levitation">Aerodynamic levitation</a></div>
<p>In aerodynamic levitation, the levitation is achieved by floating the object on a stream of gas, either produced by the object or acting on the object. For example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ping_pong_ball" title="Ping pong ball">ping pong ball</a> can be levitated with the stream of air from a vacuum cleaner set on "blow". With enough thrust, very large objects can be levitated using this method.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_film_levitation">Gas film levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gas film levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This technique enables the levitation of an object against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_force" title="Gravitational force">gravitational force</a> by floating it on a thin <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> film formed by gas flow through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porous" title="Porous">porous</a> membrane. Using this technique, high temperature melts can be kept clean from contamination and be supercooled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordine2000_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordine2000-2">[2]</a></sup> A common example in general usage includes <a href="/wiki/Air_hockey" title="Air hockey">air hockey</a>, where the puck is lifted by a thin layer of air. <a href="/wiki/Hovercraft" title="Hovercraft">Hovercraft</a> also use this technique, producing a large region of high-pressure air underneath them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_levitation">Acoustic levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acoustic levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_levitation" title="Acoustic levitation">Acoustic levitation</a></div>
<p>Acoustic levitation uses sound waves to provide a levitating force.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_levitation">Optical levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Optical levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Optical_levitation" title="Optical levitation">Optical levitation</a></div>
<p>Optical levitation is a technique in which a material is levitated against the downward force of gravity by an upward force stemming from <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> transfer (<a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buoyant_levitation">Buoyant levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Buoyant levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gases at high pressure can have a density exceeding that of some solids. Thus they can be used to levitate solid objects through <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">Noble gases</a> are preferred for their non-reactivity. <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">Xenon</a> is the densest non-radioactive noble gas, at 5.894g/L. Xenon has been used to levitate <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>, at a pressure of 154atm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casimir_force">Casimir force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Casimir force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scientists have discovered a way of levitating ultra small objects by manipulating the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casimir_force" title="Casimir force">Casimir force</a>, which normally causes objects to stick together due to forces predicted by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>. This is, however, only possible for micro-objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maglev_trains">Maglev trains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Maglev trains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maglev" title="Maglev">Maglev</a></div>
<p>Magnetic levitation is used to suspend trains without touching the track. This permits very high speeds, and greatly reduces the maintenance requirements for tracks and vehicles, as little wear occurs. This also means there is no friction, so the only force acting against it is air resistance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_levitation">Animal levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Animal levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="574" data-file-width="607" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg/220px-Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg/330px-Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg/440px-Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frog_diamagnetic_levitation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">Diamagnetic</a> levitation of a live frog.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Scientists have levitated frogs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> grasshoppers, and mice by means of powerful electromagnets utilizing superconductors, producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a> repulsion of body water. The mice acted confused at first, but adapted to the levitation after approximately four hours, suffering no immediate ill effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
