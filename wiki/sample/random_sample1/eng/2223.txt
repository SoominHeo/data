<p><b>Anaphylaxis</b> is a serious <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergic reaction</a> that is rapid in onset and may cause death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samp2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Samp2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tint10_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tint10-2">[2]</a></sup> It typically causes more than one of the following: an itchy rash, throat or tongue swelling, <a href="/wiki/Shortness_of_breath" title="Shortness of breath">shortness of breath</a>, vomiting, lightheadedness, and <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a>. These symptoms typically come on over minutes to hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common causes include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insect_bite" title="Insect bite">insect bites</a> and stings, foods, and medications. Other causes include <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> exposure and exercise. Additionally cases may occur without an obvious reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-3">[3]</a></sup> The mechanism involves the release of mediators from certain types of <a href="/wiki/White_blood_cell" title="White blood cell">white blood cells</a> triggered by either <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immunologic</a> or non-immunologic mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup> Diagnosis is based on the presenting symptoms and signs after exposure to a potential <a href="/wiki/Allergen" title="Allergen">allergen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary treatment of anaphylaxis is <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intramuscular" title="Intramuscular">injection into a muscle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous_fluid" title="Intravenous fluid">intravenous fluids</a>, and positioning the person flat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> Additional doses of epinephrine may be required. Other measures, such as <a href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">antihistamines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">steroids</a>, are complementary. Carrying an <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine_autoinjector" title="Epinephrine autoinjector">epinephrine autoinjector</a> and identification regarding the condition is recommended in people with a history of anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Worldwide, 0.05–2% of the population is estimated to experience anaphylaxis at some point in life. Rates appear to be increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> It occurs most often in young people and females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> Of people who go to a hospital with anaphylaxis in the United States about 0.3% die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%BD%CE%AC#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:ἀνά">ἀνά</a></span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>ana</i></span> "against", and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%86%CF%8D%CE%BB%CE%B1%CE%BE%CE%B9%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:φύλαξις">φύλαξις</a></span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>phylaxis</i></span> "protection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Anaphylaxis typically presents many different symptoms over minutes or hours<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with an average onset of 5 to 30 minutes if exposure is intravenous and 2 hours if from eating food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The most common areas affected include: skin (80–90%), <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory</a> (70%), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal</a> (30–45%), heart and <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">vasculature</a> (10–45%), and central nervous system (10–15%)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup> with usually two or more being involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Symptoms typically include generalized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urticaria" title="Urticaria">hives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Itch" title="Itch">itchiness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flushing_(physiology)" title="Flushing (physiology)">flushing</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Swelling_(medical)" title="Swelling (medical)">swelling</a> (<a href="/wiki/Angioedema" title="Angioedema">angioedema</a>) of the afflicted <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samp2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Samp2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Those with angioedema may describe a burning sensation of the skin rather than itchiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> Swelling of the tongue or throat occurs in up to about 20% of cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shock10_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shock10-13">[13]</a></sup> Other features may include a runny nose and swelling of the <a href="/wiki/Conjunctiva" title="Conjunctiva">conjunctiva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup> The skin may also be <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">blue tinged</a> because of <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">lack of oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiratory">Respiratory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Respiratory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Respiratory symptoms and signs that may be present include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">shortness of breath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheeze" title="Wheeze">wheezes</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Stridor" title="Stridor">stridor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samp2006_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Samp2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The wheezing is typically caused by spasms of the <a href="/wiki/Bronchus" title="Bronchus">bronchial</a> muscles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardio08_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cardio08-15">[15]</a></sup> while stridor is related to upper airway obstruction secondary to swelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup> Hoarseness, pain with swallowing, or a cough may also occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular">Cardiovascular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Angina_pectoris" title="Angina pectoris">Coronary artery spasm</a> may occur with subsequent <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_dysrhythmia" title="Cardiac dysrhythmia">dysrhythmia</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup> Those with underlying coronary disease are at greater risk of cardiac effects from anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardio08_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cardio08-15">[15]</a></sup> The coronary spasm is related to the presence of histamine-releasing cells in the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardio08_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cardio08-15">[15]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Tachycardia" title="Tachycardia">a fast heart rate</a> caused by low blood pressure is more common,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Bezold%E2%80%93Jarisch_reflex" title="Bezold–Jarisch reflex">Bezold–Jarisch reflex</a> has been described in 10% of cases where a <a href="/wiki/Bradycardia" title="Bradycardia">slow heart rate</a> is associated with <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> A drop in <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a> (either <a href="/wiki/Distributive_shock" title="Distributive shock">distributive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cardiogenic_shock" title="Cardiogenic shock">cardiogenic</a>) may cause the feeling of lightheadedness or <a href="/wiki/Unconsciousness" title="Unconsciousness">loss of consciousness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardio08_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cardio08-15">[15]</a></sup> Rarely very low blood pressure may be the only sign of anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shock10_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shock10-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gastrointestinal symptoms may include crampy <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_pain" title="Abdominal pain">abdominal pain</a>, diarrhea, and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samp2006_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Samp2006-1">[1]</a></sup> There may be confusion, a loss of bladder control or pelvic pain similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterine</a> cramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samp2006_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Samp2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup> Dilation of blood vessels around the brain may cause <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> A feeling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anxiety_(mood)" title="Anxiety (mood)">anxiety</a> or of "impending doom" has also been described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaphylaxis can occur in response to almost any foreign substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup> Common triggers include <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> from <a href="/wiki/Insect_bites_and_stings" title="Insect bites and stings">insect bites or stings</a>, foods, and <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">medication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Foods are the most common trigger in children and young adults while medications and insect bites and stings are more common in older adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Less common causes include: physical factors, biological agents such as <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormonal</a> changes, food additives such as <a href="/wiki/Monosodium_glutamate" title="Monosodium glutamate">monosodium glutamate</a> and food colors, and topical medications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus06_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Aus06-14">[14]</a></sup> Physical factors such as exercise (known as exercise-induced anaphylaxis) or temperature (either hot or cold) may also act as triggers through their direct effects on <a href="/wiki/Mast_cell" title="Mast cell">mast cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APLS07_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-APLS07-18">[18]</a></sup> Events caused by exercise are frequently associated with the ingestion of certain foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular-blocking_drug" title="Neuromuscular-blocking drug">neuromuscular blocking agents</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibiotics</a>, and latex are the most common causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The cause remains unknown in 32–50% of cases, referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiopathic" title="Idiopathic">idiopathic</a> anaphylaxis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editor_2010_223_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-editor_2010_223-20">[20]</a></sup> Six vaccines (MMR, varicella, influenza, hepatitis B, tetanus, meningococcal) are recognized as a cause for anaphylaxis, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_papillomavirus" title="Human papillomavirus">HPV</a> may cause anaphylaxis as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many foods can trigger anaphylaxis; this may occur upon the first known ingestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> Common triggering foods vary around the world. In Western cultures, ingestion of or exposure to <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, certain types of <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> like <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a> are the most prevalent causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">Sesame</a> is common in the Middle East, while rice and <a href="/wiki/Chickpea" title="Chickpea">chickpeas</a> are frequently encountered as sources of anaphylaxis in Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Severe cases are usually caused by ingesting the allergen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> but some people experience a severe reaction upon contact. Children can outgrow their allergies. By age 16, 80% of children with anaphylaxis to milk or eggs and 20% who experience isolated anaphylaxis to peanuts can tolerate these foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any medication may potentially trigger anaphylaxis. The most common are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-lactam_antibiotic" title="Beta-lactam antibiotic">β-lactam antibiotics</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a>) followed by <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-steroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug" title="Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug">NSAIDs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2015_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2015-22">[22]</a></sup> Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibiotics</a> are implicated less frequently, and the reactions to NSAIDs are agent specific meaning that those who are allergic to one NSAID can typically tolerate a different one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2015_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2015-22">[22]</a></sup> Other relatively common causes include <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protamine" title="Protamine">protamine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbalism" title="Herbalism">herbal</a> preparations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Some medications (<a href="/wiki/Vancomycin" title="Vancomycin">vancomycin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast_agent" title="Radiocontrast agent">x-ray contrast</a> among others) cause anaphylaxis by directly triggering mast cell degranulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The frequency of a reaction to an agent partly depends on the frequency of its use and partly on its intrinsic properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drug01_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drug01-23">[23]</a></sup> Anaphylaxis to penicillin or <a href="/wiki/Cephalosporin" title="Cephalosporin">cephalosporins</a> occurs only after it binds to proteins inside the body with some agents binding more easily than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> Anaphylaxis to penicillin occurs once in every 2,000 to 10,000 courses of treatment, with death occurring in fewer than one in every 50,000 courses of treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> Anaphylaxis to aspirin and NSAIDs occurs in about one in every 50,000 persons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> If someone has a reaction to penicillins, his or her risk of a reaction to cephalosporins is greater but still less than one in 1,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The old radiocontrast agents caused reactions in 1% of cases, while the newer lower osmolar agents cause reactions in 0.04% of cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drug01_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Drug01-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venom">Venom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Venom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">Venom</a> from stinging or biting insects such as <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> (ants, bees and wasps) or <a href="/wiki/Triatominae" title="Triatominae">Triatominae</a> (kissing bugs) may cause anaphylaxis in susceptible people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klotz_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2001simonga_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-2001simonga-25">[25]</a></sup> Previous <a href="/wiki/Systemic_disease" title="Systemic disease">systemic reactions</a>, which are anything more than a local reaction around the site of the sting, are a risk factor for future anaphylaxis;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> however, half of fatalities have had no previous systemic reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_factors">Risk factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Risk factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People with <a href="/wiki/Atopy" title="Atopy">atopic</a> diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema">eczema</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Allergic_rhinitis" title="Allergic rhinitis">allergic rhinitis</a> are at high risk of anaphylaxis from food, <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast_agent" title="Radiocontrast agent">radiocontrast agents</a> but not from injectable medications or stings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> One study in children found that 60% had a history of previous atopic diseases, and of children who die from anaphylaxis, more than 90% have asthma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> Those with <a href="/wiki/Mastocytosis" title="Mastocytosis">mastocytosis</a> or of a higher <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">socioeconomic status</a> are at increased risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> The longer the time since the last exposure to the agent in question, the lower the risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaphylaxis is a severe <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergic reaction</a> of rapid onset affecting many <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">body systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tint10_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tint10-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup> It is due to the release of <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory mediators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> from mast cells and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basophil_granulocyte" title="Basophil granulocyte">basophils</a>, typically due to an <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immunologic reaction</a> but sometimes non-immunologic mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immunologic">Immunologic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Immunologic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the immunologic mechanism, <a href="/wiki/Immunoglobulin_E" title="Immunoglobulin E">immunoglobulin E</a> (IgE) binds to the <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigen</a> (the foreign material that provokes the allergic reaction). Antigen-bound IgE then activates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fc%CE%B5RI" title="FcεRI">FcεRI</a> receptors on mast cells and basophils. This leads to the release of inflammatory mediators such as <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a>. These mediators subsequently increase the contraction of bronchial <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle_tissue" title="Smooth muscle tissue">smooth muscles</a>, trigger <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a>, increase the leakage of fluid from blood vessels, and cause heart muscle depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> There is also an immunologic mechanism that does not rely on IgE, but it is not known if this occurs in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-immunologic">Non-immunologic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Non-immunologic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-immunologic mechanisms involve substances that directly cause the <a href="/wiki/Degranulation" title="Degranulation">degranulation</a> of mast cells and basophils. These include agents such as <a href="/wiki/Contrast_medium" title="Contrast medium">contrast medium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioids</a>, temperature (hot or cold), and vibration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APLS07_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-APLS07-18">[18]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfites" title="Sulfites">Sulfites</a> may cause reactions by both immunologic and non-immunologic mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaphylaxis is diagnosed on the basis of a person's signs and symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> When any one of the following three occurs within minutes or hours of exposure to an allergen there is a high likelihood of anaphylaxis:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Involvement of the skin or mucosal tissue plus either <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_distress" title="Respiratory distress">respiratory difficulty</a> or a low <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> causing symptoms</li>
<li>Two or more of the following symptoms after a likely contact with an allergen:
<dl>
<dd>a. Involvement of the skin or <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucosa</a></dd>
<dd>b. <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_distress" title="Respiratory distress">Respiratory difficulties</a></dd>
<dd>c. <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">Low blood pressure</a></dd>
<dd>d. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">Gastrointestinal</a> symptoms</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li>Low blood pressure after exposure to a known allergen</li>
</ol>
<p>Skin involvement may include: hives, itchiness or a swollen tongue among others. Respiratory difficulties may include: shortness of breath, stridor, or low oxygen levels among others. Low blood pressure is defined as a greater than 30% decrease from a person's usual blood pressure. In adults a systolic blood pressure of less than 90 mmHg is often used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>During an attack, blood tests for <a href="/wiki/Tryptase" title="Tryptase">tryptase</a> or <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a> (released from mast cells) might be useful in diagnosing anaphylaxis due to insect stings or medications. However these tests are of limited use if the cause is food or if the person has a normal <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> and they are not <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_and_specificity" title="Sensitivity and specificity">specific</a> for the diagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three main classifications of anaphylaxis. Anaphylactic shock is associated with systemic <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a> that causes <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a> which is by definition 30% lower than the person's baseline or below standard values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shock10_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shock10-13">[13]</a></sup> Biphasic anaphylaxis is the recurrence of symptoms within 1–72 hours with no further exposure to the allergen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-15"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Reports of incidence vary, with some studies claiming as many as 20% of cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BI05_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-BI05-30">[30]</a></sup> The recurrence typically occurs within 8 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> It is managed in the same manner as anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> Pseudoanaphylaxis or anaphylactoid reactions are a type of anaphylaxis that does not involve an allergic reaction but is due to direct mast cell <a href="/wiki/Degranulation" title="Degranulation">degranulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His10_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-His10-31">[31]</a></sup> Non-immune anaphylaxis is the current term used by the <a href="/wiki/World_Allergy_Organization" title="World Allergy Organization">World Allergy Organization</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His10_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-His10-31">[31]</a></sup> with some recommending that the old terminology no longer be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Allergy_test" title="Allergy test">Allergy testing</a> may help in determining the trigger. <a href="/wiki/Skin_allergy_test" title="Skin allergy test">Skin allergy testing</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Patch_test" title="Patch test">patch testing</a>) is available for certain foods and venoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup> Blood testing for specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgE" title="IgE">IgE</a> can be useful to confirm milk, egg, peanut, tree nut and fish allergies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Skin testing is available to confirm <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a> allergies, but is not available for other medications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup> Non-immune forms of anaphylaxis can only be determined by history or exposure to the allergen in question, and not by skin or blood testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His10_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-His10-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_diagnosis">Differential diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Differential diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It can sometimes be difficult to distinguish anaphylaxis from <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syncope_(medicine)" title="Syncope (medicine)">syncope</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panic_attack" title="Panic attack">panic attacks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-16"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Asthma however typically does not entail itching or gastrointestinal symptoms, syncope presents with pallor rather than a rash, and a panic attack may have flushing but does not have hives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-17"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Other conditions that may present similarly include: <a href="/wiki/Scombroid_food_poisoning" title="Scombroid food poisoning">scrombroidosis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anisakis#Anisakiasis" title="Anisakis">anisakiasis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-mortem_findings">Post-mortem findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Post-mortem findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a person who died from anaphylaxis, <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a> may show an "empty heart" attributed to reduced venous return from <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a> and redistribution of intravascular volume from the central to the peripheral compartment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Other signs are laryngeal edema, eosinophilia in lungs, heart and tissues, and evidence of myocardial hypoperfusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DaBroi_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-DaBroi-33">[33]</a></sup> Laboratory findings could detect increased levels of serum <a href="/wiki/Tryptase" title="Tryptase">tryptase</a>, increase in total and specific IgE serum levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DaBroi_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-DaBroi-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Avoidance of the trigger of anaphylaxis is recommended. In cases where this may not be possible, desensitization may be an option. <a href="/wiki/Immunotherapy" title="Immunotherapy">Immunotherapy</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> venoms is effective at desensitizing 80–90% of adults and 98% of children against allergies to <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hornet" title="Hornet">hornets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_jacket" title="Yellow jacket">yellowjackets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fire_ant" title="Fire ant">fire ants</a>. Oral immunotherapy may be effective at desensitizing some people to certain food including milk, eggs, nuts and peanuts; however, adverse effects are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-18"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> For example many people develop an itchy throat, cough, or lip swelling during immunotherapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Desensitization is also possible for many medications, however it is advised that most people simply avoid the agent in question. In those who react to latex it may be important to avoid cross-reactive foods such as avocados, bananas, and potatoes among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-19"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaphylaxis is a <a href="/wiki/Medical_emergency" title="Medical emergency">medical emergency</a> that may require <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">resuscitation</a> measures such as <a href="/wiki/Airway_management" title="Airway management">airway management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">supplemental oxygen</a>, large volumes of <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">intravenous fluids</a>, and close monitoring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> Administration of epinephrine is the treatment of choice with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histamine_antagonist" title="Histamine antagonist">antihistamines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Dexamethasone" title="Dexamethasone">dexamethasone</a>) often used as <a href="/wiki/Adjuncts" title="Adjuncts">adjuncts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> A period of in-hospital observation for between 2 and 24 hours is recommended for people once they have returned to normal due to concerns of biphasic anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-11"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BI05_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-BI05-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK08_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK08-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epinephrine">Epinephrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Epinephrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epipen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="500" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Epipen.jpg/290px-Epipen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Epipen.jpg/435px-Epipen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/60/Epipen.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epipen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An old version of an EpiPen auto-injector</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">Epinephrine</a> (adrenaline) is the primary treatment for anaphylaxis with no absolute <a href="/wiki/Contraindication" title="Contraindication">contraindication</a> to its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> It is recommended that an epinephrine solution be given <a href="/wiki/Intramuscular_injection" title="Intramuscular injection">intramuscularly</a> into the mid anterolateral thigh as soon as the diagnosis is suspected. The injection may be repeated every 5 to 15 minutes if there is insufficient response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> A second dose is needed in 16-35% of episodes with more than two doses rarely required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> The intramuscular route is preferred over <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">subcutaneous</a> administration because the latter may have delayed absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi10_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epi10-36">[36]</a></sup> Minor adverse effects from epinephrine include <a href="/wiki/Tremor" title="Tremor">tremors</a>, anxiety, headaches, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palpitation" title="Palpitation">palpitations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-20"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>People on <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">β-blockers</a> may be resistant to the effects of epinephrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> In this situation if epinephrine is not effective intravenous <a href="/wiki/Glucagon" title="Glucagon">glucagon</a> can be administered which has a mechanism of action independent of <a href="/wiki/Adrenergic_receptor" title="Adrenergic receptor">β-receptors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>If necessary, it can also be given <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">intravenously</a> using a dilute epinephrine solution. Intravenous epinephrine, however, has been associated both with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_dysrhythmia" title="Cardiac dysrhythmia">dysrhythmia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine_autoinjector" title="Epinephrine autoinjector">Epinephrine autoinjectors</a> used for self-administration typically come in two doses, one for adults or children who weigh more than 25 kg and one for children who weigh 10 to 25 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjuncts">Adjuncts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Adjuncts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histamine_antagonist" title="Histamine antagonist">Antihistamines</a> (both <a href="/wiki/H1_antagonist" title="H1 antagonist">H1</a> and <a href="/wiki/H2_antagonist" title="H2 antagonist">H2</a>), while commonly used and assumed effective based on theoretical reasoning, are poorly supported by evidence. A 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochrane_Collaboration" title="Cochrane Collaboration">Cochrane</a> review did not find any good-quality studies upon which to base recommendations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and they are not believed to have an effect on airway edema or spasm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">Corticosteroids</a> are unlikely to make a difference in the current episode of anaphylaxis, but may be used in the hope of decreasing the risk of biphasic anaphylaxis. Their prophylactic effectiveness in these situations is uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BI05_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-BI05-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nebulizer" title="Nebulizer">Nebulized</a> <a href="/wiki/Salbutamol" title="Salbutamol">salbutamol</a> may be effective for <a href="/wiki/Bronchospasm" title="Bronchospasm">bronchospasm</a> that does not resolve with epinephrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-17"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methylene_blue" title="Methylene blue">Methylene blue</a> has been used in those not responsive to other measures due to its presumed effect of relaxing smooth muscle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-18"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparedness">Preparedness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Preparedness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People prone to anaphylaxis are advised to have an "allergy action plan." Parents are advised to inform schools of their children's allergies and what to do in case of an anaphylactic emergency. The action plan usually includes use of <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine_autoinjector" title="Epinephrine autoinjector">epinephrine autoinjectors</a>, the recommendation to wear a <a href="/wiki/Medical_identification_tag" title="Medical identification tag">medical alert bracelet</a>, and counseling on avoidance of triggers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mart08_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mart08-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allergen_immunotherapy" title="Allergen immunotherapy">Immunotherapy</a> is available for certain triggers to prevent future episodes of anaphylaxis. A multi-year course of subcutaneous desensitization has been found effective against stinging insects, while oral desensitization is effective for many foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In those in whom the cause is known and prompt treatment is available, the prognosis is good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Even if the cause is unknown, if appropriate preventative medication is available, the prognosis is generally good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-12"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> If death occurs, it is usually due to either respiratory (typically <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a>) or cardiovascular causes (<a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khan11_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Khan11-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-19"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> with 0.7–20% of cases causing death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-13"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardio08_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cardio08-15">[15]</a></sup> There have been cases of death occurring within minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-21"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup> Outcomes in those with exercise-induced anaphylaxis are typically good, with fewer and less severe episodes as people get older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editor_2010_223_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-editor_2010_223-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The number of people who get anaphylaxis is 4–100 per 100,000 persons per year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-20"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tej2015_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tej2015-41">[41]</a></sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_incidence" title="Cumulative incidence">lifetime risk</a> of 0.05–2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> About 30% of people get more than one attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tej2015_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tej2015-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rates appear to be increasing: with the numbers in the 1980s being approximately 20 per 100,000 per year, while in the 1990s it was 50 per 100,000 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-12">[12]</a></sup> The increase appears to be primarily for food-induced anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The risk is greatest in young people and females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAACI2014_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-EAACI2014-5">[5]</a></sup>&gt;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-21"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently, anaphylaxis leads to 500–1,000 deaths per year (2.4 per million) in the United States, 20 deaths per year in the United Kingdom (0.33 per million), and 15 deaths per year in Australia (0.64 per million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-22"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> Mortality rates have decreased between the 1970s and 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In Australia, death from food-induced anaphylaxis occur primarily in women while deaths due to insect bites primarily occur in males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-23"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> Death from anaphylaxis is most commonly triggered by medications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-24"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>aphylaxis</i> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Richet" title="Charles Richet">Charles Richet</a> in 1902 and later changed to <i>anaphylaxis</i> due to its nicer quality of speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His11_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-His11-16">[16]</a></sup> In his experiments, Richet injected a dog with <a href="/wiki/Sea_anemone" title="Sea anemone">sea anemone</a> (<i>Actinia</i>) toxin in an attempt to protect it. Although the dog had previously tolerated the toxin, on re-exposure with the same dose three weeks later it developed fatal anaphylaxis. Thus instead of inducing tolerance (prophylaxis), when lethal responses resulted from previously tolerated doses, he coined the word <i>a</i> (without) <i>phylaxis</i> (protection). <i>Ana</i> means "against" in Ancient Greek, but (as stated above) the word was changed for sound. He was subsequently awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> for his work on anaphylaxis in 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2010_11-14"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The phenomenon itself, however, has been described since ancient times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-His10_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-His10-31">[31]</a></sup> The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words ἀνά, <i>ana</i>, "against", and φύλαξις, <i>phylaxis</i>, "protection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dict_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dict-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaphylaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are ongoing efforts to develop <a href="/wiki/Sublingual_administration" title="Sublingual administration">sublingual</a> epinephrine to treat anaphylaxis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEA11_7-25"><a href="#cite_note-CEA11-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_injection" title="Subcutaneous injection">Subcutaneous injection</a> of the anti-IgE antibody <a href="/wiki/Omalizumab" title="Omalizumab">omalizumab</a> is being studied as a method of preventing recurrence, but it is not yet recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World11_6-22"><a href="#cite_note-World11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
