<p><b>Hyperpolarization</b> is a change in a <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell's</a> <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">membrane potential</a> that makes it more negative. It is the opposite of a <a href="/wiki/Depolarization" title="Depolarization">depolarization</a>. It inhibits <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potentials</a> by increasing the stimulus required to move the membrane potential to the action potential threshold.</p>
<p>Hyperpolarization is often caused by efflux of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K<sup>+</sup></a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>) through <a href="/wiki/Potassium_channel" title="Potassium channel">K<sup>+</sup> channels</a>, or influx of <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">Cl<sup>–</sup></a> (an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>) through <a href="/wiki/Chloride_channel" title="Chloride channel">Cl<sup>–</sup> channels</a>. On the other hand, influx of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na<sup>+</sup></a> through <a href="/wiki/Sodium_channel" title="Sodium channel">Na<sup>+</sup> channels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca<sup>2+</sup></a> through <a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel" title="Calcium channel">Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels</a>, inhibits hyperpolarization. If a cell has Na<sup>+</sup> or Ca<sup>2+</sup> currents at rest, then inhibition of those currents will also result in a hyperpolarization. This voltage-gated ion channel response is how the hyperpolarization state is achieved. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurons" title="Neurons">neurons</a>, the cell enters a state of hyperpolarization immediately following the generation of an action potential. While hyperpolarized, the neuron is in a refractory period that lasts roughly 2 milliseconds, during which the neuron is unable to generate subsequent action potentials. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium-potassium_ATPase" title="Sodium-potassium ATPase">Sodium-potassium ATPases</a> redistribute K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> ions until the membrane potential is back to its <a href="/wiki/Resting_potential" title="Resting potential">resting potential</a> of around –70 millivolts, at which point the neuron is once again ready to transmit another action potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voltage-gated_ion_channels_and_hyperpolarization">Voltage-gated ion channels and hyperpolarization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperpolarization_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Voltage-gated ion channels and hyperpolarization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1583" data-file-width="1117" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg/220px-Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg/330px-Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg/440px-Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ion_channel_activity_before_during_and_after_polarization.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>(a)</i> resting membrane potential is a result of different concentrations of Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> ions inside and outside the cell. A nerve impulse causes Na<sup>+</sup> to enter the cell, resulting in <i>(b)</i> depolarization. At the peak action potential, K<sup>+</sup> channels open and the cell becomes <i>(c)</i> hyperpolarized.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Voltage gated <a href="/wiki/Ion_channel" title="Ion channel">ion channels</a> respond to changes in the membrane potential, generating the action potential as well as hyperpolarization. These channels work by selecting an ion based on electrostatic attraction or repulsion allowing the ion to bind to the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becker_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-2">[2]</a></sup> This releases water molecules attached to the ion, and the ion passes through the pore.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Resting_potential" title="Resting potential">resting potential</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Gating_(electrophysiology)" title="Gating (electrophysiology)">voltage gated</a> sodium and potassium channels are closed, but as the cell membrane becomes depolarized the voltage gated sodium channels begin to open and the neuron begins to depolarize, creating a <a href="/wiki/Minor_loop_feedback" title="Minor loop feedback">current feedback loop</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Hodgkin_cycle" title="Hodgkin cycle">Hodgkin cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becker_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-2">[2]</a></sup> However, potassium ions naturally move out of the cell and if the original depolarization event was not significant enough then the neuron does not generate an action potential. If all the sodium channels are open, however, then the neuron becomes ten times more permeable to sodium than potassium, quickly depolarizing the cell to a peak of +40 mV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becker_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-2">[2]</a></sup> At this level the sodium channels begin to inactivate (<a href="/wiki/Ball_and_chain_inactivation" title="Ball and chain inactivation">ball and chain model</a>) and voltage gated potassium channels begin to open. This combination of closed sodium channels and open potassium channels leads to the neuron repolarizing. The neuron continues to repolarize until the cell reaches approximately -75 mV,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becker_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-2">[2]</a></sup> which is the equilibrium potential of potassium ions. At this point the potassium channels close and the natural permeability of the neuron to sodium and potassium allows the neuron to return to its resting potential of -70 mV. During the following <a href="/wiki/Refractory_period_(physiology)" title="Refractory period (physiology)">refractory period</a>, which is accompanied by an <a href="/wiki/Afterhyperpolarization" title="Afterhyperpolarization">afterhyperpolarization</a>, the neuron is incapable of triggering an action potential due to the sodium channels inability to open.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/HCN_channel" title="HCN channel">HCN channels</a> are activated by hyperpolarization.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_technique">Experimental technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperpolarization_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Experimental technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Patch_pipette_model.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="141" data-file-width="200" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Patch_pipette_model.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Patch_pipette_model.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This image shows a model of a patch clamp used in neuroscience. The pipette tip is placed at an ion channel opening and a current is applied and measured using a voltage clamp.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hyperpolarization is a change in membrane potential, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientists</a> measure it using a technique known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patch_clamping" title="Patch clamping">patch clamping</a>. Using this method they are able to record ion currents passing through individual channels. This is done using a glass micropipette, also called a patch pipette, with a 1 micrometer diameter. There is a small patch that contains a few ion channels and the rest is sealed off, making this the point of entry for the current. Using an amplifier and a <a href="/wiki/Voltage_clamp" title="Voltage clamp">voltage clamp</a>, which is an electronic feedback circuit, allows the experimenter to maintain the membrane potential at a fixed point and the voltage clamp then measures tiny changes in current flow. The membrane currents giving rise to hyperpolarization are either an increase in outward current or a decrease in inward current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becker_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperpolarization_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apshoot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="542" data-file-width="732" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Apshoot.jpg/300px-Apshoot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Apshoot.jpg/450px-Apshoot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Apshoot.jpg/600px-Apshoot.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apshoot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of membrane potential changes during an action potential</div>
</div>
</div>
<ol>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/Afterhyperpolarization" title="Afterhyperpolarization">afterhyperpolarization</a> period after an <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>, the membrane potential is more negative than when the cell is at the <a href="/wiki/Resting_potential" title="Resting potential">resting potential</a>. In the figure to the right, this undershoot occurs at approximately 3 to 4 milliseconds (ms) on the time scale. The afterhyperpolarization is the time when the membrane potential is hyperpolarized relative to the resting potential.</li>
<li>During the rising phase of an action potential, the membrane potential changes from negative to positive, a depolarization. In the figure, the rising phase is from approximately 1 to 2 ms on the graph. During the rising phase, once the membrane potential becomes positive, the membrane potential continues to depolarize (overshoot) until the peak of the action potential is reached at about +40 millivolts (mV). After the peak of the action potential, a hyperpolarization repolarizes the membrane potential to its resting value, first by making it less positive, until 0 mV is reached, and then by continuing to make it more negative. This repolarization occurs in the figure from approximately 2 to 3 ms on the time scale.</li>
</ol>
