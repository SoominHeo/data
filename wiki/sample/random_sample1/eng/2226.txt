<p><b>Confectionery</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> also called <b>sweets</b> or <b>candy</b>, is sweet food. The term varies among English-speaking countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In general, though, confectionery is divided into two broad and somewhat overlapping categories, <b>bakers' confections</b> and <b>sugar confections.</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bakers' confectionery, also called <b>flour confections</b>, includes principally sweet pastries, cakes, and similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baked_goods" title="Baked goods">baked goods</a>. In the Middle East and Asia, flour-based confections are more dominant.</p>
<p>Sugar confectionery includes sweets, candied nuts, chocolates, chewing gum and bubblegum, sweetmeats, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastillage" title="Pastillage">pastillage</a>, and other confections that are made primarily of sugar. In some cases, <b>chocolate confections</b> (confections made of <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>) (thanks, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Percepto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Percepto (page does not exist)">Percepto</a>) are treated as a separate category, as are sugar-free versions of sugar confections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The words <i><a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">candy</a></i> (US and Canada), <i>sweets</i> (UK and Ireland), and <i>lollies</i> (Australia and New Zealand) are common words for the most common <a href="/wiki/List_of_candies" title="List of candies">varieties of sugar confectionery</a>.</p>
<p>The confectionery industry also includes specialized training schools and extensive historical records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Traditional confectionery goes back to ancient times, and continued to be eaten through the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_cuisine#Sweets_and_desserts" title="Medieval cuisine">Middle Ages</a> into the modern era.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally, confections are low in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micronutrients" title="Micronutrients">micronutrients</a> and protein but high in calories. They may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fat-free" title="Fat-free">fat-free foods</a>, although some confections, especially fried doughs, are high-fat foods. Many confections are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empty_calories" title="Empty calories">empty calories</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States_military_chocolate" title="United States military chocolate">Specially formulated chocolate</a> has been manufactured in the past for military use as a high-density <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> source.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweetening_agents">Sweetening agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sweetening agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Confections are defined by the presence of sweeteners. These are usually sugars, but it is possible to buy sugar-free sweets, such as sugar-free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peppermints" title="Peppermints">peppermints</a>. The most common sweetener for home cooking is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_sugar" title="Table sugar">table sugar</a>, which is chemically a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a></i>. Hydrolysis of sucrose gives a mixture called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invert_sugar" title="Invert sugar">invert sugar</a>, which is sweeter and is also a common commercial ingredient. Finally confections, especially commercial ones, are sweetened by a variety of syrups obtained by <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>. These sweeteners include all types of <a href="/wiki/Corn_syrup" title="Corn syrup">corn syrup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bakers.27_confectionery">Bakers' confectionery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bakers' confectionery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cakes" title="List of cakes">List of cakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_cookies" title="List of cookies">List of cookies</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_doughnut_varieties" title="List of doughnut varieties">List of doughnut varieties</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_pastries" title="List of pastries">List of pastries</a></div>

<p>Bakers' confectionery includes sweet baked goods, especially those that are served for the <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">dessert</a> course. Bakers' confections are sweet foods that feature <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> as a main ingredient and are <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baked</a>. Major categories include <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cakes</a>, sweet <a href="/wiki/Pastry" title="Pastry">pastries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doughnut" title="Doughnut">doughnuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scone" title="Scone">scones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cookie" title="Cookie">cookies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sugar_confectionery">Sugar confectionery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sugar confectionery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sugar confections include sweet, sugar-based foods, which are usually eaten as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snack_food" title="Snack food">snack food</a>. This includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_candies" title="Sugar candies">sugar candies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candied_fruit" title="Candied fruit">candied fruits</a> and nuts, <a href="/wiki/Chewing_gum" title="Chewing gum">chewing gum</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>.</p>
<p>The United Nations' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Standard_Industrial_Classification_of_All_Economic_Activities" title="International Standard Industrial Classification of All Economic Activities">International Standard Industrial Classification of All Economic Activities</a> (ISIC) scheme (revision 4) classifies both chocolate and sugar confectionery as ISIC 1073, which includes the manufacture of chocolate and chocolate confectionery; sugar confectionery proper (caramels, cachous, nougats, fondant, white chocolate), chewing gum, preserving fruit, nuts, fruit peels, and making confectionery lozenges and pastilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the European Union, the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_Classification_of_Economic_Activities_in_the_European_Community" title="Statistical Classification of Economic Activities in the European Community">Statistical Classification of Economic Activities in the European Community</a> (NACE) scheme (revision 2) matches the UN classification, under code number 10.82. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Industry_Classification_System" title="North American Industry Classification System">North American Industry Classification System</a> (NAICS 2012) splits sugar confectionery across three categories: National industry code 311340 for all non-chocolate confectionery manufacturing, 311351 for chocolate and confectionery manufacturing from cacao beans, and national industry 311352 for confectionery manufacturing from purchased chocolate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Ice cream and sorbet are classified with dairy products under ISIC 1050, NACE 10.52, and NAICS 311520.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different dialects of English use regional terms for sugar confections:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Ireland</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth countries</a>, <i>sweets</i> or, more colloquially, <i>sweeties</i> (particularly used by children, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> word <i>suiteis</i> is a derivative).</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand</a>, <i>lollies</i>. <i>Chewy</i> and <i>Chuddy</i> are Australian slang for chewing gum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">North America</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">candy</a></i>, although this term generally refers to a specific range of confectionery and does not include some items of sugar confectionery (e.g. ice cream). <i>Sweet</i> is occasionally used, as well as <i>treat</i>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Sugar confectionery items include sweets, <a href="/wiki/Lollipop" title="Lollipop">lollipops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candy_bar" title="Candy bar">candy bars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton_candy" title="Cotton candy">cotton candy</a>, and other sweet items of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snack_food" title="Snack food">snack food</a>. Some of the categories and types of sugar confectionery include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caramel" title="Caramel">Caramels</a>: Derived from a mixture of sucrose, glucose syrup, and milk products. The mixture does not crystallize, thus remains tacky.</li>
<li>Chocolates: Bite-sized confectioneries generally made with <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Divinity_(confectionery)" title="Divinity (confectionery)">Divinity</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Nougat" title="Nougat">nougat</a>-like confectionery based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">egg</a> whites with chopped <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dodol" title="Dodol">Dodol</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Toffee" title="Toffee">toffee</a>-like delicacy popular in Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drag%C3%A9e" title="Dragée">Dragée</a>: Sugar-coated <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a> and other types of <a href="/wiki/Sugar_panning" title="Sugar panning">sugar panned</a> candy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fondant_icing" title="Fondant icing">Fondant</a>: Prepared from a warm mixture of glucose syrup and sucrose, which is partially crystallized. The fineness of the crystallites results in a creamy texture.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fudge" title="Fudge">Fudge</a>: Made by boiling milk and sugar to the soft-ball stage. In the US, it tends to be chocolate-flavored.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Halva" title="Halva">Halvah</a>: Confectionery based on <a href="/wiki/Tahini" title="Tahini">tahini</a>, a paste made from ground <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> seeds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hard_candy" title="Hard candy">Hard candy</a>: Based on sugars cooked to the hard-crack stage. Examples include suckers (known as <i>boiled sweets</i> in British English), <a href="/wiki/Lollipop" title="Lollipop">lollipops</a>, jawbreakers (or <a href="/wiki/Gobstopper" title="Gobstopper">gobstoppers</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lemon_drops" title="Lemon drops">lemon drops</a>, peppermint drops and disks, <a href="/wiki/Candy_cane" title="Candy cane">candy canes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_candy" title="Rock candy">rock candy</a>, etc. Also included are types often mixed with nuts such as <a href="/wiki/Brittle_(food)" title="Brittle (food)">brittle</a>. Others contain flavorings including coffee such as <a href="/wiki/Kopiko_(confectionery)" title="Kopiko (confectionery)">Kopiko</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">Ice cream</a>: Frozen, flavoured cream, often containing small pieces of chocolate, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>.</li>
<li>Jelly candies: Including those based on sugar and starch, <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a>, gum, or <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_Delight" title="Turkish Delight">Turkish delight (lokum)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jelly_bean" title="Jelly bean">jelly beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gumdrop" title="Gumdrop">gumdrops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jujube_(confectionery)" title="Jujube (confectionery)">jujubes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gummi_candy" title="Gummi candy">gummies</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquorice_(confectionery)" title="Liquorice (confectionery)">Liquorice</a>: Containing extract of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquorice_root" title="Liquorice root">liquorice root</a>. Chewier and more resilient than gum/gelatin candies, but still designed for swallowing. For example, <a href="/wiki/Liquorice_allsorts" title="Liquorice allsorts">Liquorice allsorts</a>. Has a similar taste to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_anise" title="Star anise">star anise</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marshmallow" title="Marshmallow">Marshmallow</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Peeps" title="Peeps">Peeps</a>" (a trade name), <a href="/wiki/Circus_peanut" title="Circus peanut">circus peanuts</a>, fluffy puff<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Jet-Puffed_Marshmallows" title="Jet-Puffed Marshmallows">Jet-Puffed Marshmallows</a> etc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marzipan" title="Marzipan">Marzipan</a>: An <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a>-based confection, doughy in consistency, served in several different ways.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithai" title="Mithai">Mithai</a>: A generic term for confectionery in India, typically made from dairy products and/or some form of flour. Sugar or molasses are used as sweeteners.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Persipan" title="Persipan">Persipan</a>: similar to marzipan, but made with peaches or apricots instead of almonds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tablet_(confectionery)" title="Tablet (confectionery)">Tablet</a>: A crumbly milk-based soft and hard candy, based on sugars cooked to the soft ball stage. Comes in several forms, such as wafers and heart shapes. Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Tableting" title="Tableting">tableting</a>, a method of candy production.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Taffy_(candy)" title="Taffy (candy)">Taffy</a> or chews: A candy that is folded many times above 120 °F (50 °C), incorporating air bubbles thus reducing its density and making it opaque.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toffee" title="Toffee">Toffee</a>: a confection made by caramelizing sugar or molasses along with butter. Toffee has a glossy surface and textures ranging from soft and sticky to a hard, brittle material. Its brown color and smoky taste arises from the caramelization of the sugars.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_roles">Cultural roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Both bakers' and sugar confections are used to offer <a href="/wiki/Hospitality" title="Hospitality">hospitality</a> to guests.</p>
<p>Confections are used to mark celebrations or events, such as a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_cake" title="Wedding cake">wedding cake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birthday_cake" title="Birthday cake">birthday cake</a> or <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tourists" title="Tourists">Tourists</a> commonly eat confections as part of their travels. The indulgence in rich, sugary foods is seen as a special treat, and choosing local specialties is popular. For example, visitors to Vienna eat <a href="/wiki/Sachertorte" title="Sachertorte">Sachertorte</a> and visitors to seaside resorts in the UK eat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackpool_rock" title="Blackpool rock">Blackpool rock</a> candy. Transportable confections like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fudges" title="Fudges">fudges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(confectionery)" title="Tablet (confectionery)">tablet</a> may be purchased as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Souvenirs" title="Souvenirs">souvenirs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks">Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Confectionery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Excessive consumption of confectionery has been associated with increased incidences of <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_2" title="Diabetes mellitus type 2">type 2 diabetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tooth_decay" title="Tooth decay">tooth decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD280109_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD280109-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contaminants and <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">coloring agents</a> in confectionery can be particularly harmful to children. Therefore, confectionery contaminants, such as high levels of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, have been restricted to 1 ppm in the US. There is no specific maximum in the EU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Candy colorants, particularly yellow colorants such as <a href="/wiki/Tartrazine" title="Tartrazine">E102 Tartrazine</a>, E104 Quinoline Yellow and E110 Sunset Yellow FCF, do have many restrictions around the world. Tartrazine, for example, can cause allergic and asthmatic reactions and was once banned in Austria, Germany, and Norway. Some countries such as the UK have asked the food industry to phase out the use of these colorants, especially for products marketed to children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
