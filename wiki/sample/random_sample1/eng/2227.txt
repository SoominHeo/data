<p>The <b>quetzal</b> (<small>locally: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[keˈtsal]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/ISO_4217" title="ISO 4217">code</a>: <b>GTQ</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, named after the national bird of Guatemala, the <a href="/wiki/Resplendent_quetzal" title="Resplendent quetzal">resplendent quetzal</a>.</p>
<p>In ancient <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Mayan</a> culture, the quetzal bird's tail feathers were used as currency. It is divided into 100 <i>centavos</i> or <i>lenes</i> in Guatemalan <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a>. The plural is <i>quetzales</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guatemalan_quetzal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The quetzal was introduced in 1925 during the term of President <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Orellana" title="José María Orellana">José María Orellana</a>, whose image appears on the obverse of the one-quetzal bill. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_peso" title="Guatemalan peso">peso</a>. Until 1987, the quetzal was pegged to and domestically equal to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> and before the pegging to the US dollar, it was pegged to the <a href="/wiki/French_franc" title="French franc">French franc</a> as well, since the quetzal utilized the gold standard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins">Coins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guatemalan_quetzal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1925, <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a> in denominations of 1, 5, 10 centavos, ¼, ½ and 1 quetzal were introduced, although the majority of the 1 quetzal coins were withdrawn from circulation and melted. ½ and 2 centavos coins were added in 1932. Until 1965, coins of 5 centavos and above were minted in 72% silver. ½ and 1 quetzal coins were reintroduced in 1998 and 1999, respectively. Coins currently in circulation are:</p>
<ul>
<li>1 centavo</li>
<li>5 centavos</li>
<li>10 centavos</li>
<li>25 centavos</li>
<li>50 centavos</li>
<li>1 quetzal</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Banknotes">Banknotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guatemalan_quetzal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Banknotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first banknotes were issued by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Guatemala" title="Central Bank of Guatemala">Central Bank of Guatemala</a> in denominations of 1, 2, 5, 10, 20 and 100 quetzales, with ½ quetzal notes added in 1933. In 1946, the Bank of Guatemala took over the issuance of <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">paper money</a>, with its first issues being overprints on notes of the Central Bank. Except for the introduction of 50 quetzales notes in 1967, the denominations of banknotes were unchanged until ½ and 1 quetzal coins replaced notes at the end of the 1990s.</p>
<p>In the top-right corner of the obverse face of each banknote, the value is displayed in <a href="/wiki/Maya_numerals" title="Maya numerals">Mayan numerals</a>, representing Guatemala's cultural history.</p>
