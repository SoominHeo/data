<p>The <b>Museum of Science and Industry</b> (<b>MSI</b>) is located in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Park_(Chicago)" title="Jackson Park (Chicago)">Jackson Park</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Chicago" title="Hyde Park, Chicago">Hyde Park</a> neighborhood between <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Chicago" title="The University of Chicago">The University of Chicago</a>. It is housed in the former Palace of Fine Arts from the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a>. Initially endowed by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Rosenwald" title="Julius Rosenwald">Julius Rosenwald</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck_and_Company" title="Sears, Roebuck and Company">Sears, Roebuck and Company</a> president and philanthropist, it was supported by the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Club_of_Chicago" title="Commercial Club of Chicago">Commercial Club of Chicago</a> and opened in 1933 during the <a href="/wiki/Century_of_Progress" title="Century of Progress">Century of Progress Exposition</a>.</p>
<p>Among the museum's exhibits are a full-size replica <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-505" title="German submarine U-505">German submarine <i>U-505</i></a> captured during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a 3,500-square-foot (330 m<sup>2</sup>) model railroad, the first diesel-powered streamlined stainless-steel passenger train (<i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Zephyr" title="Pioneer Zephyr">Pioneer Zephyr</a></i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> spacecraft that carried the first humans to orbit the Moon.</p>
<p>Based on 2009 attendance, the Museum of Science and Industry was the second largest cultural attraction in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> David R. Mosena has been President and CEO of the Museum since 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_(Chicago)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Palace of Fine Arts (also known as the Fine Arts Building) at the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_B._Atwood" title="Charles B. Atwood">Charles B. Atwood</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D._H._Burnham_%26_Co." title="D. H. Burnham &amp; Co.">D. H. Burnham &amp; Co.</a> Unlike the other "White City" buildings, it was constructed with a brick substructure under its plaster facade. After the World's Fair, it initially housed the Columbian Museum, which evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Field_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Field Museum of Natural History">Field Museum of Natural History</a>. When the Field Museum moved to a new building near downtown Chicago in 1920, the former site was left vacant.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Art_Institute_of_Chicago" title="Art Institute of Chicago">Art Institute of Chicago</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Lorado_Taft" title="Lorado Taft">Lorado Taft</a> led a public campaign to restore the building and turn it into another art museum, one devoted to sculpture. The South Park Commissioners (now part of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Park_District" title="Chicago Park District">Chicago Park District</a>) won approval in a referendum to sell $5 million in bonds to pay for restoration costs, hoping to turn the building into a sculpture museum, a technical trade school, and other things. However, after a few years, the building was selected as the site for a new science museum.</p>
<p>At this time, the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Club_of_Chicago" title="Commercial Club of Chicago">Commercial Club of Chicago</a> was interested in establishing a science museum in Chicago. <a href="/wiki/Julius_Rosenwald" title="Julius Rosenwald">Julius Rosenwald</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck_and_Company" title="Sears, Roebuck and Company">Sears, Roebuck and Company</a> president and philanthropist, energized his fellow club members by pledging to pay $3 million towards the cost of converting the Palace of Fine Arts (Rosenwald eventually contributed more than $5 million to the project). During its conversion into the MSI, the building's exterior was re-cast in limestone to retain its 1893 <a href="/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture" title="Beaux-Arts architecture">Beaux Arts</a> look. The interior was replaced with a new one in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_Moderne" title="Art Moderne">Art Moderne</a> style designed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alfred P. Shaw (page does not exist)">Alfred P. Shaw</a>.</p>
<p>Rosenwald established the museum organization in 1926 but declined to have his name on the building. For the first few years, the museum was often called the Rosenwald Industrial Museum. In 1928, the name of the museum was officially changed to the Museum of Science and Industry. Rosenwald's vision was to create a museum in the style of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, which he had visited in 1911 while in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> with his family.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Sewell_Avery" title="Sewell Avery">Sewell Avery</a>, another businessman, had supported the museum within the Commercial Club and was selected as its first president of the board of directors. The museum conducted a nationwide search for the first director. MSI's Board of Directors selected <a href="/wiki/Waldemar_Kaempffert" title="Waldemar Kaempffert">Waldemar Kaempffert</a>, then the science editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, because he shared Rosenwald's vision.</p>
<p>He assembled the museum's curatorial staff and directed the organizing and constructing the exhibits. In order to prepare the museum, Kaempffert and his staff visited the Deutsches Museum in Munich, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_Museum_(London)" title="Science Museum (London)">Science Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Technisches_Museum_Wien" title="Technisches Museum Wien">Technical Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, all of which served as models. Kaempffert was instrumental in developing close ties with the science departments of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, which supplied much of the scholarship for the exhibits. Kaempffert resigned in early 1931 amid growing disputes with the second president of the board of directors; they disagreed over the objectivity and neutrality of the exhibits and Kaempffert's management of the staff.</p>
<p>The new Museum of Science and Industry opened to the public in three stages between 1933 and 1940. The first opening ceremony took place during the <a href="/wiki/Century_of_Progress" title="Century of Progress">Century of Progress Exposition</a>. Two of the museum's presidents, a number of curators and other staff members, and exhibits came to MSI from the Century of Progress event.</p>
<p>For years visitors entered the museum through its original main entrance, but that entrance was no longer large enough to handle an increasing volume of visitors. The newer main entrance is a structure detached from the main museum building, through which visitors descend into an underground area and re-ascend into the main building, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Louvre_Pyramid" title="Louvre Pyramid">Louvre Pyramid</a>.</p>
<p>For over 55 years, admission to the MSI was free. Fees began to be charged during the early 1990s, with general admission rates increasing from $13 in 2008 to $18 in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Many "free days" are offered throughout the year, but those free days are restricted to <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> residents showing proof of residence in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The museum has over 2,000 exhibits, displayed in 75 major halls. The museum has several major permanent exhibits: The "Coal Mine" re-creates a working deep-shaft, <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a> mine inside the museum's Central Pavilion, using original equipment from Old Ben #17 circa 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Since 1954, the museum has had the <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-505" title="German submarine U-505">German submarine <i>U-505</i></a>, one of just two German submarines captured during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and the only one on display in the western hemisphere. Access to several of the exhibits (including the coal mine and tour of <i>U-505</i>) require the payment of an additional fee.</p>
<p>The museum opened "The New U-505 Experience" on June 5, 2005. "Take Flight" features a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727" title="Boeing 727">Boeing 727</a> jet plane donated by <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>, with one wing removed and holes cut on the fuselage to facilitate visitor access. Silent-film star and stock-market investor <a href="/wiki/Colleen_Moore" title="Colleen Moore">Colleen Moore</a>'s Fairy Castle "dolls house" is on display. "<a href="/wiki/The_Great_Train_Story" title="The Great Train Story">The Great Train Story</a>", a 3,500-square-foot (330 m<sup>2</sup>) model railroad, recounts the story of transportation from Chicago to <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.</p>
<p>The Transportation Zone includes exhibits on air and land transportation, including the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_and_Hudson_River_Railroad_No._999" title="New York Central and Hudson River Railroad No. 999"><i>999 Empire State Express</i></a> steam locomotive, which the museum claims is the first vehicle to exceed 100 miles per hour (160 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[a]</a></sup> The Transportation Zone also includes two <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> warplanes donated by the British government: a German Ju 87 R-2/Trop. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuka" title="Stuka">Stuka</a> divebomber — one of only two intact Stukas left in the world — and a British <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a>. The first diesel-powered streamlined stainless-steel train, the <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Zephyr" title="Pioneer Zephyr">Pioneer Zephyr</a></i>, is on permanent display in the Great Hall, renamed the Entry Hall in 2008. A complimentary tour goes through it every 10–20 minutes. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> warship models are on display, and a flight simulator for the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-35_Lightning_II" title="F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a> is featured.</p>
<p>In keeping with Rosenwald's vision, many of the exhibits are interactive, ranging from "Genetics: Decoding Life", which looks at how genetics affect human and animal development as well as containing a chick hatchery composed of an incubator where baby chickens hatch from their eggs and a chick pen for those that have already hatched, to "ToyMaker 3000", a working assembly line that lets visitors order a toy top and watch as it is made. The interactive "<a href="/wiki/Fab_Lab_MSI" title="Fab Lab MSI">Fab Lab MSI</a>" is intended as an interactive lab where members can "build anything".</p>
<p>In March 2010, the museum opened "Science Storms" in the <a href="/wiki/Allstate" title="Allstate">Allstate</a> Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> This multilevel exhibit features a 40-foot (12 m) water vapor tornado, tsunami tank, <a href="/wiki/Tesla_coil" title="Tesla coil">Tesla coil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heliostat" title="Heliostat">heliostat</a> system, and a <a href="/wiki/Wimshurst_machine" title="Wimshurst machine">Wimshurst machine</a> built by <a href="/wiki/James_Wimshurst" title="James Wimshurst">James Wimshurst</a> in the late 19th century. All artifacts allow guests to explore the physics and chemistry of the natural world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>MSI's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Crown_Space_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Crown Space Center (page does not exist)">Henry Crown Space Center</a> includes the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> spacecraft, which flew the first mission beyond low earth orbit to the Moon, enabling its crew, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">James Lovell</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>, to become the first human beings to see the Earth as a whole, as well as becoming the first to view the Moon up close (as well as the first to view its far side). Other exhibits include an <a href="/wiki/IMAX#IMAX_Dome.2FOMNIMAX" title="IMAX">OmniMax</a> theater, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Carpenter" title="Scott Carpenter">Scott Carpenter</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_7" title="Mercury-Atlas 7">Mercury-Atlas 7</a> spacecraft, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_module" title="Lunar module">lunar module</a> trainer and a life-size mockup of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a>.</p>
<p>The museum is known for unique and quirky permanent exhibits, such as a walk-through model of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_heart" title="Human heart">human heart</a>. It was removed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> in 2008<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for the construction of "YOU! the Experience",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> which replaced it with a 13-foot-tall (4.0 m), interactive, <a href="/wiki/3D_modeling" title="3D modeling">3D</a> heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Also well known are the "Body Slices" (two <a href="/wiki/Cadaver" title="Cadaver">cadavers</a> exhibited in <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>-inch-thick (13 mm) slices) in the exhibit.</p>
<p>In spring 2013, the "Art of the Bicycle" exhibit opened, showcasing history of the bicycle, and how modern bikes are still continuing to evolve.</p>
<p>Other exhibits include "Yesterday's Mainstreet", a mock-up of a Chicago street from the early 20th century, complete with a <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> roadway, old-fashioned light fixtures, <a href="/wiki/Fire_hydrant" title="Fire hydrant">fire hydrants</a>, and several shops, including the precursors to several Chicago-based businesses. Included are:</p>

<p>Unlike the other shops, Finnigan's Ice Cream Parlor and The Nickelodeon Cinema can be entered and are functional. Finnigan's serves an assortment of ice cream and The Cinema plays short silent films throughout the day.</p>
<p>A second transportation gallery is located on the museum's west wing, containing models of "Ships Through the Ages" and several historic racing cars.</p>
<p>The "FarmTech" exhibit showcases modern agricultural techniques and how farmers use modern technology like GPS systems to improve work on the farm.</p>
<p>Other upper level exhibits include "Reusable City", which focuses on recycling and other methods that could cut down harmful pollution and especially climate change and the Regenstein Hall of Science, containing a giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_table_of_the_elements" title="Periodic table of the elements">periodic table of the elements</a>. Other main level exhibits include: "Fast Forward", which features some aspects of how technology will change in the future; "NetWorld", which focuses on the Internet and how it connects society together; "Earth Revealed", featuring a "Science on a Sphere" holographic globe; and a "Whispering Gallery".</p>
<p>Some areas aimed for younger children include the "Swiss Jollyball", the world's largest pinball machine built by a British man from Switzerland using nothing but salvaged junk; the "Idea Factory", a toddler water table play area; and the "Circus", featuring animated dioramas of a miniature circus as well as containing a shadow garden and several funhouse mirrors.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-104_Starfighter" title="F-104 Starfighter">F-104 Starfighter</a> on loan to MSI from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a> since 1978 was sent to the <a href="/wiki/Mid-America_Air_Museum" title="Mid-America Air Museum">Mid-America Air Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Liberal,_Kansas" title="Liberal, Kansas">Liberal, Kansas</a> in 1993. In March 1995, Santa Fe Steam Locomotive 2903 was moved from outside the museum to the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Railway_Museum" title="Illinois Railway Museum">Illinois Railway Museum</a>.</p>
<p>The museum holds the <a href="/wiki/Junior_Achievement" title="Junior Achievement">Junior Achievement</a>'s US Business Hall of Fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_(Chicago)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to its three floors of standing exhibits, the museum hosts temporary and traveling exhibitions. Exhibitions last for five months or less and usually require a separate paid admission fee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Exhibitions at MSI have included <i>Titanic: The Exhibition</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> which was the largest display of relics from the wreck of <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>, in 2000; Gunther von Hagens' <i><a href="/wiki/Body_Worlds" title="Body Worlds">Body Worlds</a></i>, a view into the human body through use of <a href="/wiki/Plastination" title="Plastination">plastinated</a> human specimens in 2005; also in 2005 was <i><a href="/wiki/Game_On_(exhibition)" title="Game On (exhibition)">Game On</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> which features the history and culture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>: Man, Inventor, Genius</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> in the summer of 2006. Past temporary exhibitions include <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_The_Experience" title="CSI: The Experience">CSI: The Experience</a></i>, <i>Robots Like Us</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> <i>City of the Future,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> <i>Canstruction</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Where_Science_Meets_Imagination" title="Star Wars: Where Science Meets Imagination">Star Wars: Where Science Meets Imagination</a></i>,and <i>The Glass Experience</i>. <i>Harry Potter: The Exhibition</i> ran from April to September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The fourth installment of <i>Smart Home: Green + Wired</i> reopened in March 2011 and ran through January 2012, featuring the work of green architect <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Kaufmann" title="Michelle Kaufmann">Michelle Kaufmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> In May 2015, the <i>Robot Revolution</i> exhibit opened, which is sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Google.org" title="Google.org">Google.org</a> and features numerous hands-on demonstrations and advice from experts for prospective future robot scientists and engineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
