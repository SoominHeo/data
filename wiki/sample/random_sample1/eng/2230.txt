<p><b>Science fantasy</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed_genre" title="Mixed genre">mixed genre</a> within the umbrella of <a href="/wiki/Speculative_fiction" title="Speculative fiction">speculative fiction</a> which simultaneously draws upon and/or combines tropes and elements from both <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>. It also sometimes incorporates elements of <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror fiction</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fantasy_versus_science_fiction">Science fantasy versus science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science fantasy versus science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Distinguishing between science fiction and fantasy, <a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a> claimed that the former was "the improbable made possible" while the latter was "the impossible made probable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> As a combination of the two, science fantasy gives a scientific veneer of realism to things that simply could not happen in the real world under any circumstances. Where science fiction does not permit the existence of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> elements, science fantasy explicitly relies upon them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_view">Historical view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imagination_195102.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="400" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Imagination_195102.jpg/220px-Imagination_195102.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Imagination_195102.jpg/330px-Imagination_195102.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Imagination_195102.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imagination_195102.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of American magazine <i>Imagination</i>, February 1951</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The label first came into wide use after many science fantasy stories were published in the American <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp magazines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Magic,_Inc." title="Magic, Inc.">Magic, Inc.</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">L. Ron Hubbard</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Slaves_of_Sleep" title="Slaves of Sleep">Slaves of Sleep</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Pratt" title="Fletcher Pratt">Fletcher Pratt</a> and <a href="/wiki/L._Sprague_de_Camp" title="L. Sprague de Camp">L. Sprague de Camp</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harold_Shea" title="Harold Shea">Harold Shea</a> series. All were relatively <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalistic</a> stories published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_W._Campbell,_Jr." title="John W. Campbell, Jr.">John W. Campbell, Jr.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Unknown_(magazine)" title="Unknown (magazine)">Unknown</a></i> magazine. These were a deliberate attempt to apply the techniques and attitudes of science fiction to traditional fantasy subjects. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Magazine_of_Fantasy_and_Science_Fiction" title="The Magazine of Fantasy and Science Fiction">The Magazine of Fantasy and Science Fiction</a></i> published, among other things, all but the last of the <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Chaos_(novel)" title="Operation Chaos (novel)">Operation</a></i> series, by <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_Kuttner" title="Henry Kuttner">Henry Kuttner</a> and <a href="/wiki/C._L._Moore" title="C. L. Moore">C. L. Moore</a> published novels in <i><a href="/wiki/Startling_Stories" title="Startling Stories">Startling Stories</a></i>, alone and together, which were far more <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a>. These were closely related to the work that they and others were doing for outlets like <i><a href="/wiki/Weird_Tales" title="Weird Tales">Weird Tales</a></i>, such as Moore's <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Smith" title="Northwest Smith">Northwest Smith</a> stories.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ace_Books" title="Ace Books">Ace Books</a> published a number of books as science fantasy during the 1950s and 1960s.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Encyclopedia_of_Science_Fiction" title="The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction">The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction</a></i> points out that as a genre, science fantasy "has never been clearly defined", and was most commonly used in the period 1950-1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i> franchise created by <a href="/wiki/Gene_Roddenberry" title="Gene Roddenberry">Gene Roddenberry</a> is considered an example of science fantasy. Writer James F. Broderick describes <i>Star Trek</i> as science fantasy because it includes semi-futuristic as well as supernatural/fantasy elements such as <a href="/wiki/Q_(Star_Trek)" title="Q (Star Trek)">The Q</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the science fiction author <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>, many purists argue that <i>Star Trek</i> is science fantasy rather than science fiction because of its scientifically improbable elements, which he partially agrees with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dying_Earth_and_Post-apocalypse">Dying Earth and Post-apocalypse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dying Earth and Post-apocalypse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Dying_Earth_(subgenre)" title="Dying Earth (subgenre)">Dying Earth (subgenre)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction" title="Apocalyptic and post-apocalyptic fiction">Apocalyptic and post-apocalyptic fiction</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Vance" title="Jack Vance">Jack Vance</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dying_Earth" title="Dying Earth">Dying Earth</a> stories are sometimes classed as science fantasy because the cosmology used is not compatible with that conventionally accepted by science fiction. Other stories in the Dying Earth subgenre such as <a href="/wiki/M._John_Harrison" title="M. John Harrison">M. John Harrison</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Viriconium" title="Viriconium">Viriconium</a> novels or <a href="/wiki/Gene_Wolfe" title="Gene Wolfe">Gene Wolfe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_New_Sun" title="The Book of the New Sun">Book of the New Sun</a></i> are usually classed as science fantasy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Terry_Brooks" title="Terry Brooks">Terry Brooks</a>' <a href="/wiki/Shannara" title="Shannara">Shannara</a> books represent a fantasy world as the far future of Earth after supernatural events cause the downfall of civilization.</p>
<p>The last two films in George Miller's post-apocalyptic Mad Max franchise, (<i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome" title="Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome">Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max:_Fury_Road" title="Mad Max: Fury Road">Mad Max: Fury Road</a></i>), while science fiction action films, contain fantastical elements to their depiction of a devastated world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_romance">Planetary romance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Planetary romance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_romance" title="Planetary romance">Planetary romance</a></div>
<p>The planetary romance, a story set primarily or wholly on a single planet and illustrating its scenery, native peoples (if any) and cultures, offers considerable scope for science fantasy, in the sense of fantasy rationalized by reference to science-fictional conventions.</p>
<p>The works of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Rice_Burroughs" title="Edgar Rice Burroughs">Edgar Rice Burroughs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._R._Eddison" title="E. R. Eddison">E. R. Eddison</a> are probably the earliest examples of this genre, especially the <a href="/wiki/John_Carter_of_Mars" title="John Carter of Mars">John Carter of Mars</a> series. <a href="/wiki/David_Lindsay_(novelist)" title="David Lindsay (novelist)">David Lindsay</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Voyage_to_Arcturus" title="A Voyage to Arcturus">A Voyage to Arcturus</a></i>, published in 1920 is one of the earliest examples of the type, although it differs from most of them in not assuming a science-fictional background of interplanetary or interstellar travel; it is rather a philosophical romance, which uses an alien planet as a background for exploring philosophical themes. <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Silent_Planet" title="Out of the Silent Planet">Out of the Silent Planet</a></i> (1938) is an example of the same type of story, though in its case the preoccupations are theological. In both cases the magical elements are barely rationalized, and in Lewis' case stand in stark contrast to the pseudo-scientific machinery that frames the story.</p>
<p>Some examples of this type of science fantasy deliberately blur the already vague distinction between science fictional paranormal powers and magic; for instance, <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_of_Air_and_Darkness_(novella)" title="The Queen of Air and Darkness (novella)">The Queen of Air and Darkness</a></i>, in which aliens use psionic powers of illusion to imitate earthly myths of fairies—who are themselves traditionally regarded as magical illusionists.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Andre_Norton" title="Andre Norton">Andre Norton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Witch_World" title="Witch World">Witch World</a> series, the fantasy world is excused as a <a href="/wiki/Parallel_universe_(fiction)" title="Parallel universe (fiction)">parallel universe</a>. There are a few science fictional elements in the earlier stories of this series, which are absent from the later novels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sword_and_planet">Sword and planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sword and planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sword_and_planet" title="Sword and planet">Sword and planet</a></div>
<p>Many works by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Rice_Burroughs" title="Edgar Rice Burroughs">Edgar Rice Burroughs</a> fall into this category, as well as those of his imitators such as <a href="/wiki/Otis_Adelbert_Kline" title="Otis Adelbert Kline">Otis Adelbert Kline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Bulmer" title="Kenneth Bulmer">Kenneth Bulmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lin_Carter" title="Lin Carter">Lin Carter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Norman" title="John Norman">John Norman</a>. They are largely classed as "science fantasy" because of the presence of swords and, usually, an archaic aristocratic social system; Burroughs' own novels are, however, skeptical in spirit and almost free of any non-rationalized "fantastic" element (other than the never-explained mechanism by which John Carter gets to Mars). The graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Camelot_3000" title="Camelot 3000">Camelot 3000</a></i> is another good example of this. The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Krull_(film)" title="Krull (film)">Krull</a></i> also falls in this category, since that the movie depicts a story where a near omnipotent alien creature invades a fantasy world and the protagonists must find a way to fight back against the alien.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steampunk">Steampunk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Steampunk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Steampunk" title="Steampunk">Steampunk</a></div>
<p>Steampunk is a subgenre of science fantasy, but can also be a subgenre of science fiction, taking old age technology and making it into a futuristic setting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fandom">Fandom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_fantasy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fandom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Science_Fantasy_Society" title="Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society">Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society</a> [1934–present] is an example of a social grouping of fans on the genre of science fantasy and possibly other <a href="/wiki/Speculative_fiction" title="Speculative fiction">speculative fiction</a>.</p>
