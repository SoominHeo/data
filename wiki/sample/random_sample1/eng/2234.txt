<p><b>Kwak Jun-hyeok</b> is Associate Professor of Political Science at <a href="/wiki/Korea_University" title="Korea University">Korea University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> where he has taught political philosophy and theory since Fall 2007. He also serves as Director of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EAI_Center_for_Values_%26_Ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EAI Center for Values &amp; Ethics (page does not exist)">EAI Center for Values &amp; Ethics</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Head of Center for Political Theory, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_and_Democracy_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peace and Democracy Institute (page does not exist)">Peace and Democracy Institute</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> at Korea University. He received his Ph.D. from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Chicago" title="The University of Chicago">the University of Chicago</a> in 2002. Before joining the faculty at Korea University, he taught political thought at <a href="/wiki/Kyungpook_National_University" title="Kyungpook National University">Kyungpook National University</a>. His research interests lie at the crossroads of political thought from <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machiavelli" title="Machiavelli">Machiavelli</a> and contemporary political/social theory, with concentration on <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republicanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constitutionalism" title="Constitutionalism">constitutionalism</a>, democratic leadership, and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>. He teaches classes on constitutionalism and democracy, <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">multiculturalism</a>, politics and <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>, human rights, and classical and medieval political thought.</p>
<p>His research currently focuses on constructing reciprocal nondomination as a regulative principle that guides agonistic deliberation between peoples in conflict as well as cultures in tension, with special but not exclusive attention to classical republicans, such as <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, and Machiavelli. Besides, he inquires a way in which reciprocal nondomination can be applicable to various subjects, including <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotism</a> without nationalism, <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a>, democratic authority, civic responsibility, transnationalism, and multicultural coexistence, etc.. He is now working on books entitled, Liberty as Nondomination: Reconsidering Classical Republicanism, Machiavelli’s Silence, and Patriotism before Nationalism, and running projects “Inherited Responsibility” and “Republican Leadership.”</p>
