<p><b>Vascular plants</b> (from Latin <i>vasculum</i>: duct), also known as <b>tracheophytes</b> (from the equivalent Greek term <i>trachea</i>) and also <b>higher plants</b>, form a large group of plants (c. 308,312 accepted known species <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz-Byng2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz-Byng2016-3">[3]</a></sup>) that are defined as those <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_plants" title="Land plants">land plants</a> that have <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignified</a> <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Xylem" title="Xylem">xylem</a>) for conducting <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and minerals throughout the plant. They also have a specialized non-lignified tissue (the <a href="/wiki/Phloem" title="Phloem">phloem</a>) to conduct products of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. Vascular plants include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clubmoss" title="Clubmoss">clubmosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equisetum" title="Equisetum">horsetails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperms" title="Angiosperms">angiosperms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a>). Scientific names for the group include Tracheophyta<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Tracheobionta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itistrach_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-itistrach-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vascular plants are distinguished by two primary characteristics:</p>
<ol>
<li>Vascular plants have <a href="/wiki/Vascular_tissue" title="Vascular tissue">vascular tissues</a> which distribute resources through the plant. This feature allows vascular plants to evolve to a larger size than <a href="/wiki/Non-vascular_plant" title="Non-vascular plant">non-vascular plants</a>, which lack these specialized conducting tissues and are therefore restricted to relatively small sizes.</li>
<li>In vascular plants, the principal <a href="/wiki/Alternation_of_generations" title="Alternation of generations">generation phase</a> is the <i><a href="/wiki/Sporophyte" title="Sporophyte">sporophyte</a></i>, which is usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> with two sets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a> per cell. Only the germ cells and gametophytes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a>. By contrast, the principal generation phase in non-vascular plants is the <i><a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a></i>, which is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> with one set of chromosomes per cell. In these plants, only the spore stalk and capsule are diploid.</li>
</ol>
<p>One possible mechanism for the presumed switch from emphasis on the haploid generation to emphasis on the diploid generation is the greater efficiency in spore dispersal with more complex diploid structures. In other words, elaboration of the spore stalk enabled the production of more spores, and enabled the development of the ability to release them higher and to broadcast them farther. Such developments may include more photosynthetic area for the spore-bearing structure, the ability to grow independent roots, woody structure for support, and more branching.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="several points in this paragraph are conjectural and need WP:RS (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_distribution">Nutrient distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nutrient distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ficusxylem.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="991" data-file-width="1771" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ficusxylem.jpg/300px-Ficusxylem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ficusxylem.jpg/450px-Ficusxylem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Ficusxylem.jpg/600px-Ficusxylem.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ficusxylem.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photographs showing <a href="/wiki/Xylem" title="Xylem">xylem</a> elements in the shoot of a <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig</a> tree (<i>Ficus alba</i>): crushed in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, between slides and cover slips.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Water and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> in the form of inorganic solutes are drawn up from the soil by the roots and transported throughout the plant by the <a href="/wiki/Xylem" title="Xylem">xylem</a>. Organic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> in leaves are distributed by the <a href="/wiki/Phloem" title="Phloem">phloem</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sieve_tube_elements" title="Sieve tube elements">sieve tube elements</a>.</p>
<p>The xylem consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vessel_elements" title="Vessel elements">vessels</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering_plants" title="Flowering plants">flowering plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tracheid" title="Tracheid">tracheids</a> in other vascular plants, which are dead hard-walled hollow cells arranged to form files of tubes that function in water transport. A tracheid cell wall usually contains the polymer <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>. The phloem however consists of living cells called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sieve-tube_member" title="Sieve-tube member">sieve-tube members</a>. Between the sieve-tube members are sieve plates, which have pores to allow molecules to pass through. Sieve-tube members lack such organs as <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomes" title="Ribosomes">ribosomes</a>, but cells next to them, the <a href="/wiki/Phloem#companion_cells" title="Phloem">companion cells</a>, function to keep the sieve-tube members alive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transpiration">Transpiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transpiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most abundant <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> in all plants, as in all cellular organisms, is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> which serves an important structural role and a vital role in plant metabolism. <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">Transpiration</a> is the main process of water movement within plant tissues. Water is constantly transpired from the plant through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> to the atmosphere and replaced by soil water taken up by the roots. The movement of water out of the leaf stomata creates a transpiration pull or tension in the water column in the xylem vessels or tracheids. The pull is the result of water <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> within the cell walls of the mesophyll cells, from the surfaces of which evaporation takes place when the stomata are open. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonds" title="Hydrogen bonds">Hydrogen bonds</a> exist between <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, causing them to line up; as the molecules at the top of the plant evaporate, each pulls the next one up to replace it, which in turn pulls on the next one in line. The draw of water upwards may be entirely passive and can be assisted by the movement of water into the roots via <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>. Consequently, transpiration requires very little energy to be used by the plant. Transpiration assists the plant in absorbing nutrients from the soil as soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salts" title="Salts">salts</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption">Absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Living root cells passively absorb water in the absence of transpiration pull via <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a> creating root pressure. It is possible for there to be no <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> and therefore no pull of water towards the shoots and leaves. This is usually due to high temperatures, high <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, darkness or drought.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conduction">Conduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vascular_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Xylem and <a href="/wiki/Phloem" title="Phloem">phloem</a> tissues are involved in the conduction processes within plants. Sugars are conducted throughout the plant in the phloem, water and other nutrients through the xylem. Conduction occurs from a source to a sink for each separate nutrient. Sugars are produced in the leaves (a source) by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and transported to the growing shoots and roots (sinks) for use in growth, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a> or storage. Minerals are absorbed in the roots (a source) and transported to the shoots to allow cell division and growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
