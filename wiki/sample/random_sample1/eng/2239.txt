<div class="hatnote" role="note">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is <a href="/wiki/Guan" title="Guan">Kwan</a>.</div>
<p><b>Kwan Tak-hing</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> (27 June 1905 – 28 June 1996) was a <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_people" title="Hong Kong people">Hong Kong</a> actor who played the role of <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial artist</a> <a href="/wiki/Folk_hero" title="Folk hero">folk hero</a> <a href="/wiki/Wong_Fei-hung" title="Wong Fei-hung">Wong Fei-hung</a> in at least 77 films, between the 1940s and the 1980s. No-one else in cinema history has portrayed the same person as many times. In total he made over 130 films. He was elected in 1955 as the chairman of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Artist_Association_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chinese Artist Association of Hong Kong">Chinese Artist Association of Hong Kong</a>. He was awarded the MBE (Member of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a>) in 1983.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwan_Tak-hing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kwan was born in <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 1905. He was the second child in the family. His father died of a disease at a young age. To supplement his family's income, as a boy, Kwan worked as a cowherd. When he was 12, he began work in construction. At the age of 13, he worked as a waiter in a restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> before joining a <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_opera" title="Cantonese opera">Cantonese opera</a> troupe under Cheng Hsin-pei.</p>
<p>His film debut was in <i>The Singing Lovers</i> (aka <i>Romance of the Songsters</i>), made in the US for the Grandview Studios and only the second Cantonese-language <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">talkie</a> ever made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kfc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kfc-1">[1]</a></sup>His first starring roles came two years later in <i>Song of the Yesterday</i> (aka <i>Yesterday's Song</i>) and <i>Song of Sadness</i> (both 1935). Kwan's first martial arts film was <i>Knight of the Whip</i> (1936).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kfc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kfc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KungFuCinema_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-KungFuCinema-2">[2]</a></sup>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> he was in a troupe of patriotic entertainers and had a price put on his head by the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first film in which he starred as <a href="/wiki/Wong_Fei-hung" title="Wong Fei-hung">Wong Fei-hung</a> was the <i>Story of Huang Feihong part 1</i> (1949) directed by <a href="/wiki/Wu_Pang" title="Wu Pang">Wu Pang</a> and produced by the Yong Yao Film Company. The film included <a href="/wiki/Shih_Kien" title="Shih Kien">Shih Kien</a> as the villain and <a href="/wiki/Li_Lan" title="Li Lan">Li Lan</a>, the very first winner of the <a href="/wiki/Miss_Hong_Kong_Pageant" title="Miss Hong Kong Pageant">Miss Hong Kong Pageant</a>, and the first of many winners who would become famous actresses in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>). This partnership was a huge success and spawned many sequels, exploring many ideas and situations used in later action films. In 1956, a total of 25 <a href="/wiki/Wong_Fei-hung_filmography" title="Wong Fei-hung filmography">Wong Fei-hung films</a> were released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kwan worked on films with two of the students of <a href="/wiki/Lam_Sai-wing" title="Lam Sai-wing">Lam Sai-wing</a> (himself a student of the real Wong Fei-hung. First, Leong Wing-hang, who worked as action director on Wong Fei-hung films as well as other Kwan vehicles including <i>The Five Heroes' Deadly Spears</i> (1951). Later he worked with Lau Cham, father of <a href="/wiki/Lau_Kar-leung" title="Lau Kar-leung">Lau Kar-leung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lau_Kar-wing" title="Lau Kar-wing">Lau Kar-wing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Wong Fei-hung sequence of films ended in 1970 and Kwan went into semi-retirement. During this period, Kwan opened a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">martial arts school</a> and a number of herbal centres, whose branded soup and an ointment for bruises were well known in Hong Kong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Orange_Sky_Golden_Harvest" title="Orange Sky Golden Harvest">Golden Harvest</a> revived the role, pairing Kwan with <a href="/wiki/Sammo_Hung" title="Sammo Hung">Sammo Hung</a> in <i>The Skyhawk</i>. Other films followed in which Kwan played his most familiar role - <i><a href="/wiki/Magnificent_Butcher" title="Magnificent Butcher">Magnificent Butcher</a></i> (1979), <i>Magnificent Kick</i> (1980), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dreadnaught_(film)" title="Dreadnaught (film)">Dreadnaught</a></i> (1981). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_Broadcasts_Limited" title="Television Broadcasts Limited">TVB</a> also aired a 13-part television series featuring Kwan as Wong Fei-hung in 1976. By this time, he was in his 70's and although he was doubled for the more athletic scenes, he still demonstrated remarkable fitness and suppleness.</p>
<p>Kwan received an honorary <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> for his charitable work and contribution to the entertainment industry, in 1984.</p>
<p>To the Chinese, Kwan embodied <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> virtues and patriarchal authority, and he is thought to have modelled his speeches on those of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen" title="Sun Yat-sen">Sun Yat-sen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Kwan appeared in cameo roles in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Family_Strikes_Back" title="The Family Strikes Back">The Family Strikes Back</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aces_Go_Places_4" title="Aces Go Places 4">Aces Go Places 4</a></i> (both 1986). His final film appearance was in the 1994 family comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Wonderful_Life_(1994_film)" title="It's a Wonderful Life (1994 film)">It's a Wonderful Life!</a></i>, at the age of 89, some 61 years after his acting career began.</p>
<p>In Bey Logan's audio commentary for the film <i>The Magnificent Butcher</i>, he states that though there is a grave marker to Kwan Tak-hing in <a href="/wiki/Kowloon" title="Kowloon">Kowloon</a> territory, his ashes were taken to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> to rest with those of his second wife, whom he met whilst travelling in the US, whilst raising money for the Chinese war effort. He died at the age of 92 of <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Action_style">Action style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwan_Tak-hing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Action style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kwan was originally a practitioner of the Opera Martial Arts of Masters Sun Pak/Cheng Hsin-pei and Len Yuen Hang and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lama_Pai" title="Lama Pai">White Crane</a> and Hung Gar martial art of Fok Hung. Throughout his life he made friends with famous martial arts exponents and cross trained extensively. Training in Wong Fei Hung's style of Hung Gar came from Leung Wing Hang and Lau Jam, both students of the famous Lam Sai Wing. His weapons speciality included the use of the bullwhip and the pole. Kwan was also known for his skills in the <a href="/wiki/Lion_dance" title="Lion dance">lion dance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese calligraphy</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwan_Tak-hing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although modern martial arts films often feature a lot of violence, at the end of <i>The Magnificent Butcher</i>, Kwan's Wong Fei-hung saves the main villain (played by Lee Hoi-san) from being killed by Butcher Wing (Sammo Hung). This reflected Wong Fei-hung's portrayal in the original films, wherein he would defeat the villain but then heal him.</p>
<p>Kwan became so associated with the mature Wong Fei-hung that other filmmakers would only portray the character as a younger person, including <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">Jackie Chan</a> playing Wong as a young man in <i><a href="/wiki/Drunken_Master" title="Drunken Master">Drunken Master</a></i> (1978), and <a href="/wiki/Yuen_Woo-ping" title="Yuen Woo-ping">Yuen Woo-ping</a> depicting Wong as a child (played by actress <a href="/wiki/Angie_Tsang" title="Angie Tsang">Angie Tsang</a>) in <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Monkey_(1993_film)" title="Iron Monkey (1993 film)">Iron Monkey</a></i> (1993). When <a href="/wiki/Jet_Li" title="Jet Li">Jet Li</a> portrayed Wong in the <a href="/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Time_in_China_(film_series)" title="Once Upon a Time in China (film series)"><i>Once Upon a Time in China</i> series</a>, many Chinese audiences felt he was too young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
