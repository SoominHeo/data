<p>The <b>Kwantung Army</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Army_group" title="Army group">army group</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> in the first half of the 20th century. It became the largest and most prestigious command in the IJA. Many of its personnel, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_of_staff_(military)" title="Chief of staff (military)">Chiefs of staff</a> <a href="/wiki/Seishir%C5%8D_Itagaki" title="Seishirō Itagaki">Seishirō Itagaki</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Hideki Tōjō</a> were promoted to high positions in both the military and civil government in the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a> and it was largely responsible for the creation of the Japanese-dominated Empire of <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo" title="Manchukuo">Manchukuo</a>. In August 1945, the army group, only around 713,000 (from a previous total of 1,320,000) men at the time, was defeated by and surrendered to Soviet troops as a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchurian_Strategic_Offensive_Operation" title="Manchurian Strategic Offensive Operation">Manchurian Strategic Offensive Operation</a>.</p>

<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, Japan obtained the <a href="/wiki/Kwantung_Leased_Territory" title="Kwantung Leased Territory">Kwantung Leased Territory</a> and the areas adjacent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Manchurian_Railway" title="South Manchurian Railway">South Manchurian Railway</a>. "Kwantung" means "east of <a href="/wiki/Shanhai_Pass" title="Shanhai Pass">Shanhaiguan</a>", a guarded pass, east of which lies <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>. The <b>Kwantung Garrison</b> was established in 1906 to defend this territory, and originally was composed of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infantry_division" title="Infantry division">infantry division</a> and a heavy siege <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalion</a>, supplemented with six independent <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a> battalions as railway guards deployed along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Manchurian_Railway_Zone" title="South Manchurian Railway Zone">South Manchurian Railway Zone</a>, for a total troop strength of 100,000 men. It was headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%BCshunkou" title="Lüshunkou">Port Arthur</a>, known as "<i>Ryojun</i>" in Japanese. After a reorganization in 1919, the Kwantung Garrison was renamed the Kwantung Army.</p>
<p>In the highly politicized Imperial Japanese Army of the 1920s and 1930s, the Kwantung Army was a stronghold of the radical "<a href="/wiki/Imperial_Way_Faction" title="Imperial Way Faction">Imperial Way Faction</a>", and many of its senior leaders overtly advocated political change in Japan through the violent overthrow of the civilian government to bring about a <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_Restoration" title="Shōwa Restoration">Shōwa Restoration</a>, with a reorganization of society and the economy along <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Militarism-Socialism_in_Showa_Japan" title="Militarism-Socialism in Showa Japan">state fascist</a> lines. They also advocated a more aggressive, expansionist foreign policy regarding the Asian mainland. Members or former members of the Kwantung Army were active in numerous <i><a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a></i> attempts against the civilian government, culminating with the <a href="/wiki/February_26_Incident" title="February 26 Incident">February 26 Incident</a> of 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_actions">Independent actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwantung_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Independent actions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the Kwantung Army was nominally subordinate to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_General_Headquarters" title="Imperial General Headquarters">Imperial General Headquarters</a> and the senior staff at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army_General_Staff" title="Imperial Japanese Army General Staff">Army General Staff</a>, its leadership often acted in direct violation of the orders from the mainland Japan without suffering any consequence. Conspirators within the <a href="/wiki/Junior_officer" title="Junior officer">junior officer</a> corps of the Kwantung Army plotted and carried out the <a href="/wiki/Assassination" title="Assassination">assassination</a> of Manchurian warlord <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Zuolin" title="Zhang Zuolin">Chang Tsolin</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huanggutun_Incident" title="Huanggutun Incident">Huanggutun Incident</a> of 1928. Afterwards, the Kwantung Army leadership engineered the <a href="/wiki/Mukden_Incident" title="Mukden Incident">Mukden Incident</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Manchuria" title="Japanese invasion of Manchuria">invasion of Manchuria</a> in 1931 in a massive act of insubordination (<a href="/wiki/Gekokuj%C5%8D" title="Gekokujō"><i>gekokujo</i></a>) against the express orders of the political and military leadership based in Tokyo.</p>
<p>Presented with the <i>fait accompli</i>, Imperial General Headquarters had little choice but to follow up on the actions of the Kwantung Army with reinforcements in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Pacification_of_Manchukuo" title="Pacification of Manchukuo">Pacification of Manchukuo</a>. The success of the campaign meant that the insubordination of the Kwantung Army was rewarded rather than punished.</p>
<p>With the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo" title="Manchukuo">Manchukuo</a> in 1932, the Kwantung Army played a controlling role in the political administration of the new state as well as in its defense. With the Kwantung Army administering all aspects of the politics and economic development of the new state, this made the Kwantung Army commanding officer equivalent to a <a href="/wiki/Governor-general" title="Governor-general">Governor-general</a>, with the authority to approve or countermand any command from the nominal emperor of Manchukuo, <a href="/wiki/Puyi" title="Puyi">Puyi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Kwantung Army was heavily augmented over the next few years, up to a strength of 700,000 troops by 1941, and its headquarters was transferred to the new Manchukuo capital of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hsinking" title="Hsinking">Hsinking</a>. The Kwantung Army also oversaw the creation, training, and equipping of an auxiliary force, the <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo_Imperial_Army" title="Manchukuo Imperial Army">Manchukuo Imperial Army</a>. During this time, Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Takeda" title="Prince Takeda">Tsuneyoshi Takeda</a> worked as liaison officer between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Hirohito" title="Emperor Hirohito">Imperial house</a> and the Kwantung Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwantung_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the campaign to secure Manchukuo, the Kwantung Army continued to fight in numerous border skirmishes with China as part of its <a href="/wiki/North_China_Buffer_State_Strategy" title="North China Buffer State Strategy">efforts to create a Japanese-dominated buffer zone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_and_southern_China" title="Northern and southern China">northern China</a>. The Kwantung Army also fought in the opening phase of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Nekka" title="Operation Nekka">Operation Nekka</a>, and various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actions_in_Inner_Mongolia_(1933%E2%80%931936)" title="Actions in Inner Mongolia (1933–1936)">actions in Inner Mongolia</a> to extend Japanese domination over portions of northern China and <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>. When war broke out in the <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo_Bridge_Incident" title="Marco Polo Bridge Incident">Marco Polo Bridge Incident</a> in July 1937, its forces participated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Beiping-Tianjin" title="Battle of Beiping-Tianjin">Battle of Beiping-Tianjin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Chahar" title="Operation Chahar">Operation Chahar</a>. Later, Kwantung forces supported the war in China from time to time.</p>
<p>However, the much vaunted reputation of the Kwantung Army was severely challenged in battle against the Soviet Union's <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lake_Khasan" title="Battle of Lake Khasan">Battle of Lake Khasan</a> in 1938<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Khalkhin_Gol" title="Battle of Khalkhin Gol">Battle of Nomonhan</a> in 1939, during which time it sustained heavy casualties. After the Nomonhan incident, the Kwantung Army was purged of its more insubordinate elements, as well as proponents of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hokushin-ron" title="Hokushin-ron">Hokushin-ron</a></i> ("Northward Advance") doctrine who urged that Japan concentrate its expansionist efforts on <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> rather southward towards China and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although a source of constant unrest during the 1930s, the Kwantung Army remained remarkably obedient during the 1940s. As combat spread south into <a href="/wiki/Central_China" title="Central China">central China</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_China" title="Southern China">southern China</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a>, and with the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>, Manchukuo was largely a backwater to the conflict.</p>
<p>However, as the war situation began to deteriorate for the Imperial Japanese Army on all fronts, the large, well-trained, and well-equipped Kwantung Army could no longer be held in <a href="/wiki/Strategic_reserve" title="Strategic reserve">strategic reserve</a>. Many of its front line units were systematically stripped of their best units and equipment, which were sent south against the forces of the United States in the <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Pacific Islands</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. Other units were sent south into China for <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ichi-Go" title="Operation Ichi-Go">Operation Ichi-Go</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surrender_of_the_Kwantung_Army">Surrender of the Kwantung Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwantung_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Surrender of the Kwantung Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1945, the Kwantung Army consisted of a mere 713,000 personnel, divided into 31 infantry divisions, nine infantry brigades, two tank brigades, and one special purpose brigade. It also possessed 1,155 light tanks, 5,360 guns, and 1,800 aircraft. The quality of troops had fallen drastically, as all the best men and materiel were siphoned off for use in other theaters. These forces were replaced by militia, draft levies, reservists, and cannibalized smaller units, all equipped with woefully outdated equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Kwantung Army had also bacteriological weapons, prepared for use against Soviet troops (see. "<a href="/wiki/Unit_731" title="Unit 731">Detachment 731</a>"). The bulk of military equipment (artillery, tanks, aircraft) was developed in the 1930s, and very few of the soldiers had sufficient training or any real experience.</p>
<p>The final commanding officer of the Kwantung Army, General <a href="/wiki/Otoz%C5%8D_Yamada" title="Otozō Yamada">Otozō Yamada</a>, ordered a surrender on August 16, 1945, one day after Emperor Hirohito announced the <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">surrender of Japan</a> in a radio announcement. Some Japanese divisions refused to surrender, and combat continued for the next few days. <a href="/wiki/Shunroku_Hata" title="Shunroku Hata">Marshal Hata</a> received the "ultimatum to surrender" from Soviet General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Georgii_Shelakhov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Georgii Shelakhov (page does not exist)">Georgii Shelakhov</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a> on August 18, 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-6">[6]</a></sup> He was one of the senior generals who agreed with the <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">decision to surrender</a>, and on August 19, 1945, Hata met with Marshal <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Vasilevsky" title="Aleksandr Vasilevsky">Aleksandr Vasilevsky</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but asked that he be stripped of his rank of Field Marshal in atonement for the Army's failures in the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remnants of the Kwantung Army were either dead or on their way to Soviet <a href="/wiki/Prisoner-of-war_camp" title="Prisoner-of-war camp">prisoner-of-war camps</a>. Over 500,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_POW_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Japanese POW in the Soviet Union">Japanese prisoners of war</a> were sent to work in Soviet <a href="/wiki/Labor_camp" title="Labor camp">labor camps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. They were largely repatriated, in stages, over the next five years, though some continued to be held well into the 1950s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_crimes_and_trials">War crimes and trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kwantung_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: War crimes and trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the surrender of Japan, the Soviet Red Army discovered secret installations for experimenting with and producing <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical weapons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a> centered around secret Army <a href="/wiki/Unit_731" title="Unit 731">Unit 731</a> and its subsidiaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At these locations, the Kwantung Army was also responsible for some of the most infamous <a href="/wiki/Japanese_war_crimes" title="Japanese war crimes">Japanese war crimes</a>, including the operation of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_experimentation" title="Human experimentation">human experimentation</a> programs using live Chinese, American and Russian<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> civilians, and POWs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> directed by Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiro_Ishii" title="Shiro Ishii">Shiro Ishii</a>.</p>
<p>Arrested by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Commander_of_the_Allied_Powers" title="Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers">American occupation authorities</a>, Ishii and the 20,000 members of Unit 731 received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunity_from_prosecution" title="Immunity from prosecution">immunity from prosecution</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_war_crime" title="Japanese war crime">war-crimes</a> before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_tribunal" title="Tokyo tribunal">Tokyo tribunal</a> of 1948, in exchange for germ warfare data based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_experimentation" title="Human experimentation">human experimentation</a>. On May 6, 1947, General <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a> wrote to Washington that "additional data, possibly some statements from Ishii probably can be obtained by informing Japanese involved that information will be retained in intelligence channels and will not be employed as 'War Crimes' evidence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The deal was concluded in 1948.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, twelve members of Unit 731 and some members of the World War II leadership of the Kwantung Army were sentenced as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_criminals" title="War criminals">war criminals</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Khabarovsk_War_Crime_Trials" title="Khabarovsk War Crime Trials">Khabarovsk War Crime Trials</a>, while others were taken into custody by the United States, and sentenced at the 1948 <a href="/wiki/International_Military_Tribunal_for_the_Far_East" title="International Military Tribunal for the Far East">International Military Tribunal for the Far East</a> in Tokyo. Among those sentenced to death were former generals <a href="/wiki/Seishir%C5%8D_Itagaki" title="Seishirō Itagaki">Seishirō Itagaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iwane_Matsui" title="Iwane Matsui">Iwane Matsui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Doihara" title="Kenji Doihara">Kenji Doihara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Hideki Tōjō</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akira_Mut%C5%8D" title="Akira Mutō">Akira Mutō</a>.</p>
