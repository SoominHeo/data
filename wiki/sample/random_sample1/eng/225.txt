<p>The <b>1954 Asian Games</b> (<a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a>: <span lang="fil"><i>Ika-2 Palaro ng Asya</i></span>) or <b>II Juegos Asiáticos</b> in Spanish (officially known as the <b>Second Asian Games</b> – <b>Manila 1954</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> from May 1 to May 9, 1954. A total of 970 athletes from 19 <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committees" title="National Olympic Committees">National Olympic Committees</a> (NOCs) competed in 76 events from eight <a href="/wiki/Asian_Games_sports" title="Asian Games sports">sports</a>. The number of participating NOCs and athletes were larger than the previous Asian Games held in <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a> in <a href="/wiki/1951_Asian_Games" title="1951 Asian Games">1951</a>. This edition of the games has a different twist where it did not implement a medal tally system to determine the overall champion but a pointing system. The pointing system is a complex system where each athlete were given points according to their achievement like position in athletics or in swimming. In the end the pointing system showed to be worthless as it simply ranked the nations the same way in the medal tally system. The pointing system was not implemented in future games eversince.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jorge_B._Vargas" title="Jorge B. Vargas">Jorge B. Vargas</a> was the head of the Philippine Amateur Athletic Federation (In 1976, was renamed as <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Olympic_Committee" title="Philippine Olympic Committee">Philippine Olympic Committee</a>) and the Manila Asian Games Organizing Committee. With the second-place finish of the Philippines, only around 9,000 spectators attended the closing ceremony at the <a href="/wiki/Rizal_Memorial_Sports_Complex" title="Rizal Memorial Sports Complex">Rizal Memorial Stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The events were broadcast on radio live at <a href="/wiki/DZRH" title="DZRH">DZRH</a> and <a href="/wiki/DWWX-TV" title="DWWX-TV">DZAQ-TV ABS-3</a> on delayed telecast.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_ceremony">Opening ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1954_Asian_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Opening ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Games were formally opened by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Magsaysay" title="Ramon Magsaysay">Ramon Magsaysay</a> on May 1, 1954, at 16:02 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_in_the_Philippines" title="Time in the Philippines">local time</a>. Around 20,000 spectators fill the <a href="/wiki/Rizal_Memorial_Sports_Complex" title="Rizal Memorial Sports Complex">Rizal Memorial Stadium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malate" title="Malate">Malate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> for the opening ceremony. As requested by the IOC, the torch relay and lighting of the couldron were excluded from the Opening Ceremony to preserve the tradition of the Olympic Games. The torch ceremony were returned at the 1958 Asian Games. The host however gave a solution by giving a special citation to the last athlete to enter the parade. The Philippines, as host, was the last country to enter the stadium. The flag bearer for the Philippines squad was Andres Franco, who won a gold medal in the 1951 Asian Games in <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1951_Asian_Games" title="Athletics at the 1951 Asian Games">high jump</a> event, the sole gold medal of any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_people" title="Filipino people">Filipino</a> in the athletics events of the previous Asian Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gbrathletics1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gbrathletics1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 1954 Asian Games featured eight sports divided into 10 events, aquatics included three events namely diving, swimming and water polo. This version of the Asian Games comprised more sports and events than the last one, as six sports and seven events were in the calendar of <a href="/wiki/1951_Asian_Games" title="1951 Asian Games">1951 Asian Games</a>. Three sports—<a href="/wiki/Boxing_at_the_Asian_Games" title="Boxing at the Asian Games">boxing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_Asian_Games" title="Shooting at the Asian Games">shooting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wrestling_at_the_Asian_Games" title="Wrestling at the Asian Games">wrestling</a>—made their debut, while <a href="/wiki/Cycling_at_the_Asian_Games" title="Cycling at the Asian Games">cycling</a> was dropped out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-la84foundation1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-la84foundation1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the following calendar for the 1954 Asian Games, each blue box represents an event competition, such as a qualification round, on that day. The yellow boxes represent days during which medal-awarding finals for a sport were held. The numeral indicates the number of event finals for each sport held that day. On the left, the calendar lists each sport with events held during the Games, and at the right, how many gold medals were won in that sport. There is a key at the top of the calendar to aid the reader.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1954_Asian_Games_medal_table" title="1954 Asian Games medal table">1954 Asian Games medal table</a></div>
