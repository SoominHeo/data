<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis</b> (<b>XDR-TB</b>) is a form of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> caused by bacteria that are resistant to some of the most effective <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis_management#Drugs" title="Tuberculosis management">anti-TB drugs</a>. XDR-TB strains have arisen after the mismanagement of individuals with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidrug-resistant_TB" title="Multidrug-resistant TB">multidrug-resistant TB</a> (MDR-TB).</p>
<p>Almost one in four people in the world is infected with TB bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Only when the bacteria become active do people become ill with TB. Bacteria become active as a result of anything that can reduce the person’s <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a>, such as HIV, advancing age, or some medical conditions. TB can usually be treated with a course of four standard, or first-line, anti-TB drugs (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Isoniazid" title="Isoniazid">isoniazid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rifampin" title="Rifampin">rifampin</a> and any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluoroquinolone" title="Fluoroquinolone">fluoroquinolone</a>). If these drugs are misused or mismanaged, multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) can develop. MDR-TB takes longer to treat with second-line drugs (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Amikacin" title="Amikacin">amikacin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanamycin" title="Kanamycin">kanamycin</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Capreomycin" title="Capreomycin">capreomycin</a>), which are more expensive and have more side-effects. XDR-TB can develop when these second-line drugs are also misused or mismanaged and therefore also become ineffective.</p>
<p>XDR-TB raises concerns of a future TB epidemic with restricted treatment options, and jeopardizes the major gains made in TB control and progress on reducing TB deaths among people living with <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>. It is therefore vital that TB control be managed properly and new tools developed to prevent, treat and diagnose the disease.</p>
<p>The true scale of XDR-TB is unknown as many countries lack the necessary equipment and capacity to accurately diagnose it. It is estimated however that there are around 40,000 cases per year. As of June 2008, 49 countries had confirmed cases of XDR-TB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> By 2013, that number had risen to 84.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>XDR-TB is defined as TB that has developed resistance to at least <a href="/wiki/Rifampicin" title="Rifampicin">rifampicin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isoniazid" title="Isoniazid">isoniazid</a> (resistance to these first line anti-TB drugs defines <a href="/wiki/Multi-drug-resistant_tuberculosis" title="Multi-drug-resistant tuberculosis">Multi-drug-resistant tuberculosis</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MDR-TB" title="MDR-TB">MDR-TB</a>), as well as to any member of the <a href="/wiki/Quinolone" title="Quinolone">quinolone</a> family and at least one of the following second-line anti-TB injectable drugs: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanamycin" title="Kanamycin">kanamycin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capreomycin" title="Capreomycin">capreomycin</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Amikacin" title="Amikacin">amikacin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This definition of XDR-TB was agreed by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> Global Task Force on XDR-TB in October 2006. The earlier definition of XDR-TB as MDR-TB that is also resistant to three or more of the six classes of second-line drugs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_Mycobacterium_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_Mycobacterium-5">[5]</a></sup> is no longer used, but may be referred to in older publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission">Transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other forms of TB, XDR-TB is spread through the air. When a person with infectious TB coughs, sneezes, talks or spits, they propel TB germs, known as <a href="/wiki/Bacteria#Morphology" title="Bacteria">bacilli</a>, into the air. XDR-TB cannot be spread by kissing, sharing food or drinks and by shaking someone’s hand. The bacterium has the ability to stay in the air for several hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A person needs only to inhale a small number of these to be infected. People infected with TB bacilli will not necessarily become sick with the disease. The <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> "walls off" the TB bacilli which, protected by a thick waxy coat, can lie dormant for years.</p>
<p>The spread of TB bacteria depends on factors such as the number and concentration of infectious people in any one place together with the presence of people with a higher risk of being infected (such as those with <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>). The risk of becoming infected increases with the longer the time that a previously uninfected person spends in the same room as the infectious case. The risk of spread increases where there is a high concentration of TB bacteria, such as can occur in closed environments like overcrowded houses, hospitals or prisons. The risk will be further increased if ventilation is poor. The risk of spread will be reduced and eventually eliminated if infectious patients receive proper treatment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Successful diagnosis of XDR-TB depends on the patient’s access to quality health-care services. If TB bacteria are found in the <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a>, the diagnosis of TB can be made in a day or two, but this finding will not be able to distinguish between drug-susceptible and drug-resistant TB. To evaluate drug susceptibility, the bacteria need to be cultivated and tested in a suitable laboratory. Final diagnosis in this way for TB, and especially for XDR-TB, may take from 6 to 16 weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-8">[8]</a></sup> To reduce the time needed for diagnosis, new tools for rapid TB diagnosis are urgently needed.</p>
<p>The original method used to test for MDR-TB and XDR-TB was the Drug Susceptibility Testing (DST). DST is capable of determining how well four primary antitubercular drugs inhibit the growth of Mycobacterium Tuberculosis. The four primary antitubercular drugs are Isoniazid, Rifampin, Ethambutol and Pyrazinamide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Drug Susceptibility testing is done by making a Lowenstein-Jensen medium plate and spreading the bacteria on the plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Disks containing one of the four primary drugs are added to the plate. After weeks of allowing the bacteria to grow the plate is checked for clear areas around the disk. If there is a clear area, the drug has killed the bacteria and most likely the bacteria is not resistant to that drug.</p>
<p>As <i>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</i> evolved new strains of resistant bacteria were being found such as XDR-TB. The problem was that primary DST was not suitable for testing bacteria strains that were extensively drug resistant. This problem was starting to be fixed when drug susceptibility tests started including not just the four primary drugs, but secondary drugs. This secondary test is known as Bactec MGIT 960 System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodrigues.2C_C._2008_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodrigues.2C_C._2008-11">[11]</a></sup> Although Bactec MGIT 960 System was accurate it was still slow at determining the level of resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodrigues.2C_C._2008_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rodrigues.2C_C._2008-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diagnosis of MDR and XDR-TB in children is challenging. With an increasing number of cases being reported worldwide there is a great need for better diagnostic tools available for pediatric patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years drug resistant tuberculosis testing has shown a lot of progress. Some studies have found an in-house assay that could rapidly detect resistance to drugs involved in the definition of XDR-TB directly from smear-positive specimens. The assay is called Reverse Line Blot Hybridization Assay also known as RLBH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanchan.2C_A._2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kanchan.2C_A._2011-13">[13]</a></sup> The study showed that the results of RLBH were as accurate as other drug susceptibility tests, but at the same time didn`t take weeks to get results. RLBH testing only took 3 days to determine how resistant the strain of bacteria was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanchan.2C_A._2011_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kanchan.2C_A._2011-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The current research has shown progress in the testing of drug resistance. A recent study found that a research technique known as direct nitrate reductase assay (D-NRA) showed efficient accuracy for the rapid and simultaneous detection of resistance to isoniazid (INH), rifampicin (RIF), kanamycin (KAN) and ofloxacin (OFL). D-NRA results were obtained in 16.9 days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imperiale.2C_BR_2014_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imperiale.2C_BR_2014-14">[14]</a></sup> comparably less than other drug susceptibility testing. At the same time the study mentioned how D-NRA is a low-cost technology, easy to set up in clinical laboratories and suitable to be used for DST of M. tuberculosis in all smear-positive samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imperiale.2C_BR_2014_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Imperiale.2C_BR_2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principles of treatment for MDR-TB and for XDR-TB are the same. Treatment requires extensive <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> for up to two years. Second-line drugs are more toxic than the standard anti-TB regimen and can cause a range of serious side-effects including <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">hallucinations</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafness" title="Deafness">deafness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-15">[15]</a></sup> Patients are often hospitalized for long periods, in isolation. In addition, second-line drugs are extremely expensive compared with the cost of drugs for standard TB treatment.</p>
<p>XDR-TB is associated with a much higher mortality rate than MDR-TB, because of a reduced number of effective treatment options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_Mycobacterium_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_Mycobacterium-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite early fears that this strain of TB was untreatable, recent studies have shown that XDR-TB can be treated through the use of aggressive regimens. A study in the Tomsk oblast of Russia, reported that 14 out of 29 (48.3%) patients with XDR-TB successfully completed treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Successful outcomes depend on a number of factors including the extent of the drug resistance, the severity of the disease and whether the patient’s immune system is compromised. It also depends on access to laboratories that can provide early and accurate diagnosis so that effective treatment is provided as soon as possible. Effective treatment requires that all six classes of second-line drugs be available to clinicians who have special expertise in treating such cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Countries aim to prevent XDR-TB by ensuring that the work of their national TB control programmes, and of all practitioners working with people with TB, is carried out according to the International Standards for TB Care. These emphasize providing proper diagnosis and treatment to all TB patients, including those with drug-resistant TB; assuring regular, timely supplies of all anti-TB drugs; proper management of anti-TB drugs and providing support to patients to maximize adherence to prescribed regimens; caring for XDR-TB cases in a centre with proper ventilation, and minimizing contact with other patients, particularly those with HIV, especially in the early stages before treatment has had a chance to reduce the infectiousness. Also an effective disease control infrastructure is necessary for the prevention of XDR tuberculosis. Increased funding for research, and strengthened laboratory facilities are much required. Immediate detection through drug susceptibility testing's are vital, when trying to stop the spread of XDR tuberculosis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="TB_vaccine">TB vaccine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: TB vaccine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/BCG_vaccine" title="BCG vaccine">BCG vaccine</a> prevents severe forms of TB in children, such as TB <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a>. It would be expected that BCG would have the same effect in preventing severe forms of TB in children, even if they were exposed to XDR-TB, but it may be less effective in preventing pulmonary TB in adults, the most common and most infectious form of TB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The effect of BCG against XDR-TB would therefore likely be very limited. New vaccines are urgently needed, and WHO and members of the Stop TB Partnership are actively working on new vaccines.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enforced_quarantine">Enforced quarantine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enforced quarantine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carriers who refuse to wear a mask in public have been indefinitely <a href="/wiki/Civil_confinement" title="Civil confinement">involuntarily committed</a> to regular jails, and cut off from contacting the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Some have run away from the USA, complaining of abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_Factors">Risk Factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Risk Factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studies have found that men have a higher risk of getting XDR-TB than women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> One study showed that the male to female ratio was more than threefold, with statistical relevance (P&lt;0.05)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Studies done on the effect of age and XDR-TB have revealed that individuals who are 65 and up are less likely to get XDR-TB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A study in Japan found that XDR-TB patients are more likely to be younger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="XDR-TB_and_HIV.2FAIDS">XDR-TB and HIV/AIDS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: XDR-TB and HIV/AIDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>TB is one of the most common infections in people living with <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>. In places where XDR-TB is most common, people living with HIV are at greater risk of becoming infected with XDR-TB, compared with people without HIV, because of their weakened immunity. If there are a lot of HIV-infected people in these places, then there will be a strong link between XDR-TB and HIV. Fortunately, in most of the places with high rates of HIV, XDR-TB is not yet widespread. For this reason, the majority of people with HIV who develop TB will have drug-susceptible or ordinary TB, and can be treated with standard first-line anti-TB drugs. For those with HIV infection, treatment with antiretroviral drugs will likely reduce the risk of becoming infected with XDR-TB, just as it does with ordinary TB.</p>
<p>A research study titled "TB Prevalence Survey and Evaluation of Access to TB Care in HIV-Infected and Uninfected TB Patients in Asembo and Gem, Western Kenya," says that HIV/AIDS is fueling large increases in TB incidence in Africa, and a large proportion of cases are not diagnosed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms">Symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Symptoms of XDR-TB are no different from ordinary or drug-susceptible TB: a cough with thick, cloudy mucus (or sputum), sometimes with blood, for more than 2 weeks; fever, chills, and night sweats; fatigue and muscle weakness; weight loss; and in some cases shortness of breath and chest pain. A person with these symptoms does not necessarily have XDR-TB, but they should see a physician for diagnosis and a treatment plan. TB patients whose symptoms do not improve after a few weeks of treatment for TB and are taking treatment should inform their clinician or nurse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_African_epidemic">South African epidemic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extensively_drug-resistant_tuberculosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: South African epidemic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>XDR-TB was first widely publicised following the report of an outbreak in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> in 2006. 53 patients in a rural hospital in <a href="/wiki/Tugela_Ferry" title="Tugela Ferry">Tugela Ferry</a> were found to have XDR-TB of whom 52 died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandhi_2006_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gandhi_2006-25">[25]</a></sup> The median survival from sputum specimen collection to death was only 16 days and that the majority of patients had never previously received treatment for tuberculosis suggesting that they had been newly infected by XDR-TB strains, and that resistance did not develop during treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandhi_2006_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gandhi_2006-25">[25]</a></sup> This was the first epidemic for which the acronym XDR-TB was used, and although TB strains that fulfill the current definition have been identified retrospectively,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> this was the largest group of linked cases ever found. Since the initial report in September 2006, cases have now been reported in most provinces in South Africa. As of 16 March 2007, there were 314 cases reported, with 215 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It is clear that the spread of this strain of TB is closely associated with a high prevalence of HIV and poor infection control; in other countries where XDR-TB strains have arisen, drug resistance has arisen from mismanagement of cases or poor patient compliance with drug treatment instead of being transmitted from person to person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It is now clear that the problem has been around for much longer than health department officials have suggested, and is far more extensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
