<p>In the area of modern algebra known as <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>, the <b>Monster group</b> <i>M</i> (also known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Bernd_Fischer_(mathematician)" title="Bernd Fischer (mathematician)">Fischer</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Griess" title="Griess">Griess</a> Monster</b>, or the <b>Friendly Giant</b>) is the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporadic_simple_group" title="Sporadic simple group">sporadic simple group</a>, having <a href="/wiki/Order_(group_theory)" title="Order (group theory)">order</a></p>
<dl>
<dd>   2<sup>46</sup> <b>·</b> 3<sup>20</sup> <b>·</b> 5<sup>9</sup> <b>·</b> 7<sup>6</sup> <b>·</b> 11<sup>2</sup> <b>·</b> 13<sup>3</sup> <b>·</b> 17 <b>·</b> 19 <b>·</b> 23 <b>·</b> 29 <b>·</b> 31 <b>·</b> 41 <b>·</b> 47 <b>·</b> 59 <b>·</b> 71</dd>
<dd>= 808,017,424,794,512,875,886,459,904,961,710,757,005,754,368,000,000,000</dd>
<dd>≈ 8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">53</span></sup>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Finite_group" title="Finite group">finite</a> <a href="/wiki/Simple_group" title="Simple group">simple</a> <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a> have been completely <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classified</a>. Every such group belongs to one of 18 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countably" title="Countably">countably</a> infinite families, or is one of 26 sporadic groups that do not follow such a systematic pattern. The Monster group contains all but six of the other sporadic groups as <a href="/wiki/Subquotient" title="Subquotient">subquotients</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Griess" title="Robert Griess">Robert Griess</a> has called these 6 exceptions <a href="/wiki/Pariah_group" title="Pariah group">pariahs</a>, and refers to the other 20 as the <i>happy family</i>.</p>
<p>It is difficult to make a good constructive definition of the Monster because of its complexity. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a> wrote a popular account of the monster group in his June 1980 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_Games_column" title="Mathematical Games column">Mathematical Games column</a> in Scientific American.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Monster was predicted by <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Fischer_(mathematician)" title="Bernd Fischer (mathematician)">Bernd Fischer</a> (unpublished, about 1973) and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Griess" title="Robert Griess">Robert Griess</a> (<a href="#CITEREFGriess1976">1976</a>) as a simple group containing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_covering_group" title="Double covering group">double cover</a> of Fischer's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Monster_group" title="Baby Monster group">Baby Monster group</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Centralizer_and_normalizer" title="Centralizer and normalizer">centralizer</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Involution_(mathematics)" title="Involution (mathematics)">involution</a>. Within a few months the order of M was found by Griess using the <a href="/wiki/Thompson_order_formula" title="Thompson order formula">Thompson order formula</a>, and Fischer, Conway, Norton and Thompson discovered other groups as subquotients, including many of the known sporadic groups, and two new ones: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thompson_group_(finite)" title="Thompson group (finite)">Thompson group</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harada%E2%80%93Norton_group" title="Harada–Norton group">Harada–Norton group</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Character_theory" title="Character theory">character table</a> of the Monster, a 194-by-194 array, was calculated in 1979 by Fischer and Donald Livingstone using computer programs written by Michael Thorne. It was not clear in the 1970s that the Monster actually existed. <a href="#CITEREFGriess1982">Griess (1982)</a> constructed M as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automorphism_group" title="Automorphism group">automorphism group</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Griess_algebra" title="Griess algebra">Griess algebra</a>, a 196,884-dimensional commutative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonassociative_algebra" title="Nonassociative algebra">nonassociative algebra</a>; he first announced his construction in Ann Arbor Jan. 14,1980. In his 1982 paper he referred to the Monster as the Friendly Giant, but this name has not been generally adopted. <a href="/wiki/John_Horton_Conway" title="John Horton Conway">John Conway</a> (<a href="#CITEREFConway1985">1985</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Tits" title="Jacques Tits">Jacques Tits</a> (<a href="#CITEREFTits1984">1984</a>, <a href="#CITEREFTits1985">1985</a>) subsequently simplified this construction.</p>
<p>Griess's construction showed that the Monster existed. <a href="/wiki/John_G._Thompson" title="John G. Thompson">Thompson</a> (<a href="#CITEREFThompson1979">1979</a>) showed that its uniqueness (as a simple group satisfying certain conditions coming from the classification of finite simple groups) would follow from the existence of a 196,883-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Faithful_representation" title="Faithful representation">faithful representation</a>. A proof of the existence of such a representation was announced by <a href="/wiki/Simon_P._Norton" title="Simon P. Norton">Norton</a> (<a href="#CITEREFNorton1985">1985</a>), though he has never published the details. <a href="#CITEREFGriessMeierfrankenfeldSegev1989">Griess, Meierfrankenfeld &amp; Segev (1989)</a> gave the first complete published proof of the uniqueness of the Monster (more precisely, they showed that a group with the same centralizers of involutions as the Monster is isomorphic to the Monster).</p>
<p>The Monster was a culmination of a development of sporadic simple groups and can be built from any 2 of 3 subquotients: the <a href="/wiki/Fischer_group" title="Fischer group">Fischer group</a> Fi<sub>24</sub>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Monster" title="Baby Monster">Baby Monster</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Conway_group" title="Conway group">Conway group</a> Co<sub>1</sub>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Schur_multiplier" title="Schur multiplier">Schur multiplier</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Outer_automorphism_group" title="Outer automorphism group">outer automorphism group</a> of the Monster are both <a href="/wiki/Trivial_group" title="Trivial group">trivial</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representations">Representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The minimal degree of a faithful complex representation is 196,883, which is the product of the 3 largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_divisor" title="Prime divisor">prime divisors</a> of the order of M. The smallest faithful linear representation over any field has dimension 196,882 over the field with 2 elements, only 1 less than the dimension of the smallest faithful complex representation.</p>
<p>The smallest faithful permutation representation of the Monster is on 2<sup>4</sup> · 3<sup>7</sup> · 5<sup>3</sup> · 7<sup>4</sup> · 11 · 13<sup>2</sup> · 29 · 41 · 59 · 71 (about 10<sup>20</sup>) points.</p>
<p>The Monster can be realized as a <a href="/wiki/Galois_group" title="Galois group">Galois group</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Rational_number" title="Rational number">rational numbers</a> (<a href="#CITEREFThompson1984">Thompson 1984</a>, p. 443), and as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurwitz_group" title="Hurwitz group">Hurwitz group</a> (<a href="#CITEREFWilson2004">Wilson 2004</a>).</p>
<p>The Monster is unusual among simple groups in that there is no known easy way to represent its elements. This is not due so much to its size as to the absence of "small" representations. For example, the simple groups <i>A</i><sub>100</sub> and SL<sub>20</sub>(2) are far larger, but easy to calculate with as they have "small" permutation or linear representations. The alternating groups have permutation representations that are "small" compared to the size of the group, and all finite simple groups of Lie type have linear representations that are "small" compared to the size of the group. All sporadic groups other than the Monster also have linear representations small enough that they are easy to work with on a computer (the next hardest case after the monster is the baby Monster, with a representation of dimension 4370).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_computer_construction">A computer construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A computer construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Arnott_Wilson" title="Robert Arnott Wilson">Robert A. Wilson</a> has found explicitly (with the aid of a computer) two 196,882 by 196,882 matrices (with elements in the <a href="/wiki/Finite_field" title="Finite field">field of order 2</a>) which together generate the Monster group; this is one dimension lower than the 196,883-dimensional representation in characteristic 0. Performing calculations with these matrices is possible but is too expensive in terms of time and storage space to be useful, as each such matrix occupies over four and a half gigabytes.</p>
<p>Wilson asserts that the best description of the Monster is to say, "It is the automorphism group of the <a href="/wiki/Monster_vertex_algebra" title="Monster vertex algebra">monster vertex algebra</a>". This is not much help however, because nobody has found a "really simple and natural construction of the monster vertex algebra".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilson with collaborators has found a method of performing calculations with the Monster that is considerably faster. Let <i>V</i> be a 196,882 dimensional vector space over the field with 2 elements. A large subgroup <i>H</i> (preferably a maximal subgroup) of the Monster is selected in which it is easy to perform calculations. The subgroup <i>H</i> chosen is 3<sup>1+12</sup>.2.Suz.2, where Suz is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzuki_group_(mathematics)" title="Suzuki group (mathematics)">Suzuki group</a>. Elements of the Monster are stored as words in the elements of <i>H</i> and an extra generator <i>T</i>. It is reasonably quick to calculate the action of one of these words on a vector in <i>V</i>. Using this action, it is possible to perform calculations (such as the order of an element of the Monster). Wilson has exhibited vectors <i>u</i> and <i>v</i> whose joint stabilizer is the trivial group. Thus (for example) one can calculate the order of an element <i>g</i> of the monster by finding the smallest <i>i</i> &gt; 0 such that <i>g</i><sup><i>i</i></sup><i>u</i> = <i>u</i> and <i>g</i><sup><i>i</i></sup><i>v</i> = <i>v</i>.</p>
<p>This and similar constructions (in different <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra)" title="Characteristic (algebra)">characteristics</a>) have been used to find some of its non-local maximal subgroups.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moonshine">Moonshine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Moonshine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Monster group is one of two principal constituents in the <a href="/wiki/Monstrous_moonshine" title="Monstrous moonshine">Monstrous moonshine</a> conjecture by Conway and Norton, which relates discrete and non-discrete mathematics and was finally proved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Ewen_Borcherds" title="Richard Ewen Borcherds">Richard Borcherds</a> in 1992.</p>
<p>In this setting, the Monster group is visible as the automorphism group of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monster_module" title="Monster module">Monster module</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Vertex_operator_algebra" title="Vertex operator algebra">vertex operator algebra</a>, an infinite dimensional algebra containing the Griess algebra, and acts on the <a href="/wiki/Monster_Lie_algebra" title="Monster Lie algebra">Monster Lie algebra</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Generalized_Kac%E2%80%93Moody_algebra" title="Generalized Kac–Moody algebra">generalized Kac–Moody algebra</a>.</p>

<p>The Monster has at least 44 conjugacy classes of maximal subgroups. Non-abelian simple groups of some 60 isomorphism types are found as subgroups or as quotients of subgroups. The largest <a href="/wiki/Alternating_group" title="Alternating group">alternating group</a> represented is A<sub>12</sub>. The Monster contains 20 of the 26 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporadic_groups" title="Sporadic groups">sporadic groups</a> as subquotients. This diagram, based on one in the book <i>Symmetry and the Monster</i> by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ronan" title="Mark Ronan">Mark Ronan</a>, shows how they fit together. The lines signify inclusion, as a subquotient, of the lower group by the upper one. The circled symbols denote groups not involved in larger sporadic groups. For the sake of clarity redundant inclusions are not shown.</p>
<p>44 of the classes of maximal subgroups of the Monster are given by the following list, which is (as of 2016) believed to be complete except possibly for almost simple subgroups with non-abelian simple <a href="/wiki/Socle_(mathematics)" title="Socle (mathematics)">socles</a> of the form L<sub>2</sub>(13), U<sub>3</sub>(4), or U<sub>3</sub>(8) (<a href="#CITEREFWilson2010">Wilson 2010</a>), (<a href="#CITEREFNortonWilson2013">Norton &amp; Wilson 2013</a>), (<a href="#CITEREFWilson2016">Wilson 2016</a>). However, tables of maximal subgroups have often been found to contain subtle errors, and in particular at least two of the subgroups on the list below were incorrectly omitted in some previous lists.</p>
