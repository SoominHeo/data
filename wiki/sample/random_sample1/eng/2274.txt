<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bridgehead</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Bridgehead_(disambiguation)" title="Bridgehead (disambiguation)">Bridgehead (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>bridgehead</b> (or <b>bridge-head</b>) is the strategically important area of ground around the end of a bridge or other place of possible crossing over a body of water which at time of conflict is sought to be defended/taken over by the belligerent forces.</p>
<p>Bridgeheads typically exist for only a few days, the invading forces either being thrown back or expanding the bridgehead to create a secure defensive <a href="/wiki/Lodgement" title="Lodgement">lodgement</a> area, before breaking out into enemy territory, such as when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._9th_Armored_Division" title="U.S. 9th Armored Division">U.S. 9th Armored Division</a> seized the <a href="/wiki/Ludendorff_Bridge" title="Ludendorff Bridge">Ludendorff Bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Remagen" title="Remagen">Remagen</a> in 1945 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In some cases a bridgehead may exist for months.</p>

<p><i>Bridgehead</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>t√™te de pont</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> military term, which before the invention of cannons meant the military <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortification</a> that protects the end of a <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridge</a>. Like many older terms, the meaning of the word drifted with the passage of time, becoming used for something not exactly true to its initial usage.</p>
<p>With the introduction of cannons, the term was morphed to a generalized term for field fortifications lying some distance beyond the ends of the bridge that were emplaced to protect both the bridge and any troops crossing it to the far bank, so became in that era a term used when referring to both the fortifications and the small lodgement on the bank that was closest to the enemy. As the process of moving an army over bridges is slow and complicated, it is usually necessary to secure it from hostile interruption, and the works constituting the bridge-head must therefore be sufficiently far advanced to keep the enemy's artillery out of range of the bridges‚Äîhence as artillery grew in power, so did the size of the lodgements necessary. In addition, room is required for the troops to form up on the farther bank. Formerly, with short-range weapons, a bridgehead was often little more than a screen for the bridge itself, but modern conditions have rendered necessary far greater extensions of bridge defenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then armies and military formations grew, so needed more lodgement area to organize a force large enough to stage a break out against a determined enemy, and again the technical meaning of the term expanded, again referring to a large fortified area about at bridge end. With the advent of modern warfare capabilities, including long-range tube artillery and high-powered rifles with effective ranges measured in the thousands of yards (metres), the term of art again had to expand in area, but now morphed to be just an area defended and controlled by ample firepower, with or without constructed fortifications.</p>
<p>The term in colloquial usage refers to any kind of defended area that is extended into hostile territory ‚Äì also called a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/foothold" title="wikt:foothold">foothold</a> and sometimes the technically incorrect '<a href="/wiki/Beachhead" title="Beachhead">beachhead</a>', which is frequently mistaken colloquially as synonymous terminology. The technical term refers in particular to the specific area on the farside of a defended river bank or a segment of a lake or riverine coastline held by the enemy forces, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ludendorff_Bridge" title="Ludendorff Bridge">Bridge at Remagen</a>. The term is especially applied when such a territory is initially seized by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibious_assault" title="Amphibious assault">amphibious assault</a> with the tactical intent of establishing a supply line across the geographic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrain_feature" title="Terrain feature">barrier feature</a> to allow further <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operational_mobility" title="Operational mobility">operational manoeuvring</a>. In that sense, it has much in common with the popular misconception of a beachhead.</p>
