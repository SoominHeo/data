<p>A <b>parliamentary group</b>, <b>parliamentary party</b>, or <b>parliamentary caucus</b> is a group consisting of members of the same <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electoral_fusion" title="Electoral fusion">electoral fusion</a> of parties in a legislative assembly such as a <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a> or a <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a>. Parliamentary groups correspond to <b>"caucuses"</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Party_caucuses_and_conferences_in_the_United_States_Congress" title="Party caucuses and conferences in the United States Congress">United States Congress</a> and in the Canadian Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A parliamentary group is sometimes called the <i>parliamentary wing</i> of a party, as distinct from its <i>organisational wing</i>.</p>
<p>Generally, parliamentary groups have some independence from the wider party organisations. It is often thought improper for elected MPs to take instructions solely from non-elected party officials or from the small subset of the electorate represented by party members. In any case, the exigencies of government, the need to cooperate with other members of the legislature and the desire to retain the support of the electorate as a whole often preclude strict adherence to the wider party's wishes. The exact relationship between the parliamentary party and the party varies between countries, and also from party to party. For example, in some parties, the parliamentary and organisational leadership will be held by the same person or people, whether <i>ex officio</i> or not; other parties maintain a sharp distinction between the two offices. Nevertheless, in almost all cases, the parliamentary leader is the public face of the party, and wields considerable influence within the organisational wing, whether or not he or she has any official position there.</p>
<p>The term applies to a number of countries, including: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Australia" title="Politics of Australia">Australia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Austria" title="Politics of Austria">Austria</a> (where the term <i>Club</i> is used); <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Belgium" title="Politics of Belgium">Belgium</a> (<i>fractie</i>/<i>fraction</i>/<i>Fraktion</i>); <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Brazil" title="Politics of Brazil">Brazil</a> ("grupo parlamentar" or "bancadas"); <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Germany" title="Politics of Germany">Germany</a> (<i>Fraktion</i>); <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Italy" title="Politics of Italy">Italy</a> (<i>gruppo</i>), <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Finland" title="Politics of Finland">Finland</a> (eduskuntaryhmä/<i>riksdagsgrupp</i>); the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Netherlands" title="Politics of the Netherlands">Netherlands</a> (<i>fractie</i>); <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Switzerland" title="Politics of Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (<i>fraction</i>/<i>Fraktion</i>/<i>frazione</i>); and <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Romania" title="Politics of Romania">Romania</a> (<i>grup parlamentar</i>), which all have recognized multiparty systems. In some of these countries, the only way that parties and members of Parliament can receive financial and personal support and can join <a href="/wiki/Committee" title="Committee">parliamentary committees</a> is by organizing themselves in parliamentary groups. Parliamentary group leaders are often important political players. Parties that are not in government often choose the party's political leader as the chairperson. Parliamentary groups often use <a href="/wiki/Party_discipline" title="Party discipline">party discipline</a> to control the votes of their members.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Political_groups_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Political groups of the European Parliament">political groups of the European Parliament</a> are similar to other parliamentary groups. They are more regulated than other kinds of parliamentary groups: to gain financial support or to join committees, each parliamentary group must consist of no less than 25 <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEPs</a> from seven different EU <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_European_Union_member_states" title="List of European Union member states">member states</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A parliamentary group is typically led by a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_group_leader" title="Parliamentary group leader">parliamentary group leader</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chairperson" title="Chairperson">chairperson</a>, though some parliamentary groups have two or more <i>co-leaders</i>. If the parliamentary group is represented in the legislature, the leader is almost always chosen from among the sitting members; if the leader does not yet have a seat in the legislature, a sitting member of the group may be expected to resign to make way for him or her. If the party is not represented in the legislature for the time being, the leader will often be put forward at a <a href="/wiki/General_election" title="General election">general election</a> as the party's candidate for their most winnable seat. In some parties, the leader is elected solely by the members of the parliamentary group; in others, some or all members of the wider party participate in the election. Parliamentary groups often have one or more <a href="/wiki/Whip_(politics)" title="Whip (politics)">whips</a>, whose role is to support the leadership by enforcing <a href="/wiki/Party_discipline" title="Party discipline">party discipline</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="APPGs">APPGs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: APPGs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/All-party_parliamentary_group" title="All-party parliamentary group">All-party parliamentary group</a></div>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Parliament" title="United Kingdom Parliament">United Kingdom Parliament</a> there exist associations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MPs</a> called <i>all-party parliamentary groups</i>, which bring together members of different parliamentary groups who wish to involve themselves with a particular subject. This term is in a sense the opposite of the term 'parliamentary group', which designates a group that includes only members of the same party or electoral fusion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_Friendship_Groups">Parliamentary Friendship Groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parliamentary Friendship Groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One special kind of parliamentary groups are the <b>Parliamentary Friendship Groups</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> also called <b>Inter-Parliamentary Friendship Groups</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or <b>Friendship Parliamentary Groups</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or <b>Parliamentary Group of Friendship [and Cooperation]</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Parliamentary Friendship" groups are groups of congresspeople/members of parliament who voluntarily organise themselves to promote parliamentary relations between their own Parliament and another country's (or even a region's group of countries') parliament(s), and, in a broader scope, to foster the bilateral relations between said countries. Parliamentary friendship groups play an important role in New Zealand’s engagement in inter-parliamentary relations, with group members often called upon to participate and host meetings for visiting delegations from the other part, as well as often being invited by the other country's parliament to visit it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Friendship Groups do not speak for the Government of their own country, or even for the whole of the Parliament/Congress to which they belong, as they are usually self-regulating and self-fulfilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parliamentary groups are active in the national congresses/parliaments of countries such as Armenia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Brazil,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Israel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Laos,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> New Zealand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Pakistan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Peru,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Romania,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Serbia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Slovenia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Switzerland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> among many others.</p>
