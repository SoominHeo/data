<p><b>French legislative elections</b> to elect the third <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(France)" title="National Assembly (France)">National Assembly</a> of the <a href="/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic" title="French Fourth Republic">Fourth Republic</a> took place on 2 January 1956 using <a href="/wiki/Party-list_proportional_representation" title="Party-list proportional representation">party-list proportional representation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French_Elections_1951-1969_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-French_Elections_1951-1969-1">[1]</a></sup> The elections had been scheduled for June 1956, however they were brought forward by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Faure" title="Edgar Faure">Edgar Faure</a> using a constitutional sanction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shields_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The previous legislative elections in 1951 had been won by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_force_(France)" title="Third force (France)">Third Force</a>, a coalition of center-left and center-right parties, but it was divided about denominational schools question and, when faced with the colonial problem, the governments had gradually moved towards the right. A part of the <a href="/wiki/Rally_of_the_French_People" title="Rally of the French People">Rally of the French People</a> (RPF), the Gaullist party, joined the majority in opposing the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>, who then retired.</p>
<p>The defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu" title="Battle of Dien Bien Phu">Battle of Dien Bien Phu</a> in May 1954 caused a political crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shields_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-2">[2]</a></sup> The Radical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_Mend%C3%A8s-France" title="Pierre Mendès-France">Pierre Mendès-France</a> became leader of the cabinet and ended the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a>. He also began the process of independence for <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, but from November 1954 on, France was confronted by the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algerian War</a>. In February 1955, Mendès-France was replaced, at the head of the cabinet, by his rival in the Radical Party, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Faure" title="Edgar Faure">Edgar Faure</a>. This one led a more repressive policy in Algeria.</p>
<p>The far-right, led by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Poujade" title="Pierre Poujade">Pierre Poujade</a>, re-appeared at about the same time. He was a critic of "<a href="/wiki/Fiscalism" title="Fiscalism">fiscalism</a>", and leader of a shopkeepers and craftsmen's movement. Many voters seemed tired of the political system's numerous ministerial crises, and he had much support in the rural areas, which were in decline.</p>
<p>The anticipated legislative elections took place when Faure was defeated by the National Assembly. Even though the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Communist Party</a> re-emerged as the country's most popular party (for the last time in its history), it did not join the government. A coalition was formed behind Mendès-France and advocated a peaceful resolution of the Algerian conflict. This <a href="/wiki/Republican_Front_(France)" title="Republican Front (France)">Republican Front</a> was composed of the <a href="/wiki/French_Section_of_the_Workers%27_International" title="French Section of the Workers' International">French Section of the Workers' International</a> (SFIO, socialist party) of <a href="/wiki/Guy_Mollet" title="Guy Mollet">Guy Mollet</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Radical_Party" title="French Radical Party">Radical Party</a> of Pierre Mendès-France, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_and_Socialist_Union_of_the_Resistance" title="Democratic and Socialist Union of the Resistance">Democratic and Socialist Union of the Resistance</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Centre_of_Social_Republicans" title="National Centre of Social Republicans">National Centre of Social Republicans</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chaban-Delmas" title="Jacques Chaban-Delmas">Jacques Chaban-Delmas</a>. Faure was excluded from the Radical Party – in response he transformed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rally_of_the_Republican_Lefts" title="Rally of the Republican Lefts">Rally of the Republican Lefts</a> (which had been abandoned by those groups which had now joined the Republican Front) into a party that he led, and he campaigned with the center-right parties. The French Communist Party remained the largest party and the Republican Front obtained a relative majority in order to end the Algerian War.</p>
<p>The Poujadists won representation in Parliament, and were considered by the press to hold the balance of power. Media reception was mixed, with the result welcomed by communist supporters and condemned by papers such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and the French <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shade_of_Hitler_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shade_of_Hitler-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The coalition cabinet was led by the Socialist leader <a href="/wiki/Guy_Mollet" title="Guy Mollet">Guy Mollet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shields_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-2">[2]</a></sup> At the beginning he was also supported by the Communists, but pressure from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pieds-noir" title="Pieds-noir">pieds-noir</a></i> in Algeria incited him into leading a very repressive policy against the Algerian nationalists. This policy was criticized by Vice-Prime Minister Mendès-France and other members of the cabinet, who resigned, thus splitting the Republican Front. Mollet and his successors floundered in the conflict until May 1958.</p>
