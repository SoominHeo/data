<p>A <b>gallows</b> (or <b>scaffold</b>) is a frame, typically wooden, used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_(legal)" title="Execution (legal)">execution</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a>, or as a means of torture before execution, as was used when being <a href="/wiki/Hanged,_drawn_and_quartered" title="Hanged, drawn and quartered">hanged, drawn and quartered</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_hanging">Forms of hanging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forms of hanging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gallows can take several forms:</p>
<ul>
<li>The simplest form (as often used in the game "<a href="/wiki/Hangman_(game)" title="Hangman (game)">Hangman</a>") resembles an inverted "L" (or a Greek/Cyrillic "Г"), with a single upright and a horizontal beam to which the <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">rope</a> <a href="/wiki/Noose" title="Noose">noose</a> would be attached.</li>
<li>The horizontal crossbeam is supported at both ends.</li>
<li>There were even temporary gallows, which were portable, but weaker.</li>
<li>The infamous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyburn_gallows" title="Tyburn gallows">Tyburn gallows</a> was triangular in plan, with three uprights and three crossbeams, allowing up to 24 men and women to be executed simultaneously when all three sides were used.</li>
</ul>
<p>Occasionally, improvised gallows were used, usually by hanging the condemned from a tree or <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street light</a>. Hangings from such improvised gallows are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lynchings" title="Lynchings">lynchings</a> rather than judicial executions. In Afghanistan, <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> used <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_pitch#Goals" title="Association football pitch">goals</a> as gallows.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent">Permanent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Permanent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gallows may be permanent to act as a deterrent and grim symbol of the power of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_justice" title="High justice">high justice</a> (the French word for gallows, <i>potence</i>, stems from the Latin word <i>potentia</i>, meaning "power"). Many old prints of European cities show such a permanent gallows erected on a prominent hill outside the walls, or more commonly near the castle or other seat of justice. In the modern era the gallows were often installed inside a prison; freestanding on a scaffold in the yard, erected at ground level over a pit, enclosed in a small shed of stone, brick or wood, built into the gallery of a prison wing (with the beam resting in <a href="/wiki/Bracket_(architecture)" title="Bracket (architecture)">brackets</a> on opposite walls), or in a purpose-built execution suite of rooms within the wing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary">Temporary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temporary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gallows can also be temporary. In some of the cases, they were even moved to the location of the crime. In England, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirates" title="Pirates">pirates</a> were typically executed using a temporary gallows, at low tide in the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a>, then left for the sea to wash over them during the following high tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Konstan1998_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Konstan1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Photo_poultry_hall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Photo_poultry_hall.jpg/170px-Photo_poultry_hall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Photo_poultry_hall.jpg/255px-Photo_poultry_hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Photo_poultry_hall.jpg/340px-Photo_poultry_hall.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photo_poultry_hall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Gallows in <a href="/wiki/Rutland_County_Museum" title="Rutland County Museum">Rutland County Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The only surviving New Drop gallows in the UK are in <a href="/wiki/Rutland_County_Museum" title="Rutland County Museum">Rutland County Museum</a>. The gallows were portable and were set up at the gaol (jail) when needed. These gallows were first used in 1813 to hang two burglars. The New Drop design was not very effective as the drop was too short to break the neck cleanly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portable">Portable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If a crime took place inside, gallows were sometimes erected—and the criminal hanged—at the front door. In some cases of multiple offenders it was not uncommon to erect multiple temporary gallows, with one noose per condemned criminal. In one case a condemned strangled to death in agony for forty minutes until he finally died from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_and_cart">Horse and cart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Horse and cart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hanging people from early gallows sometimes involved fitting the noose around the person's <a href="/wiki/Neck" title="Neck">neck</a> while he or she was on a ladder or in a horse-drawn cart underneath. Removing the ladder or driving the cart away left the person dangling by the neck to slowly strangle. A noted example of this type of execution in the USA was the hanging of British spy <a href="/wiki/John_Andr%C3%A9" title="John André">John André</a> in 1780.</p>
<p>Later, a "scaffold" with a <a href="/wiki/Trapdoor" title="Trapdoor">trapdoor</a> tended to be used, so victims dropped down and died quickly from a broken neck rather than through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strangulation" title="Strangulation">strangulation</a>, especially if extra weights were fixed to their ankles.</p>
<p>During the era of public execution in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, England, a prominent gallows stood at <a href="/wiki/Tyburn" title="Tyburn">Tyburn</a>, on what is now <a href="/wiki/Marble_Arch" title="Marble Arch">Marble Arch</a>. Later executions occurred outside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newgate_Gaol" title="Newgate Gaol">Newgate Gaol</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a> now stands.</p>
