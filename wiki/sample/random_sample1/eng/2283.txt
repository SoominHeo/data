<p>The <b>head teacher</b>, <b>headmaster</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spelling_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spelling-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>headmistress</b>, <b>head</b>, or <b>principal</b> (sometimes another title is used) is the <a href="/wiki/Teacher" title="Teacher">teacher</a> with the greatest responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> of a <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">College</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the past, the headmaster or headmistress of a British <a href="/wiki/Independent_school_(United_Kingdom)" title="Independent school (United Kingdom)">private school</a> was often the owner of the school or a member of the owning family, and the position often remained in the family for many generations.</p>
<p>In Scotland, the holder of this position is sometimes known as the "<a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a>", most commonly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_school_(UK)" title="Independent school (UK)">independent schools</a>. In North America, Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong and Ireland (including <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>), the position holder is usually known as the "school principal", but some schools, primarily independent schools, use the term "headmaster" or "head master". As in Scotland, the term "<a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a>" is still in use in the United States in independent religious schools, as by tradition the Head of School was also a priest. Some American <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">state schools</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Boston_Latin_School" title="Boston Latin School">Boston Latin School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Latin_School" title="Brooklyn Latin School">Brooklyn Latin School</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Milpitas_High_School" title="Milpitas High School">Milpitas High School</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2010)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Head_teacher#Dubious" title="Talk:Head teacher">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> also use the term "headmaster", either because of its history or historical connections.</p>
<p>In Britain, the terms "headmaster" and "headmistress" used to be the formal titles in most state schools as well as private schools, with "head teacher" only being used as a term to refer to them collectively. In recent years, however, most state schools have switched to the gender-neutral "head teacher" as the official title. Nevertheless, the gender-specific terms are still in common use, and are still the official titles at some of the remaining state <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar schools</a> and most private schools. Some use other terms, such as "high master".</p>
<p>"Principal" or "Head of School" is often used as the title of the head administrator of an <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">elementary school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">middle school</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding school</a> in some English-speaking countries, including the United States, India, Australia and New Zealand. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)#United_States" title="Public school (government funded)">Public schools in the United States</a> generally use the title "principal" whereas <a href="/wiki/Private_school" title="Private school">private schools</a> in the United States sometimes use the title Head of School.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While some head teachers still do some teaching themselves, in most larger schools most of their duties are <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">managerial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_care" title="Pastoral care">pastoral</a>.</p>
<p>In Australia, the Head teacher is sometimes in charge of one (in the case of a major subject) or multiple (often in smaller schools) specific departments, such as English, History, Maths, Science, Writing, Technology, etc., but maintains full teaching duties and status. They are considered part of the school executive, and often a head teacher position is a stepping-stone into administration.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deputy_head">Deputy head</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deputy head">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deputy_head_teacher" title="Deputy head teacher">Deputy head teacher</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assistants">Assistants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assistants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In larger schools, the principal is assisted by one or more "<a href="/wiki/Vice-principal" title="Vice-principal">vice-principals</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assistant_principal" title="Assistant principal">assistant principals</a>", "associate principals", or "deputy principals". Their position is secondary to the principal with regard to school governance. Assistant principals generally perform specific duties such as handling student discipline, curriculum, student council or student activities whereas the principal has the ultimate responsibility for the school as a whole (including faculty and staff, physical plant, etc.).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_information">Regional information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regional information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_and_New_Zealand">Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australia and New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many Australian and New Zealand schools, a headmaster/principal is the head <a href="/wiki/Academic_administration" title="Academic administration">administrator</a> of a school who has been appointed to her/his position by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_board" title="School board">school board</a>, superintendent, or other body. The principal, often in conjunction with the school board, makes the executive decisions that govern the school, as well as having the authority over the employment (and in some cases firing) of teachers. The principal is often the chief <a href="/wiki/School_discipline" title="School discipline">disciplinarian</a> of the students.</p>

<p>In 1999, there were about 133,000 principals and assistant principals in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the early decades of public education, the full title was "principal teacher", which accounts for the present day title having an adjectival form, essentially being a foreshortened version of the original full title. Yet the term Headmaster is still used in some older schools. School principals in the United States are expected to have school administrator licensure, and, often, a master's degree in educational administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_school_leaders">Impact of school leaders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Head_teacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impact of school leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While there has been considerable anecdotal discussion about the importance of school leaders, there has been very little systematic research into their impact on student outcomes. Recent analysis in the United States has examined how the gains in student achievement at a school change after the principal changes. This outcome-based approach to measuring effectiveness of principals is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Value-added_modeling" title="Value-added modeling">value-added modeling</a> that has been applied to the evaluation of teachers. Such research in the state of Texas found that principals have a very large impact on student achievement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Effective school leaders have been shown to significantly improve the performance of all students at the school, at least in part through their impacts on selection and retention of good teachers. Ineffective principals have a similarly large negative effect on school performance, suggesting that issues of evaluation are as important for school administrators as they are for teachers. The impact of principals has also been measured in non-traditional ways.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some principals have focused their efforts on creating more inclusive schools for students with disabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
