<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Intersection number</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about algebraic geometry. For the concept in graph theory, see <a href="/wiki/Intersection_number_(graph_theory)" title="Intersection number (graph theory)">Intersection number (graph theory)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and especially in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>, the <b>intersection number</b> generalizes the intuitive notion of counting the number of times two curves intersect to higher dimensions, multiple (more than 2) curves, and accounting properly for <a href="/wiki/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangency</a>. One needs a definition of intersection number in order to state results like <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9zout%27s_theorem" title="Bézout's theorem">Bézout's theorem</a>.</p>
<p>The intersection number is obvious in certain cases, such as the intersection of <i>x</i>- and <i>y</i>-axes which should be one. The complexity enters when calculating intersections at points of tangency and intersections along positive dimensional sets. For example, if a plane is tangent to a surface along a line, the intersection number along the line should be at least two. These questions are discussed systematically in <a href="/wiki/Intersection_theory" title="Intersection theory">intersection theory</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-intersections">Self-intersections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intersection_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Self-intersections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the most interesting intersection numbers to compute are <i>self-intersection numbers</i>. This should not be taken in a naive sense. What is meant is that, in an equivalence class of <a href="/wiki/Divisor_(algebraic_geometry)" title="Divisor (algebraic geometry)">divisors</a> of some specific kind, two representatives are intersected that are in <a href="/wiki/General_position" title="General position">general position</a> with respect to each other. In this way, self-intersection numbers can become well-defined, and even negative.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intersection_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The intersection number is partly motivated by the desire to define intersection to satisfy <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9zout%27s_theorem" title="Bézout's theorem">Bézout's theorem</a>.</p>
<p>The intersection number arises in the study of <a href="/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics)" title="Fixed point (mathematics)">fixed points</a>, which can be cleverly defined as intersections of function <a href="/wiki/Graph_of_a_function" title="Graph of a function">graphs</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Diagonal" title="Diagonal">diagonals</a>. Calculating the intersection numbers at the fixed points counts the fixed points <i>with multiplicity</i>, and leads to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lefschetz_fixed_point_theorem" title="Lefschetz fixed point theorem">Lefschetz fixed point theorem</a> in quantitative form.</p>
