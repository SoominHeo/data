<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Traffic congestion</h1>

<p><b>Traffic congestion</b> is a condition on transport networks that occurs as use increases, and is characterized by slower speeds, longer trip times, and increased vehicular <a href="/wiki/Queueing_theory" title="Queueing theory">queueing</a>. The most common example is the physical use of roads by vehicles. When traffic demand is great enough that the interaction between vehicles slows the speed of the traffic stream, this results in some congestion.</p>
<p>As demand approaches the capacity of a road (or of the intersections along the road), extreme traffic congestion sets in. When vehicles are fully stopped for periods of time, this is colloquially known as a <b>traffic jam</b> or <b>traffic snarl-up</b>. Traffic congestion can lead to drivers becoming frustrated and engaging in <a href="/wiki/Road_rage" title="Road rage">road rage</a>.</p>
<p>Mathematically, congestion is usually looked at as the number of vehicles that pass through a point in a window of time, or a flow. Congestion flow lends itself to principles of fluid dynamics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="922" data-file-width="1383" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg/220px-Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg/330px-Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg/440px-Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Traffic_jam_Marginal_Pinheiros_6122_SAO_07_2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Traffic congestion on Marginal Pinheiros, near downtown <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine, São Paulo has the world's worst traffic jams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_SP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times_SP-1">[1]</a></sup> Drivers are informed through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_message_sign" title="Variable message sign">variable message signs</a> the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Queueing_theory" title="Queueing theory">queue length</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Traffic congestion occurs when a volume of traffic or <a href="/wiki/Mode_choice" title="Mode choice">modal split</a> generates demand for space greater than the available street capacity; this point is commonly termed <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_saturation_(traffic)" title="Degree of saturation (traffic)">saturation</a>. There are a number of specific circumstances which cause or aggravate congestion; most of them reduce the capacity of a road at a given point or over a certain length, or increase the number of vehicles required for a given volume of people or goods. About half of U.S. traffic congestion is recurring, and is attributed to sheer weight of traffic; most of the rest is attributed to traffic incidents, road work and weather events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traffic research still cannot fully predict under which conditions a "traffic jam" (as opposed to heavy, but smoothly flowing traffic) may suddenly occur. It has been found that individual incidents (such as accidents or even a single car braking heavily in a previously smooth flow) may cause ripple effects (a <a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">cascading failure</a>) which then spread out and create a sustained traffic jam when, otherwise, normal flow might have continued for some time longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_theories">Mathematical theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mathematical theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG/220px-Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG/330px-Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG/440px-Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Motorcycles_in_Taipei.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Congestion on a street in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a> consisting primarily of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycles" title="Motorcycles">motorcycles</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some traffic engineers have attempted to apply the rules of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> to traffic flow, likening it to the flow of a fluid in a pipe. Congestion simulations and real-time observations have shown that in heavy but free flowing traffic, jams can arise spontaneously, triggered by minor events ("<a href="/wiki/Butterfly_effect" title="Butterfly effect">butterfly effects</a>"), such as an abrupt steering maneuver by a single motorist. Traffic scientists liken such a situation to the sudden freezing of <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled fluid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ball_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ball-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, unlike a fluid, traffic flow is often affected by signals or other events at junctions that periodically affect the smooth flow of traffic. Alternative mathematical theories exist, such as <a href="/wiki/Boris_Kerner" title="Boris Kerner">Boris Kerner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_traffic_theory" title="Three-phase traffic theory">three-phase traffic theory</a> (see also spatiotemporal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion:_Reconstruction_with_Kerner%E2%80%99s_three-phase_theory" title="Traffic congestion: Reconstruction with Kerner’s three-phase theory">reconstruction of traffic congestion</a>).</p>
<p>Because of the poor correlation of theoretical models to actual observed traffic flows, transportation planners and highway engineers attempt to <a href="/wiki/Traffic_flow" title="Traffic flow">forecast traffic flow</a> using empirical models. Their working traffic models typically use a combination of macro-, micro- and mesoscopic features, and may add matrix <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> effects, by "platooning" groups of vehicles and by randomising the flow patterns within individual segments of the network. These models are then typically calibrated by measuring actual traffic flows on the links in the network, and the baseline flows are adjusted accordingly.</p>
<p>A team of MIT mathematicians has developed a model that describes the formation of "phantom jams," in which small disturbances (a driver hitting the brake too hard, or getting too close to another car) in heavy traffic can become amplified into a full-blown, self-sustaining traffic jam. Key to the study is the realization that the mathematics of such jams, which the researchers call "jamitons," are strikingly similar to the equations that describe detonation waves produced by explosions, says Aslan Kasimov, lecturer in MIT's Department of Mathematics. That discovery enabled the team to solve traffic-jam equations that were first theorized in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A method based on percolation theory was developed by Daqing Li <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiFu2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiFu2015-6">[6]</a></sup> to characterize the global quality of traffic in a city. The method can be used to identify significant and recurrent bottle necks in different rush-hours.</p>

<p>Congested roads can be seen as an example of the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>. Because roads in most places are free at the point of usage, there is little financial incentive for drivers not to over-use them, up to the point where traffic collapses into a jam, when demand becomes limited by <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a>. <a href="/wiki/Private_highway" title="Private highway">Privatization of highways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">road pricing</a> have both been proposed as measures that may reduce congestion through economic incentives and disincentives. Congestion can also happen due to non-recurring highway incidents, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident">crash</a> or <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">roadworks</a>, which may reduce the road's capacity below normal levels.</p>
<p>Economist <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Downs" title="Anthony Downs">Anthony Downs</a> argues that <a href="/wiki/Rush_hour" title="Rush hour">rush hour</a> traffic congestion is inevitable because of the benefits of having a relatively <a href="/wiki/Business_hours" title="Business hours">standard work day</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In a <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> economy, goods can be allocated either by pricing (ability to pay) or by queueing (first-come first-served); congestion is an example of the latter. Instead of the traditional solution of making the "pipe" large enough to accommodate the total demand for peak-hour vehicle travel (a supply-side solution), either by widening roadways or increasing "flow pressure" via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_highway_system" title="Automated highway system">automated highway systems</a>, Downs advocates greater use of <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">road pricing</a> to reduce congestion (a demand-side solution, effectively rationing demand), in turn plowing the revenues generated therefrom into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> projects.</p>
<p>A 2011 study in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Economic_Review" title="The American Economic Review">The American Economic Review</a></i> indicates that there may be a "fundamental law of road congestion."The researchers, from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a>, analyzed data from the U.S. Highway Performance and Monitoring System for 1983, 1993 and 2003, as well as information on population, employment, geography, transit, and political factors. They determined that the number of vehicle-kilometers traveled (VKT) increases in direct proportion to the available lane-kilometers of roadways. The implication is that building new roads and widening existing ones only results in additional traffic that continues to rise until peak congestion returns to the previous level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Qualitative classification of traffic is often done in the form of a six letter A-F <a href="/wiki/Level_of_service" title="Level of service">level of service</a> (LOS) scale defined in the <a href="/wiki/Highway_Capacity_Manual" title="Highway Capacity Manual">Highway Capacity Manual</a>, a US document used (or used as a basis for national guidelines) worldwide. These levels are used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_engineering" title="Transport engineering">transportation engineers</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Shorthand" title="Shorthand">shorthand</a> and to describe traffic levels to the lay public. While this system generally uses delay as the basis for its measurements, the particular measurements and statistical methods vary depending on the facility being described. For instance, while the percent time spent following a slower-moving vehicle figures into the LOS for a rural two-lane road, the LOS at an urban intersection incorporates such measurements as the number of drivers forced to wait through more than one signal cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traffic congestion occurs in time and space, i.e., it is a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/spatiotemporal" title="wikt:spatiotemporal">spatiotemporal</a> process. Therefore, another classification schema of traffic congestion is associated with some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion:_Reconstruction_with_Kerner%E2%80%99s_three-phase_theory" title="Traffic congestion: Reconstruction with Kerner’s three-phase theory">common spatiotemporal features of traffic congestion</a> found in measured traffic data. Common spatiotemporal empirical features of traffic congestion are those features, which are qualitatively the same for different <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a> in different countries measured during years of traffic observations. Common features of traffic congestion are independent on <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, road conditions and road infrastructure, vehicular technology, driver characteristics, day time, etc. Examples of common features of traffic congestion are the features [J] and [S] for, respectively, the <i>wide moving jam</i> and <i>synchronized flow</i> traffic phases found in Kerner’s <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_traffic_theory" title="Three-phase traffic theory">three-phase traffic theory</a>. The common features of traffic congestion can be reconstructed in space and time with the use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion:_Reconstruction_with_Kerner%E2%80%99s_three-phase_theory" title="Traffic congestion: Reconstruction with Kerner’s three-phase theory">ASDA and FOTO</a> models.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_impacts">Negative impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Negative impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Traffic congestion has a number of negative effects:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wasting time of motorists and passengers ("<a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a>"). As a non-productive activity for most people, congestion reduces regional economic health.</li>
<li>Delays, which may result in late arrival for employment, meetings, and education, resulting in lost business, disciplinary action or other personal losses.</li>
<li>Inability to forecast travel time accurately, leading to drivers allocating more time to travel "just in case", and less time on productive activities.</li>
<li>Wasted fuel increasing <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">carbon dioxide emissions</a> owing to increased idling, acceleration and braking.</li>
<li>Wear and tear on vehicles as a result of idling in traffic and frequent acceleration and braking, leading to more frequent repairs and replacements.</li>
<li>Stressed and frustrated motorists, encouraging <a href="/wiki/Road_rage" title="Road rage">road rage</a> and reduced health of motorists</li>
<li>Emergencies: blocked traffic may interfere with the passage of emergency vehicles traveling to their destinations where they are urgently needed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spillover_effect" title="Spillover effect">Spillover effect</a> from congested main arteries to secondary roads and side streets as alternative routes are attempted ('<a href="/wiki/Rat_running" title="Rat running">rat running</a>'), which may affect neighborhood <a href="/wiki/Amenity" title="Amenity">amenity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> prices.</li>
<li>Higher chance of collisions due to tight spacing and constant stopping-and-going.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_rage">Road rage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Road rage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Road_rage" title="Road rage">Road rage</a> is aggressive or angry behavior by a driver of an automobile or other motor vehicle. Such behavior might include rude gestures, verbal insults, deliberately driving in an unsafe or threatening manner, or making threats. Road rage can lead to altercations, assaults, and collisions which result in injuries and even deaths. It can be thought of as an extreme case of <a href="/wiki/Aggressive_driving" title="Aggressive driving">aggressive driving</a>.</p>
<p>The term originated in the United States in 1987–1988 (specifically, from Newscasters at <a href="/wiki/KTLA" title="KTLA">KTLA</a>, a local television station), when a rash of freeway shootings occurred on the 405, 110 and 10 freeways in Los Angeles, California. These shooting sprees even spawned a response from the AAA Motor Club to its members on how to respond to drivers with road rage or aggressive maneuvers and gestures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positives_of_traffic_congestion">Positives of traffic congestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Positives of traffic congestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Congestion has the benefit of encouraging motorists to re-time their trips so that expensive road space is in full use for a greater number of hours per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The standard response to congestion is to expand road space somehow, perhaps by widening an existing road or else by adding a new road, bridge or tunnel. However, this could well result in increased traffic flow, otherwise known as <a href="/wiki/Induced_demand" title="Induced demand">induced demand</a>, causing congestion to appear somewhere else. Moreover, <a href="/wiki/Braess%27_paradox" title="Braess' paradox">Braess' paradox</a> shows that adding road capacity might make congestion worse even if demand does not increase.</p>
<p>It has been argued that traffic congestion, by reducing road speeds in cities, could reduce the frequency and severity of road accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has been suggested by some commentators that the level of congestion that society tolerates is a rational (though not necessarily conscious)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> choice between the costs of improving the transportation system (in infrastructure or management) and the benefits of quicker travel. Others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2008)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> link it largely to subjective lifestyle choices, differentiating between car-owning and car-free households.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_planning_and_design">Urban planning and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Urban planning and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">City planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a> practices can have a huge impact on levels of future traffic congestion, though they are of limited relevance for short-term change.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">Grid plans</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fused_Grid" title="Fused Grid">fused grid</a> road network geometry, rather than tree-like <a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">network topology</a> which branches into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cul-de-sac" title="Cul-de-sac">cul-de-sacs</a> (which reduce local traffic, but increase total distances driven and discourage walking by reducing connectivity). This avoids concentration of traffic on a small number of <a href="/wiki/Arterial_road" title="Arterial road">arterial roads</a> and allows more trips to be made without a car.</li>
<li>Zoning laws that encourage <a href="/wiki/Mixed-use_development" title="Mixed-use development">mixed-use development</a>, which reduces distances between residential, commercial, retail, and recreational destinations (and encourage cycling and walking)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carfree_city" title="Carfree city">Carfree cities</a>, car-light cities, and eco-cities designed to eliminate the need to travel by car for most inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">Transit-oriented development</a> are residential and commercial areas designed to maximize access to public transport by providing a transit station or stop (<a href="/wiki/Train_station" title="Train station">train station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metro_station" title="Metro station">metro station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tram_stop" title="Tram stop">tram stop</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bus_stop" title="Bus stop">bus stop</a>).</li>
</ul>

<p>Congestion can be reduced by either increasing road capacity (supply), or by reducing traffic (demand). Capacity can be increased in a number of ways, but needs to take account of <a href="/wiki/Induced_demand" title="Induced demand">latent demand</a> otherwise it may be used more strongly than anticipated. Critics of the approach of adding capacity have compared it to "fighting <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> by letting out your belt" (inducing demand that did not exist before). For example, when new lanes are created, households with a second car that used to be parked most of the time may begin to use this second car for commuting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Reducing road capacity has in turn been attacked as removing free choice as well as increasing travel costs and times, placing an especially high burden on the low income residents who must commute to work.</p>
<p>Increased supply can include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adding more capacity at bottlenecks (such as by adding more lanes at the expense of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_shoulder" title="Hard shoulder">hard shoulders</a> or safety zones, or by removing local obstacles like bridge supports and widening tunnels)</li>
<li>Adding more capacity over the whole of a route (generally by adding more lanes)</li>
<li>Creating new routes</li>
<li>Traffic management improvements (see separate section below)</li>
</ul>
<p>Reduction of demand can include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">Parking restrictions</a>, making motor vehicle use less attractive by increasing the monetary and non-monetary costs of parking, introducing greater competition for limited city or road space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Most transport planning experts agree that free parking distorts the market in favour of car travel, exacerbating congestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Park_and_ride" title="Park and ride">Park and ride</a> facilities allowing parking at a distance and allowing continuation by <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_pool" title="Car pool">ride sharing</a>. Park-and-ride <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_park" title="Car park">car parks</a> are commonly found at <a href="/wiki/Metro_station" title="Metro station">metro stations</a>, freeway entrances in suburban areas, and at the edge of smaller cities.</li>
<li>Reduction of road capacity to force traffic onto other <a href="/wiki/Modal_share" title="Modal share">travel modes</a>. Methods include <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Shared_space" title="Shared space">shared space</a> concept.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">Road pricing</a>, charging money for access onto a road/specific area at certain times, congestion levels or for certain road users
<ul>
<li>"Cap and trade", in which only licensed cars are allowed on the roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A limited quota of car licences are issued each year and traded in a <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> fashion. This guarantees that the number of cars does not exceed road capacity while avoiding the negative effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_shortage" title="Economic shortage">shortages</a> normally associated with quotas. However, since demand for cars tends to be inelastic, the result are exorbitant purchase prices for the licenses, pricing out the lower levels of society, as seen Singapore's <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Entitlement" title="Certificate of Entitlement">Certificate of Entitlement</a> scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toh_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-toh-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Congestion_pricing" title="Congestion pricing">Congestion pricing</a>, where a certain area, such as the inner part of a congested city, is surrounded with a <a class="extiw" href="http://www.dict.org/bin/Dict?Database=*&amp;Form=Dict1&amp;Strategy=*&amp;Query=cordon" title="dict:cordon">cordon</a> into which entry with a car requires payment. The cordon may be a physical boundary (i.e., surrounded by toll stations) or it may be virtual, with enforcement being via spot checks or cameras on the entry routes. Major examples are <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Electronic_road_pricing" title="Electronic road pricing">electronic road pricing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_congestion_charge" title="London congestion charge">London congestion charge</a> system, <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_congestion_tax" title="Stockholm congestion tax">Stockholm congestion tax</a> and the use of <a href="/wiki/High-occupancy_toll_lane" title="High-occupancy toll lane">High-occupancy toll lanes</a>, predominately in North America.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Road_space_rationing" title="Road space rationing">Road space rationing</a>, where regulatory restrictions prevent certain types of vehicles from driving under certain circumstances or in certain areas.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_plate" title="Vehicle registration plate">Number plate</a> restrictions based on days of the week, as practiced in several large cities in the world, such as <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> and São Paulo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-konsult_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-konsult-23">[23]</a></sup> In effect, such cities are banning a different part of the automobile fleet from roads each day of the week. Mainly introduced to combat <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>, these measures also reduce congestion. A weakness of this method is that richer drivers can purchase a second or third car to circumvent the ban.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/License" title="License">Permits</a>, where only certain types of vehicles (such as residents) are permitted to enter a certain area, and other types (such as through-traffic) are banned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-konsult_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-konsult-23">[23]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Delano%C3%AB" title="Bertrand Delanoë">Bertrand Delanoë</a>, the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, has proposed to impose a complete ban on motor vehicles in the city's inner districts, with exemptions only for residents, businesses, and the disabled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3048" data-file-width="4573" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg/220px-TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg/330px-TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg/440px-TrafficJamBikeLane.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Bike lane constructed in congested areas to encourage use of the alternative transportation.</div>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">Policy</a> approaches, which usually attempt to provide either strategic alternatives or which encourage greater usage of existing alternatives through promotion, subsidies or restrictions.
<ul>
<li>Incentives to use <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>, increasing modal shares. This can be achieved through infrastructure investment, subsidies, transport <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_passenger_transport" title="Intermodal passenger transport">integration</a>, pricing strategies that decrease the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">marginal cost</a>/<a href="/wiki/Fixed_cost" title="Fixed cost">fixed cost</a> ratios,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simon_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-simon-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-link-26">[26]</a></sup> improved <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_timetable" title="Public transport timetable">timetabling</a> and greater priority for buses to reduce journey time e.g. [Bus Lanes], [BTR] .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Utility_cycling" title="Utility cycling">Cycling</a> promotion through legislation, cycle facilities, subsidies, and awareness campaigns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cycling_in_the_Netherlands" title="Cycling in the Netherlands">The Netherlands has been pursuing cycle friendly policies</a> for decades, and around a quarter of their commuting is done by bicycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Promotion of more flexible work place practices. For example, a flexible workplaces pilot was undertaken in Brisbane, Australia during 2009 to test the applicability of a voluntary travel behaviour change program to achieve transport system outcomes, particularly as they related to managing congestion, either through mode shift or peak spreading. During the one-month Pilot, amongst almost 900 Brisbane CBD workers across 20 private and public sector organisations, shifts of more than 30% out of the morning and afternoon peak travel was recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Telecommuting" title="Telecommuting">Telecommuting</a> encouraged through legislation and subsidies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Online_shopping" title="Online shopping">Online shopping</a> promotion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rac2050_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-rac2050-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dft_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-dft-35">[35]</a></sup> potentially with <a href="/wiki/Packstation" title="Packstation">automated delivery booths</a> helping to solve the <a href="/wiki/Last_mile_(transportation)" title="Last mile (transportation)">last mile problem</a> and reduce shopping trips made by car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_associated">Other associated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other associated">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/School" title="School">School</a> opening times arranged to avoid <a href="/wiki/Rush_hour" title="Rush hour">rush hour</a> traffic (in some countries, private car school pickup and drop-off traffic are substantial percentages of peak hour traffic).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Road_rage" title="Road rage">Considerate driving</a> behaviour promotion and enforcement. Driving practices such as <a href="/wiki/Tailgating" title="Tailgating">tailgating</a> and frequent lane changes can reduce a road's capacity and exacerbate jams. In some countries signs are placed on highways to raise awareness, while others have introduced legislation against inconsiderate driving.</li>
<li>Visual barriers to prevent drivers from slowing down out of curiosity (often called "rubbernecking" in the United States). This often includes accidents, with traffic slowing down even on roadsides physically separated from the crash location. This also tends to occur at construction sites, which is why some countries have introduced rules that motorway construction has to occur behind visual barrier</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Speed_limit" title="Speed limit">Speed limit</a> reductions, as practiced on the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">M25 motorway</a> in London. With lower speeds allowing cars to drive closer together, this increases the capacity of a road. Note that this measure is only effective if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interval_(time)" title="Interval (time)">interval</a> between cars is reduced, not the distance itself. Low intervals are generally only safe at low speeds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lane_splitting" title="Lane splitting">Lane splitting/filtering</a>, in which some jurisdictions allow <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scooter_(motorcycle)" title="Scooter (motorcycle)">scooters</a> and bicycles to travel in the space between cars, buses, and trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHSTA_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHSTA-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_country">By country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: By country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traffic during peak hours in major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian">Australian</a> cities, such as Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane and Perth, is usually very congested and can cause considerable delay for motorists. Australians rely mainly on radio and television to obtain current traffic information. GPS, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webcams" title="Webcams">webcams</a>, and online resources are increasingly being used to monitor and relay traffic conditions to motorists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traffic jams have become intolerable in Dhaka. Some other major reasons are the total absence of a <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">rapid transit</a> system; the lack of an integrated urban planning scheme for over 30 years;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> poorly maintained road surfaces, with potholes rapidly eroded further by frequent flooding and poor or non-existent drainage;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> haphazard stopping and parking;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> poor driving standards;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> total lack of alternative routes, with several narrow and (nominally) one-way roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Daily_Star_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Daily_Star-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> has the world's worst daily traffic jams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_SP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Times_SP-1">[1]</a></sup> Based on reports from the <i>Companhia de Engenharia de Tráfego</i>, the city's traffic management agency, the historical congestion record was set on May 23, 2014, with 344 kilometres (214 mi) of cumulative queues around the city during the evening rush hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BRArecord2014_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BRArecord2014-45">[45]</a></sup> The previous record occurred on November 14, 2013, with 309 kilometres (192 mi) of cumulative queues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BRArecord2014_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-BRArecord2014-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite implementation since 1997 of <a href="/wiki/Road_space_rationing" title="Road space rationing">road space rationing</a> by the last digit of the plate number during rush hours every weekday, traffic in this 20-million-strong city still experiences severe congestion. According to experts, this is due to the accelerated rate of motorization occurring since 2003 and the limited capacity of <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>. In São Paulo, traffic is growing at a rate of 7.5% per year, with almost 1,000 new cars bought in the city every day. The subway has only 61 kilometres (38 mi) of lines, though 35 further kilometers are under construction or planned by 2010. Every day, many citizens spend between three up to four hours behind the wheel. In order to mitigate the aggravating congestion problem, since June 30, 2008 the road space rationing program was expanded to include and restrict trucks and light commercial vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The August 2010 <a href="/wiki/China_National_Highway_110_traffic_jam" title="China National Highway 110 traffic jam">China National Highway 110 traffic jam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a> province, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>, is considered the world's worst traffic jam ever, as traffic congestion stretched more than 100 kilometres (62 mi) from August 14 to the 26, including at least 11 days of total <a href="/wiki/Gridlock" title="Gridlock">gridlock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0810_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0810-53">[53]</a></sup> The event was caused by a combination of road works and thousands of coal trucks from <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>’s coalfields that travel daily to Beijing. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> has called this event the "Great Chinese Gridlock of 2010."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0810_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0810-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Towards the end of 2010, Beijing announced a series of drastic measures to tackle the city's traffic jam, including limiting the number of new plates issued to passenger cars to 20,000 a month and barring cars of non-Beijing plates from entering areas within the Fifth Ring Road during rush hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The number of vehicles in India is quickly increasing as a growing middle class can now afford to buy cars. As a result, India has launched various rapid transit efforts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Kolkata_Metro" title="Kolkata Metro">Kolkata Metro</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, and the Rapid Metro, in Gurgaon.</p>
<p>India's road conditions are not expanding / improving in proportion with the increase in vehicle numbers. For example, lets take the case of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, a small southern state having the above characteristics. The state has less than average (around 30m)width of major highways in the nation (45 m), though the roads are better surfaced and maintained in Kerala. This causes considerable difficulty to the road users and causes frequent mishaps, though road discipline and adherence to traffic rules (and enforcement) is considered as better in Kerala.</p>
<p>Various causes for this include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Private encroachments</li>
<li>Commercial / religious establishment's location hampering road expansion, and rampant lobbying for this.</li>
<li>Unscientific road design</li>
<li>Lack of free ways / exit ways where local roads and main roads intersect</li>
<li>Lack of demarcated foot paths</li>
<li>Lack of bus bays</li>
<li>Lack of cycle tracks</li>
<li>Lack of coordination among various govt. departments (e.g. digging of roads by telecom/ water dept. and leaving it open)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2015 study by motor oil company <a href="/wiki/Castrol" title="Castrol">Castrol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> is found to be the worst city in the world for traffic congestion. Relying on information from <a href="/wiki/TomTom" title="TomTom">TomTom</a> navigation devices in 78 countries, the index found that drivers are stopping and starting their cars 33,240 times per year on the road. After Jakarta, the worst cities for traffic are <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daily congestion in Jakarta is not a recent problem. The expansion of commercial area shows worsening daily congestion in Jalan Jendral Sudirman, Jalan Thamrin, and Jalan Gajah Mada in mid 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, 12 people died as a result of traffic congestion in Java. They were among those stuck in a three-day traffic jam at an intersection in Java called 'Brexit'. The traffic block stretched for 21 km here and thousands of cars clogged the highway. Many people died because of carbon monoxide poisoning, fatigue or heat. This is part of a total of 400 people who died during the <a href="/wiki/Lebaran" title="Lebaran">holiday season</a> because of traffic jams in different parts of Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Zealand has followed strongly car-oriented transport policies since after World War II (especially in <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, where one third of the country's population lives, is New Zealand's most traffic congested city, and has been labelled worse than New York for traffic congestion with commuters sitting in traffic congestion for 95 hours per year),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIFF_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRIFF-59">[59]</a></sup> and currently has one of the highest car-ownership rates per capita in the world, after the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Traffic congestion in New Zealand is increasing with drivers on New Zealand's motorways reported to be struggling to exceed 20 kph on an average commute, sometimes crawling along at 8 kph for more than half an hour.</p>

<p>According to a survey by <a href="/wiki/Waze" title="Waze">Waze</a>, traffic congestion in <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Metro Manila</a> is called the "worst" in the world, after <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> It is worsened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_violation" title="Traffic violation">violations of traffic laws</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Parking_violation" title="Parking violation">illegal parking</a>, loading and unloading, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_light_running" title="Red light running">beating the red light</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wrong-way_driving" title="Wrong-way driving">wrong-way driving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Traffic congestion in <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Metro Manila</a> is caused by the large number of registered vehicles, lack of roads, and overpopulation, especially on <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pateros,_Metro_Manila" title="Pateros, Metro Manila">Pateros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caloocan" title="Caloocan">Caloocan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faqph_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-faqph-63">[63]</a></sup> Traffic caused losses of ₱137,500,000,000 on the economy in 2011, and unbuilt roads and railway projects also causes worsening congestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA) feared that daily economic losses will reach Php 6,000,000,000 by 2030 if traffic congestion cannot be controlled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Traffic_congestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Istanbul_Metropolitan_Municipality" title="Istanbul Metropolitan Municipality">Istanbul Metropolitan Municipality</a> has made huge investments on <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_transportation_system" title="Intelligent transportation system">intelligent transportation systems</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a>. Despite that, traffic is a significant problem in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%B0stanbul" title="İstanbul">İstanbul</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%B0stanbul" title="İstanbul">İstanbul</a> has chosen the second most congested<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and the most sudden-stopping traffic in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Travel times in Turkey’s largest city take on average 55 percent longer that they should, even in relatively less busy hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the United Kingdom the inevitability of congestion in some urban road networks has been officially recognized since the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department for Transport</a> set down policies based on the report <i><a href="/wiki/Traffic_in_Towns" title="Traffic in Towns">Traffic in Towns</a></i> in 1963:</p>

<p>The Department for Transport sees growing congestion as one of the most serious transport problems facing the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> On 1 December 2006, <a href="/wiki/Rod_Eddington" title="Rod Eddington">Rod Eddington</a> published a UK government-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Eddington_Transport_Study" title="Eddington Transport Study">report into the future of Britain's transport infrastructure</a>. The Eddington Transport Study set out the case for action to improve road and rail networks, as a "crucial enabler of sustained productivity and competitiveness". Eddington has estimated that congestion may cost the economy of England £22 bn a year in lost time by 2025. He warned that roads were in serious danger of becoming so congested that the economy would suffer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> At the launch of the report Eddington told journalists and transport industry representatives introducing <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">road pricing</a> to encourage drivers to drive less was an "economic no-brainer". There was, he said "no attractive alternative". It would allegedly cut congestion by half by 2025, and bring benefits to the British economy totalling £28 bn a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>

The average truck speed was just 29 mph (47 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
