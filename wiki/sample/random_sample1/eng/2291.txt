<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Symphony No. 26 (Haydn)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the symphony by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>. For the symphony by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Haydn" title="Michael Haydn">Michael Haydn</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._26_(Michael_Haydn)" title="Symphony No. 26 (Michael Haydn)">Symphony No. 26 (Michael Haydn)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>Symphony No. 26</b> in <a href="/wiki/D_minor" title="D minor">D minor</a>, Hoboken 1/26, is one of the early <a href="/wiki/Sturm_und_Drang" title="Sturm und Drang">Sturm und Drang</a> Symphonies written by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>. It is popularly known as the <b>Lamentatione</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._26_(Haydn)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haydn wrote the symphony for <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter week</a>. <a href="/wiki/H._C._Robbins_Landon" title="H. C. Robbins Landon">H. C. Robbins Landon</a> has dated it to 1768 (possibly 1769).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the absence of the original autograph,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> it is impossible to verify the date of composition although this dating is consistent with the work's appearance in the Entwurf Katalog (Haydn's own catalogue of his works). An earlier tradition suggested the symphony had been written for Christmas (at the time a much less significant religious holiday), but the oldest original extant manuscript indicates clearly that the symphony was indeed intended for Easter celebrations.</p>
<p>It is an early example of the Sturm und Drang style that characterised much of his symphonic output to 1774 or 1775.</p>
<p>Because of its association with Easter week, Haydn incorporates a melody derived from an old plainsong chant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_of_Christ" title="Passion of Christ">Passion of Christ</a>, interpolating (as the second theme) this familiar liturgical setting to contrast with the furious opening theme. The same lament is also picked up in the second movement, reinforcing the symphony's link to the Passion through evocation of a melody that would have been familiar to audiences of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickname_.28Lamentatione.29">Nickname (Lamentatione)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._26_(Haydn)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nickname (Lamentatione)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since Haydn's day, the symphony has been known as "Lamentatione" because of the Christus motif of the opening movement's second theme. As with all the nicknamed symphonies, the title is not Haydn's own.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Movements">Movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._26_(Haydn)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work is in three movements, ending with a minuet and trio. It is scored for two <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horn_(instrument)" title="Horn (instrument)">horns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Figured_bass" title="Figured bass">continuo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>) and a <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a> containing first and second violins, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">violas</a>, cellos and <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double basses</a>. Although no independent part for the double bass exists, it would have followed the cello part in octaves.</p>
