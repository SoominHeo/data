<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>A London Symphony</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/London_Symphony_(disambiguation)" title="London Symphony (disambiguation)">London Symphony (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><i><b>A London Symphony</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a> composed by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a>. The work is sometimes referred to as the Symphony No. 2, though it was not designated as such by the composer. First performed in 1914, the four-movement symphony was lost, reconstructed and later modified by Vaughan Williams.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_London_Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work is scored for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Woodwinds: three <a href="/wiki/Western_concert_flute" title="Western concert flute">flutes</a> (the third doubling <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>), two <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cor_anglais" title="Cor anglais">cor anglais</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_clarinet" title="Bass clarinet">bass clarinet</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contrabassoon" title="Contrabassoon">contrabassoon</a></li>
<li>Brass: four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horn_in_F" title="Horn in F">horns</a>, two trumpets, two <a href="/wiki/Cornet" title="Cornet">cornets</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a></li>
<li>Percussion: <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle_(musical_instrument)" title="Triangle (musical instrument)">triangle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tam-tam" title="Tam-tam">tam-tam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sleigh_bells" title="Sleigh bells">sleigh bells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">cymbals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glockenspiel" title="Glockenspiel">glockenspiel</a></li>
<li>Strings: <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">strings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_London_Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaughan Williams said that while the title may suggest a <a href="/wiki/Program_music" title="Program music">programmatic piece</a> (and the work includes sounds heard in London such as the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Quarters" title="Westminster Quarters">Westminster Quarters</a>), it was intended to be heard as <a href="/wiki/Absolute_music" title="Absolute music">absolute music</a>. In a programme note in 1920, he suggested that <i>Symphony by a Londoner</i> might be a better title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, he allowed the conductor <a href="/wiki/Albert_Coates_(musician)" title="Albert Coates (musician)">Albert Coates</a> to provide elaborate descriptions for the 1920 performance.</p>
<p>The symphony is in four movements.</p>
<dl>
<dt>1. <b>Lento – Allegro risoluto</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>The symphony opens quietly, and after a few nocturnal bars, the Westminster chimes are heard, played on the harp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><br/>

<p>After a contrasting gentle interlude scored for string sextet and harp, the vigorous themes return and bring the movement to a lively close, with full orchestra playing <i>fortissimo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>2. <b>Lento</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>The movement opens with muted strings playing <i>ppp</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup> Vaughan Williams said that the slow movement is intended to evoke "<a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury_Square" title="Bloomsbury Square">Bloomsbury Square</a> on a November afternoon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">[4]</a></sup><br/>
<img alt=" \relative c' { \clef treble \numericTimeSignature \time 4/4 \tempo &quot;Lento&quot; \partial 4*1 ees4\p( | aes4._&quot;misterioso&quot; bes8 ces4 bes8 aes | g4. f8 g4 c, | g4. c8 c4-- bes-- | g2.)\&gt;~ g8\! } " height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/score/q/2/q28twj2wxhsvwftjpkwtqruwy71w3cu/q28twj2w.png" width="506"/></p>
<div class="mediaContainer" style="width:300px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-disablecontrols="options,timedText" data-durationhint="11.900453514739" id="mwe_player_3" preload="none" style="width:300px">
<source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/score/q/2/q28twj2wxhsvwftjpkwtqruwy71w3cu/q28twj2w.ogg" type='audio/ogg; codecs="vorbis"'></source></audio></div>
<p>Quiet themes led in turn by <a href="/wiki/Cor_anglais" title="Cor anglais">cor anglais</a>, flute, trumpet and viola give way to a grave, impassioned forte section, after which the movement gradually subsides to its original quiet dynamic.</p>
<dl>
<dt>3. <b>Scherzo (Nocturne)</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>In the composer's words, "If the listener will imagine himself standing on <a href="/wiki/Westminster" title="Westminster">Westminster</a> <a href="/wiki/Thames_Embankment" title="Thames Embankment">Embankment</a> at night, surrounded by the distant sounds of <a href="/wiki/Strand,_London" title="Strand, London">The Strand</a>, with its great hotels on one side and the "<a href="/wiki/The_Cut,_London" title="The Cut, London">New Cut</a>" on the other, with its crowded streets and flaring lights, it may serve as a mood in which to listen to this movement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">[4]</a></sup> In the definitive score, the movement revolves around two <a href="/wiki/Scherzo" title="Scherzo">scherzo</a> themes, the first marked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fugato" title="Fugato">fugato</a> and the second straightforward and lively.<br/>

<div class="mediaContainer" style="width:300px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-disablecontrols="options,timedText" data-durationhint="7.4004535147392" id="mwe_player_4" preload="none" style="width:300px">

<p>The piece closes with muted strings playing <i>pppp</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>4. <b>Finale – Andante con moto – Maestoso alla marcia – Allegro – Lento – Epilogue</b></dt>
</dl>
<p><img alt=" \relative c' { \clef treble \numericTimeSignature \time 4/4 \key g \minor \tempo &quot;Maestoso alla marcia&quot; c\p c8.(-. c16-.) c4 a8-.( c-.) | d4 d8.-.( c16-.) d2-- | c4 c8.(-. bes16-.) c8( d) bes-- c-- | d4( f8. e16 d2)^&quot;ten.&quot; } " height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/score/2/j/2jeaxcashnlve42f1jl3drut4p1qsoz/2jeaxcas.png" width="715"/></p>
<div class="mediaContainer" style="width:300px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-disablecontrols="options,timedText" data-durationhint="11.600453514739" id="mwe_player_5" preload="none" style="width:300px">

<p>The finale opens on a grave march theme, punctuated with a lighter <a href="/wiki/Tempo#Italian_tempo_markings" title="Tempo">allegro</a> section, with full orchestra initially <i>forte</i> and <i>appassionato</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup> After the reappearance of the march, the main allegro theme of the first movement returns. Following this, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westminster_Chimes" title="Westminster Chimes">Westminster Chimes</a> strike again, this time the harp plays the first three-quarters of the hour chimes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup> and there is a quiet Epilogue, inspired by the last chapter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells">H.G. Wells</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Tono-Bungay" title="Tono-Bungay">Tono-Bungay</a></i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The last great movement in the London Symphony in which the true scheme of the old order is altogether dwarfed and swallowed up ... Light after light goes down. England and the Kingdom, Britain and the Empire, the old prides and the old devotions, glide abeam, astern, sink down upon the horizon, pass – pass. The river passes – London passes, England passes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_versions">History and versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_London_Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History and versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The symphony was composed from 1912 to 1913. It is dedicated to Vaughan Williams's friend and fellow composer <a href="/wiki/George_Butterworth" title="George Butterworth">George Butterworth</a> (1885–1916) who was subsequently killed by a <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">sniper</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Somme_(department)" title="Somme (department)">Somme</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borowski_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-borowski-6">[6]</a></sup> It was Butterworth who had first encouraged Vaughan Williams to write a purely orchestral symphony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-7">[7]</a></sup> Vaughan Williams recorded that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>We were talking together one day when he said in his gruff, abrupt manner: 'You know, you ought to write a symphony'. I answered... that I'd never written a symphony and never intended to... I suppose Butterworth's words stung me and, anyhow, I looked out some sketches I had made for... a symphonic poem about London and decided to throw it into symphonic form... From that moment, the idea of a symphony dominated my mind. I showed the sketches to George bit by bit as they were finished, and it was then that I realised that he possessed in common with very few composers a wonderful power of criticism of other men's work and insight into their ideas and motives. I can never feel too grateful to him for all he did for me over this work and his help did not stop short at criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The work was first performed on 27 March 1914 at <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Hall" title="Queen's Hall">Queen's Hall</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borowski_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-borowski-6">[6]</a></sup> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Toye" title="Geoffrey Toye">Geoffrey Toye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masterworks_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-masterworks-1">[1]</a></sup> The performance was a success, but shortly afterwards the composer sent the score to the conductor <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Busch" title="Fritz Busch">Fritz Busch</a> in Germany, and it disappeared in the upheaval of the outbreak of World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The second performance was given in Harrogate on 12 August by the Harrogate Municipal Orchestra under Julian Clifford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There was a short score,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which had been prepared by Bevis Ellis, Francis Toye and George Butterworth, so it is possible that version was used instead. The composer, aided by Geoffrey Toye, Butterworth and the critic <a href="/wiki/Edward_Joseph_Dent" title="Edward Joseph Dent">E. J. Dent</a>, reconstructed the score from the orchestral parts, and the reconstruction was performed on 11 February 1915 by the Bournemouth Municipal Orchestra under <a href="/wiki/Dan_Godfrey" title="Dan Godfrey">Dan Godfrey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symphony went through several revisions before reaching its final form. Vaughan Williams revised it for a performance in March 1918, and again in 1919–20. This second revision became the first published version and was recorded for the gramophone in 1925 by the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> conducted by Sir <a href="/wiki/Dan_Godfrey" title="Dan Godfrey">Dan Godfrey</a>. It was also recorded in 1941 by the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra">Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Aynsley_Goossens" title="Eugene Aynsley Goossens">Sir Eugene Goossens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup> It had already received its American premiere on 20 December 1920 when the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Symphony_Orchestra" title="New York Symphony Orchestra">New York Symphony Orchestra</a> played it under the baton of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Coates_(musician)" title="Albert Coates (musician)">Albert Coates</a>. It is most unlikely that OUP sanctioned the use of the 1920 version in Goossens' recording, since Vaughan Williams had withdrawn it and all scores of the 1933 revision carry the statement "This revised edition supersedes the Original Version which should no longer be used". However, this recording was made in the darkest days of World War II, when communications between Britain and the USA were difficult, and a "rogue" set was used. In fact, the 1920 version was already in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> in the USA, having been published before 1923, so it may simply have been cheaper to record that version. It was still possible to buy scores and parts of the 1920 version in the USA until early in the 21st century, although this is no longer the case.</p>
<p>While he was working on his <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._4_(Vaughan_Williams)" title="Symphony No. 4 (Vaughan Williams)">fourth symphony</a> in 1933, Vaughan Williams made time to revise <i>A London Symphony</i> yet again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup> He regarded this version, which was published in 1936,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-7">[7]</a></sup> as the definitive one, and it is this version that entered the repertoire, being played in concert and on record by many conductors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup> However, in 2001, when the composer's widow, <a href="/wiki/Ursula_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ursula Vaughan Williams">Ursula</a>, gave permission for a recording of the original 1914 score, there was a widely expressed view among music critics that the composer had cut many bars of interesting music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> One writer commented: "The 1913 score is more meditative, dark-shaded and tragic in tone, almost <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahleresque</a> in its inclusiveness. By 1933, Vaughan Williams's concept of symphonic architecture was becoming more aligned with a <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Sibelian</a> logic and severity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main differences between the first and last versions may be summarised as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>First movement</b>: One bar was cut from the 1914 version.</li>
<li><b>Slow movement</b>: 52 bars of the 1914 score were cut in 1933/36, chiefly from the quiet coda.</li>
<li><b>Scherzo</b>: At the end of the original is a dark andantino passage, of which no trace survives in the definitive version.</li>
<li><b>Finale</b>: In the 1914 score, the central E minor section, familiar in the definitive text, is interrupted by an orchestral "cry of anguish" based on the opening theme, after which the allegro resumes. After the conclusion of the allegro section, the 1914 score has a long andantino section for strings and woodwinds later dismissed by Vaughan Williams as "a bad hymn tune". Finally, the original Epilogue extends to 109 bars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiedman_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tiedman-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
