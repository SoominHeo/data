<p>The <b>Symphony No. 6</b> in <a href="/wiki/D_major" title="D major">D major</a> (Hoboken 1/6) is an early symphony written in 1761 by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a> and the first written after Haydn had joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Esterh%C3%A1zy" title="House of Esterházy">Esterházy</a> court. It is the first of three that are characterised by unusual virtuoso writing across the orchestral ensemble. It is popularly known as <i>Le matin</i> (Morning). </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_scoring">Background and scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._6_(Haydn)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haydn wrote this, his first symphonic work for his new employer Prince Nikolaus Eszterházy, in the spring of 1761, shortly after joining the court. The Eszterházys maintained in permanent residence an excellent chamber orchestra and with his first contribution for it in the symphonic genre, Haydn fully exploited the talents of the players. In this, Haydn was consciously drawing on the familiar tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Concerto_grosso" title="Concerto grosso">concerto grosso</a>, exemplified by the works of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Antonio Vivaldi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Tartini" title="Giuseppe Tartini">Giuseppe Tartini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tomaso_Albinoni" title="Tomaso Albinoni">Tomaso Albinoni</a> then much in vogue at courts across Europe. All three symphonies (Nos. 6, 7 and 8) feature extensive solo passages for the wind, horn and strings, including rare solo writing for the <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a> in the third movement of No. 6. The work is scored for <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>, 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oboes" title="Oboes">oboes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Natural_horn" title="Natural horn">horns</a> in D, <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> I, violin II, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> <i>ad libitum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landon_Score_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landon_Score-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been commonly suggested that Haydn's motivation was to curry favour both with his new employer (by making reference to a familiar and popular tradition) and, perhaps more importantly, with the players upon whose goodwill he depended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Typically during this period, players who performed challenging solo passages or displayed unusual virtuosity received financial reward. By highlighting virtually all of the players in this regard, Haydn was, literally, spreading the wealth.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickname_.28Le_matin.29">Nickname (<i>Le matin</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._6_(Haydn)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nickname (Le matin)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nickname (not Haydn's own, but quickly adopted) derives from the opening slow introduction of the opening movement, which clearly depicts sunrise. The remainder of the work is abstract, as, indeed, are the other two symphonies in the series. Because of the initial association, however, the remaining were quickly and complementarily named "noon" and "evening".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Following the sunrise-depicting introduction, the <i>Allegro</i> kicks off immediately with solo passages for flute and oboe. At the end of the development, a solo horn states the opening flute theme in the tonic, reminding modern listeners of the premature entry of the horn at the end of the development of the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Beethoven)">Eroica Symphony</a> composed forty years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apbrown7172_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-apbrown7172-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The slow movement is an Andante featuring passages for solo violin and solo cello bracketed by an Adagio on both ends. The leading Adagio is based on a <a href="/wiki/Hexachord" title="Hexachord">hexachord</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apbrown7172_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-apbrown7172-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The minuet also contains concertante passages. A solo flute is heard accompanied by violins as well as a section with wind-band scoring. The trio begins with solo passages by the <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a> and bassoon and are later joined by solo <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>. The finale has a concerto-grosso feel with virtuosi passages for cello, violin and flute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apbrown7172_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-apbrown7172-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
