<p><b>Pope Cornelius</b> (died June 253) was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a> from 6 or 13 March 251 to his martyrdom in 253.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_persecution">Christian persecution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Cornelius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Christian persecution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Decius" title="Emperor Decius">Emperor Decius</a>, who ruled from 249 to 251 AD, persecuted Christians in the Roman Empire rather sporadically and locally, but starting January in the year 250, he ordered all citizens to perform a religious sacrifice in the presence of commissioners, or else face death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Many Christians refused and were martyred (possibly including the pope, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Fabian" title="St Fabian">St Fabian</a>, on 20 January), while others partook in the sacrifices in order to save their own lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feast_Days_1991_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feast_Days_1991-4">[4]</a></sup> Two schools of thought arose after the persecution. One side, led by <a href="/wiki/Novatian" title="Novatian">Novatian</a>, who was a priest in the diocese of Rome, believed that those who had stopped practising Christianity during the persecution could not be accepted back into the church even if they repented. Under <a href="/wiki/Novatianism" title="Novatianism">this philosophy</a>, the only way to re-enter the church would be re-baptism. The opposing side, including Cornelius and <a href="/wiki/Cyprian" title="Cyprian">Cyprian</a> the Bishop of Carthage, did not believe in the need for re-baptism. Instead they thought that the sinners should only need to show contrition and true repentance to be welcomed back into the church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-5">[5]</a></sup> In hopes that Christianity would fade away, Decius prevented the election of a new pope. However, soon afterwards Decius was forced to leave the area to fight the invading <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> and while he was away the elections for pope were held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feast_Days_1991_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feast_Days_1991-4">[4]</a></sup> In the 14 months without a pope, the leading candidate, Moses, had died under the persecution. Novatian believed that he would be elected, however Cornelius was unwillingly elected the twenty-first <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> in March 251.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papacy">Papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Cornelius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1968" data-file-width="840" height="398" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg/170px-Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg/255px-Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg/340px-Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heiliger_Cornelius.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pope Cornelius by <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Me%C3%9Fkirch" title="Master of Meßkirch">Master of Meßkirch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Staatsgalerie_Stuttgart" title="Staatsgalerie Stuttgart">Staatsgalerie Stuttgart</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Novatian" title="Novatian">Novatian</a> was very angry not only that he was not elected pope, but that someone who did not believe in <a href="/wiki/Rebaptism" title="Rebaptism">rebaptism</a> was. He thus proclaimed himself the <a href="/wiki/Antipope" title="Antipope">antipope</a> to Cornelius, driving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a> through the church. After Cornelius's appointment to the papacy, Novatian became more rigorous in his philosophy, convinced that bishops could not pardon the worst of sins, and that such sins could only be reconciled at the <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">Last Judgment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup> Cornelius had the support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Cyprian" title="St. Cyprian">St. Cyprian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Dionysius" title="St. Dionysius">St. Dionysius</a>, and most African and Eastern bishops while Novatian had the support of a minority of clergy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laymen" title="Laymen">laymen</a> in Rome who did not acknowledge Cornelius as pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-5">[5]</a></sup> Cornelius's next action was to convene a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> of 60 <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> to restate himself as the rightful pope and the council <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excommunicated" title="Excommunicated">excommunicated</a> Novatian as well as all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novatianists" title="Novatianists">Novatianists</a>. Also addressed in the synod was that Christians who stopped practising during Emperor Decius's persecution could receive <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">communion</a> only after doing <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The verdict of the synod was sent to the Christian bishops, most notably the bishop of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, a fierce Novatian supporter, in order to convince him to accept Cornelius's power. The letters that Cornelius sent to surrounding bishops provide knowledge of the size of the church during the period. Cornelius mentions that at the time, the Roman Church had, "forty six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priests" title="Priests">priests</a>, seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deacons" title="Deacons">deacons</a>, seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-deacons" title="Sub-deacons">sub-deacons</a>, forty two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acolytes" title="Acolytes">acolytes</a>, fifty two ostiarii, and over one thousand five hundred <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Widows" title="Widows">widows</a> and persons in distress."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup> His letters also inform that Cornelius had a staff of over 150 <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> members and the church fed over 1,500 people daily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> From these numbers, it has been estimated that there were at least 50,000 Christians in Rome during the papacy of Pope Cornelius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_letters">Death and letters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Cornelius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death and letters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 251, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Decius" title="Emperor Decius">Decius</a> was killed in battle with the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>; immediately following this <a href="/wiki/Trebonianus_Gallus" title="Trebonianus Gallus">Trebonianus Gallus</a> became Emperor. Persecution began again in June 252, and Pope Cornelius was exiled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centumcellae" title="Centumcellae">Centumcellae</a>, Italy, where he died in June 253. The Liberian catalogue lists his death as being from the hardships of banishment; however, later sources claim he was beheaded. Cornelius is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_papal_tombs" title="List of papal tombs">not buried in the chapel of the popes, but in a nearby catacomb</a>, and the inscription on his tomb is in Latin, instead of the Greek of his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Pope_Fabian" title="Pope Fabian">Pope Fabian</a> and successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucius_I" title="Lucius I">Lucius I</a>. It reads, "Cornelius Martyr." The letters Cornelius sent while in exile are all written in the colloquial Latin of the period instead of the classical style used by the educated such as Cyprian, a theologian as well as a bishop, and Novatian, who was also a philosopher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup> This suggests that Cornelius did not come from an extremely wealthy family and thus was not given a sophisticated education as a child. A letter from Cornelius while in exile mentions an office of "<a href="/wiki/Exorcist" title="Exorcist">exorcist</a>" in the church for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCR-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">Canon law</a> has since then required each <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> to have an <a href="/wiki/Exorcist" title="Exorcist">exorcist</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Referenced_in_history">Referenced in history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Cornelius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Referenced in history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>St. Cornelius is not mentioned much in most texts. When he is referenced, it seems to be in conjunction with his anti-pope Novatian, who eventually founded his own church with his own bishops; his predecessor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Fabian" title="St Fabian">St Fabian</a>; or his successor <a href="/wiki/Pope_Lucius_I" title="Pope Lucius I">St. Lucius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His papacy was short, reigning two years, three months, and ten days, and little was probably circulated at the time due to the persecution in Christian centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup> Over time, St. Cornelius seems to have been overlooked and passed over for other great Catholic popes whose papacies lasted longer, had more political power, and influenced other cultures. However, while Cornelius is a rather obscure religious figure, his mandates have shaped the church in historic ways.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Cornelius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KorneliusReliquiar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4160" data-file-width="3120" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/KorneliusReliquiar.jpg/220px-KorneliusReliquiar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/KorneliusReliquiar.jpg/330px-KorneliusReliquiar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/KorneliusReliquiar.jpg/440px-KorneliusReliquiar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Relic of Pope Cornelius in Kornelimünster</div>
</div>

<p>Some of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relics" title="Relics">relics</a> were taken to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>; his head was claimed by <a href="/wiki/Kornelim%C3%BCnster_Abbey" title="Kornelimünster Abbey">Kornelimünster Abbey</a> near <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heiligenlexikon.de_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-heiligenlexikon.de-11">[11]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, he was also a <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of lovers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heiligenlexikon.de_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-heiligenlexikon.de-11">[11]</a></sup> A legend associated with Cornelius tells of a young artist who was commissioned to decorate the Corneliuskapelle in the Selikum quarter of <a href="/wiki/Neuss" title="Neuss">Neuss</a>. The daughter of a local townsman fell in love with the artist, but her father forbade the marriage, remarking that he would only consent if the pope did as well. Miraculously, the statue of Cornelius leaned forward from the altar and blessed the pair, and the two lovers were thus married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heiligenlexikon.de_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-heiligenlexikon.de-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cornelius, along with <a href="/wiki/Quirinus_of_Neuss" title="Quirinus of Neuss">Quirinus of Neuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubertus" title="Hubertus">Hubertus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_the_Great" title="Anthony the Great">Anthony the Great</a>, was venerated as one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Holy_Marshals" title="Four Holy Marshals">Four Holy Marshals</a> in the Rhineland during the late Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A legend told at <a href="/wiki/Carnac" title="Carnac">Carnac</a> states that <a href="/wiki/Carnac_stones" title="Carnac stones">its stones</a> were once pagan soldiers who had been turned into stone by Cornelius, who was fleeing from them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Catholic Church commemorated Cornelius by venerating him, with his Saint's Day on 16 September, which he shares with his good friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Cyprian" title="St. Cyprian">St. Cyprian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His Saint's Day was originally on 14 September, the date on which both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Cyprian" title="St. Cyprian">St. Cyprian</a> and St. Cornelius were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martyred" title="Martyred">martyred</a>, as proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Jerome" title="St. Jerome">St. Jerome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup> St. Cornelius's saintly name means "battle horn", and he is represented in icons by a pope either holding some form of cow's horn or with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a> nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heiligenlexikon.de_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-heiligenlexikon.de-11">[11]</a></sup> He is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patron" title="Patron">patron</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earache" title="Earache">earache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fasciculation" title="Fasciculation">twitching</a>, and also of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_animals" title="Domestic animals">domestic animals</a>, earache sufferers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epileptics" title="Epileptics">epileptics</a>, and the town of <a href="/wiki/Kornelim%C3%BCnster" title="Kornelimünster">Kornelimünster</a>, Germany, where his head is located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSI_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSI-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
