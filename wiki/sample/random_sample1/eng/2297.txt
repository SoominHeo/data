<p><b>Pope Gregory VIII</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Gregorius VIII</i></span>; c. 1100/1105 – 17 December 1187), born <b>Alberto di Morra</b>, reigned from 21 October to his death in 1187.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alberto di Morra was born about 1105 in <a href="/wiki/Benevento" title="Benevento">Benevento</a>, Italy. His father was the nobleman Sartorius di Morra. He became a monk early in life, either as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cistercian" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> in <a href="/wiki/Laon" title="Laon">Laon</a>, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> at <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>. Alberto later joined a new religious order, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premonstratensian" title="Premonstratensian">Premonstratensian</a> or Norbertine order, probably between the ages of 20-30. He was a canon at St. Martin's Abbey in Laon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He later became a professor of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardinal">Cardinal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cardinal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1156, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_IV" title="Pope Adrian IV">Pope Adrian IV</a> made him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-deacon" title="Cardinal-deacon">cardinal-deacon</a> of Sant'Adriano, and on 14 March 1158 he became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-priest" title="Cardinal-priest">cardinal-priest</a> of <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_in_Lucina" title="San Lorenzo in Lucina">San Lorenzo in Lucina</a>. As a <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_III" title="Pope Alexander III">Pope Alexander III</a>, he was sent to teach canon law throughout Europe in the 1160s, and was sent to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> to crown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afonso_II" title="Afonso II">Afonso II</a>. He also brought an offer of reconciliation in 1163 to <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_I_Barbarossa" title="Frederick I Barbarossa">Frederick I Barbarossa</a>, whom Pope Alexander III had excommunicated in 1160. Alexander also sent him to England to investigate the murder of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a>, and he absolved King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> of the murder during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Avranches" title="Council of Avranches">Council of Avranches</a>. From 1177-1179, Alberto also served as a legate in Italy and in February 1178 was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Holy_Roman_Church" title="Chancellor of the Holy Roman Church">Chancellor of the Holy Roman Church</a>. As Chancellor he generally pursued a conciliatory line toward the Emperor; in the controversy over the disputed succession of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Trier" title="Archbishop of Trier">Archbishopric</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Trier" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Trier">Trier</a> he argued strongly in favor of setting aside both the pro-papal candidate <a href="/wiki/Folmar_of_Karden" title="Folmar of Karden">Folmar of Karden</a> and the pro-imperial <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_of_Wied" title="Rudolf of Wied">Rudolf of Wied</a>, and allowing the canons of Trier to hold a new election, but was overruled by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_III" title="Pope Urban III">Pope Urban III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was in this position that di Morra "...compiled a Forma Dicendi, a collection of official papal acts, and also completed a codification of the cursus, a compilation of the very stringent rules governing the euphonious arrangements of sentence endings and phrasing in papal acts. In his honor, the cursus was called stylus gregorianus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These two documents were very influential in shaping the rhetoric used in papal documents. Shortly before his election to the papacy, Alberto founded a monastery in his hometown of Benevento.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pope">Pope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 21 October 1187, the day after the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_III" title="Pope Urban III">Pope Urban III</a>, Alberto di Morra was <a href="/wiki/Papal_election,_October_1187" title="Papal election, October 1187">elected pope</a> and took the name Gregory VIII in honor of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII" title="Pope Gregory VII">Pope Gregory VII</a>. He was consecrated on 25 October. His previous dealings with Frederick Barbarossa put the church back in a friendly relationship with the Holy Roman Emperor. In response to the defeat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">crusader</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Kingdom of Jerusalem</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hattin" title="Battle of Hattin">Battle of Hattin</a>, Gregory issued the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Audita_tremendi" title="Audita tremendi">Audita tremendi</a></i> calling for the <a href="/wiki/Third_Crusade" title="Third Crusade">Third Crusade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Gregory travelled to <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> in order to end Pisan hostilities with <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> so that both seaports and naval fleets could join together for the crusade. On the way to Pisa, he stopped at <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> and ordered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antipope_Victor_IV_(1159-1164)" title="Antipope Victor IV (1159-1164)">Antipope Victor IV</a>'s body to be removed from his tomb and his remains thrown out of the church.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gregory died in Pisa on 17 December 1187 of a fever after holding the papacy for only 57 days. He was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_dei_Miracoli" title="Piazza dei Miracoli">Duomo in Pisa</a>. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_III" title="Pope Clement III">Pope Clement III</a>. According to Joseph S. Brusher, "His pontificate though brief was glorious."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
