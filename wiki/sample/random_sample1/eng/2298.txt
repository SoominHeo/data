<p><b>Pope St. Damasus I</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; c. 305 – 11 December 384) was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> from October 366 to his death in 384.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-1">[1]</a></sup> He spoke out against major heresies in the church (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollinarianism" title="Apollinarianism">Apollinarianism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedonianism" title="Macedonianism">Macedonianism</a>) and encouraged production of the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. He has been made a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, and his feast day is December 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographical_Information">Biographical Information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biographical Information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pope St. Damasus I was born around 305,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> around or inside the city of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His life coincided with the rise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_and_Christianity" title="Constantine I and Christianity">Emperor Constantine I</a> and the reunion and re-division of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empires</a>, which is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">legitimization of Christianity</a> and its later <a href="/wiki/State_church_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="State church of the Roman Empire">adoption as the official religion of the Roman state in 380</a>.</p>
<p>Following the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Liberius" title="Pope Liberius">Pope Liberius</a>, he succeeded to the Papacy amidst factional violence. A group of Damasus' supporters, previously loyal to his opponent <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Felix_II" title="Antipope Felix II">Felix</a>, attacked and killed rivals loyal to Liberius' <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacon</a> <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Ursicinus" title="Antipope Ursicinus">Ursinus</a> in a riot that required the intervention of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperors" title="Roman Emperors">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_I" title="Valentinian I">Valentinian I</a> to quell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damasus faced accusations of murder and adultery with a married woman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in his early years as Pope. The neutrality of these claims has come into question with some suggesting that the accusations were motivated by the schismatic conflict with the supporters of <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arianism</a>. His personal problems were contrasted with his religious accomplishments, which included restoring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_fuori_le_Mura" title="San Lorenzo fuori le Mura">Saint Lawrence outside the Walls</a>, encouraging his personal secretary Saint <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> translation of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, and presiding over the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Rome" title="Council of Rome">Council of Rome</a> in 382, which may have set down the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_Christian_biblical_canon" title="Development of the Christian biblical canon">canon of Scripture</a> (based upon the <a href="/wiki/Decretum_Gelasianum" title="Decretum Gelasianum">Decretum Gelasianum</a>, which some consider a sixth-century work<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>). He also did much to encourage the veneration of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_martyrs" title="Christian martyrs">Christian martyrs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> restoring and creating access to their tombs in the <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Rome" title="Catacombs of Rome">Catacombs of Rome</a> and elsewhere, and setting up tablets with verse inscriptions composed by himself, several of which survive or are recorded in his <i>Epigrammata</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As well as various prose letters and other pieces Damasus was the author of Latin verse which modern scholars find "lame and frigid". <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cameron_(classical_scholar)" title="Alan Cameron (classical scholar)">Alan Cameron</a> describes his epitaph for a young girl called Projecta (of great interest to scholars as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projecta_Casket" title="Projecta Casket">Projecta Casket</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> may have been made for her) as "a tissue of tags and clichés shakily strung together and barely squeezed into the meter". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Damasus has been described as "the first society Pope",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and was apparently a member of a group of Iberian Christians, largely related to each other, who were close to the Iberian <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Damasus' parents were Antonius, who became a priest at the Church of St. Lawrence (San Lorenzo) in Rome, and Laurentia. He began his ecclesiastical career as a deacon in his father’s church, where he went on to serve as a priest. This later became the basilica of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_fuori_le_Mura" title="San Lorenzo fuori le Mura">Saint Lawrence outside the Walls</a> in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foley_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-foley-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>During Damasus' early years, Constantine I rose to rule the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>. As emperor, he issued the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a> (313), which granted religious freedom to Christians in all parts of the Roman Empire. A crisis precipitated by the rejection of religious freedom by <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a>, Emperor of the Eastern Roman Empire, in favor of <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">paganism</a> resulted in a civil war in 324 that placed Constantine firmly in control of a reunited Empire. This led to the establishment of Christian religious supremacy in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and gradually led to a See in that city which sought to rival the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Roman See</a>. Damasus was most likely in his twenties at the time.</p>
<p>When Pope Liberius was banished by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> to <a href="/wiki/Veria" title="Veria">Berea</a> in 354, Damasus was <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">archdeacon</a> of the Roman church and followed Liberius into exile, though he immediately returned to Rome. During the period before Liberius' return, Damasus had a great share in the government of the church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession_crisis">Succession crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Succession crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Church</a>, new Bishops of Rome were elected or chosen by the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> and the people of the diocese in the presence of the other bishops in the province, which was the manner customarily used in other <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">dioceses</a>. While this simple method worked well in a small community of Christians unified by persecution, as the congregation grew in size, the acclamation of a new bishop was fraught with division, and rival claimants and a certain class hostility between <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(ancient_Rome)" title="Patrician (ancient Rome)">patrician</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plebeian" title="Plebeian">plebeian</a> candidates unsettled some episcopal elections. At the same time, 4th-century emperors expected each new pope-elect to be presented to them for approval, which sometimes led to state domination of the Church's internal affairs.</p>
<p>On the death of Liberius on 24 September 366, one faction supported <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Ursicinus" title="Antipope Ursicinus">Ursinus</a> (or Ursicinus), who had served as deacon to Liberius, while another faction, previously loyal to the <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Felix_II" title="Antipope Felix II">Antipope Felix II</a>, supported Damasus. The upper-class partisans of Felix supported the election of Damasus, but the opposing supporters of Liberius, the deacons and laity, supported Ursinus. The two were elected simultaneously (Damasus' election was held in <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_in_Lucina" title="San Lorenzo in Lucina">San Lorenzo in Lucina</a>) in an atmosphere of rioting. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._N._D._Kelly" title="J. N. D. Kelly">J. N. D. Kelly</a> states that Damasus hired a gang of thugs that stormed the Julian Basilica, carrying out a three-day massacre of the Ursinians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Supporters already clashed at the beginning of October. Such was the violence and bloodshed that the two <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefects</a> of the city were called in to restore order, and after a first setback, when they were driven to the suburbs and a massacre of 137 was perpetrated in the basilica of Sicininus (the modern <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore</a>), the prefects banished Ursinus to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> There was further violence when he returned, which continued after Ursinus was exiled again.</p>
<p>Church historians such as <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">St. Jerome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tyrannius_Rufinus" title="Tyrannius Rufinus">Rufinus</a>, championed Damasus. At a synod in 378, Ursinus was condemned and Damasus exonerated and declared the true pope. The former antipope continued to intrigue against Damasus for the next few years and unsuccessfully attempted to revive his claim on Damasus's death. Ursinus was among the <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> party in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">Edward Gibbon</a> writes, "The enemies of Damasus styled him <i>Auriscalpius Matronarum,</i> the ladies' ear-scratcher."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papacy">Papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Damasus I was active in defending the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> against the threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schisms</a>. In two Roman synods (368 and 369) he condemned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollinarianism" title="Apollinarianism">Apollinarianism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedonianism" title="Macedonianism">Macedonianism</a>, and sent legates to the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Constantinople" title="First Council of Constantinople">First Council of Constantinople</a> that was convoked in 381 to address these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">heresies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pope Damasus appointed St <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> as his confidential secretary. Writing in 409, Jerome remarked, "A great many years ago when I was helping Damasus, bishop of Rome with his ecclesiastical correspondence, and writing his answers to the questions referred to him by the councils of the east and west..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> If "east and west" do not betray the passage as an interpolation, Jerome spent three years (382–385) in Rome in close intercourse with Pope Damasus and the leading Christians. Invited there originally to a synod of 382 convened to end the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, he made himself indispensable to the pope, and took a prominent place in his councils.</p>
<p>In order to put an end to the marked divergences in the western texts of that period, Damasus encouraged the highly respected scholar Jerome to revise the available <a href="/wiki/Vetus_Latina" title="Vetus Latina">Old Latin</a> versions of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> into a more accurate <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> on the basis of the Greek <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>. Jerome devoted a very brief notice to Damasus in his <i><a href="/wiki/De_Viris_Illustribus_(Jerome)" title="De Viris Illustribus (Jerome)">De Viris Illustribus</a></i>, written after Damasus' death: "he had a fine talent for making verses and published many brief works in heroic metre. He died in the reign of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius</a> at the age of almost eighty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>St. Damasus sat in the Chair of St. Peter for eighteen years and two months. His feast day is 11 December. He was buried beside his mother and sister in a "funerary basilica ... somewhere between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Via_Appia" title="Via Appia">Via Appia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Via_Ardeatina" title="Via Ardeatina">Via Ardeatina</a>", the exact location of which is lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emperor_Gratian">Emperor Gratian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emperor Gratian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reign of <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a>, during Damasus' papacy, forms an important epoch in ecclesiastical history, since during that period (359–383), <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Christianity</a> for the first time became dominant throughout the empire. Under the influence of Ambrose, Gratian prohibited <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan worship</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>; refused to wear the insignia of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontifex_maximus" title="Pontifex maximus">pontifex maximus</a></i> as unbefitting a Christian; removed the <a href="/wiki/Altar_of_Victory" title="Altar of Victory">Altar of Victory</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> at Rome and confiscated its revenues, despite protests from the pagan members of the Senate; forbade legacies of real property to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestals" title="Vestals">Vestals</a>; and abolished other privileges belonging to them and to the pontiffs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_the_Eastern_Church">Relations with the Eastern Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relations with the Eastern Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eastern Church, in the person of St. <a href="/wiki/Basil_of_Caesarea" title="Basil of Caesarea">Basil of Caesarea</a>, earnestly sought the aid and encouragement of Damasus against an apparently triumphant <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arianism</a>. Damasus, however, harbored some degree of suspicion against the great <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocian</a> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a>. In the matter of the <a href="/wiki/Meletius_of_Antioch" title="Meletius of Antioch">Meletian Schism</a> at Antioch, Damasus—together with Pope St. <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a> and his successor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_II_of_Alexandria" title="Peter II of Alexandria">Peter II of Alexandria</a>—sympathized with the party of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulinus,_Bishop_of_Antioch" title="Paulinus, Bishop of Antioch">Paulinus</a> as more sincerely representative of Nicene orthodoxy. On the death of <a href="/wiki/Meletius_of_Antioch" title="Meletius of Antioch">Meletius</a> he sought to secure the succession for Paulinus and to exclude <a href="/wiki/Flavian_I_of_Antioch" title="Flavian I of Antioch">Flavian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He supported the appeal of the Christian senators to Emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> for the removal of the altar of Victory from the Senate House,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and lived to welcome the famous edict of <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, "De fide Catholica" (27 February 380),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which proclaimed as the religion of the Roman State that doctrine which <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> had preached to the Romans and of which Damasus was head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ce-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his papacy, Peter II of Alexandria was obliged for a while to seek refuge in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> from the persecuting Arians. He was received by Damasus, who sympathised with him and gave him support against the Arians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ce-1">[1]</a></sup> This reconciled the relations between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Church of Rome</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Antioch" title="Church of Antioch">Church of Antioch</a>, which both supported the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Alexandria" title="Church of Alexandria">Church of Alexandria</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devotion_to_Saint_Laurence">Devotion to Saint Laurence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Devotion to Saint Laurence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Damasus rebuilt or repaired a church named for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence" title="Saint Lawrence">Saint Laurence</a>, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_fuori_le_Mura" title="San Lorenzo fuori le Mura">San Lorenzo fuori le Mura</a> ("St Lawrence outside the walls"), which by the 7th century was a station on the itineraries of the graves of the Roman martyrs.</p>
<p>Damasus' devotion for the Roman martyr is attested also by the tradition according to which the Pope built a church devoted to Laurence in his own house, <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_in_Damaso" title="San Lorenzo in Damaso">San Lorenzo in Damaso</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Letter_of_Jerome_to_Damasus">Letter of Jerome to Damasus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Letter of Jerome to Damasus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Jerome_to_Pope_Damasus" title="Letter of Jerome to Pope Damasus">Letter of Jerome to Pope Damasus</a></div>
<p>The letters from Jerome to Damasus are examples of the primacy of the See of Peter:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Yet, though your greatness terrifies me, your kindness attracts me. From the priest I demand the safe-keeping of the victim, from the shepherd the protection due to the sheep. Away with all that is overweening; let the state of Roman majesty withdraw. My words are spoken to the successor of the fisherman, to the disciple of the cross. As I follow no leader save Christ, so I communicate with none but your blessedness, that is with the chair of Peter. For this, I know, is the rock on which the church is built! This is the house where alone the paschal lamb can be rightly eaten. This is the ark of Noah, and he who is not found in it shall perish when the flood prevails. But since by reason of my sins I have betaken myself to this desert which lies between Syria and the uncivilized waste, I cannot, owing to the great distance between us, always ask of your sanctity the holy thing of the Lord. Consequently I here follow the Egyptian confessors who share your faith, and anchor my frail craft under the shadow of their great argosies. I know nothing of Vitalis; I reject Meletius; I have nothing to do with Paulinus. He that gathers not with you scatters; he that is not of Christ is of Antichrist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Damasus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of images of "DAMAS" in <a href="/wiki/Gold_glass" title="Gold glass">gold glass</a> cups probably represent him and seem to be the first contemporary images of a pope to survive, though there is no real attempt at a likeness. "Damas" appears with other figures, including a Florus who may be Projecta's father. It has been suggested that Damasus or another of the group commissioned and distributed these to friends or supporters, as part of a programme "insistently inserting his episcopal presence in the Christian (and barely Christian!) landscape".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
