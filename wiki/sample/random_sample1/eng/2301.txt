<p><b>Linus</b> <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Pope_Linus_Pronunciation.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5c/Pope_Linus_Pronunciation.ogg" title="Pope Linus Pronunciation.ogg">pronunciation</a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Pope_Linus_Pronunciation.ogg" title="File:Pope Linus Pronunciation.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> (died c. AD 76) was, according to several early sources, the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a>, and is listed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_church" title="Roman Catholic church">Catholic Church</a> as the second pope.</p>
<p>His papacy lasted from c. AD 67 to his death. According to other early sources, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_I" title="Pope Clement I">Pope Clement I</a> was the second pope; according to the Catholic Church's <i><a href="/wiki/Annuario_Pontificio" title="Annuario Pontificio">Annuario Pontificio</a></i>, however, Clement I was the fourth pope. Among those believed by the Catholic Church to have held the position of pope, only Peter, Linus and Clement are specifically mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirsch_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirsch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Bishops_of_Rome">Early Bishops of Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Linus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early Bishops of Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest witness to Linus's status as bishop was <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a>, who in about the year 180 wrote, "The blessed apostles, then, having founded and built up the Church, committed into the hands of Linus the office of the episcopate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>Oxford Dictionary of Popes</i> interprets Irenaeus as classifying Linus as the first bishop of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Linus is presented by <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> as "the first after Peter to be in charge of the Roman Church"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> as "the first to receive the episcopate of the church at Rome, after the martyrdom of Paul and Peter".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a> wrote, "This Linus, some say, was second Bishop of the Church of Rome after Peter",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> while the <i><a href="/wiki/Liberian_Catalogue" title="Liberian Catalogue">Liberian Catalogue</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> presents Peter as the first Bishop of Rome and Linus as his successor in the same office.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Pontificalis" title="Liber Pontificalis">Liber Pontificalis</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> also presents a list that makes Linus the second in the line of bishops of Rome, after Peter, while also stating that Peter consecrated two bishops, Linus and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Anacletus" title="Pope Anacletus">Anacletus</a>, for the priestly service of the community, devoting himself instead to prayer and preaching, and that it was to Clement that he entrusted the Church as a whole, appointing him as his successor. <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> too wrote of Clement as the successor of Peter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Jerome classified Clement as "the fourth bishop of Rome after Peter" (i.e., fourth in a series that included Peter), adding that, "most of the Latins think that Clement was second after the apostle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Constitutions" title="Apostolic Constitutions">Apostolic Constitutions</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> denote that Linus, who was consecrated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">Paul</a>, was the first bishop of Rome and was succeeded by Clement, who was ordained and consecrated by Peter. Cletus is considered Linus's successor by Irenaeus, and the others cited above, who present Linus either as the first bishop of Rome or, if they give Peter as the first, as the second.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Linus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liberian_Catalogue" title="Liberian Catalogue">Liberian Catalogue</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Pontificalis" title="Liber Pontificalis">Liber Pontificalis</a></i> date Linus's episcopate to 56–67, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a>, but Jerome dates it to 67–78, and Eusebius puts the end of his episcopate at the second year of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Titus" title="Titus">Titus</a> (80).</p>
<p>A man named Linus is mentioned in the closing greeting of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_to_Timothy" title="Second Epistle to Timothy">Second Epistle to Timothy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In that epistle, Linus is noted as being with Paul in Rome near the end of Paul's life. Irenæus stated that this is the same Linus who became Bishop of Rome, a view that is generally still accepted.</p>
<p>According to the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i>, Linus was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> born in <a href="/wiki/Volterra" title="Volterra">Volterra</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> region. His father's name was recorded as Herculanus. The <i>Apostolic Constitutions</i> name his mother as Claudia (immediately after the name "Linus" in 2 Timothy 4:21 a Claudia is mentioned, but the <i>Apostolic Constitutions</i> does not explicitly identify that Claudia was Linus's mother). According to <i>Liber Pontificalis</i>, Linus issued a decree that women should cover their heads in church, created the first fifteen bishops, and that he died a martyr and was buried on the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Hill" title="Vatican Hill">Vatican Hill</a> next to Peter. It gives the date of his death as 23 September, the date on which his feast is still celebrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martyr_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-martyr-13">[13]</a></sup> His name is included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Canon" title="Roman Canon">Roman Canon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a>.</p>

<p>With respect to Linus's supposed decree requiring women to cover their heads, J.P. Kirsch commented in the <i><a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a></i>: "Without doubt this decree is apocryphal, and copied by the author of the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i> from the first Epistle of St. Paul to the Corinthians (11:5) and arbitrarily attributed to the first successor of the Apostle in Rome. The statement made in the same source, that Linus suffered martyrdom, cannot be proved and is improbable. For between Nero and Domitian there is no mention of any persecution of the Roman Church; and Irenaeus (1. c., III, iv, 3) from among the early Roman bishops designates only Telesphorus as a glorious martyr."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirsch_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kirsch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Martyrology" title="Roman Martyrology">Roman Martyrology</a> does not list Linus as a martyr. The entry about him is as follows: "At Rome, commemoration of Saint Linus, Pope, who, according to Irenaeus, was the person to whom the blessed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">Apostles</a> entrusted the episcopal care of the Church founded in the City, and whom blessed <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul the Apostle</a> mentions as associated with him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martyr_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-martyr-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A tomb found in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> in 1615 by Torrigio was inscribed with the letters <i>LINVS</i>, and was once taken to be Linus's tomb. However a note by Torrigio shows that these were merely the last five letters of a longer name (e.g. Aquilinus or Anullinus). A letter on the martyrdom of Peter and Paul was once attributed to him, but in fact dates to the 6th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirsch_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-kirsch-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
