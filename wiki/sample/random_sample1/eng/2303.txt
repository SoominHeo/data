<p><b>Pope Marinus I</b> (or <b>Martin II</b>; died 15 May 884) was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 16 December 882 to his death in 884. He succeeded <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">John VIII</a> in about the end of December 882.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_history">Prior history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Marinus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prior history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born the son of a priest, he was ordained as a deacon by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_I" title="Pope Nicholas I">Pope Nicholas I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-2">[2]</a></sup> Before his election as Pope, he served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Caere" title="Bishop of Caere">Bishop of Caere</a>, which made his election controversial, because, at this stage of history, a bishop was expected never to leave office to move to another <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a>. On three separate occasions he had been employed by the three popes who preceded him as <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">legate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, his mission in each case having reference to the controversy started by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Photios_I_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Photios I of Constantinople">Photius</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Constantinople_patriarchs" title="List of Constantinople patriarchs">Patriarch of Constantinople</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrien_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-McBrien-2">[2]</a></sup> In 882, he was sent on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">Pope John VIII</a> to <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Naples" title="Athanasius of Naples">Athanasius of Naples</a> to warn him not to trade with the Muslims of southern Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_as_pope">Acts as pope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Marinus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acts as pope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among his first acts as pope were the restitution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formosus" title="Formosus">Formosus</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Bishop" title="Cardinal Bishop">Cardinal Bishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Portus" title="Portus">Portus</a> and the anathematizing of Photius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-forum-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to his respect for <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> (r. 871–899), he freed the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> of Rome from tribute and taxation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forum_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-forum-4">[4]</a></sup> He also is recorded to have sent a piece of the <a href="/wiki/True_Cross" title="True Cross">True Cross</a> to Alfred as a gift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He died in May or June 884, his successor being <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_III" title="Pope Adrian III">Adrian III</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marinus.2FMartinus_error">Marinus/Martinus error</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Marinus_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marinus/Martinus error">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the similarity of the names Marinus and Martinus, Popes Marinus I and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Marinus_II" title="Pope Marinus II">Marinus II</a> were, in some sources, mistakenly given the name Martinus (and were then listed respectively as Martinus II and Martinus III). Thus, when the new Pope in 1281 took the name Martin, he became <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_IV" title="Pope Martin IV">Pope Martin IV</a>.</p>
