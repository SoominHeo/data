<p><b>Pope Boniface VIII</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Bonifatius VIII</i></span>; c. 1230 – 11 October 1303), born <b>Benedetto Caetani</b>, was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 24 December 1294 to his death in 1303.</p>
<p>He organized the first Roman Catholic "<a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christianity)" title="Jubilee (Christianity)">jubilee</a>" year to take place in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and declared that both spiritual and temporal power were under the pope's jurisdiction, and that kings were subordinate to the power of the Roman pontiff. Today, he is probably best remembered for his feuds with King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">Philip IV of France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, who placed the pope in the Eighth Circle of Hell in his <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>, among the <a href="/wiki/Simony" title="Simony">simoniacs</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Benedetto was born in ca. 1230 in <a href="/wiki/Anagni" title="Anagni">Anagni</a>, some thirty-one miles (c. 50 kilometres) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. He was a younger son of Roffredo Caetani (Podestà of Todi in 1274/1275), a member of a baronial family of the Papal States, the <a href="/wiki/Caetani" title="Caetani">Caetani</a>, or <i>Gaetani</i> of Aquila.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Through his mother, Emilia Patrasso di Guarcino, a niece of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_IV" title="Pope Alexander IV">Pope Alexander IV</a> (Rinaldo dei Conti di Segni—who was himself a nephew of Pope Gregory IX), he was not far distant from the seat of ecclesiastical power and patronage. His father's younger brother, Atenolfo, was Podestà di Orvieto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He took his first steps in the religious life when he was sent to the monastery of the Friars Minor in <a href="/wiki/Velletri" title="Velletri">Velletri</a>, where he was put under the care of his maternal uncle Fra Leonardo Patrasso.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1252, when his paternal uncle Pietro Caetani became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Todi" title="Bishop of Todi">Bishop of Todi</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a>, Benedetto followed him to Todi and began his legal studies there. He was granted a <a href="/wiki/Canon_(priest)" title="Canon (priest)">canonry</a> of the cathedral in the family's stronghold of Anagni, with the permission of Pope Alexander (1254-1261). His uncle Pietro Caetani granted him a canonry in the Cathedral of Todi in 1260. He also came into possession of the small nearby castello of <a href="/wiki/Sismano" title="Sismano">Sismano</a>, a place with twenty-one fires (hearths, families). In later years Father Vitalis, the Prior of S. Egidio de S. Gemino in Narni testified that he knew him and conversed with him in Todi and that Benedetto was in a school run by Rouchetus, a Doctor of Laws, from that city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Benedetto never forgot his roots in <a href="/wiki/Todi" title="Todi">Todi</a>, later describing the city as "the dwelling place of his early youth," the city which "nourished him while still of tender years," and as a place where he "held lasting memories." Later in life he repeatedly expressed his gratitude to Anagni, Todi, and his family.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1264 Benedetto entered the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a>, perhaps with the office of Advocatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He served as secretary to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a> Simon de Brion, the future <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_IV" title="Pope Martin IV">Pope Martin IV</a>, on a mission to France. Cardinal Simon had been appointed by Pope Urban IV (Jacques Pantaléon), between 25 and 27 April 1264, to engage in negotiations with Charles of Anjou, Comte de Provence, over the crown of Naples and Sicily. On 1 May 1264 he was given permission to appoint two or three <i>tabelliones</i> (secretaries) for his mission, one of whom was Benedetto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 26 February 1265, only eleven days after his coronation, the new pope, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_IV" title="Pope Clement IV">Pope Clement IV</a> wrote to Cardinal Simon, telling him to break off negotiations and travel immediately to Provence, where he would receive further instructions. On the same day, Clement wrote to Charles of Anjou, informing him that the pope had 35 conditions that Charles must agree to in accepting the crown; he also wrote to Henry III of England and his son Edmund that they had never been possessors of the Kingdom of Sicily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also commended to the Cardinal the Sienese bankers who had been working for Urban IV to raise funds for Charles of Anjou, and that he should transfer to them some 7,000 pounds Tournois from the <i>decima</i> of France. On 20 March 1265, in order to expedite the business with Charles of Anjou, Cardinal Simon was authorized to provide benefices from cathedrals or otherwise within his province to five of his clerics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This may have been the occasion on which Benedetto Caetani acquired at least some of his French benefices. On 9 April 1265, on the petition of Cardinal Simon de Brion, the legation which had been assigned him by Pope Urban was declared <i>not</i> to have expired on the death of Urban IV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There would have been no point in making such a ruling if Cardinal Simon had already ceased to be Legate.</p>
<p>Benedetto also accompanied Cardinal Ottobono Fieschi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the future <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_V" title="Pope Adrian V">Pope Adrian V</a>, to England. Another member of Cardinal Ottobono's suite was Theobaldus of Piacenza, Archdeacon of Liège, who became a friend of Prince Edward, and went on Crusade with him; he was later elected Pope Gregory X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On 4 May 1265 Cardinal Ottobono was appointed Apostolic Legate to England, Scotland, Wales, and Ireland by the new Pope Clement IV;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in fact, he was sent as the successor of Cardinal Guy Folques, who had been elected Clement IV on 5 February 1265. On 29 August 1265 the Cardinal was received at the French Court by <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">King Louis IX</a>. There he learned that Simon de Montfort and his son Henry had been killed at the Battle of Evesham earlier that month. Cardinal Ottobono did not reach Boulogne until October 1265. He was in England until July 1268, working to suppress the remnants of Simon de Montfort's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barons" title="Barons">barons</a> who were still in arms against King <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III of England</a>. To finance their rebellion, the barons had imposed a 10% tax on church property, which the Pope wanted back, not only because the tithe was uncanonical, but also so that he could use the funds already collected for his own purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This drawback was a major concern of Cardinal Ottobono and his entourage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> While in England Benedetto Caetani became rector of St. Lawrence's church in <a href="/wiki/Towcester" title="Towcester">Towcester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon Benedetto's return from England, there is an eight-year period in which nothing is known about his life. This period, however, included the long vacancy of the papal throne from 29 November 1268 to February 1272, when <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_X" title="Pope Gregory X">Pope Gregory X</a> accepted the papal throne. It also includes the time span when Pope Gregory and his cardinals went to France in 1273 for the Council of Vienne, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Crusade" title="Eighth Crusade">Eighth Crusade</a>, led by Louis IX, in 1270. The Pope and some of the cardinals began their return to Italy at the end of November 1275. Pope Gregory celebrated Christmas in Arezzo and died there on 10 January 1276. In 1276, however, Benedetto was sent to France to supervise the collection of a <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithe</a>, which is perhaps when he held the office of Advocatus in the Roman Curia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and then was appointed a papal Notary in the late 1270s. During this time, Benedetto accumulated seventeen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benefices" title="Benefices">benefices</a>, which he was permitted to keep when he was promoted. Some of these are enumerated in a bull of Pope Martin IV, in which he bestows the deaconry of <a href="/wiki/San_Nicola_in_Carcere" title="San Nicola in Carcere">S. Nicolas in Carcere</a> on Cardinal Benedetto Caetani.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Orvieto, on 12 April 1281, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_IV" title="Pope Martin IV">Pope Martin IV</a> created Benedetto Caetani <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal deacon</a> of Saint Nicholas in Carcere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1288 he was sent as Legate to Umbria to attempt to calm the strife between Guelfs and Ghibbelines, which was taking the form of a war between the cities of Perugia and Foligno.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the winter of 1289, he was one of Pope Nicholas IV's advisors as he decided on a settlement of the disputes over the election or appointment of Portuguese bishops, in which King Denis played a major role. To give greater authority to the final mandate of the Pope, Cardinal Latino Orsini of Ostia, Cardinal Pietro Peregrosso of S. Marco, and Cardinal Benedetto of S. Nicola in Carcere appended their signatures and seals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Three years later, on 22 September 1291,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Pope Nicholas IV (Girolamo Maschi d'Ascoli, O.Min.) promoted him to the Order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-priest" title="Cardinal-priest">Cardinal Priests</a>, with the title of SS. Silvester and Martin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Given the fact that there were only a dozen cardinals, Cardinal Benedetto was assigned the care (<i>commenda</i>) of the deaconry of S. Agata, and his old deaconry of S. Nicola in Carcere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As cardinal, he served as <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a> in diplomatic negotiations to France, Naples, Sicily, and Aragon.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abdication_of_Celestine_V_and_Election_of_Boniface_VIII">Abdication of Celestine V and Election of Boniface VIII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Abdication of Celestine V and Election of Boniface VIII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_Celestine_V" title="Pope Celestine V">Pope Celestine V</a> (Brother Peter, the hermit of Mount Murrone near Sulmone) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_resignation" title="Papal resignation">resigned</a> on 13 December 1294 at <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, where, much to the discomfort of a number of cardinals, he had established the papal court under the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Naples" title="Charles II of Naples">Charles II of Naples</a>. He had continued to live like a monk there, even turning a room in the papal apartment into the semblance of a monastic cell. A contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_of_Lucca" title="Bartholomew of Lucca">Bartholomew of Lucca</a>, who was present in Naples in December 1294 and witnessed many of the events of the abdication and election, said that Benedetto Caetani was only one of several cardinals who pressured Celestine to resign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, it is also on record that Celestine V resigned by his own design after consultation with experts, and that Benedetto merely showed that it was allowed by Church law. Either way, Celestine V vacated the throne and Benedetto Caetani was elected in his place as pope, taking the name Boniface VIII.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave,_1294" title="Papal conclave, 1294">Papal conclave, 1294</a> began on 23 December, ten days after Celestine's resignation. The regulations promulgated by Pope Gregory X at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Lyon" title="Second Council of Lyon">Second Council of Lyon</a> of 1274 did not envision a papal resignation, but the cardinals waited the usual ten days from the papal resignation. All twenty-two cardinals were thus given the chance to assemble at the Castel Nuovo in Naples, the site of the resignation. <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Aycelin" title="Hugh Aycelin">Hugh Aycelin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> presided over the papal conclave as the senior cardinal bishop. Benedetto Caetani was elected by ballot and accession on Christmas Eve, 24 December 1294, taking the name Boniface VIII. On the first (secret) ballot, he had a majority of the votes, and at the <i>accessio</i> a sufficient number joined his majority to form the required two-thirds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He was consecrated <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> of Rome in Rome by Cardinal Hugh Aycelin on 23 January 1295.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He immediately returned the Papal Curia to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, where he was crowned at the Vatican Basilica on Sunday, 23 January 1295. One of his first acts as pontiff was to grant his predecessor residence in the Castle of Fumone in <a href="/wiki/Ferentino" title="Ferentino">Ferentino</a>, where he died the next year at the age of 81, attended by two monks of his order. Boniface VIII is occasionally discussed in academic literature as possibly implicated in the demise of his predecessor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 1300, Boniface VIII formalized the custom of the <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christianity)" title="Jubilee (Christianity)">Roman Jubilee</a>, which afterwards became a source of both profit and scandal to the church. Boniface VIII founded <a href="/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome" title="Sapienza University of Rome">Sapienza University of Rome</a> in 1303.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canon_Law">Canon Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canon Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon law</a> Boniface VIII continues to have great influence. Earlier collections of canon law had been codified in the <i><a href="/wiki/Decretales_Gregorii_IX" title="Decretales Gregorii IX">Decretales Gregorii IX</a></i>, published under the authority of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_IX" title="Pope Gregory IX">Pope Gregory IX</a> in 1234, but in the succeeding sixty years, numerous legal decisions were made by one pope after another. By Boniface's time a new and expanded edition was needed. In 1298 Boniface ordered published as a sixth part (or Book) these various papal decisions, including some 88 of his own legal decisions, as well as a collection of legal principles known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Regul%C3%A6_Juris" title="Regulæ Juris">Regulæ Juris</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> His contribution came to be known as the <i>Liber Sextus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This material is still of importance to canon lawyers or canonists today, to interpret and analyze the canons and other forms of ecclesiastical law properly. The "Regulae Iuris" appear at the end of the <i>Liber Sextus (in VI°)</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and now published as part of the five <i>Decretales</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Canonici" title="Corpus Juris Canonici">Corpus Juris Canonici</a></i>. They appear as simple aphorisms, such as "Regula VI: Nemo potest ad impossibile obligari." ('No one can be obligated for something impossible.') Other systems of law also have their own <i>Regulæ Juris</i>, whether by the same name or something serving a similar function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardinals">Cardinals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cardinals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boniface VIII was a pope who put forward some of the strongest claims to temporal as well as spiritual power of any Pope. He involved himself often with foreign affairs. In his <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">Papal bull</a> of 1302, <i><a href="/wiki/Unam_sanctam" title="Unam sanctam">Unam sanctam</a></i>, Boniface VIII stated that since the Church is one, since the Church is necessary for salvation, and since Christ appointed Peter to lead it, it is "absolutely necessary for salvation that every human creature be subject to the Roman pontiff".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These views, and his chronic intervention in "temporal" affairs, led to many bitter quarrels with the <a href="/wiki/Albert_I_of_Germany" title="Albert I of Germany">Albert I of Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">Philip IV of France</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, who wrote his treatise <i><a href="/wiki/De_Monarchia" title="De Monarchia">De Monarchia</a></i> to dispute Boniface's claims of papal supremacy.</p>
<p>In 1297, Cardinal Jacopo Colonna disinherited his brothers Ottone, Matteo, and Landolfo of their lands. The latter three appealed to Pope Boniface VIII who ordered Jacopo to return the land, and furthermore hand over the family's strongholds of Colonna, Palestrina, and other towns to the Papacy. Jacopo refused. Jacopo Colonna and his nephew Pietro Colonna had also seriously compromised themselves by maintaining highly questionable relations with the political enemies of the pope, James II of Aragon and Frederick III of Sicily. In May, Boniface removed them from the College of Cardinals and excommunicated them and their followers.</p>
<p>The Colonna family (aside from the three brothers allied with the Pope) declared that Boniface had been elected illegally following the unprecedented abdication of Pope Celestine V. The dispute lead to open warfare, and in September Boniface appointed Landolfo to the command of his army, to put down the revolt of Landolfo's relatives. By the end of 1298 Landolfo had captured Colonna, Palestrina and other towns and razed them to the ground after it surrendered peacefully under Boniface's assurances that it would be spared. Dante says it was got by treachery by "long promises and short performances" as Guido of Montefeltro counselled, but this account by the implacable Ghibelline has long since been discredited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oestereich_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oestereich-35">[35]</a></sup> Palestrina was razed to the ground, the plough driven through and salt strewn over its ruins. A new city—the Città Papale—later replaced it. Only the city's cathedral was spared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>To deal with the problem of the cardinals left to him by his predecessors, Boniface created new cardinals on five occasions during his reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In the first creation, in 1295, only one cardinal was appointed, the Pope's nephew Benedetto Caetano. This was no surprise. Nor was the second creation, on 17 December 1295. Two more relatives were appointed, Francesco Caetano, the son of Boniface VIII's brother Peter; and Jacopo (Giacomo) Tomassi Caetani, OFM, a son of the Pope's sister, was made Cardinal Priest of S. Clemente. Giacomo Caetani Stefaneschi, a grand-nephew of Pope Nicholas III, was also appointed, along with Francesco Napoleone Orsini, a nephew of Pope Nicholas III. Three years later, on 4 December 1298, four new cardinals were named: Gonzalo Gudiel (Gundisalvus Rodericus Innojosa), Archbishop of Toledo, was appointed Bishop of Albano; Teodorico Ranieri, Archbishop-elect of Pisa and papal Chamberlain, became Cardinal Priest of Santa Croce in Gerusalemme; Niccolò Boccasini, OP, of Treviso, Master General of the Dominicans, became Cardinal Priest of Santa Sabina; and Riccardo Petroni of Siena, Vice-Chancellor of the Holy Roman Church, was named a Cardinal Deacon. A pattern begins to emerge, though one sees the pattern only in terms of negatives: of the ten new cardinals, only two are monks, and neither of them Benedictine (Celestine V had been excessively partial to Benedictines); and there are no Frenchmen (Celestine had named seven Frenchmen, under the influence of Charles II of Naples). Pope Boniface was distinctly changing the complexion of the membership of the Sacred College. Without the Colonnas, the influence of the King of France was greatly diminished.</p>
<p>On 2 March 1300, during the Great Jubilee, Boniface VIII created three more cardinals. The first was Leonardo Patrasso, Archbishop of Capua, who was Boniface VIII's uncle; he replaced the Archbishop of Toledo, who had died in 1299, as Cardinal Bishop of Albano. The second was Gentile Partino, OFM, Doctor of Theology and Lector of Theology in the Roman Curia, who was made Cardinal Priest of S. Martin in montibus. The third was Luca Fieschi, of the Counts of Lavagna, of Genoa, named Cardinal Deacon of S. Maria in Via Lata (the deaconry which had once belonged to Jacopo Colonna). A relative, a Franciscan; all three Italians.</p>
<p>In his last Consistory for the promotion of Cardinals, on 15 December 1302, Boniface VIII named two more cardinals: <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(cardinal)" title="Pedro Rodríguez (cardinal)">Pedro Rodríguez</a>, bishop of Burgos, Spain, became Suburbicarian Bishop of Sabina; and Giovanni Minio da Morrovalle (or da Muro), OFM, Minister General of the Franciscans, was appointed Suburbicarian Bishop of Porto. A Franciscan, a Spaniard, no Benedictines, no French. In fact, there were only two French in the Sacred College at Boniface's death, only five regular clergy (only one Benedictine).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts_in_Sicily_and_Italy">Conflicts in Sicily and Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conflicts in Sicily and Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III_of_Sicily" title="Frederick III of Sicily">Frederick III of Sicily</a> attained his throne after the death of <a href="/wiki/Peter_III_of_Aragon" title="Peter III of Aragon">Peter III of Aragon</a>, Boniface tried to dissuade him from accepting the throne of <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>. When Frederick persisted, Boniface excommunicated him in 1296, and placed the island under <a href="/wiki/Interdict" title="Interdict">interdict</a>. Neither the king nor the people were moved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oestereich_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oestereich-35">[35]</a></sup> The conflict continued until the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Caltabellotta" title="Peace of Caltabellotta">Peace of Caltabellotta</a> in 1302, which saw Pedro's son Frederick III recognized as king of Sicily while <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Naples" title="Charles II of Naples">Charles II</a> was recognized as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Naples" title="List of monarchs of Naples">king of Naples</a>. To prepare for a Crusade, Boniface ordered <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> to sign a truce; they fought each other for three more years, and turned down his offer to mediate peace.</p>
<p>Boniface also placed the city of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> under an <a href="/wiki/Interdict" title="Interdict">interdict</a> and invited the ambitious <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Count_of_Valois" title="Charles, Count of Valois">Charles, Count of Valois</a> to enter <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in 1300 to end the feud of the <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Black and White Guelphs</a>, the poet <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> being in the party of the Whites. Boniface's political ambitions directly affected Dante when the pope invited Count Charles to intervene in the affairs of Florence. Charles's intervention allowed the Black Guelphs to overthrow the ruling White Guelphs, whose leaders, including the poet Dante, allegedly in Rome at the time to argue Florence's case before Boniface, were sentenced to exile. Dante settled his score with Boniface in the first canticle of the <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Dante)" title="Inferno (Dante)">Inferno</a></i>, by damning the pope, placing him within the circles of Fraud, in the <a href="/wiki/Malebolge" title="Malebolge">bolgia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Simony" title="Simony">simoniacs</a>. In the Inferno, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_III" title="Pope Nicholas III">Pope Nicholas III</a>, mistaking the Poet for Boniface, is surprised to see the latter, supposing him to be ahead of his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts_with_Philip_IV">Conflicts with Philip IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conflicts with Philip IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The conflict between Boniface VIII and King Philip IV of France came at a time of expanding nation states and the desire for the consolidation of power by the increasingly powerful monarchs. The increase in monarchical power and its conflicts with the Church of Rome were only exacerbated by the rise to power of Philip IV. In France, the process of centralizing royal power and developing a genuine national state began with the Capetian kings. During his reign, Philip surrounded himself with the best civil lawyers and decidedly expelled the clergy from all participation in the administration of the law. With the clergy beginning to be taxed in France and England to finance their ongoing wars against each other, Boniface took a hard stand against it. He saw the taxation as an assault on traditional clerical rights and ordered the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">bull</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Clericis_laicos" title="Clericis laicos">Clericis laicos</a></i> in February 1296, forbidding lay taxation of the clergy without prior papal approval. In the bull, Boniface states "they exact and demand from the same the half, <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithe</a>, or twentieth, or any other portion or proportion of their revenues or goods; and in many ways they try to bring them into slavery, and subject them to their authority. And also whatsoever emperors, kings, or princes, dukes, earls or barons...presume to take possession of things anywhere deposited in holy buildings...should incur sentence of <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a>." It was during the issuing of <i><a href="/wiki/Clericis_laicos" title="Clericis laicos">Clericis laicos</a></i> that hostilities between Boniface and Philip began. Philip retaliated against the bull by denying the exportation of money from France to Rome, funds that the Church required to operate. Boniface had no choice but to contest Philip's demands, informing Philip that "God has set popes over kings and kingdoms."</p>
<p>Philip was convinced that the wealth of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> should be used in part to support the state. He wanted to make war against the English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He countered the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> by decreeing laws prohibiting the export of gold, silver, precious stones, or food from France to the Papal States. These measures had the effect of blocking a main source of papal revenue. Philip also banished from France the papal agents who were raising funds for a new crusade in the Middle East. In the <a href="/wiki/Bull" title="Bull">bull</a> <i>Ineffabilis amor</i> of September 1296,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Boniface retreated. He sanctioned voluntary contributions from the clergy for the necessary defense of the state and gave the king the right to determine that necessity. Philip rescinded his ordinances regarding the exports and even accepted Boniface as arbitrator in a dispute between himself and King <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a>. Boniface decided most of those issues in Philip's favor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Jubilee_Year">First Jubilee Year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: First Jubilee Year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Pope Boniface VIII, fresco by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto di Bondone</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_St._John_Lateran" title="Basilica of St. John Lateran">Basilica of St. John Lateran</a>, Rome</div>

<p>Boniface proclaimed 1300 a "<a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christianity)" title="Jubilee (Christianity)">jubilee</a>" year, the first of many such jubilees to take place in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> He may have wanted to gather money from pilgrims to Rome<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> as a substitute for the missing money from France, or it may be that he was seeking moral and political support against the hostile behavior of the French king and his henchmen. The event was a success; Rome had never received such crowds before. It is said that on one particular day some 30,000 people were counted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Giovanni Villani estimated that some 200,000 pilgrims came to Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Boniface and his aides managed the affair well, food was plentiful, and it was sold at moderate prices. It was an advantage to the pope that the great sums of money he collected could be used according to Boniface's own judgment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continued_feud_with_Philip_IV">Continued feud with Philip IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Continued feud with Philip IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The feud between Boniface and Philip IV reached its peak in the early 14th century, when Philip began to launch a strong anti-papal campaign against Boniface. A quarrel arose between Philip's aides and a papal legate, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Saisset" title="Bernard Saisset">Bernard Saisset</a>. The legate was arrested on a charge of inciting an insurrection, was tried and convicted by the royal court, and committed to the custody of the archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Narbonne" title="Narbonne">Narbonne</a>, Giles Aycelin - one of his key ministers and allies, in 1301. In the bull <i><a href="/wiki/Ausculta_Fili" title="Ausculta Fili">Ausculta Fili</a></i> ("Listen, [My] Son", December 1301) Boniface VIII appealed to Philip IV to listen modestly to the Vicar of Christ as the spiritual monarch over all earthly kings. He protested against the trial of churchmen before Philip's royal courts and the continued use of church funds for state purposes and he announced he would summon the bishops and abbots of France to take measures "for the preservation of the liberties of the Church".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> When the bull was presented to Philip IV, <a href="/wiki/Robert_II,_Count_of_Artois" title="Robert II, Count of Artois">Robert II, Count of Artois</a>, reportedly snatched it from the hands of Boniface's emissary and flung it into the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1302 the bull <i>Ausculta Fili</i> was officially burned at Paris before Philip IV and a great multitude. Nonetheless, on 4 March 1302, Pope Boniface sent cardinal <a href="/wiki/Jean_Lemoine" title="Jean Lemoine">Jean Lemoine</a> as his legate to reassert papal control over the French clergy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> To forestall the ecclesiastical council proposed by Boniface, Philip summoned the three estates of his realm to meet at Paris in April. At this first <a href="/wiki/Estates_General_(France)" title="Estates General (France)">French Estates-General</a> in history, all three classes – nobles, clergy, and commons – wrote separately to Rome in defense of the king and his temporal power. Some forty-five French prelates, despite Philip's prohibition, and the confiscation of their property, attended the council at Rome in October 1302.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following that council, on 18 November 1302, Boniface issued the bull <i>Unam sanctam</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It declared that both spiritual and temporal power were under the pope's jurisdiction, and that kings were subordinate to the power of the Roman pontiff. The Pope also appointed Cardinal Jean le Moine as Apostolic Legate to King Philip, to attempt to find some resolution of the impasse that had developed; he was granted the specific power of absolving King Philip from excommunication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Maundy Thursday</a>, 4 April 1303, the Pope again excommunicated all persons who were impeding French clerics from coming to the Holy See, "etiam si imperiali aut regali fulgeant dignitati."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> This included King Philip IV, though not by name. In response, <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_de_Nogaret" title="Guillaume de Nogaret">Guillaume de Nogaret</a>, Philip's chief minister, denounced Boniface as a heretical criminal to the French clergy. On 15 August 1303, the Pope suspended the right of all persons in the Kingdom of France to name anyone as Regent or Doctor, including the King. And in another document of the same day, he reserved to the Holy See the provision of all present and future vacancies in cathedral churches and monasteries, until King Philip should come to the Papal Court and make explanations of his behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In a bull dated 8 September 1303, Philip and Nogaret were <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> However, on 7 September 1303, an army led by Nogaret and <a href="/wiki/Sciarra_Colonna" title="Sciarra Colonna">Sciarra Colonna</a> attacked Boniface at his Palace in <a href="/wiki/Anagni" title="Anagni">Anagni</a> next to the Cathedral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The French Chancellor and the Colonnas demanded his resignation; Boniface VIII responded that he would "sooner die". In response, Colonna allegedly slapped Boniface, a "slap" historically remembered as the <i>schiaffo di Anagni</i> ("Anagni slap").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="423" data-file-width="424" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg/220px-Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg/330px-Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/60/Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bonifac8_Boccaccio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Depiction of the death of Boniface in a 15th-century manuscript of Boccaccio's <i><a href="/wiki/De_Casibus_Virorum_Illustrium" title="De Casibus Virorum Illustrium">De Casibus</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to a modern interpreter, Boniface was probably beaten and nearly executed, but was released from captivity after three days. He died on 11 October 1303.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> On the other hand, the famous Florentine chronicler Giovanni Villani, wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>And when Sciarra and the others, his enemies, came to him, they mocked at him with vile words and arrested him and his household which had remained with him. Among others, William of Nogaret, who had conducted the negotiations for the king of France, scorned him and threatened him, saying that he would take him bound to Lyons on the Rhone, and there in a general council would cause him to be deposed and condemned.... no man dared to touch [Boniface], nor were they pleased to lay hands on him, but they left him robed under light arrest and were minded to rob the treasure of the Pope and the Church. In this pain, shame and torment, the great Pope Boniface abode prisoner among his enemies for three days.... the People of Anagni beholding their error and issuing from their blind ingratitude, suddenly rose in arms... and drove out Sciarra della Colonna and his followers, with loss to them of prisoners and slain, and freed the Pope and his household. Pope Boniface... departed immediately from Anagni with his court and came to Rome and St. Peter's to hold a council... but... the grief which had hardened in the heart of Pope Boniface, by reason of the injury which he had received, produced in him, once he had come to Rome, a strange malady so that he gnawed at himself as if he were mad, and in this state he passed from this life on the twelfth day of October in the year of Christ 1303, and in the Church of St. Peter near the entrance of the doors, in a rich chapel which was built in his lifetime, he was honorably buried.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>He died of a violent fever, on 11 October, in full possession of his senses and in the presence of eight cardinals and the chief members of the papal household, after receiving the sacraments and making the usual profession of faith.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_and_exhumation">Burial and exhumation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Burial and exhumation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The body of Boniface VIII was buried in 1303 in a special chapel that also housed the remains of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Boniface_IV" title="Pope Boniface IV">Pope Boniface IV</a> (A.D. 608-615), which had been moved by Boniface VIII from a tomb outside the Vatican Basilica in the portico. Boniface VIII had arranged that this would be done to offset the fact that his predecessor was still alive, which caused him to worry that the legitimacy of his own papacy would be thrown into doubt. In choosing such a burial, Boniface VIII was trying to show that he was a legitimate pope with the implicit support from the grave of a popular predecessor, Boniface IV.</p>
<p>The body was accidentally exhumed in 1605, and the results of the excavation recorded by <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Grimaldi" title="Giacomo Grimaldi">Giacomo Grimaldi</a> (1568-1623), Apostolic Notary and Archivist of the Vatican Basilica, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The body lay within three coffins, the outermost of wood, the middle of lead, and the innermost of <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>. The corporal remains were described as being "unusually tall" measuring seven <a href="/wiki/Hand_(unit)" title="Hand (unit)">palms</a> when examined by doctors. The body was found quite intact, especially the shapely hands, thus disproving another calumny, viz., that he had died in a frenzy, gnawing his hands, beating his brains out against the wall, and the like (<a class="external text" href="http://www.newadvent.org/cathen/02662a.htm" rel="nofollow">New Advent</a>). The body wore ecclesiastical vestments common for Boniface's lifetime: long stockings covered legs and thighs, and it was garbed also with the <a href="/wiki/Maniple_(vestment)" title="Maniple (vestment)">maniple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cassock" title="Cassock">cassock</a>, and pontifical habit made of black silk, as well as <a href="/wiki/Stole_(vestment)" title="Stole (vestment)">stole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chasuble" title="Chasuble">chasuble</a>, rings, and bejeweled gloves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>After this exhumation and examination, Boniface's body was moved to the Chapel of Pope Gregory and Andrew. His body now lies in the crypt (grotte) of St. Peter's in a large marble sarcophagus, laconically inscribed BONIFACIVS PAPA VIII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_trial">Posthumous trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Posthumous trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the papacy had been removed to <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a> in 1309, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_V" title="Pope Clement V">Pope Clement V</a>, under extreme pressure from King Philip IV, consented to a <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_trial" title="Posthumous trial">posthumous trial</a>. He said, "[I]t was permissible for any persons who wanted to proceed against the memory of Boniface VIII to proceed." He gave a mandate to the Bishop of Paris, Guillaume de Baufet d'Aurillac, and to Guillaume Pierre Godin, OP, that the complainants should choose prosecutors and determine a day on which the Inquiry would begin in the presence of the Pope (<i>coram nobis Avinione</i>). The Pope signed his mandate at his current place of residence, the Priory of Grauselle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> near Malusan (Malausène) in the diocese of Vasio (Vaison), on 18 October 1309. Both the King of Aragon and the King of Castile immediately sent ambassadors to Pope Clement, complaining that scandal was being poured into the ears of the Faithful, when they heard that a Roman pontiff was being charged with a crime of heresy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> They had a point, in that the persecution implied that a pope was not infallible in matters of faith and morals. Complaints also came from Italy, Germany, and the Netherlands.</p>
<p>On 27 April 1310, in what was certainly a peace gesture toward the French, Clement V pardoned Guillaume Nogaret for his offenses committed at Anagni against Boniface VIII and the Church, for which he had been excommunicated, with the condition that Nogaret personally go to the Holy Land in the next wave of soldiers and serve there in the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> By the end of Spring 1310, Clement was feeling the embarrassment and the pressure over the material being produced by Boniface's accusers. His patience was wearing thin. He issued a mandate on 28 June 1310, in which he complained about the quality of the testimony and the corruption of the various accusers and witnesses. Then he ordered the Quaesitores that future examinations should proceed under threat of excommunication for perjury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> A process (judicial investigation) against the memory of Boniface was held by an ecclesiastical consistory at Groseau, near Avignon, which held preliminary examinations in August and September 1310.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and collected testimonies that alleged many heretical opinions of Boniface VIII. This included the offence of <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a>, although there is no substantive evidence for this, and it is likely that this was the standard accusation Philip made against enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The same charge was brought against the Templars.</p>
<p>Before the actual trial could be held, Clement persuaded Philip to leave the question of Boniface's guilt to the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Vienne" title="Council of Vienne">Council of Vienne</a>, which met in 1311. On 27 April 1311, in a public Consistory, with King Philip's agents present, the Pope formally excused the King for everything that he had said against the memory of Pope Boniface, on the grounds that he was speaking with good intentions. This statement was written down and published as a bull, and the bull contained the statement that the matter would be referred by the Pope to the forthcoming Council. The Pope then announced that he was reserving the whole matter to his own judgment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The XV Ecumenical Council, the Council of Vienne, opened on 1 November 1311, with more than 300 bishops in attendance. When the Council met (so it is said), three cardinals appeared before it and testified to the orthodoxy and morality of the dead pope. Two knights, as challengers, threw down their gauntlets to maintain his innocence by <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_combat" title="Trial by combat">trial by combat</a>. No one accepted the challenge, and the Council declared the matter closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Clement's order disbanding the Order of the Knights Templar was signed at the Council of Vienne on 2 May 1312.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Boniface_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Opera_del_duomo_(FI),_arnolfo_di_cambio,_Bonifacio_VIII_,_1298_circa,_03.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG/220px-Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG/330px-Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG/440px-Opera_del_duomo_%28FI%29%2C_arnolfo_di_cambio%2C_Bonifacio_VIII_%2C_1298_circa%2C_03.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Statue of Pope Boniface VIII at the <a href="/wiki/Museo_dell%27Opera_del_Duomo_(Florence)" title="Museo dell'Opera del Duomo (Florence)">Museo dell'Opera del Duomo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a></div>

<ul>
<li>In his <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Dante)" title="Inferno (Dante)">Inferno</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a> portrayed Boniface VIII as destined for hell, where <a href="/wiki/Simony" title="Simony">simony</a> is punished, although Boniface was still alive at the fictional date of the poem's story. Boniface's eventual destiny is revealed to Dante by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_III" title="Pope Nicholas III">Pope Nicholas III</a>, whom he meets. A bit later in the Inferno, Dante reminds of the pontiff's feud with the Colonna family, which led him to demolish the city of <a href="/wiki/Palestrina" title="Palestrina">Palestrina</a>, killing 6,000 citizens and destroying both the home of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veneration_of_Mary_in_Roman_Catholicism" title="Veneration of Mary in Roman Catholicism">shrine to Mary</a>. Boniface's ultimate fate is confirmed by Beatrice when Dante visits Heaven. It is notable that he does not adopt Guillaume de Nogaret's aspersion that Boniface VIII was a 'sodomite', however, and does not assign him to that circle of hell (although simony was placed in the eighth circle of fraud, below sodomy, in the seventh circle of violence, designating it as a worse offense and taking precedence above activities of sodomy).</li>
<li>He is also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="François Rabelais">François Rabelais</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gargantua_and_Pantagruel" title="Gargantua and Pantagruel">Gargantua and Pantagruel</a></i>. In the chapter that Epistemos lists the inhabitants of hell and their occupations, he says that Boniface was (in one translation) "skimming the scum off soup pots".</li>
<li>The mathematician and astronomer <a href="/wiki/Campanus_of_Novara" title="Campanus of Novara">Campanus of Novara</a> served as personal physician or perhaps only as a chaplain to Pope Boniface VIII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Campano died at Viterbo in 1296.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">Decameron</a></i>, Boniface VIII is <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirically</a> depicted granting a <a href="/wiki/Highwayman" title="Highwayman">highwayman</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ghino_di_Tacco" title="Ghino di Tacco">Ghino di Tacco</a>) a priorate (Day 10, second tale). Earlier (I.i), Boniface VIII is also mentioned for his role in sending Charles, Count of Valois to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> in 1300 to end the feud between the Black and White Guelphs.</li>
<li>The Tale of Pope Boniface is told in Book 2 of <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Confessio_Amantis" title="Confessio Amantis">Confessio Amantis</a></i> as an <i>exemplum</i> of the sin of fraudulently supplanting others. Gower claims that Boniface tricked Pope Celestine V into abdicating by having a young cleric, pretending to be the voice of God, speak to him while he was sleeping and convince him to abdicate (ll. 2861-2900). Gower also repeats the rumour that Boniface died by gnawing off his own hands but attributes it to hunger rather than a deliberate suicide attempt (ll. 3027-28).</li>
<li>Boniface was a patron of <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>.</li>
<li>Boniface had the churches of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> restored for the Great Jubilee of 1300, particularly <a href="/wiki/Old_St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Old St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbasilica_of_St._John_Lateran" title="Archbasilica of St. John Lateran">Archbasilica of St. John Lateran</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore</a>.</li>
</ul>
