<p><b>Pope Sixtus V</b> or <b>Xystus V</b> (13 December 1521 – 27 August 1590), born <b>Felice Peretti di Montalto</b>, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a> from 24 April 1585 to his death in 1590. As a youth, he joined the Franciscan order, where he displayed talents as a scholar and preacher, and enjoyed the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_V" title="Pope Pius V">Pius V</a>, who made him a cardinal. As Pope, he energetically rooted out corruption and lawlessness across Rome, and launched a far-sighted rebuilding programme that continues to provoke controversy, as it involved the destruction of antiquities. The cost of these works was met by heavy taxation that caused much suffering. His foreign policy was regarded as over-ambitious, and he excommunicated both <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV of France</a>. He is recognized as a significant figure of the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Felice Peretti was born on 13 December 1521 at <a href="/wiki/Grottammare" title="Grottammare">Grottammare</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to Pier Gentile (also known as Peretto Peretti), and Marianna da Frontillo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peretti_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peretti-3">[3]</a></sup> His family was poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peretti_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peretti-3">[3]</a></sup> Felice later adopted <i>Peretti</i> as his family name in 1551, and was known as "<b>Cardinal Montalto</b>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peretti_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Peretti-3">[3]</a></sup> He himself claimed that he was "<i>nato di casa <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/illustre" title="wikt:illustre">illustre</a></i>" — born of an illustrious (i.e., "shining") house.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isidoro_Gatti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isidoro Gatti (page does not exist)">Isidoro Gatti</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> the Peretti family came from Piceno, today's Marche, in Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to another source, his father came from Montalto, a nearby village.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There is a theory that he was of Dalmatian Slavic origin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and according to Sava Nakićenović, he hailed from the Svilanović family from Kruševice in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Kotor" title="Bay of Kotor">Bay of Kotor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The theory that his family originated in Kruševice is supported by the fact that the Pope used three pears for his coat of arms (the toponym Kruševice is derived from <i>kruška</i>, "pear").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> According to this theory, <i>Peretti</i> may be an Italian rendition of the Slavic surname, as <i>Peretti</i> itself links to pears (<i>pere</i> in Italian).</p>
<p>About 1552 he was noticed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Rodolfo_Pio_da_Carpi" title="Cardinal Rodolfo Pio da Carpi">Cardinal Rodolfo Pio da Carpi</a>, Protector of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan_order" title="Franciscan order">Franciscan order</a>, Cardinal Ghislieri (later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_V" title="Pope Pius V">Pope Pius V</a>) and Cardinal Caraffa (later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_IV" title="Pope Paul IV">Pope Paul IV</a>), and from that time his advancement was assured. He was sent to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> as <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">inquisitor general</a>, but was so severe and conducted matters in such a high-handed manner that he became embroiled in quarrels. The government asked for his recall in 1560.</p>

<p>After a brief term as <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/procurator" title="wikt:procurator">procurator</a> of his order, he was attached to the Spanish legation headed by Ugo Cardinal Boncampagni (later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII" title="Pope Gregory XIII">Pope Gregory XIII</a>) in 1565, which was sent to investigate a charge of <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a> levelled against <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Carranza" title="Bartolomé Carranza">Bartolomé Carranza</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Toledo" title="Archbishop of Toledo">Archbishop of Toledo</a>. The violent dislike he conceived for Boncampagni exerted a marked influence upon his subsequent actions. He hurried back to Rome upon the accession of Pius V, who made him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostolic_vicar" title="Apostolic vicar">apostolic vicar</a> of his order, and, later (1570), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a>.</p>
<p>During the pontificate of his political enemy Gregory XIII (1572–85), Cardinal Montalto, as he was generally called, lived in enforced retirement, occupied with the care of his property, the Villa Montalto, erected by <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Fontana" title="Domenico Fontana">Domenico Fontana</a> close to his beloved church on the <a href="/wiki/Esquiline_Hill" title="Esquiline Hill">Esquiline Hill</a>, overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian" title="Baths of Diocletian">Baths of Diocletian</a>. The first phase (1576–80) was enlarged after Peretti became pope and was able to <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">clear buildings to open four new streets</a> in 1585–6. The villa contained two residences, the <i>Palazzo Sistino</i> or "di Termini" ("of the Baths") and the casino, called the <i>Palazzetto Montalto e Felice</i>.</p>
<p>Displaced Romans were furious, and resentment of this act was still felt centuries later, when the decision was taken to build the <a href="/wiki/Roma_Termini_railway_station" title="Roma Termini railway station">central pontifical railroad station</a> (begun in 1869) in the area of the Villa, marking the beginning of its destruction.</p>
<p>Cardinal Montalto's other concern was with his studies, one of the fruits of which was an edition of the works of <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>. As pope he personally supervised the printing of an improved edition of Jerome's <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> – said to be "as splendid a translation of the Bible into Latin as the King James version is into English."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papacy">Papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_as_pope">Election as pope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Election as pope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though not neglecting to follow the course of affairs, Felice carefully avoided every occasion of offence. This discretion contributed not a little to his election to the papacy on 24 April 1585, with the title of Sixtus V. The story of his having feigned decrepitude in the conclave, in order to win votes, is pure invention. One of the things that commended his candidacy to certain cardinals may have been his physical vigour, which seemed to promise a long pontificate.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sixtusv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="214" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c4/Sixtusv.jpg/200px-Sixtusv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c4/Sixtusv.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sixtusv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A statue Sixtus V in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore</a>, Rome</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The terrible condition in which Pope Gregory XIII had left the ecclesiastical states called for prompt and stern measures. Sixtus proceeded with an almost ferocious severity against the prevailing lawlessness. Thousands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigand" title="Brigand">brigands</a> were brought to justice: within a short time the country was again quiet and safe. It was claimed that there were more heads on spikes across the Ponte Sant'Angelo than melons for sale in the marketplace. And religious were executed if they broke their vows of chastity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Next Sixtus set to work to repair the finances. By the sale of offices, the establishment of new "Monti" and by levying new taxes, he accumulated a vast surplus, which he stored up against certain specified emergencies, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">crusade</a> or the defence of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>. Sixtus prided himself upon his hoard, but the method by which it had been amassed was financially unsound: some of the taxes proved ruinous, and the withdrawal of so much money from circulation could not fail to cause distress.</p>
<p>Immense sums were spent upon public works, in carrying through the comprehensive planning that had come to fruition during his retirement, bringing water to the waterless hills in the <a href="/wiki/Acqua_Felice" title="Acqua Felice">Acqua Felice</a>, feeding twenty-seven new fountains; laying out new arteries in Rome, which connected the great basilicas, even setting his engineer-architect Domenico Fontana to replan the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a> as a silk-spinning factory housing its workers.</p>
<p>Inspired by the ideal of the Renaissance city, Pope Sixtus V’s ambitious urban reform programme transformed the old environment to emulate the “long straight streets, wide regular spaces, uniformity and repetitiveness of structures, lavish use of commemorative and ornamental elements, and maximum visibility from both linear and circular perspective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Pope set no limit to his plans, and achieved much in his short pontificate, always carried through at top speed: the completion of the dome of <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Loggia" title="Loggia">loggia</a> of Sixtus in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Giovanni_in_Laterano" title="Basilica di San Giovanni in Laterano">Basilica di San Giovanni in Laterano</a>; the chapel of the Praesepe in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Santa Maria Maggiore">Santa Maria Maggiore</a>; additions or repairs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quirinal" title="Quirinal">Quirinal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lateran" title="Lateran">Lateran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a> palaces; the erection of four <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisks</a>, including that in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Peter%27s_Square" title="Saint Peter's Square">Saint Peter's Square</a>; the opening of six streets; the restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueduct" title="Roman aqueduct">aqueduct</a> of <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Acqua_Felice" title="Acqua Felice">Acqua Felice</a>"); the integration of the <a href="/wiki/Leonine_City" title="Leonine City">Leonine City</a> in Rome as XIV <a href="/wiki/Rione" title="Rione">rione</a> (<a href="/wiki/Borgo_(rione_of_Rome)" title="Borgo (rione of Rome)">Borgo</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Besides numerous roads and bridges, he sweetened the city air by financing the reclamation of the <a href="/wiki/Pontine_Marshes" title="Pontine Marshes">Pontine Marshes</a>. Consequently, the spatial organization, monumental inscriptions and restorations throughout the city reinforced the control, surveillance, and authority that alluded to the power of Pope Sixtus V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Good progress was made with more than 9,500 acres (38 km<sup>2</sup>) reclaimed and opened to agriculture and manufacture; the project was abandoned upon his death.</p>
<p>Sixtus had no appreciation of antiquities, which were employed as raw material to serve his urbanistic and Christianising programs: <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan's Column</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Column_of_Marcus_Aurelius" title="Column of Marcus Aurelius">Column of Marcus Aurelius</a> (at the time misidentified as the <a href="/wiki/Column_of_Antoninus_Pius" title="Column of Antoninus Pius">Column of Antoninus Pius</a>) were made to serve as pedestals for the statues of SS Peter and Paul; the <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Hill" title="Capitoline Hill">Capitol</a> was converted into an emblem of <i>Christian Rome</i>; the <a href="/wiki/Septizodium" title="Septizodium">Septizodium</a> of <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a> was demolished for its building materials.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_administration">Church administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Church administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The subsequent administrative system of the Catholic Church owed much to Sixtus. He limited the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Cardinals" title="College of Cardinals">College of Cardinals</a> to seventy. He doubled the number of the congregations and enlarged their functions, assigning to them the principal role in the transaction of business (1588). He regarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> with disfavour and suspicion. He meditated radical changes to their constitution, but death prevented the execution of his purpose. In 1589 was begun a revision of the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>, the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Vulgata_Sixtina" title="Vulgata Sixtina">Editio Sixtina</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5406" data-file-width="3629" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emanuel_van_Meteren_Historie_ppn_051504510_MG_8749_sixtus_de_vijfde.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pope Sixtus V</div>

<p>In his larger political relations, Sixtus entertained fantastic ambitions, such as the annihilation of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a>, the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, the transport of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Sepulchre" title="Holy Sepulchre">Holy Sepulchre</a> to Italy, and the accession of his nephew to the throne of France. The situation in which he found himself was embarrassing: he could not countenance the designs of those he considered as heretical princes, and yet he mistrusted <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a> and viewed with apprehension any extension of his power.</p>
<p>Sixtus agreed to renew the <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a> of Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>, and to grant a large subsidy to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Armada</a> of Philip II, but, knowing the slowness of Spain, would give nothing until the expedition actually landed in England. This way, he saved a fortune that would otherwise have been lost in the failed campaign. Sixtus had <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cardinal Allen (page does not exist)">Cardinal Allen</a> draw up the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_Admonition_to_the_Nobility_and_Laity_of_England" title="An Admonition to the Nobility and Laity of England">An Admonition to the Nobility and Laity of England</a></i>, a proclamation to be published in England if the invasion had been successful. The extant document comprised all that could be said against Elizabeth I, and the indictment is therefore fuller and more forcible than any other put forward by the religious exiles, who were generally very reticent in their complaints. Allen carefully consigned his publication to the fire, and we only know of it through one of Elizabeth's spies, who had stolen a copy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sixtus excommunicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_of_Navarre" title="Henry of Navarre">Henry of Navarre</a> (future Henry IV of France), and contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(French)" title="Catholic League (French)">Catholic League</a>, but he chafed under his forced alliance with Philip II of Spain, and looked for escape. The victories of Henry and the prospect of his conversion to Catholicism raised Sixtus V's hopes, and in corresponding degree determined Philip II to tighten his grip upon his wavering ally. The Pope's negotiations with Henry's representative evoked a bitter and menacing protest and a categorical demand for the performance of promises. Sixtus took refuge in evasion, and temporised until his death on 27 August 1590.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vittoria_Accoramboni_affair">Vittoria Accoramboni affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vittoria Accoramboni affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1581 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francesco_Peretti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francesco Peretti (page does not exist)">Francesco Peretti</a>, the nephew of the then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Montalto" title="Cardinal Montalto">Cardinal Montalto</a>, had married <a href="/wiki/Vittoria_Accoramboni" title="Vittoria Accoramboni">Vittoria Accoramboni</a>, a woman famous for her great beauty and accomplishments who had many admirers. The future pope's nephew was, however, soon assassinated, and his widow married the powerful <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Giordano_I_Orsini" title="Paolo Giordano I Orsini">Paolo Giordano I Orsini</a>, Duke of <a href="/wiki/Bracciano" title="Bracciano">Bracciano</a>, who was widely considered to have been involved in the killing of her first husband.</p>
<p>On becoming pope, Sixtus V immediately vowed vengeance on both the Duke of Bracciano and Vittoria Accoramboni. Warned in time, they fled - first to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and then to <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%B2" title="Salò">Salò</a> in Venetian territory. Here the Duke of Bracciano died in November 1585, bequeathing all his personal property to his widow. A month later Vittoria Accoramboni, who went to live in Padua, was assassinated by a band of bravos hired by Lodovico Orsini, a relation of her late husband.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contraception.2C_abortion.2C_adultery">Contraception, abortion, adultery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contraception, abortion, adultery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sixtus extended the penalty of excommunication relating to the Roman Catholic Church's teaching on contraception and abortion. While the Church taught that abortion and contraception were gravely sinful actions ("mortal sins"), it did not apply to all mortal sins the additional penalty of <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Although homicide had always required this penalty, contraception had not. Patristic and Medieval theologians and physicians had long speculated and debated over the exact moment the fertilised egg became a human being. While there was broad agreement among them that life was present at conception and that it could only become a human being, the thinking was that this did not necessarily mean God had infused the rational, immortal soul into the body at conception. Following <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, many in the West had theorized that the matter had to be prepared to a certain point before this could happen and, prior to then, there was only a vegetative or sensitive soul, but not a human soul. This meant that killing an organism before the human soul is infused would still be a grave sin of abortion (or at least contraception), but that it was not properly a homicide and, thus, did not require excommunication<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Some theologians argued that only after proof of the "quickening" (when the mother can feel the fetus's movement in her womb, usually about 20 weeks into gestation) that there was incontrovertible evidence that ensoulment had already occurred. Until Sixtus V, canon lawyers had applied the code from <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> whereby excommunications were only given to abortions after the quickening. In 1588, however, the pope issued a papal bull, <i>Effraenatam</i> ("Without Restraint"), which declared that the canonical penalty of excommunication would be levied for any form of contraception and for abortions at any stage in fetal development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faculty.cua.edu_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-faculty.cua.edu-15">[15]</a></sup> The reasoning on the latter would be that the soul of the unborn child would be denied Heaven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sixtus also attempted in 1586 to introduce into the secular law in Rome the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> penalty for <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a>, that is death. The measure ultimately failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Sixtus_V&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sixtus V died on 27 August 1590. He was the last Pope to date to use the name Sixtus.</p>
<p>As Sixtus V lay on his death bed, he was loathed by his political subjects, but history has recognized him as a significant figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" title="Counter Reformation">Counter Reformation</a>. On the negative side, he could be impulsive, obstinate, severe, and autocratic. On the positive side, he was open to large ideas and threw himself into his undertakings with great energy and determination. This often led to success. His pontificate saw great enterprises and great achievements. He slept little and worked hard. Having inherited a bankrupt treasury, he administered his funds with competence and care, and left five million crowns in the coffers of the Holy See at his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The changes wrought by Sixtus on the street plan of Rome were documented in a film, <i>Rome: Impact of an Idea</i>, featuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmund_N._Bacon" title="Edmund N. Bacon">Edmund N. Bacon</a> and based on sections of his book <i><a href="/wiki/Design_of_Cities" title="Design of Cities">Design of Cities</a></i>.</p>
