<p><b>Pope Alexander II</b> (<abbr title="died">d.</abbr> 21 April 1073), born <b>Anselm of Baggio</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Anselmo da Baggio</i></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lucca" title="Bishop of Lucca">bishop</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lucca" title="Diocese of Lucca">Lucca</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> from 30 September 1061 to his death in 1073.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Alexander_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> of a noble family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughlin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup> As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lucca" title="Bishop of Lucca">bishop of Lucca</a>, he had been an energetic coadjutor with <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII" title="Pope Gregory VII">Hildebrand of Sovana</a> in endeavouring to suppress <a href="/wiki/Simony" title="Simony">simony</a> and enforce <a href="/wiki/Clerical_celibacy" title="Clerical celibacy">clerical celibacy</a>. (In this role, he is sometimes known as <span class="nowrap"><b>Anselm the Elder</b></span> or <span class="nowrap"><b>Anselm I</b></span> to distinguish him from his nephew <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Lucca" title="Anselm of Lucca">St Anselm</a> who succeeded to his office.) The <a href="/wiki/Papal_election,_1061" title="Papal election, 1061">papal election of 1061</a>, which Hildebrand had arranged in conformity with the papal decree of 1059 (see <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_II" title="Pope Nicholas II">Pope Nicholas II</a>), was not sanctioned by the imperial court of Germany. True to the practice observed in preceding papal elections, the German court nominated another candidate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadalus" title="Cadalus">Cadalus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Parma" title="Bishop of Parma">bishop of Parma</a>, who was proclaimed Pope at the council of <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a> under the name of <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Honorius_II" title="Antipope Honorius II">Honorius II</a>. He marched to Rome and for a long time threatened his rival's position. At length, however, Honorius was forsaken by the German court and deposed by a council held at <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>; Alexander II's position remained unchallenged.</p>
<p>In 1065, Pope Alexander II wrote to B√©ranger, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscount_of_Narbonne" title="Viscount of Narbonne">Viscount of Narbonne</a>, and to Guifred, bishop of the city, praising them for having prevented the massacre of the Jews in their district, and reminding them that God does not approve of the shedding of innocent blood. That same year, he admonished <a href="/wiki/Landulf_VI_of_Benevento" title="Landulf VI of Benevento">Landulf VI of Benevento</a> "that the conversion of Jews is not to be obtained by force."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Also in the same year, Alexander called for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">Crusade</a> against the Moors in Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Alexander II issued orders to the Bishops of Narbonne, instructing crusaders en route "that you protect the Jews who live among you, so that they may not be killed by those who are setting out for Spain against the Saracens ... for the situation of the Jews is greatly different from that of the Saracens. One may justly fight against those [the Saracens] who persecute Christians and drive them from their towns and their own homes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1066, he entertained an embassy from <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">duke</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Normandy</a>, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breton-Norman_War" title="Breton-Norman War">his successful invasion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brittany" title="Duchy of Brittany">Brittany</a>. The embassy had been sent to obtain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessing_(Roman_Catholic_Church)" title="Blessing (Roman Catholic Church)">his blessing</a> for William's <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">prospective invasion</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a>. Alexander gave it, along with a papal ring, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_England" title="Flag of England">Standard of St. George</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and an edict to the autonomous Old English clergy guiding them to submit to the new regime. These favors were instrumental in the submission of the English church following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Count_Eustace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Count Eustace (page does not exist)">Count Eustace</a> carried his Papal insignia, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonfanon" title="Gonfanon">gonfanon</a> with three tails charged with a cross, which <a href="/wiki/William_of_Poitiers" title="William of Poitiers">William of Poitiers</a> says was given to William I to signify the Pope's blessing of his invasion to secure a submission to Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexander II oversaw the suppression of the "Alleluia" during the Latin Church's celebration of Lent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This is followed to this day, and in the Tridentine rite "Alleluia" is also omitted during the Advent season.</p>
<p>Alexander II was succeeded as pope by his associate Hildebrand, who took the title of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII" title="Pope Gregory VII">Gregory VII</a>.</p>
