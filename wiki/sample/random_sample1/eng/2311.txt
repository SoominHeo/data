<p><b>Pope Eugene I</b> (died 2 June 657), also known as <b>Eugenius I</b>, was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 10 August 654 to his death in 657.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a native of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, born to one Rufinianus.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eugene_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little is known of Pope Eugene's early life other than that he was a Roman from the Aventine and was known for his holiness, gentleness, and charity. He had been a cleric from his youth and held various positions within the Church of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_under_unusual_circumstances">Election under unusual circumstances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eugene_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Election under unusual circumstances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the banishment of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_I" title="Pope Martin I">Pope Martin I</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" title="Byzantine Emperor">Byzantine Emperor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constans_II_(Byzantine_Empire)" title="Constans II (Byzantine Empire)">Constans II</a>, he showed greater deference than his predecessor to the emperor's wishes and made no public stand against the <a href="/wiki/Monothelitism" title="Monothelitism">Monothelitism</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">patriarchs of Constantinople</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martin I was carried off from Rome on 18 June 653 and was kept in exile until his death in September 655. Little is known about what happened in Rome after Pope Martin's departure, but it was typical in those days for the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> to be governed by the <a href="/wiki/Archpriest" title="Archpriest">archpriest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon" title="Archdeacon">archdeacon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMann1909_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMann1909-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a year and two months, a successor was found to Martin in Eugene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMann1909_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMann1909-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eugene_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost immediately after his election, Eugene was forced to deal with the heresy of <a href="/wiki/Monothelitism" title="Monothelitism">Monothelitism</a>, i.e., that Christ had only one will.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constantinople_letter_affair">Constantinople letter affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eugene_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Constantinople letter affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the first acts of the new pope was to send <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legates</a> to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> with letters to Emperor Constans II informing him of his election and professing his faith. The legates unfortunately allowed themselves to be deceived, or bribed, and brought back a synodical letter from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Peter_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Peter of Constantinople">Patriarch Peter of Constantinople</a> (656‚Äì666), while the emperor's envoy, who accompanied them, brought offerings for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Peter" title="St. Peter">St. Peter</a> and a request from the emperor that the pope would enter into communion with the Patriarch of Constantinople. Peter's letter proved to be written in a difficult and obscure style and avoided making any specific declaration as to the number of "wills or operations" in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>. When its contents were read to the clergy and people in the church of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Mary_Major" title="St. Mary Major">St. Mary Major</a> in 656, they not only rejected the letter with indignation, but would not allow the pope to leave the basilica until he had promised that he would not on any account accept it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMann1909_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMann1909-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>So furious were the Byzantine officials at this harsh rejection of the wishes of their emperor and patriarch that they threatened to roast Eugene, just as they had roasted Pope Martin I. Eugene's persecution was averted by the ensuing conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Muslims</a>, who took <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> in 654 and defeated Constans himself in the naval <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Masts" title="Battle of the Masts">battle of Phoenix</a> (655).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMann1909_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMann1909-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eugene_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was almost certainly this pope who received the youthful St. <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid" title="Wilfrid">Wilfrid</a> on the occasion of his first visit to Rome (c. 654). At Rome he gained the affection of Archdeacon Boniface, a counsellor of the apostolic pope, who presented him to his master. Eugene "placed his blessed hand on the head of the youthful servant of God, prayed for him, and blessed him." Nothing more is known of Eugene except that he consecrated twenty-one bishops for different parts of the world, and that he was buried in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMann1909_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMann1909-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>He died in 657 and was acclaimed a saint, his <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">day</a> being the 2nd of June, although, according to <a href="/wiki/Anastasius_Bibliothecarius" title="Anastasius Bibliothecarius">Anastasius</a>, he died on the 1st of that month.</p>
