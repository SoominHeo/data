<p><b>Pope Eleuterus</b> (died 189), also known as <b>Eleutherius</b>, was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a> from c. 174 to his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> cites 171 or 177 to 185 or 193.) According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Pontificalis" title="Liber Pontificalis">Liber Pontificalis</a></i>, he was a <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> born in <a href="/wiki/Nicopolis" title="Nicopolis">Nicopolis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His contemporary <a href="/wiki/Hegesippus_(chronicler)" title="Hegesippus (chronicler)">Hegesippus</a> wrote that he was a <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacon</a> of the Roman Church under <a href="/wiki/Pope_Anicetus" title="Pope Anicetus">Pope Anicetus</a> (c. 154–164), and remained so under <a href="/wiki/Pope_Soter" title="Pope Soter">Pope Soter</a>, whom he succeeded around 174.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_law">Dietary law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eleuterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dietary law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 6th-century <a href="/wiki/Recension" title="Recension">recension</a> of <i>Liber Pontificalis</i> ('Book of the Popes') known as the "Felician Catalog"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[a]</a></sup> includes additional commentary to the work's earlier entry on Eleuterus. One addition ascribes to Eleutherius the reïssuance of a decree:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> "And he again affirmed that no food should be repudiated by Christians strong in their faith, as God created it, [provided] however that it is sensible and edible." Such a decree might have been issued against early continuations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_dietary_law" title="Jewish dietary law">Jewish dietary law</a> and against similar laws practiced by the <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnostics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanists</a>. It is also possible, however, that the editor of the passage attributed to Eleuterus a decree similar to another issued around the year 500 in order to give it greater authority.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_mission">British mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eleuterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: British mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lucius_of_Britain" title="Lucius of Britain">Lucius of Britain</a></div>
<p>Another addition credited Eleuterus with receiving a letter from "<a href="/wiki/Lucius_of_Britain" title="Lucius of Britain">Lucius</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_legendary_kings_of_Britain" title="List of legendary kings of Britain">King of Britain</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Britons" title="King of the Britons">King of the Britons</a>", declaring an intention to convert to Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[c]</a></sup> No earlier accounts of this mission have been found. It is now generally considered to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pious_forgery" title="Pious forgery">pious forgery</a>, although there remains disagreement over its original purpose. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_West_Haddan" title="Arthur West Haddan">Haddan</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Stubbs" title="William Stubbs">Stubbs</a>, and Wilkins<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haddan_Stubbs_Wilkins_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haddan_Stubbs_Wilkins-8">[6]</a></sup> considered the passage "manifestly written in the time and tone" of <a href="/wiki/Prosper_of_Aquitaine" title="Prosper of Aquitaine">St Prosper</a>, secretary to <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Pope Leo the Great</a> in the mid-5th century, and supportive of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christianity)" title="Mission (Christianity)">missions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germanus_of_Auxerre" title="Germanus of Auxerre">St Germanus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palladius_of_Galatia" title="Palladius of Galatia">St Palladius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haddan_Stubbs_Wilkins_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haddan_Stubbs_Wilkins-8">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis-Marie-Olivier_Duchesne" title="Louis-Marie-Olivier Duchesne">Duchesne</a> dated the entry a little later to the pontificate of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Boniface_II" title="Pope Boniface II">Boniface II</a> around 530,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Matthias_Theodor_Mommsen" title="Christian Matthias Theodor Mommsen">Mommsen</a> to the early 7th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Only the last would support the conjecture that it aimed to support the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mission" title="Gregorian mission">Gregorian mission</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Canterbury" title="Augustine of Canterbury">St Augustine</a>, who <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mission#Relations_with_the_British_Christians" title="Gregorian mission">encountered great difficulty</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">native British Christians</a>, as at the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Chester" title="Synod of Chester">Synod of Chester</a>. Indeed, the Celtic Christians invoked the antiquity of their church to generally <em>avoid</em> submission to <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Canterbury</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Wales" title="Norman invasion of Wales">Norman conquest</a>, but it is noteworthy that no arguments invoking the mission to Lucius appear to have been made by either side during the <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synods</a> among the Welsh and Saxon bishops.</p>
<p>The first Englishman to mention the story was <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup> and he seems to have taken it, not from native texts or traditions, but from <i>The Book of the Popes</i>. Subsequently, it appeared in the 9th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">History of the Britons</a></i> traditionally credited to <a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a>: The account relates that a mission from the pope baptised "Lucius, the Britannic king, with all the petty kings of the whole Britannic people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nennius_en_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nennius_en-13">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nennius_la_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nennius_la-14">[11]</a></sup> The account, however, dates this baptism to <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span> 167 (a little before Eleuterus's pontificate) and credits it to <a href="/wiki/Pope_Evaristus" title="Pope Evaristus">Evaristus</a> (reigned c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 99</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 107</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nennius_en_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nennius_en-13">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nennius_la_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nennius_la-14">[11]</a></sup> In the 12th century, more details began to be added to the story. <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-history" title="Pseudo-history">pseudohistorical</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historia_Regnum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regnum Britanniae">History of the Kings of Britain</a></i> goes into great detail concerning Lucius and names the pope's envoys to him as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Fagan" title="Saint Fagan">Fagan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Duvian" title="Saint Duvian">Duvian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[12]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[13]</a></sup> The 12th-century <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liber_Landavensis" title="Liber Landavensis">Book of Llandaf</a></i> placed the court of Lucius in southern Wales and names his emissaries to the pope as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elfan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elfan (page does not exist)">Elfan</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Medwy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Medwy (page does not exist)">Medwy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (July 2016)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An echo of this legend penetrated even to <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. In a homily preached at <a href="/wiki/Chur" title="Chur">Chur</a> and preserved in an 8th- or 9th-century manuscript, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Timothy" title="Saint Timothy">St Timothy</a> is represented as an <a href="/wiki/Apostle" title="Apostle">apostle</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, whence he went into <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> and baptised a king named Lucius, who himself became a missionary to Gaul and finally settled at Chur, where he preached the gospel with great success. In this way Lucius, the early missionary of the Swiss district of Chur, became identified with the alleged British king of the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adolf_von_Harnack" title="Adolf von Harnack">Harnack</a> suggests that in the document which the compiler of the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i> drew his information, the name found was not <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Britanio</span></i>, but <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Britio</span></i>. Now this is the name (<i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Birecik" title="Birecik">Birtha</a>-</span></i>, <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Britium</span></i>) of the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Osroene" title="Osroene">Edessa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[15]</a></sup> The king in question is, therefore, Lucius Ælius Septimus Megas <a href="/wiki/Abgar_IX" title="Abgar IX">Abgar IX</a>, of Edessa, a Christian king as is well known. The original statement of the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i>, in this hypothesis, had nothing to do with Britain; the compiler of the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i> changed <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Britio</span></i> to <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Brittanio</span></i>, and in this way made a British king of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Syria#Medieval_era" title="History of Syria">Syrian</a> Lucius.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Eleuterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i>, Pope Eleutherius died on 24 May and was buried on the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Hill" title="Vatican Hill">Vatican Hill</a> (<i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">in Vaticano</span></i>) near the body of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Peter" title="St. Peter">St. Peter</a>. Later tradition has his body moved to the church of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_della_Pigna" title="San Giovanni della Pigna">San Giovanni della Pigna</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">pantheon</a>. In 1591, his remains were again moved to the church of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susanna" title="Santa Susanna">Santa Susanna</a> at the request of Camilla Peretti, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a>. His feast is celebrated on 26 May.</p>
