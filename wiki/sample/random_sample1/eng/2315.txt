<p><b>Pope Innocent XI</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Innocentius XI</i></span>; 16 May 1611 – 12 August 1689), born <b>Benedetto Odescalchi</b>, was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 21 September 1676 to his death in 1689. He is known in Budapest as the "<i>Saviour of Hungary".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></i></p>
<p>Much of his reign was concerned with tension with <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a>. A conservative, he lowered taxes in the Papal States during his pontificate and he also produced a surplus in the papal budget. Because of this surplus he repudiated excessive nepotism within the church. Innocent XI was frugal in matters of governing the Papal States, from dress to leading a life with Christian values. Once he was elected to the papacy, he applied himself to moral and administrative reform of the Roman Curia. He abolished sinecures and pushed for greater simplicity in preaching as well as greater reverence in worship—requesting this of both the clergy and faithful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pope_Innocent_XI_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pope_Innocent_XI-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a difficult cause for canonization, starting in 1791, which caused considerable controversy over the years and stopping on several occasions, he was beatified with no opposition<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in 1956 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:157px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg/155px-Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg/233px-Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg/310px-Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg 2x" width="155"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geburtshaus_Innozenz_XI.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The birthplace of Benedetto Odescalchi at Como</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Benedetto Odescalchi was born at <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> on 16 May 1611, the son of a Como nobleman, Livio Odescalchi, and Paola Castelli Giovanelli from <a href="/wiki/Gandino" title="Gandino">Gandino</a>. His siblings were Carlo, Lucrezia, Giulio Maria, Constantino, Nicola and Paolo. He also had several collateral descendants of note through his sister: her grandson Cardinal Baldassare Erba-Odescalchi, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Erba_Odescalchi" title="Benedetto Erba Odescalchi">Benedetto Erba Odescalchi</a>, and Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Odescalchi" title="Carlo Odescalchi">Carlo Odescalchi</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odescalchi" title="Odescalchi">Odescalchi</a>, a family of minor nobility, were determined entrepreneurs. In 1619, Benedetto's brother founded a bank with his three uncles in <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> which quickly grew into a successful money-lending business. After completing his studies in grammar and letters, the 15-year-old Benedetto moved to Genoa to take part in the family business as an apprentice. Lucrative economic transactions were established with clients in the major Italian and European cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.</p>
<p>In 1626 Benedetto's father died, and he began schooling in the humane sciences taught by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> at his local college, before transferring to <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>. In 1630 he narrowly survived an outbreak of plague, which killed his mother.</p>
<p>Some time between 1632 and 1636, Benedetto decided to move to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> in order to study <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_law_(area)" title="Civil law (area)">civil law</a>. This led to his securing the offices of <a href="/wiki/Protonotary_apostolic" title="Protonotary apostolic">protonotary apostolic</a>, president of the apostolic chamber, commissary of the Marco di Roma, and governor of <a href="/wiki/Macerata" title="Macerata">Macerata</a>; on 6 March 1645, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_X" title="Pope Innocent X">Pope Innocent X</a> (1644–55) made him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-Deacon" title="Cardinal-Deacon">Cardinal-Deacon</a> with the deaconry of <i><a href="/wiki/Santi_Cosma_e_Damiano" title="Santi Cosma e Damiano">Santi Cosma e Damiano</a></i>. He subsequently became <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">legate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>. When he was sent to Ferrara in order to assist the people stricken with a severe famine, the Pope introduced him to the people of Ferrara as the "father of the poor."</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Benedetto_Odescalchi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cardinal Odescalchi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1650, Odescalchi became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Novara" title="Bishop of Novara">bishop of Novara</a>, in which capacity he spent all the revenues of his <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a> to relieve the poor and sick in his <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a>. With the permission of the pope he resigned as bishop of Novara in favor of his brother Giulio in 1656 and went to Rome. While there he took a prominent part in the consultations of the various congregations of which he was a member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly287_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kelly287-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papacy">Papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Election">Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Odescalchi was a strong papal candidate after the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_IX" title="Pope Clement IX">Pope Clement IX</a> (1667–69) in 1669, but the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> government rejected him (using the now-abolished <a href="/wiki/Jus_exclusivae" title="Jus exclusivae">veto</a>). After <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_X" title="Pope Clement X">Pope Clement X</a> (1670–76) died, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a> (1643–1715) again intended to use his royal influence against Odescalchi's election. Instead, believing that the cardinals as well as the Roman people were of one mind in their desire to have Odescalchi as their Pope, Louis reluctantly instructed the French party cardinals to acquiesce in his candidacy.</p>
<p>On 21 September 1676, Odescalchi was chosen to be Clement X's successor and took the name of Innocent XI. He chose this name in honour of Pope Innocent X, who made him a cardinal in 1645. He was formally crowned as pontiff on 4 October 1676 by the protodeacon, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maidalchini" title="Francesco Maidalchini">Francesco Maidalchini</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reforming_the_administration_of_the_papacy">Reforming the administration of the papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reforming the administration of the papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Immediately upon his accession, Innocent XI turned all his efforts towards reducing the expenses of the <a href="/wiki/Curia" title="Curia">Curia</a>. He passed strict ordinances against <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a> among the cardinals. He lived very parsimoniously and exhorted the cardinals to do the same. In this manner he not only squared the annual deficit which at his accession had reached the sum of 170,000 <a href="/wiki/Italian_scudo" title="Italian scudo">scudi</a>, but within a few years the papal income was even in excess of the expenditures. He lost no time in declaring and practically manifesting his zeal as a reformer of manners and a corrector of administrative abuses. Beginning with the clergy, he sought to raise the <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">laity</a> also to a higher moral standard of living. He closed all of the theaters in Rome (considered to be centers of vice and immorality) and famously brought a temporary halt to the flourishing traditions of Roman <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>. In 1679 he publicly condemned sixty-five propositions, taken chiefly from the writings of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Escobar_y_Mendoza" title="Antonio Escobar y Mendoza">Escobar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Suarez" title="Francisco Suarez">Suarez</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Casuistry" title="Casuistry">casuists</a> (mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> casuists, who had been heavily attacked by <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Pascal</a> in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provincial_Letters" title="Provincial Letters">Provincial Letters</a></i>) as <i>propositiones laxorum moralistarum</i> and forbade anyone to teach them under penalty of excommunication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly287_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kelly287-7">[7]</a></sup> He condemned in particular the most radical form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_mental_reservation" title="Doctrine of mental reservation">mental reservation</a> (<i>stricte mentalis</i>) which authorised deception without an outright lie.</p>
<p>Personally not unfriendly to <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Molinos" title="Miguel de Molinos">Miguel de Molinos</a>, Innocent XI nevertheless yielded to the enormous pressure brought to bear upon him to confirm in 1687 the judgement of the inquisitors by which sixty-eight <a href="/wiki/Quietism_(Christian_philosophy)" title="Quietism (Christian philosophy)">quietist</a> propositions of Molinos were condemned as blasphemous and heretical.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_relations">Jewish relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jewish relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Innocent XI showed a degree of sensitivity in his dealings with the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> within the Italian States. He compelled the city of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> to release the Jewish prisoners taken by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Morosini" title="Francesco Morosini">Francesco Morosini</a> in 1685. He also discouraged compulsory baptisms which accordingly became less frequent under his pontificate, but he could not abolish the old practice altogether.</p>
<p>More controversially on 30 October 1682, he issued an edict by which all the money-lending activities carried out by the Roman Jews were to cease. Such a move would incidentally have financially benefitted his own brothers who played a dominant role in European money-lending. However ultimately convinced that such a measure would cause much misery in destroying livelihoods, the enforcement of the edict was twice delayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Battle_of_Vienna">The Battle of Vienna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Battle of Vienna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">Battle of Vienna</a></div>
<p>Innocent XI was an enthusiastic initiator of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_League_(1684)" title="Holy League (1684)">Holy League</a> which brought together the German Estates and King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_III_of_Poland" title="John III of Poland">John III of Poland</a> who in 1683 hastened to the relief of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">Vienna</a> which was being besieged by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a>. After the siege was raised, Innocent XI again spared no efforts to induce the Christian princes to lend a helping hand for the expulsion of the Turks from <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>. He contributed millions of scudi to the Turkish war fund in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and had the satisfaction of surviving the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Belgrade_(1688)" title="Siege of Belgrade (1688)">capture of Belgrade</a>, 6 September 1688.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly288_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-kelly288-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_France">Relations with France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relations with France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_(1678-1679).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="1200" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg/220px-Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg/330px-Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg/440px-Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_%281678-1679%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piastre_%C3%A0_l%27effigie_du_Pape_Innocent_XI_(1678-1679).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Innocent XI (1678-1679)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The pontificate of Innocent XI was marked by the struggle between the absolutism and hegemonic intentions of Louis XIV, and the primacy of the Catholic Church. As early as 1673, Louis had by his own power extended the right of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gale" title="Régale">régale</a></i> over the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyenne" title="Guyenne">Guyenne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dauphin%C3%A9" title="Dauphiné">Dauphiné</a>, where it had previously not been exercised.</p>
<p>All the efforts of Innocent XI to induce Louis XIV to respect the rights and primacy of the Church proved useless. In 1682, the King convoked an <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_French_clergy" title="Assembly of the French clergy">assembly of the French clergy</a> which adopted the four articles that became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallican_Liberties" title="Gallican Liberties">Gallican Liberties</a>. Innocent XI annulled the four articles on 11 April 1682, and refused his approbation to all future episcopal candidates who had taken part in the assembly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly287_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-kelly287-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>To appease the pope, Louis XIV began to act as a zealot of Catholicism. In 1685 he revoked the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> and inaugurated a persecution of French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugenots" title="Hugenots">Hugenots</a>. Innocent expressed displeasure at these drastic measures and continued to withhold his approbation from the episcopal candidates.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1092" data-file-width="1471" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg/220px-Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg/330px-Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg/440px-Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Innocent_XI_Dec_1688.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tachard, with <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Siamese</a> envoys, translating the letter of King <a href="/wiki/Narai" title="Narai">Narai</a> to Pope Innocent XI, December 1688.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Innocent XI irritated the King still more that same year by abolishing the much abused <a href="/wiki/Right_of_asylum" title="Right of asylum">right of asylum</a>, by which foreign ambassadors in Rome had been able to harbor in embassies any criminal wanted by the papal court of justice. He notified the new French ambassador, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marquis_de_Lavardin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marquis de Lavardin (page does not exist)">Marquis de Lavardin</a>, that he would not be recognised as ambassador in Rome unless he renounced this right, but Louis XIV would not give it up. At the head of an armed force of about 800 men Lavardin entered Rome in November 1687, and took forcible possession of his palace. Innocent XI treated him as <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a> and placed under <a href="/wiki/Interdict" title="Interdict">interdict</a> the Church of St. Louis at Rome where he attended services on 24 December 1687.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly288_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-kelly288-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1688, Innocent XI also received the diplomatic mission which had been dispatched to France and the Vatican by <a href="/wiki/Narai" title="Narai">Narai</a>, the King of <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Siam</a> under Fr. Guy Tachard and Ok-khun Chamnan in order to establish relations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cologne_controversy">Cologne controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cologne controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:247px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:InocencioXIb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="625" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/InocencioXIb.jpg/245px-InocencioXIb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/InocencioXIb.jpg/368px-InocencioXIb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/InocencioXIb.jpg/490px-InocencioXIb.jpg 2x" width="245"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:InocencioXIb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_papal_tombs" title="List of papal tombs">Monument to Pope Innocent XI</a>, St. Peter's Basilica</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The tension between the pope and the King of France was increased by Innocent's procedure in filling the vacant <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">archiepiscopal see of Cologne</a>. The two candidates for the see were Cardinal <a href="/wiki/William_Egon_of_F%C3%BCrstenberg" title="William Egon of Fürstenberg">William Egon of Fürstenberg</a>, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Strasbourg" title="Bishop of Strasbourg">Bishop of Strasbourg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Clemens_of_Bavaria" title="Joseph Clemens of Bavaria">Joseph Clement</a>, a brother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Emanuel,_Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Max Emanuel, Elector of Bavaria">Max Emanuel, Elector of Bavaria</a>. The former was a willing tool in the hands of Louis XIV and his appointment as Archbishop and <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a> of Cologne would have implied French preponderance in north-western Germany.</p>
<p>Joseph Clement was not only the candidate of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold I</a> (1658–1705) but of all European rulers, with the exception of the King of France and his supporter, King <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II of England</a> (1685–88). At the election, which took place on 19 July 1688, neither of the candidates received the required number of votes. The decision, therefore, fell to Innocent XI, who designated Joseph Clement as Archbishop and Elector of Cologne.</p>
<p>Louis XIV retaliated by taking possession of the papal territory of <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>, imprisoning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_nuncio" title="Papal nuncio">papal nuncio</a> and appealing to a general council. Nor did he conceal his intention to separate the French Church entirely from Rome. The Pope remained firm. The subsequent fall of James II in England destroyed French preponderance in Europe and soon after Innocent XI's death the struggle between Louis XIV and the papacy was settled in favour of the Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly287_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-kelly287-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innocent_XI_and_William_of_Orange">Innocent XI and William of Orange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Innocent XI and William of Orange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Innocent XI dispatched <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_d%27Adda" title="Ferdinando d'Adda">Ferdinando d'Adda</a> as nuncio to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, the first representative of the Papacy to go to England for over a century. Even so, the Pope did not approve the imprudent manner in which <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a> attempted to restore Catholicism in England. He also repeatedly expressed his displeasure at the support which James II gave to the autocratic King Louis XIV in his measures against the Church. It is not surprising, therefore, that Innocent XI had less sympathy for James than for <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William of Orange</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and that he did not afford James help in his hour of trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly288_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-kelly288-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2007, fictional writers <a href="/wiki/Rita_Monaldi" title="Rita Monaldi">Rita Monaldi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sorti" title="Francesco Sorti">Francesco Sorti</a>, drew popular attention to the claim, repeatedly made by historians over the intervening centuries, that Innocent XI had secretly funded the resistance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> hero William of Orange to the French King, and even financed his overthrow of <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II of England</a>. This was done using the established Odescalchi family business in money-lending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moral_theology">Moral theology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Moral theology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abortion">Abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Innocent XI issued the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> Sanctissimus Dominus in 1679 to condemn 65 propositions that favored a liberal approach to doctrine which included two that related to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>. He first condemned proposition 34 and countered that it was unlawful to procure abortion. He also condemned proposition 35 and stated that the fetus does indeed contain a <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a> and that to abort was murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Innocent XI was no less intent on preserving the purity of faith and morals among all people. He insisted on thorough education and an exemplary lifestyle for all people and he passed strict rules in relation to the modesty of dress among Roman women. Furthermore, he put an end to the ever increasing passion for gambling by suppressing the gambling houses at Rome. By a decree of 12 February 1679 he encouraged frequent and even daily reception of Holy Communion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pope_Innocent_XI_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pope_Innocent_XI-4">[4]</a></sup> On 4 March 1679, he condemned the proposition that "the precept of keeping Holy Days is not obligatory under pain of mortal sin, aside from scandal, if contempt is absent". The document stated that the church taught it was a mortal sin to intentionally skip mass attendance on Sunday or a Holy Day without a legitimate excuse. It further stated that the faithful had to attend the mass on Sunday itself or on the Saturday evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1688, he reiterated a decree of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a> that banned women from singing on stage in all public theatres or opera houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>He elevated 43 new cardinals into the cardinalate in two <a href="/wiki/Papal_consistory" title="Papal consistory">consistories</a>. He also canonized two saints: <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Menthon" title="Bernard of Menthon">Bernard of Menthon</a> in 1681 and <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Armengol" title="Pedro Armengol">Pedro Armengol</a> on 8 April 1687. He beatified six individuals.</p>
<p>Innocent XI was hostile towards the book "Varia Opuscula Theologica" (Various Theological Brochures) that the Spanish Jesuit <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Francisco Suárez">Francisco Suárez</a> published. He ordered all copies to be burnt in 1679 but his orders went ignored. One of the books was discovered in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_days_and_death">Final days and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Final days and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Innocent XI's health declined in 1689 and he was confined to his bed since June. He cancelled a consistory of cardinals on 19 June for the examination of bishops due to ill health and did not hold meetings on 21 June. The pope suddenly took ill with a fever on 25 June and on 29 June was unable to celebrate Mass for the Feast of Saints Peter and Paul, thus, had Cardinal Chigi celebrate it in his place. The pope's condition worsened on 2 July and led his doctors to lance his left leg which caused fluid release, eventually having an operation on his right leg on 31 July, and two more in the following two days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSUN_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSUN-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pontiff received the <a href="/wiki/Viaticum" title="Viaticum">Viaticum</a> on 9 August since doctors were of the belief that the pope had little time left to live. On 11 August Cardinal Leandro Colloredo met with him to remind him that the pope was set to raise ten men into the cardinalate but the pope refused to do so despite the cardinal's insistence. On the morning of 12 August he lost the ability to speak and suffered from breathing difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSUN_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSUN-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Innocent XI died on 12 August 1689 at 22:00 (Rome time) after a long period of ill health due to kidney stones, from which he had suffered since 1682. Following his death, he was buried in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St Peter's Basilica">St Peter's Basilica</a> beneath his funeral monument near the Clementine Chapel, which his nephew, Livio Odescalchi, commissioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The monument, which was designed and sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Pierre-%C3%89tienne_Monnot" title="Pierre-Étienne Monnot">Pierre-Étienne Monnot</a>, features the pope seated upon the throne above a sarcophagus with a base-relief showing the liberation of Vienna from the Turks by <a href="/wiki/John_III_Sobieski" title="John III Sobieski">John III Sobieski</a>, flanked by two allegorical figures representing Faith and Fortitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beatification">Beatification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Innocent_XI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Beatification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The process of Innocent XI's <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">beatification</a> was introduced in 1691 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_XII" title="Pope Innocent XII">Pope Innocent XII</a> who proclaimed him a Servant of God and was continued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_XI" title="Clement XI">Clement XI</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_XII" title="Clement XII">Clement XII</a>, but French influence and the accusation of <a href="/wiki/Jansenism" title="Jansenism">Jansenism</a> caused it to be suspended in 1744 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XIV" title="Pope Benedict XIV">Pope Benedict XIV</a>. In the 20th century it was reintroduced and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> proclaimed him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venerable" title="Venerable">Venerable</a> on 15 November 1955. Pius XII announced his beatification on 7 October 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his beatification, his sarcophagus was placed under the Altar of St. Sebastian in the basilica's Chapel of St. Sebastian, where it remained until 8 April 2011 when it was moved to make way for the remains of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> to be relocated to the basilica from the grotto beneath St. Peter's in honor of his beatification and in order to make his resting place more accessible to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholicnewsagency.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-catholicnewsagency.com-22">[22]</a></sup> Innocent's body was transferred to the basilica's Altar of Transfiguration, which is located near the Clementine Chapel and the entombed remains of Pope St. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_the_Great" title="Gregory the Great">Gregory the Great</a> (590–604).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholicnewsagency.com_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-catholicnewsagency.com-22">[22]</a></sup> The altar is also across from Innocent XI's monument, which was his original site of burial before his beatification.</p>
<p>The feast day assigned to Innocent XI is 12 August, the date of his death. In the Hungarian calendar, it is commemorated on August 13.</p>
<p>Reports suggest that following the attacks on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/11" title="9/11">9/11</a>, the Church decided to advance the long-suspended cause of Innocent XI to be canonised: as the pope who had prevented the Turks from overrunning <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> in 1683, thus drawing parallels with aggressive <a href="/wiki/Islamism" title="Islamism">Islamism</a>. However, popular revelations made in the novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Imprimatur_(novel)" title="Imprimatur (novel)">Imprimatur</a></i> damaged Innocent XI's reputation and thus the planned canonisation of Benedetto Odescalchi was suspended indefinitely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was believed that the canonization would have taken place in 2003 but the book's publication halted all plans to canonize Innocent XI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
