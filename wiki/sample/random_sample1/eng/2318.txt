<p><b>Pope Clement VIII</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Clemens VIII</i></span>; 24 February 1536 – 3 March 1605), born <b>Ippolito Aldobrandini</b>, was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 2 February 1592 to his death in 1605. Born into prominent <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> family, he initially came to prominence as a <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon lawyer</a> before being made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-Priest" title="Cardinal-Priest">Cardinal-Priest</a> in 1585. In 1592 he was <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave,_1592" title="Papal conclave, 1592">elected Pope</a> and took the name of Clement. During his papacy he effected the reconciliation of <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV of France</a> to the Catholic faith and was instrumental in setting up an alliance of Christian nations to oppose the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> in the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_War_(1591%E2%80%931606)" title="Long War (1591–1606)">Long War</a>. He also successfully adjudicated in a bitter dispute between the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> on the issue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efficacious_grace" title="Efficacious grace">efficacious grace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a>. In 1600 he presided over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christian)" title="Jubilee (Christian)">jubilee</a> which saw a large number of pilgrimages to Rome. He showed little pity for his perceived opponents, presiding over the trial and execution of <a href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno" title="Giordano Bruno">Giordano Bruno</a> and introducing harsh measures against Jewish inhabitants of the Papal States. He may have been the first pope to drink coffee. Clement VIII died at the age of 69 in 1605 and his remains now rest in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Santa Maria Maggiore">Santa Maria Maggiore</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was from a <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> family, and followed his father as a <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon lawyer</a>, becoming an <a href="/wiki/Auditor_(ecclesiastical)" title="Auditor (ecclesiastical)">Auditor</a> (judge) of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rota" title="Roman Rota">Roman Rota</a>, the highest <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical court</a> constituted by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was only ordained as a priest at the age of 45, and rose to Pope in a further 12 years. He was an effective, if sometimes ruthless, administrator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardinal">Cardinal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cardinal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-Priest" title="Cardinal-Priest">Cardinal-Priest</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S._Pancrazio" title="S. Pancrazio">S. Pancrazio</a></i> in 1585 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII" title="Pope Gregory XIII">Pope Gregory XIII</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a> named him <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Penitentiary" title="Apostolic Penitentiary">major penitentiary</a> in January 1586 and in 1588 sent him as legate in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. He placed himself under the direction of the reformer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Neri" title="Philip Neri">Philip Neri</a>, who for thirty years was his confessor. Aldobrandini won the gratitude of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a> by his <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Bytom_and_B%C4%99dzin" title="Treaty of Bytom and Będzin">successful diplomatic efforts</a> in Poland to obtain the release of the imprisoned <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_III,_Archduke_of_Austria" title="Maximilian III, Archduke of Austria">Archduke Maximilian</a>, the defeated claimant to the Polish throne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughlin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IX" title="Pope Innocent IX">Pope Innocent IX</a> (1591), <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave,_1592" title="Papal conclave, 1592">another stormy conclave</a> ensued, where a determined minority of Italian Cardinals were unwilling to be dictated to by <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a>. Cardinal Aldobrandini's election on 30 January 1592, was received as a portent of more balanced and liberal Papal policy in European affairs. He took the non-politicised name Clement VIII. He proved to be an able Pope, with an unlimited capacity for work and a lawyer's eye for detail, and a wise statesman, the general object of whose policy was to free the Papacy from its dependence upon <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughlin_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_matters">Ecclesiastical matters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical matters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="De_Auxiliis_controversy"><i>De Auxiliis</i> controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: De Auxiliis controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1597, he established the <a href="/wiki/Congregatio_de_Auxiliis" title="Congregatio de Auxiliis">Congregatio de Auxiliis</a> which was to settle the theological controversy between the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican Order</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuits</a> concerning the respective role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efficacious_grace" title="Efficacious grace">efficacious grace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a>. Although the debate tended toward a condemnation of <a href="/wiki/Molinism" title="Molinism">Molinism</a>'s insistence on free will to the detriment of efficacious grace, the important influence of the Jesuit Order — among other considerations — which, beside important political and theological power in Europe, had also various missions abroad (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit_Reductions" title="Jesuit Reductions">Misiones Jesuiticas</a> in South America, <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_China_missions" title="Jesuit China missions">missions in China</a>, etc.), led the Pope to abstain from an official condemnation of the Jesuits. In 1611 and again in 1625 a decree prohibited any discussion of the matter, although it was often informally avoided by the publication of commentaries on <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jubilee_of_1600">Jubilee of 1600</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jubilee of 1600">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christian)" title="Jubilee (Christian)">jubilee</a> of 1600, three million pilgrims visited the holy places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughlin_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synod_of_Brest" title="Synod of Brest">Synod of Brest</a> was held 1595 in the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth</a>, by which a great part of the <a href="/wiki/Ruthenia" title="Ruthenia">Ruthenian</a> clergy and people were reunited to Rome.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canonisations_and_beatifications">Canonisations and beatifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canonisations and beatifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clement VIII <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonised" title="Canonised">canonised</a> <a href="/wiki/Hyacinth_of_Poland" title="Hyacinth of Poland">Hyacinth</a> (17 April 1594), <a href="/wiki/Julian_of_Cuenca" title="Julian of Cuenca">Julian of Cuenca</a> (18 October 1594), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raymond_of_Pe%C3%B1afort" title="Raymond of Peñafort">Raymond of Peñafort</a> (1601).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_France_and_Spain">Relations with France and Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relations with France and Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clement_viii.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="350" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Clement_viii.jpg/220px-Clement_viii.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Clement_viii.jpg/330px-Clement_viii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Clement_viii.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clement_viii.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Clement VIII</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most remarkable event of Clement VIII's reign was the reconciliation to the Church of <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV of France</a> (1589–1610), after long negotiations, carried on with great dexterity through Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Arnaud_d%27Ossat" title="Arnaud d'Ossat">Arnaud d'Ossat</a>, that resolved the complicated situation in France. Henry embraced Catholicism on 25 July 1593. After a pause to assess Henry IV's sincerity, Clement VIII braved Spanish displeasure, and in the autumn of 1595 he solemnly absolved Henry IV, thus putting an end to the thirty years' religious war in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>Henry IV's friendship was of essential importance to the Papacy two years later, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_II_d%27Este" title="Alfonso II d'Este">Alfonso II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Duke of Ferrara</a>, died childless (27 October 1597), and the Pope resolved to attach the stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Este" title="House of Este">Este</a> family to the states of the Church. Though Spain and the <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Rudolf II</a> encouraged Alfonso II's illegitimate cousin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesare_d%27Este" title="Cesare d'Este">Cesare d'Este</a>, to withstand the Pope, they were deterred from giving him any material aid by Henry IV's threats, and a papal army entered Ferrara almost unopposed.</p>
<p>In 1598 Clement VIII won more credit for the papacy by bringing about a definite treaty of peace between Spain and France in the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Vervins" title="Peace of Vervins">Peace of Vervins</a>, this put an end to their long contest, and he negotiated peace between France and <a href="/wiki/Savoy" title="Savoy">Savoy</a> as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_War">Long War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Long War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Holy_League_of_Pope_Clement_VIII" title="Holy League of Pope Clement VIII">Holy League of Pope Clement VIII</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_War_(1591%E2%80%931606)" title="Long War (1591–1606)">Long War (1591–1606)</a></div>
<p>In 1595, Clement VIII initiated an alliance of Christian European powers to take part in the war with the Ottoman Empire, fought mainly in <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Hungary" title="Ottoman Hungary">Hungary</a>, which would become known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_War_(1591%E2%80%931606)" title="Long War (1591–1606)">Long War</a>" and would continue past Clement's own lifetime. Facilitated by the Pope, a treaty of alliance was signed in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Rudolf II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_B%C3%A1thory" title="Sigismund Báthory">Sigismund Báthory</a> of Transylvania. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aron_Vod%C4%83" title="Aron Vodă">Aron Vodă</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_the_Brave" title="Michael the Brave">Michael the Brave</a> of Wallachia joined the alliance later that year. Clement VIII himself lent the Emperor valuable assistance in men and money.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_policies">Internal policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Internal policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement">Law enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Law enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clement VIII was as vigorous as <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a> (1585–90) in crushing banditry in the papal provinces of Umbria and the Marche and in punishing the lawlessness of the Roman nobility. Upon his ascension to the papal throne in 1592, he immediately had several noble troublemakers put to death. These included most famously Troio Savelli, scion of a powerful ancient Roman family, and the youthful and noble <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Cenci" title="Beatrice Cenci">Beatrice Cenci</a>, who had murdered her father – probably as a consequence of his repeated abuses. The latter case prompted many requests of clemency – rejected by the Pope, who passed the confiscated Cenci property to his own family.</p>
<p>Clement's strict ways also concerned philosophical and religious matters. In 1599 he ordered the Italian miller <a href="/wiki/Menocchio" title="Menocchio">Menocchio</a> – who had formed the belief that God was not eternal but had Himself once been created out of chaos – to be burned at the stake. A more famous case was the trial for <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno" title="Giordano Bruno">Giordano Bruno</a>, who was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burned_at_the_stake" title="Burned at the stake">burned at the stake</a> in 1600. Pope Clement VIII participated personally in the final phases of the trial, inviting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinals</a> in charge of the case to proceed with the verdict.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-Jewish_measures">Anti-Jewish measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anti-Jewish measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clement VIII tightened measures against the Jewish inhabitants of his territories. In 1592, the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> <i>Cum saepe accidere</i> forbade the Jewish community of the <a href="/wiki/Comtat_Venaissin" title="Comtat Venaissin">Comtat Venaissin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>, a papal enclave, to sell new goods, putting them at an economic disadvantage. In 1593, the bull <i><a href="/wiki/Caeca_et_Obdurata" title="Caeca et Obdurata">Caeca et Obdurata</a></i> reiterated <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_V" title="Pope Pius V">Pope Pius V</a>'s decree of 1569 which banned Jews from living in the Papal states outside the cities of Rome, <a href="/wiki/Ancona" title="Ancona">Ancona</a>, and Avignon. The main effect of the bull was to evict Jews who had returned to areas of the Papal States (mainly <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a>) after 1586 (following their expulsion in 1569) and to expel Jewish communities from cities like <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> (which had been incorporated under papal dominion since 1569).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foa_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-foa-3">[3]</a></sup> The bull also alleged that Jews in the Papal States had engaged in <a href="/wiki/Usury" title="Usury">usury</a> and exploited the hospitality of Clement VIII's predecessors "who, in order to lead them from their darkness to knowledge of the true faith, deemed it opportune to use the clemency of Christian piety towards them" (alluding to <i>Christiana pietas</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frag_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-frag-4">[4]</a></sup> With the bull <i>Cum Hebraeorum malitia</i> a few days later, Clement VIII also forbade the reading of the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1820" data-file-width="1200" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg/200px-Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg/300px-Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg/400px-Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clement_VIII_SM_Maggiore.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Pope Clement VIII in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore</a></div>

<p>Clement VIII was afflicted by <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>, and was forced to spend much of his later life immobilized in bed. He died in March 1605, leaving a reputation for prudence, munificence, ruthlessness and capacity for business. Clement was buried in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_V" title="Pope Paul V">Pope Paul V</a> (1605–21) had a mausoleum built for him in the Borghese Chapel of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Santa Maria Maggiore">Santa Maria Maggiore</a>, where the remains were transferred in 1646.</p>
<p>His reign is especially distinguished by the number and beauty of his medals. Clement VIII founded the <a href="/wiki/Collegio_Clementino" title="Collegio Clementino">Collegio Clementino</a> for the education of the sons of the richer classes, and augmented the number of national colleges in Rome by opening the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collegio_Scozzese" title="Collegio Scozzese">Collegio Scozzese</a> for the training of missionaries to <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coffee">Coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Clement_VIII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_coffee" title="History of coffee">History of coffee</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">Coffee</a> aficionados often claim that the spread of its popularity among Catholics is due to Pope Clement VIII's influence. He was pressed by his advisers to denounce coffee. The advisers believed coffee was the "bitter invention of <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a>" because of its popularity among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, upon tasting coffee, Pope Clement VIII declared that, "This Satan's drink is so delicious that it would be a pity to let the infidels have exclusive use of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Clement allegedly blessed the bean because it appeared better for the people than alcoholic beverages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The year often cited is 1600. It is not clear whether this is a true story, but it may have been found amusing at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
