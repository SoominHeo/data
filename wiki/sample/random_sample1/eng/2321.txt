<p><b>Pope Adrian I</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Hadrianus I</i></span> c. 700 – 25 December 795) was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 1 February 772 to his death in 795.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-1">[1]</a></sup> He was the son of Theodore, a <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Roman</a> nobleman.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_of_papacy">Start of papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Start of papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shortly after Adrian's accession in 772, the territory ruled by the papacy was invaded by <a href="/wiki/Desiderius" title="Desiderius">Desiderius</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, and Adrian was compelled to seek the assistance of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> king <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>, who entered Italy with a large army. Charlemagne besieged Desiderius in his capital of <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>. After taking the town, he banished the Lombard king to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Corbie" title="Abbey of Corbie">Abbey of Corbie</a> in France, and adopted the title "King of the Lombards" himself. The pope, whose expectations had been aroused, had to content himself with some additions to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Rome" title="Duchy of Rome">Duchy of Rome</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">Exarchate of Ravenna</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_the_Pentapolis" title="Duchy of the Pentapolis">Pentapolis</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marches" title="Marches">Marches</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> which consisted of the "five cities" on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> coast from <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ancona" title="Ancona">Ancona</a> with the coastal plain as far as the mountains. He celebrated the occasion by striking the earliest papal coin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in a mark of the direction the mediaeval papacy was to take, no longer dated his documents by the Emperor in the east, but by the reign of Charles, king of the Franks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A mark of such newly settled conditions in the Duchy of Rome is the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domusculta_Capracorum" title="Domusculta Capracorum">Domusculta Capracorum</a></i>, the central <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a> that Adrian assembled from a nucleus of his inherited estates and acquisitions from neighbors in the countryside north of <a href="/wiki/Veii" title="Veii">Veii</a>. The villa is documented in <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Pontificalis" title="Liber Pontificalis">Liber Pontificalis</a></i>, but its site was not rediscovered until the 1960s, when excavations revealed the structures on a gently-rounded hill that was only marginally capable of self-defense, but fully self-sufficient for a mixed economy of grains and vineyards, olives, vegetable gardens and piggery with its own grain mill, smithies and tile-kilns. During the 10th century villages were carved out of Adrian's Capracorum estate: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campagnano" title="Campagnano">Campagnano</a>, mentioned first in 1076; <a href="/wiki/Formello" title="Formello">Formello</a>, mentioned in 1027; <a href="/wiki/Mazzano_Romano" title="Mazzano Romano">Mazzano</a>, mentioned in 945; and Stabia (modern <a href="/wiki/Faleria" title="Faleria">Faleria</a>), mentioned in 998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the Lombards had always been openly respectful of the papacy, the popes distrusted them. The popes had sought aid from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> to keep these barbarians in check. Adrian continued this policy. Because the East could offer no direct aid, Adrian then looked to the Franks to offset the power of the Lombards.</p>
<p>Friendly relations between pope and king were not disturbed by the theological dispute about the veneration of icons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-6">[6]</a></sup> In 787, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh_Ecumenical_Council" title="Seventh Ecumenical Council">Second Council of Nicaea</a>, approved by Pope Adrian, had confirmed the practice and excommunicated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconoclasm_(Byzantine)" title="Iconoclasm (Byzantine)">iconoclasts</a>. Charlemagne, however, who had received the Council's decisions only in a bad Latin translation, consulted with his theologians and sent the Pope the <i>Capitulare contra synodum</i> (792), a response critical of several passages found in the council's acts. He also had his theologians, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodulf_of_Orleans" title="Theodulf of Orleans">Theodulf of Orleans</a>, compose the more comprehensive <i><a href="/wiki/Libri_Carolini" title="Libri Carolini">Libri Carolini</a></i>. Pope Adrian reacted to the Capitulare with a defense of the Council. In 794, a synod held at <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> in 794 discussed the issue but refused to receive the Libri and content itself with condemning extreme forms of veneration of icons.</p>
<p>In 787 Adrian elevated the English <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lichfield" title="Bishop of Lichfield">diocese of Lichfield</a> to an archdiocese at the request of the English bishops and King <a href="/wiki/Offa_of_Mercia" title="Offa of Mercia">Offa of Mercia</a> to balance the ecclesiastic power in that land between <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>. He gave the <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a> bishop <a href="/wiki/Hygeberht" title="Hygeberht">Hygeberht</a> the <a href="/wiki/Pallium" title="Pallium">pallium</a> in 788.</p>
<p>Regarding the Muslims, he maintained the prohibition of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Zachary" title="Pope Zachary">Pope Zachary</a> of selling slaves to Muslims, whom Adrian described as "the unspeakable race of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saracens" title="Saracens">Saracens</a>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in order to guarantee a labor pool and to keep the power of Muslim rivals in check.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also encouraged Charlemagne to lead his troops into Spain against <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">the Muslims there</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and was generally interested in expanding Christian influence and eliminating Muslim control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An epitaph written by Charlemagne in verse, in which he styles Adrian "father", is still to be seen at the door of the Vatican basilica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> Adrian restored some of the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(Roman)" title="Aqueduct (Roman)">aqueducts</a> of Rome and rebuilt the churches of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_in_Cosmedin" title="Santa Maria in Cosmedin">Santa Maria in Cosmedin</a>, decorated by Greek monks fleeing from the iconoclastal persecutions, and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Marco_(Rome)" title="Basilica di San Marco (Rome)">San Marco</a> in Rome. At the time of his death at the age of 95, his was the longest pontificate in Church history until it was surpassed by the 24-year papacy of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_VI" title="Pope Pius VI">Pius VI</a> in the late 18th century. Only three other popes – <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pius IX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Leo XIII</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">John Paul II</a> – have reigned for longer periods since.</p>
