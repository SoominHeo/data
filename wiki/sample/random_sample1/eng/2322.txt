<p><b>Pope Adrian VI</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Hadrianus VI</i></span>), born <b>Adriaan Florensz (Boeyens)</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (2 March 1459 ‚Äí 14 September 1523), was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> from 9 January 1522 until his death on 14 September 1523. He was the only Dutchman ever to be Pope.</p>
<p>Pope Adrian VI was born in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Utrecht" title="Bishopric of Utrecht">Prince-Bishopric of Utrecht</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and was the last non-Italian pope until <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> became pope in 1978. Pope Adrian VI and his eventual successor <a href="/wiki/Pope_Marcellus_II" title="Pope Marcellus II">Pope Marcellus II</a> are the only popes of the modern era to retain their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptismal_name" title="Baptismal name">baptismal names</a> after their election.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg/170px-Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg/255px-Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg/340px-Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geboortehuis_van_Paus_Adriaan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pope Adrian VI's birthplace in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Adriaan Florensz was born on 2 March 1459 in the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a>, which was then the capital of the Prince-Bishopric of Utrecht,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loughlin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup> a part of the <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_Netherlands" title="Burgundian Netherlands">Burgundian Netherlands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. He was born into modest circumstances as the son of Florens Boeyensz, also born in Utrecht, and his wife Geertruid. He had three older brothers, Jan, Cornelius, and Claes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Adrian consistently signed with <i>Adrianus Florentii</i> or <i>Adrianus de Traiecto</i> ("Adrian of Utrecht") in later life, suggesting that his family did not yet have a surname but used patronymics only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martens_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-martens-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adrian was probably raised in a house on the corner of the Brandsteeg and Oude Gracht that was owned by his grandfather Boudewijn (Boeyen, for short). His father, a carpenter and likely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipwright" title="Shipwright">shipwright</a>, died when Adrian was 10 years or younger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Adrian studied from a very young age under the <a href="/wiki/Brethren_of_the_Common_Life" title="Brethren of the Common Life">Brethren of the Common Life</a>, either at <a href="/wiki/Zwolle" title="Zwolle">Zwolle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deventer" title="Deventer">Deventer</a> and was also a student of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_school" title="Latin school">Latin school</a> (now <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gymnasium_Celeanum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gymnasium Celeanum (page does not exist)">Gymnasium Celeanum</a></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Zwolle" title="Zwolle">Zwolle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leuven">Leuven</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Leuven">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 1476, he started his studies at the <a href="/wiki/Old_University_of_Leuven" title="Old University of Leuven">University of Leuven</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> where he pursued philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">Canon Law</a>, thanks to a scholarship granted by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_York" title="Margaret of York">Margaret of York</a>, Duchess of Burgundy. In 1478 he had the title of <i>Primus Philosophiae</i>, as well as that of <i>Magister Artium</i> (that is, he took his undergraduate degree). In 1488 he was chosen by the Faculty of Arts to be their representative on the Council of the University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 30 June 1490, Adrian was ordained a priest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the regular 12 years of study, Adrian became a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">Doctor</a> of Theology in 1491. He had been a teacher at the University since 1490, was chosen vice-<a href="/wiki/Chancellor" title="Chancellor">chancellor</a> of the university in 1493, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dean_(religion)" title="Dean (religion)">Dean</a> of <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Church,_Leuven" title="St. Peter's Church, Leuven">St. Peter's</a> in 1498. In the latter function he was permanent vice-chancellor of the University and de facto in charge of hiring. His lectures were published, as recreated from his students' notes; among those who attended was the young <a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">Erasmus</a>. Adrian offered him a professorate in 1502, but Erasmus refused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martens_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-martens-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1506 <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Austria,_Duchess_of_Savoy" title="Margaret of Austria, Duchess of Savoy">Margaret of Austria, Duchess of Savoy</a> became <a href="/wiki/List_of_governors_of_the_Habsburg_Netherlands" title="List of governors of the Habsburg Netherlands">Governess of the Habsburg Netherlands</a> and chose Adrian as her advisor. The next year Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> appointed him also <a href="/wiki/Tutor" title="Tutor">tutor</a> to his seven-year-old grandson, and Margaret's nephew, Charles, who in 1519 would become Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>. By 1512 Adrian was Charles's advisor and his court obligations were so time consuming that he quit his positions at the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martens_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-martens-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1515, Charles sent Adrian to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to convince his maternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>, that the Spanish lands should come under his rule, and not Charles's Spanish-born younger brother <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand</a>, whom his grandfather had in mind. Adrian succeeded in that just before Ferdinand's death in January 1516.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martens_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-martens-4">[4]</a></sup> Ferdinand of Aragon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and subsequently Charles V, appointed Adrian <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Tortosa" title="Bishop of Tortosa">Bishop of Tortosa</a>, which was approved by Pope Leo X on 18 August 1516.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was consecrated by Bishop Diego Ribera de Toledo.</p>
<p>On 14 November 1516 the King commissioned him <a href="/wiki/Inquisitor" title="Inquisitor">Inquisitor</a> General of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>.</p>
<p>In his fifth Consistory for the creation of cardinals, on 1 July 1517, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a> (1513‚Äì21) named thirty-one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinals</a> among whom was Adrianus de Traiecto,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loughlin_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup> naming him Cardinal Priest of the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">Basilica</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santi_Giovanni_e_Paolo,_Rome" title="Santi Giovanni e Paolo, Rome">Saints John and Paul</a> on the Coelian Hill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the minority of Charles V, Adrian was named to serve with Cardinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Jimenez_de_Cisneros" title="Francisco Jimenez de Cisneros">Francisco Jimenez de Cisneros</a> as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_regents" title="List of Spanish regents">co-regent of Spain</a>. After the death of Jimenez, Adrian was appointed (14 March 1518) General of the Reunited <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisitions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragon</a>, in which capacity he acted until his departure for <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-13">[13]</a></sup> When Charles V left Spain for the Netherlands in 1520, he appointed Cardinal Adrian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regent_of_Spain" title="Regent of Spain">Regent of Spain</a>, during which time he had to deal with the <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Comuneros" title="Revolt of the Comuneros">Revolt of the Comuneros</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papal_election">Papal election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Papal election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the conclave after the death of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>, Leo's cousin, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Giulio de' Medici</a>, was the leading figure. With Spanish and French cardinals in a deadlock, the absent Adrian was proposed as a compromise and on 9 January 1522 he was elected by an almost unanimous vote. Charles V was delighted upon hearing that his tutor had been elected to the papacy but soon realised that Adrian VI was determined to reign impartially. <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a>, who feared that Adrian would become a tool of the Emperor, and had uttered threats of a schism, later relented and sent an embassy to present his homage.</p>
<p>Fears of a Spanish Avignon based on the strength of his relationship with the Emperor as his former tutor and regent proved baseless, and Adrian, having notified the College of Cardinals of his acceptance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> left for Italy after six months of preparations and trying to decide which route to take, making his solemn entry into Rome on 29 August. He had forbidden elaborate decorations, and many people stayed away for fear of the plague that was raging. Pope Adrian was crowned at <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> on 31 August 1522, at the age of 63.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reformer">Reformer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reformer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He immediately entered upon the path of the reformer. The 1908 edition of the <i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i> characterised the task that faced him:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"To extirpate inveterate abuses; to reform a court which thrived on corruption, and detested the very name of reform; to hold in leash young and warlike princes, ready to bound at each other's throats; to stem the rising torrent of revolt in Germany; to save Christendom from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a>, who from <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a> now threatened <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, and if <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> fell would be masters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> - these were herculean labours for one who was in his sixty-third year, had never seen Italy, and was sure to be despised by the Romans as a 'barbarian'.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loughlin_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-loughlin-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>His plan was to attack notorious abuses one by one; however, in his attempt to improve the system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indulgences" title="Indulgences">indulgences</a> he was hampered by his cardinals. He found reduction of the number of <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(canon_law)" title="Dispensation (canon law)">matrimonial dispensations</a> to be impossible, as the income had been farmed out for years in advance by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Papacy">Papacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Papacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Adrian VI was not successful as a peacemaker among Christian <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">princes</a>, whom he hoped to unite in a war against the Turks. In August 1523 he was forced into an alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a> against <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>; meanwhile, in 1522 the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suleiman_I" title="Suleiman I">Suleiman I</a> (1520‚Äì66) had conquered <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>.</p>
<p>In his reaction to the early stages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> revolt, Adrian VI did not completely understand the gravity of the situation. At the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diet of Nuremberg</a>, which opened in December 1522, he was represented by Francesco Chiericati, whose private instructions contain the frank admission that the disorder of the Church was perhaps the fault of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a> itself, and that it should be reformed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, the former professor and Inquisitor General was strongly opposed to any change in doctrine and demanded that <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> be punished for teaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">heresy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>He made only one cardinal in the course of his pontificate, <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_Enckevoirt" title="Willem van Enckevoirt">Willem van Enckevoirt</a>, made a cardinal priest in a <a href="/wiki/Papal_consistory" title="Papal consistory">consistory</a> held on September 10, 1523.</p>
<p>Adrian VI held no beatifications in his pontificate but canonized Saints <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_of_Florence" title="Antoninus of Florence">Antoninus of Florence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benno" title="Benno">Benno of Meissen</a> on 31 May 1523.</p>
<p>Charles V's ambassador in Rome, Juan Manuel, lord of Belmonte, wrote that he was worried that Charles's influence over Adrian waned after Adrian's election, writing " The Pope is "deadly" afraid of the College of Cardinals. He does whatever two or three cardinals write to him in the name of the college."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adrian VI died in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> on 14 September 1523, after one year, eight months and six days as pope. Most of his official papers were lost after his death. He published <i>Quaestiones in quartum sententiarum praesertim circa sacramenta</i> (Paris, 1512, 1516, 1518, 1537; Rome, 1522), and <i>Quaestiones quodlibeticae XII.</i> (1st ed., Leuven, 1515). He is buried in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_dell%27Anima" title="Santa Maria dell'Anima">Santa Maria dell'Anima</a> church in Rome.</p>
<p>He bequeathed property in the Low Countries for the foundation of a college at the University of Leuven that became known as <a href="/wiki/Pope%27s_College,_Leuven" title="Pope's College, Leuven">Pope's College</a>.</p>
<p>The pope was mocked by the people of Rome on the <a href="/wiki/Pasquino" title="Pasquino">Pasquino</a>, and the Romans, who had never taken a liking to a man they saw as a "barbarian", rejoiced at his death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pope_Adrian_VI_in_popular_culture">Pope Adrian VI in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Adrian_VI&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pope Adrian VI in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The first series of engravings used to educate Dutch school children at the turn of the 18th century includes Adrian VI in its woodcut on 'Famous Dutch Men and Women' with the following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">poem</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>In Utrecht wijst men nog dit huis den vreemdeling aan,</i></dd>
<dd><i>En noemt het om zijn naam 't huis van Paus Adriaan,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Nog praalt 's mans borstbeeld in den gevel. Min verheven</i></dd>
<dd><i>Was 't het stamhuis van dien Paus, een schuitemakers zoon,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Zijn naam blijft nog vol lof op duizend tongen zweeven,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Kort droeg hij, maar met roem, de pauselijke kroon.'</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><i>In Utrecht they still show the stranger this house,</i></dd>
<dd><i>And call it the house of pope Adrian,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Still his bust stands in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" title="Fa√ßade">fa√ßade</a>, only slightly elevated,</i></dd>
<dd><i>It was the birth house of this pope, the son of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boat_builder" title="Boat builder">boat builder</a>,</i></dd>
<dd><i>His name is still proudly spoken by thousands of tongues,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Short of time, but with honor, he wore the papal crown.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Pope Adrian VI was a character in <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a>'s theatre play <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Faustus_(play)" title="Doctor Faustus (play)">The Tragical History of Doctor Faustus</a></i> (published 1604).</p>
<p>Italian writer <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Malerba" title="Luigi Malerba">Luigi Malerba</a> used the confusion among the leaders of the Catholic Church, which was created by Adrian's unexpected election, as a backdrop for his 1995 novel, <i>Le maschere</i> (The Masks), about the struggle between two Roman cardinals for a well-endowed church office.</p>
<p>In an episode of the American TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i> entitled <i>Divorce</i>, a homeless man believes he is Pope Adrian VI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
