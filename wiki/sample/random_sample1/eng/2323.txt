<p><b>Pope Hormisdas</b> (450 – 6 August 523) was <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> from 20 July 514 to his death in 523.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce-hormisdas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ce-hormisdas-2">[2]</a></sup> His papacy was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Acacian_schism" title="Acacian schism">Acacian schism</a>, started in 484 by <a href="/wiki/Acacius_of_Constantinople" title="Acacius of Constantinople">Acacius of Constantinople</a>'s efforts to placate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophysites" title="Monophysites">Monophysites</a>. His efforts to resolve this schism were successful, and on 28 March 519, the reunion between Constantinople and Rome was ratified in the cathedral of Constantinople before a large crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ce-hormisdas_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ce-hormisdas-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jeffrey Richards explains Hormisdas' <a href="/wiki/Middle_Persian" title="Middle Persian">Persian</a> name as probably in honour of an exiled Persian noble, <a href="/wiki/Hormizd_(Constantinople)" title="Hormizd (Constantinople)">Hormizd</a>, "celebrated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_martyrology" title="Roman martyrology">Roman martyrology</a> (8 August) but not so honoured in the East." The names of his father and son suggest he had an otherwise "straightforward Italian pedigree."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranica" title="Iranica">Iranica</a> he was probably related to Hormizd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (August 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Pope_Hormisdas#Dubious" title="Talk:Pope Hormisdas">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pope_Hormisdas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born at <a href="/wiki/Frosinone" title="Frosinone">Frosinone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campagna_di_Roma" title="Campagna di Roma">Campagna di Roma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Before becoming a <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacon</a>, Hormisdas was married, and his son would in turn become Pope under the name of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Silverius" title="Pope Silverius">Silverius</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Laurentius" title="Antipope Laurentius">Laurentian schism</a>, Hormisdas was one of the most prominent clerical partisans of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Symmachus" title="Pope Symmachus">Pope Symmachus</a>. He was notary at the synod held at <a href="/wiki/Old_St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Old St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's</a> in 502.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Two letters of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Felix_Ennodius" title="Magnus Felix Ennodius">Magnus Felix Ennodius</a>, bishop of Pavia, survive addressed to him, written when the latter tried to regain horses and money he had lent the Pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike his predecessor Symmachus, his election lacked any notable controversies. Upon becoming Pope, one of Hormisdas' first actions was to remove the last vestiges of the schism in Rome, receiving back into the Church those adherents of the Laurentian party who had not already been reconciled. "The schism had lingered on largely out of personal hatred to Symmachus," writes Jeffrey Richards, "something with which Hormisdas was apparently not tainted."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The account of his tenure in the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Pontificalis" title="Liber Pontificalis">Liber Pontificalis</a></i>, as well as the overwhelming bulk of his surviving correspondence, is dominated by efforts to restore communion between the Sees of Rome and Constantinople caused by the <a href="/wiki/Acacian_schism" title="Acacian schism">Acacian schism</a>. This schism was the consequence of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henoticon" title="Henoticon">Henoticon</a>" of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Zeno_(emperor)" title="Zeno (emperor)">Zeno</a> and supported by his successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anastasius_I_(emperor)" title="Anastasius I (emperor)">Anastasius</a>, who became more and more inclined towards <a href="/wiki/Monophysitism" title="Monophysitism">Monophysitism</a> and persecuted those bishops who refused to repudiate the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a>.</p>
<p>The emperor Anastasius took the first steps to resolve this schism pressured by <a href="/wiki/Vitalian_(general)" title="Vitalian (general)">Vitalian</a>, the commander of the imperial cavalry, who, having taken up the cause of orthodoxy, led <a href="/wiki/Thracia" title="Thracia">Thracia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scythia_Minor" title="Scythia Minor">Scythia Minor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mysia" title="Mysia">Mysia</a> to revolt, and marched with an army of Huns and Bulgarians to the gates of Constantinople. Richards points out that there would bound to be some tentative efforts from Constantinople, "if only because there was a new man on the throne of St. Peter. Relations between Symmachus and the emperor Anastasius had been virtually non-existent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anastasius wrote to Hormisdas on 28 December 514, inviting him to a synod that would be held 1 July of the following year. A second, less courteous invitation, dated 12 January 515, was also sent by Anastasius to the pope, which reached Rome before the first. On 4 April Hormisdas answered, expressing his delight at the prospect of peace, but at the same time defending the position of his predecessors and welcoming a synod, but believing it unnecessary. The bearers of the emperor's first letter at last reached Rome on 14 May. The pope guardedly carried on negotiations, convened a synod at Rome and wrote to the emperor on 8 July to announce the departure of an embassy for Constantinople. Meanwhile, the two hundred bishops who had assembled on 1 July at <a href="/wiki/Marmara_Ere%C4%9Flisi" title="Marmara Ereğlisi">Heraclea</a> separated without accomplishing anything.</p>

<p>The pope's embassy to the imperial court consisted of two bishops, Ennodius of Pavia and Fortunatus of Catina, the priest Venantius, the deacon Vitalis, and the notary Hilarius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiberP_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiberP-9">[9]</a></sup> According to Rev. J. Barmby, Hormisdas made several demands: (1) The emperor should publicly announce his acceptance of the Council of Chalcedon and the letters of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Pope Leo</a>; (2) the Eastern bishops should make a similar public declaration, and in addition anathematizse <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">Nestorius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eutyches" title="Eutyches">Eutyches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Dioscorus" title="Antipope Dioscorus">Dioscorus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aelurus" title="Aelurus">Aelurus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Mongus" title="Peter Mongus">Peter Mongus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Fuller" title="Peter the Fuller">Peter the Fuller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acacius_of_Constantinople" title="Acacius of Constantinople">Acacius</a>, with all their followers; (3) everyone exiled in this dispute should be recalled and their cases reserved for the judgment of the Apostolic See; (4) those exiles who had been in communion with Rome and professed <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> should first be recalled; and (5) bishops accused of having persecuted the Orthodox should be sent to Rome to be judged. "Thus the emperor proposed a free discussion in council; the pope required the unqualified acceptance of orthodoxy, and submission to himself as head of Christendom, before he would treat at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>An imperial embassy of two high civil officials came to Rome bringing one letter dated 16 July 516 for the pope, and one dated 28 July for the Roman Senate; the aim of the latter was to convince the senators to take a stand against Hormisdas. However both the Senate, as well as King <a href="/wiki/Theodoric" title="Theodoric">Theodoric</a>, stayed loyal to the pope. Meanwhile, Hormisdas reported to <a href="/wiki/Avitus_of_Vienne" title="Avitus of Vienne">Avitus of Vienne</a> that an additional number of Balkan bishops had entered into relations with Rome, and Bishop <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_of_Nicopolis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John of Nicopolis (page does not exist)">John of Nicopolis</a>, who was also the archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a>, had broken communion with Constantinople and resumed it with Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second papal embassy consisting of Ennodius and Bishop Peregrinus of Misenum was as unsuccessful as the first. Anastasius even attempted to bribe the legates, but was unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiberP_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-LiberP-9">[9]</a></sup> Secure now that Vitalian had been defeated outside Constantinople, forced into hiding, and his supporters executed, Anastasius announced on 11 July 517 that he was breaking off the negotiations. But less than a year later the emperor died; the <i>Liber Pontificalis</i> claims he was struck dead by a thunderbolt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiberP_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-LiberP-9">[9]</a></sup> His successor, the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Justin_I" title="Justin I">Justin I</a>, immediately reversed Anastasius' policies. All the demands of Pope Hormisdas were granted: the name of the condemned Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Acacius_of_Constantinople" title="Acacius of Constantinople">Acacius</a> as well as the names of the Emperors Anastasius and Zeno were stricken from the church diptychs, and the <a href="/wiki/John_of_Cappadocia" title="John of Cappadocia">Patriarch John II</a> accepted the formula of Hormisdas. Some maintain that he did so with some qualifications. This argument is based on the following quote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"I declare that the see of apostle Peter and the see of this imperial city are one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Catholics hold that the only thing this quote means is that the communion between the two Sees was renewed. According to the New Testament Christ and the members of his Church are mystically united but this does not mean that they have an equal status. This is not to say that the relationship between Rome and other churches is that of Christ to the members of his body; the point is that since unity is not the same as equality, this quote cannot be the basis for claiming the equality of the two Sees. <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2013)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, despite it being one of the demands in the formula, the East continued to disregard papal demands by not condemning Acacius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989215_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989215-13">[13]</a></sup> On 28 March 519, in the cathedral of Constantinople in presence of a great throng of people, the end of the schism was concluded in a solemn ceremony.</p>
