<p>The <b>former eastern territories of Germany</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Ehemalige deutsche Ostgebiete</i></span>) are those <a href="/wiki/Province" title="Province">provinces</a> or <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">regions</a> east of the current eastern border of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Oder%E2%80%93Neisse_line" title="Oder–Neisse line">Oder–Neisse line</a>) which were lost by Germany after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and then <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The territories lost following World War I include most of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Province of Posen</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a>, and further territories lost after World War II include <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Farther_Pomerania" title="Farther Pomerania">Farther Pomerania</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Brandenburg" title="East Brandenburg">East Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>, and almost all of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Silesia" title="Lower Silesia">Lower Silesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> All territories lost in both World Wars account for 33% of the former <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, while land ceded by Germany after World War II constituted roughly 25% of its <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">pre-war Weimar territory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In present-day Germany, the term usually refers only to the territories lost in World War II,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while in Poland the territories acquired from Germany after World War II were dubbed the "<a href="/wiki/Recovered_Territories" title="Recovered Territories">Recovered Territories</a>" by the Soviet-installed Polish government.</p>
<p>The post-war border between Germany and Poland along the <a href="/wiki/Oder%E2%80%93Neisse_line" title="Oder–Neisse line">Oder–Neisse line</a> was formally recognized by <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> in 1950 by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Zgorzelec" title="Treaty of Zgorzelec">Treaty of Zgorzelec</a>, under pressure from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin" title="Stalin">Stalin</a>. In 1952, recognition of the Oder–Neisse line as a permanent boundary was one of Stalin's conditions for the Soviet Union to agree to a reunification of Germany (see <a href="/wiki/Stalin_Note" title="Stalin Note">Stalin Note</a>). The offer was rejected by West German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>. The official German government position on the status of former eastern territories of Germany vacated by settled German communities east of the Oder and Neisse rivers was that the areas were "temporarily under Polish [or Soviet] administration." In 1970, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> recognised the line as a <i>de facto</i> boundary in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Warsaw_(1970)" title="Treaty of Warsaw (1970)">Treaty of Warsaw</a>.</p>
<p>In 1990, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reunification_of_Germany" title="Reunification of Germany">reunification of Germany</a>, West Germany accepted clauses in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_With_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement With Respect to Germany">Treaty on the Final Settlement With Respect to Germany</a> whereby Germany renounced all claims to territory east of the Oder-Neisse line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Germany's recognition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_line#Recognition_of_the_border_by_Germany" title="Oder-Neisse line">Oder-Neisse line as the border</a> was formalised by the re-united <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Polish_Border_Treaty_(1990)" title="German-Polish Border Treaty (1990)">German-Polish Border Treaty</a> on November 14, 1990; and by the repeal of Article 23 of the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany</a> under which German states outside the Federal Republic could formerly apply for admission. With this repeal the post-1990 boundaries of Germany are closed to further expansion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_line" title="Oder-Neisse line">Oder-Neisse line</a> at <a href="/wiki/Usedom" title="Usedom">Usedom</a></div>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a> the description of the territories transferred is "The former German territories east of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_line" title="Oder-Neisse line">Oder-Neisse line</a>", and permutations on this description are the most commonly used to describe any former territories of Germany east of the Oder-Neisse line.</p>
<p>The name <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, a political term, used to be the common colloquial English name for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR), and mirrored the common colloquial English term for the other German state of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>. When focusing on the period before World War II, "eastern Germany" is used to describe all the territories east of the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> (<a href="/wiki/East_Elbia" title="East Elbia">East Elbia</a>), as reflected in the works of sociologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> and political theorist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schmitt" title="Carl Schmitt">Carl Schmitt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but because of the border changes in the 20th century, after World War II the term "east Germany" and eastern Germany in English has meant the territory of the German Democratic Republic.</p>
<p>In German there is only one usual term,<i>Ostdeutschland</i>, meaning East Germany or Eastern Germany. The rather ambiguous German term never gained prevailing use for the GDR as did the English term. Since <i>Ostdeutschland</i> has been used to denote the post-war and the respective five <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">states</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">reunited Germany</a>. However, because people and institutions in the states, traditionally considered as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Germany" title="Middle Germany">Middle Germany</a>, like the three southern new states <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Saxony" title="Free State of Saxony">Free State of Saxony</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Thuringia" title="Free State of Thuringia">Free State of Thuringia</a>, still use the term Middle Germany when referring to their area and its institutions, the term <i>Ostdeutschland</i> is still ambiguous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_of_German_Empire.2C_1871">Foundation of German Empire, 1871</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foundation of German Empire, 1871">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acprussiamap2.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="357" data-file-width="510" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Acprussiamap2.gif/220px-Acprussiamap2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Acprussiamap2.gif/330px-Acprussiamap2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Acprussiamap2.gif/440px-Acprussiamap2.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>At the time of the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> was the largest and dominant part of the empire. Prussian territory east of the Oder-Neisse line included <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Posen</a> (taken by Prussia in the first two <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a> in the 18th century), also <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Brandenburg" title="East Brandenburg">East Brandenburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomerania</a>. Later, these territories would come to be called in Germany <i>"Ostgebiete des deutschen Reiches"</i> (Eastern territories of the German Empire).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_of_Versailles.2C_1919">Treaty of Versailles, 1919</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treaty of Versailles, 1919">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> of 1919 that ended <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> restored the independence of Poland, known as the <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Second Polish Republic</a>, and Germany was compelled to cede territories to it, most of which were taken by Prussia in the three Partitions of Poland, and had been part of the Kingdom of Prussia and later the German Empire for over 100 years. The territories ceded to Poland in 1919 were those with an apparent Polish majority, such as the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Province of Posen</a>, the east-southern part of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Corridor" title="Polish Corridor">Polish Corridor</a>.</p>
<p>Land transfers in Central Europe as a result of Versailles included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Most of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Poland" title="Greater Poland">Greater Poland</a> ("Province of Posen") and <a href="/wiki/Pomerelia" title="Pomerelia">Pomerelia</a> (parts of <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a>), mostly what the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> had taken in the Partitions of Poland was handed over to Poland after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Poland_Uprising_(1918%E2%80%931919)" title="Greater Poland Uprising (1918–1919)">Greater Poland Uprising</a> (this land comprised an area of 53,800 km<sup>2</sup> 4,224,000 inhabitants (1931) including 510 km<sup>2</sup> and 26,000 inhabitants from <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Versailles1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Versailles1-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hlu%C4%8D%C3%ADn_Area" title="Hlučín Area">Hlučín Area</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moravian-Silesian_Region" title="Moravian-Silesian Region">Moravian-Silesian Region</a> to <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> (316 or 333 km<sup>2</sup> and 49,000 people),</li>
<li>The eastern part of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Katowice" title="Katowice">Katowice</a>), to Poland (area 3,214 km<sup>2</sup> and 965,000 people),</li>
<li>The northeastern part of East Prussia, named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memel_Territory" title="Memel Territory">Memel Territory</a>, which was placed under the control of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (and was later annexed by Lithuania, as the <a href="/wiki/Klaip%C4%97da_Region" title="Klaipėda Region">Klaipėda Region</a>),</li>
<li>The area of <a href="/wiki/Dzia%C5%82dowo" title="Działdowo">Działdowo (Soldau)</a> (area 492 km<sup>2</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) to Poland; a few villages in the eastern part of <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a> and in the southern part of <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Warmia" title="Warmia">Warmia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Masuria" title="Masuria">Masuria</a>) to Poland after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Prussian_plebiscite" title="East Prussian plebiscite">East Prussian plebiscite</a>.</li>
<li>The city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danzig" title="Danzig">Danzig</a> (Polish Gdańsk) with the delta of the <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> river at the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, was made the <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_Danzig" title="Free City of Danzig">Free City of Danzig</a> under the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> and partially Polish authority (area 1893 km<sup>2</sup>, 408,000 inhabitants 1929).</li>
</ul>

<p>In October 1938 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hlu%C4%8D%C3%ADn_Area" title="Hlučín Area">Hlučín Area</a> (<i>Hlučínsko</i> in Czech, <i>Hultschiner Ländchen</i> in German) of <a href="/wiki/Moravian-Silesian_Region" title="Moravian-Silesian Region">Moravian-Silesian Region</a> which had been ceded to Czechoslovakia under the Treaty of Versailles was annexed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> as a part of areas lost by Czechoslovakia in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich_agreement" title="Munich agreement">Munich agreement</a>. However, as distinct from other lost Czechoslovakian domains, it was not attached to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sudetengau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sudetengau (page does not exist)">Sudetengau</a> (administrative region covering <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a>) but to Prussia (<a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>).</p>
<p>By late 1938, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> had lost control over the situation in the <a href="/wiki/Klaip%C4%97da_Region" title="Klaipėda Region">Memel Territory</a>. In the early hours of 23 March 1939, after a political ultimatum caused a Lithuanian delegation to travel to Berlin, the Lithuanian Minister of Foreign Affairs <a href="/wiki/Juozas_Urb%C5%A1ys" title="Juozas Urbšys">Juozas Urbšys</a> and his German counterpart <a href="/wiki/Joachim_von_Ribbentrop" title="Joachim von Ribbentrop">Joachim von Ribbentrop</a> signed the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_the_Cession_of_the_Memel_Territory_to_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Treaty of the Cession of the Memel Territory to Germany (page does not exist)">Treaty of the Cession of the Memel Territory to Germany</a></i> in exchange for a Lithuanian Free Zone in the port of Memel, using the facilities erected in previous years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_occupation_of_Poland_in_World_War_II.2C_1939.E2.80.931945">German occupation of Poland in World War II, 1939–1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German occupation of Poland in World War II, 1939–1945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Germany1941.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="698" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Germany1941.png/220px-Germany1941.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Germany1941.png/330px-Germany1941.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Germany1941.png/440px-Germany1941.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Germany1941.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Reichsgaue in 1941</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polish_areas_annexed_by_Nazi_Germany" title="Polish areas annexed by Nazi Germany">Polish areas annexed by Nazi Germany</a></div>
<p>Between the two world wars, many in Germany claimed that the territory ceded to Poland in 1919–1922 should be returned to Germany. This claim was one of the justifications for the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland_(1939)" title="Invasion of Poland (1939)">invasion of Poland in 1939</a>, heralding the start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. The Third Reich annexed the former German lands, comprising the "<a href="/wiki/Polish_Corridor" title="Polish Corridor">Polish Corridor</a>", <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Province of Posen</a>, and parts of eastern <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>. The council of the <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_Danzig" title="Free City of Danzig">Free City of Danzig</a> voted to become a part of Germany again, although <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> were deprived of their voting rights and all non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Socialist_German_Workers_Party" title="National Socialist German Workers Party">Nazi</a> political parties were banned. In addition to taking territories lost in 1919, Germany also took additional land that had never been German.</p>
<p>Two decrees by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> (October 8 and October 12, 1939) divided the annexed areas of Poland into administrative units:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reichsgau_Wartheland" title="Reichsgau Wartheland">Reichsgau Wartheland</a> (initially <a href="/wiki/Reichsgau" title="Reichsgau">Reichsgau</a> Posen), which included the entire <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84_Voivodeship" title="Poznań Voivodeship">Poznań Voivodeship</a>, most of the <a href="/wiki/%C5%81%C3%B3d%C5%BA_Voivodeship" title="Łódź Voivodeship">Łódź Voivodeship</a>, five counties of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pomeranian_Voivodeship_(1919%E2%80%931939)" title="Pomeranian Voivodeship (1919–1939)">Pomeranian Voivodeship</a>, and one county of the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Voivodeship_(1919%E2%80%9339)" title="Warsaw Voivodeship (1919–39)">Warszawa Voivodeship</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reichsgau_Danzig-West_Prussia" title="Reichsgau Danzig-West Prussia">Reichsgau Danzig-West Prussia</a> (initially Reichsgau West Prussia), which consisted of the remaining area of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pomeranian_Voivodeship_(1919%E2%80%931939)" title="Pomeranian Voivodeship (1919–1939)">Pomeranian Voivodeship</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_Danzig" title="Free City of Danzig">Free City of Danzig</a>;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciechan%C3%B3w_District" title="Ciechanów District">Ciechanów District</a> (<a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a> Zichenau), consisting of the five northern counties of <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Voivodeship_(1919%E2%80%9339)" title="Warsaw Voivodeship (1919–39)">Warszawa Voivodeship</a> (<a href="/wiki/P%C5%82ock" title="Płock">Płock</a>, <a href="/wiki/P%C5%82o%C5%84sk" title="Płońsk">Płońsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierpc" title="Sierpc">Sierpc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ciechan%C3%B3w" title="Ciechanów">Ciechanów</a>, and <a href="/wiki/M%C5%82awa" title="Mława">Mława</a>), which became a part of East Prussia;</li>
<li>Katowice District (Regierungsbezirk Kattowitz), or <a href="/wiki/East_Upper_Silesia" title="East Upper Silesia">East Upper Silesia</a> (<i>Ost-Oberschlesien</i>), which included <a href="/wiki/Sosnowiec" title="Sosnowiec">Sosnowiec</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C4%99dzin" title="Będzin">Będzin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrzan%C3%B3w" title="Chrzanów">Chrzanów</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zawiercie" title="Zawiercie">Zawiercie</a> Counties, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Olkusz" title="Olkusz">Olkusz</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%BBywiec" title="Żywiec">Żywiec</a> Counties.</li>
</ul>
<p>These territories had an area of 94,000 km<sup>2</sup> and a population of 10,000,000 people.</p>
<p>The remainder of Polish territory was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molotov-Ribbentrop_Pact" title="Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact">Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact</a>) or made into the German-controlled <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government</a> occupation zone.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">German attack on the Soviet Union</a> in June 1941, the <a href="/wiki/Bezirk_Bialystok" title="Bezirk Bialystok">district of Białystok</a>, which included the <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82ystok" title="Białystok">Białystok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bielsk_Podlaski" title="Bielsk Podlaski">Bielsk Podlaski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grajewo" title="Grajewo">Grajewo</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%81om%C5%BCa" title="Łomża">Łomża</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sok%C3%B3%C5%82ka" title="Sokółka">Sokółka</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volkovysk" title="Volkovysk">Volkovysk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grodno" title="Grodno">Grodno</a> Counties, was "attached to" (not incorporated into) East Prussia, whilst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Galicia" title="East Galicia">East Galicia</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distrikt_Galizien" title="Distrikt Galizien">Distrikt Galizien</a>), which included the cities of <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lwów</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivano-Frankivsk" title="Ivano-Frankivsk">Stanislawów</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ternopil" title="Ternopil">Tarnopol</a>, was made part of the General Government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Yalta_Conference">The Yalta Conference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Yalta Conference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The final decision to move <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>'s boundary westward was made by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Soviets" title="The Soviets">the Soviets</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Conference</a> in February 1945, shortly before the end of the war. The precise location of the border was left open; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Allies" title="Western Allies">western Allies</a> also accepted in general the principle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder_River" title="Oder River">Oder River</a> as the future western border of Poland and of <a href="/wiki/Population_transfer" title="Population transfer">population transfer</a> as the way to prevent future border disputes. The open question was whether the border should follow the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Neisse" title="Eastern Neisse">Eastern</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_Neisse" title="Lusatian Neisse">Lusatian Neisse</a> rivers, and whether <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stettin" title="Stettin">Stettin</a>, the traditional seaport of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, should remain German or be included in Poland.</p>
<p>Originally, Germany was to retain Stettin while the Poles were to annex <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a> with <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biuletyn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-biuletyn-12">[12]</a></sup> Eventually, however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin" title="Stalin">Stalin</a> decided that he wanted Königsberg as a year-round <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm_water_port" title="Warm water port">warm water port</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a> and argued that the Poles should receive Stettin instead. The wartime <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_government_in_exile" title="Polish government in exile">Polish government in exile</a> had little to say in these decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biuletyn_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-biuletyn-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Poland_(1945).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="513" data-file-width="594" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png/220px-Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png/330px-Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png/440px-Map_of_Poland_%281945%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Poland_(1945).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Poland's old and new borders, 1945</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the Yalta Conference, it was agreed to split Germany into four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupation_zone" title="Occupation zone">occupation zones</a> after the war, with a quadripartite occupation of Berlin as well, prior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Re-unification_of_Germany" title="Re-unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>. The status of Poland was discussed, but was complicated by the fact that Poland was at this time under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>. It was agreed to reorganize the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisionary_Polish_Government" title="Provisionary Polish Government">Provisionary Polish Government</a> that had been set up by the Red Army through the inclusion of other groups such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Provisional_Government_of_National_Unity" title="Polish Provisional Government of National Unity">Polish Provisional Government of National Unity</a> and to have democratic elections. This effectively excluded the <a href="/wiki/Polish_government-in-exile" title="Polish government-in-exile">Polish government-in-exile</a> that had evacuated in 1939. It was agreed that the Polish eastern border would follow the <a href="/wiki/Curzon_Line" title="Curzon Line">Curzon Line</a>, and Poland would receive substantial territorial compensation in the west from Germany, although the exact border was to be determined at a later time. A "Committee on Dismemberment of Germany" was to be set up. The purpose was to decide whether Germany was to be divided into six nations, and if so, what borders and inter-relationships the new German states were to have.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potsdam_Agreement.2C_1945">Potsdam Agreement, 1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Potsdam Agreement, 1945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a></div>
<p>Germany subsequently lost territories east of the Oder-Neisse Line at the end of the War in 1945, when international recognition of its right to jurisdiction over any of these territories was conditionally withdrawn. The "condition" mentioned was the Final German Peace Treaty, which was to set the actual border line, which may or may not have been the Oder-Neisse line. At Potsdam, the assumption by many was that a Final German Peace Treaty was imminent, but this turned out to be incorrect.</p>
<p>After World War II, as agreed at the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> (which met from 17 July until 2 August 1945), all of the areas east of the Oder-Neisse line, whether recognised by the international community as part of Germany until 1939 or occupied by Germany during World War II, were placed under the jurisdiction of other countries.The relevant paragraphs in the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a> are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><b>V. City of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koenigsberg" title="Koenigsberg">Koenigsberg</a> and the adjacent area.</b></p>
<p><br/>
The Conference examined a proposal by the Soviet Government to the effect that pending the final determination of territorial questions at the peace settlement, the section of the western frontier of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics which is adjacent to the Baltic Sea should pass from a point on the eastern shore of the Bay of Danzig to the east, north of Braunsberg-Goldap, to the meeting point of the frontiers of Lithuania, the Polish Republic and East Prussia.<br/>
The Conference has agreed in principle to the proposal of the Soviet Government concerning the ultimate transfer to the Soviet Union of the City of Koenigsberg and the area adjacent to it as described above subject to expert examination of the actual frontier.<br/>
The President of the United States and the British Prime Minister have declared that they will support the proposal of the Conference at the forthcoming peace settlement.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><b>VIII. Poland.</b></p>
<p><br/>
...<br/>
The British and United States Governments have taken measures to protect the interest of the Polish Provisional Government of National Unity as the recognized government of the Polish State in the property belonging to the Polish State located in their territories and under their control, whatever the form of this property may be.<br/>
...<br/>
In conformity with the agreement on Poland reached at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimea_Conference" title="Crimea Conference">Crimea Conference</a> the three Heads of Government have sought the opinion of the Polish Provisional Government of National Unity in regard to the accession of territory in the north and west which Poland should receive. The President of the National Council of Poland and members of the Polish Provisional Government of National Unity have been received at the Conference and have fully presented their views. The three Heads of Government reaffirm their opinion that the final <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delimitation" title="Delimitation">delimitation</a> of the western frontier of Poland should await the peace settlement.</p>
<p>The three Heads of Government agree that, pending the final determination of Poland's western frontier, the <b>former German territories</b> east of a line running from the Baltic Sea immediately west of Swinamunde, and thence along the Oder River to the confluence of the western Neisse River and along the Western Neisse to the Czechoslovak frontier, including that portion of East Prussia not placed under the administration of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics in accordance with the understanding reached at this conference and including the area of the former free city of Danzig, shall be under the administration of the Polish State and for such purposes should not be considered as part of the Soviet zone of occupation in Germany. (<i>Emphasis added</i>)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Allies also agreed that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><b>XII. Orderly transfer of German populations.</b></p>
<p><br/>
The Three Governments [of the Soviet Union, the United States and Great Britain], having considered the question in all its aspects, recognize that the transfer to Germany of German populations, or elements thereof, remaining in Poland, Czechoslovakia and Hungary, will have to be undertaken. They agree that any transfers that take place should be effected in an orderly and humane manner.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>because in the words of <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Expulsion is the method which, in so far as we have been able to see, will be the most satisfactory and lasting. There will be no mixture of populations to cause endless trouble. A clean sweep will be made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The problem with the status of these territories was that the Potsdam Agreement was not a legally binding <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a>, but a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum" title="Memorandum">memorandum</a> between the USSR, the USA and the UK. It regulated the issue of the eastern German border, which was to be the Oder-Neisse line, but the final article of the memorandum said that the final decisions concerning Germany were subject to a separate peace treaty. This treaty was signed in 1990 as the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_with_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany">Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPiotrowiczBlaySchusterZimmermann1997.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Ds82PHINkWHgC.26lpg.3DPA66.26pg.3DPA66.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_66.5D_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPiotrowiczBlaySchusterZimmermann1997.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Ds82PHINkWHgC.26lpg.3DPA66.26pg.3DPA66.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_66.5D-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_World_War_II">Post World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the War, the so-called "German question" was an important factor of post-war German and European history and politics. The debate affected <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> politics and diplomacy and played an important role in the negotiations leading up to the reunification of Germany in 1990. In 1990 Germany officially recognized its present eastern border at the time of its <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">reunification</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_with_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany">Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany</a>, ending any residual claims to sovereignty that Germany may have had over any territory east of the Oder-Neisse line.</p>
<p>Between 1945 and 1990 the government of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> referred to these territories as "former German territories temporarily under Polish and Soviet administration". This terminology was used in relation to territories of eastern Germany within the 1937 Germany border, and was based on the terminology used in the Potsdam Agreement. It was used only by the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>; but the Polish and Soviet governments objected to the obvious implication that these territories should someday revert to Germany. The Polish government preferred to use the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Recovered_Territories" title="Recovered Territories">Recovered Territories</a>", asserting a sort of continuity because parts of these territories had centuries previously been ruled by ethnic Poles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion_of_Germans_and_resettlement">Expulsion of Germans and resettlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Expulsion of Germans and resettlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Germans_after_World_War_II" title="Expulsion of Germans after World War II">Expulsion of Germans after World War II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans_from_Poland_during_and_after_World_War_II" title="Flight and expulsion of Germans from Poland during and after World War II">Flight and expulsion of Germans from Poland during and after World War II</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="432" data-file-width="600" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg/220px-Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg/330px-Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg/440px-Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marking_Polish-German_Border_in_1945.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>With the rapid advance of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> in the winter of 1944–1945, German authorities desperately evacuated many Germans to west of the Oder–Neisse line. The majority of the remaining German-speaking population east of the Oder–Neisse line (roughly 10 million in the ostgebiete alone) that had not already been evacuated was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Germans_after_World_War_II" title="Expulsion of Germans after World War II">expelled</a>. Although in the post-war period earlier German sources often cited the number of evacuated and expelled Germans at 16 million and the death toll at between 1.7<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wehler_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wehler-19">[19]</a></sup> and 2.5 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnouw_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnouw-20">[20]</a></sup> today, the numbers are considered by some historians to be exaggerated and more likely in the range between 400,000 and 600,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biess_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biess-21">[21]</a></sup> Some present-day estimates place the numbers of German refugees at 14 million of which about half a million died during the evacuations and expulsions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biess_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biess-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overmans_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Overmans-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time, Poles from central Poland, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_population_transfers_(1944%E2%80%931946)" title="Polish population transfers (1944–1946)">expelled Poles from former eastern Poland</a>, Polish returnees from internment and forced labour, Ukrainians were forcibly resettled in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vistula" title="Operation Vistula">Operation Vistula</a> and Jewish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> survivors were settled in German territories gained by Poland, whereas the north of former <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad_Oblast" title="Kaliningrad Oblast">Kaliningrad Oblast</a> gained by the USSR) was turned into a military zone and subsequently became settled with Russians.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ostpolitik">Ostpolitik</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ostpolitik">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> adopted <i><a href="/wiki/Ostpolitik" title="Ostpolitik">Ostpolitik</a></i> in foreign relations, which strove to normalise relations with its neighbours by recognising the realities of the European order of the time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENA-1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENA-1-23">[23]</a></sup> and abandoning elements of the <a href="/wiki/Hallstein_Doctrine" title="Hallstein Doctrine">Hallstein Doctrine</a>. West Germany "abandoned, at least for the time being, its claims with respect to German self-determination and reunification, recognising <i>de facto</i> the existence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR) and the Oder-Neisse line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENA-1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ENA-1-23">[23]</a></sup> As part of this new approach, West Germany concluded friendship treaties with the Soviet Union (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Moscow_(1970)" title="Treaty of Moscow (1970)">Treaty of Moscow (1970)</a>), Poland (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Warsaw_(1970)" title="Treaty of Warsaw (1970)">Treaty of Warsaw (1970)</a>), East Germany (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_Treaty_(1972)" title="Basic Treaty (1972)">Basic Treaty (1972)</a>) and Czechoslovakia (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Prague_(1973)" title="Treaty of Prague (1973)">Treaty of Prague (1973)</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_status">Present status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Present status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the last 20 years, the "German question" has been muted by a number of related phenomena<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The passage of time resulted in fewer people being left who have firsthand experience of living in these regions under German jurisdiction.</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_With_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement With Respect to Germany">Treaty on the Final Settlement With Respect to Germany</a>, Germany renounced all claims to territory east the Oder-Neisse line. Germany's recognition of the border was repeated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Polish_Border_Treaty_(1990)" title="German-Polish Border Treaty (1990)">German-Polish Border Treaty</a> on November 14, 1990. The treaties were made by both German states and ratified in 1991 by a united Germany.</li>
<li>The expansion of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> to <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> in 2004 enabled any German wishing to live and work in Poland, and thus east of the Oder-Neisse line, to do so without requiring a permit. German expellees and refugees became free to visit their former homes and set up residence, though some restrictions remained on the purchase of land and buildings.</li>
<li>Poland entered the <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area" title="Schengen Area">Schengen Area</a> on December 21, 2007, removing all border controls on its border with Germany.</li>
</ul>
<p>Under Article 1 of the Treaty on Final Settlement, the new united, Germany committed itself to renouncing any further territorial claims beyond the boundaries of East Germany, West Germany and Berlin; "The united Germany has no territorial claims whatsoever against other states and shall not assert any in the future." Furthermore, the Basic Law of the Federal Republic was required to be amended to state explicitly that full German unification had now been achieved, such that the new German state comprised the entirety of Germany, and that all constitutional mechanisms should be removed by which any territories outside those boundaries could otherwise subsequently to be admitted; these new constitutional articles being bound by treaty not to be revoked. Article 23 of the Basic Law was repealed, closing off the possibility for any further states to apply for membership of the Federal Republic; while Article 146 was amended to state explicitly that the territory of the newly unified republic comprised the entirety of the German people; "This Basic Law, which since the achievement of the unity and freedom of Germany applies to the entire German people, shall cease to apply on the day on which a constitution freely adopted by the German people takes effect". This was confirmed in the 1990 rewording of the preamble; "Germans..have achieved the unity and freedom of Germany in free self-determination. This Basic Law thus applies to the entire German people." In place of the former Article 23 (under which the states of East Germany had been admitted), a new Article 23 established the constitutional status of accession of the Federal Republic to the European Union; hence with the subsequent accession of Poland to the EU, the constitutional bar on pursuing any claim to territories beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_Line" title="Oder-Neisse Line">Oder-Neisse Line</a> was reinforced. In so far as the former <a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a> may be claimed to continue in existence within 'Germany as a whole', former eastern German territories in Poland and Russia are now definitively and permanently excluded from ever again being united with Germany.</p>
<p>In the course of the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> accepted the territorial changes made after World War II, creating some outrage among the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Expellees" title="Federation of Expellees">Federation of Expellees</a>, while some Poles were concerned about a possible revival of their 1939 trauma through a "second German invasion", this time with the Germans buying back their land, which was cheaply available at the time. This happened on a smaller scale than many Poles expected, and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> coast of Poland has become a popular German tourist destination. The so-called "homesickness-tourism" which was often perceived as quite aggressive well into the 1990s now tends to be viewed as a good-natured nostalgia tour rather than an expression of anger and desire for the return of the lost territories<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Some organisations in Germany continue to claim the territories for Germany or property there for German citizens. The <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Trust" title="Prussian Trust">Prussian Trust</a> (or the <i>Prussian Claims Society</i>), that probably has less than a hundred members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> re-opened the old dispute when in December 2006, it submitted 23 individual claims against the Polish government to the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> asking for compensation or return of property appropriated from its members at the end of World War II. An expert report jointly commissioned by the German and Polish governments from specialists in international law have confirmed that the proposed complaints by the Prussian Trust had little hope of success. But the German government cannot prevent such requests being made and the Polish government has felt that the submissions warranted a comment by <a href="/wiki/Anna_Fotyga" title="Anna Fotyga">Anna Fotyga</a>, the Polish Minister of the Foreign Affairs to "express [her] deepest concern upon receiving the information about a claim against Poland submitted by the Prussian Trust to the European Court of Human Rights".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On 9 October 2008 the European Court of Human Rights declared the case of <i>Preussische Treuhand v. Poland</i> inadmissible, because the European Convention on Human Rights does not impose any obligations on the Contracting States to return property which was transferred to them before they ratified the Convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a>, described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Nazi" title="Neo-Nazi">neo-Nazi</a> organisation, won six seats in the parliament of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a> in September 2006, the leader of the party, <a href="/wiki/Udo_Voigt" title="Udo Voigt">Udo Voigt</a>, declared that his party demands Germany in "historical borders" and questioned the current border treaties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_eastern_territories_in_German_history">Former eastern territories in German history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Former eastern territories in German history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The former eastern territories were the scene of numerous notable events in German history, but generally viewed in modern-day Poland as being of 'foreign' rather than local interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>These include battles such as <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a>’s victories at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mollwitz" title="Battle of Mollwitz">Mollwitz</a> in 1741, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hohenfriedberg" title="Battle of Hohenfriedberg">Hohenfriedeberg</a> in 1745, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leuthen" title="Battle of Leuthen">Leuthen</a> (1757) and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Zorndorf" title="Battle of Zorndorf">Zorndorf</a> (1758), and his defeats at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gross-J%C3%A4gersdorf" title="Battle of Gross-Jägersdorf">Gross-Jägersdorf</a> in 1757 and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kunersdorf" title="Battle of Kunersdorf">Kunersdorf</a> in 1759. Historian <a href="/wiki/Norman_Davies" title="Norman Davies">Norman Davies</a> describes Kunersdorf as "Prussia's greatest disaster" and the inspiration for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Tiedge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Christian Tiedge (page does not exist)">Christian Tiedge</a>'s <i>Elegy</i> to "Humanity butchered by Delusion on the Altar of Blood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-28">[28]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> the <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomeranian</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%82obrzeg" title="Kołobrzeg">Kolberg</a> was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kolberg_(1807)" title="Siege of Kolberg (1807)">besieged</a> in 1807 (inspiring a Second World War Nazi <a href="/wiki/Kolberg_(film)" title="Kolberg (film)">propaganda film</a>) while the French <a href="/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Grande Armée">Grande Armée</a> was victorious at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Eylau" title="Battle of Eylau">Eylau</a> in East Prussia in the same year. The <a href="/wiki/Treaties_of_Tilsit" title="Treaties of Tilsit">Treaties of Tilsit</a> were separately signed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tilsit" title="Tilsit">selfsame town</a> in July 1807 between Napoleon and the Russians and Prussians. The <a href="/wiki/Iron_Cross" title="Iron Cross">Iron Cross</a>, Germany's highest military honour, was established (though not awarded) by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III" title="Frederick William III">Frederick William III</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breslau" title="Breslau">Breslau</a> on 17 March 1813.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nungesser_1987_29_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nungesser_1987_29-29">[29]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">Hindenburg</a> won critical victories at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tannenberg_(1914)" title="Battle of Tannenberg (1914)">Tannenberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_the_Masurian_Lakes" title="First Battle of the Masurian Lakes">Masurian Lakes</a>, ejecting Russian forces from East Prussia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The problem with the status of these territories was that in 1945 the concluding document of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> was not a legally binding <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a>, but a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum" title="Memorandum">memorandum</a> between the USSR, the USA and the UK. It regulated the issue of the eastern German border, which was to be the Oder-Neisse line, but the final article of the memorandum said that the final decisions concerning Germany were subject to a separate peace treaty. This treaty was signed in 1990 under the name of <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement">Treaty on the Final Settlement</a></b> by both the German states and ratified in 1991 by the united Germany. This ended the legal <a href="/wiki/Limbo" title="Limbo">limbo</a> state which meant that for 45 years, people on both sides of the border could not be sure whether the settlement reached in 1945 might be changed at some future date.</span></li>
