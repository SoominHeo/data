<p>The <b>uvular nasal</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonantal</a> sound, used in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_communication" title="Speech communication">spoken</a> <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>. The symbol in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> that represents this sound is ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɴ</span>⟩, a <a href="/wiki/Small_caps" title="Small caps">small capital</a> version of the Latin letter <a href="/wiki/N" title="N">n</a>; the equivalent <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">X-SAMPA</a> symbol is <kbd>N\</kbd>.</p>
<p>The uvular nasal is a rare sound crosslingually, presumably due to the relative difficulty involved in articulating this sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The very small oral cavity used to produce uvular consonants makes it difficult to sustain voicing. It is also difficult to allow air to escape through the nose, as is required for a nasal consonant, while simultaneously blocking oral airflow at the uvular point of articulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The uvular nasal most commonly occurs as a conditioned allophone of other sounds in specific environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, as an allophone of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span> before a uvular plosive as in <a href="/wiki/Quechuan_languages" title="Quechuan languages">Quechua</a>. However, it occurs as an independent phoneme in a small number of languages, notably <a href="/wiki/Klallam_language" title="Klallam language">Klallam</a> and the Papuan language <a href="/wiki/Mapos_Buang_language" title="Mapos Buang language">Mapos Buang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Mapos_Buang_language" title="Mapos Buang language">Mapos Buang</a>, the uvular nasal is phonemically distinct from three other phonemic dorsal nasals: a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_nasal" title="Palatal nasal">palatal nasal</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">velar nasal</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">labialized velar nasal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> This appears to be the only language known to contain a phonemic contrast between the uvular nasal and other dorsal nasals.</p>
<p>There is also the <b>pre-uvular nasal</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in some languages such as <a href="/wiki/Yanyuwa_language" title="Yanyuwa language">Yanyuwa</a>, which is articulated slightly more front compared with the place of articulation of the prototypical uvular nasal, though not as front as the prototypical velar nasal. The International Phonetic Alphabet does not have a separate symbol for that sound, though it can be transcribed as ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɴ̟</span>⟩ (<a href="/wiki/Relative_articulation#Advanced_and_retracted" title="Relative articulation">advanced</a> ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɴ</span>⟩), ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ŋ̠</span>⟩ or ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ŋ˗</span>⟩ (both symbols denote a <a href="/wiki/Relative_articulation#Advanced_and_retracted" title="Relative articulation">retracted</a> ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ŋ</span>⟩). The equivalent X-SAMPA symbols are <kbd>N\_+</kbd> and <kbd>N_-</kbd>, respectively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uvular_nasal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Features of the uvular nasal:</p>
<ul>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">manner of articulation</a> is <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">occlusive</a>, which means it is produced by obstructing airflow in the vocal tract. Because the consonant is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal</a>, the blocked airflow is redirected through the nose.</li>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> is <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a>, which means it is articulated with the back of the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a> (the dorsum) at the <a href="/wiki/Palatine_uvula" title="Palatine uvula">uvula</a>.</li>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a> is voiced, which means the vocal cords vibrate during the articulation.</li>
<li>It is a nasal consonant, which means air is allowed to escape through the nose, either exclusively (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal stops</a>) or in addition to through the mouth.</li>
<li>Because the sound is not produced with airflow over the tongue, the <a href="/wiki/Central_consonant" title="Central consonant">central</a>–<a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> dichotomy does not apply.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">airstream mechanism</a> is <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">pulmonic</a>, which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">diaphragm</a>, as in most sounds.</li>
</ul>
