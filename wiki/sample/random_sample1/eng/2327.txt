<p>The <b>conifers</b>, division <b>Pinophyta</b>, also known as division <b>Coniferophyta</b> or <b>Coniferae</b>, are one of 12 extant <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">division</a>-level <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Kingdom Plantae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Viridiplantae" title="Viridiplantae">Viridiplantae</a>) and 10 within the extant <a href="/wiki/Embryophyte" title="Embryophyte">land plants</a>. Pinophyta contains a single <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a>, <b>Pinopsida</b>. Pinophytes are <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">cone-bearing</a> seed <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">vascular</a> tissue. All <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extant</a> conifers are <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">woody plants</a> with <a href="/wiki/Secondary_growth" title="Secondary growth">secondary growth</a>. The great majority are <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, though a few are <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">cedars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pseudotsuga" title="Pseudotsuga">Douglas firs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">cypresses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">firs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">junipers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agathis" title="Agathis">kauri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">hemlocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">redwoods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taxaceae" title="Taxaceae">yews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> As of 1998, the division was estimated to contain eight families, 68 genera, and 629 living species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the total number of species is relatively small, conifers have immense <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> importance. They are the dominant plants over huge areas of land, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> but also in similar cool climates in mountains further south. Boreal conifers have many wintertime adaptations. The narrow conical shape of northern conifers, and their downward-drooping limbs, help them shed snow. Many of them seasonally alter their biochemistry to make them more resistant to freezing. While <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a> have more <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and turnover, the immense conifer forests of the world represent the largest terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>.</p>
<p>They are also of great economic value for <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">lumber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> production;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> the wood of conifers is known as <a href="/wiki/Softwood" title="Softwood">softwood</a>.</p>
<p><i>Conifer</i> is a Latin word, a compound of <i>conus</i> (cone) and <i>ferre</i> (to bear), meaning "the one that bears (a) cone(s)".</p>

<p>The earliest conifers in the fossil record date to the late <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pennsylvanian_(geology)" title="Pennsylvanian (geology)">Pennsylvanian</a>) period (about 300 million years ago),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henry_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-henry-3">[3]</a></sup> possibly arising from <i><a href="/wiki/Cordaites" title="Cordaites">Cordaites</a></i>, a seed-bearing plant with cone-like fertile structures. This plant resembled the modern <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria" title="Araucaria">Araucaria</a></i>. Pinophytes, <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">Cycadophytes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ginkgoales" title="Ginkgoales">Ginkgophytes</a> all developed at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henry_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-henry-3">[3]</a></sup> An important adaptation of these gymnosperms was allowing plants to live without being so dependent on water. Other adaptations are pollen (so fertilization can occur without water) and the seed, which lets the embryo be transported and developed elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henry_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-henry-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conifers appear to be one of the taxa that benefited from the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, and were the dominant land plants of the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a>. They were overtaken by the flowering plants, which first appeared in the Cretaceous, and became dominant in the Cenozoic era. They were the main food of <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>, and their resins and poisons would have given protection against herbivores. <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">Reproductive features</a> of modern conifers had evolved by the end of the Mesozoic era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_naming">Taxonomy and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The division name Pinophyta conforms to the rules of the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_for_algae,_fungi,_and_plants" title="International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants">International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants</a> (ICN)</i>, which state (Article 16.1) that the names of higher <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> in plants (above the rank of family) are either formed from the name of an included family (usually the most common and/or representative), in this case <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> family), or are descriptive. A descriptive name in widespread use for the conifers (at whatever rank is chosen) is <b>Coniferae</b> (Art 16 Ex 2).</p>
<p>According to the <i>ICN</i>, it is possible to use a name formed by replacing the termination <i>-aceae</i> in the name of an included family, in this case preferably <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a>, by the appropriate termination, in the case of this division <i>‑ophyta</i>. Alternatively, "<a href="/wiki/Descriptive_botanical_names" title="Descriptive botanical names">descriptive botanical names</a>" may also be used at any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rank_(botany)" title="Rank (botany)">rank</a> above family. Both are allowed.</p>
<p>This means that if conifers are considered a division, they may be called Pinophyta or Coniferae. As a class they may be called Pinopsida or Coniferae. As an order they may be called Pinales or Coniferae or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coniferales" title="Coniferales">Coniferales</a>.</p>
<p>Conifers are the largest and economically most important component group of the gymnosperms, but nevertheless they comprise only one of the four groups. The division Pinophyta consists of just one class, Pinopsida, which includes both living and fossil taxa. Subdivision of the living conifers into two or more orders has been proposed from time to time. The most commonly seen in the past was a split into two orders, <a href="/wiki/Taxales" title="Taxales">Taxales</a> (Taxaceae only) and <a href="/wiki/Pinales" title="Pinales">Pinales</a> (the rest), but recent research into <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">DNA sequences</a> suggests that this interpretation leaves the Pinales without Taxales as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>, and the latter order is no longer considered distinct. A more accurate subdivision would be to split the class into three orders, Pinales containing only Pinaceae, Araucariales containing Araucariaceae and Podocarpaceae, and Cupressales containing the remaining families (including Taxaceae), but there has not been any significant support for such a split, with the majority of opinion preferring retention of all the families within a single order Pinales, despite their antiquity and diverse <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>.</p>

<p>The conifers are now accepted as comprising seven families,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GD_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GD-6">[6]</a></sup> with a total of 65–70 genera and 600–630 species (696 accepted names).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The seven most distinct families are linked in the box above right and phylogenetic diagram left. In other interpretations, the Cephalotaxaceae may be better included within the Taxaceae, and some authors additionally recognize <a href="/wiki/Phyllocladus" title="Phyllocladus">Phyllocladaceae</a> as distinct from Podocarpaceae (in which it is included here). The family <a href="/wiki/Taxodiaceae" title="Taxodiaceae">Taxodiaceae</a> is here included in family Cupressaceae, but was widely recognized in the past and can still be found in many field guides. A new classification and linear sequence based on molecular data can be found in an article by Christenhusz et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The conifers are an ancient group, with a <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record extending back about 300 million years to the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> in the late <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period; even many of the modern genera are recognizable from fossils 60–120 million years old. Other classes and orders, now long extinct, also occur as fossils, particularly from the late Paleozoic and <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> eras. Fossil conifers included many diverse forms, the most dramatically distinct from modern conifers being some <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbaceous</a> conifers with no woody stems. Major fossil orders of conifers or conifer-like plants include the <a href="/wiki/Cordaitales" title="Cordaitales">Cordaitales</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vojnovskyales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vojnovskyales (page does not exist)">Vojnovskyales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voltziales" title="Voltziales">Voltziales</a> and perhaps also the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Czekanowskiales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Czekanowskiales (page does not exist)">Czekanowskiales</a> (possibly more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">Ginkgophyta</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All living conifers are woody plants, and most are trees, the majority having monopodial growth form (a single, straight trunk with side branches) with strong <a href="/wiki/Apical_dominance" title="Apical dominance">apical dominance</a>. Many conifers have distinctly scented <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>, secreted to protect the tree against <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> infestation and <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungal</a> infection of wounds. Fossilized resin hardens into <a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">amber</a>. The size of mature conifers varies from less than one meter, to over 100 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The world's tallest, thickest, and oldest living trees are all conifers. The tallest is a <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">Coast Redwood</a> (<i>Sequoia sempervirens</i>), with a height of 115.55 meters (although one Victorian mountain ash, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">Eucalyptus regnans</a></i>, allegedly grew to a height of 140 meters, although the exact dimensions were not confirmed).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The thickest, or tree with the greatest trunk diameter, is a <a href="/wiki/Taxodium_mucronatum" title="Taxodium mucronatum">Montezuma Cypress</a> (<i>Taxodium mucronatum</i>), 11.42 meters in diameter. The smallest is the <a href="/wiki/Lepidothamnus_laxifolius" title="Lepidothamnus laxifolius">pygmy pine</a> (<i>Lepidothamnus laxifolius</i>) of New Zealand, which is seldom taller than 30 cm when mature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The oldest is a Great Basin Bristlecone Pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_longaeva" title="Pinus longaeva">Pinus longaeva</a></i>), 4,700 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Conflicting sources claim that the largest tree by three-dimensional volume is either: a Giant Sequoia (<i><a href="/wiki/Sequoiadendron_giganteum" title="Sequoiadendron giganteum">Sequoiadendron giganteum</a></i>), with a volume 1486.9 cubic meters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Ficus_benghalensis" title="Ficus benghalensis">Ficus benghalensis</a></i> named <a href="/wiki/Thimmamma_Marrimanu" title="Thimmamma Marrimanu">Thimmamma Marrimanu</a> with volume unspecified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since most conifers are evergreens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> of many conifers are long, thin and have a needle-like appearance, but others, including most of the <a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">Cupressaceae</a> and some of the <a href="/wiki/Podocarpaceae" title="Podocarpaceae">Podocarpaceae</a>, have flat, triangular scale-like leaves. Some, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Agathis" title="Agathis">Agathis</a></i> in Araucariaceae and <i><a href="/wiki/Nageia" title="Nageia">Nageia</a></i> in Podocarpaceae, have broad, flat strap-shaped leaves. Others such as <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria_columnaris" title="Araucaria columnaris">Araucaria columnaris</a></i> have leaves that are awl-shaped. In the majority of conifers, the leaves are arranged spirally, exceptions being most of Cupressaceae and one genus in Podocarpaceae, where they are arranged in decussate opposite pairs or whorls of 3 (-4). In many species with spirally arranged leaves, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Abies_grandis" title="Abies grandis">Abies grandis</a></i> (pictured), the leaf bases are twisted to present the leaves in a very flat plane for maximum light capture. Leaf size varies from 2 mm in many scale-leaved species, up to 400 mm long in the needles of some pines (e.g. Apache Pine, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_engelmannii" title="Pinus engelmannii">Pinus engelmannii</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a> are in lines or patches on the leaves, and can be closed when it is very dry or cold. The leaves are often dark green in colour, which may help absorb a maximum of energy from weak sunshine at high <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> or under forest canopy shade. Conifers from hotter areas with high sunlight levels (e.g. Turkish Pine <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_brutia" title="Pinus brutia">Pinus brutia</a></i>) often have yellower-green leaves, while others (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Blue_spruce" title="Blue spruce">blue spruce</a>, <i>Picea pungens</i>) have a very strong glaucous wax bloom to reflect <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light. In the great majority of genera the leaves are <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a>, usually remaining on the plant for several (2-40) years before falling, but five genera (<i><a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">Larix</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudolarix" title="Pseudolarix">Pseudolarix</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Glyptostrobus" title="Glyptostrobus">Glyptostrobus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Metasequoia" title="Metasequoia">Metasequoia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Taxodium" title="Taxodium">Taxodium</a></i>) are <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>, shedding the leaves in autumn and leafless through the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> The seedlings of many conifers, including most of the Cupressaceae, and <i>Pinus</i> in Pinaceae, have a distinct juvenile foliage period where the leaves are different, often markedly so, from the typical adult leaves.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology#Growth_rings" title="Dendrochronology">Tree rings</a> are records of the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/influence" title="wikt:influence">influence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ecology#Physical_environments" title="Ecology">environmental</a> conditions, their anatomical characteristics record growth rate changes produced by these changing conditions. The microscopic <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structure</a> of conifer wood consists of two types of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>: <b>parenchyma</b>, which have an oval or polyhedral shape with approximately identical dimensions in three directions, and strongly elongated tracheids. <b>Tracheids</b> make up more than 90% of timber volume. The tracheids of earlywood formed at the beginning of a <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> have large radial sizes and smaller, thinner <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a>. Then, the first tracheids of the transition zone are formed, where the radial size of cells and thickness of their cell walls changes considerably. Finally, the latewood tracheids are formed, with small radial sizes and greater cell wall thickness. This is the basic <a href="/wiki/Pattern" title="Pattern">pattern</a> of the internal <a href="/wiki/Cel" title="Cel">cel</a> structure of conifer tree rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">Conifer cone</a></div>
<p>Most conifers are <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology#Terminology" title="Plant reproductive morphology">monoecious</a>, but some are <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology#Terminology" title="Plant reproductive morphology">subdioecious</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology#Terminology" title="Plant reproductive morphology">dioecious</a>; all are <a href="/wiki/Anemophily" title="Anemophily">wind-pollinated</a>. Conifer seeds develop inside a protective cone called a <a href="/wiki/Strobilus" title="Strobilus">strobilus</a>. The cones take from four months to three years to reach maturity, and vary in size from 2 mm to 600 mm long.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Araucariaceae" title="Araucariaceae">Araucariaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciadopityaceae" title="Sciadopityaceae">Sciadopityaceae</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">Cupressaceae</a>, the cones are <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">woody</a>, and when mature the scales usually spread open allowing the seeds to fall out and be dispersed by the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>. In some (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">firs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">cedars</a>), the cones disintegrate to release the seeds, and in others (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> that produce <a href="/wiki/Pine_nut" title="Pine nut">pine nuts</a>) the nut-like seeds are dispersed by <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> (mainly <a href="/wiki/Nutcracker_(bird)" title="Nutcracker (bird)">nutcrackers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jays</a>), which break up the specially adapted softer cones. Ripe cones may remain on the plant for a varied amount of time before falling to the ground; in some fire-adapted pines, the seeds may be stored in closed cones for up to 60–80 years, being released only when a fire kills the parent tree.</p>
<p>In the families <a href="/wiki/Podocarpaceae" title="Podocarpaceae">Podocarpaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cephalotaxaceae" title="Cephalotaxaceae">Cephalotaxaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taxaceae" title="Taxaceae">Taxaceae</a>, and one <a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">Cupressaceae</a> genus (<i><a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">Juniperus</a></i>), the scales are soft, fleshy, sweet and brightly colored, and are eaten by fruit-eating birds, which then pass the seeds in their droppings. These fleshy scales are (except in <i>Juniperus</i>) known as <a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">arils</a>. In some of these conifers (e.g. most Podocarpaceae), the cone consists of several fused scales, while in others (e.g. Taxaceae), the cone is reduced to just one seed scale or (e.g. Cephalotaxaceae) the several scales of a cone develop into individual arils, giving the appearance of a cluster of berries.</p>
<p>The male cones have structures called <a href="/wiki/Sporangium" title="Sporangium">microsporangia</a> that produce yellowish pollen through meiosis. Pollen is released and carried by the wind to female cones. Pollen grains from living pinophyte species produce pollen tubes, much like those of angiosperms. The <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperm</a> male gametophytes (pollen grains) are carried by wind to a female cone and are drawn into a tiny opening on the ovule called the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/micropyle" title="wikt:micropyle">micropyle</a>. It is within the ovule that pollen-germination occurs. From here, a pollen tube seeks out the female gametophyte and if successful, fertilization occurs. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a> develops into an <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>, which along with its surrounding integument, becomes a <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>. Eventually the seed may fall to the ground and, if conditions permit, grows into a new plant.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, the terminology of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> has commonly though inaccurately been applied to cone-bearing trees as well. The male cone and unfertilized female cone are called <i>male flower</i> and <i>female flower</i>, respectively. After fertilization, the female cone is termed <i>fruit</i>, which undergoes <i>ripening</i> (maturation).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conifers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterosporous" title="Heterosporous">heterosporous</a>, generating two different types of spores: male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microspores" title="Microspores">microspores</a> and female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaspores" title="Megaspores">megaspores</a>. These spores develop on separate male and female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporophylls" title="Sporophylls">sporophylls</a> on separate male and female cones. In the male cones, microspores are produced from microsporocytes by <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>. The microspores develop into pollen grains, which are male gametophytes. Large amounts of pollen are released and carried by the wind. Some pollen grains will land on a female cone for pollination. The generative cell in the pollen grain divides into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> sperm cells by <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> leading to the development of the pollen tube. At fertilization, one of the sperm cells unites its haploid nucleus with the haploid nucleus of an egg cell. The female cone develops two ovule, each of which contains haploid haploid megaspores. A megasporocyte is divided by meiosis in each ovule. Each winged pollen grain is a four celled male <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a> Three of the four cells break down leaving only a single surviving cell which will develop into a female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicellular" title="Multicellular">multicellular</a> gametophyte. The female gametophytes grow to produce two or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archegonia" title="Archegonia">archegonia</a>, each of which contains an egg. Upon fertilization, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> egg will give rise to the embryo, and a seed is produced. The female cone then opens, releasing the seeds which grow to a young <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedling</a>.</p>
<ol>
<li>To fertilize the ovum, the male cone releases <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> that is carried on the wind to the female cone. This is <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>. (Male and female cones usually occur on the same plant.)</li>
<li>The pollen fertilizes the female gamete (located in the female cone). Fertilization in some species does not occur until 15 months after pollination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>A fertilized female gamete (called a <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>) develops into an <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a> develops which contains the embryo. The seed also contains the integument cells surrounding the embryo. This is an evolutionary characteristic of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spermatophyta" title="Spermatophyta">Spermatophyta</a>.</li>
<li>Mature seed drops out of cone onto the ground.</li>
<li>Seed germinates and seedling grows into a mature plant.</li>
<li>When the plant is mature, it produces cones and the cycle continues.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_reproductive_cycles">Female reproductive cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Female reproductive cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conifer reproduction is synchronous with seasonal changes in temperate zones. Reproductive development slows to a halt during each winter season, and then resumes each spring. The male <a href="/wiki/Strobilus" title="Strobilus">strobilus</a> development is completed in a single year. Conifers are classified by three reproductive cycles, namely; 1-, 2-, or 3- . The cycles refers to the completion of female strobilus development from initiation to seed maturation. All three types or reproductive cycles have a long gap in between <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a>.</p>
<p><b>One year reproductive cycle</b>:The genera includes <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abies" title="Abies">Abies</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picea" title="Picea">Picea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">Cedrus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudotsuga" title="Pseudotsuga">Pseudotsuga</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">Tsuga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Keteleeria" title="Keteleeria">Keteleeria</a></i> <i>(<a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a>)</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cupressus" title="Cupressus">Cupressus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuja" title="Thuja">Thuja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryptomeria" title="Cryptomeria">Cryptomeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cunninghamia" title="Cunninghamia">Cunninghamia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sequoia_(genus)" title="Sequoia (genus)">Sequoia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">Cupressaceae</a>)</i>. Female strobili are initiated in late summer or fall in a year, then they overwinter. Female strobili emerge followed by pollination in the following spring . Fertilization takes place in summer of the following year, only 3–4 months after pollination. Cones mature and seeds are then shed by the end of that same year. Pollination and fertilization occurs in a single growing season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Two-year reproductive cycle</b>:The genera includes <i><a href="/wiki/Widdringtonia" title="Widdringtonia">Widdringtonia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sequoiadendron" title="Sequoiadendron">Sequoiadendron</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">Cupressaceae</a></i>) and most species of <i>Pinus</i>. Female <a href="/wiki/Strobilus" title="Strobilus">strobilus</a> initials are formed in late summer or fall then overwinter. It emerges and receives pollen in the first year spring and become conelets. The conelet goes through another winter rest and in the spring of the 2nd year. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archegonia" title="Archegonia">Archegonia</a> form in the conelet and fertilization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archegonia" title="Archegonia">archegonia</a> occurs by early summer of the 2nd year, so the pollination-fertilization interval exceeds a year. After fertilization, the conelet is considered an immature cone. Maturation occurs by autumn of the 2nd year, at which time seeds are shed. In summary, the 1-year and the 2-year cycles differ mainly in the duration of the pollination- fertilization interval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Three-year reproductive cycle</b>: Three of the conifer species are <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> species (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus_pinea" title="Pinus pinea">Pinus pinea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_leiophylla" title="Pinus leiophylla">Pinus leiophylla</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus_torreyana" title="Pinus torreyana">Pinus torreyana</a></i>) which have pollination and fertilization events separated by a 2-year interval. Female strobili initiated during late summer or autumn in a year, then overwinter until the following spring. Female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strobili" title="Strobili">strobili</a> emerge then pollination occurs in spring of the 2nd year then the pollinated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strobili" title="Strobili">strobili</a> become conelets in same year (i.e. the second year). The female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gametophytes" title="Gametophytes">gametophytes</a> in the conelet develop so slowly that the <a href="/wiki/Megaspore" title="Megaspore">megaspore</a> does not go through free-nuclear divisions until autumn of the 3rd year. The conelet then overwinters again in the free-nuclear female <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a> stage. Fertilization takes place by early summer of the 4th year and seeds mature in the cones by autumn of the 4th year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_development">Tree development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tree development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The growth and form of a forest tree are the result of activity in the primary and secondary <a href="/wiki/Meristem" title="Meristem">meristems</a>, influenced by the distribution of photosynthate from its needles and the hormonal gradients controlled by the apical meristems (Fraser et al. 1964).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fras_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-fras-16">[16]</a></sup> External factors also influence growth and form.</p>
<p>Fraser recorded the development of a single white spruce tree from 1926 to 1961. Apical growth of the stem was slow from 1926 through 1936 when the tree was competing with <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> and probably shaded by larger trees. Lateral branches began to show reduced growth and some were no longer in evidence on the 36-year-old tree. Apical growth totalling about 340 m, 370 m, 420 m, 450 m, 500 m, 600 m, and 600 m was made by the tree in the years 1955 through 1961, respectively. The total number of needles of all ages present on the 36-year-old tree in 1961 was 5.25 million weighing 14.25 kg. In 1961, needles as old as 13 years remained on the tree.The ash weight of needles increased progressively with age from about 4% in first-year needles in 1961 to about 8% in needles 10 years old. In discussing the data obtained from the one 11 m tall white spruce, Fraser et al. (1964)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fras_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-fras-16">[16]</a></sup> speculated that if the photosynthate used in making apical growth in 1961 was manufactured the previous year, then the 4 million needles that were produced up to 1960 manufactured food for about 600,000 mm of apical growth or 730 g dry weight, over 12 million mm<sup>3</sup> of wood for the 1961 annual ring, plus 1 million new needles, in addition to new tissue in branches, bark, and roots in 1960. Added to this would be the photosynthate to produce energy to sustain respiration over this period, an mount estimated to be about 10% of the total annual photosynthate production of a young healthy tree. On this basis, one needle produced food for about 0.19 mg dry weight of apical growth, 3 mm<sup>3</sup> wood, one-quarter of a new needle, plus an unknown amount of branch wood, bark and roots.</p>
<p>The order of priority of photosynthate distribution is probably: first to apical growth and new needle formation, then to buds for the next year's growth, with the cambium in the older parts of the branches receiving sustenance last. In the white spruce studied by Fraser et al. (1964),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fras_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-fras-16">[16]</a></sup> the needles constituted 17.5% of the over-day weight. Undoubtedly, the proportions change with time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_dispersal_mechanism">Seed dispersal mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Seed dispersal mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wind and animals dispersals are two major mechanisms involved in the dispersal of conifer seeds. Wind bore seed dispersal involves two processes, namely; local neighborhood dispersal (LND) and long- distance dispersal (LDD). Long-distance dispersal distances ranges from 11.9–33.7 kilometres (7.4–20.9 mi) from the source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The bird family, <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">Corvidae</a> is the primary distributor of the conifer seeds. These birds are known to <a href="/wiki/Hoarding_(animal_behavior)" title="Hoarding (animal behavior)">cache</a> 32,000 pine seeds and transport the seeds as far as 12–22 kilometres (7.5–13.7 mi) from the source. The birds store the seeds in the soil at depths of 2–3 centimetres (0.79–1.18 in) under conditions which favor <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wilding_conifer" title="Wilding conifer">Wilding conifer</a></div>
<p>A number of conifers originally introduced for forestry have become <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in parts of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, including Radiata pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_radiata" title="Pinus radiata">Pinus radiata</a></i>), Lodgepole pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_contorta" title="Pinus contorta">P. contorta</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Douglas_fir" title="Douglas fir">Douglas fir</a> (<i>Pseudotsuga mensiezii</i>) and European larch (<i><a href="/wiki/Larix_decidua" title="Larix decidua">Larix decidua</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeptConNZ_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeptConNZ-19">[19]</a></sup> In parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_pinaster" title="Pinus pinaster">Pinus pinaster</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_patula" title="Pinus patula">P. patula</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_radiata" title="Pinus radiata">P. radiata</a></i> have been declared invasive species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilding_conifers" title="Wilding conifers">wilding conifers</a> are a serious environmental issue causing problems for pastoral farming and for <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeptConNZ_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeptConNZ-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least 20 species of roundheaded borers of the family <a href="/wiki/Longhorn_beetle" title="Longhorn beetle">Cerambycidae</a> feed on the wood of <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">hemlock</a> (Rose and Lindquist 1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rose-21">[21]</a></sup> Borers rarely bore tunnels in living trees, although when populations are high, adult beetles feed on tender twig bark, and may damage young living trees. One of the most common and widely distributed borer species in North America is the <a href="/wiki/Monochamus_scutellatus" title="Monochamus scutellatus">whitespotted sawyer</a> (<i>Monochamus scutellatus</i>). Adults are found in summer on newly fallen or recently felled trees chewing tiny slits in the bark in which they lay eggs. The eggs hatch in about 2 weeks, and the tiny <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> tunnel to the wood and score its surface with their feeding channels. With the onset of cooler weather, they bore into the wood making oval entrance holes and tunnel deeply. Feeding continues the following summer, when larvae occasionally return to the surface of the wood and extend the feeding channels generally in a U-shaped configuration. During this time, small piles of frass extruded by the larvae accumulate under logs. Early in the spring of the second year following egg-laying, the larvae, about 30 mm long, <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupate</a> in the tunnel enlargement just below the wood surface. The resulting adults chew their way out in early summer, leaving round exit holes, so completing the usual 2-year life cycle.</p>

<p>Conifers – notably <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abies" title="Abies">Abies</a></i> (fir), <i><a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">Cedrus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Chamaecyparis_lawsoniana" title="Chamaecyparis lawsoniana">Chamaecyparis lawsoniana</a></i> (Lawson's cypress), <i><a href="/wiki/Cupressus" title="Cupressus">Cupressus</a></i> (cypress), <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picea" title="Picea">Picea</a></i> (spruce), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus" title="Pinus">Pinus</a></i> (pine), <i><a href="/wiki/Taxus" title="Taxus">Taxus</a></i> (yew), <i><a href="/wiki/Thuja" title="Thuja">Thuja</a></i> - have been the subject of selection for ornamental purposes (for more information see the <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a> page). Plants with unusual growth habits, sizes, and colours are propagated and planted in parks and gardens throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farjon_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farjon-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_for_growth">Conditions for growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conditions for growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conifers <a href="/wiki/Plant_nutrition" title="Plant nutrition">can absorb nitrogen</a> in either the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> (NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>) or <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> (NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>) form, but the forms are not physiologically equivalent. Form of nitrogen affected both the total amount and relative composition of the soluble nitrogen in white spruce tissues (Durzan and Steward 1967).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dur_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-dur-23">[23]</a></sup> Ammonium nitrogen was shown to foster <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> and lead to a large increase of free <a href="/wiki/Guanidine" title="Guanidine">guanidine</a> compounds, whereas in leaves nourished by nitrate as the sole source of nitrogen guanidine compounds were less prominent. Durzan and Steward noted that their results, drawn from determinations made in late summer, did not rule out the occurrence of different interim responses at other times of year. Ammonium nitrogen produced significantly heavier (dry weight) seedlings with higher nitrogen content after 5 weeks (McFee and Stone 1968)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcf_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcf-24">[24]</a></sup> than did the same amount of nitrate nitrogen. Swan (1960)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swan1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-swan1-25">[25]</a></sup> found the same effect in 105-day-old white spruce.</p>
<p>The general short-term effect of nitrogen fertilization on coniferous seedlings is to stimulate shoot growth more so than root growth (Armson and Carman 1961).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arm6_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-arm6-26">[26]</a></sup> Over a longer period, root growth is also stimulated. Many <a href="/wiki/Plant_nursery" title="Plant nursery">nursery</a> managers were long reluctant to apply nitrogenous <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> late in the growing season, for fear of increased danger of frost damage to succulent tissues. A very interesting presentation at the North American Forest Tree Nursery Soils Workshop at Syracuse in 1980 provided strong contrary evidence: Bob Eastman, President of the Western Maine Forest Nursery Co. stated that for 15 years he has been successful in avoiding winter “burn” to <a href="/wiki/Picea_abies" title="Picea abies">Norway spruce</a> and white spruce in his nursery operation by fertilizing with 50-80 lb/ac (56–90 kg/ha) nitrogen in September, whereas previously winter burn had been experienced annually, often severely. Eastman also stated that the overwintering storage capacity of stock thus treated was much improved (Eastman 1980).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-east_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-east-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of course, the concentrations of nutrient in plant tissues depend on many factors, including growing conditions. Interpretation of concentrations determined by analysis is easy only when a nutrient occurs in excessively low or occasionally excessively high concentration. Values are influenced by environmental factors and interactions among the 16 nutrient elements known to be essential to plants, 13 of which are obtained from the soil, including <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, all used in relatively large amounts (Buckman and Brady 1969).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buck_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-buck-28">[28]</a></sup> Nutrient concentrations in conifers also vary with season, age and kind of tissue sampled, and analytical technique. The ranges of concentrations occurring in well-grown plants provide a useful guide by which to assess the adequacy of particular nutrients, and the ratios among the major nutrients are helpful guides to nutritional imbalances.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_importance">Economic importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinophyta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economic importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Softwood" title="Softwood">softwood</a> derived from conifers are of huge economic value, primarily for timber, paper and plastic (derived from chemically treated wood pulp) production. They also provide about 45% of the world’s annual lumber production. Conifers also serve as a food source such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pine_nuts" title="Pine nuts">pine nuts</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juniper_berries" title="Juniper berries">Juniper berries</a> are also used to flavor <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a>.</p>
