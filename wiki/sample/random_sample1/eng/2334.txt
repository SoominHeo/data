<p><b>Google Cardboard</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> (VR) platform developed by <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> for use with a <a href="/wiki/VR_HMD_mount" title="VR HMD mount">head mount</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a>. Named for its fold-out cardboard viewer, the platform is intended as a low-cost system to encourage interest and development in VR applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Users can either build their own viewer from simple, low-cost components using specifications published by Google, or purchase a pre-manufactured one. To use the platform, users run Cardboard-compatible applications on their phone, place the phone into the back of the viewer, and view content through the lenses.</p>
<p>The platform was created by David Coz and Damien Henry, Google engineers at the <a href="/wiki/Google_Cultural_Institute" title="Google Cultural Institute">Google Cultural Institute</a> in Paris, in their 20% "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innovation_Time_Off" title="Innovation Time Off">Innovation Time Off</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was introduced at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_I/O_2014" title="Google I/O 2014">Google I/O 2014</a> developers conference, where a Cardboard viewer was given away to all attendees. The Cardboard <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kit</a> (SDK) is available for the <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> operating systems; the SDK's VR View allows developers to embed VR content on the web as well as in their mobile apps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through January 2016, over 5 million Cardboard viewers had shipped and over 1,000 compatible applications had been published. Following the success of the Cardboard platform, Google announced an enhanced VR platform, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daydream_(Google)" title="Daydream (Google)">Daydream</a>, at Google I/O 2016.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viewer_assembly_and_operation">Viewer assembly and operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Viewer assembly and operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Google Cardboard headsets are built out of simple, low-cost components. The headset specifications were designed by Google, which made the list of parts, schematics, and assembly instructions freely available on their website, allowing people to assemble Cardboard themselves from readily available parts. Pre-manufactured viewers were only available from third-party vendors until February 2016, when Google began selling their own through the <a href="/wiki/Google_Store" title="Google Store">Google Store</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The parts that make up a Cardboard viewer are a piece of <a href="/wiki/Cardboard" title="Cardboard">cardboard</a> cut into a precise shape, 45 mm <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a> <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Capacitive_sensing" title="Capacitive sensing">capacitive</a> tape, a <a href="/wiki/Hook_and_loop_fastener" title="Hook and loop fastener">hook and loop fastener</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">Velcro</a>), a rubber band, and an optional <a href="/wiki/Near_field_communication" title="Near field communication">near field communication</a> (NFC) tag. Google provides extra recommendations for large scale manufacturing, and pre-assembled kits based on these plans are available for less than <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$5</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> from multiple vendors, who have also created a number of Cardboard variations.</p>
<p>Once the kit is assembled, a <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> is inserted in the back of the device and held in place by the selected fastening device. A Google Cardboard–compatible app splits the smartphone display image into two, one for each eye, while also applying <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(optics)" title="Distortion (optics)">barrel distortion</a> to each image to counter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pincushion_distortion" title="Pincushion distortion">pincushion distortion</a> from the lenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The result is a <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">stereoscopic</a> ("3D") image with a wide field of view.</p>
<p>The first version of Cardboard could fit phones with screens up to 5.7 inches (140 mm) and used magnets as input buttons, which required a compass sensor in the phone. An updated design released at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_I/O_2015" title="Google I/O 2015">Google I/O 2015</a> works with phones up to 6 inches (150 mm) and replaces the magnet switch with a conductive lever that triggers a touch event on the phone's screen for better compatibility across devices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Google provides three <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kits</a> for developing Cardboard applications: one for the <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> operating system using <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a>, one for the game engine <a href="/wiki/Unity_(game_engine)" title="Unity (game engine)">Unity</a> using <a href="/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language)" title="C Sharp (programming language)">C#</a>, and one for the <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> operating system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goog_Goog_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-goog_Goog-8">[8]</a></sup> After initially supporting only Android, Google announced iOS support for the Unity plugin in May 2015 at the Google I/O 2015 conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Third-party apps with Cardboard support are available on the <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a> store<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store for iOS</a>. In addition to native Cardboard apps, there are Google Chrome VR Experiments implemented using <a href="/wiki/WebGL" title="WebGL">WebGL</a>; phones, including Apple's, that support WebGL can run Google's web experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A port of the Google Cardboard demonstration app to iOS was released at Google I/O 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-io2015_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-io2015-13">[13]</a></sup> In January 2016, Google announced that the software development kits would support <a href="/wiki/3D_audio_effect" title="3D audio effect">spatial audio</a>, a virtual reality effect intended to simulate audio coming from outside of the listeners head located anywhere in 3D space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eadicicco_2016_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eadicicco_2016-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_pancakes_2016_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Google_pancakes_2016-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2016, Google released VR View, an expansion of the Cardboard SDK allowing developers to embed 360-degree VR content on a web page or in a mobile app, across desktop, Android, and iOS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Javascript" title="Javascript">Javascript</a> and <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> code for web publishing VR content is <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open source</a> and available on <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a>, allowing developers to self-host their content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_initiatives">Related initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Related initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jump">Jump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jump is an ecosystem for virtual reality film-making developed by Google. It was announced at Google I/O on May 28, 2015. Much as Google did with the Cardboard viewer, for Jump the company developed specifications for a circular camera array made from 16 cameras that it will release to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-jump_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge-jump-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GoPro" title="GoPro">GoPro</a> partnered with Google to build an array using their own cameras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> although the Jump rig will theoretically support any camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-jump_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-verge-jump-18">[18]</a></sup> Once footage has been shot, the VR video is compiled from the individual cameras through "the assembler", Jump's back-end software. The assembler uses <a href="/wiki/Computational_photography" title="Computational photography">computational photography</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">computer vision</a>" to recreate the scene while generating thousands of in-between viewpoints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-jump_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-verge-jump-18">[18]</a></sup> Finalized video shot through Jump can then be viewed through a stereoscopic VR mode of <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> with a Cardboard viewer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-jump_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-verge-jump-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expeditions">Expeditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expeditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Expeditions is a program for providing VR experiences to school classrooms through Google Cardboard viewers, allowing educators to take their students on virtual <a href="/wiki/Field_trip" title="Field trip">field trips</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It was announced at Google I/O 2015, with plans to launch in fall 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Each classroom kit would include 30 synchronized Cardboard viewers and smartphones, along with a tablet for the teacher to act as tour guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Teachers interested in bringing the program to their school can register online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CNET" title="CNET">CNET</a> called Cardboard "the first Virtual Reality platform targeted at children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Through May 2016, over one million students had taken a VR field trip through the Expeditions program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnerships_and_promotions">Partnerships and promotions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Partnerships and promotions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2014, <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">Volvo</a> released Volvo-branded Cardboard goggles and an Android app, Volvo Reality, to let the user explore the <a href="/wiki/Volvo_XC90" title="Volvo XC90">XC90</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In February 2015, toy manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a>, in cooperation with Google, announced a VR version of the stereoscopic viewer <a href="/wiki/View-Master" title="View-Master">View-Master</a>. Android support was available at the viewer's release in fall 2015, with support for iOS and Windows smartphones available later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Google also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/LG_Electronics" title="LG Electronics">LG Electronics</a> to release a Cardboard-based headset for the <a href="/wiki/LG_G3" title="LG G3">LG G3</a> known as VR for G3. Released in February 2015, it was distributed as a free accessory with new G3 models sold in certain countries, and was perceived to be a competitor to the <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Gear_VR" title="Samsung Gear VR">Samsung Gear VR</a> accessory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet-vrforg3_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-vrforg3-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 8, 2015, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> included a Google Cardboard viewer with all home newspaper deliveries. Readers can download the <i>NYT VR</i> app on their smartphone, which displays journalism-focused immersive VR environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, Google offered free <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i>-themed Cardboard viewers through the <a href="/wiki/Google_Store" title="Google Store">Google Store</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Wireless" title="Verizon Wireless">Verizon</a> as a part of promotional tie-in for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Force_Awakens" title="Star Wars: The Force Awakens">Star Wars: The Force Awakens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ticket holders for the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Coachella_Valley_Music_and_Arts_Festival" title="Coachella Valley Music and Arts Festival">Coachella Valley Music and Arts Festival</a> received a Google Cardboard–inspired cardboard VR viewer in their welcome package that can be used with the Coachella VR mobile app. The festival's organizers partnered with Vantage.tv to offer VR content for the festival, such as 360° panoramic photos of previous events, virtual tours of the 2016 festival site, interviews, and performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 27, 2016, Google announced that in the platform's first 19 months, over 5 million Cardboard viewers had shipped, over 1,000 compatible applications had been published, and over 25 million application installs had been made. According to the company, users viewed over 350,000 hours of YouTube videos in VR during that time and 500,000 students took a VR field trip through the Expeditions program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Successor">Successor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Google_Cardboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Successor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The success of Cardboard convinced Google to develop more advanced virtual reality hardware and appoint a new chief of virtual reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Google announced an enhanced VR platform called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daydream_(Google)" title="Daydream (Google)">Daydream</a> at Google I/O on May 18, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
