<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning">Beginning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> began in 1996 as a research project by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Brin" title="Sergey Brin">Sergey Brin</a> Ph.D. students at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milestones_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In search of a dissertation theme, Page had been considering—among other things—exploring the mathematical properties of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, understanding its link structure as a huge <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">graph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup> His supervisor, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Winograd" title="Terry Winograd">Terry Winograd</a>, encouraged him to pick this idea (which Page later recalled as "the best advice I ever got"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) and Page focused on the problem of finding out which web pages link to a given page, based on the consideration that the number and nature of such backlinks was valuable information about that page (with the role of <a href="/wiki/Citation" title="Citation">citations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic publishing</a> in mind).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="BackRub"></span>In his research project, nicknamed "BackRub", Page was soon joined by Brin, who was supported by a <a href="/wiki/NSF-GRF" title="NSF-GRF">National Science Foundation Graduate Fellowship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anatomy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anatomy-5">[5]</a></sup> Brin was already a close friend, whom Page had first met in the summer of 1995, when Page was part of a group of potential new students that Brin had volunteered to show around the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup> Both Brin and Page were working on the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Digital_Library_Project" title="Stanford Digital Library Project">Stanford Digital Library Project</a> (SDLP). The SDLP's goal was “to develop the enabling technologies for a single, integrated and universal digital library" and it was funded through the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, among other federal agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anatomy_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anatomy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Page's <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a> began exploring the web in March 1996, with Page's own Stanford home page serving as the only starting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup> To convert the backlink data that it gathered for a given web page into a measure of importance, Brin and Page developed the <a href="/wiki/PageRank" title="PageRank">PageRank</a> algorithm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup> While analyzing BackRub's output—which, for a given URL, consisted of a list of backlinks ranked by importance—the pair realized that a search engine based on PageRank would produce better results than existing techniques (existing search engines at the time essentially ranked results according to how many times the search term appeared on a page).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Convinced that the pages with the most links to them from other highly relevant Web pages must be the most relevant pages associated with the search, Page and Brin tested their thesis as part of their studies, and laid the foundation for their search engine.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_1990s">Late 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally the search engine used Stanford's website with the domain <i>google.stanford.edu</i>. The domain <i>google.com</i> was registered on September 15, 1997. They formally incorporated their company, <i>Google</i>, on September 4, 1998 in their friend <a href="/wiki/Susan_Wojcicki" title="Susan Wojcicki">Susan Wojcicki</a>'s garage in <a href="/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California" title="Menlo Park, California">Menlo Park, California</a>.</p>
<p>The company's first patent was filed on August 31, 1999. This patent, filed by Siu-Leong Iu, Malcom Davis, Hui Luo, Yun-Ting Lin, Guillaume Mercier, and Kobad Bugwadia, is titled "Watermarking system and methodology for digital multimedia content" and is the earliest patent filing under the assignee name "Google Inc."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Brin and Page had been against using advertising pop-ups in a search engine, or an "advertising funded search engines" model, and they wrote a research paper in 1998 on the topic while still students. They changed their minds early on and allowed simple text ads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 1998, Google had an index of about 60 million pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon98_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-salon98-15">[15]</a></sup> The home page was still marked "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_test" title="Beta test">BETA</a>", but an article in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a> already argued that Google's search results were better than those of competitors like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotbot" title="Hotbot">Hotbot</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excite.com" title="Excite.com">Excite.com</a>, and praised it for being more technologically innovative than the overloaded <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">portal sites</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, Excite.com, <a href="/wiki/Lycos" title="Lycos">Lycos</a>, Netscape's <a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netcenter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AOL.com" title="AOL.com">AOL.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Go.com" title="Go.com">Go.com</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MSN.com" title="MSN.com">MSN.com</a>) which at that time, during the growing <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, were seen as "the future of the Web", especially by stock market investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon98_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-salon98-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1999, the company moved into offices at <a href="/wiki/165_University_Avenue" title="165 University Avenue">165 University Avenue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">Palo Alto</a>, home to several other noted <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> technology startups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165univave_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-165univave-16">[16]</a></sup> After quickly outgrowing two other sites, the company leased a complex of buildings in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_Santa_Clara_County,_California" title="Mountain View, Santa Clara County, California">Mountain View</a> at 1600 Amphitheatre Parkway from <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">Silicon Graphics</a> (SGI) in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sgibldg_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sgibldg-17">[17]</a></sup> The company has remained at this location ever since, and the complex has since become known as the <a href="/wiki/Googleplex" title="Googleplex">Googleplex</a> (a play on the word <a href="/wiki/Googolplex" title="Googolplex">googolplex</a>, a number that is equal to 1 followed by a <a href="/wiki/Googol" title="Googol">googol</a> of zeros). In 2006, Google bought the property from SGI for US$319 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googleplexpurchase_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-googleplexpurchase-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Google search engine attracted a loyal following among the growing number of Internet users, who liked its simple design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpledesign_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpledesign-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2000, Google began selling <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertisements</a> associated with search <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyword_(internet_search)" title="Keyword (internet search)">keywords</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milestones_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-2">[2]</a></sup> The ads were text-based to maintain an uncluttered page design and to maximize page loading speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milestones_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-2">[2]</a></sup> Keywords were sold based on a combination of price bid and click-throughs, with bidding starting at $.05 per click.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milestones_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-2">[2]</a></sup> This model of selling keyword advertising was pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_Marketing" title="Yahoo! Search Marketing">Goto.com</a> (later renamed Overture Services, before being acquired by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> and rebranded as <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_Marketing" title="Yahoo! Search Marketing">Yahoo! Search Marketing</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goto_strong_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-goto_strong-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet_p4p_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet_p4p-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glaser_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-glaser-22">[22]</a></sup> While many of its <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-com</a> rivals failed in the new Internet marketplace, Google quietly rose in stature while generating revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milestones_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Google's declared <a href="/wiki/Code_of_conduct" title="Code of conduct">code of conduct</a> is "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_be_evil" title="Don't be evil">Don't be evil</a>", a phrase which they went so far as to include in their <a href="/wiki/Prospectus_(finance)" title="Prospectus (finance)">prospectus</a> (aka "S-1") for their 2004 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initial_Public_Offering" title="Initial Public Offering">IPO</a>, noting that "We believe strongly that in the long term, we will be better served—as shareholders and in all other ways—by a company that does good things for the world even if we forgo some short term gains."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2008, Google launched <a href="/wiki/Knol" title="Knol">Knol</a>, their own equivalent of Wikipedia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Death_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Death-24">[24]</a></sup> which failed just four years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knol-death_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-knol-death-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, the company launched <a href="/wiki/Google%2B" title="Google+">Google+</a>, its fourth foray into social networking, following <a href="/wiki/Google_Buzz" title="Google Buzz">Google Buzz</a> (launched 2010, retired in 2011), <a href="/wiki/Google_Friend_Connect" title="Google Friend Connect">Google Friend Connect</a> (launched 2008, retired by March 1, 2012), and <a href="/wiki/Orkut" title="Orkut">Orkut</a> (launched in 2004, retired in September 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing_and_initial_public_offering">Financing and initial public offering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Financing and initial public offering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first funding for Google as a company was secured in August 1998 in the form of a US$100,000 contribution from <a href="/wiki/Andy_Bechtolsheim" title="Andy Bechtolsheim">Andy Bechtolsheim</a>, co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>, given to a corporation which did not yet exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bechtolsheim_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bechtolsheim-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 7, 1999, a round of equity funding totalling $25 million was announced;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the major investors being rival venture capital firms <a href="/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins_Caufield_%26_Byers" title="Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers">Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Capital" title="Sequoia Capital">Sequoia Capital</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bechtolsheim_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bechtolsheim-27">[27]</a></sup> While Google still needed more funding for their further expansion, Brin and Page were hesitant to take the company public, despite their financial issues. They were not ready to give up control over Google.</p>
<p>Following the closing of the $25 million financing round, Sequoia encouraged Brin and Page to hire a CEO. Brin and Page ultimately acquiesced and hired <a href="/wiki/Eric_Schmidt" title="Eric Schmidt">Eric Schmidt</a> as Google’s first CEO in March 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2003, while discussing a possible <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of shares (IPO), <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> approached the company about a possible partnership or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merger" title="Merger">merger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The deal never materialized. In January 2004, Google announced the hiring of <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs_Group" title="Goldman Sachs Group">Goldman Sachs Group</a> to arrange an IPO. The IPO was projected to raise as much as $4 billion.</p>
<p>Google's initial public offering took place on August 19, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A total of 19,605,052 <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">shares</a> were offered at a price of $85 per share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPO_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPO-32">[32]</a></sup> Of that, 14,142,135 (another mathematical reference as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_root_of_two" title="Square root of two">√2</a> ≈ 1.4142135) were floated by Google and 5,462,917 by selling stockholders. The sale raised US$1.67 billion, and gave Google a <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> of more than $23 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpost_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-washpost-33">[33]</a></sup> Many of Google's employees became instant paper millionaires. <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, a competitor of Google, also benefited from the IPO because it owns 2.7 million shares of Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahooshares_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahooshares-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is listed on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock exchange under the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> <b>GOOG</b>.</p>

<p>In February 2003, Google acquired <a href="/wiki/Pyra_Labs" title="Pyra Labs">Pyra Labs</a>, owner of <a href="/wiki/Blogger_(service)" title="Blogger (service)">Blogger</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">web log</a> hosting website. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004">2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2004, Yahoo! dropped its partnership with Google, providing an independent search engine of its own. This cost Google some <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a>, yet Yahoo!'s move highlighted Google's own distinctiveness,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and today<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the verb "<a href="/wiki/Google_(verb)" title="Google (verb)">to google</a>" has entered a number of languages (first as a <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a> verb and now as a standard word), meaning "to perform a web search" (a possible indication of "Google" becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>After the IPO, Google's stock market capitalization rose greatly and the stock price more than quadrupled. On August 19, 2004 the number of <a href="/wiki/Shares_outstanding" title="Shares outstanding">shares outstanding</a> was 172.85 million while the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Float_(finance)" title="Float (finance)">free float</a>" was 19.60 million (which makes 89% held by <a href="/wiki/Insider" title="Insider">insiders</a>). Google has a dual class stock structure in which each Class B share gets ten votes compared to each Class A share getting one. Page said in the <a href="/wiki/Prospectus_(finance)" title="Prospectus (finance)">prospectus</a> that Google has "a dual class structure that is biased toward stability and independence and that requires investors to bet on the team, especially Sergey and me."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005">2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 1, 2005, Google shares gained nearly four percent after Credit Suisse First Boston raised its price target on the stock to $350. On that same day, rumors circulated in the financial community that Google would soon be included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%26P_500" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> When companies are first listed on the S&amp;P 500 they typically experience a bump in share price due to rapid accumulation of the stock within index funds that track the S&amp;P 500. Google was not added to the S&amp;P 500 until 2006. Nevertheless, on June 7, 2005, Google was valued at nearly $52 billion, making it one of the world's biggest media companies by stock market value.</p>
<p>On August 18, 2005 (one year after the initial IPO), Google announced that it would sell 14,159,265 (another mathematical reference as <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">π</a> ≈ 3.14159265) more shares of its stock to raise money. The move would double Google's cash stockpile to $7 billion. Google said it would use the money for "acquisitions of complementary businesses, technologies or other assets".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 28, 2005, Google announced a long-term research partnership with <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> which would involve Google building a 1,000,000-square-foot (93,000 m<sup>2</sup>) R&amp;D center at NASA's <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">Ames Research Center</a>, and on December 31, 2005 <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>'s AOL unit and Google unveiled an expanded partnership—see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Google#Partnerships" title="History of Google">Partnerships</a> below.</p>
<p>Additionally in 2005, Google formed a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> to help share and distribute each other's technologies. As part of the partnership Google will hire employees to help in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> office program <a href="/wiki/OpenOffice.org" title="OpenOffice.org">OpenOffice.org</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sun_partners_with_Google_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_partners_with_Google-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>With Google's increased size came more competition from large mainstream technology companies. One such example is the rivalry between Microsoft and Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Microsoft had been touting its <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> search engine to counter Google's competitive position. Furthermore, the two companies are increasingly offering overlapping services, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web-based_email" title="Web-based email">webmail</a> (Gmail vs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotmail" title="Hotmail">Hotmail</a>), search (both online and local desktop searching), and other applications (for example, Microsoft's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_Live_Local" title="Windows Live Local">Windows Live Local</a> competes with <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a>). In addition to an <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> replacement Google designed its own <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> called Chrome OS to directly compete with <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>. There were also rumors of a Google <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>, fueled much by the fact that Google was the owner of the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> "gbrowser.com".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These were later proven when Google released <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>. This corporate feud boiled over into the courts when <a href="/wiki/Kai-Fu_Lee" title="Kai-Fu Lee">Kai-Fu Lee</a>, a former vice-president of Microsoft, quit Microsoft to work for Google. Microsoft sued to stop his move by citing Lee's <a href="/wiki/Non-compete_clause" title="Non-compete clause">non-compete contract</a> (he had access to much sensitive information regarding Microsoft's plans in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>). Google and Microsoft reached a settlement out of court on December 22, 2005, the terms of which are confidential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">Click fraud</a> also became a growing problem for Google's business strategy. Google's CFO George Reyes said in a December 2004 investor conference that "something has to be done about this really, really quickly, because I think, potentially, it threatens our business model."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006">2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the company's primary market is in the web content arena, Google has experimented with other markets, such as radio and print publications. On January 17, 2006, Google announced that it had purchased the radio advertising company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DMarc" title="DMarc">dMarc</a>, which provides an automated system that allows companies to advertise on the radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Google also began an experiment in selling advertisements from its advertisers in offline newspapers and magazines, with select advertisements in the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the third quarter 2005 Google Conference Call, Eric Schmidt said, "We don't do the same thing as everyone else does. And so if you try to predict our product strategy by simply saying well so and so has this and Google will do the same thing, it's almost always the wrong answer. We look at markets as they exist and we assume they are pretty well served by their existing players. We try to see new problems and new markets using the technology that others use and we build."</p>
<p>After months of speculation, Google was added to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" title="Standard &amp; Poor's">Standard &amp; Poor's</a> 500 index (S&amp;P 500) on March 31, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Google replaced <a href="/wiki/Burlington_Resources" title="Burlington Resources">Burlington Resources</a>, a major oil producer based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> that had been acquired by <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The day after the announcement Google's share price rose by 7%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014">2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of November 2014, Google operated over 70 offices in more than 41 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015">2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, Google reorganized their company, becoming a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Alphabet_Inc." title="Alphabet Inc.">Alphabet Inc.</a> and spinning off some of their non-internet-related brands into independent subsidiaries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Updates_and_Evolution_of_Ranking_System">Updates and Evolution of Ranking System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Updates and Evolution of Ranking System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Updates">Early Updates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early Updates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its infancy, very little was needed to fully optimize on page content in the eyes of Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mozblog_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-mozblog-47">[47]</a></sup> With an inverse relationship between query position (in the HTML tag hierarchy) and weight, it simply boiled down to putting one’s important keywords higher on the HTML tag hierarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slideshare_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-slideshare-48">[48]</a></sup> With this relatively simple algorithm in place, webmasters quickly discovered tricks to vastly boost their SERP (Search Engine Ranking Position). One of the first instances of deceitful SEO came in the form of link keyword stuffing under on page content. As a response, Google removed these sites from its index, a practice it occasionally reverts to as a way of punishing disingenuous webmasters looking to cheat the system. The seemingly arbitrary changes to Googles PageRank algorithm however, led to both community outrage (“many declaring the “death of PageRank”) and a noticeable decline in precision of search results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-49">[49]</a></sup> Google was also constructing its index via a large monthly crawl. Not only did this lock search results to this one month window, it also meant that results would show stale content. An update dubbed “Everflux” introduced fresh crawling (daily crawls) to supplement the larger, primary crawl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everfluxforum_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-everfluxforum-50">[50]</a></sup> Daily crawling added another layer of relevancy (based upon date and time), to content ranking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everfluxforum_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-everfluxforum-50">[50]</a></sup> The inconsistencies of index versions across data centers during the early implementation of Everflux panicked users, who saw their SERPs fluctuate wildly from day to day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston.2FCassandra.2FDominic_.282003.29">Boston/Cassandra/Dominic (2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Boston/Cassandra/Dominic (2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Google's “Boston” update in February 2003 saw major algorithmic changes and the promise of frequent index updates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moztimeline_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-moztimeline-51">[51]</a></sup> “Cassandra” marked a much more aggressive attack on shady SEO techniques like hidden and disguised keyword links, by emphasizing link quality<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moztimeline_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-moztimeline-51">[51]</a></sup> This was taken a step further in “Dominic”, which sought to analyze the quality of all backlinks to prevent the then emerging practice of splogging (the practice of creating nonsensical offsite content to boost SERP of another site).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> To combat practices like “Googlebombing” (putting irrelevant, often negative anchor text linking to popular websites) Dominic tinkered with the weighting of anchor text while stringently scrutinizing back links and internal linking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> An exploit where webmasters would link to the same site using different anchor text (thereby allowing both links to unfairly contribute to sites PageRank) was addressed by allowing only one site (given duplicate site links with differing anchor text) link to flow to PageRank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anchortextpagerank_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-anchortextpagerank-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fritz.2FSupplemental_Index.2FFlorida_.282004.29">Fritz/Supplemental Index/Florida (2004)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fritz/Supplemental Index/Florida (2004)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fritz finalized the “Everflux” implementation, meaning Google's index would receive some degree of updating every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moztimeline_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-moztimeline-51">[51]</a></sup> Daily crawling added another layer of relevancy, (based upon date and time), to content ranking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everfluxforum_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-everfluxforum-50">[50]</a></sup> The creation of a supplemental index was designed to house content Google felt didn’t quite fit in its main index (due either to a low PageRank or shady linking practices).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supplementalindex_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-supplementalindex-55">[55]</a></sup> Storing some sites in a separate index that was to be searched only when no good match was found in the primary index, Google hoped, would seamlessly filter out duplicate and untrustworthy content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supindexgoogleblog_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-supindexgoogleblog-56">[56]</a></sup> Questions about the efficacy of a multi-index system (particularly on improving recall) arose, and it remains unclear as to whether Google has retained this system. Google's “Florida” update looked to be the proverbial “nail in the coffin” for SEO abusers. With Florida, context and relevancy were determined not just by the appearance of keywords, but of synonyms and supporting vocabulary throughout the page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howtosurviveseo_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-howtosurviveseo-57">[57]</a></sup> This sought to eliminate keyword and inbound anchor text dumping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seochat_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-seochat-58">[58]</a></sup> Features that had long been experimented on, like phrase proximity and keyword stemming, became official and users were now being penalized for overly using commercial/popular keywords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howtosurviveseo_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-howtosurviveseo-57">[57]</a></sup> With Florida coming down hard on SEO, many webmasters with commercial presences on the web took a drastic hit. The real estate community in particular, was hit hard by Florida’s changes to Google rankings. In its attempt at wide spread reform, the quality of results took a hit and many site owners felt they took undeserved drops.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austin.2FBrandy_.282004.29">Austin/Brandy (2004)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Austin/Brandy (2004)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Building off of Florida’s aggressive approach to SEO, Austin targeted Free-For-All pages (pages that allow users to enter a url thereby creating a free backlink) and meta-tag stuffing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-austinupdateweblink_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-austinupdateweblink-59">[59]</a></sup> Google also began to implement elements of the “Hilltop” algorithm, which focuses on identifying “expert” (pages that link to many relevant documents) pages and then giving a boost to “authority” pages with several outgoing links from that expert page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moztimeline_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-moztimeline-51">[51]</a></sup> Brandy saw the growth of the already sizable Google index as well as two important tools for analyzing relevance: Latent Semantic Indexing and link relevancy (“neighborhoods”).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandy_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-brandy-60">[60]</a></sup> By looking at the relationship among terms and the overlap of term use across documents, Latent Semantic Indexing went beyond simple text matching to provide a more robust and “intelligent” search service. The idea of link “neighborhoods” was introduced in Brandy and it made it important to have backlinks that were relevant to the linked site. A site's use of keywords in its HTML tag hierarchy was also devalued in what was another attempt to curb SEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandy_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-brandy-60">[60]</a></sup> Austin and Brandy marked a decided shift towards content analysis in site ranking.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nofollow_Attribute">Nofollow Attribute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nofollow Attribute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nofollow" title="Nofollow">nofollow</a> HTML attribute allowed site owners to tell Google not to crawl specific links on their page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nofollowgoogleblog_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-nofollowgoogleblog-61">[61]</a></sup> By giving this kind of control directly to the user, untrusted content and paid links could be removed from Google’s crawl at the site-owners discretion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personalized_Search_.282005.29">Personalized Search (2005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Personalized Search (2005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To create seamless personalization of search (beyond manual filtering) Google began tapping into users browsing histories to deliver more relevant, personal results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searchpersonalgoogle_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-searchpersonalgoogle-62">[62]</a></sup> Promising to grow with the users browsing history, Personalized Search added a new dimension to search by incorporating past user behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searchpersonalgoogle_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-searchpersonalgoogle-62">[62]</a></sup> The implementation of personalized search was a blow to those relying on SEO techniques, as user browsing history was an element outside of their control.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XML_Sitemaps_.282005.29">XML Sitemaps (2005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: XML Sitemaps (2005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By allowing webmasters to create and submit XML files dictating URLs to be crawled as well as procedural information regarding how the page should be crawled, Google expanded the scope of its index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitemapnews_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-sitemapnews-63">[63]</a></sup> Providing an additional method of organically increasing site exposure, Google hoped, would decrease the need for shady SEO practices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Daddy_.282005.29">Big Daddy (2005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Big Daddy (2005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Big Daddy was less an algorithmic change as it was a change of Google's crawling and indexing infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigdaddyarticle_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigdaddyarticle-64">[64]</a></sup> Pages with superfluous, reciprocal linking schemes and irrelevant outbound and inbound links would be demoted in the new crawler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigdaddyarticle_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-bigdaddyarticle-64">[64]</a></sup> Whereas previous updates handled the issue of link reliability through algorithmic changes, the Big Daddy modified how many pages a site would have crawled, and subsequently added to the index. The number of pages crawled on a site was again dependent on the relevance and quality of its links.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigdaddyarticle_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-bigdaddyarticle-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universal_Search_.282007.29">Universal Search (2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Universal Search (2007)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2007 Google implemented Universal search to its standard web results page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universalsearchgoogleblog_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-universalsearchgoogleblog-65">[65]</a></sup> Search results were now a compilation of all relevant results across all of Google’s “vertical” search engines, (Google Images, Google Video etc.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blendedsearch_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-blendedsearch-66">[66]</a></sup> With the implementation of Universal Search, coverage of all types of content (not simply text) rose significantly. It now became important to optimize all forms of on-site content, not just on-site text content, increasing the complexity and breadth of SEO.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Google_Suggest.2FInstant">Google Suggest/Instant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Google Suggest/Instant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Google Suggest marked the addition of real-time query suggestion. As users began to enter a query, a list of possible query matches would dynamically appear beneath the search bar, allowing for quicker and more accurate searches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googleinstantblog_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-googleinstantblog-67">[67]</a></sup> According to Google, only about 2% of all user queries were tracked and monitored, in an effort to better improve the service, quelling concerns over privacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googleinstantblog_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-googleinstantblog-67">[67]</a></sup> The addition of instant suggestions added yet another dynamic to SEO, as webmasters now vied to associate their site with high ranking instant suggestion queries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-univseo_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-univseo-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_Time_Search_.282009.29">Real Time Search (2009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Real Time Search (2009)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2009, Google integrated real time search results to its main SERP. Newly indexed, relevant content from social media and news sources would be dynamically inserted into a user’s SERP, thus providing “real time content.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realtimesearchgoogleblog_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-realtimesearchgoogleblog-69">[69]</a></sup> Google also announced its desire to prioritize original, user driven social media over authoritative, corporate social media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realtimesearchse_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-realtimesearchse-70">[70]</a></sup> Additionally, a separate “updates” filter was added, allowing the user to receive all incoming content from social media, sports, and news sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realtimesearchgoogleblog_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-realtimesearchgoogleblog-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caffeine_.282010.29">Caffeine (2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Caffeine (2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Google’s Caffeine was the result of a complete overhaul of Google’s indexing structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caffeineblog_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-caffeineblog-71">[71]</a></sup> To accommodate for the explosion of new forms of content (video, real-time content, images) and growing user expectations, Google ditched its old linear system of indexing in favor of the more flexible Caffeine, capable of indexing thousands of pages in parallel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caffeineblog_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-caffeineblog-71">[71]</a></sup> Google would no longer execute lengthy crawls supplemented by smaller daily crawls, but instead dynamically add to its index whenever new information appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caffeineblog_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-caffeineblog-71">[71]</a></sup> Caffeine was reported to offer 50% fresher content than Google’s previous index, and required around 100 million gigabytes of memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caffeineblog_71-3"><a href="#cite_note-caffeineblog-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="JCPenney_SEO_Incident_.282011.29">JCPenney SEO Incident (2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: JCPenney SEO Incident (2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2011, the New York Times published an article titled “The Dirty Little Secrets of Search” detailing back link purchasing by the clothing retailer JCPenney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesseo_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesseo-72">[72]</a></sup> By purchasing thousands of backlinks (often on unrelated sites), JCPenney dominated Google’s SERP across many clothing related queries, and even queries specifying a brand (JCPenney topped Samsonite for the query “Samsonite carryon luggage”).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesseo_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesseo-72">[72]</a></sup> Given Google’s previous updates and their emphasis on backlink analysis, the JCPenney incident revealed that flaws still existed within the Google ranking algorithm. Google quickly demoted JCPenney’s search position, crippling online sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesseo_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesseo-72">[72]</a></sup> Google has since lifted the demotion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Google" originated from a misspelling of "<a href="/wiki/Googol" title="Googol">googol</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanley_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanley-74">[74]</a></sup> which refers to the number represented by a 1 followed by one-hundred zeros. Page and Brin write in their original paper on <a href="/wiki/PageRank" title="PageRank">PageRank</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-originalpaper_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-originalpaper-75">[75]</a></sup> "We chose our systems name, Google, because it is a common spelling of googol, or 10<sup>100</sup> and fits well with our goal of building very large-scale search engines."</p>
<p>There are uses of the name going back at least as far as the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strip</a> character <a href="/wiki/Barney_Google_and_Snuffy_Smith" title="Barney Google and Snuffy Smith">Barney Google</a> in 1919. British children's author <a href="/wiki/Enid_Blyton" title="Enid Blyton">Enid Blyton</a> used the phrase "Google Bun" in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_Faraway_Tree_(novel)" title="The Magic Faraway Tree (novel)">The Magic Faraway Tree</a></i> (published 1941) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faraway_Tree#The_Folk_of_the_Faraway_Tree" title="The Faraway Tree">The Folk of the Faraway Tree</a></i> (published 1946),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and called a clown character "Google" in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Circus_Series" title="The Circus Series">Circus Days Again</a></i> (published 1942).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> There is also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Googleplex_Star_Thinker" title="Googleplex Star Thinker">Googleplex Star Thinker</a> from <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Adams" title="Douglas Adams">Douglas Adams</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy">The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</a></i>. In March 1996 a business called Groove Track Productions applied for a United States trademark for "Google" for various products including several categories of clothing, stuffed toys, board games, and candy. The firm abandoned its application in July 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having found its way increasingly into everyday language, the verb "<a href="/wiki/Google_(verb)" title="Google (verb)">google</a>" was added to the <i><a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam Webster Collegiate Dictionary</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> in 2006, meaning "to use the Google search engine to obtain information on the Internet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The use of the term itself reflects their mission to organize a seemingly infinite amount of information on the web.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanley_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hanley-74">[74]</a></sup> In November 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Global_Language_Monitor" title="Global Language Monitor">Global Language Monitor</a> named "Google" No. 7 on its Top Words of the Decade list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> In December 2009 the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> highlighted Google in their "Portrait of the Decade (Words)" series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> In May 2012, David Elliott filed a complaint against Google, Inc. claiming that Google’s once distinctive mark GOOGLE® has become generic and lacks trademark significance due to its common use as a transitive verb. After losing to Google in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDRP" title="UDRP">UDRP</a> proceedings involving many “Google-related” domain name registrations that he owns, Elliott later sought a declaratory judgment that his domain names are rightfully his, that they do not infringe any trademark rights Google may own, and that all Google’s registered GOOGLE® marks should be cancelled since “Google” is now a common generic word worldwide that means “to search the Internet.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Philanthropy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Google.org" title="Google.org">Google.org</a></div>
<p>In 2004, Google formed a non-profit philanthropic wing that gave all the details to the user with a non profit aim and determination unlike, <a href="/wiki/Google.org" title="Google.org">Google.org</a>, giving it a starting fund of $1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The express mission of the organization is to help with the issues of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>), global public health, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_poverty" title="Global poverty">global poverty</a>. Among its first projects is to develop a viable <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> that can attain <span class="nowrap">0.24 <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">litre/10 km</a>.</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Acquisitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Google_acquisitions" title="List of Google acquisitions">List of Google acquisitions</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Pyra_Labs">Acquisition of Pyra Labs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Pyra Labs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2001, Google has acquired several small companies, often consisting of innovative teams and products. One of the earlier companies that Google bought was <a href="/wiki/Pyra_Labs" title="Pyra Labs">Pyra Labs</a> in 2003. They were the creators of <a href="/wiki/Blogger_(service)" title="Blogger (service)">Blogger</a>, a weblog publishing platform, first launched in 1999. This acquisition led to many premium features becoming free. Pyra Labs was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/Evan_Williams_(Internet_entrepreneur)" title="Evan Williams (Internet entrepreneur)">Evan Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meg_Hourihan" title="Meg Hourihan">Meg Hourihan</a>. Williams would later co-found and launch <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> in 2006, after leaving <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> in 2004. In early 2006, Google acquired Upstartle, a company responsible for the online collaborative word processor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writely" title="Writely">Writely</a>. The technology in this product was combined with Google Spreadsheets to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Docs" title="Google Docs">Google Docs &amp; Spreadsheets</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Android.2C_Inc.">Acquisition of Android, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Android, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Google acquired Android, Inc., and in 2007 announced the <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android mobile operating system</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_YouTube">Acquisition of YouTube</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Acquisition of YouTube">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 9, 2006, Google announced that it would buy the popular online video site <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> for $1.65 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> and maintain YouTube as a separate brand, rather than merging it with Google Video. Meanwhile, Google Video signed an agreement with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_BMG_Music_Entertainment" title="Sony BMG Music Entertainment">Sony BMG Music Entertainment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a>, for both companies to deliver music videos to the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The deal was finalized by November 13, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_JotSpot">Acquisition of JotSpot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Acquisition of JotSpot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 31, 2006, Google announced that it had purchased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JotSpot" title="JotSpot">JotSpot</a>, a company that helped pioneer the market for collaborative, web-based business software, to bolster its position in the online document arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Trendalyzer_and_Adscape">Acquisition of Trendalyzer and Adscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Trendalyzer and Adscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 17, 2007, Google announced its acquisition of two more companies. The first is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gapminder" title="Gapminder">Gapminder</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Trendalyzer" title="Trendalyzer">Trendalyzer</a> software, a company that specializes in developing information technology for provision of free statistics in new visual and animated ways<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> On the same day, Google also announced its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Adscape" title="Adscape">Adscape Media</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/In-game_advertising" title="In-game advertising">in-game advertising</a> company based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnerships">Partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Google has worked with several corporations, in order to improve production and services. On September 28, 2005, Google announced a long-term research partnership with NASA which would involve Google building a 1,000,000-square-foot (93,000 m<sup>2</sup>) R&amp;D center at NASA's <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">Ames Research Center</a>. NASA and Google are planning to work together on a variety of areas, including large-scale data management, massively distributed computing, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">bio</a>-<a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">info</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nano_technology" title="Nano technology">nano</a> convergence, and encouragement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneurial_space_industry" title="Entrepreneurial space industry">entrepreneurial space industry</a>. The new building would also include labs, offices, and housing for Google engineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> In October 2006, Google formed a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> to help share and distribute each other's technologies. As part of the partnership Google will hire employees to help the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> office program <a href="/wiki/OpenOffice.org" title="OpenOffice.org">OpenOffice.org</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sun_partners_with_Google_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_partners_with_Google-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> unit and Google unveiled an expanded partnership on December 21, 2005, including an enhanced global advertising partnership and a US$1 billion investment by Google for a 5% stake in AOL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> As part of the collaboration, Google plans to work with AOL on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_search" title="Video search">video search</a> and offer AOL's premium-video service within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Video" title="Google Video">Google Video</a>. This did not allow users of Google Video to search for AOL's premium-video services. Display advertising throughout the Google network will also increase.</p>
<p>In August 2006, Google signed a $900 million offer with <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corp.'s</a> Fox Interactive Media unit to provide search and advertising on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MySpace" title="MySpace">MySpace</a> and other News Corp. websites including <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">AmericanIdol.com</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox.com" title="Fox.com">Fox.com</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_(USA)" title="Fox Sports (USA)">Fox Sports</a> is not included as a deal already exists between News Corp. and <a href="/wiki/MSN" title="MSN">MSN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 6, 2006, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Sky_Broadcasting" title="British Sky Broadcasting">British Sky Broadcasting</a> released details of a Sky and Google alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> This includes a feature where Gmail will link with Sky and host a mail service for Sky, incorporating the email domain "@sky.com".</p>
<p>In 2007, Google displaced <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">America Online</a> as a key partner and sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/NORAD_Tracks_Santa" title="NORAD Tracks Santa">NORAD Tracks Santa</a> program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-For_more_than_50.C2.A0years.2C_NORAD_is_Tracking_Santa.2C_14_Dec_2007_by_Glenn_Letham_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-For_more_than_50.C2.A0years.2C_NORAD_is_Tracking_Santa.2C_14_Dec_2007_by_Glenn_Letham-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tracking_Santa:_NORAD_.26_Google_Team_Up_For_Christmas.2C_Dec_1.2C_2007.2C_Danny_Sullivan_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tracking_Santa:_NORAD_.26_Google_Team_Up_For_Christmas.2C_Dec_1.2C_2007.2C_Danny_Sullivan-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tracking_Santa.2C_Then_and_Now.2C_November_30.2C_2007.2C_by_Carrie_Farrell_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tracking_Santa.2C_Then_and_Now.2C_November_30.2C_2007.2C_by_Carrie_Farrell-99">[99]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a> was used for the first time to give visitors to the website the impression that they were following <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">Santa Claus</a>' progress in <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">3-D</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behind_the_scenes:_NORAD.27s_Santa_tracker_for_Thur.2C_Dec_21.2C_2009_By_Daniel_Terdiman.2C_CNET_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Behind_the_scenes:_NORAD.27s_Santa_tracker_for_Thur.2C_Dec_21.2C_2009_By_Daniel_Terdiman.2C_CNET-100">[100]</a></sup> The program also made its presence known on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> in 2007 as part of its partnership with Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Instructions_On_Tracking_Santa_With_NORAD_.26_Google:_The_2007_Edition.2C_Dec_24.2C_2007.2C_Danny_Sullivan_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Instructions_On_Tracking_Santa_With_NORAD_.26_Google:_The_2007_Edition.2C_Dec_24.2C_2007.2C_Danny_Sullivan-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2009, Google announced a partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Council_for_Social_Communications" title="Pontifical Council for Social Communications">Pontifical Council for Social Communications</a>, allowing the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> to have his own channel on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2013, Google announced a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Kia_Motors" title="Kia Motors">Kia Motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyundai" title="Hyundai">Hyundai</a>. The partnership integrates Google Maps and Place into new car models to be released later in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".mobi_top-level_domain_.282007.29">.mobi top-level domain (2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: .mobi top-level domain (2007)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In coordination with several of the major corporations, including <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LG" title="LG">LG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ericsson" title="Ericsson">Ericsson</a>, Google provided financial support in the launch of the <a href="/wiki/.mobi" title=".mobi">.mobi</a> top level domain created specifically for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_internet" title="Mobile internet">mobile internet</a>, stating that it is supporting the new domain extension to help set the standards that will define the future of mobile content and improve the experience of Google users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-investors_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-investors-104">[104]</a></sup> In early 2006, Google launched <i>Google.mobi</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_search" title="Mobile search">mobile search</a> portal offering several Google mobile products, including stripped-down versions of its applications and services for mobile users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-105">[105]</a></sup> On September 17, 2007, Google launched "Adsense for Mobile", a service to its publishing partners providing the ability to monetize their mobile websites through the targeted placement of mobile text ads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsense_mobile_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-adsense_mobile-106">[106]</a></sup> Also in September, Google acquired the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_social_network" title="Mobile social network">mobile social networking</a> site, <a href="/wiki/Zingku" title="Zingku">Zingku</a>.mobi to "provide people worldwide with direct access to Google applications, and ultimately the information they want and need, right from their mobile devices."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zingku_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-zingku-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_battles">Legal battles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Legal battles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gonzales_v._Google">Gonzales v. Google</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Gonzales v. Google">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Wednesday, January 18, 2006, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Justice Department</a> filed a <a href="/wiki/Motion_to_compel" title="Motion to compel">motion to compel</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_district_court" title="United States district court">United States district court</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a> seeking a court order that would compel search engine company Google Inc. to turn over "a multi-stage random sample of one million <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URL’s</a>" from Google’s database, and a computer file with "the text of each search string entered onto Google’s search engine over a one-week period (absent any information identifying the person who entered such query)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> Google maintains that their policy has always been to assure its users' privacy and anonymity, and challenged the <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a>. On March 18, 2006, a federal judge ruled that while Google must surrender 50,000 random URLs, the Department of Justice did not meet the necessary burden to force Google to disclose any search terms entered by its users in Google.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bedrock_Computer_Technologies.2C_LLC_vs._Google.2C_Inc">Bedrock Computer Technologies, LLC vs. Google, Inc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Bedrock Computer Technologies, LLC vs. Google, Inc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A jury in Texas awarded Bedrock Computer Technologies $5 million in a <a href="/wiki/Patent_infringement" title="Patent infringement">patent</a> lawsuit against Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> The patent allegedly covered use of <a href="/wiki/Hash_table" title="Hash table">hash tables</a> with <a href="/wiki/Garbage_collection_(computer_science)" title="Garbage collection (computer science)">garbage collection</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separate_chaining" title="Separate chaining">separate chaining</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hat" title="Red Hat">Red Hat</a> <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a>. Google and Bedrock later settled the case and the judgment was <a href="/wiki/Vacated_judgment" title="Vacated judgment">vacated</a> by the court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_tax_avoidance_investigation">UK tax avoidance investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Google&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: UK tax avoidance investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2012, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_government" title="UK government">UK government</a> announced plans to investigate Google, along with <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, for possible <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">tax avoidance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> On 20 January 2016, Google announced that it would pay £130m in back taxes to settle the investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> However, only 8 days later, it was announced that Google could end up paying more, and UK tax officials were under investigation for what has been termed a "sweetheart deal" for Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
