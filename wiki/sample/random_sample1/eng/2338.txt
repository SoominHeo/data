<p><b>Stomatitis</b> is <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of the mouth and lips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaoutis_2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaoutis_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> It refers to any inflammatory process affecting the <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a> of the mouth and lips, with or without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oral_ulceration" title="Oral ulceration">oral ulceration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its widest meaning, stomatitis can have a multitude of different causes and appearances. Common causes include infections, nutritional deficiencies, allergic reactions, radiotherapy, and many others.</p>
<p>When inflammation of the gums and the mouth generally presents itself, sometimes the term <i>gingivostomatitis</i> is used, though this is also sometimes used as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Herpetic_gingivostomatitis" title="Herpetic gingivostomatitis">herpetic gingivostomatitis</a>.</p>
<p>The term is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>stoma</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">στόμα</span>), meaning "mouth", and the suffix <i>-itis</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ῖτις</span>), meaning "inflammation."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_deficiency">Nutritional deficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nutritional deficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a> (improper dietary intake) or <a href="/wiki/Malabsorption" title="Malabsorption">malabsorption</a> (poor absorption of nutrients into the body) can lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrition_disorder" title="Nutrition disorder">nutritional deficiency states</a>, several of which can lead to stomatitis. For example, deficiencies of <a href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency" title="Iron deficiency">iron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B2" title="Vitamin B2">vitamin B2</a> (riboflavin),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:490</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B3" title="Vitamin B3">vitamin B3</a> (niacin), <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B6" title="Vitamin B6">vitamin B6</a> (pyridoxine), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B9" title="Vitamin B9">vitamin B9</a> (folic acid) or <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B12</a> (cobalamine) may all manifest as stomatitis. Iron is necessary for the upregulation of transcriptional elements for cell replication and repair. Lack of iron can cause genetic downregulation of these elements, leading to ineffective repair and regeneration of epithelial cells, especially in the mouth and lips. Many disorders which cause malabsorption can cause deficiencies, which in turn causes stomatitis. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Tropical_sprue" title="Tropical sprue">tropical sprue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada2009_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:49</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aphthous_stomatitis">Aphthous stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aphthous stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aphthous_stomatitis" title="Aphthous stomatitis">Aphthous stomatitis</a></div>
<p>Aphthous stomatitis (canker sores) is the recurrent appearance of mouth ulcers in otherwise healthy individuals. The cause is not completely understood, but it is thought that the condition represents a <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cell</a> mediated immune response which is triggered by a variety of factors. The individual ulcers (aphthae) recur periodically and heal completely, although in the more severe forms new ulcers may appear in other parts of the mouth before the old ones have finished healing. Aphthous stomatitis is one of the most common diseases of the oral mucosa, and is thought to affect about 20% of the general population to some degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neville_2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neville_2002-4">[4]</a></sup> The symptoms range from a minor nuisance to being disabling in their impact on eating, swallowing and talking, and the severe forms can cause people to lose weight. There is no cure for aphthous stomatitis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and therapies are aimed at alleviating the pain, reducing the inflammation and promoting healing of the ulcers, but there is little evidence of efficacy for any treatment that has been used.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angular_stomatitis">Angular stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Angular stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Angular_cheilitis" title="Angular cheilitis">Angular cheilitis</a></div>
<p>Inflammation of the corners (angles) of the lips is termed angular stomatitis or angular cheilitis. In children a frequent cause is repeated lip-licking, and in adults it may be a sign of underlying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency_anemia" title="Iron deficiency anemia">iron deficiency anemia</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B" title="Vitamin B">vitamin B</a> deficiencies (<i>e.g.</i>, B<sub>2</sub>-<a href="/wiki/Riboflavin" title="Riboflavin">riboflavin</a>, B<sub>9</sub>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">B<sub>12</sub></a>-<a href="/wiki/Cobalamin" title="Cobalamin">cobalamin</a>, which in turn may be evidence of poor diets or malnutrition such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a>).</p>
<p>Also, angular cheilitis can be caused by a patient's jaws at rest being 'overclosed' due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edentulous" title="Edentulous">edentulousness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tooth_wear" title="Tooth wear">tooth wear</a>, causing the jaws to come to rest closer together than if the complete/unaffected <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a> were present. This causes skin folds around the angle of the mouth which are kept moist by saliva, which in turn favours infection; mostly by <i>Candida albicans</i> or similar species. Treatment usually involves the administration of topical <a href="/wiki/Nystatin" title="Nystatin">nystatin</a> or similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antifungal_medication" title="Antifungal medication">antifungal</a> agents. Another treatment can be to correct the jaw relationship with dental treatment (<i>e.g.</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denture" title="Denture">dentures</a> or <a href="/wiki/Occlusion_(dentistry)" title="Occlusion (dentistry)">occlusal</a> adjustment).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denture-related_stomatitis">Denture-related stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Denture-related stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Denture-related_stomatitis" title="Denture-related stomatitis">Denture-related stomatitis</a></div>
<p>This is a common condition present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denture" title="Denture">denture</a> wearers. It appears as reddened but painless mucosa beneath the denture. 90% of cases are associated with <a href="/wiki/Candida_(fungus)" title="Candida (fungus)">Candidia species</a>, and it is the most common form of <a href="/wiki/Oral_candidiasis" title="Oral candidiasis">oral candidiasis</a>. Treatment is by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antifungal_medication" title="Antifungal medication">antifungal medication</a> and improved dental hygiene, such as not wearing the denture during sleep.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allergic_contact_stomatitis">Allergic contact stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allergic contact stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Allergic contact stomatitis (also termed "allergic gingivostomatitis" or "allergic contact gingivostomatitis")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Type_IV_hypersensitivity" title="Type IV hypersensitivity">type IV (delayed) hypersensitivity</a> reaction that occurs in susceptible <a href="/wiki/Atopy" title="Atopy">atopic</a> individuals when <a href="/wiki/Allergen" title="Allergen">allergens</a> penetrate the skin or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mucosa" title="Mucosa">mucosa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Allergens, which may be different for different individuals, combine with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epithelial" title="Epithelial">epithelial</a>-derived <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Hapten" title="Hapten">haptens</a> which bind with <a href="/wiki/Langerhans_cell" title="Langerhans cell">Langerhans cells</a> in the mucosa, which in turn present the antigen on their surface to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_lymphocyte" title="T lymphocyte">T lymphocytes</a>, sensitizing them to that antigen and causing them to produce many specific <a href="/wiki/Clone_(cell_biology)" title="Clone (cell biology)">clones</a>. The second time that specific antigen is encountered, an inflammatory reaction is triggered at the site of exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> Allergic contact stomatitis is less common than <a href="/wiki/Allergic_contact_dermatitis" title="Allergic contact dermatitis">allergic contact dermatitis</a> because the mouth is coated in saliva, which washes away antigens and acts as a barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> The oral mucosa is also more vascular (has a better blood supply) than skin, meaning that any antigens are more quickly removed from the area by the circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> Finally, there is substantially less <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oral_mucosa" title="Oral mucosa">oral mucosa</a>, meaning that there is less likelihood that haptans will form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Allergic contact stomatitis appears as non-specific inflammation, so it may be mistaken for chronic physical irritation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> There may be burning or soreness of the mouth and ulceration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> Chronic exposure to the allergen may result in a <a href="/wiki/Lichen_planus" title="Lichen planus">lichenoid lesion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plasma_cell_gingivitis" title="Plasma cell gingivitis">Plasma cell gingivitis</a> may also occur, which may be accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Glossitis" title="Glossitis">glossitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheilitis" title="Cheilitis">cheilitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epikutanni-test.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/180px-Epikutanni-test.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/270px-Epikutanni-test.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/360px-Epikutanni-test.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Patch_test" title="Patch test">Patch test</a></div>

<p>Allergens that may cause allergic contact stomatitis in some individuals include <a href="/wiki/Cinnamaldehyde" title="Cinnamaldehyde">cinnamaldehyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balsam_of_Peru" title="Balsam of Peru">Balsam of Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peppermint" title="Peppermint">peppermint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyrophosphate" title="Pyrophosphate">pyrophosphates</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zinc_citrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zinc citrate (page does not exist)">zinc citrate</a>, free <a href="/wiki/Acrylate_polymer" title="Acrylate polymer">acrylic</a> monomer, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_lauryl_sulfate" title="Sodium lauryl sulfate">sodium lauryl sulfate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-google2-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google5_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-google5-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dermnetnz1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dermnetnz1-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These allergens may originate from many sources, including various foods and drink, chewing gum, toothpaste, mouthwash, dental floss, dental fillings, dentures, orthodontic bands or wires, and many other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup> If the substance containing the allergen comes into contact with the lips, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergic_contact_cheilitis" title="Allergic contact cheilitis">allergic contact cheilitis</a> can occur, together with allergic contact stomatitis.</p>
<p>The diagnosis is confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Patch_test" title="Patch test">patch test</a>, and management is by avoidance of exposure to the allergen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glick2003_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Glick2003-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migratory_stomatitis">Migratory stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Migratory stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geographic_tongue" title="Geographic tongue">Geographic tongue</a></div>
<p>Migratory stomatitis (or geographic stomatitis) is an atypical presentation of a condition which normally presents on the tongue, termed geographic tongue. Geographic tongue is so named because there are <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">atrophic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erythema" title="Erythema">erythematous</a> areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depapillation" title="Depapillation">depapillation</a> that migrate over time, giving a map-like appearance.</p>
<p>In migratory stomatitis, other mucosal sites in the mouth, such as the ventral surface (undersurface) of the tongue, buccal mucosa, labial mucosa, soft palate, or floor of mouth may be afflicted with identical lesions, usually in addition to the tongue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruch_2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruch_2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Apart from not being restricted to the tongue, migratory stomatitis is an identical condition in every regard to geographic tongue. Another synonym for geographic tongue which uses the term stomatitis is "stomatitis areata migrans".</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Herpetic_gingivostomatitis" title="Herpetic gingivostomatitis">Herpetic gingivostomatitis</a></div>
<p>This is inflammation of the mouth caused by <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">herpes simplex virus</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irradiation_and_chemotherapy">Irradiation and chemotherapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Irradiation and chemotherapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stomatitis may also be caused by chemotherapy, or radiation therapy of the oropharyngeal area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The term <i><a href="/wiki/Mucositis" title="Mucositis">mucositis</a></i> is sometimes used synonymously with stomatitis, however the former refers to mucosal reactions to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>, and may occur anywhere in the gastrointestinal tract and not just in the mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Necrotizing_ulcerative_gingivostomatitis">Necrotizing ulcerative gingivostomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Necrotizing ulcerative gingivostomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See <a href="/wiki/Necrotizing_periodontal_diseases" title="Necrotizing periodontal diseases">Necrotizing periodontal diseases</a></div>
<p>The term <i>necrotizing ulcerative gingivostomatitis</i> is sometimes used as a synonym of the necrotizing periodontal disease more commonly termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necrotizing_ulcerative_gingivitis" title="Necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis">necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis</a>, or a more severe form (also termed necrotizing stomatitis). The term <i>necrotizing gingivostomatitis</i> is also sometimes used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stomatitis_nicotina">Stomatitis nicotina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stomatitis nicotina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stomatitis_nicotina" title="Stomatitis nicotina">Stomatitis nicotina</a></div>
<p>Also called smoker's palatal keratosis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulthard2008_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coulthard2008-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:176</sup> this condition may occur in smokers, especially pipe smokers. The palate appears dry and cracked, and white from <a href="/wiki/Keratosis" title="Keratosis">keratosis</a>. The minor <a href="/wiki/Salivary_gland" title="Salivary gland">salivary glands</a> appear as small, red and swollen bumps. It is not a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premalignant_condition" title="Premalignant condition">premalignant condition</a>, and the appearance reverses if the smoking is stopped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulthard2008_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coulthard2008-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:176</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic_ulcerative_stomatitis">Chronic ulcerative stomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chronic ulcerative stomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chronic ulcerative stomatitis is a recently discovered condition with specific immunopathologic features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solomon_2008_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solomon_2008-19">[19]</a></sup> It is characterized by <a href="/wiki/Mouth_ulcer#Definition" title="Mouth ulcer">erosions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oral_ulceration" title="Oral ulceration">ulcerations</a> which relapse and remit. Lesions are located on the buccal mucosa (inside of the cheeks) or on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gingiva" title="Gingiva">gingiva</a> (gums).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fourie_2011_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fourie_2011-21">[21]</a></sup> The condition resembles <a href="/wiki/Lichen_planus#Oral_lichen_planus" title="Lichen planus">Oral lichen planus</a> when biopsied.</p>
<p>The diagnosis is made with <a href="/wiki/Immunofluorescence" title="Immunofluorescence">Immunofluorescence</a> techniques, which shows circulating and tissue-bound <a href="/wiki/Autoantibody" title="Autoantibody">autoantibodies</a> (particulate stratified squamous-epithelium-specific antinuclear antibody) to DeltaNp63alpha protein, a normal component of the <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a>. Treatment is with <a href="/wiki/Hydroxychloroquine" title="Hydroxychloroquine">hydroxychloroquine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solomon_2008_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Solomon_2008-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasma_cell_gingivostomatitis">Plasma cell gingivostomatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stomatitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Plasma cell gingivostomatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plasma_cell_gingivitis" title="Plasma cell gingivitis">plasma cell gingivitis</a></div>
<p>Terms such as <i>plasma cell gingivostomatitis</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neville_2001_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neville_2001-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>atypical gingivostomatitis</i> and <i>idiopathic gingivostomatitis</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janam_2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janam_2012-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> are sometimes a synonym for plasma cell gingivitis, or specifically to refer to a severe form of plasma cell gingivitis.</p>
