<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Saseongam&amp;params=35.179684_N_127.480772_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°10′47″N</span> <span class="longitude">127°28′51″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.179684°N 127.480772°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.179684; 127.480772</span></span></span></a></span></span></span> <b>Saseong Hermitage</b>, <b>Saseongam</b>, is located in Gurye County on Mount Osan, a foothill of <a href="/wiki/Jirisan" title="Jirisan">Jirisan</a> (Jiri Mountain) in the province of Jeollanam-do, South Korea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saseongam is said to have been built in 544, the 22nd year of the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Seong_of_Baekje" title="Seong of Baekje">Seong</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baekche" title="Baekche">Baekche</a>, by Priest Yeongi Josa (연기조사), but there are no records to substantiate it. Yeongi Josa is also credited with establishing the nearby Hwaseomsa and Yeongoksa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-san1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-san1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initially the hermitage was called Osanam, being positioned near the 531 meter/1,742 foot summit of mount Osan, before it was renamed Saseongam, or the Hermitage of Four Saints, in honor of the four high priests Yongi Chosa, <a href="/wiki/Wonhyo" title="Wonhyo">Wonhyo</a> Daesa, Toseon Kuksa, and Chinkak Seonsa, that lived, worked, and studied here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sites_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sites-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Records do indicate that both Great Master Wonhyo Daesa and National Master <a href="/wiki/Doseon" title="Doseon">Doseon</a> Guksa practiced the <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a> in retreat on these cliffs when they visited this area.</p>
<p>Hermitage buildings were reconstructed under supervision of National Meditation-Master Jingak Seonsa (1178–1234). The Main Hall was reconstructed in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-san1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-san1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is believed that the high priests practiced <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a> (the doctrine that through renunciation of worldly pleasures it is possible to achieve a high spiritual or intellectual state by rigorous self-denial and active self-restraint) here for an interval from the closing period of Unified <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> era through the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> Kingdom era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two halls, at the front of the hermitage are supported by large painted pillars, are set against the steep granite cliff, and two other halls rest atop a crest to the west of the main hall. Around Mount Osan are found many peculiarly shaped rocks, one of which has an engraving of a standing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_hall">Main hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Main hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG/250px-Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG/375px-Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG/500px-Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Saseongam_11-08020_Main_Hall.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Maaeyeoraeipsang carved into the rock face is visible through a large glass window on back wall of the main hall</div>

<p>The Main Hall sits atop three massive red wood pillars positioned in front a standing Buddha cut into rock face, called Maaeyeoraeipsang (마애여래입상). The Buddha carved into the rock face is visible through a large glass window that makes up most of the back wall of the main hall.</p>
<p>Cultural authorities estimate that Maaeyeoraeipsang dates back to the early <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> Dynasty. A steep stone stairway leads up to a bridge that crosses over to the main hall.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jijangjeon">Jijangjeon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jijangjeon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jijangjeon (hall) is found up a second set of stone stairways to the west of the main hall behind a lesser hall perched atop two large red wood pillars. Jijangjeon is a tribute building for the Bodhisattva <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ksitigarbha" title="Ksitigarbha">Jijang</a>, or Ksitigarbha, the Bodhisattva who cures suffering. Jijang goes to the various levels of hell and pleads with the souls of men to repent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanshingak">Sanshingak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sanshingak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sanshingak (Mountain-spirit Shrine) is a small hall dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shanshen" title="Shanshen">Sanshin</a> behind Jijangjeon. Sanshin are the local gods of mountains typically represented as an elder male figure surrounded by tigers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-dk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doseongul">Doseongul</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Doseongul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doseongul (cave) is really just a passageway between gigantic cliff-boulders next to Sanshingak. It is said that Great Monk Wonhyo Daesa spent some time meditating in here, and that 200 years later National Master Doseon Guksa lived here for a short time while working on the theory of Korea's adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Feng_shui" title="Feng shui">Feng shui</a>, Pungsu-jiri-seol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-dk-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-san2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-san2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_Property_Material">Cultural Property Material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saseongam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultural Property Material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saseongam is Jeollanamdo Cultural Property Material #33.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-dk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
