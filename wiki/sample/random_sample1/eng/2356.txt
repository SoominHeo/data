<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Symbion</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Symbion</b></i> is the name of a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of aquatic animals, less than ½ mm wide, found living attached to the bodies of cold-water lobsters. They have sac-like bodies, and three distinctly different forms in different parts of their two-stage life-cycle. They appear so different from other animals that they were assigned their own, new <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> <b>Cycliophora</b> shortly after they were discovered in 1995. This was the first new <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> of multicelled organism to be discovered since the <a href="/wiki/Loricifera" title="Loricifera">Loricifera</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symbion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_bilaterial_animal_orders" title="List of bilaterial animal orders">List of bilaterial animal orders</a></div>
<p><i><b>Symbion</b></i> was discovered in 1995 by <a href="/wiki/Reinhardt_Kristensen" title="Reinhardt Kristensen">Reinhardt Kristensen</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Funch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Funch (page does not exist)">Peter Funch</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-KF-2">[2]</a></sup> on the mouthparts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norway_lobster" title="Norway lobster">Norway lobster</a> (<i>Nephrops norvegicus</i>), and other, related, species have since been discovered on:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/American_lobster" title="American lobster">American lobster</a> (<i>Homarus americanus</i>, host to <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Symbion_americanus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Symbion americanus (page does not exist)">Symbion americanus</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18937332_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18937332-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_lobster" title="European lobster">European lobster</a> (<i>Homarus gammarus</i>, host to an as yet unnamed species of <i>Symbion</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The genus is so named because of its <a href="/wiki/Commensalism" title="Commensalism">commensal relationship</a> with the lobster (a form of <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a>) – it feeds on the leftovers from the lobster's own meals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Symbion</b></i> are peculiar microscopic <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, with no obvious close relatives, and which was therefore given its own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylum_(biology)" title="Phylum (biology)">phylum</a>, called <b>Cycliophora</b>. The phylogenetic position of <i>Symbion</i> remains unclear: originally the phyla <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectoprocta" title="Ectoprocta">Ectoprocta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entoprocta" title="Entoprocta">Entoprocta</a> were considered possible relatives of <i>Symbion</i>, based on structural similarities. However, genetic studies suggest that <i>Symbion</i> may be more closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnathifera_(phylum)" title="Gnathifera (phylum)">Gnathifera</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symbion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Symbion pandora</i> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_(biology)#Bilateral_symmetry" title="Symmetry (biology)">bilateral</a>, sac-like body with no <a href="/wiki/Body_cavity" title="Body cavity">coelom</a>. There are three basic life stages:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Asexual Feeding Stage</b> – At this stage, <i>S. pandora</i> is neither male nor female. It has a length of 347 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">μm</a> and a width of 113 μm. On the posterior end of the sac-like body is a stalk with an adhesive disc, which attaches itself to the host. On the anterior end is a ciliated funnel (mouth) and an anus.</li>
<li><b>Sexual Stage</b>
<ul>
<li><b>Male</b> – <i>S. pandora</i> has a length of 84 μm and a width of 42 μm during this stage. It has no mouth or anus, which signifies the absence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a>. It also has two reproductive organs.</li>
<li><b>Female</b> – <i>S. pandora</i> is the same size as the male in this stage. It does, however, have a digestive system which collapses and reconstitutes itself as a <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KF_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-KF-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Symbion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Symbion</i> can reproduce both <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexually</a> by <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a> and sexually. In sexual reproduction the male attaches to a feeding stage and impregnates a budding female. The female then separates from the feeding stage and attaches herself to another host, where the larva in her develops. The female dies, and the larva escapes. The larval stage may be unscientifically referred to as sea worms. The sexual reproductive cycle is triggered when the host crustacean molts its skin in order to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
