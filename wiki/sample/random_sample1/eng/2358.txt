<p><b>Copper Canyon</b> (Spanish: <b>Barrancas del Cobre</b>) is a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canyons" title="Canyons">canyons</a> consisting of six distinct canyons in the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre Occidental</a> in the southwestern part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_state" title="Mexican state">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua_(state)" title="Chihuahua (state)">Chihuahua</a> in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The canyons were formed by six <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> that drain the western side of the Sierra Tarahumara (a part of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre Occidental</a>). All six rivers merge into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Fuerte" title="Rio Fuerte">Rio Fuerte</a> and empty into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a>. The walls of the canyon are a copper/green color, which is where the name originates.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spanish</a> arrived in the Copper Canyon area in the 17th century and encountered the indigenous locals throughout Chihuahua. For the New Spanish, <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a> was a new land to explore for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> and also to spread Christianity. The New Spanish named the people they encountered "<a href="/wiki/Rar%C3%A1muri_people" title="Rarámuri people">Tarahumara</a>", derived from the word Rarámuri, which is what the indigenous people call their men. Some scholars theorize that this word may mean ‘The running people'<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. During the 17th century, silver was discovered by the Hispanic in the land of the Tarahumara tribe. Some were enslaved for mining efforts<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. There were small uprisings by the Tarahumara, but to little avail<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. They eventually were forced off the more desirable lands and up into the canyon cliffs.</p>

<p>The alpine climate of the mountainous regions of Copper Canyon has moderate<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2017)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> temperatures from October to November and March to April. The bottom of the canyons are humid and warm<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2017)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> and remain that way throughout the year. During the warmest months, April through June, drought is a chronic problem with little rainfall until July when the rainy season begins.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sierra Tarahumara Occidental region contains some twenty-three species of <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> and two hundred species of <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_Douglas-fir" title="Mexican Douglas-fir">Mexican Douglas-fir</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pseudotsuga_lindleyana" title="Pseudotsuga lindleyana">Pseudotsuga lindleyana</a></i>) trees cover the high plateaus in altitudes over 8,000 feet (2,400 m), but due to deforestation in the area, many species of wildlife are endangered. <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">Cougars</a> live in the remotest of regions and are rarely seen. After the summer rainy season these upper regions blossom with wildflowers until October.</p>
<p>From 4,000–8,000 feet (1,200–2,400 m), <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees grow in the huge forests as well as the more shade-tolerant types of trees. In the fall the forests become brilliant with color from Andean alder (<i><a href="/wiki/Alnus_acuminata" title="Alnus acuminata">Alnus acuminata</a></i>) and poplar (<i><a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">Populus</a></i> spp.) trees. Brushwood and scrubby trees grow on the canyon slopes, which can accommodate the dry season. Huge fig (<i><a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">Ficus</a></i> spp.) and <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palm trees</a> thrive at the bottom where water is plentiful and the climate is tropical.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_to_the_ecosystem">Threats to the ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Threats to the ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to increases in human population, there are many threats to the ecosystems of the Sierra Tarahumara Occidental region. The government funding to build a “tourist friendly” atmosphere poses threats to the environment and indigenous cultures. Roads have been built in the former isolated mountainous zones. Agriculture and grazing as well as the cutting of hardwoods and other trees for <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">firewood</a> has accelerated a soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> problem. <a href="/wiki/Mesquite" title="Mesquite">Mesquite</a> (<i>Prosopis</i> spp.) and desert ironwood (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olneya_tesota" title="Olneya tesota">Olneya tesota</a></i>) trees are cut and exported primarily to the U.S. for charcoal. Amapa (<i><a href="/wiki/Tabebuia_chrysantha" title="Tabebuia chrysantha">Tabebuia chrysantha</a></i>) trees yield highly prized lumber for building and furniture making. Other trees are also cut and sold for their high-priced lumber. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over_harvesting" title="Over harvesting">Over harvesting</a> of the forests in the area has caused the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_woodpecker" title="Imperial woodpecker">imperial woodpecker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexican_wolf" title="Mexican wolf">Mexican wolf</a>. Approximately, two percent of the original <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">old-growth forest</a> remains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, a massive forest-harvesting project in the region has been abandoned, for now, by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>. The Mexican forestry department deemed these species of trees “legally protected,” but enforcement is difficult.</p>
<p>The government has taken measures to halt or slow down the cultivation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opium_poppy" title="Opium poppy">opium poppies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> by spraying crops with <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a>, which threaten the populations of many different species. A large <a href="/wiki/Saturniinae" title="Saturniinae">saturnid moth</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rothschildia" title="Rothschildia">Rothschildia</a> cincta</i>, are one of the species that are threatened by the spraying. Their <a href="/wiki/Pupa#Cocoon" title="Pupa">cocoons</a> are used by the native population for ceremonial purposes.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Open-pit_mining" title="Open-pit mining">Open-pit mining</a> for <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and other metals not only produces <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> from <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelters</a>, but has been linked to the serious decline of the Tarahumara frog (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rana_tarahumarae" title="Rana tarahumarae">Rana tarahumarae</a></i>). Every river system has been dammed causing fresh water shortages in nearby desert communities. An enormous dam is being constructed on the Rio Fuerte, which poses major environmental problems and may lead to massive losses of tropical forest and habitats.</p>
<p>Conservation is underway, but remains informal and slow. Mexico has environmental laws, but suffers from lack of financial resources. Enforcement has been lax or non-existent. Agencies are actively trying to increase the protection for natural preserves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Copper Canyon traditional inhabitants are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarahumara" title="Tarahumara">Tarahumara</a> or Rarámuri. With no official census, the population of the Rarámuri people probably ranges between 35,000 and 70,000. Many Rarámuri reside in the cooler, mountainous regions during the hot summer months and migrate deeper into the canyons in the cooler winter months, where the climate is more temperate. Their survival strategies have been to occupy areas that are too remote for city people, way off-the-beaten-path, to remain isolated and independent, so as to avoid losing their culture. Their diet is largely domestic agrarian, but does consist of meat from domesticated cows, chickens and goats, wild game, and freshwater fish. Corn (maize) is the most important staple of the Rarámuri’s diet. The Rarámuri people are known for their endurance running. Living in the canyons, they travel great vertical distances, which they often do by running nonstop for hours. A popular Rarámuri community race called <a href="/wiki/Rarajipari" title="Rarajipari">rarajipari</a>, is played by kicking a wooden ball along the paths of the steep canyons. Tourism is a growing industry for Copper Canyon, but the acceptance of it is debated in the local communities. Some communities accept government funding for building roads, restaurants and lodging to make the area attractive for tourists. Many other groups of Rarámuri maintain their independence by living in areas that are as far away from city life as possible. Their way of life is protected by the mountainous landscape.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many other ways to explore Copper Canyon such as hiking, biking, driving or horseback riding. The most popular way is by train, as the <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarril_Chihuahua_al_Pac%C3%ADfico" title="Ferrocarril Chihuahua al Pacífico">Ferrocarril Chihuahua al Pacífico</a> or ChePe, runs along the main canyon called Canyon Urique, between Chihuahua and Los Mochis, on the Gulf of California.</p>
<p>The Chihuahua al Pacifico began in the late 19th century. The <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">revolution</a>, lack of funding, and the overall difficulty of building a railroad over such terrain hindered its completion until 1961. The railroad comprises 405 miles of rails with 39 bridges and 86 tunnels. The total trip takes approximately 15 hours and passes through towns, as well as the towering cliffs of the canyons. Along the railway, many Tarahumarans lay out their food, crafts and other wares for sale.</p>
<p>Mexico established the Parque Nacional Barranca del Cobre (Copper Canyon National Park) to showcase this remote area. The park is located in the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Batopilas_Municipality" title="Batopilas Municipality">Batopilas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bocoyna_Municipality" title="Bocoyna Municipality">Bocoyna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guachochi_Municipality" title="Guachochi Municipality">Guachochi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urique_Municipality" title="Urique Municipality">Urique</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basaseachic_Falls_National_Park" title="Basaseachic Falls National Park">Basaseachic Falls National Park</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Basaseachic_Falls" title="Basaseachic Falls">Basaseachic Falls</a> is located within the canyon area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cities_and_towns">Cities and towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cities and towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the villages located in or on the Copper Canyon are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bahuichivo,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bahuichivo, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Bahuichivo</a>, ChePe train stop for Cerocahui, Urique, Piedras Verdes, Tubares</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Basaseachi,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Basaseachi, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Basaseachi</a>, located near the pouroff of 840 ft (246m) Cascada Basaseachi in the Barranca Candameña. The towns and ranchos of San Lorenzo, Cahuisori, and Huahumar encircle the rim of the canyon, also home to Mexico's highest waterfall <a href="/wiki/Piedra_Volada" title="Piedra Volada">Piedra Volada</a> (1200 m). It is on the main <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Federal_Highway_16" title="Mexican Federal Highway 16">Federal Highway 16</a> between Chihuahua, Chihuahua, and Hermosillo, Sonora. The Rio Candameña is a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Mayo_River_(Mexico)" title="Mayo River (Mexico)">Rio Mayo</a> which flows into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Batopilas,_Chihuahua" title="Batopilas, Chihuahua">Batopilas</a>, elevation 600 m, a town on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Batopilas_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Batopilas River (page does not exist)">Batopilas River</a> at the bottom of a canyon; first established by the Spanish around 1632 to mine silver. It is located 30 km southeast of Urique.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bocoyna,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bocoyna, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Bocoyna</a>, 30 km east of Creel, and on the eastern escarpment of the continental divide. The nearby <a href="/wiki/Rio_Conchos" title="Rio Conchos">Rio Conchos</a> flows into the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Bravo (Rio Grande)</a> on the Mexico-Texas border. <a href="/wiki/Carich%C3%AD" title="Carichí">Carichí</a>, Sisoguichi, and Panalachi are important Tarahumara settlements on the Rio Conchos drainage system.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cerocahui,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cerocahui, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Cerocahui</a>, 14 km S. of the train stop at Bahuichivo.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Creel,_Chihuahua" title="Creel, Chihuahua">Creel</a>, atop the canyon and, at 2,340 metres (7,677 ft) altitude, marking one of the highest points on the <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarril_Chihuahua_al_Pac%C3%ADfico" title="Ferrocarril Chihuahua al Pacífico">ChePe railroad</a> route (San Juanito is higher at 2,400 metres (7,874 ft)); a central point for commerce and tourism.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Divisadero,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Divisadero, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Divisadero</a>, a key train stop and vista point with amazing views down into the Urique Canyon of the Barranca del Cobre. The ChePe train allows a 15-20 minute stop for visitors to enjoy the view. Divisadero and nearby Areponapuchi (located 4 km south) are major canyon-rim trailheads for hiking into the Rio Urique canyon. This high mesa is home to three tourist class hotels strategically located on the canyon rim, and several low budget guesthouses offering basic accommodations with meals included.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Temoris,_Chihuahua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Temoris, Chihuahua (page does not exist)">Témoris</a>, a dual town located 400 m apart in elevation. The ChePe train traverses the valley 3 times including a mile long tunnel to gain elevation. Located on the Rio Septentrion, lower Temoris is at 1000 m.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Urique" title="Urique">Urique</a>, 560 m. Located at the bottom of the canyon rim below Bahuichivo, on the Urique River. It is 30 km NW of Batopilas, now connected by a rough road.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Copper Canyon was featured on Season 1 Episode 12, of <i><a href="/wiki/Man_vs._Wild" title="Man vs. Wild">Man vs. Wild</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>, on <i>Raramuri Tale</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since January 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> a short film about a Raramuri boy, his mother and the teachings of the Tarahumaras, in the Film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Burials_of_Melquiades_Estrada" title="The Three Burials of Melquiades Estrada">The Three Burials of Melquiades Estrada</a></i>, and in an animated series called Max Steel on Disney XD. Copper Canyon was also a destination in Motorcycle Mania 3, a feature that aired on the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>.</p>
<p><i>In the Sierra Madre</i>, a book by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Biggers" title="Jeff Biggers">Jeff Biggers</a>, looks at a Tarahumara village in the Copper Canyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The nonfiction book <i>Born to Run</i> by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_McDougall" title="Christopher McDougall">Christopher McDougall</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> chronicling the story of ultra-runner <a href="/wiki/Micah_True" title="Micah True">Micah True</a> in the Copper Canyon with the Tarahumara Indians, who taught him a better way to run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOX_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOX-5">[5]</a></sup> True was the race director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_Canyon_Ultra_Marathon" title="Copper Canyon Ultra Marathon">Copper Canyon Ultra Marathon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outside_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Outside-7">[7]</a></sup> which ends in <a href="/wiki/Urique" title="Urique">Urique</a>'s plaza. The race covers 50 miles (80 km) of single track trail and dirt road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outside_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Outside-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
