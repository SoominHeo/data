<p><b><span class="nowrap">(357439) 2004 BL<sub>86</sub></span></b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150113_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150113-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">provisionally known</a> as <b><span class="nowrap">2004 BL<sub>86</sub></span></b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid" title="Near-Earth asteroid">near-Earth asteroid</a> estimated to be about 325 meters (1,066 feet) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA20150126_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA20150126-3">[3]</a></sup> It was discovered on 30 January 2004 by <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Near-Earth_Asteroid_Research" title="Lincoln Near-Earth Asteroid Research">LINEAR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2004-B80_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2004-B80-1">[1]</a></sup> It passed 1,199,600 km (745,400 mi), or 3.1 <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">lunar distances</a>, from Earth on 26 January 2015 at 16:20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150113_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150113-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-6">[6]</a></sup> During the 2015 approach it was determined to have a satellite.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_Earth_approach">2015 Earth approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(357439)_2004_BL86&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 2015 Earth approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 26–27 January 2015, the asteroid briefly peaked around <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> 9 and was near the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2015-7">[7]</a></sup> The asteroid was visible in <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> with <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">objectives</a> of 100 mm (4 in) or larger; high-end <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> under a dark sky may also have worked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musgrave20150123_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Musgrave20150123-8">[8]</a></sup> Near closest approach the asteroid was moving about 2.5 degrees per hour (2.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> per second).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2015_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2015-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st20150122_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-st20150122-9">[9]</a></sup> The asteroid came to <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(planets)" title="Opposition (planets)">opposition</a> (furthest <a href="/wiki/Elongation_(astronomy)" title="Elongation (astronomy)">elongation</a> in the sky from the Sun) on 27 January 2015 at 04:37 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEODyS2015_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-NEODyS2015-7">[7]</a></sup> Around 5:00 UTC, the asteroid was near M44 (the <a href="/wiki/Beehive_Cluster" title="Beehive Cluster">Beehive Cluster</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st20150122_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-st20150122-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>26 January 2015 approach of 3.1 lunar distances was the closest approach of <span class="nowrap">2004 BL<sub>86</sub></span> for at least the next 200 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150113_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150113-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twitter20150207_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-twitter20150207-10">[10]</a></sup> For comparison, <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/2015_TB145" title="2015 TB145">2015 TB<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">145</span></span></a></span> about twice the size of <span class="nowrap">2004 BL<sub>86</sub></span>, passed 486,800 km (302,500 mi), or 1.3 lunar distances, from Earth on 31 October 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015TB145_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-2015TB145-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite">Satellite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(357439)_2004_BL86&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Satellite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A satellite was first detected by ground-based telescopes by Joe Pollock and Petr Pravec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRAO20151030_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRAO20151030-12">[12]</a></sup> Observations by the <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Bank_Telescope" title="Green Bank Telescope">Green Bank Telescope</a> confirmed that it is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">binary asteroid</a> with a secondary roughly 70 meters (230 feet) across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA20150126_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA20150126-3">[3]</a></sup> The secondary is estimated to orbit at least 500 metres (1,600 ft) from the primary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> About 16% of asteroids over 200 metres (660 ft) in diameter are thought to be binaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA20150126_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA20150126-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
