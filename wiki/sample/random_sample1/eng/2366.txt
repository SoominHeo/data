<p>A <b>lifeboat</b> is a small, rigid or inflatable <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> carried for emergency evacuation in the event of a disaster aboard a ship. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifeboat_drill" title="Lifeboat drill">Lifeboat drills</a> are required by law on larger commercial ships. Rafts (<a href="/wiki/Raft" title="Raft">liferafts</a>) are also used. In the military, a lifeboat may double as a <a href="/wiki/Whaleboat" title="Whaleboat">whaleboat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dinghy" title="Dinghy">dinghy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Captain%27s_gig" title="Captain's gig">gig</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_tender" title="Ship's tender">ship's tenders</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ship" title="Cruise ship">cruise ships</a> often double as lifeboats. Recreational sailors usually carry inflatable life rafts, though a few prefer small proactive lifeboats that are harder to sink and can be sailed to safety.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="720" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg/220px-Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg/330px-Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg/440px-Portland_Pudgy_lifeboat_sailing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Inflatable lifeboats may be equipped with auto-inflation (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>) canisters or mechanical pumps. A quick release and pressure release mechanism is fitted on ships so that the canister or pump automatically inflates the lifeboat, and the lifeboat breaks free of the sinking vessel. Commercial aircraft are also required to carry auto-inflating life rafts in case of an emergency <a href="/wiki/Water_landing" title="Water landing">water landing</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offshore_oil_platform" title="Offshore oil platform">offshore oil platforms</a> also have liferafts.</p>
<p>Ship-launched lifeboats are lowered from <a href="/wiki/Davit" title="Davit">davits</a> on a ship's deck, and are hard to sink in normal circumstances. The cover serves as protection from sun, wind and rain, can be used to collect rainwater, and is normally made of a reflective or fluorescent material that is highly visible. Lifeboats have oars, flares and mirrors for signaling, first aid supplies, and food and water for several days. Some lifeboats are more capably equipped to permit self-rescue, with supplies such as a radio, an engine and sail, heater, navigational equipment, solar water stills, rainwater catchments and fishing equipment.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" title="International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea">International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea</a> (SOLAS) and the International Life-Saving Appliance Code (LSA) requires certain emergency equipment be carried on each lifeboat and liferaft used on international voyages. Modern lifeboats carry an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_Position-Indicating_Radio_Beacon" title="Emergency Position-Indicating Radio Beacon">Emergency Position-Indicating Radio Beacon</a> (EPIRB) and either a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_reflector" title="Radar reflector">radar reflector</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_and_Rescue_Transponder" title="Search and Rescue Transponder">Search and Rescue Transponder</a> (SART).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifeboat_(shipboard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_boat" title="Ship's boat">ship's boats</a> were often also used as lifeboats in case of emergency.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_lifeboat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1880" data-file-width="2759" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Titanic_lifeboat.jpg/220px-Titanic_lifeboat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Titanic_lifeboat.jpg/330px-Titanic_lifeboat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Titanic_lifeboat.jpg/440px-Titanic_lifeboat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_lifeboat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A collapsible lifeboat carrying survivors of the sunken <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In March 1870, answering a question at the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a> about the sinking of <a href="/wiki/PS_Normandy" title="PS Normandy">PS <i>Normandy</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Shaw-Lefevre,_1st_Baron_Eversley" title="George Shaw-Lefevre, 1st Baron Eversley">George Shaw-Lefevre</a> said that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...in the opinion of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>, it will not be possible to compel the passenger steamers running between England and France to have boats sufficient for the very numerous passengers they often carry. They would encumber the decks, and rather add to the danger than detract from it.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— George Shaw-Lefevre<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>By the turn of the 20th century larger ships meant more people could travel, but safety rules regarding lifeboats remained out of date: for example, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> legislation concerning the number of lifeboats was based on the tonnage of a vessel and only encompassed vessels of "10,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_register_tons" title="Gross register tons">gross register tons</a> (grt) and over". It was not until after the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a> on April 15, 1912, that a broader movement began to require a sufficient number of lifeboats on passenger ships for all people on board. The <i>Titanic</i>, with a gross tonnage of 46,000 tonnes and carrying <a href="/wiki/Lifeboats_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Lifeboats of the RMS Titanic">20 lifeboats</a>, exceeded the regulations laid down by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>, which required a ship of her size (i.e. over 10,000 grt) to carry boats capable of carrying a total of 1,060 people. The <i>Titanic</i>′s boats had a capacity of 1,178 people on a ship capable of carrying 3,330 people.</p>
<p>The need for so many more lifeboats on the decks of passenger ships after 1912 led to the use of most of the deck space available even on the large ships, creating the problem of restricted passageways. This was resolved by the wider use of collapsible lifeboats, a number of which (<a href="/wiki/Berthon_Boat" title="Berthon Boat">Berthon Boats</a>) had been carried on the <i>Titanic</i>.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Atlantic" title="Battle of the Atlantic">Battle of the Atlantic</a> with convoys going to northern <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Russia</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> it was found that the chance of the crews of merchant ships surviving in open lifeboats was not very good unless they were rescued in a couple of hours. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> asked various groups and manufacturers to suggest solutions. The result was the first enclosed, unsinkable, self-righting lifeboat, manufactured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delanco,_New_Jersey" title="Delanco, New Jersey">Delanco</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, USA; the first units were delivered in 1944. These radically new lifeboats were 24 feet in length and weighed 5,000 lbs. They had two enclosed cabins at each end which could hold a total of 25 persons. The space in between was designed to help persons in the water be pulled aboard, and could be enclosed with a canvas top. The new type lifeboat could be driven either by a small motor or sail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, in 1943 the US developed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balsa" title="Balsa">balsa</a> wood liferaft that would not sink, irrespective of the number of holes (from enemy fire) in it. These balsa liferafts were designed to hold five to ten men on a platform suspended on the inside or fifteen to twenty-five hanging lines placed on the outsides. They were inexpensive, and during the war thousands were stored in any space possible on US warships and merchant ships. These liferafts were intended only for use during a short term before lifeboats or another ship in the convoy or group could bring them aboard. When the <a href="/wiki/USS_Indianapolis_(CA-35)" title="USS Indianapolis (CA-35)">USS <i>Indianapolis</i></a>, a cruiser operating alone, was sunk in 1945, none of its larger lifeboats were launched, and the survivors had to rely on balsa liferafts automatically released as the ship sank; many of the crew perished, but the balsa liferafts saved others; ultimately 316 of 1,196 crew survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, enclosed lifeboats are the preferred lifeboats fitted on modern merchant ships because of their superior protection against the elements (especially heat, cold and rough seas).</p>
<p>Generally each merchant ship has one lifeboat fitted on the port side and one on the starboard side, so that a lifeboat is always available even if the ship is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_(watercraft)" title="List (watercraft)">listing</a> to one side. Lifeboat capacity is specified<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and listed on the ship's "safety equipment certificate". Further details of the boats are found in "Form E" of this certificate.</p>
<p>Ships fitted with "free fall" lifeboats are an exception – they have only one boat, at the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liferaft_versus_lifeboat">Liferaft versus lifeboat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifeboat_(shipboard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Liferaft versus lifeboat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Delmas_(3).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2508" data-file-width="3888" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg/220px-Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg/330px-Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg/440px-Caroline_Delmas_%283%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Delmas_(3).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Modern lifeboat on the <i>Caroline Delmas</i>. Note the fully enclosed nature, and the small ducted screw providing mobility.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Liferafts in general are collapsible, and stored in a heavy-duty <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> canister, and also contain some high-pressure gas (in commercial models, usually <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>) to allow automatic inflation to the operations size. SOLAS and military regulations require these to be sealed, never opened by the ship's crew; they are removed at set intervals (annually on merchant vessels) and sent to a certified facility to open and inspect the liferaft and contents. In contrast, a lifeboat is open, and regulations require a crew member to inspect it periodically and ensure all required equipment is present.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1488" data-file-width="1984" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg/220px-Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg/330px-Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg/440px-Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inside_lifeboat_Gdynia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Inside lifeboat</div>

<p>Modern lifeboats have a motor; liferafts usually do not. Large lifeboats use a davit or launching system (there might be multiple lifeboats on one), that requires a human to launch. Lifeboat launching takes longer and has higher risk of failure due to human factors. However lifeboats do not suffer from inflation system failures as inflatable liferafts do.</p>
<p>Since 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> smaller self-rescue lifeboats have been introduced for use by boats with fewer people aboard: these are rigid dinghies with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO2</a>-inflated exposure canopies and other safety equipment. Like the lifeboats used before the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Petrol_engine" title="Petrol engine">petrol engine</a>, these self-rescue dinghies are designed to let the passengers propel themselves to safety by sailing or rowing. In addition to their use as proactive lifeboats, these self-rescue dinghies are also designed to function as unsinkable yacht tenders.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" title="International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea">International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea</a> (SOLAS) makes it a requirement for merchant ships to have liferafts on each side of the ship, sufficient for <i>all</i> the people on board (the stated capacity of the lifeboat, irrespective of the fact that there may actually be fewer people on board). However, if the lifeboats are "easily transferable" (viz. have an open deck between port and starboard lifeboat decks), the number of liferafts may be reduced to a total sufficient for the ship's capacity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The equipment carried in a liferaft is much less than a lifeboat. Vis a vis lifeboats, liferafts are not self-righting and have no motor.</p>

<p>Some ships have <b>freefall lifeboats</b>, stored on a downward sloping slipway normally on stern of vessel. These free fall lifeboats drop into the water as holdback is released. Such lifeboats are considerably heavier as they are strongly constructed to survive the impact with water. Freefall lifeboats are used for their capability to launch nearly instantly and high reliability in any conditions, and since 2006 are required<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/Bulk_carrier" title="Bulk carrier">bulk carriers</a> that are in danger of sinking too rapidly for conventional lifeboats to be released. Seagoing oil rigs are also customarily equipped with this type of lifeboat.</p>
<p>Tankers are required to carry <b>fireproof lifeboats</b>, tested to survive a flaming oil or petroleum product spill from the tanker. Fire protection of such boats is provided by insulation and sprinkler system, which has a pipe system on top, through which water is pumped and sprayed to cool the surface. This system, while not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Failsafe" title="Failsafe">failsafe</a> against engine failure, allows fireproof lifeboats to be built of fiberglass.</p>
<p>Vessels that house <a href="/wiki/Saturation_diving" title="Saturation diving">saturation divers</a> carry <b>hyperbaric lifeboats</b> which incorporate a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_chamber" title="Hyperbaric chamber">hyperbaric chamber</a> to allow the divers to escape without undergoing decompression.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Navy_liferafts">United States Navy liferafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifeboat_(shipboard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States Navy liferafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> ensures the proper type and number of lifeboats are in good repair on large ships.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> uses five types of custom inflatable life rafts as well as a number of commercially available Coast Guard approved life rafts. The 25-person MK-6 and MK-7 are used on surface ships, the 50-person MK-8 on aircraft carriers and LRU-13A and LRU-12A on aircraft and submarines respectively. Smaller combatant craft often use 6, 10 or 15-person commercial life rafts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The number of life rafts carried on USN ships is determined based on the maximum number of personnel carried aboard plus 10% as a safety margin. Aircraft carriers carry either 254 MK7 life rafts or 127 MK8 life rafts. While both are similar to heavy-duty commercial life rafts, USN life rafts use breathable air as the inflation gas rather than carbon dioxide to ensure full inflation within 30 seconds in Arctic environments.</p>
<p>Base material used on MK7 life rafts is <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> coated fabric which has very high durability. Old MK6 and a few MK8 life rafts are manufactured of <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a>-coated fabric, however, the majority of MK8 life rafts are also manufactured of polyurethane fabric. The lifeboat is compact and made of separate compartments, or <i>tubes</i>, as a redundancy against puncture. Two <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">air cylinders</a> containing dry, breathable compressed air provide initial inflation. Depending on the model life raft, each cylinder may contain up to 5000 psi of compressed air. Each life raft is equipped with an external, automatically actuated light beacon and internal lighting. Power is provided by <a href="/wiki/Lithium_battery" title="Lithium battery">lithium batteries</a>.</p>
<p>USN life rafts are stowed in heavy-duty fiberglass canisters and can be launched manually or automatically should the ship begin to sink. Automatic launching and inflation is actuated by a change in pressure sensed by a hydrostatic release device should the ship begin to sink. A hand pump is provided to "top-off" pressure at night when temperatures drop and internal air pressure decreases. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_relief_valve" title="Pressure relief valve">Relief valves</a> are installed in each tube to prevent overpressure. Repairs to holes or rips up to six inches in length can be made using special sealing clamps. Occupants in USN life rafts are protected from wind, rain and sun by built-in canopies which automatically inflate. Hatches are sealable to prevent rain and seawater from entering the life rafts. Survival equipment includes: manual <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalinator</a> (MROD), bottles of fresh water, individual <a href="/wiki/Emergency_rations" title="Emergency rations">food packets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_gear" title="Fishing gear">fishing kit</a>, signaling mirror, rocket and smoke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flare_(pyrotechnic)" title="Flare (pyrotechnic)">flares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">flashlight</a>, spare <a href="/wiki/Sea_anchor" title="Sea anchor">sea anchor</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_aid_kit" title="First aid kit">first aid kit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oar" title="Oar">paddles</a>, spare batteries and bulbs, and aluminized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mylar" title="Mylar">mylar</a> sheets ("<a href="/wiki/Space_blanket" title="Space blanket">space blankets</a>") to aid in caring for victims of <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.</p>
<p>USN inflatable life rafts are serviced every five years. Each life raft is test inflated before repacking. The USN life rafts have a high reliability rate of inflation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usages">Other usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifeboat_(shipboard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1870 the small <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Ragusa_(lifeboat)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="City of Ragusa (lifeboat) (page does not exist)">City of Ragusa</a></i> became the first small lifeboat to cross the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> with two men crew, John Charles Buckley and Nikola Primorac (di Costa), only. They upgraded it with two sails and took advantage of favorable winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
