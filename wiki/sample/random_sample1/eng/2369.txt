<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Constructive proof</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a <b>constructive proof</b> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">proof</a> that demonstrates the existence of a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_object" title="Mathematical object">mathematical object</a> by creating or providing a method for creating the object. This is in contrast to a <b>non-constructive proof</b> (also known as an <i>existence proof</i> or <a href="/wiki/Existence_theorem" title="Existence theorem"><i>pure existence theorem</i></a>) which proves the existence of a particular kind of object without providing an example.</p>
<p>Some non-constructive proofs show that if a certain proposition is false, a contradiction ensues; consequently the proposition must be true (<a href="/wiki/Proof_by_contradiction" title="Proof by contradiction">proof by contradiction</a>). However, the <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_explosion" title="Principle of explosion">principle of explosion</a> (<i>ex falso quodlibet</i>) has been accepted in some varieties of constructive mathematics, including <a href="/wiki/Intuitionism" title="Intuitionism">intuitionism</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(mathematics)" title="Constructivism (mathematics)">Constructivism</a> is a mathematical philosophy that rejects all but constructive proofs in mathematics. This leads to a restriction on the proof methods allowed (prototypically, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_the_excluded_middle" title="Law of the excluded middle">law of the excluded middle</a> is not accepted) and a different meaning of terminology (for example, the term "or" has a stronger meaning in constructive mathematics than in classical).</p>
<p>Constructive proofs can be seen as defining certified mathematical <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>: this idea is explored in the <a href="/wiki/Brouwer%E2%80%93Heyting%E2%80%93Kolmogorov_interpretation" title="Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation">Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructive_logic" title="Constructive logic">constructive logic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Curry%E2%80%93Howard_correspondence" title="Curry–Howard correspondence">Curry–Howard correspondence</a> between proofs and programs, and such logical systems as <a href="/wiki/Per_Martin-L%C3%B6f" title="Per Martin-Löf">Per Martin-Löf</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intuitionistic_Type_Theory" title="Intuitionistic Type Theory">Intuitionistic Type Theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Coquand" title="Thierry Coquand">Thierry Coquand</a> and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Huet" title="Gérard Huet">Gérard Huet</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calculus_of_Constructions" title="Calculus of Constructions">Calculus of Constructions</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constructive_proof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-constructive_proofs">Non-constructive proofs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constructive_proof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-constructive proofs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>First consider the theorem that there are an infinitude of <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">prime numbers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_theorem" title="Euclid's theorem">proof</a> is constructive. But a common way of simplifying Euclid's proof postulates that, contrary to the assertion in the theorem, there are only a finite number of them, in which case there is a largest one, denoted <i>n</i>. Then consider the number <i>n</i>! + 1 (1 + the product of the first <i>n</i> numbers). Either this number is prime, or all of its prime factors are greater than <i>n</i>. Without establishing a specific prime number, this proves that one exists that is greater than <i>n</i>, contrary to the original postulate.</p>

<p>A more substantial example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graph_minor_theorem" title="Graph minor theorem">graph minor theorem</a>. A consequence of this theorem is that a <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">graph</a> can be drawn on the <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a> if, and only if, none of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_(graph_theory)" title="Minor (graph theory)">minors</a> belong to a certain finite set of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_minors" title="Forbidden minors">forbidden minors</a>". <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brouwerian_counterexamples">Brouwerian counterexamples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constructive_proof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brouwerian counterexamples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructive_mathematics" title="Constructive mathematics">constructive mathematics</a>, a statement may be disproved by giving a <a href="/wiki/Counterexample" title="Counterexample">counterexample</a>, as in classical mathematics. However, it is also possible to give a <b>Brouwerian counterexample</b> to show that the statement is non-constructive. This sort of counterexample shows that the statement implies some principle that is known to be non-constructive. If it can be proved constructively that a statement implies some principle that is not constructively provable, then the statement itself cannot be constructively provable. For example, a particular statement may be shown to imply the law of the excluded middle. An example of a Brouwerian counterexample of this type is <a href="/wiki/Diaconescu%27s_theorem" title="Diaconescu's theorem">Diaconescu's theorem</a>, which shows that the full <a href="/wiki/Axiom_of_choice" title="Axiom of choice">axiom of choice</a> is non-constructive in systems of <a href="/wiki/Constructive_set_theory" title="Constructive set theory">constructive set theory</a>, since the axiom of choice implies the law of excluded middle in such systems. The field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructive_reverse_mathematics" title="Constructive reverse mathematics">constructive reverse mathematics</a> develops this idea further by classifying various principles in terms of "how nonconstructive" they are, by showing they are equivalent to various fragments of the law of the excluded middle.</p>
<p>Brouwer also provided "weak" counterexamples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Such counterexamples do not disprove a statement, however; they only show that, at present, no constructive proof of the statement is known. One weak counterexample begins by taking some unsolved problem of mathematics, such as <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_conjecture" title="Goldbach's conjecture">Goldbach's conjecture</a>, which asks whether every even natural number larger than 4 is the sum of two primes. <p>For each <i>n</i>, the value of <i>a</i>(<i>n</i>) can be determined by exhaustive search, and so <i>a</i> is a well defined sequence, constructively. Moreover, because <i>a</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Cauchy_sequence" title="Cauchy sequence">Cauchy sequence</a> with a fixed rate of convergence, <i>a</i> converges to some real number α, according to the usual treatment of real numbers in constructive mathematics.</p>
<p>Several facts about the real number α can be proved constructively. However, based on the different meaning of the words in constructive mathematics, if there is a constructive proof that "α = 0 or α ≠ 0" then this would mean that there is a constructive proof of Goldbach's conjecture (in the former case) or a constructive proof that Goldbach's conjecture is false (in the latter case). Because no such proof is known, the quoted statement must also not have a known constructive proof. However, it is entirely possible that Goldbach's conjecture may have a constructive proof (as we do not know at present whether it does), in which case the quoted statement would have a constructive proof as well, albeit one that is unknown at present. The main practical use of weak counterexamples is to identify the "hardness" of a problem. For example, the counterexample just shown shows that the quoted statement is "at least as hard to prove" as Goldbach's conjecture. Weak counterexamples of this sort are often related to the <a href="/wiki/Limited_principle_of_omniscience" title="Limited principle of omniscience">limited principle of omniscience</a>.</p>
