<p>In <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> is sometimes referred to as a <b>Redeemer</b>. This refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_soteriology" title="Christian soteriology">salvation</a> he is believed to have accomplished, and is based on the metaphor of <a href="/wiki/Redemption_(theology)#In_Christianity" title="Redemption (theology)">redemption</a>, or "buying back". Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a> do not use the title "Redeemer", the word "redemption" is used in several of <a href="/wiki/Pauline_epistles" title="Pauline epistles">Paul's letters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leon_Morris" title="Leon Morris">Leon Morris</a> says that "Paul uses the concept of redemption primarily to speak of the saving significance of the death of Christ."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> word redemption means "repurchase" or "buy back", and in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> referred to the <a href="/wiki/Ransom" title="Ransom">ransom</a> of slaves (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 21:8).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the New Testament the redemption word group is used to refer both to deliverance from sin and freedom from captivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of the redeemer is used in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ruth" title="Book of Ruth">Book of Ruth</a> to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Goel" title="Goel">kinsman-redeemer</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">Book of Isaiah</a> to refer to God, the "Redeemer of Israel".</p>
<p>Many Christian churches are named "Redeemer", such as <a href="/wiki/Redeemer_Presbyterian_Church_(New_York_City)" title="Redeemer Presbyterian Church (New York City)">Redeemer Presbyterian Church (New York City)</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church_of_the_Redeemer,_Jerusalem" title="Lutheran Church of the Redeemer, Jerusalem">Lutheran Church of the Redeemer, Jerusalem</a>. Other institutions which carry the name are the Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Congregation_of_the_Most_Holy_Redeemer" title="Congregation of the Most Holy Redeemer">Congregation of the Most Holy Redeemer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redeemer_University_College" title="Redeemer University College">Redeemer University College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancaster,_Ontario" title="Ancaster, Ontario">Ancaster, Ontario</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Christ_the_Redeemer_(statue)" title="Christ the Redeemer (statue)">Christ the Redeemer</a> statue in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> is a famous landmark.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Job</a> 19:25, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_(Biblical_figure)" title="Job (Biblical figure)">Job</a> makes the statement, "I know that my Redeemer liveth." This has been used in Christian hymnody, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley" title="Charles Wesley">Charles Wesley</a>'s <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/I_Know_That_My_Redeemer_Lives" title="wikisource:I Know That My Redeemer Lives">I Know That My Redeemer Lives</a></i>, and the first words to the song "Antioch 277" in the <a href="/wiki/Shape_note" title="Shape note">shape note</a> <a href="/wiki/Song_book" title="Song book">song book</a> <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Harp" title="Sacred Harp"><i>The Sacred Harp</i></a> are <i>"I know that my Redeemer lives, Glory Hallelujah!"</i>. It also appears as an aria, <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_III#45" title="Messiah Part III">I know that my Redeemer liveth</a></i>, in <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">Handel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_(Handel)" title="Messiah (Handel)">Messiah</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Universality">Universality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redeemer_(Christianity)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Universality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="970" data-file-width="800" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg/200px-Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg/300px-Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg/400px-Titian_-_Christ_the_Redeemer_-_WGA22796.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> speaks of Christ as the one Saviour for all people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_of_John" title="First Epistle of John">First Epistle of John</a> says that Jesus is "the propitiation for our sins and not for ours only but also for the sins of the world" (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/1_John#2:2" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/1 John">1 John 2:2</a>). Adherents of <a href="/wiki/Unlimited_atonement" title="Unlimited atonement">unlimited atonement</a> interpret this to mean that Jesus' redemptive role is for all people without exception, while adherents of <a href="/wiki/Limited_atonement" title="Limited atonement">limited atonement</a> interpret it as being for all people without <i>distinction</i>—for Gentiles as well as Jews. The first Christians also recognized Jesus' redemptive role to be unique (without parallel), complete (as One who conveys the fullness of salvation), and definitive (beyond any possibility of being equalled, let alone surpassed, in his salvific function). In particular, his universal role means that through him the deadly forces of evil are overcome, sin is forgiven, their contamination purified, and the new existence as God's beloved, adopted children has been made available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOC1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOC1-5">[5]</a></sup> This New Testament sense of Christ's indispensable and necessary role for human salvation could be summarized by a new <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axiom</a>: <i>extra Christum nulla salus</i> ("outside Christ no salvation"). This sense of his all-determining role in the whole redemptive drama is suggested by a fact: unlike the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>, where various human beings could be called "saviour" (e.g., <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Judges#3:9" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Judges">Judges 3: 9</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Judges#3:15" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Judges">15</a>, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(American_Standard)/Judges#3:31" title="wikisource:Bible (American Standard)/Judges">31</a>), the New Testament gives the title "Saviour" only to God (eight times) and to Christ (sixteen times).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOC1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-GOC1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
