<p><i><b>Goosebumps</b></i> is a series of children's horror fiction novellas by American author <a href="/wiki/R._L._Stine" title="R. L. Stine">R. L. Stine</a>, published by <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic Publishing</a>. The stories follow child characters, who find themselves in scary situations; usually involving monsters and other supernatural elements. From 1992 to 1997, 62 books were published under the <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(original_series)" title="Goosebumps (original series)">Goosebumps</a></i> umbrella title. Various spin-off series were written by Stine: <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Series_2000" title="Goosebumps Series 2000">Goosebumps Series 2000</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Give_Yourself_Goosebumps" title="Give Yourself Goosebumps">Give Yourself Goosebumps</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Goosebumps_books#Tales_to_Give_You_Goosebumps" title="List of Goosebumps books">Tales to Give You Goosebumps</a></i>, <i>Goosebumps Triple Header</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_HorrorLand" title="Goosebumps HorrorLand">Goosebumps HorrorLand</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Most_Wanted" title="Goosebumps Most Wanted">Goosebumps Most Wanted</a></i>. Another series, <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Goosebumps_books#Goosebumps_Gold_.28Planned_2001.29" title="List of Goosebumps books">Goosebumps Gold</a></i>, was never released. <i>Goosebumps</i> has spawned a <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(TV_series)" title="Goosebumps (TV series)">television series</a> and merchandise, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(film)" title="Goosebumps (film)">feature film</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a> as Stine.</p>
<p>Since the release of its first novel, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welcome_to_Dead_House" title="Welcome to Dead House">Welcome to Dead House</a></i>, in July 1992, the series has sold over 350 million books worldwide in 32 languages. Individual books in the series have been listed in several <a href="/wiki/Bestseller" title="Bestseller">bestseller</a> lists, including the <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> Best Seller list</a> for children.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_genre">Structure and genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goosebumpscastwithstine.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="287" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/88/Goosebumpscastwithstine.PNG/220px-Goosebumpscastwithstine.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/88/Goosebumpscastwithstine.PNG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goosebumpscastwithstine.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration of R. L. Stine with some of his creations. This illustration was from the cover of Stine's autobiography, <i>It Came from Ohio!: My Life as a Writer</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Goosebumps</i> series falls under the children's fiction, horror and <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller</a> genres, although Stine characterizes the series as "scary books that are also funny".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoerstel2002222_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoerstel2002222-3">[2]</a></sup> Each book features different child characters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-characters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-characters-4">[3]</a></sup> and settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestfahl200044_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestfahl200044-5">[4]</a></sup> The primary <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonists</a> are middle class and can be either male or female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200069_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200069-6">[5]</a></sup> The primary protagonists of a <i>Goosebumps</i> story are often situated in a remote location or somehow isolated from typical societal conventions. This can range anywhere from comfortable suburban areas to <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding schools</a>, foreign villages or campsites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foreign_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-foreign-7">[6]</a></sup> Books typically feature characters who either recently moved to a new neighborhood or are sent to stay with <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">relatives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200069_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200069-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The books in the <i>Goosebumps</i> series feature similar plot structures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200068_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200068-8">[7]</a></sup> with fictional children being involved in scary situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200071_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200071-9">[8]</a></sup> The books are mostly written in first person <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a>, often concluding with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_ending" title="Twist ending">twist endings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoats2004170_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoats2004170-10">[9]</a></sup> They contain <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surreal</a> horror,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200069_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200069-6">[5]</a></sup> with characters encountering the strange and <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECullinanPerson2005707_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECullinanPerson2005707-11">[10]</a></sup> The author has plot devices that he follows throughout his <i>Goosebumps</i> books. Stine says he does not have any death in his stories, and the children in his novels are never put into situations that would be considered too serious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-12">[11]</a></sup> He attributed the success of his books to their absence of drugs, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/depravity" title="wikt:depravity">depravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beavercountytimes_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-beavercountytimes-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspiration_and_themes">Inspiration and themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inspiration and themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Books and characters in the series were inspired by books and films. For example, the character <a href="/wiki/Slappy_the_Dummy" title="Slappy the Dummy">Slappy the Dummy</a> was inspired by the literary classic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Pinocchio" title="The Adventures of Pinocchio">The Adventures of Pinocchio</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inspiration_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-inspiration-14">[13]</a></sup> Some of Stine's ideas for the books also came from real life; Stine got the idea for the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Haunted_Mask" title="The Haunted Mask">The Haunted Mask</a></i> after his son, Matt, had a <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> mask that he had trouble getting off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idea_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-idea-15">[14]</a></sup> Stine also uses his childhood fears to help him write his books. The author said, "Luckily, I have a great memory. As I write a story, I can remember what it feels like to be afraid and panicky".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Stine states that he often thinks of a title to a novel first, then lets the title lead him to a story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two common themes in the series are children triumphing over evil and children facing horrid or frightening situations and using their own <a href="/wiki/Wit" title="Wit">wit</a> and imagination to escape them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-common_themes_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-common_themes-18">[17]</a></sup> Stine does not attempt to incorporate moral lessons into his novels, and says his books are "strictly reading motivation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_Goosebumps_series">Original <i>Goosebumps</i> series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Original Goosebumps series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(original_series)" title="Goosebumps (original series)">Goosebumps (original series)</a></div>
<p>Following the success of Stine's <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult</a> horror novels, the co-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" title="Entrepreneur">founder</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parachute_Press" title="Parachute Press">Parachute Press</a> (the company that developed the series),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rights_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-rights-20">[19]</a></sup> Joan Waricha, persuaded him to write scary books for younger children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waricha_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waricha-21">[20]</a></sup> Stine says the name for the book series came to him after he saw a TV station's ad in <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> that stated "It’s goosebumps week on Channel 11".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen199939_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen199939-23">[22]</a></sup> He originally signed a six-book deal with the publisher <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publisher_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-publisher-24">[23]</a></sup> but went on to write 62 books in the original series, the first book being <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welcome_to_Dead_House" title="Welcome to Dead House">Welcome to Dead House</a></i>, released in July 1992. The series was originally aimed at girls, but both boys and girls enjoyed the series equally with half of Stine's fan mail being sent from boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-12">[11]</a></sup> The cover illustrations for this series were first done by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Jacobus" title="Tim Jacobus">Tim Jacobus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illustrations_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-illustrations-25">[24]</a></sup> Twenty-nine of the books from the original series were later re-released with new artwork under the <i>Classic Goosebumps</i> rename.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off_series">Spin-off series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spin-off series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The books in the <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Goosebumps_books#Tales_to_Give_You_Goosebumps" title="List of Goosebumps books">Tales to Give You Goosebumps</a></i> and <i>Goosebumps Triple Header</i> series were written as short story anthologies, featuring a collection of stories in each book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarcovitz2005117_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarcovitz2005117-27">[26]</a></sup> From 1994 to 1997, six <i>Tales to Give You Goosebumps</i> books were published. Two <i>Goosebumps Triple Header</i> books were released from 1997 to 1998, beginning with <i>Three Shocking Tales of Terror: Book 1</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fifty <i><a href="/wiki/Give_Yourself_Goosebumps" title="Give Yourself Goosebumps">Give Yourself Goosebumps</a></i> books were published from 1995 to 2000, starting with <i>Escape from the Carnival of Horrors</i>. The books in this series were written as <a href="/wiki/Gamebook" title="Gamebook">gamebooks</a>, featuring multiple endings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWestfahl200046_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWestfahl200046-29">[28]</a></sup> The books in this series were ghostwritten by several authors, including <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Lance" title="Kathryn Lance">Kathryn Lance</a> and Stine's sister-in-law Megan Stine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-number_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-number-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1998169_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1998169-32">[31]</a></sup> Many of the cover illustrations for this series were done by Mark Nagata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illustrations1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-illustrations1-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to declining <i>Goosebumps</i> sales and increasing competition, Scholastic and R. L. Stine decided to create <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Series_2000" title="Goosebumps Series 2000">Goosebumps Series 2000</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1998152.E2.80.93153_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1998152.E2.80.93153-34">[33]</a></sup> From 1998 to 2000, 25 books in the series were published, beginning with <i>Cry of the Cat</i>. The books in this series were written in a similar format and featured similar content to the original series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200067_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200067-35">[34]</a></sup> but Stine classified them as being "much scarier."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publisher_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-publisher-24">[23]</a></sup> The covers in this series were illustrated by Tim Jacobus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illustrations_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-illustrations-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The books in the <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Goosebumps_books#Goosebumps_Gold" title="List of Goosebumps books">Goosebumps Gold</a></i> series appeared on illustrator Tim Jacobus's website<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goosebumps_gold_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-goosebumps_gold-36">[35]</a></sup> and marketing sites but were never released. In this series, Stine intended to write a sequel to <i>The Haunted Mask II</i> (<i>The Haunted Mask Lives!</i>), and a sequel to <i>Welcome to Dead House</i> (<i>Happy Holidays from Dead House</i>). It was one of the two book series by Stine that was planned to be released in 2000 (the other was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nightmare_Room" title="The Nightmare Room">The Nightmare Room</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-series_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-series-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series was renewed in 2008 following the release of the first book in the <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_HorrorLand" title="Goosebumps HorrorLand">Goosebumps HorrorLand</a></i> series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revenge_of_the_Living_Dummy" title="Revenge of the Living Dummy">Revenge of the Living Dummy</a></i>, that was published on April 1, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goosebumps_horrorland_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-goosebumps_horrorland-38">[37]</a></sup> Before the 2008 release of <i>Revenge of the Living Dummy</i>, there had not been a <i>Goosebumps</i> book published in almost 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Stine decided to start writing <i>Goosebumps</i> books again after receiving mail asking him to write new books in the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> Nineteen <i>Goosebumps HorrorLand</i> books were published,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> and books in the series mainly featured two stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-common_themes_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-common_themes-18">[17]</a></sup> The series continued in 2012 with new stories featuring some of the series' most memorable villains, including <a href="/wiki/Slappy_the_Dummy" title="Slappy the Dummy">Slappy the Dummy</a>, the Lawn Gnomes and others. The first book of the spin-off series <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Most_Wanted" title="Goosebumps Most Wanted">Goosebumps Most Wanted</a></i>, <i>Planet of the Lawn Gnomes</i>, was released in October 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements.2C_reception_and_controversy">Achievements, reception and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Achievements, reception and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements">Achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_books" title="List of best-selling books">List of best-selling books</a></div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"The first 27 paperback <a href="/wiki/Backlist" title="Backlist">backlist</a> titles on our list are all Goosebumps. The <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> is even more astounding when the sales figures are added up. Scholastic sold 19,125,700 copies of Goosebumps frontlist titles in 1995, and 12,906,800 backlist titles, for a grand total of 32,032,500 copies sold".</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Diane Roback, an editor for <i>Publishers Weekly</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris200064_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris200064-43">[42]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Following the release of the first novel in the series, the books quickly became popular, selling a million copies a month soon after they first appeared,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-characters_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-characters-4">[3]</a></sup> and four million copies a month by the mid-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mid_90s_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-mid_90s-44">[43]</a></sup> Individual <i>Goosebumps</i> books appeared in the <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> Best Seller list</a> for children<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-45">[44]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> bestseller list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-welcome_dead_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-welcome_dead-46">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cheese_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-cheese-47">[46]</a></sup> In 2001, <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i> listed 46 books in the series in its list of bestselling children's paperback books of all-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> <i>Goosebumps</i> was a bestseller in many countries, including the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen199951_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen199951-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, the book series accounted for almost 15% of Scholastic's annual revenue. Following the decline of <i>Goosebumps</i> sales in 1997, Scholastic's sales had dropped 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plunge_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-plunge-50">[49]</a></sup> The decline in <i>Goosebumps</i> book sales had made front page news of most newspaper business sections, which Patrick Jones stated "demonstrates the impact and importance of R. L. Stine. One writer, it seems, influences the fate of an entire company".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1998xx.E2.80.93xxi_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1998xx.E2.80.93xxi-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2008, the <i>Goosebumps</i> series maintains an 82% brand awareness among children 7–12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-media_awareness_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-media_awareness-52">[51]</a></sup> It is listed as the number two bestselling children's book series of all time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bestselling_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-bestselling-53">[52]</a></sup> and as Scholastic's bestselling children's book series of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGunelius200858_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGunelius200858-54">[53]</a></sup> By 2014, according to Scholastic, there were 350 million copies of <i>Goosebumps</i> books sold in 32 languages,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen199951_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen199951-49">[48]</a></sup> As of 2008, the book series sells about two million copies annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waricha_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Waricha-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three books from the <i>Goosebumps</i> series have won the <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_Kids%27_Choice_Awards" title="Nickelodeon Kids' Choice Awards">Nickelodeon Kids' Choice Awards</a> for Favorite Book: <i>Deep Trouble</i> in 1995 (the award category's first year),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book12_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-book12-56">[55]</a></sup> the book <i>Tales to Give You Goosebumps</i> in 1996,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-choice_awards_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-choice_awards-57">[56]</a></sup> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Trouble_II" title="Deep Trouble II">Deep Trouble II</a></i> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-favebook_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-favebook-58">[57]</a></sup> In 2000, the series was ranked as the number two children's books by the <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">National Education Association</a>, as chosen by children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-children_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-children-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)"><i>Slate</i></a>'s Katy Waldman classified a classic <i>Goosebumps</i> story as "funny, icky, and just a bit menacing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-60">[59]</a></sup> Following the release of the first <i>Goosebumps HorrorLand</i> book, <i>Publishers Weekly</i> stated in a starred review that the new <i>Goosebumps</i> series was "deliciously chilling".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> Two reviewers of the <i>Goosebumps</i> books did not feel that the books were high quality literature. <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a>'</i>s Marc Silver thought the series was "quite tame". He called the <i>Goosebumps</i> books "<a href="/wiki/Subliterature" title="Subliterature">subliterature</a>", stating the plotting in the books was careless and that characters in the stories rarely grew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> Roderick McGillis, from the academic journal <i><a href="/wiki/Bookbird:_A_Journal_of_International_Children%27s_Literature" title="Bookbird: A Journal of International Children's Literature">Bookbird</a></i>, described the books as <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">camp</a>, writing the books "are so artificial, so formulaic, so predictable, so repetitive". McGillis also felt that the content of the <i>Goosebumps</i> series is "thin in the extreme".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stine's books have a reputation for getting children excited about reading, which the writer is very proud of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-17">[16]</a></sup> James Carter, writing in <i>Talking Books: Children's Authors Talk About the Craft, Creativity and Process of Writing</i>, stated "regarding <i>Point Horrors</i> and <i>Goosebumps</i>, I feel that anything that children read avidly is a good thing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarter1999216_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarter1999216-64">[63]</a></sup> Librarian and writer Patrick Jones commented that "[t]he real horror is a culture where kids, especially boys, don't read—and Stine has done his best to stop that turn of the screw from happening in his lifetime".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1998203_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1998203-65">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_challenges">Book challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Book challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Goosebumps</i> was listed 15th in the list of most frequently <a href="/wiki/Challenge_(literature)" title="Challenge (literature)">challenged</a> books during 1990–1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-challenge1_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-challenge1-66">[65]</a></sup> and 94th in the list of top banned/challenged books during 2000–2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-challenge2_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-challenge2-67">[66]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> (ALA). According to the ALA, a challenge is an attempt by a person or group to remove or restrict materials from a library or school curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup> The series was challenged for being too frightening for young people and depicting occult or <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">satanic</a> themes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-occult_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-occult-69">[68]</a></sup> By 1997, the ALA was informed of 46 challenges, over 75% of which occurred in school libraries. The rest of the challenges were held in public libraries or the location of the challenges were unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckerStan200257_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckerStan200257-70">[69]</a></sup> In 1997, a hearing by the <a href="/wiki/Anoka-Hennepin_School_District_11" title="Anoka-Hennepin School District 11">Anoka-Hennepin School District</a> to ban the books was broadcast by <a href="/wiki/C-SPAN" title="C-SPAN">C-SPAN</a>. In the hearing, most of the parents and children felt the books should not be banned,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup> and the school district's book review committee decided to keep the books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckerStan200257.E2.80.9358_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckerStan200257.E2.80.9358-72">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_and_merchandise">Adaptations and merchandise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adaptations and merchandise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audiobooks">Audiobooks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Audiobooks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Goosebumps_audiobooks" title="List of Goosebumps audiobooks">List of Goosebumps audiobooks</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Records" title="Walt Disney Records">Walt Disney Records</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic</a></i> <i>Audiobooks</i> have adapted <i>Goosebumps</i> into numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audiobooks" title="Audiobooks">audiobooks</a> from 1996–present.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_adaptation">Television adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Television adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(TV_series)" title="Goosebumps (TV series)">Goosebumps (TV series)</a></div>
<p>In the 1990s, a <i>Goosebumps</i> TV series was produced in Canada by <a href="/wiki/Protocol_Entertainment" title="Protocol Entertainment">Protocol Entertainment</a> in association with <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic Productions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-production-73">[72]</a></sup> The TV <a href="/wiki/Anthology_series" title="Anthology series">anthology series</a> ran for four seasons from 1995 to 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGunelius200858_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGunelius200858-54">[53]</a></sup> premiering on the Fox network on October 27, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-premiere_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-premiere-74">[73]</a></sup> The series mainly featured plots based on the <i>Goosebumps</i> books, among them <i>The Haunted Mask</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(Original_series)#Books" title="Goosebumps (Original series)">Cuckoo Clock of Doom</a></i>. The TV series aired in over 100 countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-countries_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-countries-75">[74]</a></sup> and it was the number one rated children's TV show for three years in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-number_one_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-number_one-76">[75]</a></sup> Margaret Loesch, formerly the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fox_Kids" title="Fox Kids">Fox Kids</a>, offered Scholastic a TV deal after her son responded positively to the <i>Goosebumps</i> book <i>Say Cheese and Die</i> she had bought for him a day earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mid_90s_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-mid_90s-44">[43]</a></sup> A book series, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Presents" title="Goosebumps Presents">Goosebumps Presents</a></i>, was based on the TV series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-number_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-number-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptation">Film adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Film adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(film)" title="Goosebumps (film)">Goosebumps (film)</a></div>
<p>The first attempt at a <i>Goosebumps</i> film was in 1998, which <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a> was going to produce. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Meledandri" title="Chris Meledandri">Chris Meledandri</a>, the president of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Family_Films" title="Fox Family Films">Fox Family Films</a>, said: "I think you'll see us tackling a scale of story that would be prohibitive to do on the small screen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weekly_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weekly-77">[76]</a></sup> However, the film did not materialize since they could not find a script they liked or determine which book or monster to adapt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup> In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> acquired rights to create a <i>Goosebumps</i> film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[78]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Neal_H._Moritz" title="Neal H. Moritz">Neal H. Moritz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Forte" title="Deborah Forte">Deborah Forte</a>, the latter of whom developed the TV series, producing the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coming_soon_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-coming_soon-80">[79]</a></sup> Duo <a href="/wiki/Scott_Alexander_and_Larry_Karaszewski" title="Scott Alexander and Larry Karaszewski">Scott Alexander and Larry Karaszewski</a> were hired as screenwriters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> and wrote the original script for the film. They felt that the individual books in the series were too short to adapt into a film, so they chose instead to do a fake <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical film</a> in which R. L. Stine writes a book and all the monsters within it become real.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 14, 2012, it was reported that a new draft of the screenplay was written by Darren Lemke. Lemke co-wrote the screenplays for <i><a href="/wiki/Shrek_Forever_After" title="Shrek Forever After">Shrek Forever After</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Jack_the_Giant_Slayer" title="Jack the Giant Slayer">Jack the Giant Slayer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup> In September 2013, it was revealed that <a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a> was to play a fictionalized version of R. L. Stine "whose scary characters literally leap off the page, forcing him to hide from his own creepy creations". <a href="/wiki/Rob_Letterman" title="Rob Letterman">Rob Letterman</a> was also confirmed as the director, which would reunite him with Black, after working together on <i><a href="/wiki/Shark_Tale" title="Shark Tale">Shark Tale</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels_(2010_film)" title="Gulliver's Travels (2010 film)">Gulliver's Travels</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was announced in February 2014 that <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Minnette" title="Dylan Minnette">Dylan Minnette</a> was cast as Zach Cooper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Odeya_Rush" title="Odeya Rush">Odeya Rush</a> was cast as R. L. Stine's fictional daughter, Hannah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comingsoon_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-comingsoon-86">[85]</a></sup> In the film, which was released on October 16, 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[86]</a></sup> Hannah's father R. L. Stine keeps all the monsters in the series locked up in his books. When Zach unintentionally releases the monsters from the books, Zach, Hannah, and Stine team up in order to put the monsters back where they came from.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comingsoon_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-comingsoon-86">[85]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> on the film began on April 23, 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Candler_Park" title="Candler Park">Candler Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[87]</a></sup> they also shot the film in <a href="/wiki/Conyers,_Georgia" title="Conyers, Georgia">Conyers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Georgia" title="Madison, Georgia">Madison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comingsoon_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-comingsoon-86">[85]</a></sup> Principal photography ended on July 16, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are five <i>Goosebumps</i> video games, two of which have been created for the PC by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EA_Los_Angeles" title="EA Los Angeles">DreamWorks Interactive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escape_from_horrorland_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-escape_from_horrorland-90">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-attack_of_the_mutant_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-attack_of_the_mutant-91">[90]</a></sup> A 1996 game entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosebumps:_Escape_from_Horrorland" title="Goosebumps: Escape from Horrorland">Escape from HorrorLand</a></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Interactivity" title="Interactivity">interactive</a> sequel to the book <i><a href="/wiki/One_Day_at_HorrorLand" title="One Day at HorrorLand">One Day at HorrorLand</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interactive_sequel_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-interactive_sequel-92">[91]</a></sup> and a 1997 game entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attack_of_the_Mutant_(video_game)" title="Attack of the Mutant (video game)">Attack of the Mutant</a></i> was based on the book <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosebumps_(Original_series)#Books" title="Goosebumps (Original series)">of the same name</a>. Scholastic released a <i>Goosebumps</i> video game in October 2008 entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosebumps_Horrorland_(video_game)" title="Goosebumps Horrorland (video game)">Goosebumps HorrorLand</a></i>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Goosebumps_HorrorLand" title="Goosebumps HorrorLand">series of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-review-93">[92]</a></sup> Another video game, <i>Goosebumps: The Game</i>, a prequel to the 2015 film, was released on October 13, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[93]</a></sup> <i>Goosebumps: Night of Scares</i>, a mobile game based on the film and the book series was released for iOS and Android devices on October 15 of the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>The books <i>One Day at HorrorLand</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_in_Terror_Tower" title="A Night in Terror Tower">A Night in Terror Tower</a></i> were adapted into two separate board games in 1996. Both games were published by <a href="/wiki/Milton_Bradley_Company" title="Milton Bradley Company">Milton Bradley</a> and designed by Craig Van Ness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Goosebumps</i> has spawned merchandise, including T-shirts, <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board games</a>, puzzles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoerstel2002222_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoerstel2002222-3">[2]</a></sup> hats, fake skulls, dolls,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1998163_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1998163-98">[97]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_helmet" title="Bicycle helmet">bike helmets</a>, fake blood, and boxer shorts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merch_3_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-merch_3-99">[98]</a></sup> <i>Goosebumps</i> was also adapted into a stage play by Rupert Holmes in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stage_play_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-stage_play-100">[99]</a></sup> <i>Goosebumps</i> has an official website, which garners 1.5 million page views each month as of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup> An attraction based on the series, the Goosebumps HorrorLand Fright Show and FunHouse, opened in October 1997 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney-MGM_Studios" title="Disney-MGM Studios">Disney-MGM Studios</a>'s New York Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[101]</a></sup> Before it closed, the attraction consisted of a stage play which featured characters from the series; this show played five times a day. The attraction also featured a <a href="/wiki/Funhouse" title="Funhouse">funhouse</a>, called the Goosebumps HorrorLand Hall of Mirrors, which contained a maze of mirrors along with other props and gags from the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[102]</a></sup> In 2008, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Sally_Corporation" title="Sally Corporation">Sally Corporation</a> would market <i>Goosebumps</i> rides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-themepk_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-themepk-104">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Goosebumps_Graphix"></span>A comic book series, titled <i>Goosebumps Graphix</i>, was written based on books from the original series. There were three books published in the series; the first one, <i><a href="/wiki/Creepy_Creatures" title="Creepy Creatures">Creepy Creatures</a></i>, was published on September 1, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publication_date_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-publication_date-105">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_dispute">Legal dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goosebumps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legal dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 1996, Scholastic, the publisher of the series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[105]</a></sup> and Parachute Press, the developer of the series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rights_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-rights-20">[19]</a></sup> agreed to a new contract. Scholastic retained control of book publishing and the TV series, but gave Parachute Press merchandising rights to the series. In September 1997, following a dispute between Scholastic and Parachute Press, Scholastic accused Parachute Press of <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breaching the contract</a>. Scholastic claimed that Parachute Press had been making merchandising deals and issuing press releases without Scholastic's required consent, and had begun withholding payments from them. In November 1997, Parachute responded by alleging Scholastic had repudiated its financial obligations, claiming Scholastic had voided its rights to publish 54 books. Parachute Press filed a lawsuit, which followed with numerous other suits and counter lawsuits<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwcharges_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwcharges-107">[106]</a></sup> over who controls certain rights to the series. In 2003, the two sides reached an agreement, with Scholastic receiving the <i>Goosebumps</i> trademark and all other rights to the series for <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$9.65 million</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rights_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-rights-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
