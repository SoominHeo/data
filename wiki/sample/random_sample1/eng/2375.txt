<p>A <b>marble</b> is a small spherical toy often made from <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agate" title="Agate">agate</a>. These balls vary in size. Most commonly, they are about 1 cm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in) in diameter, but they may range from less than 1 mm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>30</sub></span> in) to over 8 cm (3 in), while some art glass marbles for display purposes are over 30 cm (12 in) wide. Marbles can be used for a variety of <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a> called <b>marbles</b>. They are often <a href="/wiki/Collecting" title="Collecting">collected</a>, both for nostalgia and for their aesthetic colors. In the North of England the objects and the game are called "taws", with larger taws being called <i>bottle washers</i> after the use of a marble in <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottles</a>.</p>
<p>Sizes range from <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Pee-Wee" title="Pee-Wee">Pee-Wee</a> (Smallest) at 1/5 to 1/4 Inch to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Toebreaker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Toebreaker (page does not exist)">Toebreaker</a> (largest) which measure a diametre of 2inches</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Various balls of stone were found on excavation near <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Marbles are also often mentioned in Roman literature, like <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s poem <i>Nux</i> about nuts playing and there are many examples of marbles from Chaldeans of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and ancient Egypt. They were commonly made of clay, stone or glass.</p>
<p>Marbles were first manufactured in Germany in the 1800s. The game has become popular throughout the U.S. and other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">Ceramic</a> marbles entered inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> in the 1870s.</p>
<p>A German <a href="/wiki/Glassblowing" title="Glassblowing">glassblower</a> invented <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_scissors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marble scissors (page does not exist)">marble scissors</a> in 1846, a device for making marbles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first mass-produced toy marbles (clay) made in the U.S. were made in <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron, Ohio</a>, by S. C. Dyke, in the early 1890s. Some of the first U.S.-produced glass marbles were also made in <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron</a>, by James Harvey Leighton. In 1903, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Frederick_Christensen" title="Martin Frederick Christensen">Martin Frederick Christensen</a>—also of <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron, Ohio</a>—made the first machine-made glass marbles on his patented machine. His company, The M. F. Christensen &amp; Son Co., manufactured millions of toy and industrial glass marbles until they ceased operations in 1917. The next U.S. company to enter the glass marble market was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Akro_Agate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Akro Agate (page does not exist)">Akro Agate</a>. This company was started by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Akronites&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Akronites (page does not exist)">Akronites</a> in 1911, but was located in Clarksburg, West Virginia. Today, there are only two American-based toy marble manufacturers: Jabo Vitro in <a href="/wiki/Reno,_Ohio" title="Reno, Ohio">Reno, Ohio</a>, and Marble King, in <a href="/wiki/Paden_City,_West_Virginia" title="Paden City, West Virginia">Paden City, West Virginia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marbles_games">Marbles games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marbles games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Australia, games were played with marbles of different sizes. The smallest and most common was about 15 mm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> in) in diameter. The two larger, more valuable sizes were referred to as semi-bowlers and tom-bowlers, being about 20 mm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> in) and 25 mm (1 in) in diameter respectively. They were used in much the same way as ordinary marbles, although sometimes they would be declared invalid because of the advantage of their larger mass and inertia. Owners of large marbles were also afraid to use them lest they be lost to another player as "keepsies". They were usually of the clear "cat's eye" or milk glass type, just bigger.</p>
<p>"Firing" a marble meant that a player had to flick his/her marble from a stationary position of his hand. No part of the hand firing the marble was permitted to be in front of the position where the marble had been resting on the ground. Using that hand, (s)he would flick or fire the marble from his/her hand, usually with the knuckle on the back of his/her hand resting on the ground, and usually using the thumb of that hand to do so. All shots of the game were conducted in this manner throughout except the very initial pitch towards the bunny hole that commenced the game.</p>
<p>Once a player was able to land his/her marble within the hole, (s)he would immediately then fire his marble at his opponents' marbles. However, if any player hit another player's marble before his/her own marble had been to 'visit' the bunny hole, the act would be referred to as "a kiss"; the game would be over, and all or both players (in the case of two players only) would have to retreat back to the starting line to re-commence the game, without result. This, of course, could be quite annoying or frustrating if a player had already built up quite a few hits on another player's marble! So, most skilled players did not resort to this kind of tactic.</p>
<p>The overall aim was to hit a particular marble 3 times after getting into the hole, then you had to "run away", before the final contact shot was allowed to be played - which was called "the kill". Once a player made a kill on another marble, if the game was 'for keeps', (s)he would then get to keep the marble [bunny] (s)he had 'killed'. The format of playing this game was that each time you successfully hit another player's marble, you were to have another shot - even if it was not the marble you had originally intended to hit.</p>
<p>Of course, the ploy was to hit the particular opponent marble 3 times, and then 'run away' to the bunny hole, because once you rested the marble into the hole, you immediately had your shot again, thus leaving no opportunity at all for your opponent to retreat his/her marble before "the Kill" was made on it.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, a popular marbles game is called <i>dool</i>. It requires a small pit dug in the ground for two or more players, each with his own marble. To improvise, Ugandans also use the seeds of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candlenut" title="Candlenut">candlenut</a> tree, locally referred to as <i>Kabakanjagala</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (The <a href="/wiki/Kabaka_of_Buganda" title="Kabaka of Buganda">King</a> loves me). To start a game, a throwing line is drawn on the ground using chalk or a stick about a meter (or some feet) from the pit. Then the players roll their marbles close to the pit. The one whose marble falls in gets points equivalent to one game. If a second marble falls in and hits the first, a player gets more points than the previous player, but all have to return to the throwing line. When no marble falls in, the player whose marble rolls closest to the pit starts the firing session. When he misses, the next opponent fires. You can only fire 24 consecutive times per turn earning one point for each hit. But all that time, a player must make sure the gap between the marbles is bigger than two stretches of the hand. If an opponent realises that it isn't, then he can make a call, pick his marble and place it anywhere. When a player is targeting a marble placed near the hole, he must avoid knocking it into the hole or else give away an advantage. There are various rules for <i>dool</i> but the player with the most points wins. Favoured fingers include the middle finger for blasting strength and small finger for accurate long distance target.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_championship">World championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_and_World_Marbles_Championship" title="British and World Marbles Championship">British and World Marbles Championship</a> has been held at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tinsley_Green" title="Tinsley Green">Tinsley Green</a>, West Sussex, England, every year since 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideOut_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-InsideOut-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins88_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins88-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-7">[7]</a></sup> (Marbles has been played in Tinsley Green and the surrounding area for many centuries:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideOut_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-InsideOut-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheTimes_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheTimes-8">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i> magazine traces its origins to 1588.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-9">[9]</a></sup>) Traditionally, the marbles-playing season started on <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday" title="Ash Wednesday">Ash Wednesday</a> and lasted until midday on <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a>: playing after that brought bad luck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins88_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collins88-6">[6]</a></sup> More than 20 teams from around the world take part in the championship, each Good Friday; German teams have been successful several times since 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideOut_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-InsideOut-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheTimes_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheTimes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson-10">[10]</a></sup> although local teams from Crawley, <a href="/wiki/Copthorne,_West_Sussex" title="Copthorne, West Sussex">Copthorne</a> and other Sussex and <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> villages often take part as well;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideOut_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-InsideOut-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gwynne172_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gwynne172-11">[11]</a></sup> the first championship in 1932 was won by Ellen Geary, a young girl from London.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marble_terminology">Marble terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marble terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>"Knuckle down": the position adopted at the start line at the beginning of a match. The player begins with his or her knuckle against the ground.</li>
<li>"Quitsies": allows any opponent to stop the game without consequence. Players can either have "quitsies" (able to quit) or "no quitsies".</li>
<li>"Keepsies" (or "for keeps"): the player keeps all the marbles he or she wins.</li>
<li>"Elephant stomps": when called, it allows a player to stomp his or her marble level with the ground surface, making it very difficult for other players to hit.</li>
<li>"Bombies": when called, it allows a player to take one or two steps while holding his or her marble and, while closing one eye, will line up over one of the opponent's marbles and drop the marble trying to hit the marble on the ground.</li>
<li>"Leaning tops": when called, a shooter leans in on his or her off hand for leverage over an indentation on any type of surface or obstacle.</li>
<li>A "taw" or "shooter" is generally a larger marble used to shoot with, and "ducks" are marbles to be shot at.</li>
<li>Various names refer to the marbles' size. Any marble larger than the majority may be termed a boulder, bonker, masher, plumper, popper, shooter, thumper, smasher, taw, bumbo, crock, bumboozer, bowler, tonk, tronk, godfather, tom bowler, fourer, giant, dobber, <a href="/wiki/Dobbert" title="Dobbert">dobbert</a>, hogger, biggie or toebreaker. A marble smaller than the majority is a peawee, peewee or mini. A "grandfather" is the largest marble, the size of a billiards ball or tennis ball.</li>
<li>Various names for different marble types (regional playground talk, <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>, UK): Marleys (marbles), prit (white marble), Kong (large marble), King Kong (larger than a bosser), steely (metal bearing-ball). Names can be combined: e.g. prit-Kong (large white marble). There are many more such names, as discussed in the next section.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marble_collecting">Marble collecting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marble collecting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Historic_Marbles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="394" data-file-width="525" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Historic_Marbles.jpg/220px-Historic_Marbles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Historic_Marbles.jpg/330px-Historic_Marbles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Historic_Marbles.jpg/440px-Historic_Marbles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Historic_Marbles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some historic marbles</div>

</div>
<p>Marble players often grow to collect marbles after having outgrown the game. Marbles are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Categorize" title="Categorize">categorized</a> by many factors including condition, size, type, manufacturer/<a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisan</a>, age, style, materials, scarcity, and the existence of original packaging (which is further rated in terms of condition). A marble's worth is primarily determined by type, size, condition and eye-appeal, coupled with the law of supply and demand. Ugly, but rare marbles may be valued as much as those of very fine quality. However, this is the exception, rather than the rule - "Condition is King" when it comes to marbles. Any surface damage (characterized by missing glass, such as chips or pits) typically cuts book value by 50% or more.</p>
<p>Due to the large market, there are many related side businesses that have sprung up such as numerous books and guides, web sites dedicated to live <a href="/wiki/Auction" title="Auction">auctions</a> of marbles only, and collector <a href="/wiki/Convention_(meeting)" title="Convention (meeting)">conventions</a>. Additionally, many glass artisans produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_marbles" title="Art marbles">art marbles</a> for the collectors' market only, with some selling for hundreds of dollars.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marble_Machine,_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="384" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg/170px-Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg/255px-Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg/340px-Marble_Machine%2C_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marble_Machine,_at_House_of_Marbles_-_geograph.org.uk_-_506883.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Marbles are made using many techniques. They can be categorized into two general types: <a href="/wiki/Lapidary" title="Lapidary">hand-made</a> and machine-made.</p>
<p>Marbles were originally made by hand. <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">Stone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> marbles can be fashioned by grinding. <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">Clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> marbles can be made by rolling the material into a ball, and then letting dry, or firing, and then can be left natural, painted, or <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">glazed</a>. Clay marbles, also known as <i>crock marbles</i> or <i>commies</i> (<i>common</i>), are made of slightly porous clay, traditionally from local clay or leftover earthenware ("crockery"), rolled into balls, then glazed and fired at low heat, creating an opaque imperfect sphere that is frequently sold as the poor boy's "old timey" marble. Glass marbles can be fashioned through the production of glass rods which are stacked together to form the desired pattern, cutting the rod into marble-sized pieces using marble scissors, and rounding the still-malleable glass.</p>
<p>One mechanical technique is dropping globules of molten glass into a groove made by two interlocking parallel screws. As the screws rotate, the marble travels along them, gradually being shaped into a sphere as it cools. Color is added to the main batch glass and/or to additional glass streams that are combined with the main stream in a variety of ways. For example, in the "cat's-eye" style, colored glass veins are injected into a transparent main stream. Applying more expensive colored glass to the surface of cheaper transparent or white glass is also a common technique.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_locations">Manufacturing locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manufacturing locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were a lot of businesses that made marbles in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> One major marble manufacturing company is Marble King, located in <a href="/wiki/Paden_City,_West_Virginia" title="Paden City, West Virginia">Paden City, West Virginia</a>, which was featured in the television shows <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Ratzenberger%27s_Made_in_America" title="John Ratzenberger's Made in America">Made in America</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Assembly_Required_(TV_series)" title="Some Assembly Required (TV series)">Some Assembly Required</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">The Colbert Report</a></i>. Currently, the world's largest manufacturer of playing marbles is Vacor de Mexico. The company makes 90 percent of the world’s marbles. Over 12 million are produced daily.</p>
<p>Marbles are also made in China and may contain lead, arsenic, and/or cadmium due to the manufacturing process of old glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_marbles">Art marbles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Art marbles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Art_marble" title="Art marble">Art marble</a></div>
<p>Art marbles are high-quality collectible marbles arising out of the art glass movement. They are sometimes referred to as contemporary glass marbles to differentiate them from collectible antique marbles, and are spherical works of art glass.</p>
<p>Collectible contemporary marbles are made mostly in the United States by individual artists such as <a href="/wiki/Josh_Simpson_(glass_artist)" title="Josh Simpson (glass artist)">Josh Simpson</a>.</p>
<p>They are used for art Art marbles are usually around the size of a 50mm marble, also known as a "toe breaker", but can vary, depending on the artist and the print.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marble_(toy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abalone_(board_game)" title="Abalone (board game)">Abalone (board game)</a>, a board game in which white and black marbles try to knock each other into a gutter that lines the outside of the board</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/B-Daman" title="B-Daman">B-Daman</a>, a toy that fires marbles and can be played under several game rules</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Bakugan_Battle_Brawlers" title="Bakugan Battle Brawlers">Bakugan Battle Brawlers</a></i>, a game which uses magnetic spring loading marbles which open up to reveal creatures used to play the game</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_checkers" title="Chinese checkers">Chinese checkers</a></i>, often called "Marble Checkers", a board game for two to six players using marbles as game pieces</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Hungry_Hungry_Hippos" title="Hungry Hungry Hippos">Hungry Hungry Hippos</a></i>, a tabletop game for two to four players involving marbles</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/KerPlunk_(game)" title="KerPlunk (game)">Ker-Plunk</a></i>, a game for two to four players involving marbles.</li>
<li>Marble Silde: also known as <a href="/wiki/Rolling_ball_sculpture" title="Rolling ball sculpture">Rolling ball sculpture</a>, Marble maze, marble run, marble rail, marble coaster. Used in such things as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinball_machine" title="Pinball machine">pinball machines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rube_Goldberg_machine" title="Rube Goldberg machine">Rube Goldberg machines</a>. A game of skill, involving building using; rails, tracks, cones, wheels, levers, and ramps.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Tock" title="Tock">Tock</a></i>, also known as Tuck, is a cards/board game in which players race their four marbles (or tokens) around the board, with the objective being to be the first to take all of one's marbles "home".</li>
</ul>
