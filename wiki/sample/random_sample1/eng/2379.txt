<p><b>Guano</b> (via <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, ultimately from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a> <i>wanu</i>) is the accumulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excrement" title="Excrement">excrement</a> of seabirds, seals, or cave-dwelling <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h2g2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-h2g2-1">[1]</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, guano is a highly effective <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> due to its exceptionally high content of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>: nutrients essential for plant growth. The 19th-century guano trade played a pivotal role in the development of modern input-intensive farming practices and inspired the formal human colonization of remote bird islands in many parts of the world. During the twentieth century, guano-producing birds became an important target of conservation programs and influenced the development of environmental consciousness. Today, guano is increasingly sought after by organic farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2013_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2013-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seabird guano consists of nitrogen-rich <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">urate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphates</a>, as well as some earth salts and impurities. Unleached guano from favored locales, such as the <a href="/wiki/Chincha_Islands" title="Chincha Islands">Chincha Islands</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, typically contains 8 to 16 percent nitrogen (the majority of which is <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>), 8 to 12 percent equivalent phosphoric acid, and 2 to 3 percent equivalent <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SzpakPLOS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SzpakPLOS-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SzpakJAS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SzpakJAS-4">[4]</a></sup> The fresh excrement of insect-eating bats can have nitrogen levels approximating that of seabird guano and high phosphate levels, but bat guano typically has less fertilizing value than seabird guano from arid areas because its nitrogen content is more easily leached within cave environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerson2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The word "guano" originates from the Andean indigenous language <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a>, which refers to any form of dung used as an agricultural fertilizer. Archaeological evidence suggests that Andean people have collected guano from small islands and points located off the desert coast of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> for use as a soil amendment for well over 1,500 years. Spanish colonial documents suggest that the rulers of the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a> assigned great value to guano, restricted access to it, and punished any disturbance of the birds with death. The <a href="/wiki/Guanay_cormorant" title="Guanay cormorant">Guanay cormorant</a> has historically been the most abundant and important producer of guano. Other important guano producing <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> off the coast of Peru are the <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_pelican" title="Peruvian pelican">Peruvian pelican</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_booby" title="Peruvian booby">Peruvian booby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2013_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2013-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SzpakJAS_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-SzpakJAS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1802, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> was the first European to encounter guano and began investigating its fertilizing properties at <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a> in Peru, and his subsequent writings on this topic made the subject well known in Europe. During the guano boom of the nineteenth century, the vast majority of seabird guano was harvested from Peruvian guano islands, but large quantities were also exported from the Caribbean, atolls in the Central Pacific, and islands off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>. At that time, massive deposits of guano existed on some islands, in some cases more than 50 m deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1950_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1950-6">[6]</a></sup> In this context the United States passed the <a href="/wiki/Guano_Islands_Act" title="Guano Islands Act">Guano Islands Act</a> in 1856, which gave U.S. citizens discovering a source of guano on an unclaimed island exclusive rights to the deposits. Nine of these islands are still officially U.S. territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skaggs1994_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skaggs1994-7">[7]</a></sup> Control over guano played a central role in the <a href="/wiki/Chincha_Islands_War" title="Chincha Islands War">Chincha Islands War</a> (1864–1866) between Spain and a Peruvian-Chilean alliance. Indentured workers from China played an important role in guano harvest. The first group of 79 Chinese workers arrived in Peru in 1849; by the time that trade ended a quarter of a century later, over 100,000 of their fellow countrymen had been imported. There is no documentary evidence that enslaved Pacific Islanders participated in guano mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A9ndez1987_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A9ndez1987-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1870, the use of Peruvian guano as a fertilizer was eclipsed by <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">saltpeter</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Caliche" title="Caliche">caliche</a> extraction from the interior of the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>, not far from the guano areas. During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific" title="War of the Pacific">War of the Pacific</a> (1879–1883) Chile seized much of the guano as well as Peru's nitrate-producing area, enabling its national treasury to grow by 900% between 1879 and 1902 thanks to taxes coming from the newly acquired lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Contrary to popular belief, seabird guano does not have high concentrations of nitrates, and was never important to the production of <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" title="Explosive material">explosives</a>; bat and cave-bird deposits have been processed to produce gunpowder, however. High-grade rock <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> deposits on <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banaba_Island" title="Banaba Island">Banaba Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Island" title="Christmas Island">Christmas Island</a> (Indian Ocean), and other raised atolls were not produced by marine birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glenn2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glenn2000-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1909, when the Peruvian government took over guano extraction for use by Peru farmers, the industry has relied on production by living populations of marine birds. U.S. ornithologists <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cushman_Murphy" title="Robert Cushman Murphy">Robert Cushman Murphy</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Vogt" title="William Vogt">William Vogt</a> promoted the Peruvian industry internationally as a supreme example of <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_conservation" title="Wildlife conservation">wildlife conservation</a>, while also drawing attention to its vulnerability to the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> phenomenon. <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> independently developed its own guano industry based on sustained-yield production from marine birds during this period, as well. Both industries eventually collapsed due to pressure from overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2013_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2013-2">[2]</a></sup> The importance of guano deposits to agriculture elsewhere in the world faded after 1909 when <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber" title="Fritz Haber">Fritz Haber</a> developed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber-Bosch_process" title="Haber-Bosch process">Haber-Bosch process</a> of industrial <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>, which today generates the ammonia-based fertilizer responsible for sustaining an estimated one-third of the Earth's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>DNA testing has suggested that new potato varieties imported alongside Peruvian seabird guano in 1842 brought a virulent strain of potato blight that began the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish Potato Famine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dwyer-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.C3.B3mez2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-G.C3.B3mez2007-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sourcing">Sourcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sourcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>The ideal type of guano is found in exceptionally <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#GROUP_B:_Dry_.28arid_and_semiarid.29_climates" title="Köppen climate classification">dry climates</a>, as rainwater volatilizes and leaches nitrogen-containing ammonia from guano. In order to support large colonies of marine birds and the fish they feed on, these islands must be adjacent to regions of intense marine <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>, such as those along the eastern boundaries of the Pacific and South Atlantic Oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1950_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1950-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Post-depositional decomposition and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> volatilization of <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguin</a> guano also plays an important role in the evolution of ornithogenic sediments in the cold and arid environment of Antarctica (<a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Sound" title="McMurdo Sound">McMurdo Sound</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">Ross Sea</a> region, <a href="/wiki/East_Antarctica" title="East Antarctica">East Antarctica</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bat guano is usually mined in caves and this mining is associated with a corresponding loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troglobite" title="Troglobite">troglobytic</a> <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a> and diminishing of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. Guano deposits support a great variety of cave-adapted <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> that rely on bat feces as their sole source of nutrition. In addition to the biological component, deep guano deposits of both kinds contain local <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatic</a> records in strata that have built up over thousands of years, which are unrecoverable once disturbed.</p>
<p>The greatest damage caused by mining to caves with extant guano deposits is to the bat colonies themselves. Bats are highly vulnerable to regular disturbance to their roosts. Some species, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamaican_Flower_Bat" title="Jamaican Flower Bat">Phyllonycteris aphylla</a></i>, have low <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">fat reserves</a>, and will starve to death when regularly disturbed and put into a panic state during their resting period. Many species will drop pups when in panic, with subsequent death, leading to a steady reduction in population. Research in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> has shown that mining for bat guano is directly related to the loss of bat species, associated invertebrates and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and is the greatest threat to bat caves on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, when done with caution, extraction of guano can be done alongside marine bird colonies without causing them significant harm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and gardening guano has a number of uses, including as: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_amendment" title="Soil amendment">soil builder</a>, lawn treatments, <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicide</a> (when fed to plants through the leaves), <a href="/wiki/Nematicide" title="Nematicide">nematicide</a> (decomposing microbes help control <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>), and as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composting" title="Composting">composting</a> activator (nutrients and microbes speed up decomposition).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his poem '<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guanosong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guanosong (page does not exist)">Guanosong</a>', <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Victor_von_Scheffel" title="Joseph Victor von Scheffel">Joseph Victor von Scheffel</a> described the development of the manure in humouristic verses in the middle of the 19th century. He used poetry for a version of the then-popular polemic against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegel" title="Hegel">Hegel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Naturphilosophie" title="Naturphilosophie">Naturphilosophie</a>. The poem starts with highly sophisticated wording and allegations to <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heinrich Heines</a> <a href="/wiki/Lorelei" title="Lorelei">Lorelei</a> and may be sung along the same tunes, as from 1837 by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Silcher" title="Friedrich Silcher">Friedrich Silcher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The poem ends however with the grunt statement of a Swabian <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a> farmer from <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6blingen" title="Böblingen">Böblingen</a>, which praises the seagulls, providing better <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On_bullshit" title="On bullshit">'birdshit'</a> even than fellow countryman Hegel. It has been translated among others by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Godfrey_Leland" title="Charles Godfrey Leland">Charles Godfrey Leland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Guano is featured quite heavily in the 1995 American Comedy, <a href="/wiki/Ace_Ventura:_When_Nature_Calls" title="Ace Ventura: When Nature Calls">Ace Ventura: When Nature Calls</a>.</p>
