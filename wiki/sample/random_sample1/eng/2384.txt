<p><b>Kuwana Domain</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">桑名藩</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kuwana-han</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_(Japan)" title="Han (Japan)">domain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Ise_Province" title="Ise Province">Ise Province</a> (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Mie_Prefecture" title="Mie Prefecture">Mie Prefecture</a>), <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It was centered on <a href="/wiki/Kuwana_Castle" title="Kuwana Castle">Kuwana Castle</a> in what is now the city of <a href="/wiki/Kuwana,_Mie" title="Kuwana, Mie">Kuwana, Mie</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuwana_Domain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the late <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muromachi_period" title="Muromachi period">Muromachi period</a>, the area of modern Kuwana was known as Juraku-no-tsu <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">十楽の津</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>and was a major seaport on the east coast of Japan, controlled by a guild of merchants. The poet Socho described it in 1515 as a major city with over a thousand houses, temples and inns. During the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a>, the area came under the control of <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>, who assigned it to his retainer, <a href="/wiki/Takigawa_Kazumasu" title="Takigawa Kazumasu">Takigawa Kazumasu</a>. After Nobunaga’s death, the area came under the control of <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a>, who initially installed Nobunaga’s younger son <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobukatsu" title="Oda Nobukatsu">Oda Nobukatsu</a> as ruler as all of Ise Province. However, following the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Odawara_(1590)" title="Siege of Odawara (1590)">Battle of Odawara</a>, Hideyoshi demoted Oda Nobukatsu, divided Ise Province into several domains, and assigned Hitotsuyanagi Naomori as ruler of Kuwana. In 1595, the area was reassigned to <a href="/wiki/Ujiie_Yukihiro" title="Ujiie Yukihiro">Ujiie Yukihiro</a> as a 22,000 <i><a href="/wiki/Koku" title="Koku">koku</a></i> domain. Ujiie Yukihiro sided with the pro-Toyotomi armies in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sekigahara" title="Battle of Sekigahara">Battle of Sekigahara</a> and was dispossessed by <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">Tokugawa Ieyasu</a>.</p>
<p>In January 1601, one of Ieyasu’s main generals, <a href="/wiki/Honda_Tadakatsu" title="Honda Tadakatsu">Honda Tadakatsu</a> was installed as <a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyō</a> of Kuwana Domain, with revenues of 100,000 <i>koku</i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Shogunate" title="Tokugawa Shogunate">Tokugawa Shogunate</a> recognized the strategic value of the location as both a seaport, and also as <a href="/wiki/Kuwana-juku" title="Kuwana-juku">Kuwana-juku</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Shukuba" title="Shukuba">post station</a> on the vital <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkaid%C5%8D_(road)" title="Tōkaidō (road)">Tōkaidō</a> highway connecting <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> with <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>. In 1609, Tadakatsu was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Honda_Tadamasa" title="Honda Tadamasa">Honda Tadamasa</a>, who distinguished himself at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Osaka" title="Siege of Osaka">Siege of Osaka</a> and was rewarded with a transfer to the more lucrative <a href="/wiki/Himeji_Domain" title="Himeji Domain">Himeji Domain</a> in 1617.</p>
<p>The strategic Kuwana Domain was then assigned to Ieyasu’s half-brother, <a href="/wiki/Hisamatsu_Sadakatsu" title="Hisamatsu Sadakatsu">Hisamatsu Sadakatsu</a>, whose descendants ruled until they were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Takada_Domain" title="Takada Domain">Takada Domain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Echigo_Province" title="Echigo Province">Echigo Province</a> in 1710, and their place taken by the Okudaira branch of the <a href="/wiki/Matsudaira_clan" title="Matsudaira clan">Matsudaira clan</a>, who ruled to 1823, when a branch of the Hisamatsu returned to Kuwana from <a href="/wiki/Shirakawa_Domain" title="Shirakawa Domain">Shirakawa Domain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mutsu_Province" title="Mutsu Province">Mutsu Province</a>. The Hisamatsu continued to rule Kuwana until the end of the Tokugawa shogunate.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matsudaira_Sadaaki" title="Matsudaira Sadaaki">Matsudaira Sadaaki</a>, the next-to-last daimyō of Kuwana served as the last <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_shoshidai" title="Kyoto shoshidai">Kyoto shoshidai</a></i> and supported his brother, <a href="/wiki/Matsudaira_Katamori" title="Matsudaira Katamori">Matsudaira Katamori</a>, daimyo of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aizu_Domain" title="Aizu Domain">Aizu Domain</a>. He fought in the <a href="/wiki/Boshin_War" title="Boshin War">Boshin War</a>, finally surrendering to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiji_government" title="Meiji government">Meiji government</a> after the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ezo" title="Republic of Ezo">Republic of Ezo</a>.The final daimyō of Kuwana, Matsudaira Sadanori, was still a child during the Boshin War. He capitulated Kuwana Castle to the <a href="/wiki/Satch%C5%8D_Alliance" title="Satchō Alliance">Satchō Alliance</a> forces without a battle. He was later educated in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and joined the Meiji government, serving as Japanese ambassador to Italy. He was later ennobled with the <i><a href="/wiki/Kazoku" title="Kazoku">kazoku</a></i> peerage title of <i>shishaku</i> (<a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscount</a>).</p>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_han_system" title="Abolition of the han system">abolition of the han system</a> in July 1871, Kuwana Domain became “Kuwana Prefecture”, which later became part of Mie Prefecture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territory">Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kuwana_Domain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with all of the Japanese feudal domains, Kuwana Domain was not a single contiguous territory, but consisted of a number of scattered holdings. At the end of the Edo period, it consisted of numerous villages in Ise Province and also in <a href="/wiki/Echigo_Province" title="Echigo Province">Echigo Province</a>:</p>
<p>Ise Province</p>
<ul>
<li>64 villages in Kuwana District</li>
<li>83 villages in Inabe District</li>
<li>30 villages in Asake District</li>
<li>9 villages in Mie District</li>
</ul>
<p>Echigo Province</p>
<ul>
<li>4 villages in Koshi District</li>
<li>22 villages in Uonuma District</li>
<li>82 villages in Kariwa District</li>
<li>30 villages in Santo District</li>
<li>7 villages in Kambara District</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition to the above, Kuwana Domain also administered 212 villages within Echigo Province which were tenryo territory on behalf of the shogunate.</p>
