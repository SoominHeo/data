<p><b>Kyūjitai</b>, literally "old character forms" (Kyūjitai: <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">舊字體</span> or <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">旧字体</span>), are the traditional forms of <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese</a> written characters used in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>. Their simplified counterparts are <a href="/wiki/Shinjitai" title="Shinjitai">shinjitai</a> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新字体</span>), "new character forms". Some of the simplified characters arose centuries ago and were in everyday use in both <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, but they were considered inelegant, even uncouth. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, simplified character forms were made official in both these countries. However, in Japan fewer and less drastic simplifications were made: e.g. "electric" is still written as <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">電</span> in Japan, as it is also written in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macao" title="Macao">Macao</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, which continue to use <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese characters</a>, but has been simplified to <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">电</span> in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>. Prior to the promulgation of the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Tōyō kanji">Tōyō kanji</a> list in 1946, kyūjitai were known as <i>seiji</i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">正字</span>; meaning "proper/correct characters") or <i>seijitai</i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">正字體</span>). Even after <i>kyūjitai</i> were officially marked for discontinuation with the promulgation of the Tōyō kanji list, they were used in print frequently into the 1950s due to logistical delays in changing over <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a> equipment. <i>Kyūjitai</i> continue in use to the present day because when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_government" title="Japanese government">Japanese government</a> adopted the simplified forms, it did not ban the traditional forms. Thus traditional forms are used when an author wishes to use traditional forms and the publisher agrees.</p>
<p>Unlike in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, where all personal names were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_character" title="Simplified Chinese character">simplified</a> as part of the character simplification reform carried out in the 1950s, the Japanese reform only applied to a subset of the characters in use (the Toyo Kanji) and excluded characters used in proper names. Therefore, <i>kyūjitai</i> are still used in personal names in Japan today (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jinmeiyo_kanji" title="Jinmeiyo kanji">Jinmeiyo kanji</a>). In modern Japanese, <i>kyūjitai</i> that appear in the official spelling of proper names are sometimes replaced with the modern <i>shinjitai</i> form.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="J.C5.8Dy.C5.8D_Kanji">Jōyō Kanji</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ky%C5%ABjitai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Jōyō Kanji">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Jōyō kanji">Jōyō kanji</a></div>
<p>In the 2,136 <i>Jōyō Kanji</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">常用漢字</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, there are 364 pairs of simplified and traditional characters (for example, <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">亜</span> is the simplified form of <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">亞</span>). Note that the kanji <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">弁</span> is used to simplify three different traditional kanji (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">辨</span>, <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">瓣</span>, and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">辯</span>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_characters_that_may_cause_problems_displaying">Traditional characters that may cause problems displaying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ky%C5%ABjitai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditional characters that may cause problems displaying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of the traditional kanji are not included in the Japanese font of Windows XP/2000, and only rectangles are shown. Downloading the Meiryo font from the Microsoft website (VistaFont_JPN.EXE) and installing it will solve this problem.</p>
<p>Note that within the jōyō kanji there are 62 characters the old forms of which may cause problems displaying:</p>
<p>Kyōiku kanji (26):</p>
<ul>
<li>Grade 2 (2 kanji): <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">海 社</span></li>
<li>Grade 3 (8 kanji): <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">勉 暑 漢 神 福 練 者 都</span></li>

<li>Grade 5 (1 kanji): <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">祖</span></li>
<li>Grade 6 (9 kanji): <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">勤 穀 視 署 層 著 諸 難 朗</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Secondary-school kanji (36):</p>
<ul>
<li>Secondary school (36 kanji): <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">欄 廊 虜 隆 塚 祥 侮 僧 免 卑 喝 嘆 塀 墨 悔 慨 憎 懲 敏 既 煮 碑 祉 祈 禍 突 繁 臭 褐 謁 謹 賓 贈 逸 響 頻</span></li>
</ul>
<p>These characters are Unicode CJK Unified Ideographs for which the old form (kyūjitai) and the new form (shinjitai) have been unified under the Unicode standard. Although the old and new forms are distinguished under the JIS X 0213 standard, the old forms map to Unicode CJK Compatibility Ideographs which are considered by Unicode to be canonically equivalent to the new forms and may not be distinguished by user agents. Therefore, depending on the user environment, it may not be possible to see the distinction between old and new forms of the characters. In particular, all Unicode normalization methods merge the old characters with the new ones.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kokuji">Kokuji</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ky%C5%ABjitai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Kokuji">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kokuji are characters that were created in Japan and were not taken over from China. Some of them, e.g. <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">働</span>, are now also used in Chinese, but most of them are unknown to the Chinese.</p>
<p>Currently, there are no kokuji that have been simplified after their introduction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kokuji_used_as_J.C5.8Dy.C5.8D_Kanji_or_Jinmeiy.C5.8D_Kanji_.28as_of_2010.29">Kokuji used as Jōyō Kanji or Jinmeiyō Kanji (as of 2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ky%C5%ABjitai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Kokuji used as Jōyō Kanji or Jinmeiyō Kanji (as of 2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Jōyō Kanji List currently contains 9 kokuji (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">働</span> and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">畑</span> are Kyōiku Kanji):</p>
<dl>
<dd><span lang="ja" style="font-family:Meiryo; font-size:large; line-height:125%" xml:lang="ja">働 匂 塀 峠 腺 枠 栃 畑 込</span></dd>
</dl>
<p><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">匁</span> was removed from the Jōyō Kanji List in 2010, but is still used as Jinmeiyō Kanji.</p>
<p>The Jinmeiyō Kanji List currently contains 16 kokuji:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span lang="ja" style="font-family:Meiryo; font-size:large; line-height:125%" xml:lang="ja">匁 俣 凧 凪 喰 柾 椛 榊 樫 畠 笹 籾 辻 雫 鰯 麿</span></dd>
</dl>
