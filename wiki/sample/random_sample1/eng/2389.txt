<p><i><b>The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">九章算术</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">九章算術</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Jiǔzhāng Suànshù</span></i>) is a Chinese <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> book, composed by several generations of scholars from the 10th–2nd century BCE, its latest stage being from the 2nd century CE. This book is one of the earliest surviving mathematical texts from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the first being <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Su%C3%A0n_sh%C3%B9_sh%C5%AB" title="Suàn shù shū">Suàn shù shū</a></i> (202 BCE – 186 BCE) and <i><a href="/wiki/Zhoubi_Suanjing" title="Zhoubi Suanjing">Zhoubi Suanjing</a></i> (compiled throughout the Han until the late 2nd century CE). It lays out an approach to mathematics that centres on finding the most general methods of solving problems, which may be contrasted with the approach common to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> mathematicians, who tended to deduce propositions from an initial set of <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a>.</p>
<p>Entries in the book usually take the form of a statement of a problem, followed by the statement of the solution, and an explanation of the procedure that led to the solution. These were commented on by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Hui" title="Liu Hui">Liu Hui</a> in the 3rd century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Nine_Chapters_on_the_Mathematical_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_of_the_Han_Dynasty" title="Science and technology of the Han Dynasty">Science and technology of the Han Dynasty</a></div>
<p>The full title of <i>The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art</i> appears on two <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> standard measures which are dated to 179 CE, but there is speculation that the same book existed beforehand under different titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_24_25_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_24_25-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most scholars believe that Chinese mathematics and the mathematics of the ancient Mediterranean world had developed more or less independently up to the time when the Nine Chapters reached its final form. The method of chapter 7 was not found in Europe until the 13th century, and the method of chapter 8 uses <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_elimination" title="Gaussian elimination">Gaussian elimination</a> before <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> (1777–1855).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There is also the mathematical proof given in the treatise for the <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_22_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_22-3">[3]</a></sup> The influence of The Nine Chapters greatly assisted the development of ancient mathematics in the regions of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Its influence on mathematical thought in China persisted until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> era.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Liu_Hui" title="Liu Hui">Liu Hui</a> wrote a very detailed commentary on this book in 263. He analyses the procedures of the Nine Chapters step by step, in a manner which is clearly designed to give the reader confidence that they are reliable, although he is not concerned to provide formal proofs in the <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclidean</a> manner. Liu's commentary is of great mathematical interest in its own right. Liu credits the earlier mathematicians <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Cang" title="Zhang Cang">Zhang Cang</a> (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 165 BCE - d. 142 BCE) and Geng Shouchang (fl. 75 BCE-49 BCE) (see <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">armillary sphere</a>) with the initial arrangement and commentary on the book, yet Han Dynasty records do not indicate the names of any authors of commentary, as they are not mentioned until the 3rd century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_24_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_24-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Nine Chapters</i> is an anonymous work, and its origins are not clear. Until recent years, there was no substantial evidence of related mathematical writing that might have preceded it, with the exception of mathematical work by those such as <a href="/wiki/Jing_Fang" title="Jing Fang">Jing Fang</a> (78–37 BCE), <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xin" title="Liu Xin">Liu Xin</a> (d. 23), and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">Zhang Heng</a> (78–139) and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_geometry#Chinese_geometry" title="History of geometry">geometry clauses</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Mozi" title="Mozi">Mozi</a></i> of the 4th century BCE. This is no longer the case. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Su%C3%A0n_sh%C3%B9_sh%C5%AB" title="Suàn shù shū">Suàn shù shū</a> (算數書) or <i>writings on reckoning</i> is an ancient Chinese text on mathematics approximately seven thousand characters in length, written on 190 bamboo strips. It was discovered together with other writings in 1983 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a> opened a tomb in <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> province. It is among the corpus of texts known as the <a href="/wiki/Zhangjiashan_Han_bamboo_texts" title="Zhangjiashan Han bamboo texts">Zhangjiashan Han bamboo texts</a>. From documentary evidence this tomb is known to have been closed in 186 BCE, early in the Western <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>. While its relationship to the <i>Nine Chapters</i> is still under discussion by scholars, some of its contents are clearly paralleled there. The text of the <i>Suàn shù shū</i> is however much less systematic than the Nine Chapters; and appears to consist of a number of more or less independent short sections of text drawn from a number of sources. The <i><a href="/wiki/Zhoubi_Suanjing" title="Zhoubi Suanjing">Zhoubi Suanjing</a></i>, a mathematics <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">and astronomy</a> text, was also compiled during the Han, and was even mentioned as a school of mathematics in and around 180 CE by <a href="/wiki/Cai_Yong" title="Cai Yong">Cai Yong</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_of_contents">Table of contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Nine_Chapters_on_the_Mathematical_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Table of contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contents of <i>The Nine Chapters</i> are as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>方田 <i>Fangtian</i> - Bounding fields. <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">Areas</a> of fields of various shapes; <a href="/wiki/Rod_calculus#Fractions" title="Rod calculus">manipulation of vulgar fractions</a>. Liu Hui's commentary includes a method for calculation of π and the approximate value of 3.14159.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>粟米 <i>Sumi</i> - Millet and rice. Exchange of commodities at different rates; pricing.</li>
<li>衰分 <i>Cuifen</i> - Proportional distribution. Distribution of commodities and money at proportional rates; deriving arithmetic and geometric sums.</li>
<li>少廣 <i>Shaoguang</i> - Reducing dimensions. <a href="/wiki/Rod_calculus#Division" title="Rod calculus">Division</a> by mixed <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>; <a href="/wiki/Rod_calculus#Extraction_of_Square_root" title="Rod calculus">extraction of square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cube_root" title="Cube root">cube roots</a>; <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perimeter" title="Perimeter">perimeter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a>.</li>
<li>商功 <i>Shanggong</i> - Figuring for construction. Volumes of <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> of various shapes.</li>
<li>均輸 <i>Junshu</i> - Equitable taxation. More advanced problems on proportion.</li>
<li>盈不足 <i>Yingbuzu</i> - Excess and deficit. Linear problems solved using the principle known later in the West as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rule_of_false_position" title="Rule of false position">rule of false position</a></i>.</li>
<li>方程 <i><a href="/wiki/Fangcheng_(mathematics)" title="Fangcheng (mathematics)">Fangcheng</a></i> - The two-sided reference (i.e. Equations). <a href="/wiki/Rod_calculus#System_of_linear_equations" title="Rod calculus">Systems of linear equations</a>, solved by a principle similar to <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_elimination" title="Gaussian elimination">Gaussian elimination</a>.</li>
<li>勾股 <i>Gougu</i> - Base and altitude. Problems involving the principle known in the West as the <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean theorem</a>.</li>
</ol>
