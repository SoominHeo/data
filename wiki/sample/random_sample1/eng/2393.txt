<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <b>structuralism</b> is the methodology that elements of human culture must be understood in terms of their relationship to a larger, overarching system or structure. It works to uncover the structures that underlie all the things that humans do, think, perceive, and feel. Alternatively, as summarized by philosopher <a href="/wiki/Simon_Blackburn" title="Simon Blackburn">Simon Blackburn</a>, structuralism is "the belief that phenomena of human life are not intelligible except through their interrelations. These relations constitute a structure, and behind local variations in the surface phenomena there are constant laws of abstract culture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackburn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blackburn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Structuralism in Europe developed in the early 1900s, in the <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">structural linguistics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure" title="Ferdinand de Saussure">Ferdinand de Saussure</a> the subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_Linguistic_Circle" title="Prague Linguistic Circle">Prague</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d170_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-d170-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Moscow_linguistic_circle" title="Moscow linguistic circle">Moscow</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d170_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-d170-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Copenhagen_school_(linguistics)" title="The Copenhagen school (linguistics)">Copenhagen</a> schools of linguistics. In the late 1950s and early 1960s, when structural linguistics was facing serious challenges from the likes of <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> and thus fading in importance, an array of scholars in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> borrowed Saussure's concepts for use in their respective fields of study. French anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a> was arguably the first such scholar, sparking a widespread interest in structuralism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackburn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blackburn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The structuralist mode of reasoning has been applied in a diverse range of fields, including <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary criticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structuralist_economics" title="Structuralist economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Structuralism_(architecture)" title="Structuralism (architecture)">architecture</a>. The most prominent thinkers associated with structuralism include Lévi-Strauss, linguist <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalyst</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a>. As an intellectual movement, structuralism was initially presumed to be the heir apparent to <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a>. However, by the late 1960s, many of structuralism's basic tenets came under attack from a new wave of predominantly French intellectuals such as the philosopher and historian <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a>, the philosopher and social commentator <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Louis_Althusser" title="Louis Althusser">Louis Althusser</a>, and the literary critic <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">Roland Barthes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d170_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-d170-2">[2]</a></sup> Though elements of their work necessarily relate to structuralism and are informed by it, these theorists have generally been referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-structuralists" title="Post-structuralists">post-structuralists</a>. In the 1970s, structuralism was criticized for its rigidity and <a href="/wiki/Ahistoricism" title="Ahistoricism">ahistoricism</a>. Despite this, many of structuralism's proponents, such as Lacan, continue to assert an influence on <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">continental philosophy</a> and many of the fundamental assumptions of some of structuralism's post-structuralist critics are a continuation of structuralism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sturrock_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sturrock-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "structuralism" is a related term that describes a particular philosophical/literary movement or moment. The term appeared in the works of <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">anthropologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a> and gave rise in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> to the "structuralist movement," which influenced the thinking of other writers such as <a href="/wiki/Louis_Althusser" title="Louis Althusser">Louis Althusser</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalyst</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Structural_Marxism" title="Structural Marxism">structural Marxism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nicos_Poulantzas" title="Nicos Poulantzas">Nicos Poulantzas</a>, most of whom disavowed themselves as being a part of this movement.</p>
<p>The origins of structuralism connect with the work of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure" title="Ferdinand de Saussure">Ferdinand de Saussure</a> on <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, along with the linguistics of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_Linguistic_Circle" title="Prague Linguistic Circle">Prague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moscow_linguistic_circle" title="Moscow linguistic circle">Moscow</a> schools. In brief, de Saussure's structural linguistics propounded three related concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackburn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blackburn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>De Saussure argued for a distinction between <i>langue</i> (an idealized abstraction of language) and <i>parole</i> (language as actually used in daily life). He argued that the "sign" was composed of both a <i>signified</i>, an abstract concept or idea, and a "signifier", the perceived sound/visual image.</li>
<li>Because different languages have different words to describe the same objects or concepts, there is no intrinsic reason why a specific sign is used to express a given signifier. It is thus "arbitrary".</li>
<li>Signs thus gain their meaning from their relationships and contrasts with other signs. As he wrote, "in language, there are only differences 'without positive terms.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Proponents of structuralism would argue that a specific domain of culture may be understood by means of a structure—modelled on language—that is distinct both from the organizations of reality and those of ideas or the imagination—the "third order".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In Lacan's <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalytic</a> theory, for example, the structural order of "<a href="/wiki/The_Symbolic" title="The Symbolic">the Symbolic</a>" is distinguished both from "<a href="/wiki/The_Real" title="The Real">the Real</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Imaginary_(psychoanalysis)" title="The Imaginary (psychoanalysis)">the Imaginary</a>"; similarly, in Althusser's <a href="/wiki/Structural_Marxism" title="Structural Marxism">Marxist</a> theory, the structural order of the <a href="/wiki/Capitalist_mode_of_production_(Marxist_theory)" title="Capitalist mode of production (Marxist theory)">capitalist mode of production</a> is distinct both from the actual, real agents involved in its relations and from the <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideological</a> forms in which those relations are understood.</p>
<p>Blending Freud and de Saussure, the French (post)structuralist <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a> applied structuralism to <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a> and, in a different way, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Piaget" title="Jean Piaget">Jean Piaget</a> applied structuralism to the study of <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>. But Jean Piaget, who would better define himself as <a href="/wiki/Constructivist_epistemology" title="Constructivist epistemology">constructivist</a>, considers structuralism as "a method and not a doctrine" because for him "there exists no structure without a construction, abstract or genetic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the French theorist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Althusser" title="Louis Althusser">Louis Althusser</a> is often associated with a brand of structural <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">social analysis</a> which helped give rise to "<a href="/wiki/Structural_Marxism" title="Structural Marxism">structural Marxism</a>", such association was contested by Althusser himself in the Italian foreword to the second edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Reading_Capital" title="Reading Capital">Reading Capital</a></i>. In this foreword Althusser states the following:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Despite the precautions we took to distinguish ourselves from the 'structuralist' ideology ..., despite the decisive intervention of categories foreign to 'structuralism' ..., the terminology we employed was too close in many respects to the 'structuralist' terminology not to give rise to an ambiguity. With a very few exceptions ... our interpretation of Marx has generally been recognized and judged, in homage to the current fashion, as 'structuralist'... We believe that despite the terminological ambiguity, the profound tendency of our texts was not attached to the 'structuralist' ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a later development, <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theory" title="Feminist theory">feminist theorist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alison_Assiter" title="Alison Assiter">Alison Assiter</a> enumerated four ideas that she says are common to the various forms of structuralism. First, that a structure determines the position of each element of a whole. Second, that every system has a structure. Third, structural laws deal with co-existence rather than change. Fourth, structures are the "real things" that lie beneath the surface or the appearance of meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_linguistics">In linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">Structural linguistics</a></div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Course_in_General_Linguistics" title="Course in General Linguistics">Course in General Linguistics</a></i> the analysis focuses not on the <i>use</i> of language (called "<i><a href="/wiki/Langue_and_parole" title="Langue and parole">parole</a></i>", or speech), but rather on the underlying <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> of language (called "<i><a href="/wiki/Langue_and_parole" title="Langue and parole">langue</a></i>"). This approach examines how the elements of language relate to each other in the present, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronic_analysis" title="Synchronic analysis">synchronically</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">diachronically</a>. Saussure argued that <a href="/wiki/Sign_(linguistics)" title="Sign (linguistics)">linguistic signs</a> were composed of two parts:</p>
<ol>
<li>a "signifier" (the "sound pattern" of a word, either in mental projection—as when one silently recites lines from signage, a poem to one's self—or in actual, any kind of text, physical realization as part of a <a href="/wiki/Speech_act" title="Speech act">speech act</a>)</li>
<li>a "signified" (the concept or meaning of the word)</li>
</ol>
<p>This was quite different from previous approaches that focused on the relationship between words and the things in the world that they designate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other key notions in structural linguistics include <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a>, syntagm, and value (though these notions were not fully developed in Saussure's thought). A structural "idealism" is a class of linguistic units (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexemes" title="Lexemes">lexemes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphemes" title="Morphemes">morphemes</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">constructions</a>) that are possible in a certain position in a given linguistic environment (such as a given sentence), which is called the "syntagm". The different functional role of each of these members of the paradigm is called "value" (<i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">valeur</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>).</p>
<p>Saussure's <i>Course</i> influenced many linguists between <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In the United States, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bloomfield" title="Leonard Bloomfield">Leonard Bloomfield</a> developed his own version of structural linguistics, as did <a href="/wiki/Louis_Hjelmslev" title="Louis Hjelmslev">Louis Hjelmslev</a> in Denmark and <a href="/wiki/Alf_Sommerfelt" title="Alf Sommerfelt">Alf Sommerfelt</a> in Norway. In France <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Meillet" title="Antoine Meillet">Antoine Meillet</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Benveniste" title="Émile Benveniste">Émile Benveniste</a> continued Saussure's project, and members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_linguistic_circle" title="Prague linguistic circle">Prague school</a> of linguistics such as <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Trubetzkoy" title="Nikolai Trubetzkoy">Nikolai Trubetzkoy</a> conducted research that would be greatly influential. However, by the 1950s Saussure's linguistic concepts were under heavy criticism and were soon largely abandoned by practicing linguists:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Saussure's views are not held, so far as I know, by modern linguists, only by literary critics and the occasional philosopher. [Strict adherence to Saussure] has elicited wrong film and literary theory on a grand scale. One can find dozens of books of literary theory bogged down in signifiers and signifieds, but only a handful that refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chomsky" title="Chomsky">Chomsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The clearest and most important example of Prague school structuralism lies in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemics" title="Phonemics">phonemics</a>. Rather than simply compiling a list of which sounds occur in a language, the Prague school sought to examine how they were related. They determined that the inventory of sounds in a language could be analysed in terms of a series of contrasts. Thus in English the sounds /p/ and /b/ represent distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemes" title="Phonemes">phonemes</a> because there are cases (<a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">minimal pairs</a>) where the contrast between the two is the only difference between two distinct words (e.g. 'pat' and 'bat'). Analyzing sounds in terms of <a href="/wiki/Contrastive_distribution" title="Contrastive distribution">contrastive features</a> also opens up comparative scope—it makes clear, for instance, that the difficulty <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> speakers have differentiating /r/ and /l/ in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> is because these sounds are not contrastive in Japanese. <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">Phonology</a> would become the paradigmatic basis for structuralism in a number of different fields.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_anthropology">In anthropology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In anthropology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Structural_anthropology" title="Structural anthropology">Structural anthropology</a></div>
<p>According to structural theory in anthropology and social anthropology, <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(semiotics)" title="Meaning (semiotics)">meaning</a> is produced and reproduced within a culture through various practices, phenomena and activities that serve as systems of signification. A structuralist approach may study activities as diverse as food-preparation and serving rituals, religious rites, games, literary and non-literary texts, and other forms of entertainment to discover the deep structures by which meaning is produced and reproduced within the culture. For example, <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Lévi-Strauss</a> analysed in the 1950s cultural phenomena including mythology, kinship (the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_theory" title="Alliance theory">alliance theory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Incest_taboo" title="Incest taboo">incest taboo</a>), and food preparation. In addition to these studies, he produced more linguistically focused writings in which he applied Saussure's distinction between <a href="/wiki/Langue_and_parole" title="Langue and parole"><i>langue</i> and <i>parole</i></a> in his search for the fundamental structures of the human mind, arguing that the structures that form the "deep grammar" of society originate in the mind and operate in people unconsciously. Lévi-Strauss took inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another concept used in structural anthropology came from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_Linguistic_Circle" title="Prague Linguistic Circle">Prague school of linguistics</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a> and others analysed sounds based on the presence or absence of certain features (such as voiceless vs. voiced). Lévi-Strauss included this in his conceptualization of the universal structures of the mind, which he held to operate based on pairs of <a href="/wiki/Binary_opposition" title="Binary opposition">binary oppositions</a> such as hot-cold, male-female, culture-nature, cooked-raw, or marriageable vs. tabooed women.</p>
<p>A third influence came from <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mauss" title="Marcel Mauss">Marcel Mauss</a> (1872–1950), who had written on gift-exchange systems. Based on Mauss, for instance, Lévi-Strauss argued that kinship systems are based on the exchange of women between groups (a position known as 'alliance theory') as opposed to the 'descent'-based theory described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Evans-Pritchard" title="Edward Evans-Pritchard">Edward Evans-Pritchard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meyer_Fortes" title="Meyer Fortes">Meyer Fortes</a>. While replacing Marcel Mauss at his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecole_Pratique_des_Hautes_Etudes" title="Ecole Pratique des Hautes Etudes">Ecole Pratique des Hautes Etudes</a></i> chair, Lévi-Strauss' writing became widely popular in the 1960s and 1970s and gave rise to the term "structuralism" itself.</p>
<p>In Britain, authors such as <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Needham" title="Rodney Needham">Rodney Needham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Leach" title="Edmund Leach">Edmund Leach</a> were highly influenced by structuralism. Authors such as <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Godelier" title="Maurice Godelier">Maurice Godelier</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Emmanuel_Terray&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Emmanuel Terray (page does not exist)">Emmanuel Terray</a> combined <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> with structural anthropology in France. In the United States, authors such as <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Sahlins" title="Marshall Sahlins">Marshall Sahlins</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Boon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Boon (page does not exist)">James Boon</a> built on structuralism to provide their own analysis of human society. Structural anthropology fell out of favour in the early 1980s for a number of reasons. D'Andrade suggests that this was because it made unverifiable assumptions about the universal structures of the human mind. Authors such as <a href="/wiki/Eric_Wolf" title="Eric Wolf">Eric Wolf</a> argued that <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> should be at the forefront of anthropology. More generally, criticisms of structuralism by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bourdieu" title="Pierre Bourdieu">Pierre Bourdieu</a> led to a concern with how cultural and social structures were changed by human agency and practice, a trend which <a href="/wiki/Sherry_Ortner" title="Sherry Ortner">Sherry Ortner</a> has referred to as '<a href="/wiki/Practice_theory" title="Practice theory">practice theory</a>'.</p>
<p>Some anthropological theorists, however, while finding considerable fault with Lévi-Strauss's version of structuralism, did not turn away from a fundamental structural basis for human culture. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogenetic_Structuralism" title="Biogenetic Structuralism">Biogenetic Structuralism</a> group for instance argued that some kind of structural foundation for culture must exist because all humans inherit the same system of brain structures. They proposed a kind of <a href="/wiki/Neuroanthropology" title="Neuroanthropology">neuroanthropology</a> which would lay the foundations for a more complete scientific account of cultural similarity and variation by requiring an integration of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">cultural anthropology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>—a program that theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Victor_Turner" title="Victor Turner">Victor Turner</a> also embraced.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literary_theory_and_criticism">In literary theory and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In literary theory and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Semiotic_literary_criticism" title="Semiotic literary criticism">Semiotic literary criticism</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Literary_theory" title="Literary theory">literary theory</a>, structuralist criticism relates literary texts to a larger structure, which may be a particular <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genre</a>, a range of <a href="/wiki/Intertextuality" title="Intertextuality">intertextual</a> connections, a model of a universal <a href="/wiki/Narrative_structure" title="Narrative structure">narrative structure</a>, or a system of recurrent patterns or motifs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Structuralism argues that there must be a structure in every text, which explains why it is easier for experienced readers than for non-experienced readers to interpret a text. Hence, everything that is written seems to be governed by specific rules, or a "grammar of literature", that one learns in educational institutions and that are to be unmasked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A potential problem of structuralist interpretation is that it can be highly reductive, as scholar <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Belsey" title="Catherine Belsey">Catherine Belsey</a> puts it: "the structuralist danger of collapsing all difference."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> An example of such a reading might be if a student concludes the authors of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Side_Story_(musical)" title="West Side Story (musical)">West Side Story</a></i> did not write anything "really" new, because their work has the same structure as Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i>. In both texts a girl and a boy fall in love (a "formula" with a symbolic operator between them would be "Boy <b>+</b> Girl") despite the fact that they belong to two groups that hate each other ("Boy's Group <b>-</b> Girl's Group" or "Opposing forces") and conflict is resolved by their death. Structuralist readings focus on how the structures of the single text resolve inherent narrative tensions. If a structuralist reading focuses on multiple texts, there must be some way in which those texts unify themselves into a coherent system. The versatility of structuralism is such that a literary critic could make the same claim about a story of two <i>friendly</i> families ("Boy's Family <b>+</b> Girl's Family") that arrange a marriage between their children despite the fact that the children hate each other ("Boy <b>-</b> Girl") and then the children commit suicide to escape the arranged marriage; the justification is that the second story's structure is an 'inversion' of the first story's structure: the relationship between the values of love and the two pairs of parties involved have been reversed.</p>
<p>Structuralistic literary criticism argues that the "literary banter of a text" can lie only in new structure, rather than in the specifics of character development and voice in which that structure is expressed. Literary structuralism often follows the lead of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Propp" title="Vladimir Propp">Vladimir Propp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algirdas_Julien_Greimas" title="Algirdas Julien Greimas">Algirdas Julien Greimas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a> in seeking out basic deep elements in stories, <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myths</a>, and more recently, anecdotes, which are combined in various ways to produce the many versions of the ur-story or ur-myth.</p>
<p>There is considerable similarity between structural literary theory and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Frye" title="Northrop Frye">Northrop Frye</a>'s archetypal criticism, which is also indebted to the anthropological study of myths. Some critics have also tried to apply the theory to individual works, but the effort to find unique structures in individual literary works runs counter to the structuralist program and has an affinity with <a href="/wiki/New_Criticism" title="New Criticism">New Criticism</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_background">History and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout the 1940s and 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a>, such as that propounded by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>, was the dominant European intellectual movement. Structuralism rose to prominence in France in the wake of existentialism, particularly in the 1960s. The initial popularity of structuralism in France led to its spread across the globe.</p>
<p>Structuralism rejected the concept of human freedom and choice and focused instead on the way that human experience and thus, behaviour, is determined by various structures. The most important initial work on this score was <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>'s 1949 volume <i>The Elementary Structures of Kinship</i>. Lévi-Strauss had known Jakobson during their time together at the <a href="/wiki/The_New_School" title="The New School">New School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WWII</a> and was influenced by both Jakobson's structuralism as well as the American <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropological</a> tradition. In <i>Elementary Structures</i> he examined <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> systems from a structural point of view and demonstrated how apparently different social organizations were in fact different permutations of a few basic kinship structures. In the late 1950s he published <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_Anthropology" title="Structural Anthropology">Structural Anthropology</a></i>, a collection of essays outlining his program for structuralism.</p>
<p>By the early 1960s structuralism as a movement was coming into its own and some believed that it offered a single unified approach to human life that would embrace all disciplines. <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">Roland Barthes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a> focused on how structuralism could be applied to <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2011)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Structuralism#Dubious" title="Talk:Structuralism">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The so-called "Gang of Four" of structuralism was Lévi-Strauss, Lacan, Barthes, and Foucault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_and_general_criticisms">Interpretations and general criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Structuralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interpretations and general criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Structuralism is less popular today than other approaches, such as <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">deconstruction</a>. Structuralism has often been criticized for being ahistorical and for favouring deterministic structural forces over the ability of people to act. As the political turbulence of the 1960s and 1970s (and particularly the student uprisings of May 1968) began affecting academia, issues of power and political struggle moved to the center of people's attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">deconstruction</a>—and its emphasis on the fundamental ambiguity of language rather than its crystalline logical structure—became popular. By the end of the century structuralism was seen as an historically important school of thought, but the movements that it spawned, rather than structuralism itself, commanded attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several social thinkers and academics have strongly criticized structuralism or even dismissed it <i>in toto</i>. The French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermeneutic" title="Hermeneutic">hermeneutic</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ric%C5%93ur" title="Paul Ricœur">Paul Ricœur</a> (1969) criticized Lévi-Strauss for constantly overstepping the limits of validity of the structuralist approach, ending up in what Ricœur described as "a <a href="/wiki/Kantianism" title="Kantianism">Kantianism</a> without a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcendental_subject" title="Transcendental subject">transcendental subject</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Adam_Kuper" title="Adam Kuper">Adam Kuper</a> (1973) argued that "'Structuralism' came to have something of the momentum of a millennial movement and some of its adherents felt that they formed a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> of the seeing in a world of the blind. Conversion was not just a matter of accepting a new paradigm. It was, almost, a question of salvation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_Noel_Pettit" title="Philip Noel Pettit">Philip Noel Pettit</a> (1975) called for an abandoning of "the <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">positivist</a> dream which Lévi-Strauss dreamed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiology" title="Semiology">semiology</a>" arguing that semiology is not to be placed among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Castoriadis" title="Cornelius Castoriadis">Cornelius Castoriadis</a> (1975) criticized structuralism as failing to explain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolic_system" title="Symbolic system">symbolic mediation</a> in the social world;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> he viewed structuralism as a variation on the "<a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">logicist</a>" theme, and he argued that, contrary to what structuralists advocate, language—and symbolic systems in general—cannot be reduced to logical organizations on the basis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-valued_logic" title="Two-valued logic">binary logic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Square_of_opposition" title="Square of opposition">oppositions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_theorist" title="Critical theorist">Critical theorist</a> <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jürgen Habermas">Jürgen Habermas</a> (1985) accused structuralists, such as Foucault, of being positivists; he remarked that while Foucault is not an ordinary positivist, he nevertheless paradoxically uses the tools of science to criticize science<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> (see <i><a href="/wiki/Performative_contradiction#Usage_in_philosophy" title="Performative contradiction">Performative contradiction</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Foucault%E2%80%93Habermas_debate" title="Foucault–Habermas debate">Foucault–Habermas debate</a></i>). Sociologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthony_Giddens,_Baron_Giddens" title="Anthony Giddens, Baron Giddens">Anthony Giddens</a> (1993) is another notable critic; while Giddens draws on a range of structuralist themes in his theorizing, he dismisses the structuralist view that the reproduction of <a href="/wiki/Social_system" title="Social system">social systems</a> is merely "a mechanical outcome".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
