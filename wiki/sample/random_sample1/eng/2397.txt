<p><b>Coccidia</b> (<b>Coccidiasina</b>) are a <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">subclass</a> of microscopic, <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spore</a>-forming, single-celled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obligate_intracellular_parasite" title="Obligate intracellular parasite">obligate intracellular parasites</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Apicomplexa" title="Apicomplexa">apicomplexan</a> class <a href="/wiki/Conoidasida" title="Conoidasida">Conoidasida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UTS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-UTS-1">[1]</a></sup> As obligate intracellular parasites, they must live and reproduce within an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> cell. Coccidian parasites infect the intestinal tracts of animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iziko_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-iziko-2">[2]</a></sup> and are the largest group of <a href="/wiki/Apicomplexa" title="Apicomplexa">apicomplexan</a> protozoa.</p>
<p>Infection with these parasites is known as <a href="/wiki/Coccidiosis" title="Coccidiosis">coccidiosis</a>. Coccidia can infect all mammals, some birds, some fish, some reptiles, and some amphibians. Most species of coccidia are species-specific in their choice of host. An exception is <i><a href="/wiki/Toxoplasma_gondii" title="Toxoplasma gondii">Toxoplasma gondii</a></i>, which can infect all mammals, although it can only undergo sexual reproduction in cats. Depending on the species of coccidia, infection can cause fever, vomiting, diarrhea, muscle pain, and nervous system effects and changes to behavior, and may lead to death. Healthy adults may recover without medication, but those which are immunocompromised or young will almost certainly require medication to prevent death. Humans generally become infected by eating undercooked meat, but can contract infection with <i>T. gondii</i> by poor hygiene when handling cat waste.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The class is divided into four orders which are distinguished by the presence or absence of various asexual and sexual stages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Order <a href="/wiki/Agamococcidiorida" title="Agamococcidiorida">Agamococcidiorida</a> Levine-1979</li>
<li>Order <a href="/wiki/Eucoccidiorida" title="Eucoccidiorida">Eucoccidiorida</a> Léger &amp; Duboscq-1910</li>
<li>Order <a href="/wiki/Ixorheorida" title="Ixorheorida">Ixorheorida</a> Levine-1984</li>
<li>Order <a href="/wiki/Protococcidiorida" title="Protococcidiorida">Protococcidiorida</a> Kheisin-1956</li>
</ul>
<p>The order Eucoccidiorida is divided into two suborders. These two groups differ in their sexual development: <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">syzygy</a> for <a href="/wiki/Adeleorina" title="Adeleorina">Adeleorina</a> and independent gametes for <a href="/wiki/Eimeriorina" title="Eimeriorina">Eimeriorina</a>.</p>
<p>The ﬁrst suborder, Adeleorina, comprises coccidia of invertebrates and the coccidia that alternate between blood-sucking invertebrates and various vertebrates; this group includes <i><a href="/wiki/Haemogregarina" title="Haemogregarina">Haemogregarina</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hepatozoon" title="Hepatozoon">Hepatozoon</a></i>. There are seven families in this suborder.</p>
<p>The second suborder, Eimeriorina, comprises coccidia of a variety of coccidia many of form cysts. A number of genera, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxoplasma" title="Toxoplasma">Toxoplasma</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sarcocystis" title="Sarcocystis">Sarcocystis</a></i>, infect vertebrates.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coccidiosis">Coccidiosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coccidiosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission">Transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Infected animals spread spores called <a href="/wiki/Apicomplexan_life_cycle" title="Apicomplexan life cycle">oocysts</a> in their stool. The oocysts mature, called sporulation. When another animal passes over the location where the feces were deposited, they may pick up the spores, which they then ingest when grooming themselves. The spores may also be ingested by mice; when another animal eats the mouse it becomes infected.</p>
<p>Some species of coccidia are transmissible to humans, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxoplasma" title="Toxoplasma">toxoplasma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium" title="Cryptosporidium">cryptosporidium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvista_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-marvista-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infection">Infection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Infection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inside the host, the sporulated oocyst opens, and eight sporozites are released. Each one finds a home in an intestinal cell and starts the process of reproduction. These offspring are called merozoites. When the cell is stuffed full of merozoites, it bursts open, and each merozoite finds its own intestinal cell to continue the cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvista_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-marvista-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms_of_infection">Symptoms of infection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Symptoms of infection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the infection continues, thousands and thousands of intestinal cells may become infected. As they break open, they produce a bloody, watery diarrhea. This can cause dehydration, and can lead to death in young or small pets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvista_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-marvista-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis_and_treatment">Diagnosis and treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diagnosis and treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coccidiosis can be diagnosed by finding oocysts in <a href="/wiki/Stool_test" title="Stool test">fecal smears</a>. In early stages of the disease, there may be very few oocysts being shed, and a negative test does not rule out the disease.</p>
<p>Coccidiosis is most commonly treated through the administration of <a href="/wiki/Coccidiostat" title="Coccidiostat">coccidiostats</a>, a group of medications that stop coccidia from reproducing. In dogs and cats, the most commonly administered coccidiostat is <a href="/wiki/Sulfonamide_(medicine)" title="Sulfonamide (medicine)">sulfa-based</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>. Once reproduction stops, the animal can usually recover on its own, a process that can take a few weeks, depending on the severity of the infection and the strength of the animal's immune system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marvista_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-marvista-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
