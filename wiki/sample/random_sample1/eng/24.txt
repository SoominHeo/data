<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anti-tobacco movement in Nazi Germany</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>

<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> was the first country which initiated a strong <b>anti-tobacco movement</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHCM252_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHCM252-2">[2]</a></sup> and led the first public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-smoking_movement" title="Anti-smoking movement">anti-smoking campaign</a> in <a href="/wiki/Modern_history" title="Modern history">modern history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STR15_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-STR15-3">[3]</a></sup> Anti-<a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> movements grew in many nations from the beginning of the 20th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMMUESHP_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMMUESHP-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but these had little success, except in Germany, where the campaign was supported by the government after the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nazi_Party_leaders_and_officials" title="List of Nazi Party leaders and officials">Nazis</a> came to power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMMUESHP_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-SMMUESHP-4">[4]</a></sup> It was the most powerful anti-<a href="/wiki/Smoking" title="Smoking">smoking</a> movement in the world during the 1930s and early 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> The National Socialist leadership condemned smoking<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMT375_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMT375-7">[7]</a></sup> and several of them openly criticized tobacco consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> Research on smoking and its effects on health thrived under Nazi rule<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADLNMPHP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADLNMPHP-8">[8]</a></sup> and was the most important of its type at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1373-74_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1373-74-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler's</a> personal distaste for tobacco<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC219_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC219-10">[10]</a></sup> and the Nazi <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Nazi_Germany#The_Nazi_feminine_ideal" title="Women in Nazi Germany">reproductive policies</a> were among the motivating factors behind their campaign against smoking, and this campaign was associated with both <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJGDS_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMJGDS-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nazi anti-tobacco campaign included <a href="/wiki/Smoking_ban" title="Smoking ban">banning smoking</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Germany" title="Trams in Germany">trams</a>, buses and city trains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> promoting <a href="/wiki/Health_education" title="Health education">health education</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SGHS328_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SGHS328-12">[12]</a></sup> limiting cigarette rations in the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>, organizing medical lectures for soldiers, and raising the <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking#Taxation" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco tax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> The National Socialists also imposed restrictions on <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_advertising" title="Tobacco advertising">tobacco advertising</a> and smoking in public spaces, and regulated restaurants and coffeehouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> The anti-tobacco movement did not have much effect in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Germany#Nazi_revolution_or_.22Seizure_of_Power.22" title="History of Germany">early years of the Nazi regime</a> and tobacco use increased between 1933 and 1939,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC228_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC228-13">[13]</a></sup> but smoking by military personnel declined from 1939 to 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup> Even by the end of the 20th century, the anti-smoking movement in postwar Germany had not attained the influence of the Nazi anti-smoking campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC228_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC228-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anti-tobacco sentiment existed in <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> in the early 20th century. Critics of smoking organized the first anti-tobacco group in the country named the <i>Deutscher Tabakgegnerverein zum Schutze der Nichtraucher</i> (German Tobacco Opponents' Association for the Protection of Non-smokers). Established in 1904, this organization existed for a brief period only. The next anti-tobacco organization, the <i>Bund Deutscher Tabakgegner</i> (Federation of German Tobacco Opponents), was established in 1910 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trautenau" title="Trautenau">Trautenau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. Other anti-smoking organizations were established in 1912 in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. In 1920, a <i>Bund Deutscher Tabakgegner in der Tschechoslowakei</i> (Federation of German Tobacco Opponents in Czechoslovakia) was formed in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> was separated from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. A <i>Bund Deutscher Tabakgegner in Deutschösterreich</i> (Federation of German Tobacco Opponents in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Austria" title="German Austria">German Austria</a>) was established in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> in 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>These groups published journals advocating nonsmoking. The first such <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a> journal was <i>Der Tabakgegner</i> (The Tobacco Opponent), published by the Bohemian organization between 1912 and 1932. <i>Deutscher Tabakgegner</i> (German Tobacco Opponent) was published in Dresden from 1919 to 1935, and was the second journal on this subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC177_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC177-16">[16]</a></sup> The anti-tobacco organizations were also against consumption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC178_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC178-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons">Reasons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reasons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hitler.27s_attitude_towards_smoking">Hitler's attitude towards smoking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hitler's attitude towards smoking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882,_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="799" data-file-width="505" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882%2C_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-S33882,_Adolf_Hitler_retouched.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitler" title="Hitler">Hitler</a> encouraged his close associates to quit smoking.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> was a heavy smoker in his early life—he used to smoke 25 to 40 cigarettes daily—but gave up the habit, concluding that it was "a waste of money".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC219_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC219-10">[10]</a></sup> In later years, Hitler viewed smoking as "decadent"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup> and "the wrath of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Red Man</a> against the <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">White Man</a>, vengeance for having been given <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">hard liquor</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC219_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWC219-10">[10]</a></sup> lamenting that "so many excellent men have been lost to tobacco poisoning".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC173_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC173-18">[18]</a></sup> He was unhappy because both <a href="/wiki/Eva_Braun" title="Eva Braun">Eva Braun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bormann" title="Martin Bormann">Martin Bormann</a> were smokers and was concerned over <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a>'s continued smoking in public places. He was angered when a statue portraying a cigar-smoking Göring was commissioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC219_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-NWC219-10">[10]</a></sup> Hitler is often considered to be the first national leader to advocate nonsmoking, however <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a>, king of Scotland and England, was openly <a href="/wiki/A_Counterblaste_to_Tobacco" title="A Counterblaste to Tobacco">against smoking</a> 330 years prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BDDENI119_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BDDENI119-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hitler disapproved of the military personnel's freedom to smoke, and during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> he said on 2 March 1942, "it was a mistake, traceable to the army leadership at the time, at the beginning of the war". He also said that it was "not correct to say that a soldier cannot live without smoking". He promised to end the use of tobacco in the military after the end of the war. Hitler personally encouraged close friends not to smoke. He even began to offer a gold watch to any of his inner circle who could <a href="/wiki/Smoking_cessation" title="Smoking cessation">quit</a>. However, Hitler's personal distaste for tobacco was only one of several catalysts behind the anti-smoking campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC219_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-NWC219-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_policies">Reproductive policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproductive policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nazi reproductive policies were a significant factor behind their anti-tobacco campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJGDS_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BMJGDS-11">[11]</a></sup> Women who smoked were considered to be vulnerable to premature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging" title="Aging">aging</a> and loss of <a href="/wiki/Physical_attractiveness" title="Physical attractiveness">physical attractiveness</a>; they were viewed as unsuitable to be wives and mothers in a German family. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_Huttig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Werner Huttig (page does not exist)">Werner Huttig</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/NSDAP_Office_of_Racial_Policy" title="NSDAP Office of Racial Policy">Rassenpolitisches Amt</a></i> (Office of Racial Politics) said that a <a href="/wiki/Smoking_and_pregnancy" title="Smoking and pregnancy">smoking mother</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Breast_milk" title="Breast milk">breast milk</a> contained <a href="/wiki/Nicotine" title="Nicotine">nicotine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC187_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC187-20">[20]</a></sup> a claim that modern research has proven correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Staemmler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Martin Staemmler (page does not exist)">Martin Staemmler</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Staemmler" title="de:Martin Staemmler">de</a>)</span>, a prominent physician during the Third Reich, opined that smoking by pregnant women resulted in a higher rate of <a href="/wiki/Stillbirth" title="Stillbirth">stillbirths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriages</a>. This opinion was also supported by well-known female <a href="/wiki/Racial_hygiene" title="Racial hygiene">racial hygienist</a> <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Bluhm" title="Agnes Bluhm">Agnes Bluhm</a>, whose book published in 1936 expressed the same view. The Nazi leadership was concerned over this because they wanted German women to be as <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany#Women.27s_rights" title="Nazi Germany">reproductive</a> as possible. An article published in a German <a href="/wiki/Gynaecology" title="Gynaecology">gynecology</a> journal in 1943 stated that women smoking three or more cigarettes per day were more likely to remain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Childless" title="Childless">childless</a> compared to nonsmoking women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC189_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC189-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research and studies on tobacco's effects on the population's health were more advanced in Germany than in any other nation by the time the Nazis came to power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer#Smoking" title="Lung cancer">link</a> between <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> and tobacco was first proven in Nazi Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC173_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC173-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OANFRIPH_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-OANFRIPH-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SUF155_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-SUF155-27">[27]</a></sup> contrary to the popular belief that <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> scientists first discovered it in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC173_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWC173-18">[18]</a></sup> The term "<a href="/wiki/Passive_smoking" title="Passive smoking">passive smoking</a>" ("<i>Passivrauchen</i>") was coined in Nazi Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STR15_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-STR15-3">[3]</a></sup> Research projects funded by the Nazis revealed many disastrous effects of smoking on health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup> Nazi Germany supported <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiological research</a> on the harmful effects of tobacco use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHCM252_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-PHCM252-2">[2]</a></sup> Hitler personally gave financial support to the <i>Wissenschaftliches Institut zur Erforschung der Tabakgefahren</i> (Institute for Tobacco Hazards Research) at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Astel" title="Karl Astel">Karl Astel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC207_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC207-29">[29]</a></sup> Established in 1941, it was the most significant anti-tobacco institute in Nazi Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC207_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC207-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Franz_H._M%C3%BCller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Franz H. Müller (page does not exist)">Franz H. Müller</a> in 1939 and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dietrich_Eberhard_Schairer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dietrich Eberhard Schairer (page does not exist)">Dietrich Eberhard Schairer</a> in 1943 first used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case-control" title="Case-control">case-control</a> epidemiological methods to study lung cancer among smokers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1939, Müller published a study report in a reputed cancer journal in Germany which claimed that prevalence of lung cancer was higher among smokers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHCM252_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-PHCM252-2">[2]</a></sup> Müller, described as the "forgotten father of experimental epidemiology",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC191_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC191-30">[30]</a></sup> was a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_Motor_Corps" title="National Socialist Motor Corps">National Socialist Motor Corps</a> (NSKK) and the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> (NSDAP). Müller's 1939 medical dissertation was the world's first controlled epidemiological study of the relationship between tobacco and lung cancer. Apart from mentioning the increasing incidence of lung cancer and many of the causes behind it such as dust, exhaust gas from cars, <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, X-ray and pollutants emitted from factories, Müller's paper pointed out that "the significance of tobacco smoke has been pushed more and more into the foreground".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC194_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC194-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Physicians in the Third Reich were aware that smoking was responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_disease" title="Cardiac disease">cardiac diseases</a>, which were considered to be the most serious diseases resulting from smoking. Use of nicotine was sometimes considered to be responsible for increasing reports of <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> in the country. In the later years of World War II, researchers considered nicotine a factor behind the coronary heart failures suffered by a significant number of military personnel in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a>. A pathologist of the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">Heer</a> examined thirty-two young soldiers who had died from myocardial infarction at the front, and documented in a 1944 report that all of them were "enthusiastic smokers". He cited the opinion of pathologist Franz Buchner that cigarettes are "a coronary poison of the first order".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC187_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC187-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures">Measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Nazis used several public relations tactics to convince the general population of Germany not to smoke. Well-known health magazines like the <i>Gesundes Volk</i> (Healthy People),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup> <i>Volksgesundheit</i> (People's Health) and <i>Gesundes Leben</i> (Healthy Life)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHNG_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SHNG-32">[32]</a></sup> published warnings about the health consequences of smoking<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHNG_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-SHNG-32">[32]</a></sup> and posters showing the harmful effects of tobacco were displayed. Anti-smoking messages were sent to the people in their workplaces,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup> often with the help of the <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler-Jugend</a> (HJ) and the <a href="/wiki/League_of_German_Girls" title="League of German Girls">Bund Deutscher Mädel</a> (BDM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJGDS_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-BMJGDS-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHNG_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-SHNG-32">[32]</a></sup> The anti-smoking campaign undertaken by the Nazis also included health education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SGHS328_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-SGHS328-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OANFRIPH_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-OANFRIPH-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MHSDPHB13_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-MHSDPHB13-33">[33]</a></sup> In June 1939, a Bureau against the Hazards of Alcohol and Tobacco was formed and the <i>Reichsstelle für Rauschgiftbekämpfung</i> (Reich Office for Drug Control) also helped in the anti-tobacco campaign. Articles advocating nonsmoking were published in the magazines <i>Die Genussgifte</i> (The Recreational Stimulants), <i>Auf der Wacht</i> (On the Guard) and <i>Reine Luft</i> (Clean Air).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC199_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC199-34">[34]</a></sup> Out of these magazines, <i>Reine Luft</i> was the main journal of the anti-tobacco movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSFTENQRP_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSFTENQRP-35">[35]</a></sup> Karl Astel's Institute for Tobacco Hazards Research at Jena University purchased and distributed hundreds of reprints from <i>Reine Luft</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSFTENQRP_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-SSFTENQRP-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>After recognizing the harmful effects of smoking on health, several items of anti-smoking legislation were enacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJSD_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMJSD-36">[36]</a></sup> The later 1930s increasingly saw anti-tobacco laws implemented by the Nazis. In 1938, the <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Reichspost" title="Reichspost">Reichspost</a> imposed a ban on smoking. Smoking was also banned not only in health care institutions, but also in several public offices and in rest homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> Midwives were restricted from smoking while on duty. In 1939, the Nazi Party outlawed smoking in all of its offices premises, and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a>, the then chief of the <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">Schutzstaffel</a> (SS), restricted police personnel and SS officers from smoking while they were on duty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC203_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC203-37">[37]</a></sup> Smoking was also outlawed in schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-5"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1941, tobacco smoking in trams was outlawed in sixty German cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC203_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC203-37">[37]</a></sup> Smoking was also outlawed in <a href="/wiki/Bomb_shelter" title="Bomb shelter">bomb shelters</a>; however, some shelters had separate rooms for smoking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> Special care was taken to prevent women from smoking. The President of the Medical Association in Germany announced, "German women don't smoke".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCMCCEA169_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCMCCEA169-38">[38]</a></sup> Pregnant women and women below the age of 25 and over the age of 55 were not given tobacco ration cards during World War II. Restrictions on selling tobacco products to women were imposed on the hospitality and food retailing industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC203_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWC203-37">[37]</a></sup> Anti-tobacco films aimed at women were publicly shown. Editorials discussing the issue of smoking and its effects were published in newspapers. Strict measures were taken in this regard and a district department of the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_Factory_Cell_Organization" title="National Socialist Factory Cell Organization">National Socialist Factory Cell Organization</a> (NSBO) announced that it would expel <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Nazi_Germany" title="Women in Nazi Germany">female members</a> who smoked publicly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFWTR108_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCFWTR108-39">[39]</a></sup> The next step in the anti-tobacco campaign came in July 1943, when public smoking for persons under the age of 18 was outlawed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJGDS_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-BMJGDS-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHNG_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-SHNG-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC203_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-NWC203-37">[37]</a></sup> In the next year, smoking in buses and city trains was made illegal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup> on the personal initiative of Hitler, who feared female ticket takers might be the victims of passive smoking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Restrictions were imposed on the advertisement of tobacco products,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB206_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-GB206-40">[40]</a></sup> enacted on 7 December 1941 and signed by Heinrich Hunke, the President of the Advertising Council. Advertisements trying to depict smoking as harmless or as an expression of masculinity were banned. Ridiculing anti-tobacco activists was also outlawed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC204_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC204-41">[41]</a></sup> as was the use of advertising posters along rail tracks, in rural regions, stadiums and racing tracks. Advertising by loudspeakers and <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamps_and_postal_history_of_Germany#Nazi_Germany.2C_1933-1945" title="Postage stamps and postal history of Germany">mail</a> was also prohibited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC206_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC206-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Restrictions on smoking were also introduced in the Wehrmacht. Cigarette rations in the military were limited to six per soldier per day. Extra cigarettes were often sold to the soldiers, especially when there was no military advance or retreat in the battleground, however these were restricted to 50 for each person per month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> Teenaged soldiers serving in the <a href="/wiki/12th_SS_Panzer_Division_Hitlerjugend" title="12th SS Panzer Division Hitlerjugend">12th SS Panzer Division Hitlerjugend</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler Youth</a> members, were given confectionery instead of tobacco products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Access to cigarettes was not allowed for the Wehrmacht's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%27s_roles_in_the_World_Wars#Germany" title="Women's roles in the World Wars">female auxiliary personnel</a>. Medical lectures were arranged to persuade military personnel to quit smoking. An ordinance enacted on 3 November 1941 raised tobacco taxes by approximately 80–95% of the retail price. It would be the highest rise in tobacco taxes in Germany until more than 25 years after the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany#Capitulation_of_German_forces" title="Nazi Germany">collapse of the Nazi regime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effectiveness">Effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The early anti-smoking campaign was considered a failure, and from 1933 to 1937 there was a rapid increase in tobacco consumption in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC228_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWC228-13">[13]</a></sup> The rate of smoking in the nation increased faster even than in neighboring <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, where the anti-tobacco movement was tiny and far less influential. Between 1932 and 1939, <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a> cigarette consumption in Germany increased from 570 to 900 per year, while the corresponding numbers for France were from 570 to 630.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LEE_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-LEE-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cigarette manufacturing companies in Germany made several attempts to weaken the anti-tobacco campaign. They published new journals and tried to depict the anti-tobacco movement as "fanatic" and "unscientific".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_industry" title="Tobacco industry">tobacco industry</a> also tried to counter the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany#Government" title="Nazi Germany">government</a> campaign to prevent women from smoking and used smoking models in their advertisements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCMCCEA169_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCMCCEA169-38">[38]</a></sup> Despite government regulations, many women in Germany regularly smoked, including the wives of many high-ranking Nazi officials. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Magda_Goebbels" title="Magda Goebbels">Magda Goebbels</a> smoked even while she was interviewed by a journalist. Fashion illustrations displaying women with cigarettes were often published in prominent publications such as the <i>Beyers Mode für Alle</i> (Beyers Fashion For All). The cover of the popular song <i><a href="/wiki/Lili_Marleen" title="Lili Marleen">Lili Marleen</a></i> featured singer <a href="/wiki/Lale_Andersen" title="Lale Andersen">Lale Andersen</a> holding a cigarette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFWTR108_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCFWTR108-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Nazis implemented more anti-tobacco policies at the end of the 1930s and by the early years of World War II, the rate of tobacco usage declined. As a result of the anti-tobacco measures implemented in the Wehrmacht,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup> the total tobacco consumption by soldiers decreased between 1939 and 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRCPL1374_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-HRCPL1374-14">[14]</a></sup> According to a survey conducted in 1944, the number of smokers increased in the Wehrmacht, but average tobacco consumption per military personnel declined by 23.4% compared to the immediate pre-World War II years. The number of people who smoked 30 or more cigarettes per day declined from 4.4% to 0.3%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJATCNLKAPHG_6-15"><a href="#cite_note-BMJATCNLKAPHG-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nazi anti-tobacco policies were not free of contradictions. For example, the <i>Volksgesundheit</i> (People's Health) and <i>Gesundheitspflicht</i> (Duty to be Healthy) policies were enforced in parallel with the active distribution of cigarettes to people who the Nazis saw as "deserving" groups (e.g. frontline soldiers, members of the Hitler Youth). On the other hand, "undeserving" and stigmatized groups (Jews, war prisoners) were denied access to tobacco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_with_antisemitism_and_racism">Association with antisemitism and racism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Association with antisemitism and racism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from public health concerns, the Nazis were heavily influenced by ideology;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-6"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup> specifically, the movement was influenced by concepts of <a href="/wiki/Racial_hygiene" title="Racial hygiene">racial hygiene</a> and bodily purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC174_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC174-46">[46]</a></sup> Nazi leaders believed that it was wrong for the <a href="/wiki/Master_race" title="Master race">master race</a> to smoke<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INJPR98_28-7"><a href="#cite_note-INJPR98-28">[28]</a></sup> and that tobacco consumption was equal to "racial degeneracy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC220_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC220-47">[47]</a></sup> The Nazis viewed tobacco as a "genetic poison".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC174_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC174-46">[46]</a></sup> Racial hygienists opposed tobacco use, fearing that it would "corrupt" the "German germ plasm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC179_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC179-48">[48]</a></sup> Nazi anti-tobacco activists often tried to depict tobacco as a vice of the degenerate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negroes" title="Negroes">Negroes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC174_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-NWC174-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Nazis claimed that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a> were responsible for introducing tobacco and its harmful effects. The <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a> in Germany announced that smoking was an unhealthy vice spread by the Jews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC179_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWC179-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johann_von_Leers" title="Johann von Leers">Johann von Leers</a>, editor of the <i>Nordische Welt</i> (Nordic World), during the opening ceremony of the <i>Wissenschaftliches Institut zur Erforschung der Tabakgefahren</i> in 1941, proclaimed that "Jewish capitalism" was responsible for the spread of tobacco use across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. He said that the first tobacco on German soil was brought by the Jews and that they controlled the tobacco industry in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, the principal European entry point of <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana" title="Nicotiana">Nicotiana</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC208_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC208-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_World_War_II">After World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-tobacco_movement_in_Nazi_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: After World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the collapse of Nazi Germany at the <a href="/wiki/End_of_World_War_II_in_Europe" title="End of World War II in Europe">end of World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Tobacco_companies_of_the_United_States" title="Category:Tobacco companies of the United States">American cigarette manufacturers</a> quickly entered the German black market. Illegal <a href="/wiki/Smuggling" title="Smuggling">smuggling</a> of tobacco became prevalent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC245_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWC245-50">[50]</a></sup> and the anti-smoking campaign started by the Nazis ceased to exist after the fall of the Third Reich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADLNMPHP_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADLNMPHP-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1949, approximately 400 million cigarettes manufactured in the United States entered Germany illegally every month. In 1954, nearly two billion <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> cigarettes were smuggled into Germany and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. As part of the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Plan" title="Marshall Plan">Marshall Plan</a>, the United States sent tobacco to Germany free of charge; the amount of tobacco shipped into Germany in 1948 was 24,000 tons and was as high as 69,000 tons in 1949. Per capita yearly cigarette consumption in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Germany_since_1945" title="History of Germany since 1945">post-war Germany</a> steadily rose from 460 in 1950 to 1,523 in 1963. At the end of the 20th century, the anti-tobacco campaign in Germany was unable to approach the level of the Nazi-era climax in the years 1939–41 and German tobacco health research was described by <a href="/wiki/Robert_N._Proctor" title="Robert N. Proctor">Robert N. Proctor</a> as "muted".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWC228_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-NWC228-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
