<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Diplomatic recognition</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"State recognition" redirects here. For state recognized Native American tribes, see <a href="/wiki/State_recognized_tribes_in_the_United_States" title="State recognized tribes in the United States">State recognized tribes in the United States</a>.</div>
<p><b>Diplomatic recognition</b> in <a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" title="Public international law">international law</a> is a unilateral political act with domestic and international legal consequences, whereby a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> acknowledges an act or status of another state or government in control of a state (may be also a recognized state). Recognition can be accorded either <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i>. Recognition can be a declaration to that effect by the recognizing government, or an act of recognition such as entering into a treaty with the other state. A vote by a country in the United Nations in favour of the membership of another country is an implicit recognition of that country by the country so voting, as only states may be members of the UN.</p>
<p>The non-recognition of particular acts of a state does not normally effect the recognition of the state itself. For example, the international rejection of the occupation of particular territory by a recognised state does not imply non-recognition of the state itself, nor a rejection of a change of government by illegal means.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_of_states_and_governments">Recognition of states and governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_recognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recognition of states and governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diplomatic recognition must be distinguished from formal recognition of states or their governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The fact that states do not maintain bilateral diplomatic relations does not mean that they do not recognize or treat one another as states. A state is not required to accord formal bilateral recognition to any other state, and some have a general policy of not doing so, considering that a vote for its membership of an international organisation restricted to states, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, is proof of recognition.</p>
<p>Some consider that a state has a responsibility not to recognize as a state any entity that has attained the qualifications for statehood by a violation of basic principles of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Charter" title="UN Charter">UN Charter</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Security_Council" title="UN Security Council">UN Security Council</a> has in several instances (Resolution 216 (1965) and Resolution 217 (1965), concerning <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a>; Resolution 541 (1983), concerning <a href="/wiki/Northern_Cyprus" title="Northern Cyprus">Northern Cyprus</a>; and Resolution 787 (1992), concerning the <a href="/wiki/Republika_Srpska" title="Republika Srpska">Republika Srpska</a>) issued Chapter VII resolutions (binding in international law) that denied their statehood and precluded recognition. In the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> decision, the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a> decided that "general international law contains no applicable prohibition of declarations of independence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Court carefully noted "that in all of those instances the Security Council was making a determination as regards the concrete situation existing at the time that those declarations of independence were made; the illegality attached to the declarations of independence thus stemmed not from the unilateral character of these declarations as such, but from the fact that they were, or would have been, connected with the unlawful use of force or other egregious violations of norms of general international law, in particular those of a peremptory character (jus cogens). In the context of Kosovo, the Security Council has never taken this position. The exceptional character of the resolutions enumerated above appears to the Court to confirm that no general prohibition against unilateral declarations of independence may be inferred from the practice of the Security Council."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>States can exercise their recognition powers either explicitly or implicitly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The recognition of a government implies recognition of the state it governs, but even countries which have a policy of formally recognising states may not have a policy of doing the same regarding governments.</p>
<p>De facto recognition of states, rather than de jure, is rare. De jure recognition is stronger, while de facto recognition is more tentative and recognizes only that a government exercises control over a territory. An example of the difference is when the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> recognized the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Soviet_Russia_and_the_Soviet_Union_(1917%E2%80%9327)" title="History of Soviet Russia and the Soviet Union (1917–27)">Soviet state</a> de facto in 1921, but de jure only in 1924. Another example is the state of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in 1948, whose government was immediately recognized de facto by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and three days later by <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> de jure recognition. Also, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a>, commonly known as "<a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>", is generally recognized as de facto independent and sovereign, but is not universally recognized as de jure independent due to the complex <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan" title="Political status of Taiwan">political status of Taiwan</a> related to the United Nation's de-recognization of it in favor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> in 1971.</p>
<p>Renewing recognition of a government is not necessary when it changes in a normal, constitutional way (such as an <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">election</a> or <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a>), but is necessary in the case of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup_d%27etat" title="Coup d'etat">coup d'etat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolution</a>. Recognition of a new government by other states can be important for its long-term survival. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> government of the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Afghanistan" title="Islamic State of Afghanistan">Islamic State of Afghanistan</a>, which lasted from 1996 to 2001, was recognized by only <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, while far more had recognized the government of ousted President <a href="/wiki/Burhanuddin_Rabbani" title="Burhanuddin Rabbani">Burhanuddin Rabbani</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disputed_territory" title="Disputed territory">disputed territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jammu_and_Kashmir" title="Jammu and Kashmir">Jammu and Kashmir</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_India" title="Republic of India">Republic of India</a> is not recognized by either <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.</p>
<p>Recognition can be implied by other acts, like the visit of the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>, or the signing of a bilateral treaty. If implicit recognition is possible, a state may feel the need to explicitly proclaim that its acts do not constitute diplomatic recognition, like when the United States commenced its dialogue with the <a href="/wiki/Palestine_Liberation_Organization" title="Palestine Liberation Organization">Palestine Liberation Organization</a> in 1988.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Withdrawal_of_recognition">Withdrawal of recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_recognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Withdrawal of recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A state may withdraw diplomatic recognition of another state, or simply refuse to deal with that other country, after withdrawing from all diplomatic relations with that country, such as embassies and consulates, and requiring the other country to do the same. The state will appoint a <a href="/wiki/Protecting_power" title="Protecting power">protecting power</a> to represent its interests in the other state.</p>
<p>The doctrine of non-recognition of illegal or immoral situations, like territorial gains achieved by <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">force</a>, is called the <a href="/wiki/Stimson_Doctrine" title="Stimson Doctrine">Stimson Doctrine</a>, and has become more important since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> where it is a method of ensuring compliance with <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> – for instance, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a> in 1965. Withdrawal of recognition of a government is a more severe act of disapproval than the breaking of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diplomatic_relations" title="Diplomatic relations">diplomatic relations</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_of_governments">Recognition of governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_recognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recognition of governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides recognizing other states, states also can recognize the governments of states. This can be problematic particularly when a new government comes to power by illegal means, such as a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a>, or when an existing government stays in power by fixing an election. States once formally recognized both the government of a state and the state itself, but many no longer follow that practice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> even though, if diplomatic relations are to be maintained, it is necessary that there be a government with which to engage in diplomatic relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Countries such as the United States answer queries over the recognition of governments with the statement: "The question of recognition does not arise: we are conducting our relations with the new government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrecognized_state">Unrecognized state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_recognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unrecognized state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">Unrecognized states</a></div>
<p>Several of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geo-political" title="Geo-political">geo-political</a> entities lack general international recognition, but wish to be recognized as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a>. The degree of <i>de facto</i> control these entities exert over the territories they claim varies.</p>
<p>Most are subnational <a href="/wiki/Subregion" title="Subregion">regions</a> with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic" title="Ethnic">ethnic</a> or <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">national identity</a> of their own that have <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separated</a> from the original parent <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>. Such states are commonly referred to as "break-away" states. Some of these entities are in effect internally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" title="Autonomous entity">self-governing</a> <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorates</a> that enjoy military protection and informal diplomatic representation abroad through another state to prevent forced reincorporation into their original states.</p>
<p>Note that the word "control" in this list refers to <i>control</i> over the area <i>occupied</i>, not <i>occupation</i> of the area <i>claimed</i>. Unrecognized countries may have either full control over their occupied territory, or only partial control (such as <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>). In the former, the <i>de jure</i> governments have little or no influence in the areas they claim to rule, whereas in the latter they have varying degrees of control, and may provide essential services to people living in the areas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_recognition">Other types of recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_recognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other types of recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other elements that may be recognized, include <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">occupation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Annexation" title="Annexation">annexation</a> of territory, or <a href="/wiki/Belligerent" title="Belligerent">belligerent</a> rights of a party in a conflict. Recognition of the latter does not imply recognition of a state. Examples of recognition of <a href="/wiki/Belligerent" title="Belligerent">belligerent</a> status include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> recognized the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> as a belligerent in the first month of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> recognized the <a href="/wiki/Sandinista_National_Liberation_Front" title="Sandinista National Liberation Front">Sandinista National Liberation Front</a> as a belligerent in Nicaragua's civil war in June 1979.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> recognized the <a href="/wiki/Farabundo_Mart%C3%AD_National_Liberation_Front" title="Farabundo Martí National Liberation Front">FMLN</a> as a belligerent in the civil war in <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> on August 28, 1981.</li>
</ul>
