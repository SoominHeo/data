<p>The <b>Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana</b> (English: <b>National Library of St Mark's</b>) is a library and Renaissance building in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, northern Italy; it is one of the earliest surviving public manuscript depositories in the country, holding one of the greatest classical texts collections in the world. The library is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Mark" title="St. Mark">St. Mark</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of Venice. It is not to be confused with the State Archive of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>, which is housed in a different part of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioteca_Marciana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The library was provided with a building designed by <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sansovino" title="Jacopo Sansovino">Jacopo Sansovino</a>. The first sixteen arcaded bays of his design were constructed during 1537 to 1553, with work on <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> and other decorations continuing until 1560. Sansovino died in 1570, but in 1588, <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Scamozzi" title="Vincenzo Scamozzi">Vincenzo Scamozzi</a> undertook the construction of the additional five bays, still to Sansovino's design, which brought the building down to the <i>molo</i> or embankment, next to Sansovino's building for the Venetian mint, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zecca,_Venice" title="Zecca, Venice">Zecca</a></i>. One of the early librarians, from 1530, was <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">Pietro Bembo</a>. However, the library stock began to be collected before the construction of the building. For example, the gem of the collections in the library was the gift to the <i>Serenissima</i> of the manuscript collection assembled by <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantine</a> <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist</a>, scholar, patron and collector, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Bessarion" title="Cardinal Bessarion">Cardinal Bessarion</a>; he made a gift of his collection on 31 May 1468: some 750 <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">codices</a> in Latin and Greek, to which he added another 250 manuscripts and some printed books (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incunabula" title="Incunabula">incunabula</a></i>), constituting the first "public" library open to scholars in Venice. (In 1362 <a href="/wiki/Petrarch%27s_library" title="Petrarch's library">Petrarch's library</a> was donated to Venice but this collection of manuscripts, ancient books, and personal letters was lost or dispersed.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> at later times, the <i>Biblioteca Marciana</i> profited from a law of 1603 that required that a copy be deposited in the <i>Marciana</i> of all books printed at Venice, the first such law.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <i>Marciana</i> was enriched by the transfer in the late eighteenth century of the collections accumulated in several monasteries, such as <i>SS. <a href="/wiki/Santi_Giovanni_e_Paolo,_Venice" title="Santi Giovanni e Paolo, Venice">Giovanni e Paolo</a></i> in Venice and S. Giovanni di Verdara in <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a>.</p>
<p>Major additions made to the collection include:</p>
<ul>
<li>1589: Melchiorre Guilandino of Marienburg (2.200 printed books);</li>
<li>1595: Jacopo Contarini da S. Samuele, delayed until the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Contarini" title="Contarini">Contarini</a> in the male line, in 1713 (175 mss and 1500 printed books);</li>
<li>1619: Girolamo Fabrici d'Acquapendente (13 volumes with hand-colored anatomical illustrations);</li>
<li>1624: Giacomo Gallicio (20 Greek mss);</li>
<li>1734: Gian Battista Recanati (216 mss, among them the codices of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">house of Gonzaga</a>) ;</li>
<li>1792: Tommaso Giuseppe Farsetti (350 mss and printed books);</li>
<li>1794: Amedeo Svajer (more than 340 mss among which is the last will of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>);</li>
<li>1797: Jacopo Nani (over 1000 mss, largely Greek and Eastern)</li>
<li>1798:</li>
</ul>
<p>With the fall of the Venetian Republic in 1797, the <i>Marciana</i> was enriched by the transfer of manuscripts and books from religious houses that were suppressed under the <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleonic</a> regime. In 1811 the library was moved to more spacious quarters in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doge%27s_Palace,_Venice" title="Doge's Palace, Venice">Doge's Palace</a>, where further collections entered:</p>
<dl>
<dd>1814: Girolamo Ascanio Molin (2209 fine printed books, 3835 prints and 408 drawings, housed in the <a href="/wiki/Museo_Correr" title="Museo Correr">Museo Correr</a> for the most part;</dd>
<dd>1843: Girolamo Contarini (906 mss and 4000 printed books);</dd>
<dd>1852: Giovanni Rossi (470 mss and a collection of Venetian <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>In 1904 the collection was moved to Sansovino's <i>Zecca</i> (built 1537-47 as a mint). The Library has since expanded back into its adjacent original quarters and even into sections of the <i><a href="/wiki/Procuratie" title="Procuratie">Procuratie</a> Nuove</i> facing <a href="/wiki/Piazza_San_Marco" title="Piazza San Marco">Piazza San Marco</a>.</p>
<p>Today, besides about a million printed books, the Biblioteca Marciana contains about 13,000 manuscripts and 2883 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incunabula" title="Incunabula">incunabula</a> and 24,055 works printed between 1500 and 1600. There are many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscripts" title="Illuminated manuscripts">illuminated manuscripts</a>. Among the irreplaceable treasures are unique scores of operas by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Cavalli" title="Francesco Cavalli">Francesco Cavalli</a> and sonatas by <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Scarlatti" title="Domenico Scarlatti">Domenico Scarlatti</a>.</p>
