<p><b>National Palace Museum of Korea</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_museum" title="National museum">national museum</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung_Palace" title="Gyeongbokgung Palace">Gyeongbokgung Palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Palace_Museum_of_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum first began as the "Korean Imperial Museum", which was established in September 1908 and was originally located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changgyeonggung_Palace" title="Changgyeonggung Palace">Changgyeonggung Palace</a>. On November of the following year, the museum was opened to the public. However, on April, 1938, the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">ruling Japanese government</a> renamed the museum to the "Museum of Yi dynasty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March, 1946, after the liberation of Korea, it was renamed "Deoksugung Museum". In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Heritage_Administration" title="Cultural Heritage Administration">Cultural Heritage Administration</a> instituted the museum in Seokjojeon (석조전, Stone Hall) of <a href="/wiki/Deoksugung" title="Deoksugung">Deoksugung Palace</a>, and in 2005, the museum was relocated to a modern building inside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung_Palace" title="Gyeongbokgung Palace">Gyeongbokgung Palace</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Palace_Museum_of_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>National Palace Museum of Korea houses over 40,000 artifacts and royal treasures, from the palaces of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> and the Korean Empire, of which 14 are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Treasures_of_South_Korea" title="National Treasures of South Korea">National Treasures of South Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It displays records, state rites, architecture, clothing, royal life, education, culture, paintings and music of the dynasty’s ruling era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It also has among its collection the royal seal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Gojong_of_Joseon" title="King Gojong of Joseon">King Gojong of Joseon</a>, which was used for his personal letters to Russian czar and Italian emperor after 1903. It disappeared during <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese rule</a> and was re-covered from a US-based Korean collector in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_collection">Special collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Palace_Museum_of_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Special collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Museum houses 1,200 volumes of historial texts including 150 copies of <a href="/wiki/Uigwe" title="Uigwe">Uigwe</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> that were looted in 1922, during <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">rule by Japan</a> under the supervision of then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resident-General_of_Korea" title="Resident-General of Korea">Resident-General of Korea</a> <a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Hirobumi" title="Itō Hirobumi">Itō Hirobumi</a>. They were repatriated in December 2011 and a special exhibition was held from 27 December 2011 to 5 February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The copies chronicled the royal rituals of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Gojong_of_Joseon" title="King Gojong of Joseon">King Gojong</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Sunjong_of_Joseon" title="King Sunjong of Joseon">King Sunjong</a>, the last two emperors of Joseon Dynasty and Daehan Empire before Korea was annexed in 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
