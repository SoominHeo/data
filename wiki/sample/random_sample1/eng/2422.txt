<p>The <b>South Korean illegal surveillance incident</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">민간인 불법사찰 사건</span>, or <i>illegal civilian surveillance incident</i>) was alleged to have occurred in 2010 when the Civil Service Ethics Division (공직윤리지원관실) under the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_South_Korea" title="List of Prime Ministers of South Korea">Prime Minister's Office</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> inspected a civilian, a political action that is illegal under the South Korean conventions. The incident re-emerged in early 2012 as the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_legislative_election,_2012" title="South Korean legislative election, 2012">election</a> approached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kim_Jong-ik">Kim Jong-ik</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kim Jong-ik">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The surveillance scandal originally developed as an investigation of a South Korean civilian named Kim Jong-ik (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">김종익</span>), a bank worker who posted in his blog a video clip <i>Jwiko</i> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">쥐코</span>) that lampooned President <a href="/wiki/Lee_Myung-bak" title="Lee Myung-bak">Lee Myung-bak</a> as a rat in a fashion from the movie, <a href="/wiki/Sicko" title="Sicko">Sicko</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This has generated multiple controversies such as concerns of <a href="/wiki/Internet_censorship_in_South_Korea" title="Internet censorship in South Korea">Internet censorship</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and political misconducts. In a March 2012 interview of Kim Jong-ik, he said has been "emotionally scarred" and was forced to resign his position at his company and move to Japan. He had lost his means to support his family and accumulated debt. Kim did not know why he himself was singled out as a target, as many others had posted the same video. He expressed deep concerns towards the government of this happening again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Prosecutors%27_Office_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Supreme Prosecutors' Office of the Republic of Korea">Supreme Prosecutors' Office of the Republic of Korea</a> (SPO) has decided to reopen the case based on testimonies of the bureau insider, Jang Jin-su (장진수).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A special team of prosecutors was formed to investigate the surveillance incident and a potential cover up.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Prosecutors%27_Office_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Supreme Prosecutors' Office of the Republic of Korea">Supreme Prosecutors' Office of the Republic of Korea</a> raided the Prime Minister's Office for the first time in its history on July 9, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The SPO eventually discovered an alleged concealment of evidences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Former Presidential Secretary for Employment and Labor Relations, Lee Young-ho (이영호), was banned from leaving the country for his involvement in incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former Presidential Secretary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Young-ho" title="Lee Young-ho">Lee Young-ho</a> and former Presidential Labor Advisor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Choi_Jong-seok&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Choi Jong-seok (page does not exist)">Choi Jong-seok</a> were arrested on April 3, 2012 for allegedly ordering Jang Jin-su to destroy the information related to the surveillance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On April 4, 2012, Jang Jin-su revealed pictures of 50 million won he claims he received from Rhyu Chung-ryeol, a former Prime Minister's Office official, as a bribe to destroy the data, through the independent South Korean news source, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OhMyNews" title="OhMyNews">OhMyNews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roh_Moo-hyun_and_Lee_Myung-bak_administrations">Roh Moo-hyun and Lee Myung-bak administrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roh Moo-hyun and Lee Myung-bak administrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the scandal was revisited during the <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_presidential_election,_2012" title="South Korean presidential election, 2012">2012 Korea elections</a>, debate has expanded over into conducts by both the current and previous regimes. Some have alleged that surveillance was done during the <a href="/wiki/Roh_Moo-hyun" title="Roh Moo-hyun">previous</a> regime, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Lee_Myung-bak_government" title="Lee Myung-bak government">current</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_reports">Media reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Media reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Munhwa_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation">Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation</a>, a notable media outlet, claimed to expose the incident in its journalist-oriented investigative program, <a href="/wiki/PD_Notebook" title="PD Notebook">PD Notebook</a>, on June 29, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The KBS-affiliated <a href="/wiki/Korean_Broadcasting_System#Strikes" title="Korean Broadcasting System">journalists</a> who are on strike claimed to have obtained 2,619 surveillance-related cases of reports from the Lee Myung-bak government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, it was later reported that over 80% of the alleged illegal surveillance records were actually created during the previous <a href="/wiki/Roh_Moo-hyun" title="Roh Moo-hyun">Roh Moo-hyun</a> regime. Of the 2,619 documents, only 120 were considered actual reports, and 2 reports were of civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2-18">[18]</a></sup> Two former executives of <a href="/wiki/National_Intelligence_Service_(South_Korea)" title="National Intelligence Service (South Korea)">National Intelligence Service</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://ko.wikipedia.org/wiki/%EA%B3%A0%EC%98%81%EA%B5%AC" title="ko:고영구">Koh Yeong-gu</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://ko.wikipedia.org/wiki/%EA%B9%80%EB%A7%8C%EB%B3%B5_(1946%EB%85%84)" title="ko:김만복 (1946년)">Kim Man-bok</a> denied the claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The KBS union immediately posted a tweet calling the explanation a lie, but soon changed stance and apologized for the inaccurate information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref2-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The KBS journalists who are on strike later refuted the claim. They claimed that the total of cases including the duplicated entities are 2,837; total of cases during the Roh administration are 2,356 and written by police forces who follow <i>normal procedures</i> in which only 10 cases are involved with civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Dailian criticised the KBS union for creating a large fabrications of facts used for political purposes, especially during election season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_opinion">Public opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Korean_illegal_surveillance_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main opposition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_United_Party" title="Democratic United Party">Democratic United Party</a> demanded a full reinvestigation of the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korea_Times" title="Korea Times">Korea Times</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> compared the scandal to the American <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a>, referring to it as the "Watergate of Korea". The conservative political activist and a current Saenuri Party political reformer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Sang-don_(1951)" title="Lee Sang-don (1951)">Lee Sang-don</a>, made a remark during a radio interview on 5 April 2012:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The illegal surveillance case is exactly the same as the Watergate scandal. [U.S. President Richard] <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Nixon</a> didn’t instruct the illegal spying but stepped down from presidency for covering up the case.</p>
</blockquote>
