<p>The <b>National Front</b> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>: <span lang="ms"><i>Barisan Nasional</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbrev</a>: <b>BN</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing">right-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Malaysia" title="List of political parties in Malaysia">political party in Malaysia</a> that was founded in 1973 as a coalition of right-wing and centre parties. It is the largest party in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Malaysia" title="Parliament of Malaysia">Parliament of Malaysia</a>. Party Chairman <a href="/wiki/Najib_Razak" title="Najib Razak">Najib Razak</a> has been <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Prime Minister of Malaysia">Prime Minister of Malaysia</a> from 3 April 2009. In state level, it is also the largest party in the 10 of 13 <a href="/wiki/State_legislative_assemblies_of_Malaysia" title="State legislative assemblies of Malaysia">state legislative assemblies of Malaysia</a>.</p>
<p>The Barisan Nasional employs the same inter-communal governing model of its predecessor the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_(Malaysia)" title="Alliance Party (Malaysia)">Alliance</a> coalition but on a wider scale, with up to 14 communal political parties involved in the coalition at one point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liow_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-liow-1">[1]</a></sup> It dominated Malaysian politics for over thirty years after it was founded, but since 2008 has faced stronger challenges from opposition parties, notably the <a href="/wiki/Pakatan_Rakyat" title="Pakatan Rakyat">Pakatan Rakyat</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Pakatan_Harapan" title="Pakatan Harapan">Pakatan Harapan</a> alliances. Along with its predecessor (Alliance), it is considered the longest continuing ruling party in the <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic world</a>. Although Barisan Nasional never achieved more than 67% of the popular vote in elections, it maintained consecutive two-thirds majority of seats in the <a href="/wiki/Dewan_Rakyat" title="Dewan Rakyat">Dewan Rakyat</a> from 1974 until 2008, benefitting from Malaysia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> <a href="/wiki/Voting_system" title="Voting system">voting system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barisan_Nasional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barisan_Nasional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Barisan Nasional is the direct successor to the three-party <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_(Malaysia)" title="Alliance Party (Malaysia)">Alliance</a> coalition formed of <a href="/wiki/United_Malays_National_Organisation" title="United Malays National Organisation">United Malays National Organisation</a> (UMNO), <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese_Association" title="Malaysian Chinese Association">Malaysian Chinese Association</a> (MCA), <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indian_Congress" title="Malaysian Indian Congress">Malaysian Indian Congress</a> (MIC). It was founded in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1969" title="Malaysian general election, 1969">1969 general election</a> and the <a href="/wiki/13_May_Incident" title="13 May Incident">13 May riots</a>. The Alliance Party lost ground in the 1969 election to the opposition parties, in particular the two newly formed parties <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Action_Party" title="Democratic Action Party">Democratic Action Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parti_Gerakan_Rakyat_Malaysia" title="Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia">Gerakan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pan-Malaysian_Islamic_Party" title="Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party">Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party</a> (PAS). Although the Alliance won a majority of seats, it gained less than half the popular vote, and the resulting tension between different communities led to riots and the declaration of a state of emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ency_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ency-5">[5]</a></sup> After the Malaysian Parliament reconvened in 1971, negotiations began with former opposition parties such as Gerakan and <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Progressive_Party_(Malaysia)" title="People's Progressive Party (Malaysia)">People's Progressive Party</a>, both of which joined the Alliance in 1972, quickly followed by PAS.</p>
<p>In 1973, the Alliance Party was replaced by Barisan Nasional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liow_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-liow-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Barisan Nasional, which included regional parties from <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a> (Sabah Alliance Party, <a href="/wiki/Sarawak_United_Peoples%27_Party" title="Sarawak United Peoples' Party">Sarawak United Peoples' Party</a> (SUPP), <a href="/wiki/Parti_Pesaka_Bumiputera_Bersatu" title="Parti Pesaka Bumiputera Bersatu">Parti Pesaka Bumiputera Bersatu</a> (PBB)), was formed as a grand coalition of 11 parties under the leadership of the prime minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tun_Abdul_Razak" title="Tun Abdul Razak">Tun Abdul Razak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It registered in June 1974 to contest the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1974" title="Malaysian general election, 1974">1974 general election</a>, which it won with considerable success.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977.E2.80.932007">1977–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barisan_Nasional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1977–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1977, PAS was expelled from the Barisan Nasional following a revolt within the <a href="/wiki/Kelantan_State_Legislative_Assembly" title="Kelantan State Legislative Assembly">Kelantan state legislature</a> against a chief minister appointed by the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liow_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-liow-1">[1]</a></sup> Barisan Nasional nevertheless won the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1978" title="Malaysian general election, 1978">1978 general election</a> convincingly, and it continued to dominate Malaysian politics in the 1980s and 1990s despite some losses in state elections, such as the loss of <a href="/wiki/Kelantan" title="Kelantan">Kelantan</a> to PAS, and <a href="/wiki/Sabah_State_Legislative_Assembly" title="Sabah State Legislative Assembly">Sabah</a> to <a href="/wiki/Parti_Bersatu_Sabah" title="Parti Bersatu Sabah">Parti Bersatu Sabah</a>.</p>
<p>By 2003, Barisan Nasional had grown to a coalition formed of more than a dozen communal parties. It performed particularly well in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2004" title="Malaysian general election, 2004">2004 general election</a>, winning 198 out of 219 seats.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.93present">2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barisan_Nasional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2008" title="Malaysian general election, 2008">2008 general elections</a>, Barisan Nasional lost more than one-third of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Malaysia" title="Parliament of Malaysia">parliamentary</a> seats to <a href="/wiki/Pakatan_Rakyat" title="Pakatan Rakyat">Pakatan Rakyat</a>, a loose alliance of opposition parties. This marked Barisan's first failure to secure a two-thirds <a href="/wiki/Supermajority" title="Supermajority">supermajority</a> in Parliament since 1969. Five state governments, <a href="/wiki/Kelantan" title="Kelantan">Kelantan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kedah" title="Kedah">Kedah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penang" title="Penang">Penang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perak" title="Perak">Perak</a> (which was later returned via court ruling following a <a href="/wiki/2009_Perak_constitutional_crisis" title="2009 Perak constitutional crisis">constitutional crisis</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Selangor" title="Selangor">Selangor</a>, fell to <a href="/wiki/Pakatan_Rakyat" title="Pakatan Rakyat">Pakatan Rakyat</a>. Since 2008, the coalition has seen its non-Malay component parties greatly diminished in the Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The losses continued in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2013" title="Malaysian general election, 2013">2013 general election</a>, and it recorded its worst ever election result. BN regained <a href="/wiki/Kedah" title="Kedah">Kedah</a>, but lost several more seats in Parliament along with the popular vote to Pakatan. It won only 47% of the popular vote, nevertheless it managed to gain 60% of the 222 parliamentary seats, thereby retaining control of the parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barisan_Nasional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2013, the vast majority of Barisan Nasional's seats are held by its two largest <a href="/wiki/Bumiputera_(Malaysia)" title="Bumiputera (Malaysia)">Bumiputera</a>-based political parties—the <a href="/wiki/United_Malays_National_Organisation" title="United Malays National Organisation">United Malays National Organisation</a> (UMNO), and <a href="/wiki/Parti_Pesaka_Bumiputera_Bersatu" title="Parti Pesaka Bumiputera Bersatu">Parti Pesaka Bumiputera Bersatu</a> (PBB). For most of its history, both the Malaysian Chinese Association and Malaysian Indian Congress have played major roles in Barisan Nasional, but their representation in Parliament and state legislatures has become much more diminished. Nevertheless, practically each component party purports to represent – and limit membership – to a certain race: UMNO for the Malays, MCA for the Chinese and so on. In the view of some scholars:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Since its inception the Alliance remained a coalition of communal parties. Each of the component parties operated to all intents and purposes, save that of elections, as a separate party. Their membership was communal, except perhaps Gerakan, and their success was measured in terms of their ability to achieve the essentially parochial demands of their constituents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Although both the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_(Malaysia)" title="Alliance Party (Malaysia)">Alliance</a> and BN registered themselves as political parties, membership is only possible indirectly through one of the constituent parties. In the Alliance, one could hold direct membership, but this was abolished with the formation of the Barisan Nasional. The BN defines itself as a "<a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederation</a> of political parties which subscribe to the objects of the Barisan Nasional". Although in elections, all candidates stand under the BN symbol, and there is a BN manifesto, each individual constituent party also issues its own manifesto, and there is intra-coalition competition for seats prior to nomination day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of January 2017, Barisan Nasional's member parties include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Malays_National_Organisation" title="United Malays National Organisation">United Malays National Organisation</a> (UMNO)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese_Association" title="Malaysian Chinese Association">Malaysian Chinese Association</a> (MCA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indian_Congress" title="Malaysian Indian Congress">Malaysian Indian Congress</a> (MIC)</li>
