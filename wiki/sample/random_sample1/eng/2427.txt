<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">National wealth</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>National (net) wealth</b>, also <b>net wealth</b> (in Singapore), <b>national net worth</b>, <b>gross national wealth</b> (GNW), and <b>total national wealth</b>, is the total sum value of monetary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assets" title="Assets">assets</a> minus <a href="/wiki/Liability_(financial_accounting)" title="Liability (financial accounting)">liabilities</a> of a given nation. It refers to the total value of <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> possessed by the citizens of a nation at a set point in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This figure is an important indicator of a nation's ability to take on debt and sustain spending, and is influenced by not only real estate prices, but also by the stock market, <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a>, technological advancements which may create new assets or render others worthless, national infrastructure and exchange rates. The most significant component by far among most developed nations is commonly reported as <i>household net wealth or worth</i> and reflects infrastructure investment. National wealth can fluctuate, as evidenced in the US data following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_financial_crisis" title="2008 financial crisis">2008 financial crisis</a> and subsequent economic recovery.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_by_country">Differences by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Differences by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In Taiwan, Gross national wealth and net national wealth are two different measures.</li>
<li>In the USA, the figure regularly reported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a> of the US is <i>household net worth</i>, and includes corporations as they are essentially owned by American households. The United States Federal Reserve previously published the figure <i>total national wealth</i> in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, this figure ignores the current balance of accumulated borrowing and assets of the federal and state governments.</li>
<li>Japan publishes two figures, gross national assets and net national assets. By gross national assets, Japan reports a figure of 7954 trillion yen ($103 trillion), but claims liabilities of 5242 trillion ($68 trillion) for a net 2712 trillion in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, these numbers are suspiciously large compared with GDP, only liabilities that could be worth that amount given Japan's ultra low reliance on foreign debt (in government and private sectors) is future promises of healthcare and pension for its aging citizens. Therefore, Japan compiles extra figures (which other nations do not compile) and makes their figures less meaningful to compare with other nations.</li>
</ul>
