<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Principle of locality</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, the <b>principle of locality</b> states that an object is only directly influenced by its immediate surroundings. A theory which includes the principle of locality is said to be a "local theory". This is an alternative to the older concept of instantaneous "<a href="/wiki/Action_at_a_distance" title="Action at a distance">action at a distance</a>". Locality evolved out of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_field_theory" title="Classical field theory">field theories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classical_physics" title="Classical physics">classical physics</a>. The concept is that for an action at one point to have an influence at another point, something in the space between those points (such as a <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">field</a>, wave, or particle) must carry (i.e. "mediate") the action. To exert an influence, something must travel through the space between the two points, carrying the influence.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Theory_of_Relativity" title="Special Theory of Relativity">Special Theory of Relativity</a> limits the speed at which such influences can travel to the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<p>In 1935 <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Podolsky" title="Boris Podolsky">Boris Podolsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rosen" title="Nathan Rosen">Nathan Rosen</a> (in their <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">EPR paradox</a>) theorised that <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> might not be a local theory, because a measurement made on one of a pair of separated but <i><a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a></i> particles causes a <i>simultaneous</i> effect, the collapse of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">wavefunction</a>, in the remote particle (i.e. an effect exceeding the speed of light). But because of the probabilistic nature of wavefunction collapse, this violation of locality cannot be used to transmit <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> faster than light. In 1964 <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_Bell" title="John Stewart Bell">John Stewart Bell</a> formulated the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_inequality" title="Bell inequality">Bell inequality</a>", which, if violated in actual experiments, implies that quantum mechanics violates either locality or <i>realism</i>, another principle which relates to the value of unmeasured quantities. The two principles are commonly referred to as a single principle, <b>local realism</b>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alain_Aspect" title="Alain Aspect">Experimental tests</a> of the Bell inequality, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Alain_Aspect" title="Alain Aspect">Alain Aspect</a>'s 1972 experiments, show that quantum mechanics seems to violate the inequality, so it must violate either locality or realism. However, critics have noted these experiments contained "<a href="/wiki/Loopholes_in_Bell_test_experiments" title="Loopholes in Bell test experiments">loopholes</a>", which prevented a definitive answer to this question. This might now be resolved: in 2015 Dr Ronald Hanson at Delft University performed what has been called the first loophole-free experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hanson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hanson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-quantum_mechanics">Pre-quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Action_at_a_distance" title="Action at a distance">Action at a distance</a></div>
<p>In the 17th Century <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">Newton's law of universal gravitation</a> was formulated in terms of "action at a distance", thereby violating the principle of locality.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is inconceivable that inanimate Matter should, without the Mediation of something else, which is not material, operate upon, and affect other matter without mutual Contact…That Gravity should be innate, inherent and essential to Matter, so that one body may act upon another at a distance thro' a Vacuum, without the Mediation of any thing else, by and through which their Action and Force may be conveyed from one to another, is to me so great an Absurdity that I believe no Man who has in philosophical Matters a competent Faculty of thinking can ever fall into it. Gravity must be caused by an Agent acting constantly according to certain laws; but whether this Agent be material or immaterial, I have left to the Consideration of my readers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep-qm-action-distance_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sep-qm-action-distance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Isaac Newton, Letters to Bentley, 1692/3</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb's law">Coulomb's law</a> of electric forces was initially also formulated as instantaneous action at a distance, but was later superseded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_Equations" title="Maxwell's Equations">Maxwell's Equations</a> of electromagnetism which obey locality.</p>
<p>In 1905 <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Theory_of_Relativity" title="Special Theory of Relativity">Special Theory of Relativity</a> postulated that no material or energy can travel faster than the speed of light, and Einstein thereby sought to reformulate physical laws in a way which obeyed the principle of locality. He later succeeded in producing an alternative theory of gravitation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Relativity" title="General Relativity">General Relativity</a>, which obeys the principle of locality.</p>
<p>However, a different challenge to the principle of locality subsequently emerged from the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_Mechanics" title="Quantum Mechanics">Quantum Mechanics</a>, which Einstein himself had helped to create.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativity">Relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Locality is a key axiom of Einstein's relativistic <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>, where it is essential to <a href="/wiki/Causality_(physics)" title="Causality (physics)">causality</a> that effects do not propagate faster than the speed of light.</p>
<p>Einstein's quantum theory (currently termed the <i>old quantum theory</i>) is said to be <i>relativistic</i> because it does NOT violate either his general or special theory of relativity: speed of light is a limiting factor.</p>
<p>In Einstein's theory, two <a href="/wiki/Observable" title="Observable">observable</a> objects are localised, each within its own distinct spacetime region (<i>frame</i>), which regions are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacelike" title="Spacelike">separated</a> from each other in space, and effects pass from one object to the other at the speed of light or slower. This is a key property of spacetime flowing from the special theory of relativity.</p>
<p>A solution of Einstein's field equations is <i>local</i> if the underlying equations are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentz_invariant" title="Lorentz invariant">invariant</a> (a condition where the laws of physics are invariant - that is, the same - in all frames which are moving with uniform velocity with respect to one another).</p>
<p>Alternatively, a solution of Einstein's field equations is still <i>local</i> if the underlying equations are <a href="/wiki/General_covariance" title="General covariance">co-variant</a>: i.e. if all (non-gravitational) laws make the same predictions for identical experiments taking place at the same time in two different inertial (that is, non-accelerating) frames; such that the variations from the resting state are the same (i.e. vary equally) for each frame.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics">Quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EPR_paradox">EPR paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EPR paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> argues that <a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_quantum_mechanics" title="Introduction to quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompleteness_of_quantum_physics" title="Incompleteness of quantum physics">incomplete theory</a>, because he has shown that, as originally formulated, it leads to a paradox.</p>
<p>Einstein, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Podolsky" title="Boris Podolsky">Podolsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rosen" title="Nathan Rosen">Rosen</a> (dubbed the "EPR" group) identified a <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">paradox</a> in the theory: quantum mechanics predicts non-locality (in breach of special relativity), unless position and momentum are simultaneously real properties of the particle.</p>
<p>Einstein based this conclusion on two assumptions, which he termed axioms: namely, that the principle of locality is necessary; and that there can be no violation of it. He termed this "the principle of Local Action":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>"The ... idea characterises the relative independence of objects far apart in space, A and B: external influence on A has no direct influence on B."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He said that without this principle, the idea of the existence of quasi-enclosed systems, and thereby the formulation of laws which can be checked experimentally, would be impossible.</p>
<p>Einstein's conclusion was unverifiable experimentally until, in 1964, <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_Bell" title="John Stewart Bell">John Stewart Bell</a> derived a <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem" title="Bell's theorem">theorem</a> that makes QM predictions which no competing theories based on <a href="/wiki/Local_hidden_variable_theory" title="Local hidden variable theory">hidden variables</a> (the "local realism" principle) have been able to reproduce.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_realism">Local realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Local realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Einstein's principle of <b>local realism</b> is the combination of the principle of locality (limiting cause-and-effect to the speed of light) with the assumption that a particle must objectively have a pre-existing value (i.e. a real value) for any <a href="/wiki/Measurement_problem" title="Measurement problem">possible measurement</a>, i.e. a value existing <i>before</i> that measurement is made.</p>
<p>Local realism is a feature of classical mechanics, and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamics" title="Electrodynamics">classical electrodynamics</a>; but <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> theories reject the principle, based on the experimental evidence of distant <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglements</a>: an interpretation that Einstein rejected (as being a <a href="/wiki/EPR_paradox" title="EPR paradox">paradox</a>), but which is supported by a 1972 experiment based on Bell's 1964 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell%27s_inequalities" title="Bell's inequalities">inequality theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>If an experiment shows quantum mechanics to have violated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell%27s_inequalities" title="Bell's inequalities">Bell's theorem</a>, then, by definition, QM must have violated either locality or realism. But it is unclear whether the 1972 experiment demonstrates a genuine violation, because it did not test the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bells_Theorem#Two_classes_of_Bell_inequalities" title="Bells Theorem">sub-class of inequalities</a>, and because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_test_loopholes" title="Bell test loopholes">experimental limitations in the test</a>.</p>
<p>In current theory, post-1972, various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpretation_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretation of quantum mechanics">interpretations</a> (i.e. theories) of quantum mechanics violate different aspects of Local Realism. But some interpretations only violate aspects of a related principle, known as <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_definiteness" title="Counterfactual definiteness">counterfactual definiteness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Counter-factual definiteness (CFD) is the idea that it is valid to describe as definite the result of a measurement that has not in fact been performed (i.e. to assume the existence of values that have not been measured).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Realism">Realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Realism in the sense used in physics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is the idea that nature exists independently of man's mind: that even if the result of a possible measurement does not exist before the act of measuring it, that does not mean it is a creation of the mind of the observer (contrary to the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consciousness_causes_collapse" title="Consciousness causes collapse">consciousness causes collapse</a>" theory in quantum mechanics).</p>
<p>A mind-independent property does not have to be a value of a physical variable, such as position or <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>. A property can be <i>potential</i> (i.e. can be a capacity): in the way that a glass object has the potential (or capacity) to break, if subjected to a particular force, but otherwise will not <i>actually</i> break.</p>
<p>Even though the result of striking a glass object with a hammer does not exist before the act of striking it, that does not mean the broken glass is a creation of the observer. A particle accelerator is a sophisticated type of hammer, and the target particles are liable to end up as a heap of broken shards.</p>
<p>Such a response, i.e. breaking, is a conditional response: a response to a particular application of force. Applied to quantum systems, <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Schrödinger</a> recognised that they too have a conditional response: a tendency to respond (i.e. a specific probability of responding) to a particular measuring force with a particular value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In a sense, they are pre-programmed with a particular outcome.</p>
<p>Such an outcome would be realistic in a metaphysical sense, without being realistic in the physicist's sense of <i>local realism</i> (which requires that a single value be produced with certainty).</p>
<p>When dealing with an "entangled" pair of particles, what we are really dealing with is a pair which we know for certain to have a common origin. It is logical to make an assumption that because they have a common origin they will have properties in common, an assumption we cannot possibly make if we choose two particles entirely at random. So we should not be surprised to find that the laws of certainty, rather than of mere statistical probability, apply to entangled pairs.</p>
<p>A related concept is "<a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_definiteness" title="Counterfactual definiteness">counterfactual definiteness</a>", the idea that it is possible to meaningfully describe as definite the result of a measurement which, in fact, has not been performed (i.e. the ability to assume the existence of objects, and assign values to their properties, even when they have not been measured).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copenhagen_interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Copenhagen interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In most of the conventional interpretations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> and the interpretation based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consistent_Histories" title="Consistent Histories">Consistent Histories</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">wavefunction</a> is not assumed to physically exist in real <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a>, it is local realism that is rejected. These interpretations propose that actual definite properties of a physical system "do not exist" prior to the measurement; and the wavefunction is nothing more than a mathematical tool used to calculate the probabilities of experimental outcomes.</p>
<p>If the wavefunction is assumed to physically exist in real spacetime, the principle of locality is violated during the measurement process by the occurrence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">wavefunction collapse</a>. This is a non-local process because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Born%27s_Rule" title="Born's Rule">Born's Rule</a>, when applied to the system's wavefunction, yields a probability density for all regions of space and time. Upon actual measurement of the physical system, the probability density vanishes everywhere instantaneously, except where (and when) the measured entity is found to exist. This "vanishing" is theorised to be a <i>real</i> physical process, and clearly non-local (i.e. faster than light), if the wavefunction is considered physically real and the probability density has converged to zero at arbitrarily far distances during the finite time required for the measurement process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bohm_interpretation">Bohm interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bohm interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/De_Broglie%E2%80%93Bohm_theory" title="De Broglie–Bohm theory">Bohm interpretation</a> preserves realism, hence it needs to violate the principle of locality in order to achieve the required correlations. It does so by maintaining that both the position and momentum of a particle are determinate, in that they correspond to the definite trajectory of the particle, but that trajectory cannot be known without knowing the physical state of the entire universe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Many-worlds_interpretation">Many-worlds interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Many-worlds interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">many-worlds interpretation</a>, both realism <i>and</i> locality are retained, but <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_definiteness" title="Counterfactual definiteness">counterfactual definiteness</a> is rejected by the extension of the notion of reality to allow the existence of <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">parallel universes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pragmatic_interpretation">Pragmatic interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pragmatic interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the differences between the different interpretations are mostly philosophical ones (except for the Bohm and many-worlds interpretations), physicists usually employ language in which the important statements are neutral with regard to all of the interpretations.</p>
<p>In this framework, only the measurable action at a distance —a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superluminal" title="Superluminal">superluminal</a> propagation of real, physical information— would usually be considered in violation of the principle of locality by physicists. Such phenomena have never been seen, and are not predicted by the current theories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loophole-free_violation">Loophole-free violation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principle_of_locality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Loophole-free violation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, Ronald Hanson reported observing a loophole-free violation in an experimental test of <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem" title="Bell's theorem">Bell's theorem</a>: in other words, a result which—for the first time—is free of any additional assumptions (previous experiments, going all the way back to 1972, had required that various assumptions be made in order to obtain an unambiguous contradiction of local realism).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hanson was reporting on entanglement regarding distant electron spins, in 245 trials, which found that S = 2.42 (+/- 0.20), with a probability of p = 0.039.</p>
<p>This result rules out large classes of local realism theories.</p>
