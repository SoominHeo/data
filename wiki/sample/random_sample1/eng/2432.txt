<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">State ownership</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>State ownership</b> (also called <b>public ownership</b> and <b>government ownership</b>) refers to property interests that are vested in the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_body" title="Public body">public body</a> representing a community as opposed to an <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> State ownership may refer to ownership and control of any <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">enterprise</a> at any level (<a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">national</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_government" title="Regional government">regional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal" title="Municipal">municipal</a>); or to non-governmental public ownership. The process of bringing an asset into state ownership is called <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalization</a> or <a href="/wiki/Municipalization" title="Municipalization">municipalization</a>. State ownership is one of the three major forms of property ownership, differentiated from private, <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">cooperative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">common ownership</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In market-based economies, state-owned assets are frequently managed and operated as joint-stock corporations with a government owning either all or a controlling stake of the shares. This form is often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/State-owned_enterprise" title="State-owned enterprise">state-owned enterprise</a>. A state-owned enterprise might variously operate as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Not-for-profit_corporation" title="Not-for-profit corporation">not-for-profit corporation</a>, as it may not be required to generate a profit; as a commercial enterprise in competitive sectors; or as a <a href="/wiki/Natural_monopoly" title="Natural monopoly">natural monopoly</a>. Governments may also use the profitable entities they own to support the <a href="/wiki/Government_budget" title="Government budget">general budget</a>. The creation of a state-owned enterprise from other forms of public property is called <a href="/wiki/Corporatization" title="Corporatization">corporatization</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Soviet-type_economic_planning" title="Soviet-type economic planning">Soviet-type economies</a>, state property was the dominant form of industry as property. The state held a monopoly on land and natural resources, and enterprises operated under the legal framework of a nominally <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">planned economy</a>, operating according to different criteria than state and private enterprises in capitalistic market and mixed economies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State-owned_enterprise">State-owned enterprise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_ownership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: State-owned enterprise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/State-owned_enterprise" title="State-owned enterprise">State-owned enterprise</a></div>
<p>A state-owned enterprise is a commercial enterprise owned by a government entity in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitalist" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> market or <a href="/wiki/Mixed_economy" title="Mixed economy">mixed economy</a>. Reasons for state ownership of commercial enterprises are that the enterprise in question is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_monopoly" title="Natural monopoly">natural monopoly</a> or because the government is promoting economic development and industrialization. State-owned enterprises may or may not be expected to operate in a broadly commercial manner and may or may not have <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopolies</a> in their areas of activity. The transformation of public entities and government agencies into government-owned corporations is sometimes a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_capitalist" title="State capitalist">State capitalist</a> economies are capitalist market economies that have high degrees of government-owned businesses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_socialism">Relation to socialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_ownership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to socialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">Social ownership</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/State_socialism" title="State socialism">State socialism</a></div>
<p>Public ownership of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> is a subset of <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">social ownership</a>, which is the defining characteristic of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> economy. However, state ownership and nationalization by themselves are not socialist, as they can exist under a wide variety of different <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic systems</a> for a variety of different reasons. State ownership by itself does not imply social ownership where income rights belong to society as a whole. As such, state ownership is only one possible expression of public ownership, which itself is one variation of the broader concept of social ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the context of socialism, public ownership implies that the <a href="/wiki/Surplus_product" title="Surplus product">surplus product</a> generated by publicly owned assets accrues to all of society in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Social_dividend" title="Social dividend">social dividend</a>, as opposed to a distinct class of private capital owners. There is a wide variety of organizational forms for state-run industry, ranging from specialized technocratic management to direct <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_self-management" title="Workers' self-management">workers' self-management</a>. In traditional conceptions of non-market socialism, public ownership is a tool to consolidate the means of production as a precursor to the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Economic_planning" title="Economic planning">economic planning</a> for the allocation of resources between organizations, as required by government or by the state.</p>
<p>State ownership is advocated as a form of social ownership for practical concerns, with the state being seen as the obvious candidate for owning and operating the means of production. Proponents assume that the state, as the representative of the <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a>, would manage resources and production for the benefit of the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As a form of social ownership, state ownership may be contrasted with cooperatives and common ownership. Socialist theories and political ideologies that favor state ownership of the means of production may be labelled <a href="/wiki/State_socialism" title="State socialism">state socialism</a>.</p>
<p>State ownership was recognized by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> in <i>Socialism: Utopian and Scientific</i> as, by itself, not doing away with capitalism, including the process of capital accumulation and structure of wage labor. Engels argued that state ownership of commercial industry would represent the final stage of capitalism, consisting of ownership and management of large-scale production and manufacture by the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="User_rights">User rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_ownership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: User rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>When ownership of a resource is vested in the state, or any branch of the state such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_authority" title="Local authority">local authority</a>, individual use "rights" are based on the state's management policies, though these rights are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_rights" title="Property rights">property rights</a> as they are not transmissible. For example, if a family is allocated an apartment that is state owned, it will have been granted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenancy" title="Tenancy">tenancy</a> of the apartment, which may be lifelong or inheritable, but the management and control rights are held by various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_department" title="Government department">government departments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_property">Public property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_ownership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_property" title="Public property">public property</a></div>
<p>There is a distinction to be made between state ownership and <a href="/wiki/Public_property" title="Public property">public property</a>. The former may refer to assets operated by a specific state institution or branch of government, used exclusively by that branch, such as a research laboratory. The latter refers to assets and resources that are available to the entire public for use, such as a public park (see <a href="/wiki/Public_space" title="Public space">public space</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_theory">Economic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_ownership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In economics, the desirability of state ownership has been studied in <a href="/wiki/Contract_theory" title="Contract theory">contract theory</a>. According to the property rights approach based on <a href="/wiki/Incomplete_contracts" title="Incomplete contracts">incomplete contracting</a> (developed by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hart_(economist)" title="Oliver Hart (economist)">Oliver Hart</a> and his co-authors), ownership matters because it determines what happens in contingencies that were not considered in prevailing contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The work by Hart, Shleifer and Vishny (1997) is the leading application of the property rights approach to the question whether state ownership or private ownership is desirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In their model, the government and a private firm can invest to improve the quality of a public good and to reduce its production costs. It turns out that private ownership results in strong incentives to reduce costs, but it may also lead to poor quality. Hence, depending on the available investment technologies, there are situations in which state ownership is better. The Hart-Shleifer-Vishny theory has been extended in many directions. For instance, some authors have also considered mixed forms of private ownership and state ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Moreover, the Hart-Shleifer-Vishny model assumes that the private party derives no utility from provision of the public good. Besley and Ghatak (2001) have shown that if the private party (a non-governmental organization) cares about the public good, then the party with the larger valuation of the public good should always be the owner, regardless of the parties' investment technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Yet, more recently some authors have shown that the investment technology also matters in the Besley-Ghatak framework if an investing party is indispensable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or if there are bargaining frictions between the government and the private party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
