<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">International unit</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a>, the <b>international unit</b> is a unit of measurement for the amount of a substance; the mass or volume that constitutes one international unit varies based on which substance is being measured, and the variance is based on the <a href="/wiki/Biological_activity" title="Biological activity">biological activity</a> or effect, for the purpose of easier comparison across substances. International units are used to quantify <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">medications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_product" title="Blood product">blood products</a>, and similar biologically active substances.</p>
<p>The name <b>international unit</b> has often been capitalized (in English and other languages), although major English-language dictionaries treat it as a common noun and thus use lower case. The name has several accepted abbreviations. It is usually abbreviated as <b>IU</b> in English, <b>UI</b> in <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> (for example <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <i>unidad internacional</i>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>unité internationale</i>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <i>unità internazionale</i>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> <i>unitate internațională</i>), <b>IE</b> in several <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> (for example <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>internationale Einheit</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>internationale eenheid</i>) or as other forms (for example <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> <b>МЕ</b>, международная единица [<i>mezhdunarodnaya jedinica</i>], <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> <b>NE</b>, <i>nemzetközi egység</i>). In order to remove the possibility of having the letter "I" confused with the digit "1", some hospitals have it as a stated policy to omit the "I", that is, to only use <b>U</b> or <b>E</b> when talking and writing about dosages, while other hospitals require the word "units" (or words "international units") to be written out entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCAHO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-JCAHO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many biological agents exist in different forms or preparations (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Retinol" title="Retinol">retinol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beta-Carotene" title="Beta-Carotene"><i>beta</i>-carotene</a>). The goal of the IU is to be able to compare these, so that different forms or preparations with the same biological effect will contain the same number of IUs. To do so, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> <a href="/wiki/WHO_Expert_Committee_on_Biological_Standardization" title="WHO Expert Committee on Biological Standardization">Expert Committee on Biological Standardization</a> provides a reference preparation of the agent, arbitrarily sets the number of IUs contained in that preparation, and specifies a biological procedure to compare other preparations of the same agent to the reference preparation. Since the number of IUs contained in a new substance is arbitrarily set, there is no equivalence between IU measurements of different biological agents. For instance, one IU of vitamin E cannot be equated with one IU of vitamin A in any way, including <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">efficacy</a>.</p>
<p>Despite its name, IU is not part of the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. The IU should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_unit" title="Enzyme unit">enzyme unit</a>, also known as the <i>international unit of enzyme activity</i> and abbreviated as <b>U</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equality_and_equivalency_of_IU_for_different_substances">Equality and equivalency of IU for different substances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Equality and equivalency of IU for different substances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To define the IU for a substance, an international collaborative study is organized by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> <a href="/wiki/WHO_Expert_Committee_on_Biological_Standardization" title="WHO Expert Committee on Biological Standardization">Expert Committee on Biological Standardization</a> using various assay systems in several laboratories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCC_1992_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSCC_1992-2">[2]</a></sup> The main goal of the study is to reach a consensus regarding methods of analysis and the approach to standardization so that results can be compared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffcoate_1988_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffcoate_1988-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wicher_1991_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wicher_1991-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekins_1991_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ekins_1991-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The study is performed using highly purified preparations of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a>, typically in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyophilize" title="Lyophilize">lyophilized</a> form, called "international reference preparations" or IRPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCC_1992_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSCC_1992-2">[2]</a></sup> Each preparation is divided into precisely weighed samples, with each sample stored in its own ampoule labeled with a code corresponding to the source IRP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCC_1992_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSCC_1992-2">[2]</a></sup> Assays are performed using these samples and are calibrated against the previously available IU standard. These results can be quite variable; the final IU value for samples of a given IRP are determined by consensus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffcoate_1988_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffcoate_1988-3">[3]</a></sup> The IRP that provides the best results and shows the best long term stability is selected to define the next IU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCC_1992_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-CSCC_1992-2">[2]</a></sup> This IRP is then referred to as the "international standard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCC_1992_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-CSCC_1992-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_equivalents_of_1_IU">Mass equivalents of 1 IU</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mass equivalents of 1 IU">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For some substances, the precise mass equivalent of one IU is later established. If that happens, the former IU mass for that substance is officially abandoned in favor of a newly established mass. The unit count will often still remain in use.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">Vitamin A</a></b>: 1 IU is the biological equivalent of 0.3 μg <a href="/wiki/Retinol" title="Retinol">retinol</a>, or of 0.6 μg beta-<a href="/wiki/Carotene" title="Carotene">carotene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">Vitamin C</a></b>: 1 IU is 50 μg L-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">Vitamin D</a></b>: 1 IU is the biological equivalent of 25 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanogram" title="Nanogram">ng</a> <a href="/wiki/Cholecalciferol" title="Cholecalciferol">cholecalciferol</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ergocalciferol" title="Ergocalciferol">ergocalciferol</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_E" title="Vitamin E">Vitamin E</a></b>: 1 IU is the biological equivalent of about 0.667 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milligram" title="Milligram">mg</a> d-alpha-<a href="/wiki/Tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">tocopherol</a> (2/3 mg exactly), or of 0.45 mg of dl-alpha-tocopherol acetate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">Insulin</a></b>: 1 IU is equivalent to 0.0347 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milligram" title="Milligram">mg</a> of human insulin (28.8 IU/mg).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Oxytocin_(medication)" title="Oxytocin (medication)">Oxytocin</a></b>: 1 IU is equivalent to 2 μg of pure peptide.</li>
</ul>
