<p><b>International maritime signal flags</b> refers to various flags used to communicate with ships. The principal system of flags and associated codes is the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Signals" title="International Code of Signals">International Code of Signals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Various navies have flag systems with additional flags and codes, and other flags are used in special uses, or have historical significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_maritime_signal_flags&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various methods by which the flags can be used as signals:</p>
<ul>
<li>Each flag spells an alphabetic message, letter by letter.</li>
<li>Individual flags have specific and standard meanings;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> for example, <a href="/wiki/Diving_support_vessel" title="Diving support vessel">diving support vessels</a> raise the "A" flag indicating their inability to move from their current location because they have a diver underwater and to warn other vessels to keep clear to avoid endangering the diver(s) with their propellers.</li>
<li>One or more flags form a code word whose meaning can be looked up in a code book held by both parties. An example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popham_numeric_code" title="Popham numeric code">Popham numeric code</a> used at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Battle of Trafalgar</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Yacht_racing" title="Yacht racing">yacht racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinghy_racing" title="Dinghy racing">dinghy racing</a>, flags have other meanings; for example, the P flag is used as the "preparatory" flag to indicate an imminent start, and the S flag means "shortened course" (for more details see <a href="/wiki/Racing_Rules_of_Sailing#Race_signals" title="Racing Rules of Sailing">Race signals</a>).</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> uses the same flags, with a few unique to warships, alone or in short sets to communicate various unclassified messages. The NATO usage generally differs from the international meanings, and therefore warships will fly the <a href="/wiki/File:ICS_Answer.svg" title="File:ICS Answer.svg">Code/answer flag</a> above the signal to indicate it should be read using the international meaning.</p>
<p>During the Allied occupations of Axis countries after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, use and display of those nations' national flags was banned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In order to comply with the international legal requirement that a ship identify its registry by displaying the appropriate national ensign, swallow-tailed versions of the C, D, and E signal flags were designated as, respectively, provisional <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany" title="Allied-occupied Germany">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Ryukyu_Islands#Post-war_occupation" title="History of the Ryukyu Islands">Okinawan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Japan" title="Occupation of Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_ensign" title="Civil ensign">civil ensigns</a>. Being swallowtails, they are commonly referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Germany#After_World_War_II" title="Flag of Germany">C-pennant</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Germany#After_World_War_II" title="Flag of Germany">C-Doppelstander</a>), "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Japanese_flags#Historical_flags" title="List of Japanese flags">D-pennant</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Japanese_flags#Historical_flags" title="List of Japanese flags">E-pennant</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_meanings">Additional meanings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_maritime_signal_flags&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Additional meanings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="wikitable sortable">
<tr>
<th>Flag</th>
<th>NATO nations' naval meaning - replenishment at sea</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Romeo</td>
<td>Romeo at dip: Romeo flag is located <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> way up toward the point of the hoist. On the control ship, it means, "I am steady on course and speed and am prepared to receive you alongside on side indicated." On the approaching ship, it means, "I am ready to come alongside."<br/>
Romeo close up: Romeo flag is at the top of the hoist, touching the point of the hoist or as high as it will go. On the control ship, it means, "I am ready for your approach.: On the approach ship, it means, "I am commencing my approach."<br/>
Romeo hauled down: This means the first messenger is in hand for controlling and receiving ship.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Bravo</td>
<td>Bravo at dip: Bravo flag is located <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> way up toward the point of the hoist. On the delivery ship, it means, "I have temporarily stopped supplying." On the receiving ship, it means, "I have temporarily stopped receiving."<br/>
Bravo close up: Bravo flag is at the top of the hoist, touching the point of the hoist or as high as it will go. On both ships, it means fuel or explosives are being transferred.<br/>
Bravo hauled down: On both ships, it means delivery is complete.</td>
</tr>
