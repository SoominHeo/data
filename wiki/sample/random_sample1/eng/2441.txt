<p>The <i><b>Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile</b></i> (<b>FIA</b>, English: International Automobile Federation) is an association established as the <i>Association Internationale des Automobile Clubs Reconnus</i> (AIACR, English: 'International Association of Recognized Automobile Clubs') on 20 June 1904 to represent the interests of motoring organisations and motor car users. To the general public, the FIA is mostly known as the governing body for many <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">auto racing</a> events. The FIA also promotes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_safety" title="Road safety">road safety</a> around the world.</p>
<p>Headquartered at 8 <a href="/wiki/Place_de_la_Concorde" title="Place de la Concorde">Place de la Concorde</a>, Paris, the FIA consists of 245 national member organisations in 143 countries worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Its current president is <a href="/wiki/Jean_Todt" title="Jean Todt">Jean Todt</a>.</p>
<p>The FIA is generally known by its French name or initials, even in non-French-speaking countries, but is occasionally rendered as 'International Automobile Federation'.</p>
<p>Its most prominent role is in the licensing and arbitration of <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a> motor racing. The FIA along with the <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_Motocyclisme" title="Fédération Internationale de Motocyclisme">Fédération Internationale de Motocyclisme</a></i> (FIM) also certify <a href="/wiki/Land_speed_record" title="Land speed record">land speed record</a> attempts. The <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> provisionally recognized the federation in 2011, and granted full recognition in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Association Internationale des Automobile Clubs Reconnus</i> (AIACR) was founded in Paris on 20 June 1904, as an association of national <a href="/wiki/Motor_club" title="Motor club">motor clubs</a>. The association was designed to represent the interests of motor car users, as well as to oversee the burgeoning international motor sport scene. In 1922, the AIACR delegated the organisation of automobile racing to the <i>Commission Sportive Internationale</i> (CSI), which would set the regulations for international <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_motor_racing" title="Grand Prix motor racing">Grand Prix motor racing</a>. The <a href="/wiki/European_Championship_(auto_racing)" title="European Championship (auto racing)">European Drivers' Championship</a> was introduced in 1931, a title awarded to the driver with the best results in the selected Grands Prix. Upon the resumption of motor racing after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the AIACR was renamed the <i>Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile</i>. The FIA established a number of new racing categories, among them Formulas One and Two, and created the first World Championship, the Formula One World Drivers' Championship, in 1950.</p>
<p>The CSI determined the regulations for holding Grands Prix and selected the races that formed part of the World Championships – a <a href="/wiki/World_Sportscar_Championship" title="World Sportscar Championship">World Sportscar Championship</a> was established in 1953 – but the organisers of the individual races were responsible for accepting entries, paying prize money, and the general running of each event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F-F_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-F-F-4">[4]</a></sup> In Formula One, this led to tension between the teams, which formed themselves into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_One_Constructors_Association" title="Formula One Constructors Association">Formula One Constructors Association</a> (FOCA) founded in 1974, event organisers and the CSI. The FIA and CSI were largely amateur organisations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FISA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FISA-5">[5]</a></sup> and FOCA under the control of <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Ecclestone" title="Bernie Ecclestone">Bernie Ecclestone</a> began to take charge of various aspects of organising the events, as well as setting terms with race organisers for the arrival of teams and the amount of prize money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F-F_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-F-F-4">[4]</a></sup> This led to the FIA President Prince Metternich attempting to reassert its authority by appointing <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Balestre" title="Jean-Marie Balestre">Jean-Marie Balestre</a> as the head of the CSI, who promptly reformed the committee into the autonomous <i>Fédération Internationale du Sport Automobile</i> (FISA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F-F_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-F-F-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under Balestre's leadership FISA and the manufacturer-backed teams became involved in a dispute with FOCA (named the "<a href="/wiki/FISA%E2%80%93FOCA_war" title="FISA–FOCA war">FISA–FOCA war</a>"). The conflict saw several races being cancelled or boycotted, and large-scale disagreement over the technical regulations and their enforcement. The dispute and the <a href="/wiki/Concorde_Agreement" title="Concorde Agreement">Concorde Agreement</a> that was written to end it, would have significant ramifications for the FIA. The agreement led to FOCA acquiring commercial rights over Formula One, while FISA and the FIA would have control over sport's regulations. FOCA chief Bernie Ecclestone became an FIA Vice-President with control over promoting the FIA's World Championships, while FOCA legal advisor and former <a href="/wiki/March_Engineering" title="March Engineering">March Engineering</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Max_Mosley" title="Max Mosley">Max Mosley</a> would end up becoming FISA President in 1991. Mosley succeeded Balestre as President of the FIA in 1993 and restructured the organisation, dissolving FISA and placing motor racing under the direct management of the FIA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FISA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FISA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/1994_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1994 San Marino Grand Prix">1994 San Marino Grand Prix</a>, which saw the deaths of Ayrton Senna and Roland Ratzenberger, the FIA formed an Expert Advisory Safety Committee to research and improve safety in motor racing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIA_Ins_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FIA_Ins-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F1_Safety_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-F1_Safety-7">[7]</a></sup> Chaired by Formula One medical chief Professor <a href="/wiki/Sid_Watkins" title="Sid Watkins">Sid Watkins</a>, the committee worked with the <a href="/wiki/MIRA_Ltd." title="MIRA Ltd.">Motor Industry Research Association</a> to strengthen the crash resistance of cars and the restraint systems and to improve the drivers personal safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F1_Safety_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-F1_Safety-7">[7]</a></sup> The recommendations of the committee led to significantly more stringent <a href="/wiki/Crash_test" title="Crash test">crash tests</a> for racing vehicles, new safety standards for helmets and race suits, and the eventual introduction of the <a href="/wiki/HANS_device" title="HANS device">HANS device</a> as compulsory in all international racing series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The committee also worked on improving circuit safety. This led to a number of changes at motor racing circuits around the world, and the improvement of crash barriers and trackside medical procedures.</p>
<p>The FIA was a founder member of the <a href="/wiki/Euro_NCAP" title="Euro NCAP">European New Car Assessment Programme</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_safety" title="Car safety">car safety</a> programme that crash-tests new models and publishes safety reports on vehicles. Mosley was the first chairman of the organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Commission_investigation">European Commission investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: European Commission investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_Competition" title="Directorate-General for Competition">Competition Directorate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the FIA were involved in a dispute over the commercial administration of motorsport during the 1990s. The Competition Commissioner, <a href="/wiki/Karel_Van_Miert" title="Karel Van Miert">Karel Van Miert</a> had received a number of complaints from television companies and motorsport promoters in 1997 that the FIA had been abusing its position as motorsport's governing body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup> Van Miert's initial inquiry had not concluded by 1999,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPFIAEU_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPFIAEU-11">[11]</a></sup> which resulted in the FIA <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">suing</a> the European Commission, alleging that the delay was causing damaging uncertainty, and successfully receiving an apology from the Commission over the leaking of documents relating to the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mario_Monti" title="Mario Monti">Mario Monti</a> took over as Commissioner in 1999, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> opened a formal investigation into the FIA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup> The Commission alleged a number of breaches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_competition_law" title="European competition law">European competition law</a>, centred around the FIA's administration of licences required to participate in motorsport and the control of television rights of the motorsport events it authorised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup> In order to compete in events the FIA authorised, the competitor had to apply for a licence, which prohibited licensees from entering a series not controlled by the FIA. This provision, which also applied to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racing_circuit" title="Racing circuit">racing circuits</a> and promoters, prevented rival championships competing against the FIA championships, by restricting their access to facilities, drivers, and vehicle manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, the FIA also claimed the television rights to all international motorsport events, which were then transferred to <a href="/wiki/International_Sportsworld_Communicators" title="International Sportsworld Communicators">International Sportsworld Communicators</a>, a company controlled by Ecclestone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup> This meant organisers were forced into having their championships promoted by the same company that managed the affairs of other motorsport events, a potential <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a>. The combination of these requirements meant Ecclestone's <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_Group" title="Formula One Group">Formula One Administration</a>, which now controlled Formula One's commercial rights, was protected from competition from any rival championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Open_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Open-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPFIAEU_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPFIAEU-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The investigation was closed in 2001 after the FIA and FOA agreed to a number of conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Close_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Close-13">[13]</a></sup> In order to fairly regulate all international motorsport, the FIA agreed to limit its role to that of a sporting regulator, and would sell the commercial rights to its championships, including Formula One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIA_release_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FIA_release-14">[14]</a></sup> This was to prevent a conflict of interest between the FIA's regulatory role, and any commercial advantages it may gain from the success of certain championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Close_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Close-13">[13]</a></sup> The FIA could no longer prevent non-FIA administered events from being established, neither could it use its powers to prevent competition to Formula One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Close_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Close-13">[13]</a></sup> Ecclestone and FOA would no longer handle the commercial rights to other motorsport events outside of Formula One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIA_release_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FIA_release-14">[14]</a></sup> Ecclestone had sold the ISC company, which now only controlled the rights to rallying,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and would stand down from his role as an FIA Vice-President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Close_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Close-13">[13]</a></sup> As a result of this ruling, the FIA sold the commercial rights to Formula One to the Formula One Group for 100 years for $360 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Mosley_years">Later Mosley years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later Mosley years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mosley was elected unopposed to his third term as President in 2001, the first election which reduced the term from five to four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The FIA also moved back to Paris, having been based in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> (outside of the EU) for the previous two years during the European Commission's investigation.</p>
<p>The FIA Foundation was established in 2001 as the FIA's charitable arm. The Foundation received a US$300 million grant from the sale of Formula One's rights, to fund research into road safety, the environmental impact of motoring, and to support sustainable motoring. In 2004 the FIA and the Foundation established the FIA Institute for Motor Sport Safety, which brought together the various safety research groups into one organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIA_Ins_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FIA_Ins-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Make_Roads_Safe" title="Make Roads Safe">Make Roads Safe</a> campaign was set up in 2006 by the FIA Foundation, targeting the creation of safe roads across the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2000s the FIA and its president became increasingly embroiled in controversy over Formula One, while facing threats from teams to establish a breakaway series. A grouping of the car manufacturers involved in F1, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_Manufacturers_Association" title="Grand Prix Manufacturers Association">Grand Prix Manufacturers Association</a>, proposed a <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_World_Championship" title="Grand Prix World Championship">new world championship</a>, which would allow them greater control over the regulations and revenue distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A new Concorde Agreement eventually ended the threat, but the breakaway series would resurface during each dispute between the FIA, teams and the Formula One Group. The FIA's handling of the tyre situation at the <a href="/wiki/2005_United_States_Grand_Prix" title="2005 United States Grand Prix">2005 United States Grand Prix</a> was criticised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Mosley had refused any modification to the circuit or the holding of a non-championship event in place of the Grand Prix, having stated that running on an untested circuit was unsafe. The FIA also threatened to punish the teams who withdrew from the event, but later exonerated the teams of any wrongdoing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having again been re-elected unopposed in 2005, Mosley faced his first leadership challenge in a vote of confidence called in June 2008. The vote was in response to allegations concerning Mosley's sex life published by the British media. Mosley won the vote by 103 votes in support to 55 against,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> though he continued to face criticism from several motoring clubs and motorsport figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In mid-2009, the FIA and the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_Teams_Association" title="Formula One Teams Association">Formula One Teams Association</a> <a href="/wiki/FIA%E2%80%93FOTA_dispute" title="FIA–FOTA dispute">disagreed</a> over the pending implementation of a budget cap for the 2010 season. The teams again threatened a breakaway championship, with the FIA in response opening an entry process for new teams. The dispute also focused on a lack of confidence in Mosley's control over the sport, and there was a stand-off until Ecclestone negotiated a settlement to establish a new Concorde Agreement. In return for the teams joining the championship and ending the dispute, the budget cap would be replaced by a series of cost cutting measures, and Mosley agreed to stand down at the end of his term in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Todt_Presidency">Todt Presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Todt Presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Former <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Scuderia Ferrari</a> boss <a href="/wiki/Jean_Todt" title="Jean Todt">Jean Todt</a> was elected the new President of the FIA in 2009, beating former World Rally champion <a href="/wiki/Ari_Vatanen" title="Ari Vatanen">Ari Vatanen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Event_history">Event history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Event history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The true history of <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> began in 1946 with the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile's (FIA's) standardisation of rules. Then in 1950, the FIA organised the first <a href="/wiki/Formula_One#Distinction_between_Formula_One_and_World_Championship_races" title="Formula One">World Championship for Drivers</a>. From 1958, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Constructors%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Constructors' Champions">Constructors Championship</a> title was introduced.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Sportscar_Championship" title="World Sportscar Championship">World Sportscar Championship</a> was created in 1953, and was the first points series for <a href="/wiki/Sports_car_racing" title="Sports car racing">sports car racing</a> in the world. The championship was solely for manufacturers up to 1981. From 1981, a Drivers Championship title was introduced and from 1985 the manufacturers title was replaced by a Teams Championship.</p>
<p>In 1973, the FIA organised the first <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a>: the <a href="/wiki/1973_Monte_Carlo_Rally" title="1973 Monte Carlo Rally">42nd Auto Rally of Monte-Carlo</a>. From 1977, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Rally_Championship_Drivers%27_champions" title="List of World Rally Championship Drivers' champions">Drivers Championship</a> title was introduced (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1977_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="1977 World Rally Championship season">1977</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1978_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="1978 World Rally Championship season">1978</a> as an <i>FIA Cup for Drivers</i> title).</p>
<p>In 1987, the FIA sanctioned the first <a href="/wiki/1987_World_Touring_Car_Championship" title="1987 World Touring Car Championship">first World Touring Car Championship</a>. Initially a one-off series, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_World_Touring_Car_Championship_season" title="2005 World Touring Car Championship season">the title was revived</a> in 2005.</p>
<p>After the 1992 season the World Sportscar Championship was cancelled and dissolved.</p>
<p>In 1993, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hot_Rod_Association" title="National Hot Rod Association">National Hot Rod Association</a> was officially recognised by the <a href="/wiki/FIA_World_Motor_Sport_Council" title="FIA World Motor Sport Council">FIA World Motor Sport Council</a> and the FIA Drag Racing Commission was formed. The FISA was dissolved, and its activities placed directly under the FIA.</p>
<p>There were no sports car world championships until 2010. The <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Ratel_Organisation" title="Stéphane Ratel Organisation">SRO Group</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/FIA_GT1_World_Championship" title="FIA GT1 World Championship">FIA GT1 World Championship</a>, which was a championship consisting of one-hour sprint races. After a successful 2010 season, the series began having difficulties and after the switch from GT1 to GT3 cars in 2012 the series folded at the end of the 2012 season.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Automobile_Club_de_l%27Ouest" title="Automobile Club de l'Ouest">Automobile Club de l'Ouest</a> (ACO) successfully organised the <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_Le_Mans_Cup" title="Intercontinental Le Mans Cup">Intercontinental Le Mans Cup</a> (ILMC) in 2010 and 2011, the FIA and ACO organised together the rebirth of the World Sportscar Championship from 2012 onward, now known as the <a href="/wiki/FIA_World_Endurance_Championship" title="FIA World Endurance Championship">FIA World Endurance Championship</a> (WEC).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisational_structure">Organisational structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organisational structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The FIA General Assembly is the Federation's supreme governing body, consisting of representatives from each of the FIA's member associations. Meetings of the General Assembly are usually held once a year, though extraordinary meetings can be convened for urgent matters. The General Assembly has responsibility for amending the FIA's statutes and regulations, approving the annual budget and reports, deciding upon the membership, and electing the officers and members to the Federation's governing bodies. The FIA Senate overseas the finances and management of the FIA, and can take decisions required between meetings of the relevant committee or World Council.</p>
<p>The head of the FIA and chairman of the General Assembly is the President, an office currently held by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Todt" title="Jean Todt">Jean Todt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The President coordinates the activities of the Federation and proposes resolutions to the various commissions and committees. The President also acts as the representative of the FIA to external organisations. There is also a Deputy and seven Vice-Presidents for Sport and Mobility, who assist the President in managing the activities in their respective area. The President is elected to a four-year term by the General Assembly. Candidates must produce an <a href="/wiki/Electoral_list" title="Electoral list">electoral list</a> consisting of their proposed Deputy Presidents, Vice-Presidents for Sport, and the President of the Senate, as well as demonstrate support from a number of member clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The FIA has two World Councils. The Mobility and Automobile Council governs all non-sporting activities, comprising transport policy, road safety, tourism and environmental concerns. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Motor_Sport_Council" title="World Motor Sport Council">World Motor Sport Council</a> (WMSC) governs all sporting events regulated by the FIA, and writes the regulations for every FIA championship. It also supervises <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karting" title="Karting">Karting</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_de_Karting" title="Commission Internationale de Karting">Commission Internationale de Karting</a> (CIK). Beneath the WMSC are a number of specialised commissions, which are either focused on individual championships, or general areas such as safety.</p>
<p>The FIA's judicial bodies include the International Tribunal, which exercises disciplinary powers that are not dealt with by the meeting <a href="/wiki/Motorsport_marshal#Stewards" title="Motorsport marshal">stewards</a> and the International Court of Appeal. The ICA is the final appeal tribunal for international motor sport, which resolves disputes brought before it by National Sporting Authorities worldwide, or by the President of the FIA. It can also settle non-sporting disputes brought by national motoring organizations affiliated to the FIA.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FIA_Institute_Young_Driver_Excellence_Academy">FIA Institute Young Driver Excellence Academy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: FIA Institute Young Driver Excellence Academy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FIA_Institute_Young_Driver_Excellence_Academy" title="FIA Institute Young Driver Excellence Academy">FIA Institute Young Driver Excellence Academy</a></div>
<p>In October 2010, the FIA Institute Young Driver Excellence Academy was announced; a new programme to develop young driver talent worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> After a three-day shootout in <a href="/wiki/Melk" title="Melk">Melk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> on 6–8 February, twelve drivers were selected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations_and_standards">Regulations and standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Regulations and standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the <a href="/wiki/Formula_Student" title="Formula Student">Formula Student</a> regulations also refer to FIA standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Brundle" title="Martin Brundle">Martin Brundle</a> wrote a column in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunday_Times" title="Sunday Times">Sunday Times</a></i> entitled "Witch-hunt threatens to spoil world title race" in which he accused the FIA of a witch-hunt against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_(racing)" title="McLaren (racing)">McLaren</a>. The World Motor Sport Council responded by issuing a <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writ</a> against the <i>Sunday Times</i> on charges of libel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Brundle hit back saying that "I have earned the right to have an opinion" and suggesting the writ was a "warning sign to other journalists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2007_Formula_One_espionage_controversy" title="2007 Formula One espionage controversy">2007 Formula One espionage controversy</a> involved accusations of theft made against McLaren, who were accused of stealing technological secrets from Ferrari.</p>
<p>In 2008, accusations surfaced that FIA President Max Mosley was involved in scandalous sexual behaviour. Following a June 2008 decision of the FIA to retain Max Mosley as president, the German branch of the FIA, the <a href="/wiki/ADAC" title="ADAC">ADAC</a> (the largest European motoring body), announced, "We view with regret and incredulity the FIA general assembly's decision in Paris, confirming Max Mosley in office as FIA president". It froze all its activities with the FIA until Max Mosley leaves office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Press reports also claimed that <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Ecclestone" title="Bernie Ecclestone">Bernie Ecclestone</a> was investigating creating a rival to the Formula 1 series due to the scandal.</p>
