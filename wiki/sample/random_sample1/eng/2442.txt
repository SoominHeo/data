<p>The <b>International Cricket Council</b> (<b>ICC</b>) is the international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" title="Sport governing body">governing body</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>. It was founded as the <b>Imperial Cricket Conference</b> in 1909 by representatives from England, Australia and South Africa, renamed the <b>International Cricket Conference</b> in 1965, and took up its current name in 1989.</p>
<p>The ICC has 105 members: 10 <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Cricket_Council_members#Full_Members" title="List of International Cricket Council members">Full Members</a> that play <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a>, 39 <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Cricket_Council_members#Associate_Members" title="List of International Cricket Council members">Associate Members</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-new-4">[4]</a></sup> and 56 <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Cricket_Council_members#Affiliate_Members" title="List of International Cricket Council members">Affiliate Members</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The ICC is responsible for the organisation and governance of cricket's major international tournaments, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Cricket_World_Cup" title="Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup</a>. It also appoints the <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(cricket)" title="Umpire (cricket)">umpires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Referee" title="Referee">referees</a> that officiate at all sanctioned Test matches, <a href="/wiki/One_Day_International" title="One Day International">One Day International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twenty20_International" title="Twenty20 International">Twenty20 Internationals</a>. It promulgates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Code_of_Conduct" title="ICC Code of Conduct">ICC Code of Conduct</a>, which sets professional standards of discipline for international cricket,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and also co-ordinates action against corruption and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Match-fixing" title="Match-fixing">match-fixing</a> through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Anti_Corruption_and_Security_Unit" title="ICC Anti Corruption and Security Unit">Anti-Corruption and Security Unit</a> (ACSU). The ICC does not control bilateral fixtures between member countries (which include all <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a>), it does not govern domestic cricket in member countries, and it does not make the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_cricket" title="Laws of cricket">laws of the game</a>, which remain under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Cricket_Club" title="Marylebone Cricket Club">Marylebone Cricket Club</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_International_Cricket_Council" title="Chairman of the International Cricket Council">Chairman</a> heads the board of directors and on 26 June 2014, <a href="/wiki/N._Srinivasan" title="N. Srinivasan">N. Srinivasan</a>, the former president of <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Control_for_Cricket_in_India" title="Board of Control for Cricket in India">BCCI</a>, was announced as the first chairman of the council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The role of ICC president has become a largely honorary position since the establishment of the chairman role and other changes were made to the ICC constitution in 2014. It has been claimed that the 2014 changes have handed control to the so-called 'Big Three' nations of England, India and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kamal_Quits_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kamal_Quits-8">[8]</a></sup> The current ICC president is <a href="/wiki/Zaheer_Abbas" title="Zaheer Abbas">Zaheer Abbas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abbas_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abbas-3">[3]</a></sup> who was appointed in June 2015 following the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kamal_(politician)" title="Mustafa Kamal (politician)">Mustafa Kamal</a> in April 2015. Kamal, the former president of the <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_Cricket_Board" title="Bangladesh Cricket Board">Bangladesh Cricket Board</a>, resigned shortly after the <a href="/wiki/2015_Cricket_World_Cup" title="2015 Cricket World Cup">2015 World Cup</a>, claiming the organisation operated both unconstitutionally and unlawfully. The current CEO is <a href="/wiki/Dave_Richardson_(cricketer)" title="Dave Richardson (cricketer)">David Richardson</a>, who succeeded <a href="/wiki/Haroon_Lorgat" title="Haroon Lorgat">Haroon Lorgat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISSC-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 15 June 1909 representatives from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_cricket_team" title="English cricket team">England</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_cricket_team" title="Australian cricket team">Australia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_African_cricket_team" title="South African cricket team">South Africa</a> met at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Cricket_Ground" title="Lord's Cricket Ground">Lord's</a> and founded the <i>Imperial Cricket Conference</i>. Membership was confined to the governing bodies of cricket within the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> where <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> was played. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Indian_cricket_team" title="West Indian cricket team">West Indies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_cricket_team" title="New Zealand cricket team">New Zealand</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_cricket_team" title="Indian cricket team">India</a> were elected as Full Members in 1926, doubling the number of Test-playing nations to six. That year it was also agreed to make a change in membership, with election being for; "governing bodies of cricket in countries within the Empire to which cricket teams are sent, or which send teams to England." However the United States did not meet these criteria and was not made a member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> After the formation of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in 1947, it was given <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Test_status" title="Test status">Test status</a> in 1952, becoming the seventh Test-playing nation. In May 1961 South Africa left the Commonwealth and therefore lost membership.</p>
<p>In 1965, it was renamed as the <i>International Cricket Conference</i> and new rules adopted to permit the election of countries from outside the Commonwealth. This led to the expansion of the Conference, with the admission of Associate Members. Associates were each entitled to one vote, while the Foundation and Full Members were entitled to two votes on ICC resolutions. Foundation Members retained a right of veto.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sri_Lankan_cricket_team" title="Sri Lankan cricket team">Sri Lanka</a> was admitted as a Full Member in 1981, returning the number of Test-playing nations to seven. In 1989, new rules were adopted and the current name, the <i>International Cricket Council</i> came into existence. South Africa was re-elected as a Full Member of the ICC in 1991, after the end of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>; this was followed in 1992 by the admission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zimbabwean_cricket_team" title="Zimbabwean cricket team">Zimbabwe</a> as the ninth Test-playing nation. Then, in the year 2000 <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> received test status.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ICC_Dubai_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1453" data-file-width="1766" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ICC_Dubai_1.jpg/200px-ICC_Dubai_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ICC_Dubai_1.jpg/300px-ICC_Dubai_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ICC_Dubai_1.jpg/400px-ICC_Dubai_1.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ICC_Dubai_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ICC's offices in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From its formation the ICC had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Cricket_Ground" title="Lord's Cricket Ground">Lord's Cricket Ground</a> as its home, and from 1993 had its offices in the "Clock Tower" building at the nursery end of the ground. The independent ICC was funded initially by commercial exploitation of the rights to the World Cup of One Day International cricket. As not all Member countries had double-tax agreements with the United Kingdom, it was necessary to protect cricket's revenues by creating a company, ICC Development (International) Pty Ltd – known as IDI, outside the UK. This was established in January 1994 and was based in Monaco.</p>
<p>For the remainder of the nineties, the administration of IDI was a modest affair. But with the negotiation of a bundle of rights to all ICC events from 2001–2008, revenues available to International cricket and the ICC member countries rose substantially. This led to a growth in the number of commercial staff employed by IDI in Monaco. It also had the disadvantage that the Council's cricket administrators, who remained at Lord's, were separated from their commercial colleagues in Monaco. The Council decided to seek ways of bringing all of their staff together in one office while protecting their commercial income from tax.</p>
<p>The option of staying at Lord's was investigated and a request was made, through <a href="/wiki/Sport_England" title="Sport England">Sport England</a>, to the British Government to allow the ICC to have all its personnel (including those working on commercial matters) in London – but be given special exemption from paying UK <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporation_tax" title="Corporation tax">corporation tax</a> on its commercial income. The British Government was unwilling to create a precedent and would not agree to this request. As a consequence the ICC examined other locations and eventually settled on the emirate of <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>. ICC is registered in <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>. In August 2005 the ICC moved its offices to <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, and subsequently closed its offices at Lord's and Monaco. The move to Dubai was made after an 11–1 vote by the ICC's Executive Board in favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the principal driver of the ICC's move to <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> was the wish to bring its main employees together in one tax efficient location, a secondary reason was the wish to move offices closer to the increasingly important new centres of cricketing power in South Asia. <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s" title="Lord's">Lord's</a> had been a logical venue when the ICC had been administered by the <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Cricket_Club" title="Marylebone Cricket Club">MCC</a> (a situation that lasted until 1993). But the growing power of India and Pakistan in world cricket had made the continued control of international cricket by a British private members club (the <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Cricket_Club" title="Marylebone Cricket Club">MCC</a>) anachronistic and unsustainable. A direct consequence of the changes and reforms instituted in 1993 was eventually to be the move away from Lord's to a more neutral venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules_and_regulation">Rules and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rules and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The International Cricket Council overlooks playing conditions, bowling reviews, and other ICC regulations. Even though the ICC doesn't have copyright to the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_cricket" title="Laws of cricket">laws of cricket</a> and only the <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Cricket_Club" title="Marylebone Cricket Club">MCC</a> may change the laws, nowadays this would usually only be done after discussions with the game's global governing body, the ICC. The ICC also has a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Code_of_Conduct" title="ICC Code of Conduct">Code of Conduct</a>" to which teams and players in international matches are required to adhere. Where breaches of this code occur the ICC can apply sanctions, usually fines. In 2008 the ICC imposed 19 penalties on players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tournaments_and_income_generation">Tournaments and income generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tournaments and income generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ICC_logo_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="90" data-file-width="160" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a5/ICC_logo_2010.jpg" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ICC_logo_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Variant ICC Logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ICC generates income from the tournaments it organises, primarily the <a href="/wiki/Cricket_World_Cup" title="Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup</a>, and it distributes the majority of that income to its members. Sponsorship and television rights of the World Cup brought in over US$1.6 billion between 2007 and 2015, by far the ICC's main source of income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the nine-month accounting period to 31 December 2007 the ICC had operating income of USD 12.66 million, mainly from member subscriptions and sponsorship. In contrast event income was USD 285.87 million, including USD 239 million from the 2007 World Cup. There was also investment income of USD 6.695 million in the period.</p>
<p>The ICC has no income streams from the bilateral international cricket matches (<a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a>, <a href="/wiki/One_Day_International" title="One Day International">One Day International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twenty20" title="Twenty20">Twenty20 Internationals</a>), that account for the great majority of the international playing schedule, as they are owned and run by its members. It has sought to create other new events to augment its World Cup revenues. These include the <a href="/wiki/ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="ICC Champions Trophy">ICC Champions Trophy</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Super_Series" title="ICC Super Series">ICC Super Series</a> played in Australia in 2005. However these events have not been as successful as the ICC hoped. The Super Series was widely seen as a failure and is not expected to be repeated, and India called for the Champions Trophy to be scrapped in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2004_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2004 ICC Champions Trophy">Champions Trophy 2004</a> event was referred to in <i><a href="/wiki/Wisden_Cricketers%27_Almanack" title="Wisden Cricketers' Almanack">Wisden 2005</a></i> by the editor as a "turkey of a tournament" and a "fiasco"; although the <a href="/wiki/2006_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2006 ICC Champions Trophy">2006 edition</a> was seen as a greater success due to a new format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/ICC_World_Twenty20" title="ICC World Twenty20">ICC World Twenty20</a>, first played <a href="/wiki/2007_ICC_World_Twenty20" title="2007 ICC World Twenty20">in 2007</a>, was a success. The ICC's current plan is to have an international tournament every year, with a Twenty20 World Cup played in even number years, the World Cup continuing to be held the year before the <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, and the ICC Champions Trophy in the remaining year of the cycle. This cycle will begin <a href="/wiki/2010_ICC_World_Twenty20" title="2010 ICC World Twenty20">in 2010</a>, one year after the <a href="/wiki/2009_ICC_World_Twenty20" title="2009 ICC World Twenty20">2009 edition</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Umpires_and_referees">Umpires and referees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Umpires and referees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ICC appoints international <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(cricket)" title="Umpire (cricket)">umpires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Match_referee" title="Match referee">Match referees</a> who officiate at all sanctioned Test matches, One-Day Internationals and Twenty20 Internationals. The ICC operates 3 panels of umpires: namely the <a href="/wiki/Elite_Panel_of_ICC_Umpires" title="Elite Panel of ICC Umpires">Elite Panel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Panel_of_Umpires_and_Referees" title="International Panel of Umpires and Referees">International Panel</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Associates_and_Affiliates_umpire_panel" title="ICC Associates and Affiliates umpire panel">Associates and Affiliates Panel</a>.</p>
<p>As of April 2012, the Elite Panel includes twelve umpires. In theory, two umpires from the Elite Panel officiate at every Test match, while one Elite Panel umpire stands in ODI matches together with an umpire from the International Panel. In practice, members of the International Panel stand in occasional Test matches, as this is viewed as a good opportunity to see whether they can cope at the Test level, and whether they should be elevated to the Elite Panel. The Elite Panel are full-time employees of the ICC, although do still, very occasionally umpire <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class cricket</a> in their country of residence. The average, annual, officiating schedule for Elite Umpires is 8–10 Test matches and 10–15 ODIs, a potential on-field workload of 75 days plus travel and preparation time per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The International Panel is made up of officials nominated from each of the ten Test-playing cricket boards. The Panel Members officiate in ODI matches in their home country, and assist the Elite Panel at peak times in the cricket calendar when they can be appointed to overseas ODI and Test matches. International Panel members also undertake overseas umpiring assignments such as the ICC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U/19_Cricket_World_Cup" title="U/19 Cricket World Cup">Under 19 Cricket World Cup</a> to improve their knowledge and understanding of overseas conditions, and help them prepare for possible promotion onto the Elite Panel. Some of these umpires also officiates in the Cricket World Cup. Each of the Test cricket boards nominates a "third umpire" who can be called upon to review certain on-field decisions through instant television replays. All third umpires are first-class umpires in their own county, and the role is seen as a step onto the International Panel, and then the Elite Panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inaugural ICC Associate and Affiliate International Umpires Panel was formed in June 2006. It superseded the ICC Associate and Affiliate International Umpires Panel, created in 2005, and serves as the pinnacle for umpires from non-Test playing Members, with selection achieved through each of the five ICC Development Program Regional Umpires Panels. Members of the Associate and Affiliate International Umpires Panel are eligible for appointments to ODIs involving ICC Associate Members, ICC Intercontinental Cup matches and other Associate and Affiliate tournaments. High-performing umpires may also be considered for other ICC events, including the ICC U/19 Cricket World Cup, and could also be invited to be involved in the ICC Champions Trophy and ICC Cricket World Cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is also an <a href="/wiki/Elite_Panel_of_ICC_Referees" title="Elite Panel of ICC Referees">Elite Panel of ICC Referees</a> who act as the independent representative of the ICC at all Test and ODI matches. As of January 2009, it has 6 members, all highly experienced former international cricketers. The Referees do not have the power to report players or officials (which has to be done by the umpires), but they are responsible for conducting hearings under the ICC Code of Conduct and imposing penalties as required at matches, ranging from an official reprimand to a lifetime ban from cricket. Decisions can be appealed, but the original decision is upheld in most cases.</p>
<p>The Council failed to achieve consensus among the cricket playing nations as of June 2012, on application of Umpire's Decision Review System universally due to opposition by BCCI .It will continue to be applied subject to mutual agreement of the playing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In July 2012, ICC decided to send a delegation to show the ball tracking research done by Dr Ed Rosten, an expert on computer vision and technology, to BCCI to remove the scepticism about the use of DRS technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>The ICC has three classes of membership:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Full Members</b> - the ten governing bodies of teams that play official Test matches;</li>
</ul>
<p>The ten full members are: Australia, Bangladesh, England, India, New Zealand, Pakistan, South Africa, Sri Lanka, West Indies, Zimbabwe</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Associate Members</b> - the 39 governing bodies in countries where cricket is firmly established and organised but have not yet been granted Full membership;</li>
<li><b>Affiliate Members</b> - the 56 governing bodies in countries where the ICC recognises that cricket is played according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The ICC has recently come under significant criticism due to its failure to grant Full membership (and consequentially Test status) to certain deserving countries, primarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ireland_national_cricket_team" title="Ireland national cricket team">Ireland</a>, but also including <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_cricket_team" title="Scotland national cricket team">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan_national_cricket_team" title="Afghanistan national cricket team">Afghanistan</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Despite the mission statement of the ICC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> to be a "leading global sport" and to help in "promoting the global game", Full membership has not been expanded since <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_national_cricket_team" title="Bangladesh national cricket team">Bangladesh</a> was granted Full membership in 2000. Also, affiliate members have even less opportunities to be granted into an associate membership. Both affiliate members and associate members compete against each other in the qualifying stage of <a href="/wiki/ICC_World_Twenty20" title="ICC World Twenty20">ICC World T20</a> for the 6 qualifying spots of the group stage, whereas 8 out of 10 full members automatically qualify for Super 10.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-corruption_and_security">Anti-corruption and security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Anti-corruption and security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ICC has also had to deal with drugs and <a href="/wiki/Betting_controversies_in_cricket" title="Betting controversies in cricket">bribery scandals</a> involving top cricketers. Following the corruption scandals by cricketers connected with the legal and illegal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bookmaking" title="Bookmaking">bookmaking</a> markets, the ICC set up an Anti-Corruption and Security Unit (ACSU) in 2000 under the retired <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Police_of_the_Metropolis" title="Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis">Commissioner</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Metropolitan_Police" title="London Metropolitan Police">London Metropolitan Police</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Condon,_Baron_Condon" title="Paul Condon, Baron Condon">Lord Condon</a>. Among the corruption on which they have reported was that of former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_African_national_cricket_captains" title="South African national cricket captains">South African captain</a> <a href="/wiki/Hansie_Cronje" title="Hansie Cronje">Hansie Cronje</a> who had accepted substantial sums of money from an Indian bookmaker for under-performing or ensuring that certain matches had a pre-determined result. Similarly, the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_national_cricket_captains" title="Indian national cricket captains">Indian captain</a> <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Azharuddin" title="Mohammad Azharuddin">Mohammad Azharuddin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ajay_Jadeja" title="Ajay Jadeja">Ajay Jadeja</a> were investigated, found guilty of match-fixing, and banned from playing cricket (for life and for five years, respectively). The ACSU continues to monitor and investigate any reports of corruption in cricket and protocols have been introduced, which for example prohibit the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_telephone" title="Mobile telephone">mobile telephones</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changeroom" title="Changeroom">dressing rooms</a>.</p>
<p>Prior to the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Cricket_World_Cup" title="Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup</a> ICC chief executive <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Speed" title="Malcolm Speed">Malcolm Speed</a> warned against any corruption and said that the ICC would be vigilant and intolerant against it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_cricket_spot-fixing_scandal" title="Pakistan cricket spot-fixing scandal">scandal that occurred</a> during the 2010 Pakistan tour of England, 3 Pakistani players, <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Amir" title="Mohammad Amir">Mohammad Amir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Asif" title="Mohammad Asif">Mohammad Asif</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salman_Butt" title="Salman Butt">Salman Butt</a> were found to be guilty of spot-fixing, and were banned for 5 years, 7 years and 10 years respectively. On 3 November 2011, jail terms were handed down of 30 months for Butt, one year for Asif, six months for Amir and two years eight months for Majeed, the sports agent that facilitated the bribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Cricket_Academy">Global Cricket Academy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global Cricket Academy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICC_Global_Cricket_Academy" title="ICC Global Cricket Academy">ICC Global Cricket Academy</a> (GCA) is located at <a href="/wiki/Dubai_Sports_City" title="Dubai Sports City">Dubai Sports City</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>. The GCA's facilities include two ovals, each with 10 turf pitches, outdoor turf and synthetic practice facilities, indoor practice facilities including hawk eye technology and a cricket specific gymnasium. <a href="/wiki/Rod_Marsh" title="Rod Marsh">Rod Marsh</a> has been appointed as the Academy's Director of Coaching. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cricket_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Journalist Peter Della Penna, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESPN_Cricinfo" title="ESPN Cricinfo">ESPN Cricinfo</a>, has criticised the ICC for what he has perceived as attempts to minimise reports of security issues relating to unruly fans at matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cricket_Ireland" title="Cricket Ireland">Cricket Ireland</a>'s chief executive Warren Deutrom has criticised them for double standards between Test-playing and Associate nations and the lack of clearly defined criteria allowing new countries to achieve <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test status</a>, or for that matter: Affiliate members to achieve Associate status.</p>
<p>In 2015, Sam Collins and <a href="/wiki/Jarrod_Kimber" title="Jarrod Kimber">Jarrod Kimber</a> made the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Death_of_a_Gentleman" title="Death of a Gentleman">Death of a Gentleman</a></i> on the internal organization of the ICC.</p>
