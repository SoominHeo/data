<p>The <i><b>Fédération Internationale d'Escrime</b></i> (English: <b>International Fencing Federation</b>), commonly known by the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> <b>FIE</b>, is the international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" title="Sport governing body">governing body</a> of <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">fencing</a>. Today, its head office is at the <a href="/wiki/Maison_du_Sport_International" title="Maison du Sport International">Maison du Sport International</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, Switzerland. The FIE is composed of 145 national federations, each of which is recognized by its country's <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee" title="National Olympic Committee">Olympic Committee</a> as the sole representative of Olympic-style fencing in that country.</p>
<p>Since its inception in 1913, there have been fourteen different presidents. The current <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a> of the federation is <a href="/wiki/Alisher_Usmanov" title="Alisher Usmanov">Alisher Usmanov</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_d%27Escrime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6048" data-file-width="4032" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg/220px-Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg/330px-Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg/440px-Allegory_of_fencer_-_V%C3%A1clav_Cesak_-_Lausanne.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Fédération Internationale d'Escrime is the heir of the <span lang="fr">Société d'encouragement de l'escrime</span> founded in France in 1882, which took part in the global movement of structuring sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The first international fencing congress was held in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, Belgium in 1897 at the instigation of the <span lang="fr">Fédération belge des cercles d'escrime</span>, followed by another one in Paris in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At this occasion the <span lang="fr">Société</span> organised one of the first international fencing events; French, Italian, Spanish, and Belgian fencers attended the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Dissensions rapidly arose between <a href="/wiki/%C3%89p%C3%A9e" title="Épée">epeists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foil_(fencing)" title="Foil (fencing)">foilists</a>, which held the majority at the <span lang="fr">Société</span>. The third congress held in Brussels in 1905 voted the creation of an international fencing committee whose mission would be of fostering friendship amongst all fencers, establishing national rules, and supporting the organization of fencing competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The 3rd congress also adopted the French rules as the basis for upcoming international competitions. New tensions appeared, this time between France and Italy, about the regulatory weapon grip. They led to the boycott by France of the fencing events of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1912_Olympic_Games" title="1912 Olympic Games">1912 Olympic Games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A new international congress was called together in <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, Belgium, in July 1913. The main matter was the adoption of international regulations for each of the three weapons. The French rules were adopted in épée and foil; the Hungarian rules were chosen for <a href="/wiki/Sabre" title="Sabre">sabre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Frenchman René Lacroix also campaigned for the creation of an international fencing federation.</p>
<p>The Fédération Internationale d'Escrime was founded on November 29, 1913, in the conference rooms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile_Club_de_France" title="Automobile Club de France">Automobile Club de France</a> in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The nine founding nations were Belgium, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> (now the Czech Republic), France, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, Hungary, Italy, the Netherlands, and Norway. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Feyerick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Albert Feyerick (page does not exist)">Albert Feyerick</a>, president of the Federation of fencing clubs of Belgium, was elected as the first president. The FIE held its first congress on June 23, 1914 and accepted the adhesion of seven new countries: Austria, Denmark, Monaco, Romania, Russia, Switzerland, and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_d%27Escrime&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Competitions organized by the FIE include the senior <a href="/wiki/World_Fencing_Championships" title="World Fencing Championships">World Championships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fencing_World_Cup" title="Fencing World Cup">World Cup</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Junior_World_Fencing_Championships" title="Junior World Fencing Championships">Junior World Championships</a> and Junior World Cup, the Cadets World Championships and the Veterans World Championships. The FIE delegates to regional confederations the organization of the zone championships.</p>
<p>The FIE assists the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> in the organization of <a href="/wiki/Fencing_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Fencing at the Summer Olympics">fencing events at the Summer Olympics</a>. The number of events is a matter of contention between the FIE and the CIO since the introduction of women's <a href="/wiki/Sabre_(fencing)" title="Sabre (fencing)">sabre</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1999_World_Fencing_Championships" title="1999 World Fencing Championships">1999 World Championships</a>: since then, the World Championships feature twelve events–an individual and a team weapon for each of the three weapons, for men and for women. However, the CIO refuses to increase the number of Olympic medals allocated to fencing. After much dithering the FIE decided to organize all six individual events, but only four team events decided on a rotational basis. The two team events excluded from the Olympic programme, one for men and one for women, compete instead in World championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
