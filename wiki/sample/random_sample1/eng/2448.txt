<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">International human rights law</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>International human rights law</b> is the body of international law designed to promote human rights on social, regional, and domestic levels. As a form of international law, international human rights law is primarily made up of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaties" title="Treaties">treaties</a>, agreements between Sovereign state|state sintered to have binding legal effect between the parties that have agreed to them; and <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a>, rules of law derived from the consistent conduct of states acting out of the belief that had the law required them to act that way. Other <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a> while not legally binding contribute to the implementation, understanding and development of international human rights law and have been recognised as a source of <i>political</i> obligation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Enforcement of international human rights law can occur on either a domestic, a regional or an international level. States that ratify human rights treaties commit themselves to respecting those rights and ensuring that their domestic law is compatible with international legislation. When domestic law fails to provide a remedy for human rights abuses, parties may be able to resort to regional or international mechanisms for enforcing human rights.</p>
<p>The relationship between international human rights law and humanitarian law is disputed among international law scholars. This discussion forms part of a larger discussion on fragmentation of international law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While pluralist scholars conceive international human rights law as being distinct from international humanitarian law, proponents of the constitutionalist approach regard the latter as a subset of the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In a nutshell, those who favors separate, self-contained regimes emphasize the differences in applicability; international humanitarian law applies only during armed conflict. On the other hand, a more systemic perspective explains that international humanitarian law represents a function of international human rights law; it includes general norms that apply to everyone at all time as well as specialized norms which apply to certain situations such as armed conflict between both state and military occupation (i.e. IHL) or to certain groups of people including refugees (e.g. the 1951 Refugee Convention), children (the Convention on the Rights of the Child), and prisoners of war (the 1949 Geneva Convention III).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In primitive societies, organisation was based on communalism<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The emergence of states saw the organisation and distribution of power based on law. With this came the growth of "rights" and evolving notions of what they constitute, and eventually the development of "human rights law."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The emergence of the State is a crucial development in the evolution of human rights precisely because so many rights, if not most of them, are state-centred. The State is a bearer of duties in respect of individual people, who</p>
<ul>
<li>depend on it for the protection of their rights;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and</li>
<li>are entitled to claim against it for violations of those rights.</li>
</ul>
<p>Human rights law provides the tools and mechanisms with which these protections and claims may be realised.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_system">United Nations system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United Nations system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_the_United_Nations" title="General Assembly of the United Nations">General Assembly of the United Nations</a> adopted the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Declaration_and_Programme_of_Action" title="Vienna Declaration and Programme of Action">Vienna Declaration and Programme of Action</a> in 1993, in terms of which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" title="United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights">United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights</a> was established.</p>
<p>In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Commission on Human Rights">United Nations Commission on Human Rights</a> was replaced with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council" title="United Nations Human Rights Council">United Nations Human Rights Council</a> for the enforcement of international human rights law.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Bill_of_Human_Rights">International Bill of Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International Bill of Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Universal Declaration of Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> (UDHR) is a UN General Assembly declaration that does not in form create binding international human rights law. Many legal scholars cite the UDHR as evidence of customary international law.</p>
<p>More broadly, the UDHR has become an authoritative human rights reference. It has provided the basis for subsequent <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a> that form non-binding, but ultimately authoritative international human rights law.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_human_rights_treaties">International human rights treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International human rights treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>

<p>Besides the adoption in 1966 of the two wide-ranging Covenants that form part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Bill_of_Human_Rights" title="International Bill of Human Rights">International Bill of Human Rights</a> (namely the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a>), a number of other treaties have been adopted at the international level. These are generally known as <i>human rights instruments</i>. Some of the most significant include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Prevention_and_Punishment_of_the_Crime_of_Genocide" title="Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide">Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide</a> (<b>CPCG</b>) (adopted 1948 and entered into force in 1951);</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees</a> (<b>CSR</b>) (adopted in 1951 and entered into force in 1954);</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Racial_Discrimination" title="Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination">Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination</a> (<b>CERD</b>) (adopted in 1965 and entered into force in 1969);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_Against_Women" title="Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women">Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women</a> (<b>CEDAW</b>) (entered into force in 1981);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_Against_Torture" title="United Nations Convention Against Torture">United Nations Convention Against Torture</a> (<b>CAT</b>) (adopted in 1984 and entered into force in 1987);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> (<b>CRC</b>) (adopted in 1989 and entered into force in 1990);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Protection_of_the_Rights_of_All_Migrant_Workers_and_Members_of_Their_Families" title="United Nations Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families">International Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of their Families</a> (<b>ICRMW</b>) (adopted in 1990 and entered into force in 2003);</li>
<li>the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_on_the_Rights_of_People_with_Disabilities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Convention on the Rights of People with Disabilities (page does not exist)">Convention on the Rights of People with Disabilities</a>(<b>CRPD</b>) (entered into force on 3 May 2008);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_All_People_from_Enforced_Disappearance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Convention for the Protection of All People from Enforced Disappearance (page does not exist)">International Convention for the Protection of All People from Enforced Disappearance</a> (adopted in 2006 and entered into force in 2010).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_protection_and_institutions">Regional protection and institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regional protection and institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regional systems of international human rights law supplement and complement national and international human rights law by protecting and promoting human rights in specific areas of the world. There are three key regional human rights instruments which have established human rights law on a regional basis:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> for Africa of 1981, in force since 1986;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a> for the Americas of 1969, in force since 1978; and</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> for Europe of 1950, in force since 1953.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas_and_Europe">Americas and Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Americas and Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_of_American_States" title="Organisation of American States">Organisation of American States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, like the UN, have adopted treaties (albeit with weaker implementation mechanisms) containing catalogues of <a href="/wiki/Economic,_social_and_cultural_rights" title="Economic, social and cultural rights">economic, social and cultural rights</a>, in addition to the aforementioned conventions dealing mostly with <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/European_Social_Charter" title="European Social Charter">European Social Charter</a> for Europe of 1961, in force since 1965 (whose complaints mechanism, created in 1995 under an Additional Protocol, has been in force since 1998); and</li>
<li>the Protocol of San Salvador to the ACHR for the Americas of 1988, in force since 1999.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Africa" title="Human rights in Africa">Human rights in Africa</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> (AU) is a supranational union consisting of 53 African countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Established in 2001, the AU's purpose is to help secure Africa's democracy, human rights, and a sustainable economy, in particular by bringing an end to intra-African conflict and creating an effective common market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> is the region's principal human rights instrument. It emerged under the aegis of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity" title="Organisation of African Unity">Organisation of African Unity</a> (OAU) (since replaced by the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>). The intention to draw up the <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> was announced in 1979. The Charter was unanimously approved at the OAU's 1981 Assembly.</p>
<p>Pursuant to Article 63 (whereby it was to "come into force three months after the reception by the Secretary General of the instruments of ratification or adherence of a simple majority" of the OAU's member states), the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights came into effect on 21 October 1986, in honour of which 21 October was declared <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=African_Human_Rights_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="African Human Rights Day (page does not exist)">African Human Rights Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/African_Commission_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> (ACHPR) is a quasi-judicial organ of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>, tasked with promoting and protecting human rights and collective (peoples') rights throughout the African continent, as well as with interpreting the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights, and considering individual complaints of violations of the Charter. The Commission has three broad areas of responsibility:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mandate_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mandate-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>promoting human and peoples' rights;</li>
<li>protecting human and peoples' rights; and</li>
<li>interpreting the <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>In pursuit of these goals, the Commission is mandated to "collect documents, undertake studies and researches on African problems in the field of human and peoples' rights, organise seminars, symposia and conferences, disseminate information, encourage national and local institutions concerned with human and peoples' rights and, should the case arise, give its views or make recommendations to governments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mandate_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mandate-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the creation of the <a href="/wiki/African_Court_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> (under a protocol to the Charter which was adopted in 1998 and entered into force in January 2004), the Commission will have the additional task of preparing cases for submission to the Court's jurisdiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-court_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-court-14">[14]</a></sup> In a July 2004 decision, the AU Assembly resolved that the future Court on Human and Peoples' Rights would be integrated with the African Court of Justice.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Court_of_Justice" title="African Court of Justice">Court of Justice of the African Union</a> is intended to be the "principal judicial organ of the Union."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although it has not yet been established, it is intended to take over the duties of the African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights, as well as to act as the supreme court of the African Union, interpreting all necessary laws and treaties. The Protocol establishing the African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights entered into force in January 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but its merging with the Court of Justice has delayed its establishment. The Protocol establishing the Court of Justice will come into force when ratified by fifteen countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many countries in Africa accused of human rights violations by the international community and NGOs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inter-American_system">Inter-American system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Inter-American system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Human_rights_in_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Human rights in North America (page does not exist)">Human rights in North America</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Human_rights_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Human rights in South America (page does not exist)">Human rights in South America</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a> (OAS) is an international organization, headquartered in Washington, DC. Its members are the thirty-five independent states of the Americas.</p>
<p>Over the course of the 1990s, with the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the return to democracy in Latin America,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the thrust toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globalisation" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a>, the OAS made major efforts to reinvent itself to fit the new context. Its stated priorities now include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>strengthening democracy;</li>
<li>working for peace;</li>
<li>protecting human rights;</li>
<li>combating corruption;</li>
<li>the rights of indigenous peoples; and</li>
<li>promoting sustainable development.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="Inter-American Commission on Human Rights">Inter-American Commission on Human Rights</a> (IACHR) is an autonomous organ of the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a>, also based in Washington, D.C. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="Inter-American Court of Human Rights">Inter-American Court of Human Rights</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/San_Jos%C3%A9,_Costa_Rica" title="San José, Costa Rica">San José</a>, Costa Rica, it is one of the bodies that comprise the inter-American system for the promotion and protection of human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The IACHR is a permanent body which meets in regular and special sessions several times a year to examine allegations of human rights violations in the hemisphere. Its human rights duties stem from three documents:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_the_Organization_of_American_States" title="Charter of the Organization of American States">OAS Charter</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/American_Declaration_of_the_Rights_and_Duties_of_Man" title="American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man">American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man</a>; and</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>The Inter-American Court of Human Rights was established in 1979 with the purpose of enforcing and interpreting the provisions of the American Convention on Human Rights. Its two main functions are therefore adjudicatory and advisory:</p>
<ul>
<li>Under the former, it hears and rules on the specific cases of human rights violations referred to it.</li>
<li>Under the latter, it issues opinions on matters of legal interpretation brought to its attention by other OAS bodies or member states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Many countries in the Americas, including Colombia, Cuba, Mexico and Venezuela,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have been accused of human rights violations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_system">European system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: European system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Europe" title="Human rights in Europe">Human rights in Europe</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, founded in 1949, is the oldest organisation working for European integration. It is an international organisation with legal personality recognised under public international law, and has observer status at the United Nations. The seat of the Council is in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> in France.</p>
<p>The Council of Europe is responsible for both the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-24">[24]</a></sup> These institutions bind the Council's members to a code of human rights which, although strict, is more lenient than that of the UN Charter on human rights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Council also promotes the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Social_Charter" title="European Social Charter">European Social Charter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Membership is open to all European states which seek <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a>, accept the principle of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>, and are able and willing to guarantee democracy, fundamental human rights and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_(political)" title="Freedom (political)">freedoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Council of Europe is separate from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but the latter is expected to accede to the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. The Council includes all the member states of European Union. The EU also has a separate human rights document, the <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_Fundamental_Rights_of_the_European_Union" title="Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union">Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> has since 1950 defined and guaranteed human rights and fundamental freedoms in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUCourt_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUCourt-28">[28]</a></sup> All 47 member states of the Council of Europe have signed this Convention, and are therefore under the jurisdiction of the European Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUCourt_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-EUCourt-28">[28]</a></sup> In order to prevent torture and inhuman or degrading treatment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_the_Prevention_of_Torture" title="Committee for the Prevention of Torture">Committee for the Prevention of Torture</a> was established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Council of Europe also adopted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_Action_against_Trafficking_in_Human_Beings" title="Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings">Convention on Action against Trafficking in Human Beings</a> in May 2005, for protection against <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_slavery" title="Sexual slavery">sexual exploitation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe_Convention_on_the_Protection_of_Children_against_Sexual_Exploitation_and_Sexual_Abuse" title="Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse">Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse</a> in October 2007, and the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_preventing_and_combating_violence_against_women_and_domestic_violence" title="Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence">Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence</a> in May 2011.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> is the only international court with jurisdiction to deal with cases brought by individuals rather than states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUCourt_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-EUCourt-28">[28]</a></sup> In early 2010, the court had a backlog of over 120,000 cases and a multi-year waiting list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> About one out of every twenty cases submitted to the court is considered admissible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2007, the court issued 1,503 verdicts. At the current rate of proceedings, it would take 46 years for the backlog to clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring.2C_implementation_and_enforcement">Monitoring, implementation and enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Monitoring, implementation and enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There is currently no international court to administer international human rights law, but quasi-judicial bodies exist under some UN treaties (like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Committee" title="Human Rights Committee">Human Rights Committee</a> under the ICCPR). The <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a> (ICC) has jurisdiction over the crime of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a>. The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="Inter-American Court of Human Rights">Inter-American Court of Human Rights</a> enforce regional human rights law.</p>
<p>Although these same international bodies also hold jurisdiction over cases regarding international humanitarian law, it is crucial to recognise, as discussed above, that the two frameworks constitute different legal regimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United Nations human rights bodies do have some quasi-legal enforcement mechanisms. These include the treaty bodies attached to the seven currently active treaties, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council" title="United Nations Human Rights Council">United Nations Human Rights Council</a> complaints procedures, with <a href="/wiki/Universal_Periodic_Review" title="Universal Periodic Review">Universal Periodic Review</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Special_Rapporteur" title="United Nations Special Rapporteur">United Nations Special Rapporteur</a> (known as the 1235 and 1503 mechanisms respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The enforcement of international human rights law is the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a>; it is the primary responsibility of the State to make the human rights of its citizens a reality.</p>
<p>In practice, many human rights are difficult to enforce legally, due to the absence of consensus on the application of certain rights, the lack of relevant national legislation or of bodies empowered to take legal action to enforce them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In over 110 countries, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_human_rights_institutions" title="National human rights institutions">national human rights institutions</a> (NHRIs) have been set up to protect, promote or monitor human rights with jurisdiction in a given country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Although not all NHRIs are compliant with the Paris Principles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> the number and effect of these institutions is increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paris_Principles" title="Paris Principles">Paris Principles</a> were defined at the first International Workshop on National Institutions for the Promotion and Protection of Human Rights in Paris from 7 to 9 October 1991, and adopted by UN Human Rights Commission Resolution 1992/54 of 1992 and General Assembly Resolution 48/134 of 1993. The Paris Principles list a number of responsibilities for national institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universal_jurisdiction">Universal jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_human_rights_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Universal jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Universal_jurisdiction" title="Universal jurisdiction">universal jurisdiction</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Universal_jurisdiction" title="Universal jurisdiction">Universal jurisdiction</a> is a controversial principle in international law, whereby states claim criminal jurisdiction over people whose alleged crimes were committed outside the boundaries of the prosecuting state, regardless of nationality, country of residence or any other relationship to the prosecuting country. The state backs its claim on the grounds that the crime committed is considered a crime against all, which any state is authorised to punish. The concept of universal jurisdiction is therefore closely linked to the idea that certain international norms are <i><a href="/wiki/Erga_omnes" title="Erga omnes">erga omnes</a></i>, or owed to the entire world community, as well as the concept of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jus_cogens" title="Jus cogens">jus cogens</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1993, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> passed a "law of universal jurisdiction" to give its courts jurisdiction over crimes against humanity in other countries. In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a> was arrested in London following an indictment by Spanish judge <a href="/wiki/Baltasar_Garz%C3%B3n" title="Baltasar Garzón">Baltasar Garzón</a> under the universal-jurisdiction principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The principle is supported by <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> and other <a href="/wiki/List_of_human_rights_organisations" title="List of human rights organisations">human rights organisations</a>, which believe that certain crimes pose a threat to the international community as a whole, and that the community has a moral duty to act.</p>
<p>Others, like <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> argue that "widespread agreement that human rights violations and crimes against humanity must be prosecuted has hindered active consideration of the proper role of international courts. Universal jurisdiction risks creating universal tyranny—that of judges".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
