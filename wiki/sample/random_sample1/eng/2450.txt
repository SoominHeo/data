<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Group cohomology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about homology and cohomology <i>of</i> a group. For homology or cohomology groups of a space or other object, see <a href="/wiki/Homology_(mathematics)" title="Homology (mathematics)">Homology (mathematics)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <b>group cohomology</b> is a set of mathematical tools used to study <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cohomology_theory" title="Cohomology theory">cohomology theory</a>, a technique from <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>. Analogous to <a href="/wiki/Group_representation" title="Group representation">group representations</a>, group cohomology looks at the <a href="/wiki/Group_action" title="Group action">group actions</a> of a group <i>G</i> in an associated <a href="/wiki/G-module" title="G-module"><i>G</i>-module</a> <i>M</i> to elucidate the properties of the group. By treating the <i>G</i>-module as a kind of topological space with elements of <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

The cohomology groups in turn provide insight into the structure of the group <i>G</i> and <i>G</i>-module <i>M</i> themselves. Group cohomology plays a role in the investigation of fixed points of a group action in a module or space and the <a href="/wiki/Quotient_module" title="Quotient module">quotient module</a> or space with respect to a group action. Group cohomology is used in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">algebraic number theory</a>, as well as in applications to <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> proper. As in algebraic topology, there is a dual theory called <a href="/wiki/Group_cohomology#Group_homology" title="Group cohomology"><i>group homology</i></a>. The techniques of group cohomology can also be extended to the case that instead of a <i>G</i>-module, <i>G</i> acts on a nonabelian <i>G</i>-group; in effect, a generalization of a module to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-Abelian_group" title="Non-Abelian group">non-Abelian</a> coefficients.</p>
<p>These algebraic ideas are closely related to topological ideas. The group cohomology of a discrete group <i>G</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singular_cohomology" title="Singular cohomology">singular cohomology</a> of a suitable space having <i>G</i> as its <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_group" title="Fundamental group">fundamental group</a>, namely the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93MacLane_space" title="Eilenberg–MacLane space">Eilenberg–MacLane space</a>. Thus, the group cohomology of <b>Z</b> can be thought of as the singular cohomology of the circle <b>S</b><sup>1</sup>, and similarly for <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b> and <b>P</b><sup>∞</sup>(<b>R</b>).</p>
<p>A great deal is known about the cohomology of groups, including interpretations of low-dimensional cohomology, functoriality, and how to change groups. The subject of group cohomology began in the 1920s, matured in the late 1940s, and continues as an area of active research today.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A general paradigm in <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> is that a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a> <i>G</i> should be studied via its <a href="/wiki/Group_representation" title="Group representation">group representations</a>. A slight generalization of those representations are the <a href="/wiki/G-module" title="G-module"><i>G</i>-modules</a>: a <i>G</i>-module is an <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian group</a> <i>M</i> together with a <a href="/wiki/Group_action" title="Group action">group action</a> of <i>G</i> on <i>M</i>, with every element of <i>G</i> acting as an <a href="/wiki/Automorphism" title="Automorphism">automorphism</a> of <i>M</i>. We will write <i>G</i> multiplicatively and <i>M</i> additively.</p>

<p>Now, if <i>N</i> is a <i>G</i>-submodule of <i>M</i> (i.e. a subgroup of <i>M</i> mapped to itself by the action of <i>G</i>), it isn't in general true that the invariants in <i>M/N</i> are found as the quotient of the invariants in <i>M</i> by those in <i>N</i>: being invariant 'modulo <i>N</i> ' is broader. The purpose of the first group cohomology <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>G</i>,<i>N</i>) is to precisely measure this difference.</p>
<p>The group cohomology functors <i>H*</i> in general measure the extent to which taking invariants doesn't respect <a href="/wiki/Exact_sequence" title="Exact sequence">exact sequences</a>. This is expressed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_exact_sequence" title="Long exact sequence">long exact sequence</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The collection of all <i>G</i>-modules is a <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category</a> (the morphisms are group homomorphisms <i>f</i> with the property <i>f</i>(<i>gx</i>) = <i>g</i>(<i>f</i>(<i>x</i>)) for all <i>g</i> in <i>G</i> and <i>x</i> in <i>M</i>). Sending each module <i>M</i> to the group of invariants <i>M<sup>G</sup></i> yields a <a href="/wiki/Functor" title="Functor">functor</a> from the category of <i>G</i>-modules to the category <b>Ab</b> of abelian groups. This functor is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_exact_functor" title="Left exact functor">left exact</a> but not necessarily right exact. We may therefore form its right <a href="/wiki/Derived_functor" title="Derived functor">derived functors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Their values are abelian groups and they are denoted by <i>H<sup>n</sup></i>(<i>G</i>, <i>M</i>), "the <i>n</i>-th cohomology group of <i>G</i> with coefficients in <i>M</i>". <i>H</i><sup>0</sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>M</i>) is identified with <i>M<sup>G</sup></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Group_homology">Group homology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Group homology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Note that the superscript/subscript convention for cohomology/homology agrees with the convention for group invariants/coinvariants, while which is denoted "co-" switches:</p>
<ul>
<li>superscripts correspond to cohomology <i>H*</i> and invariants <i>X<sup>G</sup></i> while</li>
<li>subscripts correspond to homology <i>H</i><sub>∗</sub> and coinvariants <i>X<sub>G</sub></i> := <i>X</i>/<i>G</i>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-dimensional_cohomology_groups">Low-dimensional cohomology groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Low-dimensional cohomology groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="H1"><i>H</i><sup>1</sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: H1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first cohomology group is the quotient of the so-called <i>crossed homomorphisms</i>, i.e. maps (of sets) <i>f</i> : <i>G</i> → <i>M</i> satisfying <i>f</i>(<i>ab</i>) = <i>f</i>(<i>a</i>) + <i>af</i>(<i>b</i>) for all <i>a</i>, <i>b</i> in <i>G</i>, modulo the so-called <i>principal crossed homomorphisms</i>, i.e. maps <i>f</i> : <i>G</i> → <i>M</i> given by <i>f</i>(<i>a</i>) = <i>am</i>−<i>m</i> for some fixed <i>m</i> ∈ <i>M</i>. This follows from the definition of cochains above.</p>
<p>If the action of <i>G</i> on <i>M</i> is trivial, then the above boils down to <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>(<i>G</i>,<i>M</i>) = Hom(<i>G</i>, <i>M</i>), the group of <a href="/wiki/Group_homomorphism" title="Group homomorphism">group homomorphisms</a> <i>G</i> → <i>M</i>.</p>

The so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connecting_homomorphism" title="Connecting homomorphism">connecting homomorphisms</a> δ<sup><i>n</i></sup> : <i>H</i><sup>n</sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>N</i>) → <i>H</i><sup>n+1</sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>L</i>) can be described in terms of inhomogeneous cochains as follows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> If <i>c</i> is an element of <i>H</i><sup>n</sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>N</i>) represented by an <i>n</i>-cocycle φ : <i>G</i><sup>n</sup> → N, then δ<sup>n</sup>(<i>c</i>) is represented by <i>d</i><sup>n</sup>(ψ), where ψ is an <i>n</i>-cochain <i>G</i><sup>n</sup> → M "lifting" φ (i.e. such that φ is the composition of ψ with the surjective map <i>M</i> → <i>N</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functoriality">Functoriality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Functoriality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hochschild%E2%80%93Serre_spectral_sequence" title="Hochschild–Serre spectral sequence">Hochschild–Serre spectral sequence</a> relates the cohomology of a normal subgroup <i>N</i> of <i>G</i> and the quotient <i>G/N</i> to the cohomology of the group <i>G</i> (for (pro-)finite groups <i>G</i>). <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohomology_of_finite_groups">Cohomology of finite groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cohomology of finite groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_cohomology_groups_are_torsion">Higher cohomology groups are torsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Higher cohomology groups are torsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cohomology groups <i>H</i><sup><i>n</i></sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>M</i>) of finite groups <i>G</i> are all torsion for all <i>n</i>≥1. Indeed, by <a href="/wiki/Maschke%27s_theorem" title="Maschke's theorem">Maschke's theorem</a> the category of representations of a finite group is semi-simple over any field of characteristic zero (or more generally, any field whose characteristic does not divide the order of the group), hence, viewing group cohomology as a derived functor in this abelian category, one obtains that it is zero. The other argument is that over a field of characteristic zero, the group algebra of a finite group is a direct sum of matrix algebras (possibly over division algebras which are extensions of the original field), while a matrix algebra is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morita_equivalent" title="Morita equivalent">Morita equivalent</a> to its base field and hence has trivial cohomology.</p>
<p>If the order of G is invertible in a <i>G</i>-module <i>M</i> (for example, if <i>M</i> is a <b>Q</b>-vector space), the transfer map can be used to show that the higher cohomology groups <i>H</i><sup><i>n</i></sup>(<i>G</i>, <i>M</i>) = <i>0</i> for <i>n</i>≥1. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohomology_of_topological_groups">Cohomology of topological groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Cohomology of topological groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_relation_to_other_fields">History and relation to other fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_cohomology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: History and relation to other fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The low-dimensional cohomology of a group was classically studied in other guises, long before the notion of group cohomology was formulated in 1943–45. The first theorem of the subject can be identified as <a href="/wiki/Hilbert%27s_Theorem_90" title="Hilbert's Theorem 90">Hilbert's Theorem 90</a> in 1897; this was recast into <i><a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">Noether</a>'s equations</i> in <a href="/wiki/Galois_theory" title="Galois theory">Galois theory</a> (an appearance of cocycles for <i>H</i><sup>1</sup>). The idea of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factor_set" title="Factor set">factor sets</a></i> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extension_problem" title="Extension problem">extension problem</a> for groups (connected with <i>H</i><sup>2</sup>) arose in the work of <a href="/wiki/Otto_H%C3%B6lder" title="Otto Hölder">Hölder</a> (1893), in <a href="/wiki/Issai_Schur" title="Issai Schur">Issai Schur</a>'s 1904 study of projective representations, in <a href="/wiki/Otto_Schreier" title="Otto Schreier">Schreier</a>'s 1926 treatment, and in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Brauer" title="Richard Brauer">Richard Brauer</a>'s 1928 study of <a href="/wiki/Simple_algebra" title="Simple algebra">simple algebras</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brauer_group" title="Brauer group">Brauer group</a>. A fuller discussion of this history may be found in (<a href="#CITEREFWeibel1999">Weibel 1999</a>, pp. 806–811).</p>
<p>In 1941, while studying <i>H</i><sub>2</sub>(<i>G</i>, <b>Z</b>) (which plays a special role in groups), <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Hopf" title="Heinz Hopf">Hopf</a> discovered what is now called <b>Hopf's integral homology formula</b> (<a href="#CITEREFHopf1942">Hopf 1942</a>), which is identical to Schur's formula for the <a href="/wiki/Schur_multiplier" title="Schur multiplier">Schur multiplier</a> of a finite, finitely presented group:</p>

<p>Hopf's result led to the independent discovery of group cohomology by several groups in 1943-45: <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg" title="Samuel Eilenberg">Eilenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saunders_Mac_Lane" title="Saunders Mac Lane">Mac Lane</a> in the USA (<a href="#CITEREFRotman1995">Rotman 1995</a>, p. 358); Hopf and <a href="/wiki/Beno_Eckmann" title="Beno Eckmann">Eckmann</a> in Switzerland; and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Freudenthal" title="Hans Freudenthal">Freudenthal</a> in the Netherlands (<a href="#CITEREFWeibel1999">Weibel 1999</a>, p. 807). The situation was chaotic because communication between these countries was difficult during World War II.</p>
<p>From a topological point of view, the homology and cohomology of G was first defined as the homology and cohomology of a model for the topological <a href="/wiki/Classifying_space" title="Classifying space">classifying space</a> <i>BG</i> as discussed above. In practice, this meant using topology to produce the chain complexes used in formal algebraic definitions. From a module-theoretic point of view this was integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Henri_Cartan" title="Henri Cartan">Cartan</a>–<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg" title="Samuel Eilenberg">Eilenberg</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a> in the early 1950s.</p>
<p>The application in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">algebraic number theory</a> to <a href="/wiki/Class_field_theory" title="Class field theory">class field theory</a> provided theorems valid for general <a href="/wiki/Galois_extension" title="Galois extension">Galois extensions</a> (not just <a href="/wiki/Abelian_extension" title="Abelian extension">abelian extensions</a>). The cohomological part of class field theory was axiomatized as the theory of <a href="/wiki/Class_formation" title="Class formation">class formations</a>. In turn, this led to the notion of <a href="/wiki/Galois_cohomology" title="Galois cohomology">Galois cohomology</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">étale cohomology</a> (which builds on it) (<a href="#CITEREFWeibel1999">Weibel 1999</a>, p. 822). Some refinements in the theory post-1960 have been made, such as continuous cocycles and <a href="/wiki/John_Tate" title="John Tate">Tate</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tate_cohomology_group" title="Tate cohomology group">redefinition</a>, but the basic outlines remain the same. This is a large field, and now basic in the theories of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_group" title="Algebraic group">algebraic groups</a>.</p>
<p>The analogous theory for <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebras</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra_cohomology" title="Lie algebra cohomology">Lie algebra cohomology</a>, was first developed in the late 1940s, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevalley" title="Chevalley">Chevalley</a>–Eilenberg, and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Koszul" title="Jean-Louis Koszul">Koszul</a> (<a href="#CITEREFWeibel1999">Weibel 1999</a>, p. 810). It is formally similar, using the corresponding definition of <i>invariant</i> for the action of a Lie algebra. It is much applied in <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">representation theory</a>, and is closely connected with the <a href="/wiki/BRST_quantization" title="BRST quantization">BRST quantization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a>.</p>
<p>Group cohomology theory also has a direct application in condensed matter physics. Just like group theory being the mathematical foundation of <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking" title="Spontaneous symmetry breaking">spontaneous symmetry breaking</a> phases, group cohomology theory is the mathematical foundation of a class of quantum states of matter—short-range entangled states with symmetry. Short-range entangled states with symmetry are also known as <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_protected_topological_order" title="Symmetry protected topological order">symmetry protected topological states</a>.</p>
