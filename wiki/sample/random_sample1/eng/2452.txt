<p><b>Gunda Niemann-Stirnemann</b> (née <b>Kleemann</b>, born 7 September 1966) is a former <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating" title="Speed skating">speed skater</a>. She is a three-time Olympic gold medallist, winning the 3000 metres in 1992 and 1998 and the 5000 metres in 1992. She won a total of eight Olympic medals.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunda_Niemann-Stirnemann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born as <b>Gunda Kleemann</b> in <a href="/wiki/Sondershausen" title="Sondershausen">Sondershausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bezirk_Erfurt" title="Bezirk Erfurt">Bezirk Erfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, she has lived in <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> for most of her life. She changed her name to <b>Gunda Niemann</b> after her marriage in 1991 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judoka" title="Judoka">judoka</a> Detlev Niemann. After their divorce in 1995, she kept the name <i>Niemann</i>. She then changed her name to <i>Niemann-Stirnemann</i> after marrying her longtime <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> manager Oliver Stirnemann on 11 July 1997. The speed skating oval in Erfurt (the <i>Gunda-Niemann-Stirnemann-Halle</i>) was named after her. Before the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990, she skated for East Germany.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunda_Niemann-Stirnemann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Niemann-Stirnemann dominated women's speed skating for several years, especially on the longer distances. She has competed in four Olympics, from 1988 to 1998, and won eight Olympic medals (3 gold, 4 silver, and 1 bronze). In the nine years from 1991 to 1999, she won the <a href="/wiki/World_Allround_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="World Allround Speed Skating Championships">World Allround Championships</a> every year except 1994. She has a record number of 98 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speed_Skating_World_Cup" title="Speed Skating World Cup">World Cup</a> single distance victories and has won 19 overall World Cup titles. She was <a href="/wiki/European_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="European Speed Skating Championships">European Allround Champion</a> 8 times. Over the course of her career, she set 18 <a href="/wiki/List_of_world_records_in_speed_skating" title="List of world records in speed skating">world records</a>. For her performances, she received the <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Mathisen_Award" title="Oscar Mathisen Award">Oscar Mathisen Award</a> three times: in 1995, 1996 and 1997.</p>
<p>Niemann-Stirnemann left speed skating in 2001 to give birth to a daughter, but later returned to competition. She planned to make one last comeback and participate in the <a href="/wiki/2006_Winter_Olympics" title="2006 Winter Olympics">2006 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a>, but a lingering back injury – which she suffered from since the 2004–05 season – made her quit. At the end of October 2005, a few days before the German Championships, she announced her retirement.</p>
<p>Except for one day in March 1998, Niemann-Stirnemann was number one in the <a href="/wiki/Adelskalender_(skating)" title="Adelskalender (skating)">Adelskalender</a>, the all-time allround speed skating ranking, from 24 January 1993, until 2 March 2001 – a total of 2,958 days. She skated in Olympic, World, World Cup, European and national championships to 215 medals, thereof 163 gold medals.</p>
