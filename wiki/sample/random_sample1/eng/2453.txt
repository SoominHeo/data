<p>The name <b>army ant</b> (or <b>legionary ant</b> or <i><b>marabunta</b></i>) is applied to over 200 <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> species, in different lineages, due to their aggressive predatory foraging groups, known as "raids", in which huge numbers of ants forage simultaneously over a certain area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another shared feature is that, unlike most ant species, army ants do not construct permanent nests: an army ant colony moves almost incessantly over the time it exists. All species are members of the true ant family, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formicidae" title="Formicidae">Formicidae</a>, but several groups have independently evolved the same basic behavioral and ecological syndrome. This syndrome is often referred to as "legionary behavior", and may be an example of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[n 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> army ants belong to the subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitoninae" title="Ecitoninae">Ecitoninae</a>, which contains two <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribes</a>: Cheliomyrmecini and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitonini" title="Ecitonini">Ecitonini</a>. The former contains only the genus <i>Cheliomyrmex</i>, whereas the latter contains four genera: <i><a href="/wiki/Neivamyrmex" title="Neivamyrmex">Neivamyrmex</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nomamyrmex" title="Nomamyrmex">Nomamyrmex</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Labidus" title="Labidus">Labidus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eciton" title="Eciton">Eciton</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup> The largest genus is <i>Neivamyrmex</i>, which contains more than 120 species; the most predominant species is <i><a href="/wiki/Eciton_burchellii" title="Eciton burchellii">Eciton burchellii</a></i>; its common name "army ant" is considered to be the archetype of the species. <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> army ants are divided between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aenictini" title="Aenictini">Aenictini</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorylini" title="Dorylini">Dorylini</a> tribes. Aenictini contains more than 50 species of army ants in the single genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Aenictus" title="Aenictus">Aenictus</a></i>. However, the Dorylini contain the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Dorylus" title="Dorylus">Dorylus</a></i>, the most aggressive group of driver ants; 60 species are known.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Originally, the Old World and New World lineages of army ants were thought to have evolved independently, in an example of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>. In 2003, though, genetic analysis of various species suggests that they all evolved from a single common ancestor, which lived approximately 100 million years ago at the time of the separation of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">the continents of Africa and South America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup> Army ant taxonomy remains in flux, and genetic analysis will likely continue to provide more information about the relatedness of the various taxa.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Workers">Workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The workers of army ants are usually blind or can have compound eyes that are reduced to a single lens. There are species of army ants where the worker caste may show <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">polymorphism</a> based on physical differences and job allocations; however, there are also species that show no polymorphism at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> The worker caste is usually composed of sterile female worker ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourke.2C_Andrew_F._G._1995_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bourke.2C_Andrew_F._G._1995-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soldiers">Soldiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soldiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The soldiers of army ants are larger than the workers, and they have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandibles" title="Mandibles">mandibles</a>, with older workers possessing larger heads and stronger mandibles than the younger ones. They protect the colony, and help carry the heaviest loads of prey to the nest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Males">Males</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Males">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Males are large in size and have a large cylindrical abdomen, highly modified mandibles and uncommon genitalia not seen in other ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trager.2C_James_C._1988_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trager.2C_James_C._1988-6">[5]</a></sup> They have 13 segments on their antennae, are <a href="/wiki/Alate" title="Alate">alated</a> (have wings) and therefore can resemble <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> Males are born as part of a sexual brood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trager.2C_James_C._1988_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trager.2C_James_C._1988-6">[5]</a></sup> As soon as they are born, they will fly off in search of a queen to mate with. In some instances where males seek to mate with a queen from an existing colony, the receiving workers will forcibly remove the wings in order to accommodate the large males into the colony for mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> Because of their size, males are sometimes called "sausage flies" or "sausage ants."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen">Queen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Queen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Colonies of real army ants always have only one queen, while some other ant species can have several queens. The queen is dichthadiigyne (a blind ant with large <a href="/wiki/Gaster_(insect_anatomy)" title="Gaster (insect anatomy)">gaster</a>) but may sometimes possess vestigial eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> The queens of army ants are unique in that they do not have wings, have an enlarged gaster size and an extended cylindrical abdomen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> They are significantly larger than worker army ants and possess 10–12 segments on their antennae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> Queens will mate with multiple males and because of their enlarged gaster, can produce 3 to 4 million eggs a month, resulting in synchronized brood cycles and colonies composed of millions of individuals all related to a single queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2004-8">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Army_ant_syndrome">Army ant syndrome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Army ant syndrome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The army ant syndrome refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral" title="Behavioral">behavioral</a> and reproductive traits such as obligate collective foraging, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadism" title="Nomadism">nomadism</a> and highly specialized queens that allow these organisms to become the most ferocious social hunters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most ant species will send individual scouts to find food sources and later recruit others from the colony to help; however, army ants dispatch a cooperative, leaderless group of foragers to detect and overwhelm the prey at once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> Army ants do not have a permanent nest but instead form many <a href="/wiki/Bivouac_(ants)" title="Bivouac (ants)">bivouacs</a> as they travel. The constant traveling is due to the need to hunt large amounts of prey to feed its enormous colony <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup> Their queens are wingless and have abdomens that expand significantly during egg production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> This allows for the production of 3-4 million eggs every month and often results in synchronized brood cycles, thus each colony will be formed of millions of individuals that descend from a single queen. All three of these traits are found to be in all army ant species and the defining traits of army ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brady2003_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-brady2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson.26H.C3.B6lldobler_2005_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson.26H.C3.B6lldobler_2005-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomadic_and_stationary_phase">Nomadic and stationary phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nomadic and stationary phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Army ants have two phases of activity—a nomadic (wandering) phase and a stationary (statary) phase—that constantly cycle, and can be found throughout all army ants species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nomadic phase begins around 10 days after the queen lays her eggs. This phase will last approximately 15 days to let the larvae develop. The ants move during the day, capturing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insects" title="Insects">insects</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiders" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, and small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a> to feed their brood. At dusk, they will form their nests or <a href="/wiki/Bivouac_(ants)" title="Bivouac (ants)">bivouac</a>, which they change almost daily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> At the end of the nomadic phase, the larvae will spin pupal cases and no longer require food. The colony can then live in the same bivouac site for around 20 days, foraging only on approximately two-thirds of these days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> Among the army ants are some species that venture out only at night, but no adequate studies of their activities have been made.</p>
<p>The stationary phase, which lasts about two to three weeks, begins when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larvae" title="Larvae">larvae</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pupate" title="Pupate">pupate</a>. From this point on, the prey that were previously fed to the larvae are now fed exclusively to the queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> The abdomen (<a href="/wiki/Gaster_(insect_anatomy)" title="Gaster (insect anatomy)">gaster</a>) of the queen swells significantly, and she lays her eggs. At the end of the stationary phase, both the pupae emerge from their cocoons (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eclosion" title="Eclosion">eclosion</a>) and the next generation of eggs hatch so the colony has a new group of workers and larvae. After this, the ants resume the nomadic phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Gotwald.2C_William_H._1982-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colony_fission">Colony fission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colony fission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Army ants will split into groups when the size of the colony has reached a size threshold which happens approximately every three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_2009-10">[9]</a></sup> Wingless virgin queens will hatch among a male sexual brood that will hatch at a later date. When the colony fissions, there are two ways new queens are decided. A possible outcome is a new queen will stay at the original nest with a portion of the workers and the male brood while the old queen will leave with the other portion of workers and find a new nest. Another possibility is that the workers will reject the old queen and new queens will head the two new colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schneirla.2C_T._C._1971-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup> The workers will affiliate with individual queens based on the pheromone cues that are unique to each queen. When new bivouacs are formed, communication between the original colony and the new bivouacs will cease to exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen_behavior">Queen behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Queen behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Being the largest ants on Earth, army ants, such as African <i><a href="/wiki/Dorylus" title="Dorylus">Dorylus</a></i> queens hold the world record in reproductive potential among the insects, with an egg-laying capacity of several millions per month. Army ant queens never have to leave the protection of the colony, where they mate with foreign incoming males which disperse on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuptial_flights" title="Nuptial flights">nuptial flights</a>. The exact mating behavior of the army ant queen is still unknown, but observations seem to imply that queens may be fertilized by multiple males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer.26Johnson.26Boomsma_2007_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer.26Johnson.26Boomsma_2007-11">[10]</a></sup> Due to the queen's large reproductive potential, one colony of army ants can be related to a single queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2004_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2004-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the queen ant dies, there is no replacement and army ants cannot rear emergency queens. Most of the time, if the queen dies, the colony will most likely die too. Queen loss can occur due to accidents during emigrations, predator attack, old age or illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_2009_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_2009-10">[9]</a></sup> However, there are possibilities to avoid colony death. When a colony loses its queen, the worker ants will usually fuse with another colony that has a queen in a few days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourke.2C_Andrew_F._G._1995_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bourke.2C_Andrew_F._G._1995-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2010_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2010-12">[11]</a></sup> Sometimes, the workers will backtrack along the paths of prior emigrations to search for a queen that has been lost or merge with a sister colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2010_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2010-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneirla_AMNH_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schneirla_AMNH-13">[12]</a></sup> By merging with a related colony, the workers would increase their overall <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_fitness" title="Inclusive fitness">inclusive fitness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_2009_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_2009-10">[9]</a></sup> The workers that merge into a new colony may cause the colony to increase in size by 50%.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_selection_by_workers">Sexual selection by workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sexual selection by workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Workers in army ant species have a unique role in selecting both the queen and the male mate.</p>
<p>When the queens emerge, the workers in the colony will form two ‘systems’ or arms in opposite directions. These queens that are hatched will move down either the arms and only two queens will succeed, one for each branch. The remaining new queens will be left in the middle and are abandoned to die. Two new bivouacs will be formed and break off into different directions. The workers will surround the two to-be queens and ensure they reach the arms and survive. These workers that surround the queens are affected by the CHC (<a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromone</a>) profile emitted from the new queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>When males hatch from their <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a>, they will fly off to find a mate. For males to access the queen and mate, they must run through the workers in the colony. Males that are favored are superficially similar in size and shape to the queen. The males also produce large quantities of pheromones to pacify the worker ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Franks.26Nigel.26Holldobler_1987-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction_responsibilities_and_problems">Reproduction responsibilities and problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reproduction responsibilities and problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a>, the queen is the primary individual responsible for reproduction in the colony. Analysis of genotypes have confirmed that workers are, on average, more closely related to the offspring of the queen than to that of other workers, and that workers rarely, if ever, reproduce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2006_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2006-14">[13]</a></sup> Three factors have been suggested to rationalize the loss of worker reproduction in the presence of a queen. First, if the worker reproduces, it lowers the general performance of the colony because it is not working. Second, workers increase their inclusive fitness by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policing" title="Policing">policing</a> other workers because they themselves are more related to the queen’s offspring than other worker’s offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2006_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2006-14">[13]</a></sup> Lastly, the large male larvae become too large to be transported, forcing colonies with a sexual brood to nest for a period of 41–56 days, as compared to non-reproductive colonies that remain in the nest an average of 17 days before returning to a nomadic phase. This suggests that if workers produced male offspring, they might be hatched out of sync with the queen's sexual brood and not likely to be successfully reared to adulthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer_et_al_2006_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer_et_al_2006-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foraging">Foraging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Foraging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The whole colony of army ants can consume up to 500,000 prey animals each day, so can have a significant influence on the <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>, diversity, and behavior of their prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks__Fletcher_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franks_Fletcher-15">[14]</a></sup> The prey selection differs with the species. Underground species prey primarily on ground-dwelling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthropods" title="Arthropods">arthropods</a> and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larvae" title="Larvae">larvae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthworms" title="Earthworms">earthworms</a>, and occasionally also the young of vertebrates, turtle eggs, or oily seeds. A majority of the species, the "colony robbers", specialize in the offspring of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ants" title="Ants">ants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasps" title="Wasps">wasps</a>. Only a few species seem to have the very broad spectrum of prey seen in the raiding species. Even these species do not eat every kind of animal. Although small vertebrates that get caught in the raid will be killed, the jaws of the American <i><a href="/wiki/Eciton" title="Eciton">Eciton</a></i> are not suited to this type of prey, in contrast to the African <i><a href="/wiki/Dorylus" title="Dorylus">Dorylus</a></i>. These undesired prey are simply left behind and consumed by scavengers or by the flies that accompany the ant swarm. Only a few species hunt primarily on the surface of the earth; they seek their prey mainly in leaf litter and in low vegetation. About five species hunt in higher trees, where they can attack birds and their eggs, although they focus on hunting other social insects along with their eggs and larvae. Colonies of army ants are large compared to the colonies of other Formicidae. Colonies can have over 15 million workers and can transport 3000 prey (items) per hour during the raid period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kronauer.26Johnson.26Boomsma_2007_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kronauer.26Johnson.26Boomsma_2007-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couzin.26Franks_2003_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Couzin.26Franks_2003-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>When army ants forage, the trails that are formed can be over 20 m wide and over 100 m long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couzin.26Franks_2003_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Couzin.26Franks_2003-16">[15]</a></sup> They stay on the path through the use of a concentration gradient of pheromones. The concentration of pheromone is highest in the middle of the trail, splitting the trail into two distinct regions: area with high concentration and two areas with low concentrations of pheromones. The outbound ants will occupy the outer two lanes and the returning ants will occupy the central lane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couzin.26Franks_2003_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Couzin.26Franks_2003-16">[15]</a></sup> The returning worker ants have also to be found to emit more pheromones than those leaving the nest, causing the difference in concentration of pheromone in the trails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deneubourg_et_al_1989_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deneubourg_et_al_1989-17">[16]</a></sup> The pheromones will allow foraging to be much more efficient by allowing the army ants to avoid their own former paths and those of their conspecifics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franks__Fletcher_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franks_Fletcher-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>While foraging, army ants cause many invertebrates to flee from their hiding places under leaves of the forest floor, under tree bark, and other such locations, thereby allowing predators to catch them more easily. For example, in the tropical rainforests of Panama, swarms of army ants attract many species of birds to this feast of scrambling insects, spiders, scorpions, worms, and other animals. Some of these birds are named “antbirds” due to this tendency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> While focused on feeding on these invertebrates, birds at army-ant swarms typically allow very close approach by people—within 1 or 2 meters in many cases—often providing the best opportunities to see many of these species. Depending on the size of the ant swarm and the amount of prey the ants stir up, birds can number from a few to dozens of individuals. Birds that frequent army-ant swarms include the <a href="/wiki/White-whiskered_puffbird" title="White-whiskered puffbird">white-whiskered puffbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rufous_motmot" title="Rufous motmot">rufous motmot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rufous-vented_ground_cuckoo" title="Rufous-vented ground cuckoo">rufous-vented ground cuckoo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray-necked_wood_rail" title="Gray-necked wood rail">gray-necked wood rail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plain-brown_woodcreeper" title="Plain-brown woodcreeper">plain-brown woodcreeper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_barred_woodcreeper" title="Northern barred woodcreeper">northern barred woodcreeper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_woodcreeper" title="Cocoa woodcreeper">cocoa woodcreeper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-striped_woodcreeper" title="Black-striped woodcreeper">black-striped woodcreeper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fasciated_antshrike" title="Fasciated antshrike">fasciated antshrike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-crowned_antshrike" title="Black-crowned antshrike">black-crowned antshrike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spotted_antbird" title="Spotted antbird">spotted antbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicolored_antbird" title="Bicolored antbird">bicolored antbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocellated_antbird" title="Ocellated antbird">ocellated antbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chestnut-backed_antbird" title="Chestnut-backed antbird">chestnut-backed antbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-faced_antthrush" title="Black-faced antthrush">black-faced antthrush</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray-headed_tanager" title="Gray-headed tanager">gray-headed tanager</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>Army ants do not build a nest like most other ants. Instead, they build a living nest with their bodies, known as a <a href="/wiki/Bivouac_(ants)" title="Bivouac (ants)">bivouac</a>. Bivouacs tend to be found in tree trunks or in burrows dug by the ants. The members of the bivouac hold onto each other's legs and so build a sort of ball, which may look unstructured to a layman's eyes, but is actually a well-organized structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawkins1986_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-dawkins1986-20">[19]</a></sup> The older female workers are located on the exterior; in the interior are the younger female workers. At the smallest disturbance, soldiers gather on the top surface of the bivouac, ready to defend the nest with powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pincer_(arthropod_anatomy)" title="Pincer (arthropod anatomy)">pincers</a> and (in the case of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aenictinae" title="Aenictinae">Aenictinae</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitoninae" title="Ecitoninae">Ecitoninae</a>) stingers. The interior of the nest is filled with numerous passages and contains many chambers with food, the queen, the larvae, and the eggs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbionts">Symbionts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Symbionts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many species of army ant are widely considered to be <a href="/wiki/Keystone_species" title="Keystone species">keystone species</a>, due to the high number of vertebrate and invertebrate associates that rely on army ant colonies for nutrition or protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boswell_et_al._1998_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boswell_et_al._1998-21">[20]</a></sup> During their hunt, many surface-raiding army ants are accompanied by various birds, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antbirds" title="Antbirds">antbirds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrushes" title="Thrushes">thrushes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovenbirds" title="Ovenbirds">ovenbirds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrens" title="Wrens">wrens</a>, which devour the insects that are flushed out by the ants, a behavior known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleptoparasitism" title="Cleptoparasitism">cleptoparasitism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrege_et_al._2005_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wrege_et_al._2005-22">[21]</a></sup> A wide variety of arthropods including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphylinid" title="Staphylinid">staphylinid</a> beetles and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mites" title="Mites">mites</a> also follow colonies. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropical" title="Neotropical">Neotropical</a> army ant <i><a href="/wiki/Eciton_burchellii" title="Eciton burchellii">Eciton burchellii</a></i> has an estimated 350 to 500 animal associates, the most of any one species known to science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rettenmeyer_2011_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rettenmeyer_2011-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_ant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, "army ant" in the broad sense, referred to various members of five different ant subfamilies: in two of these cases, the <a href="/wiki/Ponerinae" title="Ponerinae">Ponerinae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myrmicinae" title="Myrmicinae">Myrmicinae</a>, only a few species and genera exhibit legionary behavior; in the other three lineages, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitoninae" title="Ecitoninae">Ecitoninae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorylinae" title="Dorylinae">Dorylinae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leptanillinae" title="Leptanillinae">Leptanillinae</a>, all of the constituent species are legionary. More recently, ant classifications now recognize an additional New World subfamily, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leptanilloidinae" title="Leptanilloidinae">Leptanilloidinae</a>, which also consists of obligate legionary species, so is another group now included among the army ants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A 2003 study of 30 species (by Sean Brady of <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>) indicates that army ants of subfamilies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitoninae" title="Ecitoninae">Ecitoninae</a> (South America), <a href="/wiki/Dorylinae" title="Dorylinae">Dorylinae</a> (Africa) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aenictinae" title="Aenictinae">Aenictinae</a> (Asia) together formed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group, based on data from three molecular genes and one mitochondrial gene. Brady concluded that these two groups are, therefore, a single lineage that evolved in the mid-Cretaceous period in <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[n 2]</a></sup> so the two subfamilies are now generally united into a single subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecitoninae" title="Ecitoninae">Ecitoninae</a>, though this is still not universally recognized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engel2005_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-engel2005-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
